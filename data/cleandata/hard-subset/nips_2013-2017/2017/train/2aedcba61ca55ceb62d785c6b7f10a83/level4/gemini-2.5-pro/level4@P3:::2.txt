### **Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by exploring group-additive models. These models serve as a flexible intermediate between overly restrictive additive models, which ignore variable interactions, and fully nonparametric models, which suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The core problem tackled is that the group structure, which defines which variables interact, is typically unknown and must be learned from data.

The authors make several key contributions. First, they formalize the concept of an "intrinsic group additive structure," defining it as the unique, minimal partition of variables that explains the data. Second, and most importantly, they propose a novel method to identify this structure by introducing a structure-penalized kernel ridge regression objective. The penalty term is the paper's main innovation: a complexity measure for a given group structure derived from the covering numbers of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The resulting objective function is optimized over both the regression function and the space of possible group partitions. The paper provides two algorithms for this optimization: an exhaustive search for a small number of predictors and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger problems. The proposed method is supported by theoretical results proving the consistency of the group structure estimator and is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—quantifying the complexity of a group-additive structure using a penalty based on RKHS covering numbers—is novel, principled, and elegant. This provides a formal, data-driven approach to a significant problem in nonparametric regression: automatically discovering meaningful interaction structures. If the authors' review of prior work is complete, this method appears to be a substantial advance over existing techniques that lie between additive and fully nonparametric models.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and comprehensive. The theoretical section provides a consistency proof, demonstrating that under appropriate conditions, the estimated group structure converges in probability to the true intrinsic structure as the sample size increases. This theoretical backing adds significant weight to the proposed method. The empirical evaluation, which includes simulations and a real-data application, effectively demonstrates the method's potential.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. It begins by motivating the problem well, rigorously defines the concepts of amiable and intrinsic group structures, and then systematically develops the proposed method and its theoretical justification. The overall argument is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The main weakness is the computational difficulty of solving the optimization problem over the space of group structures. The number of possible partitions grows extremely rapidly with the number of predictors (`p`). While the paper acknowledges this and proposes a backward stepwise algorithm as a practical heuristic, this greedy approach is not guaranteed to find the global optimum and, as shown in the simulations (Model 4), can fail to identify the true structure. This scalability issue may limit the method's immediate practical impact on problems with a large number of variables.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

The paper is a strong submission, and my comments are primarily aimed at clarification and minor improvements.

*   **Characterization of Interaction:** Is there a simpler or more intuitive way to characterize an "interaction" between two variables than their co-occurrence within a group in the model `f(X) = Σ f_u(X_u)`? For instance, could one relate this to concepts from functional ANOVA decomposition? A brief comment could help connect the group-additive framework to other familiar concepts of interaction.

*   **Use of "Almost Surely":** In Section 2.3.1, the paper states that for an amiable structure `G`, we have `f_G* = f_G` "almost surely (in population)". This phrasing is a bit ambiguous. Does this refer to the functions being equal almost everywhere with respect to the data distribution `P_X`, or is there an asymptotic sense (as `n -> ∞`) intended here? Please clarify.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The manuscript would benefit from a careful proofreading to correct several typos and grammatical errors. Addressing these would improve the overall polish and readability of an already clear paper.

*   In Equation (3), the summation over the data points `i=1` to `n` is missing from the empirical risk term `(1/n) Σ (y_i - f_G(x_i))^2`.
*   The term "convolution kernel" is used. While correct, "translation-invariant kernel" is perhaps a more common term in the machine learning literature.
*   Minor Typos:
    *   "on one hand... whereas on the other hand" (Introduction, par 1) -> "on the one hand... on the other hand"
    *   "The key drawback of the additive model is that it does not assume interactions" -> "...is that it assumes no interactions"
    *   "G∗ exists and is unique." (Section 2.3.1)
    *   "tuning parameters" (Section 2.4)
    *   In Algorithm 2, Line 1: "Start with" instead of "State with".
    *   "by comparing" (not in the provided draft text, but noted from original draft)

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and well-supported method for an important problem. The main weakness is the computational scalability of the search over group structures, which is a common challenge for methods of this type. The strengths in originality and theoretical soundness, however, make this a significant contribution to the field.
