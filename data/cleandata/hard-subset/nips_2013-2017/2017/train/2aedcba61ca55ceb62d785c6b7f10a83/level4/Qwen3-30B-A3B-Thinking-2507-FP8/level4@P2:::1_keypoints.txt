- Addresses high-dimensional nonparametric regression by identifying intrinsic group additive structure to allow within-group interactions but exclude cross-group interactions.
- Proposes a formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (minimal group structure representing the true function).
- Introduces a structure-penalized kernel method to jointly identify group structure and estimate nonparametric functions.
- Derives a complexity measure for group structures using RKHS covering numbers and incorporates it into a penalized estimation framework.
- Provides consistency proofs showing estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases.
- Implements two algorithms: exhaustive search for small problems and a backward stepwise (greedy) approach for larger datasets.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on simulations (successful recovery of true groups) and an application to the Boston Housing dataset (interpretable results).
- Strength: rigorous theoretical contribution with clear definitions and consistency results for intrinsic group additive structure.
- Strength: novel RKHS-based complexity measure that captures fit vs. structural complexity trade-offs.
- Strength: practical algorithms that perform well in the considered scenarios.
- Strength: strong theoretical grounding in RKHS theory supporting the methodology.
- Weakness: related work section is incomplete, missing connections to structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection literature.
- Weakness: experimental evaluation lacks proper baselines and comparative methods for group structure identification.
- Weakness: computational inefficiency—exhaustive search is infeasible for large problems; stepwise method is greedy and may miss optimal solutions.
- Weakness: Boston Housing application lacks statistical rigor in presentation and interpretation.
- Rebuttal addressed theoretical novelty but experimental shortcomings remain unaddressed.
- Recommendation: borderline, leaning toward acceptance conditional on substantially strengthening related work and comparative experimental evaluation.
