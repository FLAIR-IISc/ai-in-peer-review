The paper explores kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, starting with the "additive model" where regressors are of the form f = ∑ fp(Xp), ignoring correlations between different dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors introduce the "group additive model", which groups dimensions to model correlations between them. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose algorithms for finding optimal groupings, although these methods seem somewhat ad hoc. The results are presented for small synthetic and real-world cases.

A primary concern with the paper is its inadequate discussion of related work, making it challenging for non-experts to assess the novelty of the contributions. The task of finding optimal groups in data is also explored in fields like "structured sparsity" and learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these are not mentioned. Furthermore, the problem can be viewed as an instance of "feature selection", which is also not discussed. The related work section primarily references a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient to evaluate the paper's novelty.

The theoretical treatment, however, appears to be novel. The authors define a complexity measure for groupings, favoring many small groups, derived as an upper bound on the covering number. Although this measure is sensible, it does not lend itself to efficient optimization, leading the authors to propose either exhaustive search, which scales poorly, or a greedy method akin to forward feature selection. While acknowledging the challenges of optimizing discrete functions, it is disappointing that the authors did not develop a more efficiently optimizable measure. The authors mention exploring and comparing different measures (line 174), but do not provide further insights, making it difficult to assess the suitability of the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the authors validate their framework using simple synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset, but do not provide a baseline or compare their method to others. This makes it challenging to evaluate the quality of the presented results.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. Although I still believe the experiments are limited and the authors failed to adequately relate their work to existing machine learning concepts, I acknowledge the paper's valid contributions and have improved my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to enhance their discussion of related work in the published version of the paper.
