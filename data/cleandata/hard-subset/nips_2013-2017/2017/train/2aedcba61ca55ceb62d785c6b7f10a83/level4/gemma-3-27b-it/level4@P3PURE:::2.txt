Summary:
This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, where the regression function is additive between groups of predictor variables, determined by a partitioning. This model offers a compelling interpolation between fully nonparametric models – which can be difficult to fit – and traditional additive models, which may be overly restrictive. The paper specifically addresses the scenario where the group structure is unknown and must be learned from the data. To this end, the authors propose a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) balls. This penalty is incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective, resulting in an optimization problem over both the group structure (which, together with the kernel, defines a function space via the direct sum of RKHSs over each group of variables) and the regression estimate within each group. Two algorithms are presented for approximately solving this compound optimization problem, and theoretical results demonstrate (a) the rate at which the empirical risk of the estimate converges to the true risk of the optimal estimator, and (b) the consistency of the group structure estimate – specifically, that the probability of correctly identifying the true group structure approaches 1 as the sample size *n* approaches infinity. The paper concludes with experimental results on both synthetic and real datasets.

Main Comments:
The key innovation of this work appears to be the recognition that the complexity of a given group structure can be quantified through the covering numbers of its corresponding direct sum space. The paper is comprehensive, presenting a well-motivated and novel method, along with reasonably strong theoretical and empirical results. While I am not deeply familiar with alternative approaches for modeling dependencies between fully additive and nonparametric models, assuming the discussion in Lines 31-50 provides a complete overview, this work represents a potentially significant advance in the field. As noted in the Discussion section, a primary limitation of the method is the computational difficulty of solving the optimization problem over the group structure when the number of variables is large. The paper is generally clearly written, though it contains a number of typographical errors.
