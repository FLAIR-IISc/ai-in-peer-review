The paper studies kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It starts from the additive model, which considers regressors of the form f(x) = sum_p f_p(x_p), i.e., cross-dimensional correlations are ignored. Recognizing the limitations of this assumption, the authors introduce a group additive model in which features are partitioned into groups so that correlations within groups can be modeled. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose somewhat ad hoc algorithms for selecting groupings. Results are presented on small synthetic datasets and the Boston Housing dataset.

My main concern is the inadequate discussion of related work, which makes it difficult for a non-expert to assess the paper’s novelty. The task of discovering groups of features arises in several established areas, such as structured sparsity and the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not discussed. Similarly, the problem can be viewed through the lens of feature selection, but this is not addressed either. In effect, the related work section largely reduces to a single KDD 2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for evaluating the contribution’s originality.

That said, the theoretical development appears novel. The authors derive a simple complexity measure over groupings—one that favors many small groups—based on an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. However, the resulting measure is not easy to optimize. The paper proposes either exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy scheme akin to forward feature selection. While I recognize that discrete optimization over groupings is intrinsically challenging, I was disappointed that the authors did not arrive at a criterion that is more amenable to efficient optimization than cross-validation over discrete choices. The paper mentions that they have been “investigating and comparing different measures” (line 174), but no further insight or guidance is provided, leaving it hard to judge whether the chosen measure is appropriate.

Experimentally, the paper presents simple synthetic validations and a small real-data example on Boston Housing. There are no baselines or comparisons with alternative methods. As a result, it is difficult to assess the empirical quality of the proposed approach.

== Post-rebuttal ==
The authors clarified some of my concerns in the rebuttal. I still find the experiments thin and the discussion of connections to core ideas in machine learning unacceptably weak. Nonetheless, I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions, and I have raised my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially improve the related work section in the final version.
