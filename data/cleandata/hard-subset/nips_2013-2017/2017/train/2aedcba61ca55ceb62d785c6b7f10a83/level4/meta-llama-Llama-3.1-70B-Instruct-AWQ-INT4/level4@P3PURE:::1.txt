The paper explores kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, focusing on the "additive model" as its starting point. This model considers regressors of the form f = ∑ fp(Xp), ignoring correlations between different dimensions. However, neglecting these correlations is problematic, prompting the authors to consider the "group additive model." This approach groups different dimensions to model correlations between some dimensions. The authors provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose algorithms for finding optimal groupings, albeit somewhat ad hoc. They present results for small synthetic and real-world cases.

A key issue with the paper is its inadequate description of related work, making it challenging for non-experts to assess the true novelty of the research. The task of finding optimal groups in data is also addressed in "structured sparsity" and when learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet the authors fail to mention these fields. Similarly, the problem can be viewed as an instance of "feature selection," which is not discussed. The related work section primarily references a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient to determine the actual novelty of the paper.

The theoretical treatment, however, appears to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure for a grouping, favoring many small groups, derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This approach is sensible, but the measure does not lend itself to easy optimization. Consequently, the authors propose either an exhaustive search, which scales poorly, or a greedy method akin to forward feature selection. While acknowledging that discrete functions, such as those describing optimal groupings, are generally difficult to optimize, I was somewhat disappointed that the authors did not develop a measure that could be optimized more efficiently than using cross-validation. The authors mention investigating and comparing different measures (line 174), but provide no further insights, making it challenging to evaluate the suitability of the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the authors provide simple synthetic validation of their framework and a basic example using the Boston Housing data. However, they do not include a baseline or compare their method with other approaches, making it difficult to assess the quality of the presented results.

Post rebuttal:

The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. Although I still believe the experiments are limited, and the authors did an unacceptably poor job of relating their work to machine learning concepts, I acknowledge that the paper makes valid contributions. Consequently, I have improved my score somewhat. I strongly encourage the authors to enhance their discussion of related work when the paper is published.
