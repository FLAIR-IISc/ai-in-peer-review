This paper addresses kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, building upon the additive model framework. Recognizing the limitations of assuming complete independence between regressors, the authors explore the “group additive model,” where dimensions are grouped to allow for correlations within groups. They provide a formal treatment of this model, deriving a complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers and proposing algorithms for identifying optimal groupings – an exhaustive search and a greedy stepwise approach. The paper presents results on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The core contribution lies in the rigorous definition of the “intrinsic group additive structure” and the corresponding complexity measure. While the concept of grouping variables to capture interactions isn't entirely new, the formalization and the derived penalty term based on covering numbers appear novel. However, a significant weakness is the limited discussion of related work. The paper overlooks relevant fields such as structured sparsity, graphical model learning, and feature selection, which all address similar problems of identifying dependencies and reducing dimensionality. The lack of connection to these areas makes it difficult to assess the true novelty of the approach. While the authors cite a KDD 2015 paper, this isn't sufficient to establish the position of the work within the broader machine learning landscape.

The theoretical derivation of the complexity measure is a strength, providing a principled basis for the penalty term in the optimization problem. The measure, favoring smaller groups, seems reasonable. However, its practical impact is hampered by the difficulty of optimizing the resulting objective function. The proposed algorithms—exhaustive search and a greedy method—are either computationally expensive or potentially suboptimal. The authors mention exploring alternative complexity measures but provide limited insight into why the chosen measure was preferred. A discussion of the trade-offs between different measures would strengthen the paper.

The experimental evaluation is rather limited. The synthetic results demonstrate the method's ability to recover known structures, but lack comparison to established baselines. The application to the Boston Housing dataset is similarly unconvincing without any comparative analysis. It's difficult to judge the effectiveness of the proposed approach in the absence of a benchmark. The authors should include comparisons to relevant methods, even if those methods require different modeling assumptions.

**Strengths:**

*   Rigorous formalization of the group additive model and the intrinsic group additive structure.
*   Novel complexity measure based on covering numbers.
*   Clear presentation of the proposed algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficient discussion of related work in structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection.
*   Limited scalability of the proposed algorithms (exhaustive search) or potential suboptimality (greedy approach).
*   Lack of comparative experimental evaluation against relevant baselines.
*   Limited insight into the choice of the specific complexity measure.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   The theoretical contributions, particularly the complexity measure, are novel and potentially valuable.
*   The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the theoretical foundations of their work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough literature review weakens the claim of novelty.
*   The limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The scalability issues and the absence of comparisons to alternative algorithms raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for group additive modeling, but suffers from a limited discussion of related work and a lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the practical impact is unclear. I currently rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The authors should significantly expand the related work section and include a more thorough experimental comparison to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
