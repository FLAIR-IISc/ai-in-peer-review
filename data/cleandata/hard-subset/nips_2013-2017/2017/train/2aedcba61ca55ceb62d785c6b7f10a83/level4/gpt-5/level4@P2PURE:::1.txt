The paper studies kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It begins with the additive model, which uses regressors of the form f = ∑_p f_p(X_p), thereby ignoring correlations across dimensions. Since this is problematic, the authors instead consider a group additive model that partitions dimensions into groups so that correlations within groups can be modeled. They provide a formal treatment of this framework and propose somewhat ad hoc algorithms for identifying optimal groupings. Results are presented on small synthetic datasets and a small real-world example.

My main concern is the inadequate discussion of related work, which makes it difficult for me (as a non-expert) to assess the true novelty. The task of discovering optimal groups also appears in structured sparsity and in learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not discussed. The problem can also be cast as feature selection, but this is likewise omitted. In effect, the related work largely reduces to a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for me to gauge the paper’s novelty.

That said, the theoretical development appears novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure over groupings—one that favors many small groups—derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. The drawback is that the measure is not easy to optimize: the authors resort either to exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or to a greedy approach akin to forward feature selection. While I recognize that discrete objectives (such as those defining groupings) are generally hard to optimize, I was disappointed that the paper did not produce a measure that can be optimized more efficiently than via cross-validation. The authors mention they have been “investigating and comparing different measures” (line 174), but provide no further insight, making it hard to judge the suitability of the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the paper offers simple synthetic validations and a small Boston Housing example. There are no baselines and no comparisons to other methods, leaving me unable to assess the quality of the results.

Post rebuttal:
The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. I still find the experiments thin and believe the paper does an unacceptably poor job of situating the work within broader machine learning ideas. However, I acknowledge that the paper makes valid contributions and have raised my score somewhat. I strongly encourage the authors to at least improve the discussion of related work in the final version.
