This paper addresses kernel regression in the high-dimensional setting. The authors' starting point is the standard "additive model," f = Σ_p f_p(X_p), which assumes independence between dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this assumption, the paper introduces a "group additive model" that clusters dimensions into groups, allowing for interactions to be modeled within each group. The authors provide a formal theoretical treatment of this model and propose several heuristic algorithms for finding optimal groupings. The approach is evaluated on small synthetic and real-world datasets.

A primary weakness of the paper is its inadequate survey of related work. This makes it difficult for a reader who is not an expert in this specific area to assess the novelty of the contributions. For example, the task of identifying optimal groups of variables is a central theme in fields such as structured sparsity and graphical model learning (e.g., Bayesian networks), yet the authors do not mention these connections. Similarly, the problem can be framed as a form of feature selection, another relevant area that is not discussed. The discussion of prior art is largely limited to a single KDD 2015 paper [8], which is insufficient context for evaluating the paper's contribution to the field.

That being said, the theoretical treatment appears to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure for a given grouping, which favors a larger number of smaller groups. This measure is derived as an upper bound on the covering number of the function class, which is a sensible approach. The downside, however, is that this complexity measure is not amenable to efficient optimization. Consequently, the authors resort to an exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy method analogous to forward feature selection. While the optimization of discrete functions is known to be challenging, it is somewhat disappointing that the proposed measure does not lead to a more efficient optimization scheme. The authors mention that they have been "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) but offer no further discussion, making it difficult to assess the suitability of the one chosen for this work.

The experimental validation is limited. The authors present results from a small-scale synthetic experiment and a single application to the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, the paper provides no baselines and does not compare the proposed method against any alternatives. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to evaluate the practical significance of the presented results.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors' rebuttal clarified some of my concerns. However, I maintain that the experiments are insufficient and that the paper's failure to connect its contributions to the broader machine learning literature is a significant weakness. Nevertheless, I acknowledge the paper's valid contributions and have increased my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to significantly expand their discussion of related work in the final version of the paper.
