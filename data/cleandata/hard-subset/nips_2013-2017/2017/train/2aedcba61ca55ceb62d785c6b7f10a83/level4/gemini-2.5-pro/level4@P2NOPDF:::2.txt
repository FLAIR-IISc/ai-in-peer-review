### Summary

This paper investigates group-additive nonparametric regression models. These models assume the regression function is additive across distinct groups of predictor variables, effectively interpolating between fully nonparametric models, which are often difficult to fit, and purely additive models, which can be overly restrictive. The central problem addressed is learning this group structure from data when it is not known a priori.

To achieve this, the authors propose a novel penalty function derived from the covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) balls. This penalty is incorporated into the standard kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting objective function can then be optimized jointly over the group structure and the regression function itself. The choice of group structure defines the overall function space as a direct sum of RKHSs, one for each group of variables.

The authors present two algorithms for approximately solving this challenging optimization problem. The paper provides theoretical guarantees, including (a) a convergence rate for the empirical risk and (b) proof of consistency for the group structure estimation, showing that the estimated structure converges to the true structure as the sample size increases. Finally, the method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

### Main Comments

The key innovation of this work is the insight that the complexity of a given group structure can be quantified using the covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum function space. Overall, the paper is comprehensive, presenting a well-motivated and novel method supported by solid theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

While I am not an expert on all alternative approaches for models that bridge the gap between additive and fully nonparametric regression, the literature review appears thorough. Assuming the discussion in Lines 31-50 provides a fair and complete overview of the state of the art, this paper represents a potentially significant advance.

The authors rightly acknowledge a key limitation in their Discussion section: the computational challenge of optimizing over the group structure when the number of predictor variables is large. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, though it would benefit from careful proofreading to correct numerous typographical errors.
