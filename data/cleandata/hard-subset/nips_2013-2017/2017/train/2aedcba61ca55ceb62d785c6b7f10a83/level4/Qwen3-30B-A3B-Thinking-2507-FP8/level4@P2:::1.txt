

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: balancing model flexibility with computational feasibility and interpretability. The authors propose a novel framework for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in kernel nonparametric regression, where predictor variables are grouped such that interactions within groups are permitted while interactions across groups are excluded. The key contribution is a formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal group structure that can represent the true function) and a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneously identifying this structure and estimating the nonparametric functions.

The paper builds on previous work in additive models [5, 13] and group additive models [11], but makes a critical distinction by rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure, which resolves the nonidentifiability issue present in prior work. The authors derive a complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), which they use to develop a penalized estimation framework. The theoretical contribution is strengthened by consistency proofs showing that the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases.

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness through simulation studies and a real-world application on the Boston Housing dataset. The authors propose two algorithms: an exhaustive search for small-scale problems and a backward stepwise approach for larger datasets. The results show successful recovery of true group structures in synthetic data and meaningful interpretations in the housing application.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. A clear theoretical contribution with rigorous definitions and consistency proofs for the intrinsic group additive structure
2. A novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers that properly captures the trade-off between model fit and structural complexity
3. Practical algorithms for structure identification that work well in the scenarios considered
4. Theoretical grounding of the approach through RKHS theory, which provides a solid foundation for the complexity measure

Weaknesses include:
1. The related work section is insufficiently comprehensive. The paper fails to engage with key literature on structured sparsity [2, 7], graphical models [9], and feature selection, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the proposed approach. The authors acknowledge investigating multiple complexity measures (line 174) but do not discuss why their specific choice was optimal or how it compares to alternatives.
2. The experimental evaluation lacks proper baselines for comparison. The authors do not compare their method with existing approaches for group structure identification or additive modeling, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of their method.
3. The computational efficiency of the proposed methods is limited. The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally prohibitive for large problems, and the stepwise approach is greedy and may not find the optimal solution, as the authors themselves acknowledge.
4. The Boston Housing application provides interesting visualizations but lacks statistical rigor in its presentation and interpretation.

The paper makes a valid theoretical contribution to the problem of group structure identification in nonparametric regression. However, the insufficient engagement with related work and lack of comparative experiments limit the paper's impact. The authors have addressed some concerns in their rebuttal, particularly regarding the theoretical novelty, but the experimental evaluation remains thin.

Pro acceptance arguments:
- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for group additive structure identification, which has been a gap in the literature
- The intrinsic group additive structure is well-defined and the consistency proofs are sound
- The proposed complexity measure is theoretically grounded in RKHS theory
- The method shows promising results in synthetic and real-world applications

Con rejection arguments:
- The related work section is inadequate, failing to properly contextualize the contribution within the broader literature on structured learning
- The experimental evaluation lacks baselines for comparison, making it difficult to assess performance improvements
- The computational limitations of the proposed algorithms are not adequately addressed
- The paper does not clearly articulate how the proposed method differs from and improves upon existing approaches for group structure identification

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the insufficient contextualization within the machine learning literature and lack of comparative experiments prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. I recommend a borderline decision, leaning toward acceptance with the caveat that the authors must substantially strengthen the related work section and experimental evaluation in the final version. The authors should explicitly compare their approach with relevant methods in structured sparsity, graphical models, and additive modeling literature, and provide more comprehensive experimental results with appropriate baselines.
