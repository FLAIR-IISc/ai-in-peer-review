The paper explores kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, introducing the "additive model" as its starting point. This model considers regressors of the form f = ∑ fp(Xp), which ignores correlations between different dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors propose the "group additive model," where dimensions are grouped to capture correlations between some dimensions. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and present algorithms for finding optimal groupings, although these algorithms seem somewhat ad hoc. The authors demonstrate their approach using small synthetic and real-world datasets.

A major concern with the paper is its inadequate discussion of related work, making it challenging for non-experts to assess the novelty of the research. The problem of finding optimal groups in data is also addressed in "structured sparsity" and when learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet the authors fail to mention these connections. Furthermore, the problem can be viewed as an instance of "feature selection," which is also not discussed. The related work section primarily references a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient to determine the actual novelty of the paper.

The theoretical treatment, however, appears to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure for groupings, favoring many small groups, which is derived as an upper bound on the covering number. While this measure seems reasonable, it does not lend itself to easy optimization. As a result, the authors propose either an exhaustive search, which scales poorly, or a greedy method akin to forward feature selection. Although discrete functions, such as those describing optimal groupings, are generally difficult to optimize, it is disappointing that the authors did not develop a more efficient optimization method. The authors mention investigating and comparing different measures, but they do not provide further insights, making it difficult to evaluate the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the authors provide simple synthetic validation of their framework and a basic example using the Boston Housing data. However, they do not include a baseline or compare their method to others, making it challenging to assess the quality of the results.

After the rebuttal, the authors addressed some of my concerns. While I still believe the experiments are limited and the authors did a poor job of relating their work to existing machine learning ideas, I acknowledge that the paper makes valid contributions. I have improved my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to enhance their discussion of related work when the paper is published.
