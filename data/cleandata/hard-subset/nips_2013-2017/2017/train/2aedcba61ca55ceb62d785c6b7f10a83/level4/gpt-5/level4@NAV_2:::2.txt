Summary:
This paper considers group-additive nonparametric regression, where for some partition of predictors the regression function is additive across groups of variables. This model bridges fully nonparametric regression (flexible but hard to learn) and the standard additive model (more tractable but potentially too restrictive). The focus is on the setting where the unknown group structure must be learned from data. The authors introduce a new penalty, derived from covering numbers of RKHS balls, and add it to a kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting criterion is optimized jointly over the group structure (which determines a direct-sum RKHS) and the within-group regression functions. They present two algorithms to approximately solve this joint problem, and provide theory showing (a) convergence of empirical risk to the true risk of the population optimum, and (b) selection consistency of the learned group structure, i.e., its probability of matching the true structure tends to 1 as n grows. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets illustrate the method.

Main Comments:
The central contribution is to quantify the complexity of a candidate group structure via the covering numbers of the corresponding direct-sum RKHS, and to use this as a structural penalty. The paper is thorough, with a well-motivated, novel approach and generally solid theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence. I am not deeply familiar with other methods that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models, but assuming the discussion in Lines 31–50 is reasonably comprehensive, this looks potentially impactful. As the Discussion notes, the main practical limitation is the combinatorial search over group structures when p is large. The exposition is mostly clear, albeit marred by numerous typos.
