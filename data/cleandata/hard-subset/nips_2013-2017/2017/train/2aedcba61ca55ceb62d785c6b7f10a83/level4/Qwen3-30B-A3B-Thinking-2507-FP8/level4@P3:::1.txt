

The paper proposes a novel approach for identifying group additive structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, addressing a significant limitation of the standard additive model that ignores interactions between predictors. The authors rigorously define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group partition that can represent a function, and develop a structure-penalized kernel method using a complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. The method is theoretically justified with consistency proofs and validated through simulation studies and a Boston Housing data application.

The paper builds on the additive model framework (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990) and the group additive model proposed in Pan et al. (KDD 2015), but makes a significant contribution by formalizing the concept of intrinsic group additive structure and providing a systematic method for its identification. This is particularly valuable as previous work on group additive models did not address the non-uniqueness of group structures or provide a principled approach for their identification. While the paper acknowledges connections to graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009), it fails to adequately contextualize its work within the broader machine learning literature, particularly regarding structured sparsity methods (Bach et al., NIPS 2008; Jacob et al., NIPS 2009) and feature selection techniques, which would have strengthened the novelty claim.

**Quality:** The theoretical development is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and prove its uniqueness. The derivation of the complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is mathematically grounded, and the consistency proof for the proposed method is thorough. The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of nonparametric regression and RKHS theory. The only minor concern is that the complexity measure, while theoretically justified, may not be the most practical choice for optimization, as the authors acknowledge.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The algorithms are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors effectively use examples to illustrate key concepts, such as the non-uniqueness of group structures in Section 2.1. The visualizations in Figure 1 for the Boston Housing application are particularly helpful for interpreting the results.

**Originality:** The concept of the intrinsic group additive structure is novel and well-motivated. The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing two key limitations in previous work on group additive models: non-uniqueness of group structures and the lack of a systematic identification method. While the group additive model itself is not new (Pan et al., KDD 2015), the authors' formalization and extension of this framework represents a significant advance. The paper's distinction between group additive structure and conditional dependence structure (which is more complex) is particularly valuable for understanding the scope of the contribution.

**Significance:** The approach addresses a meaningful challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, offering a potentially valuable middle ground between the standard additive model (which ignores interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). The ability to identify relevant interactions while maintaining interpretability and estimation efficiency has clear practical value. However, the empirical validation is limited in scope, with no comparison to relevant baselines or other state-of-the-art methods, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the approach.

**Strengths:** The theoretical development is rigorous and well-executed, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method. The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is clearly defined and well-motivated. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature by addressing two important limitations in previous work on group additive models. The clarity of presentation and organization is excellent.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the inadequate contextualization within the broader machine learning literature. The authors fail to properly discuss connections to structured sparsity methods (NIPS 2008-2017), graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009), and feature selection techniques, which are directly relevant to the problem of identifying group structures. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the contribution. Additionally, the empirical evaluation is limited, with no comparison to relevant baselines or other state-of-the-art methods. The paper also does not adequately address the computational scalability of the proposed algorithms, particularly for high-dimensional settings.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pros:**
- The paper provides a theoretically sound framework for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression.
- The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is novel, well-defined, and well-motivated.
- The theoretical consistency results are rigorous and valuable for the field.
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Cons:**
- The related work section inadequately contextualizes the contribution within the broader machine learning literature.
- The empirical evaluation lacks comparison with relevant baselines, limiting the assessment of practical significance.
- The proposed algorithms have limited scalability for high-dimensional data.
- The complexity measure, while theoretically justified, is not clearly better than alternatives, and the authors do not provide sufficient justification for their choice.

**Overall Assessment:** The paper presents a theoretically interesting and well-executed approach to group additive structure identification in nonparametric regression. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper's limited contextualization within the machine learning literature and insufficient empirical validation prevent it from making a compelling case for significance. The authors should substantially strengthen the related work section and provide more comprehensive experimental validation to demonstrate the practical utility of their method. Given the current state of the paper, I lean toward rejection, though I acknowledge the theoretical merits of the work. I would encourage the authors to address these concerns in a revised version before considering the paper for publication.
