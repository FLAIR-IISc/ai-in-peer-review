- Addresses limitation of additive models by introducing "group additive structure" and a method to identify intrinsic variable groupings in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.  
- Rigorously defines intrinsic group additive structure as the unique minimal partition preserving interpretability (Theorem 1).  
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel estimator that jointly identifies group structure and estimates nonparametric functions.  
- Introduces a tractable complexity penalty C(G)=∑j α^{|uj|} derived from RKHS covering numbers to regularize group structures.  
- Proves consistency of the proposed method under specified conditions (Theorem 4).  
- Theoretical development is sound, well-motivated, and connects appropriately to RKHS theory and capacity measures.  
- Mathematical presentation and organization are clear; distinction from functional ANOVA and graphical models is articulated.  
- Related work discussion is inadequate—insufficient comparison to structured sparsity, feature grouping, and block-structured graphical models.  
- Choice of the RKHS-based complexity measure is not justified against alternatives (e.g., VC-dimension, Rademacher complexity).  
- Experimental evaluation is limited (small synthetic experiments and one Boston Housing application) and lacks baselines and rigorous performance metrics.  
- Main strengths: rigorous theoretical foundation, clear formalization, practical appeal for interpretability and dimension reduction.  
- Main weaknesses: poor related-work positioning, limited experiments without comparisons, and unclear practical advantage of the chosen complexity measure.  
- Authors’ rebuttal partially addressed related work, but the paper still needs stronger contextualization and more comprehensive experiments.  
- Review summary by criteria: Quality—strong theory but weak experiments; Clarity—mathematically clear but related work needs work; Originality—murky due to limited comparisons; Significance—promising but unproven empirically.  
- Recommendation: borderline acceptance, leaning toward accept if related work and experimental comparisons are substantially improved for the camera-ready version.
