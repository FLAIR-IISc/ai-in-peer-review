

Summary:  
This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, where the regression function is additive across groups of predictors under an unknown partition of the predictor variables. The model interpolates between fully nonparametric regression (difficult to fit) and additive models (often too restrictive). The key contribution lies in learning the group structure from data, for which the authors propose a novel penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls. This penalty is incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective, yielding an optimization problem over both the group structure (defining a function space via direct sums of RKHSs over groups) and the regression estimate within each group. Two algorithms approximate the compound optimization, and theoretical results establish (a) the convergence rate of empirical risk to true risk, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure (probability of matching the true structure approaches 1 as \(n \to \infty\)). Experimental results on synthetic and real data support the approach.  

Main Comments:  
The paper’s key innovation is recognizing that the complexity of a group structure can be quantified via covering numbers of the direct sum space. It presents a well-motivated, novel method with comprehensive theoretical and empirical validation. While I am not deeply familiar with alternative approaches to bridging additive and nonparametric models, the discussion in Lines 31–50 appears sufficiently complete, suggesting this work represents a potentially significant advance. As noted in the Discussion, the primary limitation is computational difficulty in optimizing over group structure when the number of variables is large. The paper is otherwise clearly written, aside from numerous typos.
