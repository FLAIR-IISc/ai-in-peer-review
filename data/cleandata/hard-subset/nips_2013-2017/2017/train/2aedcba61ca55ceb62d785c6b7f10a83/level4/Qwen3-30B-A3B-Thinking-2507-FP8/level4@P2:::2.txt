

This paper addresses an important gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression by proposing a method for identifying group additive structures, which generalizes the standard additive model while avoiding the curse of dimensionality that plagues fully nonparametric approaches. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure for any square integrable function and develop a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous identification of this structure and nonparametric function estimation. The key innovation lies in deriving a novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to penalize group structures, enabling consistent identification of the minimal group additive structure.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by bridging the gap between standard additive models (which assume no interactions) and higher-order functional ANOVA models (which become computationally intractable in high dimensions). Unlike previous work on group additive models (e.g., [11]), this paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the intrinsic group structure and a systematic method for its identification. The authors demonstrate that their approach consistently recovers the true group structure as sample size increases, which is a substantial theoretical advance over prior work.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-justified theoretical results. The authors prove consistency of their method under appropriate conditions, which is a strong theoretical contribution. The experimental results on synthetic and real data (Boston Housing) demonstrate practical effectiveness, though the real-data application could benefit from more detailed interpretation of the identified group structures. The method's computational efficiency is a notable strength, with two algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) addressing scalability concerns for large p.

The paper is generally clear and well-organized, though some technical explanations could be improved. The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) is particularly elegant and addresses a fundamental issue with previous group additive approaches. The connection to graphical models (Section 1) is well-motivated, though the distinction between group additive structures and conditional dependence structures could be clarified. The writing would benefit from more explicit discussion of how the proposed method compares to related approaches like functional ANOVA models [4, 13] and the higher-order interaction model [6].

Regarding originality, the paper presents a novel approach to group additive structure identification that is distinct from existing methods. While previous work has considered group additive structures [11], this paper provides the first systematic method for identifying the intrinsic group structure. The theoretical foundation using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure is particularly innovative, and the authors provide a clear justification for their choice of capacity measure.

The significance of this work is substantial for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. By enabling automatic identification of group structures that balance model flexibility and interpretability, the method has the potential to improve both the accuracy and practical utility of nonparametric models in real-world applications. The approach directly addresses a key limitation of standard additive models (inability to capture interactions) while avoiding the computational burden of higher-order interaction models. The authors' claim that the group additive model "lies in the middle between the original additive model and the functional ANOVA or higher order interaction models" is well-supported by both theory and experiments.

Strengths:
1. Rigorous theoretical foundation with consistency proofs
2. Novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers
3. Clear distinction between intrinsic and other amiable group structures
4. Well-designed experiments with both synthetic and real data
5. Practical algorithms for implementation (exhaustive search and stepwise)

Weaknesses:
1. The real-data application (Section 5) lacks detailed interpretation of the identified group structures and their practical implications
2. The connection to graphical models could be more thoroughly explored
3. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to related approaches like sparse additive models [5] and functional ANOVA [4, 13]
4. Some technical explanations (e.g., the use of "almost surely" in Line 169) could be clarified

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a clear contribution to the theoretical and practical aspects of nonparametric regression. The method addresses a significant limitation in current approaches to high-dimensional regression, and the theoretical results provide strong justification for the proposed approach. The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and appropriate experimental validation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the connection to graphical models, improve the real-data interpretation, and address the minor technical points noted below. The paper's theoretical contribution is particularly strong and would be valuable to the NIPS community.

Minor suggestions:
1. In Line 169, clarify whether "almost surely" refers to the population or sample setting
2. Correct Equation (3) to include the summation over i
3. Replace "convolutional kernel" with "translation-invariant kernel" in Line 205
4. Address typos: "G^ exists and unique" (Line 160), "turning parameters" (Line 247), "State with" (Algorithm 2 Line 1)
5. In Section 5, provide more interpretation of the identified group structures (e.g., why certain variables group together) to strengthen the practical significance

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of nonparametric regression with clear theoretical and practical implications. The paper's focus on automatically identifying the group structure represents an important step forward in making nonparametric methods more applicable to high-dimensional problems.
