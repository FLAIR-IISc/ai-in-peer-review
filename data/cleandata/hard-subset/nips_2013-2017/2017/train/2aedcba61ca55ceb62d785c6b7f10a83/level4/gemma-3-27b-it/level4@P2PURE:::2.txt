Summary:
This work investigates group-additive nonparametric regression models, offering a framework between fully nonparametric models (which can be difficult to fit) and standard additive models (which may be overly restrictive). The paper specifically addresses the scenario where the underlying group structure is unknown and must be learned from the data. The authors propose a novel penalty function, grounded in the covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) balls, which is incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective. This yields an objective function that is optimized jointly over both the group structure—which, in conjunction with the kernel, defines a function space via the direct sum of RKHSs over each group of variables—and the regression estimate within each group. Two algorithms are presented for approximately solving this composite optimization problem, accompanied by theoretical results demonstrating (a) the convergence rate of the empirical risk to the true risk of the optimal estimator, and (b) the consistency of the estimated group structure, i.e., its probability of matching the true group structure approaches 1 as the sample size increases. The method’s performance is validated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

Main Comments:
The key contribution of this paper lies in recognizing that the complexity of a given group structure can be effectively quantified using covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum space. The paper presents a comprehensive approach, featuring a well-motivated and novel method, supported by robust theoretical and empirical results. While I am not deeply familiar with alternative methods for modeling dependencies between additive and nonparametric models, based on the discussion in Lines 31-50, this work appears to represent a potentially significant advancement in the field. As the authors acknowledge in the Discussion section, a primary limitation of the method is the computational difficulty of optimizing over the group structure, particularly when the number of variables is large. Overall, the paper is clearly written, aside from a number of typographical errors.
