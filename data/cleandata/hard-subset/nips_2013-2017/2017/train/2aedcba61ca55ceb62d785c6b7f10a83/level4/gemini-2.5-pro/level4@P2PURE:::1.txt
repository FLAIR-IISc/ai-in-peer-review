This paper investigates kernel regression in the high-dimensional setting, starting from the "additive model" framework, which represents regressors as a sum of functions of individual features (`f = sum f_p(X_p)`), thereby ignoring correlations between dimensions. The authors correctly identify this as a limitation and propose a "group additive model" that partitions dimensions into groups, allowing for correlations within each group to be modeled. The paper provides a formal treatment of this setting and proposes several heuristic algorithms for identifying optimal groupings. The approach is evaluated on small synthetic and real-world datasets.

The primary weakness of this paper is its inadequate discussion of related work, which makes it difficult for a non-expert to assess the novelty of the contributions. The task of identifying optimal groups of variables is central to several other fields, such as structured sparsity and the learning of graphical models or Bayesian networks, yet these areas are not mentioned. The problem can also be framed as a form of feature selection, another connection the authors fail to make. Consequently, the discussion of prior art is effectively limited to a single KDD 2015 paper [8], which is insufficient context for evaluating the paper's specific contributions.

That said, the theoretical treatment appears to be novel. The authors derive a simple complexity measure for a given grouping, which favors a larger number of smaller groups, and justify it as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems like a sensible approach. A significant downside, however, is that this measure does not lend itself to efficient optimization. The authors propose either an exhaustive search, which scales poorly, or a greedy method analogous to forward feature selection. While optimizing discrete functions is admittedly challenging, it is disappointing that the proposed complexity measure does not enable a more efficient optimization scheme than what could be achieved with standard cross-validation. The authors state they have been "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) but offer no further insights, making it difficult to judge the suitability of their final choice.

The experimental evaluation is limited. The authors present a simple synthetic validation and a single application to the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, they provide no baselines and do not compare their approach against any alternative methods. This omission makes it impossible to determine whether the presented results are competitive or meaningful in practice.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors' rebuttal clarified some of my concerns. However, I still find the experiments to be insufficient and believe the paper does an unacceptably poor job of relating its contributions to established ideas in machine learning. Nevertheless, I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions and have raised my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to significantly expand their discussion of related work should the paper be accepted for publication.
