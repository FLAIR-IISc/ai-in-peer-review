

This paper addresses a key limitation of additive models in high-dimensional nonparametric regression by introducing the concept of "group additive structure" and proposing a method to identify the intrinsic grouping of predictor variables. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group partition that preserves the additive model's interpretability while accommodating variable interactions. They develop a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies this structure and estimates the nonparametric functions, using a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers.

The paper makes several technically sound contributions. First, it provides a formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure as the unique minimal partition that satisfies the additive model constraints (Theorem 1). Second, the authors derive a tractable complexity measure for group structures (C(G) = ∑j α^{|uj|}) based on covering numbers of RKHSs, which is used as a penalty in their estimation procedure. Third, they prove consistency of their method under certain conditions (Theorem 4). The theoretical development is sound and well-motivated, with appropriate connections to RKHS theory and complexity measures.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-organized and the mathematical presentation is precise. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from functional ANOVA models and graphical models (Section 1), and they effectively motivate their complexity measure through the relationship between group structure and RKHS capacity (Section 2.3.2). However, the connection to related work could be strengthened, which impacts the originality assessment.

The originality of the work is somewhat unclear due to insufficient comparison with relevant literature. The authors cite [11] as the primary work on group additive structures but fail to adequately distinguish their contribution from other approaches to structured sparsity, feature grouping, and graphical model learning. For instance, the problem of identifying relevant groupings bears similarities to work on structured sparsity [1, 2] and block-structured graphical models [3, 4], which are not properly contextualized. The authors mention "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) but provide no justification for why their complexity measure was chosen over alternatives like VC-dimension or Rademacher complexity, which have been successfully used in similar contexts. This omission makes it difficult to assess the novelty of their approach.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the experimental evaluation. The authors present simulations on small synthetic datasets and a single application to the Boston Housing dataset (Section 4-5), but they provide no baselines for comparison or rigorous performance metrics. Without comparison to alternative methods (such as functional ANOVA models or other structured sparsity approaches), it's challenging to evaluate whether their method offers meaningful improvements in practice. The results show promising identification of group structures in synthetic data, but the lack of comparison with existing methods weakens the claim of practical significance.

The paper's main strengths are its rigorous theoretical foundation and clear formalization of the group additive structure problem. The consistency proof (Theorem 4) is technically sound and addresses an important gap in the literature. The method also has practical appeal for high-dimensional regression where interpretability and dimension reduction are crucial.

The main weaknesses are:
1. Inadequate related work discussion that fails to position the contribution within existing literature on structured sparsity and feature grouping
2. Limited experimental evaluation without appropriate baselines for comparison
3. The complexity measure, while theoretically motivated, lacks clear advantages over simpler alternatives that might enable more efficient optimization

The authors' rebuttal addressed some concerns about related work, but the paper still needs to better contextualize their contribution. The theoretical framework is strong, but the practical impact would be significantly enhanced with more thorough comparisons to existing methods and a clearer justification for the chosen complexity measure.

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: Good theoretical development, but experimental evaluation is limited
- Clarity: Well-written mathematically, but related work section needs improvement
- Originality: Contribution is somewhat obscured by insufficient comparison with related work
- Significance: Potential significance in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, but limited by lack of comparative experiments

The paper presents a technically interesting approach to a meaningful problem in high-dimensional regression, but its novelty and practical significance would be better established with more comprehensive related work and experimental comparisons. The method itself is sound, but the paper would benefit from better positioning within the broader literature on structured sparsity and model selection. I recommend a borderline decision, leaning toward acceptance if the authors can substantially improve the related work section and experimental comparisons in the camera-ready version.
