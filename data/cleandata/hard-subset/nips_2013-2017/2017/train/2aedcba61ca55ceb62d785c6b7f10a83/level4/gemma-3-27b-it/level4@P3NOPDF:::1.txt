## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper addresses kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, motivated by the limitations of traditional additive models which assume independence between dimensions. The authors propose a “group additive model” that allows for modeling correlations within groups of dimensions, offering a more flexible approach. They provide a formal treatment of this framework and propose algorithms for learning optimal groupings, demonstrating their approach on small synthetic and real-world datasets.

My primary concern with this work lies in the insufficient discussion of related literature. As someone not intimately familiar with this specific subfield, it’s difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution. The problem of identifying optimal groupings is closely related to established areas like structured sparsity and learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not explored. Similarly, the work bears relevance to feature selection, which also receives no mention. The related work section largely focuses on a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is inadequate for establishing the paper’s unique contribution within the broader machine learning landscape. 

Despite this weakness, the theoretical development is a noteworthy aspect of the paper. The authors define a complexity measure for groupings – favoring numerous small groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This derivation appears sound and the complexity measure itself is intuitively justifiable. However, this measure proves difficult to optimize directly. Consequently, the authors resort to either an exhaustive search (computationally expensive) or a greedy approach (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While I understand the inherent challenges of optimizing discrete functions like those defining optimal groupings, I found the lack of a more efficiently optimizable measure disappointing. The authors mention ongoing investigation of alternative measures (line 174), but offer no further details, hindering a clear evaluation of their chosen approach.

The experimental evaluation consists of straightforward synthetic validation and a demonstration on the Boston Housing dataset. Critically, the paper lacks baselines and comparisons to existing methods. Without such comparisons, it is challenging to judge the effectiveness of the proposed approach and determine whether the observed results represent a genuine improvement. The absence of baselines significantly weakens the empirical validation.

**Post-Rebuttal:**

The authors’ rebuttal addressed some of my initial concerns, clarifying aspects of their methodology. However, I remain unconvinced by the limited scope of the experimental evaluation and reiterate my criticism of the insufficient engagement with related work. Nevertheless, I acknowledge that the paper presents valid theoretical contributions and have adjusted my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially expand their discussion of related work – particularly within structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection – should this paper be accepted for publication. This will significantly strengthen the paper's position within the existing body of knowledge.
