Summary
This paper addresses nonparametric regression in high dimensions by moving beyond the standard additive model to a group-additive model: the regression function is additive across groups of variables, allowing interactions within groups. The authors formalize the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal partition admitting an additive decomposition) and show it is unique under a natural partial order. They then propose a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously estimates the regression functions (via direct sums of RKHSs per group) and the group structure. The key idea is to measure the complexity of a group structure through covering numbers of the induced direct-sum RKHS; this motivates a penalty that depends on group sizes. Two algorithms (exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic) are provided. Theoretical results include bounds showing that, for amiable structures, empirical risk approaches the optimal population risk, and a separation result for non-amiable structures, culminating in consistency of group structure selection. Experiments on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset illustrate the method’s potential.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself between classical generalized additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) and interaction-rich models such as smoothing-spline ANOVA (Gu) and higher-order interaction kernels (e.g., ESP kernels as in Kandasamy & Yu). It builds on OKGT (Pan et al.), which introduced group-wise transformations but did not resolve identifiability or provide a systematic structure-selection procedure. Connections to graphical models are discussed, emphasizing differences between conditional dependence graphs and the group-additive partition targeted here.

Evaluation

Quality
- Theory: The formalization of intrinsic group structure and its uniqueness under the proposed partial order is clearly motivated. The risk bounds via covering numbers and the amiable/non-amiable separation are plausible and, together, support structure-selection consistency. Assumptions should be stated more explicitly (e.g., when independence among predictors is required to obtain strict inclusions in function spaces; regularity of kernels; universality for unbiasedness). Guidance on how µ and α should scale with n and model size (and any conditions needed for consistency) would improve practical interpretability of Theorem 4.
- Method: The complexity penalty derived from covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs is well-justified. However, the practical choice of α (introduced via reparameterizing ln(r/ε)) is somewhat ad hoc in the experiments; more discussion or a data-driven calibration would be helpful. Algorithmically, exhaustive search is infeasible for moderate p, and the backward stepwise heuristic can miss the truth; providing stronger heuristics (e.g., beam search, randomized restarts, or MCMC over partitions) would improve robustness.
- Experiments: The synthetic studies demonstrate recovery of the true structure in several settings, but the degradation in a tuned setting (Model 1) suggests sensitivity to λ and the solver for KRR. The real-data example illustrates interpretability, but there are no comparisons to baselines (e.g., GAM, functional ANOVA kernels, SALSA, OKGT, hierarchical kernel learning). Including predictive performance and structure recovery comparisons would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, but there are many typos and a few notational/terminology issues (detailed below). It would help to:
- Clearly separate L2(X) vs RKHS notation and avoid overloading G (structure) and H_G (space).
- State upfront the conditions under which intrinsic group structure exists/unique and where independence is used.
- Explain the role of universal kernels in guaranteeing approximation without bias, and discuss practical kernel choices.

Originality
The definition of intrinsic group additive structure and the use of covering-number-based penalties for structure selection in direct-sum RKHSs appear novel. While related ideas exist (e.g., structured sparsity/hierarchical kernel learning), the specific formulation and consistency results for group partitions are new to my knowledge. Consider discussing connections to hierarchical kernel learning (e.g., Bach, NIPS 2008/2010) and sparse additive models with interactions, to further clarify distinctions.

Significance
If made scalable, this framework could be impactful: it offers a principled middle ground between additive models and high-order interactions, with theoretical guarantees and interpretable outputs. Current limitations are primarily computational and empirical: stronger optimization strategies and comparisons against established baselines would increase confidence in the method’s practical utility.

Pros
- Clear and useful formalization of intrinsic group additive structure; uniqueness under a partial order.
- Novel, RKHS-based structural complexity penalty with supporting theory.
- Consistency of structure selection under reasonable conditions.
- Interpretability benefits in real-data illustration.

Cons
- Scalability: search over partitions remains challenging; the greedy heuristic can miss true structures.
- Sensitivity to tuning (λ, µ, α) with limited guidance on selection/scaling.
- Empirical evaluation lacks baseline comparisons and broader datasets.
- Some assumptions and terminology (e.g., “almost surely” usage) need clarification.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Interaction characterization: Is there a population-level characterization of “no interaction across groups” that does not rely on explicitly writing Eq. (1)? For example, can it be tied to orthogonal decomposition, conditional expectations, or independence of certain projection operators?
- Assumptions: Where exactly is predictor independence needed (e.g., for strict inclusions in Proposition 1)? Does Theorem 1 rely on independence, or does the unique minimal element exist more generally?
- Universality: Consistency arguments rely on universal kernels to avoid approximation bias. How sensitive are results/practice to non-universal kernels or mismatched bandwidths?
- Penalty calibration: Provide guidance on choosing µ and α as functions of n, p, and kernel parameters. Can α be linked to kernel hyperparameters or estimated via data complexity measures?
- Algorithmic enhancements: Consider and discuss more robust search strategies (beam search, stochastic local search, split-merge moves, or relaxations via structured sparsity/convex surrogates).
- Ties and near-ties: How are ties in the penalized risk handled when multiple amiable partitions have close scores at finite n?

Overall
A well-motivated and theoretically solid contribution with a novel structural penalty and a clear conceptual advance. I encourage acceptance if the authors can strengthen the experimental comparisons and clarify assumptions/tuning guidance; otherwise, this is still a promising direction that merits consideration.
