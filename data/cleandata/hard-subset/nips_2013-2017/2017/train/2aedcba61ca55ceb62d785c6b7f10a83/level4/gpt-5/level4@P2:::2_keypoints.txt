- Paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression where group partition is unknown and must be learned from data.  
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel ridge regression using direct-sum RKHSs and a novel covering-number–based complexity penalty.  
- Introduces two search algorithms for partitions: exhaustive search (small p) and backward stepwise heuristic (larger p).  
- Theoretical results: empirical risk approaches oracle risk under "amiable" structures, separation from non-amiable structures, and consistency of partition estimation under conditions.  
- Empirical demonstration includes simulations and one real-data example (Boston Housing).  

- Extends classical additive models toward richer within-group interactions, positioned between fully nonparametric and standard additive approaches.  
- Contrasts with methods enumerating many interaction terms and with graphical-model approaches by targeting coarser group partitions.  

- Clear formalization of intrinsic group-additive structure with existence and uniqueness of a minimal intrinsic partition.  
- Novel structural penalty derived from covering numbers tied to group sizes and input dimensionality.  
- Solid theoretical guarantees for risk convergence and partition consistency under suitable conditions.  
- Practical proposals for search strategies and encouraging empirical recovery of partitions in several settings.  

- Computational scalability is a major concern: partition search is combinatorial and the greedy backward strategy can get trapped without guarantees.  
- Tuning and implementation are under-specified: α (from covering-number bounds) treated as a free integer and sensitivity to (λ, µ, α) lacks guidance.  
- Some theoretical assumptions (e.g., translation-invariant kernel bounds, independence in propositions) and their practical implications are not clearly stated.  
- Empirical comparisons are limited: few baselines that capture interactions, only one real dataset, and stepwise search struggles in some simulations.  

- Methodology is sound and grounded in learning-theory tools; selection consistency is plausible given risk separation and complexity control.  

- Paper is generally understandable but contains typos, ambiguities (e.g., “almost surely”), and notation issues that reduce clarity.  

- Using covering-number–based capacity of direct-sum RKHSs as a structural penalty and formalizing intrinsic group-additive identifiability are novel contributions.  

- Approach is promising for learning interactions with interpretability and efficiency, but impact depends on addressing computational issues and broader empirical validation.  

- Ask for a population-level interaction notion relating to ANOVA/Hoeffding decomposition or conditional independence that forces co-residence in groups.  
- Request clarification of “almost surely” (likely meaning equality almost everywhere w.r.t. PX).  
- Ask for discussion on the scale-dependence of α and how it should relate to kernel bandwidths and data scaling.  
- Suggest exploring stochastic or beam search (e.g., MCMC over partitions, randomized splits/merges) and providing complexity analysis for the algorithms.  
- Recommend comparisons against functional ANOVA, ESP-kernel KRR, SALSA, hierarchical interaction selection, and neural additive models with interactions.  
- Request practical guidance for choosing λ, µ, α, kernel hyperparameters, and sharing code.  

- Pros: well-motivated problem, principled structural penalty with theory, and demonstrated partition recovery in several settings.  
- Cons: scalability and search suboptimality for large p, tuning sensitivity with limited guidance, and limited empirical comparisons/real-world validation.  

- Minor errors/typos to fix: missing sum in empirical risk (Eq.3); wording fixes (“on RHS”, “reduce”, “exists and is unique”); clarify “almost surely”; replace “convolutional kernel” with “translation-invariant kernel”; “turning parameters” → “tuning parameters”; Algorithm 2 wording and grammar fixes.
