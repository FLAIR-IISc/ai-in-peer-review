Review- Note: Because the supplement appears to contain the main paper, I reviewed that version; all line numbers below refer to the supplement.

Summary:
This work investigates group-additive nonparametric regression, where for a partition of the covariates, the regression function is additive across variable groups. This setting bridges fully nonparametric models, which are hard to fit, and purely additive models, which can be overly restrictive. The paper addresses the case where the grouping is unknown and must be learned from data. To that end, it introduces a new penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, incorporated into a kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting formulation allows joint optimization over the group partition (which, together with the kernel, defines a function class via a direct sum of RKHSs over each group) and the per-group regression functions. The authors propose two algorithms to approximately solve this joint problem and provide theory showing (a) a rate at which the empirical risk of the estimator converges to the true risk of the population optimum, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure, in the sense that the probability it equals the true grouping tends to 1 as n → ∞. Finally, experiments on both synthetic and real data are presented.

Main Comments:
The main insight is to quantify the complexity of a given grouping via covering numbers of the associated direct-sum space. The paper is thorough, offering a well-motivated, novel method with reasonably strong theoretical and empirical support. I am not very familiar with other approaches that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models, but if the discussion in Lines 31–50 is reasonably complete, this seems like a potentially meaningful advance. As noted in the Discussion, the major challenge is the difficulty of the optimization over groupings when the number of variables is large. The exposition is generally clear, notwithstanding numerous typos.
