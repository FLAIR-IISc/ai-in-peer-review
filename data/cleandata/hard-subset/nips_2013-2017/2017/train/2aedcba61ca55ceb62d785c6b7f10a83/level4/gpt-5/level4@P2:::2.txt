Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression models with a group-additive structure: the regression function is additive across groups of predictors, with potentially rich interactions within each group. This framework lies between fully nonparametric models (flexible but hard to estimate in high dimensions) and standard additive models (efficient but possibly too restrictive). The key problem addressed is that the group partition is unknown and must be learned from data. The authors propose a structure-penalized kernel method: for a candidate partition, they define a direct-sum RKHS across groups and fit via kernel ridge regression, while penalizing a novel complexity measure derived from covering numbers of RKHS balls. Two algorithms (exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic) are proposed to search over partitions. Theoretical results include bounds showing empirical risk approaches the oracle risk under amiable structures, a separation from non-amiable structures, and consistency of the estimated partition for the intrinsic group-additive structure. Simulations and one real-data example (Boston Housing) illustrate the method.

Relation to prior work
The model extends classical additive models (Hastie and Tibshirani) and sits between them and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA; ESP kernel–based interaction models; SALSA). Unlike methods that enumerate many interaction terms, the group-additive approach controls complexity by constraining interactions within small groups. The use of direct-sum RKHSs is standard; the novelty is penalizing group structures via covering-number–based capacity of the induced function space. The paper also contrasts the approach with graphical models: rather than learning a full conditional dependence graph, it directly targets the coarser group-additive partition.

Strengths
- Clear modeling target: intrinsic group-additive structure formalized via a partial order on partitions, with existence and uniqueness of a minimal “intrinsic” structure.
- Methodological novelty: a principled structural penalty derived from covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs, tied to group sizes and input dimensionality.
- Theory: convergence of empirical to population risk under amiable structures, separation from non-amiable structures, and consistency of partition selection under suitable tuning.
- Practicality: two search strategies for small p (exhaustive) and larger p (backward splitting), plus encouraging empirical demonstrations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational scalability: searching partitions is combinatorial (number of set partitions grows super-exponentially). The greedy backward strategy may get trapped and miss the true partition; no guarantees or diagnostics are provided.
- Tuning and implementation: the complexity penalty introduces α and µ; α emerges from covering-number bounds but is treated as a free integer (1–10). The sensitivity of performance to (λ, µ, α) and guidance for choosing them (beyond validation) are limited.
- Assumptions and clarity: portions of the theory hinge on capacity bounds for translation-invariant kernels and on independence in Proposition 1; the prerequisites and their practical implications could be stated more explicitly.
- Empirical evaluation: comparisons to strong baselines that capture interactions (e.g., SALSA, ANOVA kernels, hierarchical interaction selection, neural additive models with interactions) are limited. Only one real dataset is shown, and stepwise search struggles on some synthetic settings.

Quality
The methodological construction is sound and grounded in standard learning-theory tools. The selection consistency result is plausible given the risk separation between amiable and non-amiable structures and the complexity control across amiable ones. Some technical details (e.g., role and scaling of α; dependence on kernel hyperparameters; conditions for the covering-number bounds) would benefit from clearer statements and discussion.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable, but there are numerous typos and a few ambiguities noted below. Clarifying the meaning of “almost surely” in key statements, fixing missing sums, and standardizing terminology (translation-invariant vs convolution) would improve readability.

Originality
Using covering-number–based capacity of direct-sum RKHSs as a structural penalty for partition learning is, to my knowledge, novel. The formalization of intrinsic group-additive structure and its identifiability is also a useful contribution.

Significance
The approach offers a promising route to learn interactions while maintaining interpretability and statistical efficiency. If computational issues are addressed and empirical comparisons broadened, the contribution could be impactful for high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Interaction characterization: Is there a population-level notion of (non)interaction between two variables that implies they must (or must not) co-reside in a group, independent of explicitly writing (1)? For example, can this be related to the Hoeffding/ANOVA decomposition or conditional independence of suitable projections?
- “Almost surely” (around line 169): Please clarify whether you mean equality almost everywhere with respect to PX (a property of the function spaces), rather than a statement about n → ∞.
- Complexity penalty: α arises from ln(r/ε) in covering-number bounds, but is treated as an integer hyperparameter. Can you discuss scale-invariance and how α should depend on kernel bandwidths and data scaling?
- Search strategy: Could stochastic search (e.g., MCMC over partitions, randomized splits/merges) or beam search improve robustness over the greedy backward algorithm? Any computational complexity analysis of Algorithms 1–2?
- Baselines: Please compare against functional ANOVA, ESP-kernel KRR, SALSA, and other interaction-learning methods on the same synthetic and real setups.
- Practical guidance: Offer recommendations for choosing λ, µ, α, and kernel hyperparameters, and consider releasing code.

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and clear formal framework.
- Principled structural penalty with supporting theory.
- Demonstrated ability to recover true partitions in several settings.

Cons
- Scalability and search suboptimality for large p.
- Tuning sensitivity and limited guidance.
- Limited empirical comparisons and real-world validation.
