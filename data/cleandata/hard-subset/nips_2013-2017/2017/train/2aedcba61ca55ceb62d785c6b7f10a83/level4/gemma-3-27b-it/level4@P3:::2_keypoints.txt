- Paper proposes learning group-additive nonparametric regression by jointly optimizing group structure and group-wise kernel ridge regression using a novel RKHS covering-number penalty.  
- Two approximate optimization algorithms are provided, with theoretical guarantees for empirical risk convergence and consistency of group-structure recovery.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real data support the method's effectiveness.  
- Key innovation: using covering numbers of the direct-sum RKHS to quantify and penalize group-structure complexity.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: consistency results for both group identification and regression estimates.  
- Comprehensive treatment: addresses both statistical formulation and computational optimization, with two practical algorithms.  
- Clear motivation: fills gap between fully nonparametric, additive, and functional ANOVA models.  
- Main weakness: computational scalability — searching over group structures is prohibitive in high dimensions; stepwise algorithm only partially mitigates this.  
- Algorithmic details are insufficiently specified (choices of hyperparameters λ, μ, α and stopping criteria need more detail).  
- Presentation issues: numerous typographical errors and occasional awkward phrasing that reduce readability.  
- Limited empirical comparison: more benchmarks against related methods (e.g., sparse additive models, variable selection methods) would strengthen validation.  
- Specific methodological questions: request for a simpler definition of “interaction” between two variables without full model specification.  
- Clarifications needed: meaning of “almost surely” at Line 169 (likely asymptotic) and missing summation in Equation (3).  
- Terminology note: “convolutional kernel” may be more commonly called “translation-invariant kernel.”  
- Detailed typos/minor edits listed (examples: “on RHS” → “on the RHS”; “reduces” → “reduce”; “G^ exists and unique” → “G^ exists and is unique”; “turning parameters” → “tuning parameters”; Algorithm 2 Line 1 “State with” → “start with”; “by compare” → “by comparing”).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound method with supportive empirical results addressing an important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns, numerous minor typos, and limited empirical comparisons.  
- Overall assessment and recommendation: solid contribution; accept with minor revisions to fix typos, clarify algorithmic details, discuss computational complexity/scalability, and expand comparisons.
