This study examines group-additive nonparametric regression models, which interpolate between the fully nonparametric model and the additive model. The paper focuses on learning the group structure from the data when it is not known in advance. To achieve this, the authors propose a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of RKHS balls, which is added to the kernel ridge regression objective. This results in an objective that can be optimized over both the group structure and the regression estimate within each group.

The paper is comprehensive, including a well-motivated and novel method, and reasonably solid theoretical and empirical results. The key innovation appears to be recognizing that the complexity of a particular group structure can be quantified in terms of covering numbers of the direct sum space. Assuming the discussion in Lines 31-50 is fairly complete, this paper seems like a potentially significant advance in fitting models between the additive and nonparametric models.

The paper is fairly clearly written, aside from a few typos. However, some minor comments and questions arise. For instance, is there a simple characterization of "interaction" between two variables that doesn't rely on writing the whole model in Equation (1)? Additionally, the use of "almost surely" in Line 169 is unclear, and it would be helpful to specify whether this is meant as n -> infinity.

Some minor errors were found, including a missing summation in Equation (3) and several typos throughout the paper. These include "on RHS" instead of "on the RHS," "not only can the bias of \hat f{\lambda,G} reduces" instead of "not only can the bias of \hat f{\lambda,G} reduce," and "G^ exists and unique" instead of "G^ exists and is unique."

The authors present two algorithms for approximately solving the compound optimization problem, and theoretical results show the rate at which the empirical risk of the estimate approaches the true risk of the true optimum and the consistency of the group structure estimate. Experimental results are also presented on both synthetic and real data. Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of RKHS balls.
* The method is comprehensive, including a well-motivated approach and reasonably solid theoretical and empirical results.
* The paper provides a significant advance in fitting models between the additive and nonparametric models.

Weaknesses:

* The optimization problem over group structure can be difficult to solve when the number of variables is large.
