This study investigates group-additive nonparametric regression models, which interpolate between fully nonparametric models, often difficult to fit, and additive models, sometimes too restrictive. The regression function in these models is additive between groups of variables for a given partitioning of the predictor variables. Specifically, the paper addresses the problem where the group structure is unknown in advance and must be learned from the data. 

The authors propose a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of RKHS balls, which is added to the kernel ridge regression objective. This results in an objective that can be optimized over both the group structure and the regression estimate within each group. The group structure, along with the kernel, determines a function space via the direct sum of RKHSs over each group of variables. Two algorithms are presented for approximately solving this compound optimization problem. 

Theoretical results are presented, showing the rate at which the empirical risk of the estimate approaches the true risk of the true optimum and the consistency of the group structure estimate. The probability that the estimated group structure matches the true group structure approaches 1 as the sample size n approaches infinity. Experimental results are also presented on both synthetic and real data.

The key innovation of the paper appears to be recognizing that the complexity of a particular group structure can be quantified in terms of covering numbers of the direct sum space. The paper is comprehensive, including a well-motivated and novel method, and reasonably solid theoretical and empirical results. Assuming the discussion in Lines 31-50 is fairly complete, this paper seems like a potentially significant advance in fitting models between the additive and nonparametric models. 

As noted in the Discussion section, the main issue with the method appears to be the difficulty in solving the optimization problem over group structure when the number of variables is large. The paper is also fairly clearly written, aside from several typos.
