This paper addresses the problem of kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It begins by discussing the standard "additive model," where the regressor is a sum of functions each applied to a single feature (f = Σ_p f_p(X_p)), thereby ignoring interactions between dimensions. To overcome this limitation, the authors propose a "group additive model," which partitions the features into groups and models interactions within each group. The paper provides a formal theoretical treatment of this model and introduces heuristic algorithms for identifying an optimal feature grouping. The proposed framework is evaluated on small-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.

A significant weakness of the paper is its limited engagement with related work, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the contributions. The core problem of identifying optimal groups of variables is central to several other areas of machine learning, yet these connections are not explored. For instance, the literature on "structured sparsity" explicitly deals with learning models with group-based penalties. Similarly, the task of discovering variable dependencies is a primary focus in the fields of learning graphical models and Bayesian networks. The problem can also be viewed as a form of "feature selection" or "feature grouping." By neglecting to discuss these established fields, the paper fails to properly contextualize its approach. The discussion of prior art is almost entirely confined to a single KDD 2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for establishing the paper's unique contributions within the broader machine learning landscape.

On the theoretical side, the derivation of a complexity measure for a given grouping appears to be a novel contribution. This measure, which favors a larger number of smaller groups, is sensibly derived from an upper bound on the model's covering number. However, a major limitation arises from the practical application of this measure: it does not lend itself to efficient optimization. The authors propose either an exhaustive search, which is computationally infeasible for even a moderate number of features, or a greedy forward-selection-style algorithm. While optimizing discrete functions over partitions is inherently challenging, the proposed optimization strategies are elementary. The practical utility of the complexity measure is questionable if finding the optimal grouping it defines is no more efficient than standard model selection techniques like cross-validation. The authors mention that they have investigated other measures (line 174) but offer no details, leaving the reader unable to evaluate the rationale behind their final choice.

The experimental evaluation is another area of significant concern. The authors demonstrate their framework on a simple synthetic dataset and the Boston Housing dataset. Critically, the experiments lack any baselines or comparisons to alternative methods. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to gauge the performance of the proposed group additive model relative to existing techniques for high-dimensional regression or feature grouping. The results presented are therefore insufficient to validate the effectiveness of the method.

== Post-rebuttal comments ==
The authors' rebuttal has clarified some of the concerns raised in the initial review. However, significant weaknesses remain. The experimental validation is still insufficient, and the discussion of related work remains unacceptably narrow, failing to properly situate the paper within the broader context of machine learning. Despite these shortcomings, I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions, particularly in its theoretical formulation. Consequently, I have raised my score slightly. I strongly recommend that the authors thoroughly revise the related work section to provide a comprehensive comparison with existing ideas before the publication of a final version.
