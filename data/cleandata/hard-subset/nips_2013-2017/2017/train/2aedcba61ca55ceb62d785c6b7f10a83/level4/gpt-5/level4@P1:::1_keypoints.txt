- Paper studies kernel nonparametric regression with a group additive model that partitions variables into groups and sums multivariate functions over groups to capture interactions.  
- Formalizes group structures with a partial order, defines “amiable” structures and proves existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure.  
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel estimator in RKHSs that jointly estimates the function and selects the group structure.  
- Defines a complexity penalty for groupings via covering-number bounds of the induced direct-sum RKHS, favoring simpler (smaller) groups.  
- Proves nontrivial uniform risk bounds and selection consistency for group recovery under suitable conditions and tuning.  
- Provides two search strategies for structure selection: exhaustive search for small p and a greedy stepwise procedure for larger p.  
- Empirical illustrations include simulations and a Boston Housing example, but experiments are limited in scale.  
- Strength: clear, useful formalization of group additive structures that separates identifiability from estimation issues.  
- Strength: principled penalty grounded in RKHS capacity control and covering-number theory.  
- Strength: solid theoretical development including consistency and uniform risk bounds.  
- Strength: appealing idea to target group structure directly rather than infer from full graphical models.  
- Weakness: related work discussion is insufficient; limited comparison to structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature-selection literature.  
- Weakness: complexity measure yields a difficult discrete optimization problem; only exhaustive or greedy search is proposed, lacking scalable relaxations.  
- Weakness: experiments are thin, lack baselines (e.g., additive models, SALSA, interaction-regularized kernels) and comparative metrics, limiting practical assessment.  
- Weakness: limited practical guidance on tuning parameters (λ, µ, α) and apparent sensitivity to them.  
- Quality: theory is technically sound but optimization is ad hoc and empirical evaluation is unconvincing.  
- Clarity: exposition of framework and RKHS connection is clear, but related-work and interpretation/tuning discussion need expansion.  
- Originality: intrinsic structure formalization and covering-number–based penalty appear novel, but novelty relative to existing structured-sparsity and structure-learning work is unclear.  
- Significance: could be impactful if scalable and competitive, but current lack of baselines and limited experiments hinder assessment.  
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous identifiability formulation, principled complexity measure, and nontrivial consistency theory.  
- Cons for acceptance: inadequate related-work positioning, scalability concerns from search strategy, and sparse empirical comparisons.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors clarified some points, but experiments remain sparse and related-work discussion is still insufficient; stronger baselines and expanded literature context are encouraged.
