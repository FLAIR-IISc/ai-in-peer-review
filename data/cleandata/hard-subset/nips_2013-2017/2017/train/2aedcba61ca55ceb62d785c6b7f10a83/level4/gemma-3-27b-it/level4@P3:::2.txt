This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, offering a flexible approach that interpolates between fully nonparametric models and traditional additive models. The core problem addressed is learning the group structure when it’s not known *a priori*. The authors propose a novel penalty function, based on covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs), incorporated into a kernel ridge regression objective. This yields an objective optimized over both group structure and regression estimates within each group. Two algorithms are presented for approximate optimization, accompanied by theoretical results demonstrating empirical risk convergence and consistency of the group structure estimate. Empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real data supports the method's effectiveness.

The key innovation lies in quantifying the complexity of a group structure using covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum space. The paper is comprehensive, presenting a well-motivated and novel method alongside robust theoretical and empirical results. While I am not deeply familiar with alternatives for modeling between additive and nonparametric models, the paper, based on the literature review (Lines 31-50), appears to be a potentially significant contribution. A key limitation, acknowledged in the Discussion, is the computational challenge of solving the optimization problem when the number of predictors is large. The paper is generally clearly written, though it contains numerous minor typographical errors.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The use of covering numbers to penalize group structure complexity is a genuinely novel idea. This provides a principled way to balance model fit and model parsimony.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides consistency results for both the group structure identification and the regression estimate, demonstrating a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Comprehensive Approach:** The paper addresses both the statistical estimation problem and the computational challenges of optimizing over the group structure, proposing two algorithms for practical implementation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and real data application provide evidence that the proposed method performs well in practice.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (fully nonparametric, additive, and functional ANOVA) and motivates the need for a group-additive model.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Scalability:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, the computational cost of searching over the group structure can be prohibitive for high-dimensional data. While the stepwise algorithm addresses this to some extent, its performance (as shown in the simulations) appears to be somewhat limited.
* **Algorithmic Details:** More details about the implementation of the algorithms, particularly the choice of hyperparameters (λ, μ, α) and the stopping criteria, would be beneficial.
* **Typos and Minor Clarity Issues:** The paper contains numerous typographical errors and some instances of awkward phrasing that detract from readability.
* **Limited Comparison:** While results are shown for synthetic data and a single real dataset, a more extensive comparison with other related methods (e.g., sparse additive models, variable selection methods) would strengthen the empirical validation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for group-additive nonparametric regression.
* The proposed penalty function based on covering numbers is a significant contribution to the field.
* The empirical results support the effectiveness of the method.
* The paper addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational scalability of the method is a concern, particularly for very high-dimensional data.
* The paper contains numerous minor typographical errors that detract from readability.
* The comparison to other related methods is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. While the computational limitations and minor presentation issues are concerns, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the proposed method are compelling.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the typos and clarity issues, and to include a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability improvements. A more thorough comparison to existing methods would also be beneficial.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
