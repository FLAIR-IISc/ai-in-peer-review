### Summary

This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models. These models assume that for some partition of the predictor variables, the regression function is additive across groups. This provides a flexible middle ground between fully nonparametric models, which are often difficult to fit, and purely additive models, which can be too restrictive. The central problem addressed is learning this group structure from data when it is not known a priori.

To this end, the authors propose a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of RKHS balls. This penalty is incorporated into the standard kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting objective function can be optimized over both the group structure and the regression function itself. The group structure, in conjunction with the chosen kernel, defines the function space as a direct sum of RKHSs corresponding to each variable group. The paper presents two algorithms for approximately solving this complex optimization problem. Theoretical results are also provided, establishing (a) a convergence rate for the empirical risk and (b) the consistency of the group structure estimate, showing that the probability of recovering the true group structure approaches one as the number of samples increases. Finally, the method is validated with experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

### Main Comments

The key innovation of this work is the idea of quantifying the complexity of a given group structure using the covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum space. Overall, the paper is comprehensive. It presents a well-motivated and novel method, supported by solid theoretical analysis and empirical results.

While I am not an expert on methods that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models, assuming the literature review in Lines 31-50 is reasonably complete, this work appears to represent a significant advance. As the authors acknowledge in the discussion, a primary limitation is the computational difficulty of optimizing over the group structure, especially when the number of variables is large. The paper is generally well-written and clear, although it would benefit from careful proofreading to correct numerous typographical errors.
