This paper investigates group-additive nonparametric regression, a modeling framework that bridges the gap between fully nonparametric regression (which can be computationally challenging) and traditional additive models (which may be overly restrictive). The core problem addressed is learning both the regression function *and* the underlying group structure from data, without prior knowledge of how the predictor variables should be partitioned. The authors propose a novel penalty term, grounded in the covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) balls, added to a kernel ridge regression objective. This results in a joint optimization problem – simultaneously estimating the group structure (which, alongside the kernel, defines a function space through direct sums of RKHSs) and the regression estimate within each group. The paper presents two algorithms for approximate optimization and supports the method with theoretical results concerning the convergence rate of the empirical risk and the consistency of the estimated group structure. Empirical validation is provided through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its insightful connection between group structure complexity and covering numbers, providing a principled way to regularize the model selection process. The paper is thorough, presenting a well-motivated and novel method backed by solid theoretical and empirical analysis. While I am not intimately familiar with all existing approaches to modeling between fully additive and fully nonparametric functions, the discussion presented in Lines 31-50 appears comprehensive, suggesting this paper represents a potentially significant contribution to the field. The identified limitation of computational difficulty when dealing with a large number of variables, noted in the Discussion, is a crucial point and honestly acknowledged by the authors. The paper is generally well-written, although it contains a number of minor typographical errors.
