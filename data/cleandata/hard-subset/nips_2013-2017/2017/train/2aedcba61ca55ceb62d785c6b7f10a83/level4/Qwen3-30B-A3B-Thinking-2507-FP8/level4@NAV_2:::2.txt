

Summary:
This paper investigates group-additive nonparametric regression models, where the regression function exhibits additivity across groups of predictor variables rather than individual variables. This model serves as an intermediate approach between fully nonparametric models (which suffer from high dimensionality) and standard additive models (which cannot capture variable interactions). The primary contribution lies in addressing the problem of unknown group structure, proposing a novel penalty function based on covering numbers of RKHS balls to be incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting optimization problem simultaneously determines the group structure (which defines a function space via direct sum of RKHSs over groups) and estimates the regression function within each group. The authors develop two algorithms for solving this compound optimization problem and provide theoretical guarantees showing both the convergence rate of the empirical risk to the true risk and the consistency of the group structure estimate as sample size increases. Experimental validation is provided through synthetic data simulations and a real-world application on the Boston Housing dataset.

Main Comments:
The paper makes a significant contribution by recognizing that the complexity of group structures can be quantified using covering numbers of direct sum RKHS spaces. This is a novel approach to the problem of identifying group structures in nonparametric regression. The work is comprehensive, offering a well-motivated method, thorough theoretical analysis, and reasonable empirical validation. The theoretical results establish the consistency of the proposed method, which is a strong point for scientific contribution. The paper appears to address an important gap in the literature, as it properly situates itself between additive models and higher-order interaction models, providing a more nuanced approach to capturing variable interactions without the computational burden of fully nonparametric methods.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through its technical soundness, with clear theoretical arguments and reasonable empirical results. The significance of the work is evident in its potential to improve high-dimensional nonparametric regression by providing a more interpretable model that can capture meaningful interactions. The originality is demonstrated through the novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers, which represents a fresh perspective on the group additive structure identification problem. The clarity of the paper is generally good, though it contains numerous typos that detract from readability.

The main limitation appears to be the computational complexity of the optimization problem over group structures when the number of predictor variables becomes large. The paper acknowledges this and proposes a stepwise algorithm for larger problems, but this remains a practical challenge. The authors could strengthen the significance by discussing more concrete applications where this method would be particularly valuable.

Pro: Well-motivated problem, novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers, comprehensive theoretical analysis, solid empirical validation, clear connection to existing work.

Con: Computational challenges for large-scale problems, numerous typos throughout the manuscript, limited discussion of practical applications beyond the Boston Housing dataset.
