

Summary:  
This paper investigates group-additive nonparametric regression, where the regression function is additive across unknown partitions of predictor variables. The approach bridges the gap between fully nonparametric (computationally intractable) and additive (potentially restrictive) models. The authors propose a novel penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, integrated into kernel ridge regression to jointly learn the group structure and estimate the regression function. Two algorithms approximate the compound optimization, and the paper establishes theoretical guarantees: (a) convergence rates for empirical risk to true risk, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure as \(n \to \infty\). Empirical validation is provided on synthetic and real datasets.  

Main Comments:  
The paper’s key innovation lies in quantifying the complexity of a group structure via covering numbers of the direct sum RKHS, enabling a principled penalty for structure learning. This addresses a significant gap in models interpolating between additive and nonparametric forms. The method is well-motivated, theoretically rigorous, and supported by solid experiments. The discussion in Lines 31–50 adequately contextualizes the work relative to existing approaches (e.g., sparse additive models), positioning it as a meaningful advance. The theoretical results (Theorems 1–3) are compelling, particularly the structure consistency guarantee, which is rarely achieved in similar settings. The writing is generally clear, though minor grammatical issues and typos (detailed below) slightly hinder readability.  

The primary limitation is computational scalability when the number of predictors \(p\) is large, as noted in the Discussion. While the algorithms (Algorithm 1 and 2) offer practical solutions, the paper acknowledges this as a challenge for high-dimensional settings. This is a natural constraint of the approach and warrants future exploration, but it does not undermine the paper’s core contributions.  

Minor Comments:  
1. **Equation (1) and interaction characterization (Line 169):** The paper defines "interaction" via Equation (1), but a simpler characterization (e.g., based on pairwise functional terms or conditional independence) would enhance accessibility. Could the authors clarify if such a characterization exists without expanding the model?  
2. **Asymptotic notation (Line 169):** The phrase "almost surely" requires clarification. Is this intended as \(n \to \infty\)? If so, the phrasing should explicitly state the limit.  
3. **Equation (3) (Line 169):** The summation over \(i\) is missing; the expression should read \(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \cdots\).  
4. **Terminology (Line 205):** "Convolutional kernel" is non-standard; "translation-invariant kernel" is the conventional term.  
5. **Typos and grammar (corrected):**  
   - Line 145: "on RHS" → "on the RHS"  
   - Line 152: "bias... reduces" → "bias... reduce"  
   - Line 160: "G^ exists and unique" → "G^ exists and is unique"  
   - Line 247: "turning parameters" → "tuning parameters"  
   - Algorithm 2, Line 1: "State with" → "Start with"  
   - Line 251: "by compare" → "by comparing"  

Overall Assessment:  
This work makes a substantial contribution to nonparametric modeling by introducing a theoretically grounded method for learning group-additive structures. The penalty based on covering numbers is novel and well-justified, and the convergence results are strong. The empirical results robustly support the methodology. The minor grammatical errors and typos are easily fixable, and the computational scalability issue is a common challenge in structure learning that the authors appropriately acknowledge. The paper is well-organized, technically sound, and advances the state of the art in a meaningful way.  

Recommendation: **Accept**. The paper is a strong candidate for publication at NeurIPS 2017. It offers a clear, innovative solution to a non-trivial problem, with thorough theoretical analysis and practical validation. The minor issues noted above do not detract from the paper’s overall quality or significance.
