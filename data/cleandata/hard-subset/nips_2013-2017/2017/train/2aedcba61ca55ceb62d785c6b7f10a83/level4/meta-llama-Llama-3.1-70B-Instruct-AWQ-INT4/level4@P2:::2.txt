This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, which interpolate between the fully nonparametric model and the additive model. The authors propose a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of RKHS balls to learn the group structure from the data. The paper is comprehensive, including a well-motivated method, solid theoretical results, and empirical results on both synthetic and real data.

The key innovation of the paper is recognizing that the complexity of a particular group structure can be quantified in terms of covering numbers of the direct sum space. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and their approach. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is generally clear, aside from a few typos.

The authors present two algorithms for approximately solving the compound optimization problem and provide theoretical results showing the rate at which the empirical risk of the estimate approaches the true risk of the true optimum and the consistency of the group structure estimate. The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

One potential issue with the method is the difficulty in solving the optimization problem over the group structure when the number of variables is large. The authors acknowledge this limitation and propose a backward stepwise algorithm to address it. However, further research is needed to develop more efficient algorithms.

Some minor comments and questions:

* Is there a simple characterization of "interaction" between two variables that doesn't rely on writing the whole model in Equation (1)?
* The use of "almost surely" in Line 169 is not entirely clear. Is this meant as n -> infinity?
* Equation (3) is missing a summation (over i).
* Perhaps "translation invariant kernel" is a more common term for this than "convolutional kernel."
* There are several typos throughout the paper, including "on RHS" instead of "on the RHS," "not only can the bias of \hat f{\lambda,G} reduces" instead of "not only can the bias of \hat f{\lambda,G} reduce," "G^ exists and unique" instead of "G^ exists and is unique," "turning parameters" instead of "tuning parameters," and "by compare" instead of "by comparing."
