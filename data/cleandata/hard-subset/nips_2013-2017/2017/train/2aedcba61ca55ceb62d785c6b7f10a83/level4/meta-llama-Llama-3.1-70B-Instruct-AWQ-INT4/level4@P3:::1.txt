The paper "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression" proposes a novel approach to nonparametric regression in high-dimensional settings. The authors introduce the concept of a group additive model, which extends the traditional additive model by allowing for correlations between certain dimensions. This approach enables the modeling of interactions between predictor variables while avoiding the curse of dimensionality.

The authors provide a rigorous treatment of the group additive model, including a formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and a complexity measure based on the covering number of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs). They also propose two algorithms for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure: an exhaustive search method and a backward stepwise approach.

The theoretical contributions of the paper are significant, particularly the derivation of the complexity measure and its relation to the covering number of RKHSs. This measure favors many small groups, which is a sensible approach for nonparametric regression. However, the authors acknowledge that this measure does not lend itself to easy optimization, leading to the proposal of the two algorithms.

The experimental evaluation of the paper is limited, consisting of synthetic data and a single real-world example (the Boston Housing data). While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, they are not compared to any baseline methods or other relevant approaches in the literature. This makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and impact of the work.

A major concern with the paper is its lack of discussion of related work. The authors fail to mention relevant fields such as structured sparsity and graphical models/Bayesian networks, which also deal with the task of finding optimal groups in data. Additionally, the paper does not provide a thorough comparison with existing methods, making it challenging to evaluate the contributions of the work.

In the rebuttal, the authors addressed some of the concerns, but the experimental evaluation and discussion of related work remain areas for improvement. Overall, the paper has valid contributions, particularly in its theoretical treatment, but requires further development in its experimental evaluation and discussion of related work.
