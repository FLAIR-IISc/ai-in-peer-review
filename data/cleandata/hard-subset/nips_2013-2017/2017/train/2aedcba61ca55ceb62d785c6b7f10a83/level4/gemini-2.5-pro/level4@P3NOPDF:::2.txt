**Summary**

This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, where the regression function is a sum of functions, each acting on a distinct group of predictor variables. This model class provides a flexible interpolation between fully nonparametric models, which are often difficult to fit, and standard additive models, which can be overly restrictive. The paper focuses on the challenging problem of learning this group structure from data when it is not known a priori.

To address this, the authors propose a novel penalty function derived from the covering numbers of RKHS balls. This penalty, which quantifies the complexity of a given group structure, is incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting objective function is optimized jointly over the partition of variables (the group structure) and the regression function itself. The group structure, in turn, defines the function space as a direct sum of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs) over the variable groups. The authors present two algorithms for approximately solving this complex optimization problem.

The theoretical contributions include an analysis of the convergence rate of the estimator's risk and a proof of consistency for the group structure selection, showing that the probability of recovering the true group structure approaches one as the sample size increases. Finally, the method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Main Comments**

The paper's key innovation is the insight that the complexity of a given group structure can be quantified by the covering numbers of the corresponding function space (a direct sum of RKHSs). This provides a principled way to penalize model complexity and search for the underlying group structure.

The paper is comprehensive, presenting a well-motivated and novel method supported by solid theoretical analysis and empirical results. Assuming the literature review (Lines 31-50) is a fair representation of the state of the art, this work represents a potentially significant advance in fitting models that bridge the gap between simple additive models and fully nonparametric ones.

As the authors acknowledge in the Discussion, a primary limitation is the computational challenge of optimizing over the combinatorial space of group structures, especially as the number of variables grows. This may limit the method's applicability to high-dimensional problems in its current form.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and presents a compelling new approach to a challenging problem. It would, however, benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct numerous minor typos.
