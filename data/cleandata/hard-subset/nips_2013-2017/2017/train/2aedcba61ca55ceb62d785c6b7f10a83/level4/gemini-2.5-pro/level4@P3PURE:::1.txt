This paper addresses kernel regression in the high-dimensional setting. The work departs from the standard "additive model," f = Σ_p f_p(X_p), which ignores correlations between dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors propose a "group additive model," which partitions features into groups to model correlations within each group. The paper provides a formal treatment of this setting, proposes several algorithms for finding an optimal grouping, and presents results on small-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.

A significant weakness of this paper is its inadequate discussion of related work. This makes it difficult to assess the work's novelty in the broader context of machine learning. The problem of identifying optimal feature groupings is central to several established fields, such as structured sparsity and graphical model/Bayesian network learning, yet these connections are not discussed. Similarly, the work can be framed as a form of feature selection, another relevant area that is not mentioned. Effectively, the related work section is confined almost entirely to a single reference [8], which is insufficient for situating the paper's contributions within the existing literature.

Despite the issues with positioning, the theoretical treatment appears to be a novel contribution. The authors define a complexity measure for a given feature partition, which favors a larger number of smaller groups. This measure is sensibly derived as an upper bound on the covering number. A practical limitation, however, is that this measure is not amenable to efficient optimization. Consequently, the authors resort to an exhaustive search, which is computationally infeasible for even a moderate number of features, and a greedy heuristic analogous to forward selection. While optimizing discrete functions is inherently challenging, it is somewhat disappointing that the proposed framework does not yield a measure that is more efficiently optimizable than standard cross-validation. The authors state that they have been "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) but provide no details, making it difficult for the reader to evaluate the suitability of the chosen complexity measure over potential alternatives.

The experimental validation is limited, consisting of a simple synthetic example and a demonstration on the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, the authors do not compare their method against any baselines or alternative approaches. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to assess the practical utility or performance of the proposed framework.

== Post rebuttal ==
The author rebuttal clarified some of the concerns raised in the initial review. However, the experimental evaluation remains weak, and the failure to adequately position the work within the broader machine learning literature is a major shortcoming. Nevertheless, I acknowledge the paper's valid theoretical contributions and have raised my score accordingly. I strongly urge the authors to substantially expand the discussion of related work in the final version of the paper.
