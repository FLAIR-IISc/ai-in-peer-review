The paper considers kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It begins with the observation that the “additive model,” which assumes regressors of the form  f = ∑<sub>p</sub> f<sub>p</sub>(X<sub>p</sub>) – effectively ignoring correlations between dimensions – is often problematic. To address this, the authors introduce the “group additive model,” allowing correlations between dimensions *within* groups while still simplifying the modeling of inter-group relationships. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose several, somewhat ad hoc, algorithms for finding optimal groupings. Results are presented for small synthetic and real-world datasets.

My primary concern with this paper is its limited discussion of related work. This makes it difficult, particularly for a non-expert, to assess the true novelty of the contribution. The problem of finding optimal groupings in data is also central to fields like “structured sparsity” and the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these areas receive no mention. Similarly, the problem can be viewed as an instance of feature selection, but this connection is also unexplored. The related work section effectively focuses on a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient to establish the paper’s originality.

However, the theoretical treatment itself appears novel. The authors define a complexity measure for a grouping – favoring many small groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems a reasonable approach. The drawback is that this measure is not easily optimized, leading the authors to propose either an exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy method (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While I recognize that optimizing discrete functions, such as those describing optimal groupings, is generally challenging, I was disappointed that the authors did not develop a measure that could be optimized more efficiently than through cross-validation. They mention ongoing investigations into alternative measures (line 174), but offer no further insights, hindering a proper evaluation of their chosen complexity measure.

Experimentally, the authors present a simple synthetic validation and a single example using the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, no baselines are provided, and the method is not compared to existing approaches. Consequently, it is difficult to judge the quality of the presented results in the absence of comparative benchmarks.

== Post rebuttal ==

The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. While I still find the experiments limited and the related work discussion unacceptably weak, I acknowledge the paper’s valid contributions and have accordingly adjusted my score. I strongly encourage the authors to significantly expand their discussion of related work prior to publication.
