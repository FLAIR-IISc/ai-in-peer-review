

The paper examines kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, beginning with the additive model where regressors are expressed as $f = \sum_p f_p(X_p)$, effectively ignoring correlations across dimensions. Recognizing this limitation, the authors propose a "group additive model" that groups dimensions to capture correlations within groups. They provide a formal treatment of this framework and introduce algorithms for finding groupings, though these are somewhat ad hoc. The results are demonstrated on small synthetic and real-world datasets.

The primary concern lies in the insufficient discussion of related work, making it difficult to assess the novelty. The problem of optimal grouping is closely related to structured sparsity, graphical model learning, and feature selection—fields the authors fail to address. Instead, the related work is reduced to a single citation (KDD-2015 [8]), which is inadequate for evaluating novelty. 

The theoretical contribution is novel: the authors define a complexity measure for groupings (favoring many small groups) derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This approach is theoretically sound, but the measure does not permit efficient optimization. Consequently, the authors resort to exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy method (analogous to forward feature selection). While acknowledging the inherent difficulty of optimizing discrete grouping functions, I find it disappointing that the authors did not propose a measure amenable to efficient optimization beyond cross-validation. The reference to "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) provides no substantive insights into the chosen measure's suitability.

Experimentally, the paper presents simple synthetic validation and a Boston Housing example but offers no baselines or comparisons to alternative methods. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to evaluate the empirical performance. 

== Post rebuttal ==  
The authors partially addressed my concerns in the rebuttal. I still find the experimental evaluation insufficient and the related work discussion inadequate. However, I acknowledge the paper's valid theoretical contributions, which modestly improved my assessment. I strongly recommend the authors substantially enhance the related work discussion in the final version.
