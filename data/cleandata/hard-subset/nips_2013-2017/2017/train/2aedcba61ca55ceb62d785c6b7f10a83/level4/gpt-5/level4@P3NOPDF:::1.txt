Summary
The paper studies kernel regression in high-dimensional settings by moving from the classical additive model f(x) = sum_p f_p(x_p), which ignores cross-dimensional dependencies, to a group additive model that allows interactions within predefined variable groups. The authors develop a formal framework for this setting, derive a complexity measure (via a covering number bound) that favors many small groups, and propose simple search procedures—exhaustive and greedy—to identify good groupings. Experiments are shown on small synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

Strengths
- The problem is clearly motivated: standard additive models can be too restrictive when interactions are localized within subsets of variables.
- The theoretical treatment appears sound. The derived complexity measure from covering number arguments is intuitive and helps formalize the trade-off between model capacity and grouping structure.
- The algorithms are easy to implement and likely to be useful in small to moderate dimensions or when the group structure is relatively simple.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Related work is inadequately covered, making it hard to assess novelty. The paper largely cites a single KDD 2015 paper, which is not sufficient. Closely related areas that should be discussed include:
  - Structured sparsity (e.g., group lasso, overlapping group lasso) and its kernelized or nonlinear extensions.
  - Additive and ANOVA kernels, kernel decompositions, and multiple kernel learning that select or weight groups of features.
  - Structure learning in probabilistic graphical models and Bayesian networks, where identifying interacting subsets is central.
  - Feature selection and interaction discovery methods (including forward selection and hierarchical interaction models).
  Without a clear positioning relative to these literatures, the contribution’s novelty and significance are difficult to evaluate.
- The proposed complexity measure, while theoretically motivated, is not accompanied by an optimization strategy beyond exhaustive search or a greedy forward procedure with cross-validation. There are no guarantees for the greedy search (e.g., conditions under which it is near-optimal), nor discussion of whether the objective has properties (e.g., submodularity) that could justify the heuristic.
- Computational aspects and scalability are not analyzed in depth. It is unclear how the search scales with the number of features, candidate groups, and kernel evaluations, or how it performs under high-dimensional, low-sample regimes.
- The experiments are thin:
  - Only small synthetic studies and a single real dataset are used.
  - There are no baselines. At a minimum, comparisons against standard additive kernels (no grouping), structured sparsity methods with nonlinear basis expansions, MKL-based grouping, and strong nonparametric baselines (e.g., random forests/gradient boosting) are needed.
  - No ablations (e.g., exhaustive vs. greedy, effect of the complexity penalty) or sensitivity analyses are provided.
  - The synthetic setup should more clearly probe recovery of true groups, robustness to misspecified groupings, and performance as group sizes and overlap vary.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well written at a high level, but several key definitions and assumptions could be stated more precisely. For example, whether groups can overlap, how kernels are constructed across groups (sum vs. product within a group), and how regularization is applied per group.
- The statement that “we have been investigating and comparing different measures” without presenting alternatives or insights does not help the reader judge the suitability of the chosen measure.

Suggestions for improvement
- Substantially expand the related work section to position the paper within:
  - Structured sparsity (group/overlapping group lasso; kernelized or nonlinear analogs).
  - Additive/ANOVA kernels and decomposable RKHSs; multiple kernel learning for group selection.
  - Interaction discovery and hierarchical modeling; structure learning in graphical models/Bayesian networks.
  Clearly articulate what is new here relative to these fields (e.g., the specific complexity measure, the way groups are scored/selected in an RKHS, or theoretical guarantees).
- Provide stronger and broader empirical evaluation:
  - Include multiple real datasets of varying dimensionality.
  - Compare against solid baselines as noted above.
  - Report predictive performance, calibration, and computational cost.
  - Add ablations and sensitivity analyses (e.g., effect of the complexity penalty, number of CV folds, group search budget).
  - In synthetic studies, evaluate group recovery accuracy and performance under misspecification and noise.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability of the search procedures, with guidance on practical settings (e.g., candidate group generation, pruning strategies).
- If possible, provide theoretical or empirical evidence supporting the greedy search (e.g., approximate submodularity, monotonicity, or bounded marginal gains), or propose a more principled surrogate objective amenable to efficient optimization.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a relevant problem and offers a coherent theoretical framework for group additive kernel regression. The complexity measure derived from covering numbers is a notable contribution, and the idea of selecting groups to capture localized interactions is compelling. However, the current presentation falls short on two fronts: positioning within the extensive related literature and empirical validation against meaningful baselines. These gaps make it difficult to judge the true novelty and practical impact of the work.

Post-rebuttal
The authors provided helpful clarifications that alleviated some concerns. Nevertheless, the experimental evaluation remains too limited, and the discussion of related work is still insufficient relative to core machine learning literatures touching this problem. I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions and have raised my score accordingly, but I strongly urge the authors to significantly expand the related work discussion and strengthen the empirical comparisons in any final version.
