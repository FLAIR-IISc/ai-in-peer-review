This paper investigates kernel regression within high-dimensional spaces, moving beyond the limitations of traditional additive models – which assume independence between dimensions – by introducing a “group additive model” to capture correlations within defined groups of features. The authors offer a formal analysis of this framework and propose algorithms for identifying optimal groupings, demonstrating their approach on limited synthetic and real-world datasets.

A significant weakness of the paper lies in its insufficient contextualization within the broader research landscape. The problem of optimal grouping bears strong connections to established fields like structured sparsity and graphical model learning/Bayesian networks, yet these areas receive no mention. Similarly, the work relates to feature selection, which is also not discussed. The related work section largely focuses on a single KDD-2015 paper, hindering a clear assessment of the contribution’s novelty.

Despite this, the theoretical development appears promising. The authors define a complexity measure for groupings, favoring numerous small groups, justified as an upper bound on the covering number. While logically sound, this measure presents optimization challenges. The proposed solutions – exhaustive search and a greedy approach – suffer from scalability issues or resemble standard forward feature selection. Although optimizing discrete functions is inherently difficult, the lack of a more efficiently optimizable complexity measure is a drawback. The authors allude to exploring alternative measures, but provide no details regarding their investigation or rationale for the chosen metric.

The experimental evaluation consists of basic synthetic validation and a demonstration on the Boston Housing dataset, lacking any baseline comparisons. This absence makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

== Post rebuttal ==

The authors’ rebuttal addressed some of my initial concerns. While the experimental validation remains limited and the connection to existing machine learning literature remains inadequately addressed, I recognize the paper presents a valid contribution. I have adjusted my score accordingly. I strongly recommend the authors substantially expand their discussion of related work prior to publication.
