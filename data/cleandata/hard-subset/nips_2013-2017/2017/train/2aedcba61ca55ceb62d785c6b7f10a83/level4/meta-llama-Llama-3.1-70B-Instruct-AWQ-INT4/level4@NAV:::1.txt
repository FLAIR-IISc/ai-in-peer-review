The paper explores kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, introducing the concept of an "additive model" that assumes a function f can be expressed as the sum of individual functions fp(X_p), effectively disregarding correlations between different dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors propose a "group additive model" that groups dimensions to capture correlations between certain variables. This framework is formally developed, accompanied by algorithms for identifying optimal groupings, which are evaluated using small-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.

A major concern with this paper is its inadequate discussion of related work, making it challenging for non-experts to assess the novelty of the research. Notably, the task of finding optimal groups is also addressed in structured sparsity and learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not explored. Furthermore, the problem can be viewed as an instance of feature selection, but this is not acknowledged. The related work section primarily references a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for evaluating the paper's novelty.

However, the theoretical treatment appears to be novel, as the authors define a complexity measure for groupings that favors many small groups, derived from an upper bound on the covering number. Although this measure is sensible, it does not lend itself to efficient optimization, prompting the authors to propose an exhaustive search or a greedy method. While acknowledging the challenges of optimizing discrete functions, it is disappointing that the authors did not develop a more efficient optimization method beyond cross-validation.

The experimental evaluation is limited, comprising simple synthetic validation and a basic example using the Boston Housing data, without providing a baseline or comparing with alternative methods. This makes it difficult to assess the quality of the results.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors' rebuttal addressed some of my concerns, but I still believe the experimental section is underdeveloped and the related work discussion is inadequate. Nevertheless, I acknowledge the paper's valid contributions and have revised my score accordingly. I strongly recommend that the authors enhance their discussion of related work in the published version of the paper.
