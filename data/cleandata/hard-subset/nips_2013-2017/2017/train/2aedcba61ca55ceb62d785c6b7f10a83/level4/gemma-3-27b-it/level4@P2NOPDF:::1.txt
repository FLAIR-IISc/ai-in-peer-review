This paper addresses kernel regression in high-dimensional spaces, motivated by the limitations of traditional additive models which assume independence between dimensions. The authors propose a “group additive model” as a more flexible alternative, allowing for the modeling of correlations within predefined groups of features. They present a formal analysis of this grouping approach and develop algorithms for identifying optimal groupings, evaluating their performance on small synthetic datasets and a real-world case study.

My primary concern lies with the paper’s insufficient contextualization within the broader machine learning literature. Determining the true novelty of this work is difficult due to a limited discussion of related fields. The problem of finding optimal groupings in data is central to areas like structured sparsity and the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not explored. Similarly, the work bears a relationship to feature selection, which is also absent from the related work discussion. The current review of related work is largely confined to a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is inadequate for evaluating the paper’s unique contributions.

Despite this weakness, the theoretical development is a notable strength. The authors define a complexity measure for groupings – favoring numerous smaller groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This derivation appears logically sound. However, this complexity measure presents optimization challenges. The proposed solution relies on either an exhaustive search (computationally expensive) or a greedy approach (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While acknowledging the inherent difficulty of optimizing discrete functions representing optimal groupings, I found the lack of a more efficiently optimizable measure disappointing. The authors mention exploring alternative measures (line 174), but offer no further details, hindering an assessment of the chosen measure’s suitability.

The experimental validation consists of simple synthetic examples and an application to the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, the paper lacks baseline comparisons with other methods, making it difficult to assess the significance of the reported results. Without benchmarks, it’s hard to determine whether the observed performance represents a genuine improvement. 

== Post rebuttal ==

The authors addressed some of my initial concerns in the rebuttal. However, I still find the experimental evaluation lacking in scope and the treatment of related work demonstrably insufficient. Nevertheless, I acknowledge that the paper presents valid contributions and have adjusted my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to significantly expand their discussion of related work before publication to properly position their contribution within the existing literature.
