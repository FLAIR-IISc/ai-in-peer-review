

Summary:  
This paper proposes a group-additive nonparametric regression model where the regression function is additive across predefined groups of predictors, bridging the gap between fully nonparametric models (computationally challenging) and restrictive additive models. The key contribution lies in learning the unknown group structure from data. The authors introduce a novel penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, integrated into kernel ridge regression. This penalty enables joint optimization over both the group partition (defining a function space via direct sums of RKHSs per group) and regression estimates within groups. Two algorithms approximate this compound optimization, and theoretical guarantees establish (a) convergence rates of empirical risk to true risk, and (b) consistency of the group structure estimate (probability of matching the true structure approaches 1 as n→∞). Empirical validation includes synthetic and real-data experiments.  

Main Comments:  
The paper’s core innovation is quantifying the complexity of a group structure using covering numbers of the direct sum RKHS space—a well-motivated approach that significantly advances the literature on flexible nonparametric modeling. The method is comprehensive, with a clear problem formulation, novel penalty design, solid theoretical analysis, and convincing empirical results. Assuming the literature review (Lines 31–50) adequately covers existing approaches for models between additive and fully nonparametric forms (which the reviewer finds plausible), this work represents a meaningful contribution. The primary limitation noted is computational scalability when the number of variables is large, as highlighted in the Discussion. The writing is generally clear, though it contains numerous typographical errors requiring correction.  

Minor Comments:

2. **Line 169**: The phrase "almost surely" appears contextually ambiguous. Clarify whether this refers to convergence as n→∞ (standard in asymptotic analysis).  
3. **Equation (3)**: A summation over i is missing in the expression.  
4. **Line 205**: "Convolutional kernel" is less standard than "translation-invariant kernel" for this context.

Overall, this is a strong submission that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically grounded approach. The minor issues noted are primarily typographical or require minor clarification, and the work’s significance is evident given the current landscape of nonparametric regression methods. The proposed framework offers a promising path for learning flexible group structures in high-dimensional settings, with clear potential for future extensions.
