The paper studies kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It starts from the additive model, f(x) = Σ_p f_p(x_p), which ignores cross-dimensional dependencies. To address this limitation, the authors propose a “group additive model,” in which variables are partitioned into groups so that correlations within groups can be modeled. The paper provides a formal analysis of this setting and proposes somewhat ad hoc procedures for discovering groupings. Experiments are presented on small synthetic datasets and a simple real-world example.

My primary concern is the weak treatment of related work, which makes it hard (as a non-specialist) to assess the true novelty. The problem of finding optimal groups is closely connected to areas such as structured sparsity, and to learning graphical models or Bayesian networks; yet these literatures are not discussed. The task is also related to feature selection, but this is likewise not covered. The related work section essentially centers on a single KDD 2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for establishing the paper’s position and contribution.

On the positive side, the theoretical development appears novel. The authors derive a simple complexity measure for a grouping—one that tends to favor many small groups—via an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. However, the resulting criterion is hard to optimize. The paper therefore turns to either exhaustive search, which does not scale, or a greedy procedure reminiscent of forward feature selection. While I recognize that optimizing over discrete groupings is difficult, I was disappointed that the paper did not arrive at an objective that admits more efficient optimization than cross-validation over candidate partitions. The authors state they have been “investigating and comparing different measures” (line 174), but provide no details, making it difficult to judge whether the chosen measure is appropriate or competitive.

Experimentally, the paper offers simple synthetic validations and a small demonstration on the Boston Housing dataset. There are no baselines or comparisons to existing methods. Without such comparisons, it is hard to evaluate the empirical quality or practical impact of the approach.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors’ rebuttal clarified several of my questions. Nevertheless, I still find the experimental section thin and the discussion of related work unacceptably limited relative to well-established ideas in machine learning. That said, I acknowledge the paper’s substantive contributions and have raised my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially expand and improve the related-work discussion in any final version.
