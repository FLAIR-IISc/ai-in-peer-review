

This paper proposes a novel method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors recognize that while additive models are interpretable and efficient in high dimensions, they cannot capture interactions between variables, whereas fully nonparametric models or higher-order interaction models (like functional ANOVA) suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The group additive model offers a middle ground, where predictor variables are partitioned into groups such that the response function is additive across groups but can capture interactions within groups.

The key innovation is the development of a complexity measure for group additive structures based on RKHS covering numbers. This measure is incorporated into a penalized kernel ridge regression objective, enabling simultaneous estimation of the nonparametric function and identification of the intrinsic group structure. The authors rigorously prove the consistency of their method and propose two algorithms (exhaustive search and a stepwise approach) for practical implementation. Experimental results on synthetic and real data demonstrate the method's effectiveness in recovering true group structures.

The paper excels in quality with its technically sound approach and comprehensive theoretical analysis. The consistency proofs are thorough, and the experimental design appropriately supports the claims. The method is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation of existing approaches. However, the paper could better discuss computational complexity for very large p, as noted in the discussion of the stepwise algorithm.

Clarity is generally good, though several typos and grammatical errors detract from the reading experience. For instance, "G^ exists and unique" (Line 160) should be "G^ exists and is unique," and "turning parameters" (Line 247) should be "tuning parameters." The mathematical notation is clear, but the discussion of "interaction" between variables (Lines 31-50) could be more precise. The authors state that variables in different groups do not interact, but it's not entirely clear how this relates to standard statistical definitions of interaction.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by defining the intrinsic group additive structure and developing a novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers. The paper effectively differentiates itself from related work on functional ANOVA models and graphical models. While there is a connection to Pan et al. (2015) on Optimal Kernel Group Transformation, this paper addresses their two key limitations (non-uniqueness of group structure and lack of systematic identification method) that were not addressed in previous work.

The significance of this work is substantial. The group additive model provides a useful middle ground between fully additive and fully nonparametric models, offering a balance between interpretability and flexibility. The method has potential applications across various domains of high-dimensional regression where practitioners seek to understand variable relationships without the computational burden of full interaction models. The Boston Housing data application demonstrates practical utility, with interpretable group structures that yield meaningful visualizations.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated problem with clear connection to existing work
- Strong theoretical foundation with consistency proofs
- Novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers
- Comprehensive experimental validation
- Clear distinction from related work (graphical models, functional ANOVA)

Weaknesses:
- Several typos and minor grammatical errors
- Vague discussion of "interaction" concept
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for large p
- Could better explain when this method would be preferable to alternatives

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The minor issues noted above do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and recommend acceptance.
