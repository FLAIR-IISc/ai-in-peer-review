- Addresses group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression in high dimensions.  
- Rigorously defines “intrinsic group additive structure” and proposes a structure-penalized kernel method to identify structure and estimate functions.  
- Derives a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on RKHS covering numbers.  
- Establishes consistency results (Theorem 4) for the proposed method.  
- Well-motivated problem with technically sound approach and generally consistent notation.  
- Positions work appropriately relative to additive models, functional ANOVA, higher-order interaction models, and OKGT, noting prior limitations.  
- Connects briefly to graphical models for additional context.  
- Comprehensive simulation studies and a real-data application; supplementary material enhances reproducibility.  
- Develops both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms to address computational feasibility.  
- Theoretical framework is dense and may be inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with RKHS and covering numbers.  
- Simulation results indicate sensitivity to tuning parameters (µ, α) and potential problematic dependence on λ.  
- Stepwise algorithm may fail to find optimal structure in complex scenarios and needs further emphasis and improvement.  
- Lacks comparison of computational cost between exhaustive and stepwise algorithms.  
- Real-data (Boston Housing) analysis is limited; deeper interpretation and variable-importance analysis within groups would strengthen impact.  
- Paper focuses on universal kernels; discussion of alternative kernels and their impact is missing.  
- Overall: strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and solid empirical evaluation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel method, theoretical guarantees, and effective empirical performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical complexity limiting accessibility and parameter/algorithmic limitations needing further work.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—add intuitive theory explanations, guidance on parameter selection, expand stepwise limitations discussion, and deepen real-data interpretation.
