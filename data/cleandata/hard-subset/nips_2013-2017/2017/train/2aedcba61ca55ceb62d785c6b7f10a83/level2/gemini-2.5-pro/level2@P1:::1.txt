This paper tackles the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by proposing a method to learn a "group additive model". Such a model, `f(X) = sum(f_j(X_uj))`, serves as a compromise between the simplicity of standard additive models, which ignore variable interactions, and the complexity of fully nonparametric models or higher-order ANOVA models, which suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The main contributions are threefold: 1) A rigorous definition of the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique minimal structure among all possible valid groupings of variables. 2) A structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this intrinsic structure and estimate the component functions. 3) The derivation of a novel complexity penalty for group structures, motivated by the covering number of the associated reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors provide a theoretical proof of consistency for their structure identification method and demonstrate its performance on synthetic and real-world data.

This work builds upon prior ideas of group additive models, such as [11], but provides a much-needed formalization of the identification problem and a principled, penalized estimation framework to solve it.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem: how to model interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression in a way that is both statistically efficient and interpretable. The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" (Definition 1, Theorem 1) is a strong conceptual contribution that clarifies what it means to find the "correct" grouping of interacting variables. This provides a solid foundation for the subsequent estimation procedure.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed penalty term for group structure complexity is a key novelty and is well-motivated. Grounding the penalty `C(G)` in the capacity of the corresponding RKHS (specifically, using an upper bound on the covering number) is elegant and provides a principled alternative to more ad-hoc penalties. The consistency proof (Theorem 4), showing that the method correctly identifies the intrinsic structure with enough data, provides strong theoretical support for the approach.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, and the development from the definition of the intrinsic structure to the penalized optimization problem is easy to follow. The example in Section 2.1 is helpful for building intuition.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Computational Feasibility:** The primary weakness of the proposed method lies in its computational complexity. The number of possible group structures (partitions of `p` variables) is given by the Bell numbers, which grow extremely rapidly. The exhaustive search (Algorithm 1) is therefore only feasible for very small `p` (e.g., `p < 10`). This limitation should be stated more explicitly.
    The proposed backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a necessary practical concession, but as a greedy heuristic, it is not guaranteed to find the global optimum and can get stuck in poor local minima. The failure to recover the true structure for Model 4 in the simulations highlights this weakness. Could the authors comment on why the algorithm might have failed in this specific case? Have they considered other search strategies, such as forward selection or stochastic search methods (e.g., simulated annealing), which might be less susceptible to local optima?

2.  **Algorithm 2 Clarity:** The description of the backward stepwise algorithm could be clarified. Step 4 reads: "G' <- either split j as a new group or add j to an existing group". This sounds more like a forward-stepwise or variable-moving procedure. A backward procedure would typically start with the single group `{(1, ..., p)}` and, at each step, consider splitting one of the current groups into two. Please clarify the exact set of moves considered at each step of the algorithm.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** While the simulation results demonstrate that the method can work, the experiments lack a comparison to alternative methods. It would significantly strengthen the paper to compare GASI against other methods that can learn sparse interaction structures, such as COSSO, SpAM, or even interaction-detecting methods like Random Forests or MARS. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical advantage of this new method over the existing state of the art.

4.  **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity:** The method relies on three tuning parameters: `lambda` (regularization), `mu` (structure penalty weight), and `alpha` (group size penalty). Tuning all three simultaneously via grid search and cross-validation is computationally demanding. The connection between `alpha` as a tunable integer and its theoretical motivation from `ln(r/epsilon)` feels somewhat loose. A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of these parameters, particularly `alpha`, would be valuable. For instance, how does the choice of `alpha` impact the size of the groups that are discovered?

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for identifying interaction structures in nonparametric regression. The formalization of the problem and the RKHS-based penalty are significant contributions. However, the practical utility is currently hampered by major computational challenges and the limitations of the proposed greedy search algorithm. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the experimental validation could be made more convincing with a broader comparison to competing methods.

Overall, I believe the strengths, particularly the novel theoretical framework, outweigh the weaknesses. This is a solid contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance but strongly encourage the authors to address the points above, especially regarding the clarification of Algorithm 2 and by adding discussion on the computational limitations and potential comparison to other methods in the final version.
