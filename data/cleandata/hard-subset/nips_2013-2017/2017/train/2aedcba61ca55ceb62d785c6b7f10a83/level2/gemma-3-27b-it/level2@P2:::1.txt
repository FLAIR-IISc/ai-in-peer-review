This paper addresses the problem of group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression, a crucial task in high-dimensional settings where traditional additive models fall short due to neglected interactions. The authors rigorously define the “intrinsic group additive structure” and propose a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. A novel complexity measure for group additive structures is derived, and consistency results are established. The paper is well-motivated, tackling a relevant problem with a technically sound approach.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on nonparametric regression, highlighting the drawbacks of standard approaches in high dimensions. It appropriately references the additive model [5], functional ANOVA models [4, 13], and higher-order interaction models [6], demonstrating an understanding of the trade-offs between flexibility and interpretability. The connection to the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method [11] is clearly explained, and the authors effectively articulate the limitations of previous work – specifically, the non-identifiability problem and the lack of a systematic structure identification approach.  The brief mention of connections to graphical models [2, 7, 9] provides additional context.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is characterized by a strong theoretical foundation. The definition of the “intrinsic group additive structure” provides a mathematically sound basis for the problem. The consistency proof (Theorem 4) is a significant contribution, assuring the reliability of the proposed method under appropriate conditions.
*   **Novel Complexity Measure:** The derivation of a tractable complexity measure for group additive structures, based on covering numbers of RKHSs, is a key innovation. This measure is crucial for the penalized estimation framework.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly define the problem, motivations, and contributions.  The notation is generally consistent and facilitates understanding.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The simulation studies are thorough, exploring different model settings and demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The inclusion of a real-data application adds practical relevance. The provision of supplementary material containing detailed results enhances reproducibility.
*   **Algorithm Design:** The development of both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms demonstrates consideration of computational feasibility in different scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of the Theory:** While the theoretical framework is strong, it's quite dense and might be inaccessible to readers not deeply familiar with RKHS theory and covering numbers. More intuitive explanations of the theoretical concepts could improve readability.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The simulation results suggest sensitivity to the choice of tuning parameters (µ and α). While the authors provide a grid search approach, a more systematic method for parameter selection, or a theoretical guide for choosing appropriate values, would be beneficial.  The dependence on λ is also mentioned as potentially problematic.
*   **Stepwise Algorithm Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that the stepwise algorithm may not always find the optimal structure, especially in complex scenarios (Model 4 in the simulations). This limitation should be further emphasized, and potential improvements explored.  A comparison of the computational cost of both algorithms would also be useful.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The application to the Boston Housing data is somewhat limited. A more in-depth analysis of the identified group structure and its implications for understanding the housing market would strengthen the practical impact of the work.  Exploring variable importance within each group could be beneficial.
* **Discussion of alternative kernels:** The paper focuses on using universal kernels. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential impact of choosing different kernels and their relevance to the specific problem.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and a well-executed empirical evaluation. The authors have addressed a challenging problem in nonparametric regression with a novel and rigorous approach. While the theoretical complexity and parameter sensitivity are potential limitations, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for group additive structure identification.
*   The consistency results provide strong guarantees for the reliability of the method.
*   The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach in various settings.
*   The work addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical complexity might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
*   Parameter sensitivity and the limitations of the stepwise algorithm require further investigation.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should consider adding more intuitive explanations of the theoretical concepts, providing guidance on parameter selection, and expanding the discussion of the limitations of the stepwise algorithm. Further exploration of the practical implications of the identified group structure in the real-data application would also be valuable.
