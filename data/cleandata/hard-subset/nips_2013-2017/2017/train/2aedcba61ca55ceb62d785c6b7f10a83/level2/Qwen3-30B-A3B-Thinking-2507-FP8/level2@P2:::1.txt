

This paper proposes a method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors address a key limitation of previous group additive models (e.g., [11]) by rigorously defining the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent the target function, and developing a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. The method uses a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers as a penalty term, and the authors prove consistency of their approach.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it resolves the non-uniqueness problem of group additive structures by introducing the concept of intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1), which is the minimal group structure that can represent the function. Second, it develops a tractable complexity measure for group structures based on RKHS covering numbers (Section 2.3.2), which is the key innovation enabling the identification method. Third, it provides theoretical consistency results (Theorem 4) and practical algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) for structure identification.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the theoretical development is rigorous. The authors effectively contextualize their work by discussing the relationship between group additive models and functional ANOVA models ([4], [13]), as well as graphical models ([2], [7]), clearly differentiating their approach from these alternatives. The simulation studies (Section 4) and real data application (Section 5) demonstrate the method's effectiveness in identifying true group structures.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear definitions (Definitions 1-2), sound propositions (Propositions 1-3), and a rigorous consistency proof (Theorem 4).
2. The complexity measure C(G) = ∑j α|uj|+1 is well-motivated and practically implementable, addressing a key gap in the literature.
3. The two algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) provide practical solutions for different problem sizes.
4. The paper properly acknowledges limitations of previous work, particularly the non-uniqueness problem in [11].

Weaknesses:
1. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with alternative structure learning methods for nonparametric regression. While the connection to graphical models is noted, the authors should explicitly compare their approach to methods like those in [6] or [10] that also handle interactions, discussing relative advantages and limitations.
2. The scalability of the method to extremely high-dimensional settings (p >> n) is not adequately addressed. The paper states "when the number of predictor variables increases, we use a backward stepwise algorithm," but doesn't provide theoretical or empirical evidence of how the method scales with p (e.g., computational complexity, performance degradation).
3. The choice of the tuning parameter α (which affects the penalty weight) is not well-justified. The paper uses α as an integer in [1, 10] without explaining how this choice impacts the method's performance or how it should be selected in practice.
4. The simulation study (Table 2) shows some deterioration in performance for Model 1 when using the validation-based parameter selection (Algorithm 1), but the authors don't investigate why this occurs or how to prevent it.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The problem of identifying group additive structures is important, as it provides a middle ground between the standard additive model (which ignores interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which become computationally intractable). The proposed method is theoretically sound, with a clear path to practical implementation, and the empirical results support its effectiveness.

The most significant potential improvement would be to strengthen the comparison with alternative methods and provide more detailed discussion of the method's scalability. Additionally, the authors should clarify how to select α in practice, as this appears to be a critical tuning parameter.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on nonparametric regression. The theoretical developments are sound, the method is well-motivated, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS: it is of high quality, clearly written, original in its approach to the group additive structure identification problem, and significant for the field of high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

I recommend acceptance.
