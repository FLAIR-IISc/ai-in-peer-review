Summary
The paper tackles nonparametric regression in high dimensions by introducing and formalizing “group additive structures” (GAS), where variables are partitioned into disjoint groups and f(X) decomposes additively across groups. The authors define a partial order on group structures and introduce the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure G*, the minimal amiable partition under this order. They propose a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly estimates the regression function in a direct-sum RKHS and identifies the group structure via a complexity penalty derived from covering-number bounds of the corresponding RKHS. They prove selection consistency for G* under appropriate choices of tuning parameters, and demonstrate the method on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset. For larger p, a backward stepwise heuristic is used.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions GAS between additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani] and functional ANOVA/interaction models (e.g., SS-ANOVA, ESP kernels; Gu; Kandasamy & Yu). It relates to OKGT (Pan et al.) by directly addressing identifiability and structure learning, which OKGT did not resolve. The penalty leverages RKHS capacity measures (Smola & Schölkopf; Steinwart & Christmann; Zhou; Kühn). Connections to graphical models are noted (Koller & Friedman), though the focus is on function structure rather than conditional independencies.

Quality
- Theory: The partial-order framework is sensible, and the direct-sum RKHS formulation is standard. The covering-number-based penalty is principled; Proposition 3 (sum of logs of covering numbers for direct sums) is plausible and useful. The deviation bounds (Proposition 4, Theorem 2) and the separation of amiable vs non-amiable structures (Theorem 3) underpin Theorem 4 (consistency). However:
  - The uniqueness of the intrinsic group structure (Theorem 1) is asserted with limited assumptions. Given the finiteness of partitions, minimal elements exist, but uniqueness generally requires additional conditions. Please clarify assumptions (e.g., distributional conditions such as independence or non-degeneracy of f) that rule out multiple incomparable minimal amiable partitions.
  - Proposition 1 requires independence for strict inclusion of L2 spaces; do later results (especially uniqueness and consistency) rely on independence? If so, state this prominently.
  - The complexity penalty C(G) moves from a bound of order ∑u α(ε)^{|u|+1} to the implemented ∑u α|u| by “absorbing” factors into μ. This jump elides the dependence on ε and kernel constants. Provide a detailed derivation, specify how α is chosen/tuned, and discuss sensitivity. At present α is an abstract knob rather than a calibrated capacity proxy.
  - The theoretical selection consistency is for the global minimizer of the penalized objective. The stepwise algorithm used for large p lacks guarantees; the experiments show it can fail. This gap should be emphasized.

- Experiments: Simulations show recovery frequencies of G*, but:
  - There is no comparison to baselines (e.g., additive models without interactions, SS-ANOVA, SALSA/ESP kernels up to D=2/3, OKGT, or lasso-based structured interaction selection). Without predictive and structural comparisons, significance is hard to assess.
  - Real-data analysis focuses on interpretability; predictive comparisons are missing (CV MSE vs strong baselines).
  - Hyperparameter selection: λ, μ, α are tuned via validation/CV, but the protocol (nested CV vs reuse of validation) could bias performance. Please clarify and, ideally, nest CV.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured. Useful definitions and examples are provided. Some notational slips and informal steps should be tightened:
- Clearly separate the roles of r, ε, α in the penalty and how they relate to kernel hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidths). If α is intended to reflect dimensionality only, say so and justify.
- State all assumptions needed for Theorem 1 and Theorem 4.
- Provide more detail or a proof sketch for Proposition 3.
- Several references lack years and details.

Originality
The formalization of intrinsic group additive structure and its identification via a structural penalty rooted in RKHS capacity is novel in this specific context. While using capacity penalties is standard, applying covering-number-based penalties to partition selection in RKHSs is an interesting idea. The conceptual bridge between additive, grouped, and interaction-rich models is useful.

Significance
If robust, GAS could offer a practical middle ground between additive and fully interaction-rich models, improving interpretability and sample efficiency when interactions are localized. However, the current empirical evidence is limited (no baselines, modest p), and the stepwise scalability story lacks guarantees. Strengthening experiments and clarifying assumptions would improve impact.

Constructive suggestions
1) Theory:
- Clarify and, if needed, add assumptions ensuring uniqueness of G*. If uniqueness cannot be guaranteed, discuss identifiability up to equivalence classes and adapt Theorem 4 accordingly.
- Provide a complete derivation of C(G) from covering numbers, including constants’ dependence on kernels and dimension; describe how α is set in practice and how kernel bandwidth selection interacts with C(G).
- Give guidance on asymptotic scaling of μ and λ with n, and practical heuristics for tuning.

2) Algorithms:
- Add a stochastic or beam-search variant of the stepwise algorithm with multiple random restarts; report stability.
- Consider leveraging submodularity-like properties or surrogate relaxations to obtain approximation guarantees.

3) Experiments:
- Include baselines (GAM, SS-ANOVA/KRR with pairwise ESP kernels, SALSA, OKGT) and report both structure recovery (precision/recall over edges) and prediction (CV MSE/RMSE).
- Scale studies in p and n with runtime.
- Sensitivity analyses for μ and α; ablations of the penalty form.

4) Clarity:
- Improve presentation of assumptions and proofs; fix reference details and typos.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem motivation; formal notion of intrinsic group structure.
- Principled penalty using RKHS capacity; selection consistency result.
- Potentially useful mid-complexity model class with interpretability.

Cons for acceptance
- Unclear uniqueness/identifiability assumptions; some theoretical steps are informal.
- Limited empirical validation; no baselines or scalability guarantees.
- Heuristic search may miss G*; practical tuning of α/μ insufficiently justified.

Overall, this is a promising and original direction, but I recommend strengthening theory (assumptions and derivations) and substantially expanding empirical comparisons before acceptance.
