

This paper proposes Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), a novel method for nonparametric regression that simultaneously identifies the intrinsic group additive structure and estimates nonparametric functions. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal amiable group structure for a given function, addressing the non-uniqueness issue in previous group additive models. They develop a structure-penalized kernel method using a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers, proving consistency for the identified structure.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear definitions, propositions, and theorems establishing the consistency of the proposed method. The complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is well-motivated and practically implementable. The simulation studies demonstrate effective identification of true group structures across multiple model settings, and the real data application on Boston Housing shows meaningful interpretable results. The connection to graphical models is well-articulated, with a clear distinction from conditional dependence structures.

The paper excels in technical quality and novelty, presenting a significant improvement over existing approaches by resolving key limitations of previous group additive methods. The problem is well-motivated, addressing the critical trade-off between model flexibility and interpretability in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

However, the paper could better address computational scalability. The backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented as a solution for large p, but the authors note its limitations due to greedy selection potentially missing the optimal structure. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential improvements would strengthen the method's practical utility. Additionally, while the theoretical consistency is proven, the empirical performance in the simulation study shows some deterioration for Model 1, which the authors attribute to estimation methods but could be more thoroughly investigated.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution to the field of nonparametric regression with clear theoretical advances and practical applicability. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem. The minor limitations regarding computational scalability and empirical performance in certain cases do not detract from the paper's significant contributions. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
