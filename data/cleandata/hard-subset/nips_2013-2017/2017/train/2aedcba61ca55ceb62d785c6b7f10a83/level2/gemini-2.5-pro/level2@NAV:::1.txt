This paper presents a novel method for Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI) in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors aim to find a middle ground between overly simple additive models, which ignore variable interactions, and overly complex functional ANOVA models. They formalize the concept of an "intrinsic group additive structure"—the most parsimonious grouping of predictor variables that explains the data—and propose a structure-penalized kernel method to identify it. The key contribution is a theoretically-grounded complexity penalty for group structures, derived from the covering number of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This allows for the simultaneous estimation of the nonparametric functions and identification of the underlying group structure.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The problem is highly relevant, and the proposed solution is elegant and novel. Defining the structural penalty based on the capacity of the RKHS provides a principled way to compare different groupings, which is a significant advance over more heuristic approaches. The formalization of an "intrinsic" structure is also a valuable theoretical contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical section is a major strength, providing a proof of consistency for the structure identification method (Theorem 4). This rigorous support for the central claim is commendable. The empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data effectively demonstrates the method's practical utility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively motivates the problem by situating it within existing literature and walks the reader logically from the problem definition to the proposed solution and its theoretical justification.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms present a practical limitation. The exhaustive search is computationally prohibitive for even a moderate number of predictors. The backward stepwise algorithm is a reasonable heuristic, but it is greedy and not guaranteed to find the optimal solution, as acknowledged by its failure on one of the simulation models. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of these limitations or an exploration of more advanced search strategies.
*   **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity:** The method relies on three tuning parameters (λ, µ, α). While cross-validation is proposed, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on their practical selection and the method's sensitivity to their values. For instance, the main text states that λ is assumed to be given, which slightly undermines the completeness of the proposed procedure.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant and original contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical grounding is excellent, and the method is well-motivated. While the computational aspects could be further developed, the core ideas are compelling and likely to inspire future work.
