

This paper proposes a method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in nonparametric regression, addressing a key limitation of traditional additive models that cannot capture interactions between predictors. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group partition that can represent the regression function, and develop a structure-penalized kernel method to identify this structure. The method introduces a novel complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs to balance model fit and structural complexity, and provides theoretical consistency guarantees.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it formally defines the intrinsic group additive structure, resolving the nonidentifiability issue that plagues previous work on group additive models. Second, it develops a practical method for simultaneously identifying the group structure and estimating the nonparametric functions using kernel methods. Third, it establishes theoretical consistency for the proposed method, showing that the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the method's effectiveness on both simulated data and real-world applications, including the Boston Housing dataset.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation. The mathematical foundations are sound, and the connection to previous work on additive models and functional ANOVA is well-explained. The authors properly acknowledge related work, particularly the OKGT method from [11], and clearly articulate how their approach differs and improves upon it.

The theoretical contributions are strong, with the consistency proofs for the structure identification being particularly noteworthy. The derivation of the complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs is well-motivated and connects existing theory to the specific problem at hand. The paper's connection to graphical models is thoughtfully presented, with a clear explanation of how the group additive structure differs from conditional dependence structures.

The empirical evaluation is thorough, with simulation studies across multiple model settings and a real data application. The results demonstrate that the method successfully recovers the true group structures in most cases, with the stepwise algorithm providing a computationally feasible alternative for larger problems. The visualization of estimated functions for the Boston Housing data adds interpretability to the results.

The paper's main limitations relate to computational scalability and theoretical scope. The exhaustive search algorithm for structure identification becomes computationally infeasible as the number of predictors increases, though the stepwise algorithm provides a practical alternative. The paper does not explore how the method scales to extremely high-dimensional settings (p >> n), which would be valuable for many modern applications. Additionally, while the paper discusses connections to graphical models, it does not compare the proposed method to other interaction detection techniques like LASSO-based approaches for interaction terms.

The significance of this work lies in its practical contribution to high-dimensional nonparametric regression. By identifying the minimal group additive structure, the method maintains the interpretability and efficiency of additive models while accommodating meaningful interactions between predictors. This represents a meaningful middle ground between the simplicity of the standard additive model and the complexity of functional ANOVA models with higher-order interactions.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, particularly for the stepwise algorithm. While the paper mentions the greedy nature of the stepwise approach might miss the true structure in some cases, a more systematic analysis of when this might occur would be valuable. Additionally, the paper could explore how the choice of α (used in the complexity measure) affects performance, potentially suggesting a principled way to select this parameter rather than treating it as a hyperparameter to be tuned.

The method's applicability to other kernel-based nonparametric models beyond the specific kernel methods used in the paper would also be worth exploring. The paper's focus on kernel methods is appropriate for the problem, but a brief discussion of potential extensions to other nonparametric frameworks would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is theoretically sound and practically implementable, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and provides a valuable tool for researchers working with high-dimensional data where interactions between predictors are important but the full interaction structure is unknown. The work advances the state of the art by providing a principled approach to structure identification that maintains the interpretability and efficiency of additive models while accommodating meaningful interactions. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.
