- Addresses high-dimensional nonparametric regression by learning group additive structure to balance additivity and interactions.
- Defines "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique minimal partition, resolving prior non-identifiability.
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel estimator to jointly identify the intrinsic partition and estimate component functions.
- Introduces a novel complexity penalty for group structures derived from RKHS covering numbers.
- Proves consistency of the proposed structure identification estimator (Theorem 4) with sound supporting proofs.
- Offers an exhaustive search algorithm for small problems and a backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems.
- Technical quality is high; theoretical contributions are the main strength and appear well-founded.
- Empirical validation is limited to low-dimensional settings and could be stronger.
- Backward stepwise algorithm shows mixed performance, failing on one of five simulated models.
- Real-data demonstration (Boston Housing) is illustrative but lacks ground truth and is qualitative.
- Paper is very well-written and organized; explanations and algorithms are clear.
- Minor clarity issues: justification for dropping “+1” in the exponent of the penalty feels arbitrary and needs clearer explanation.
- Additional clarity desired on main-theorem assumptions (noise, kernels, tuning µ scaling) rather than deferring to supplement.
- Originality: first systematic formalization of intrinsic structure and novel RKHS-capacity-based structure penalty.
- Significance: addresses an important practical/theoretical problem and could influence other structure-learning areas.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, novelty, clarity, and potential impact.
- Arguments for rejection: scalability concerns (exhaustive vs. heuristic search), limited empirical evaluation, and sensitivity to tuning parameters (µ, α).
- Recommendation: accept, despite computational/empirical weaknesses, due to strong theoretical contribution.
- Suggested improvements: clarify exponent simplification, discuss advanced optimization strategies beyond greedy search, analyze the backward algorithm’s failure case, and investigate tuning instability (e.g., Model 1 degradation).
