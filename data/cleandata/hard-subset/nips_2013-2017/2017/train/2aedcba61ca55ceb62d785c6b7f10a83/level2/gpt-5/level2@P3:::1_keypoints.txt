- Paper introduces the intrinsic group-additive structure: the unique minimal partition making a regression function group-additive, formalized with a partial order.

- Proposes an RKHS-based estimator that jointly estimates the regression function and the group partition via a structural penalty derived from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs.

- Proves selection consistency: with appropriate regularization the estimated partition converges in probability to the intrinsic partition (for fixed p).

- Provides algorithms: exhaustive search with validation for small p and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic for moderate p.

- Empirical demonstration via simulations and Boston Housing example shows feasibility and interpretable recovered groups.

- Connects to prior work: additive models, functional ANOVA/smoothing-spline ANOVA, SALSA, ESP-kernel models, OKGT — advances theory by formalizing intrinsic partition and providing RKHS capacity-based penalization.

- Strength: rigorous formalization of group-additive structure with existence/uniqueness proofs for the intrinsic partition.

- Strength: novel structural penalty grounded in covering-number bounds, giving a principled route to selection consistency.

- Strength: distinguishes amiable vs non-amiable structures and provides risk bounds quantifying detectability.

- Strength: flexible kernel framework (universal kernels) supporting approximation in L2 and structure recovery.

- Strength: practical algorithms and examples showing structure recovery in simulations and interpretability in real data.

- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited (small p) and lacks comparisons to competitive baselines (GAM/SpAM, functional ANOVA, SALSA, OKGT, sparse interaction methods).

- Weakness: scalability concerns — exhaustive search infeasible for larger p and the backward heuristic lacks guarantees and failed in at least one simulation (Model 4); no runtime/complexity analysis or large-p results.

- Weakness: penalty notation and calibration unclear (α|u| vs α|u|+1, α treated as tunable integer linked to ln(r/ε)); constants and kernel bandwidth dependence not discussed.

- Weakness: theoretical scope limited — results assume fixed p and particular λn, µn regimes; lacks analysis for growing p, correlated covariates, and kernel hyperparameter selection; some propositions rely on independence assumptions not revisited.

- Weakness: limited practical guidance on tuning λ, µ, and kernel parameters and on sensitivity to these choices.

- Quality: theoretical development is sound at a high level with plausible proof strategy, but technical details/conditions (rates, constants, kernel assumptions) need clearer exposition.

- Clarity: generally well written and structured, but the penalty definition needs tighter notation and Algorithm 2 needs more detail (stopping criteria, tie-breaking, multiple splits).

- Originality: intrinsic group-additive notion and RKHS covering-number-based penalty for partition selection appear novel and combine into meaningful consistency results.

- Significance: promising for interpretable nonparametric modeling with small-group interactions if made scalable and benchmarked, but empirical support is currently insufficient.

- Essential suggestions: clarify and consistently present the structural penalty (α dependence, constants, kernel assumptions); explicitly state all theorem assumptions and provide tuning guidance for λ, µ; add baseline comparisons and broader empirical metrics.

- Essential suggestions: expand Algorithm 2 details, provide runtime/scalability analysis, and consider less-greedy search strategies (multiple splits/beam search).

- Optional suggestions: explore alternative penalties (AIC/MDL/HSIC surrogates), jointly select kernel bandwidths, assess robustness when true function is not group-additive, and release open-source code with additional datasets.

- Arguments for acceptance: conceptual/theoretical novelty, principled RKHS-based framework linking capacity to structure selection, and potential for interpretable structure discovery.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation, limited scalability and heuristic guarantees, and practical clarity gaps around the penalty and tuning.

- Overall recommendation: weak reject — interesting and potentially impactful theory but needs clearer penalty specification, stronger empirical comparisons, and improved algorithmic practicality for larger p.
