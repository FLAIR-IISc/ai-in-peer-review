Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies nonparametric regression with interactions through “group additive” models, sitting between univariate additive models and high-order ANOVA kernels. The main conceptual contribution is to formalize an intrinsic group additive structure: a minimal partition of predictors under which f(X) decomposes additively over groups. Building on RKHS theory, the authors propose a structure-penalized kernel estimator that jointly fits f and selects groups by penalizing a complexity measure derived from covering number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs. They prove (i) existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure, (ii) risk concentration for amiable structures versus separation for non-amiable ones, and (iii) structure selection consistency under appropriate tuning. Algorithms include exhaustive search for small p and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p. Simulations and a Boston Housing case study illustrate feasibility. The work relates to classic additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), kernel ANOVA/ESP kernels, SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu), and connects to but distinguishes itself from graphical models by targeting functional, not conditional, structure.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of a minimal “intrinsic” group structure under a natural partial order; useful conceptual advance.
- Principled structural penalty from capacity control of direct-sum RKHSs; theoretically motivated rather than ad hoc.
- Nonasymptotic bounds that distinguish amiable vs non-amiable structures and a consistency theorem for selection.
- Sensible algorithms and empirical demonstrations; visualization on real data is compelling for interpretability.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small datasets, no statistical tests, and no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., sparse additive models/SpAM, kernel ANOVA with ESP kernels, SALSA, OKGT).
- Scalability remains a concern; the backward stepwise heuristic is greedy and can miss true partitions, with no search quality guarantees or runtime analysis.
- The penalty derivation simplifies constants (e.g., converting covering number bounds to µ∑α|u|) without sensitivity analysis; the role and interpretation of α and its dependence on kernel/scale are underexplained.
- Some theory relies on assumptions (e.g., independence in Prop. 1 for strict inclusion, universality of kernels) that may not hold; robustness under correlated covariates and non-universal kernels is not studied.
- Reproducibility details (kernel choices, λ/µ/α tuning, code) are sparse.

Suggestions:
- Add comprehensive comparisons on synthetic and real benchmarks (p ≥ 50–100) against SALSA, SpAM, kernel ANOVA, and OKGT, with ablations on the penalty and search.
- Provide stronger search strategies (beam/MCMC over partitions, submodularity analysis, or relaxations) and complexity/runtime results.
- Clarify the penalty mapping from covering numbers, sensitivity to α/µ/λ, and guidance for practitioners.
- Discuss dependence/correlation in X and extend theory or experiments to that setting; release code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid and elegant theory; well-motivated penalty; interpretable modeling framework.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation and comparisons; scalability and practicality not convincingly demonstrated.
