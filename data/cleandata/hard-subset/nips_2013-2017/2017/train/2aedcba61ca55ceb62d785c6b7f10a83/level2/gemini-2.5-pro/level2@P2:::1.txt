This paper addresses the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by proposing a method to automatically identify group additive structure. Additive models are efficient but ignore variable interactions, while fully nonparametric models suffer from the curse of dimensionality. Group additive models offer a compromise by partitioning variables into groups and assuming additivity across groups, allowing for interactions within groups. The paper makes several key contributions. First, it formally defines the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique, minimal partition that describes the true underlying function, resolving the issue of non-identifiability in previous work. Second, it proposes a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this intrinsic structure and estimate the component functions. The core of this method is a novel complexity penalty for group structures, which is motivated by and derived from the covering number of the associated Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). Third, the authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that their proposed estimator for the group structure is consistent. Finally, they propose both an exhaustive search algorithm for small-scale problems and a backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on synthetic and real-world data.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions are the main strength. The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" using a partial ordering over partitions is elegant and provides a solid foundation for the problem. The derivation of the structure penalty from the capacity of RKHSs (specifically, covering numbers) is well-motivated and provides a principled alternative to ad-hoc penalties. The main theoretical result, the consistency of the structure identification (Theorem 4), is significant and lends strong support to the proposed method. The proofs, outlined in the main text and detailed in the supplement, appear sound.

The empirical validation, however, could be stronger. While the simulations show that the method can work well, they are limited to low-dimensional settings. The performance of the backward stepwise algorithm, which is crucial for practical applications, is mixed, failing to recover the true structure for one of the five models. The discussion of this failure is brief, and a deeper analysis would be beneficial. The real data application on the Boston Housing dataset is illustrative and produces interpretable results, but without ground truth, it primarily serves as a qualitative demonstration.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction. Section 2 expertly walks the reader through the problem definition, the definition of the intrinsic structure, the kernel-based estimation framework, and the derivation of the complexity penalty. The logic is easy to follow. The distinction between amiable and non-amiable structures is a clear and useful concept that aids in understanding the theoretical arguments. The algorithms are presented clearly.

There are minor points of confusion. In Section 2.3.2, the capacity measure is motivated by the bound `(ln(r/epsilon))^(p+1)`. This is then parameterized as `alpha^(p+1)`. However, the final penalty term in Equation (6) is given as `sum(alpha^|u_j|)`. The justification for dropping the "+1" in the exponent is described as a simplification but feels somewhat arbitrary and could be explained more thoroughly. Additionally, the precise assumptions for the theorems (e.g., conditions on the noise, the kernels, and the scaling of the tuning parameter µ with n) are deferred to the supplement. Including a more complete, albeit informal, summary of these conditions in the main text would improve clarity for readers.

**Originality**
The paper makes several original contributions. While the idea of group additive models has been explored before (e.g., [11]), this work is the first to systematically address the non-uniqueness of the structure by defining the intrinsic structure and providing a consistent method to identify it. The central novelty lies in the design of the structure penalty. Using RKHS capacity measures to define a penalty over a combinatorial space of variable partitions is a novel and powerful idea that connects model selection to learning theory in a compelling way. This is a significant departure from previous approaches and provides a new direction for structure learning in nonparametric models.

**Significance**
The problem addressed is of high practical and theoretical importance. The proposed method offers a flexible and data-driven tool for balancing model complexity and predictive power in high-dimensional regression. It allows researchers to discover interaction patterns that would be missed by standard additive models, without resorting to overly complex models that are difficult to estimate and interpret. If the computational challenges can be effectively managed, this approach could become a valuable tool for exploratory data analysis in many scientific fields. The theoretical framework connecting RKHS capacity to structure selection is also significant and could inspire similar approaches for other structure learning problems (e.g., in graphical models or deep learning).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** The paper provides a novel, principled, and theoretically sound (proven consistent) method for an important problem.
*   **Novelty:** The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" and the derivation of the structure penalty from RKHS covering numbers are highly original.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Potential Impact:** The method provides a powerful new tool for exploratory nonparametric regression, with the potential for wide applicability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms are either computationally infeasible (exhaustive search) or heuristic with no performance guarantees (backward stepwise). The scalability of the method to genuinely high-dimensional problems remains a major concern.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are somewhat limited. The backward algorithm's failure on one of the test cases is concerning and not fully explored. More extensive comparisons with other structure-learning methods and tests on higher-dimensional problems would strengthen the paper.
*   **Tuning Parameters:** The method introduces new tuning parameters (µ, α) whose selection adds a layer of complexity. The sensitivity to these parameters and the robustness of the validation procedure are not deeply investigated. The drop in performance for Model 1 after tuning (Table 2) is a red flag that needs more explanation.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and significance, outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a very elegant and principled framework for a challenging problem. While the computational and empirical aspects could be improved, the core contribution is strong and provides a solid foundation for future work. I recommend acceptance.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Please clarify the simplification of the penalty term from `alpha^(|u|+1)` to `alpha^|u|`.
*   A more thorough discussion of the computational challenges would be welcome. Could you suggest more sophisticated optimization strategies beyond a greedy backward search to better navigate the combinatorial search space?
*   It would be beneficial to add more analysis regarding the failure case (Model 4) in the simulation study. What properties of this model make it difficult for the greedy algorithm?
*   Please provide more insight into the concerning result for Model 1, where performance degrades after tuning parameters via validation. This could point to instability in the estimation or tuning process that warrants discussion.
