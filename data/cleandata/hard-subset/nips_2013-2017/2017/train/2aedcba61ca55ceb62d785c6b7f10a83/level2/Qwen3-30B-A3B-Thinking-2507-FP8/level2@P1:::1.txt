

This paper proposes a novel approach for identifying group additive structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors address two key limitations in previous work on group additive models: the non-uniqueness of group structures and the lack of systematic methods for structure identification. They rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, and propose a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies this structure and estimates the nonparametric functions. The method uses a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers, which they prove leads to consistent identification of the intrinsic structure. The paper develops two algorithms for practical implementation (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) and demonstrates effectiveness through simulations and a real data application on the Boston Housing dataset.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure resolves the non-uniqueness issue that has plagued previous work. Second, the proposed complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is theoretically well-motivated and provides a principled way to penalize group structures. Third, the consistency proof (Theorem 4) is rigorous and addresses important theoretical questions about the proposed method. The experimental results demonstrate that the method successfully identifies true group structures in various simulation settings and provides interpretable results on real data.

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical development is clear and progresses logically from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation. The connection to related work (particularly functional ANOVA models and graphical models) is well-articulated, and the distinction between group additive structure identification and conditional dependence structure is particularly insightful. The real data application is well-chosen and the visualizations of the estimated functions provide meaningful insights about the data.

Some minor weaknesses should be addressed. The connection to previous work on group additive models (specifically [11]) could be more thoroughly discussed in the introduction. While the paper references [11], it doesn't explicitly state how this work extends or differs from their approach beyond the two specific issues mentioned. Additionally, the computational complexity of the exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is not fully explored. The paper mentions that it becomes computationally expensive for large p, but doesn't provide concrete complexity analysis or discuss the trade-offs between the two algorithms in terms of computational cost versus accuracy.

The theoretical results are strong, but the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion about the choice of tuning parameters. The paper states that µ and α can be selected via validation, but doesn't provide guidance on how to choose appropriate ranges for these parameters in practice. The simulation results in Table 2 show that the identification success rate varies with (µ, α), but the paper doesn't explain why certain parameter values work better than others for different models.

The real data application on Boston Housing is compelling, but the paper could provide more insight into why the identified group structure makes sense for the housing market. For example, the grouping {(1, 6), (2, 11), ...} could be interpreted in terms of the actual variables (crime rate, average rooms per dwelling, etc.) to provide more meaningful context for the results.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The problem is well-motivated, the method is theoretically sound, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper advances the state of the art in high-dimensional nonparametric regression by providing a principled method for identifying group structures that balances model complexity with interpretability.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses fundamental problems in statistical learning with novel theoretical contributions and practical algorithms. It represents a meaningful advancement over previous work on additive models and group additive structures.

Strengths: Clear problem definition, rigorous theoretical foundation, well-justified complexity measure, strong experimental validation, practical algorithms, and meaningful real-world application.

Weaknesses: Some minor gaps in connecting to prior work, limited discussion of tuning parameter selection, and opportunity to provide more interpretation of the real data results.
