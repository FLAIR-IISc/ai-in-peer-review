This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure in nonparametric regression, a generalization of the standard additive model that allows for interactions between variables within groups. The core contribution lies in rigorously defining the “intrinsic” group additive structure, and developing a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the underlying functions. A complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs is derived and incorporated into the penalty term. The theoretical consistency of the proposed method is established, and its effectiveness is demonstrated through simulations and a real data application.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presenting the limitations of standard additive models and the advantages of a group-wise approach. The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure is a valuable contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for identifying the most concise and interpretable model. The derivation of the complexity measure, while technical, is clearly presented and justified. The theoretical results regarding consistency are a strong point, assuring the asymptotic correctness of the method. The experimental validation, including both simulations and a real data example, adds credibility to the claims. The backward stepwise algorithm provides a practical approach for handling higher dimensional data.

However, several aspects could be improved. While the paper mentions connections to graphical models, the discussion feels somewhat cursory. A more detailed comparison, perhaps highlighting scenarios where GASI is advantageous over graphical model-based approaches, would be beneficial. The simulations, while comprehensive, primarily focus on scenarios where the group structure is relatively clear. Exploring more complex scenarios – overlapping variable importance across groups, varying degrees of interaction strength – would strengthen the evaluation. The impact of the choice of kernel function on the performance of the method isn’t thoroughly investigated; sensitivity analysis regarding kernel parameters is missing. The discussion of the tuning parameters (λ, µ, α) is limited. A more systematic approach to tuning these parameters, beyond the grid search with validation, could be explored, perhaps using cross-validation strategies. The computational complexity of the exhaustive search algorithm is mentioned but not quantified. Providing a more detailed analysis of the computational cost, particularly as the number of variables increases, would be helpful. The real data analysis is limited in its interpretation. While the identified groups are presented, deeper insights into the meaning of these groups within the context of the Boston Housing dataset would increase the impact.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression. The consistency results provide a strong guarantee of performance, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the method. The introduction of the intrinsic group additive structure is a valuable contribution.

Arguments against acceptance: The simulations could be more comprehensive, exploring a wider range of scenarios. The discussion on computational complexity and parameter tuning is limited. The connection to related work (e.g., graphical models) could be explored in greater detail.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of nonparametric regression. While there are areas for improvement, the core ideas and theoretical results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. A revision addressing these points is recommended.
