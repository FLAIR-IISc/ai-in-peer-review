- Problem: Identifies group additive structure in nonparametric regression, generalizing additive models to allow interactions within groups.
- Core contribution: Rigorously defines the “intrinsic” group additive structure and proposes a structure-penalized kernel method to identify structure and estimate functions.
- Complexity measure: Derives a penalty based on covering numbers of RKHSs and incorporates it into the method.
- Theory: Proves consistency, providing asymptotic guarantees for the proposed estimator.
- Empirical validation: Shows effectiveness via simulations and a Boston Housing real-data example.
- Practical algorithm: Proposes a backward stepwise algorithm to handle higher-dimensional problems.
- Motivation and clarity: Well-motivated and clearly explains limitations of standard additive models and benefits of group-wise modeling.
- Interpretability: The intrinsic group additive structure offers a theoretical basis for concise, interpretable models.
- Technical clarity: The complex derivation of the RKHS-based complexity measure is clearly presented and justified.
- Related work gap: Connections to graphical models are mentioned but treated cursorily; needs deeper comparison and discussion of relative advantages.
- Simulation scope: Simulations focus on clear group structures; should include harder cases (overlapping variable importance, varying interaction strengths).
- Kernel sensitivity: Impact of kernel choice and kernel-parameter sensitivity analysis is missing.
- Tuning strategy: Discussion of tuning parameters (λ, µ, α) is limited; more systematic approaches (e.g., cross-validation) should be explored.
- Computational analysis: Computational complexity of exhaustive search is noted but not quantified; needs detailed cost analysis as dimensionality grows.
- Real-data interpretation: Real-data results lack deeper substantive interpretation of the identified groups in the Boston Housing context.
- Overall verdict: Strong, theoretically sound contribution; recommend revision to address simulation breadth, computational/tuning analysis, and related-work comparisons.
