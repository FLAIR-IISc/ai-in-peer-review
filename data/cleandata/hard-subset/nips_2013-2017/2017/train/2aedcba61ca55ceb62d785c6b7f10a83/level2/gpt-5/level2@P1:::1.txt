Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression with a group additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables into groups, allowing interactions within groups but not across groups. The authors formalize a partial order over groupings, define amiable group structures (those that can represent f), and prove the existence and uniqueness of a minimal element in this poset, termed the intrinsic group additive structure G*. They propose to simultaneously identify G* and estimate f via a structure-penalized kernel method over the direct-sum RKHS HG, adding a penalty on the structural complexity C(G) derived from covering number bounds. They provide generalization bounds based on covering numbers, show that non-amiable structures have a positive risk gap, and prove selection consistency for G under appropriate tuning. They implement an exhaustive search (small p) and a backward stepwise algorithm (larger p), and present simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on additive models and functional ANOVA (Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu), SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu), and OKGT (Pan et al.). It also connects to RKHS capacity control (Smola & Schölkopf; Steinwart & Christmann; Zhou; Kühn), and briefly contrasts with graphical models. Compared to functional ANOVA or ESP kernels, the paper targets a middle ground: parsimonious groupings with within-group interactions. Compared to SALSA and hierarchical kernel/ANOVA approaches, the novelty centers on (i) an explicit notion of intrinsic grouping with a lattice-theoretic minimal element, and (ii) a structure penalty derived from RKHS covering numbers for direct sums.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of group additive structures, including a partial order and the intrinsic minimal structure. This is a useful conceptual contribution.
- The structure-penalized objective blending empirical loss, RKHS norm, and a principled structural complexity term derived from covering numbers is interesting and grounded in learning theory.
- Consistency analysis: the decomposition into amiable vs. non-amiable structures, uniform bounds across amiable structures, and a positive risk gap for non-amiable structures provide an intelligible route to selection consistency.
- Practical algorithms for small and moderate p, with simulations showing frequent recovery of the true structure, and an interpretable real-data example.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Dependence on α and ε in the penalty: The complexity measure C(G) transitions from ln N ≤ C(ln(r/ε))p+1 to C(G) ∝ ∑u α(ε)|u|+1, then “simplifies” to µ∑u α|u|. The practical choice of ε (hence α), and the justification for dropping the “+1” in the exponent, are unclear. As written, the penalty depends on an arbitrary resolution ε and may bias too strongly toward small groups. Please clarify the exact penalty used in theory and practice, and provide guidance or data-dependent calibration for α and µ (e.g., scaling with n, p).
- Assumptions on X and universality: Proposition 1’s strict inclusion requires independence of covariates. Many real datasets have correlated predictors; how do your results (especially selection consistency) extend beyond independence? More broadly, selection consistency seems to rely on universal kernels and on HG being rich enough to approximate f within amiable structures; explicit assumptions on kernels (bandwidths) and data distributions should be stated with theorems.
- Capacity bound applicability: The upper bound ln N ≤ Ck,p(ln r/ε)p+1 is for convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transforms. The paper then aggregates across groups. A precise statement of conditions ensuring Proposition 3 and the resulting penalty form for general Gaussian/Laplace kernels over variable subsets would strengthen the argument. A comparison with alternative data-dependent capacities (e.g., localized Rademacher complexities) could also be informative.
- Algorithmic scalability and guarantees: The backward stepwise procedure is greedy and, as noted, can miss the true structure (Model 4). No approximation guarantee or consistency under stepwise is provided. Some heuristic enhancements (multi-start, beam search, swap moves) or a complexity analysis would help, as would a discussion of when stepwise is expected to work.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Experiments emphasize recovery frequency but lack comparisons against strong baselines that also model interactions sparsely, e.g., functional ANOVA kernels, hierarchical kernel learning (e.g., Bach, NIPS’09), SALSA (ICML’16), and sparse additive models (Ravikumar et al., NIPS’07). Predictive accuracy, structure recovery, and runtime comparisons would make the case for significance more compelling. Kernel choices, bandwidths, and λ-tuning should be reported; sensitivity analyses to µ, α, λ would be helpful.
- Notational and presentation issues: Some notation is overloaded (p vs. |u| in capacity exponents), and the transition from ln N bounds to α-based penalties is informal. Minor typos and occasional ambiguity in definitions could be cleaned up to improve readability.

Quality
- The theoretical direction is promising, and several results are sound in spirit. However, some steps (penalty derivation, independence assumptions, tuning prescriptions) need to be tightened to make the guarantees fully convincing and practically actionable.

Clarity
- Generally readable, with a good high-level flow. Clarifying the capacity-to-penalty mapping, listing assumptions with each theorem, and expanding the algorithm descriptions would improve clarity.

Originality
- The notion of an intrinsic group additive structure and its identification via a structure penalty derived from RKHS capacity is novel to my knowledge. The connection to covering numbers for direct sums is a nice angle.

Significance
- Potentially useful for interpretable regression with moderate-dimensional interactions. Current empirical evidence is suggestive but not yet compelling without comparisons and stronger large-p performance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise, data-driven procedure for selecting µ and α (and possibly ε), with theoretical scaling in n and p (e.g., µn rates ensuring consistency).
- Clarify assumptions (covariate dependence, kernel universality/bandwidths) for Theorems 2–4; extend results to correlated X if possible.
- Strengthen Prop. 3 with a full proof and conditions; consider data-dependent complexities (Rademacher/localized bounds) for sharper penalties.
- Enhance the large-p search with richer moves and report computational complexity and ablations.
- Add comprehensive empirical comparisons to functional ANOVA kernels, hierarchical/multiple kernel learning, SALSA, and sparse additive models; report both structure recovery and predictive performance.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ, µ, α and kernel hyperparameters.

Overall evaluation
A thoughtful and potentially impactful contribution with a solid conceptual core and promising theory. I recommend acceptance only after clarifying the penalty construction/tuning and strengthening the empirical evaluation and large-p algorithmic story; as it stands, I lean toward a borderline/weak reject for NIPS due to these gaps, but I encourage the authors to address them, as the direction is worthwhile.
