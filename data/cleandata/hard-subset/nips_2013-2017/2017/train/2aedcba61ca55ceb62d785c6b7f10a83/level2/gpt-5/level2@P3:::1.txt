Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression with interactions by introducing and formalizing a group additive model in which variables interact within small groups but not across groups. The main conceptual contribution is the definition of an intrinsic group additive structure: the unique minimal partition of variables (under a partial order on partitions) for which the regression function is group-additive (amiable). The paper proposes an RKHS-based estimator that jointly estimates the regression function and identifies the group partition via a structural penalty derived from covering-number capacity bounds of direct-sum RKHSs. Consistency is shown: with appropriate regularization, the selected partition converges in probability to the intrinsic group structure. Algorithms are provided for small p (exhaustive search with validation) and for moderate p (a backward stepwise splitting heuristic). Simulations and a Boston Housing case study illustrate feasibility.

The work connects to additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA (Gu), additive approximations with bounded interaction order such as SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), and the ESP-kernel interaction models. It is closely related to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15), but advances the theory by formalizing the intrinsic partition and providing a selection-consistent penalization based on RKHS capacity rather than relying on exploratory transformations. The paper also relates conceptually to graphical models, noting that the proposed approach targets function-structure directly rather than full conditional dependence.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of group-additive structure with a rigorous partial order and proof of existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic (minimal) partition.
- Novel structural penalty derived from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs; provides a principled path to selection consistency.
- Separation of amiable vs non-amiable structures with risk bounds that quantify their distinguishability; consistency theorem under reasonable fixed-p conditions.
- Flexible kernel framework; use of universal kernels supports approximation to L2 and structure recovery.
- Practical algorithms presented, with demonstrations that correct structures can be recovered in simulations; interpretable groups in the Boston Housing example.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small p, no comparisons to competitive baselines such as GAM/SpAM, functional ANOVA, SALSA, OKGT, or sparse/hierarchical interaction selection methods; limited metrics beyond frequency of recovery.
- Scalability and search: exhaustive search is infeasible beyond small p; the backward stepwise heuristic lacks guarantees and struggles in at least one setting (Model 4). No complexity/runtime analysis or results for larger p (e.g., 50–100).
- Clarity and calibration of the penalty: notation for the capacity penalty alternates between α|u| and α|u|+1 and treats α (which depends on ln(r/ε)) as a tunable integer, which obscures the link to the theory. Constants and kernel bandwidth dependence in the covering-number bounds are not discussed.
- Assumptions and scope: theoretical consistency appears to require fixed p and appropriate choices of λn, µn; growth regimes (p→∞), correlated covariates, and kernel hyperparameter selection are not analyzed. Some results (e.g., strict inclusion in Proposition 1) depend on independence assumptions that are not revisited later.
- Limited guidance on practical tuning of λ, µ, and kernel parameters, and on sensitivity to these choices.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound at a high level, with clear statements and a plausible proof strategy invoking covering-number bounds for RKHSs and direct-sum constructions. Some technical details and conditions (rates/choices for µ and λ, constants in bounds, kernel assumptions) need clearer exposition to assess applicability. The empirical section supports feasibility but does not yet establish superiority or robustness.

Clarity
Generally well written with a helpful motivating example and structured presentation. However, the definition and use of the penalty require tighter notation and clearer explanation, including explicit dependence on ε, r, and kernel parameters, and a consistent final form used in practice. Algorithm 2 would benefit from more detail (stopping criteria, tie-breaking, multiple splits per iteration).

Originality
The formal notion of intrinsic group additive structure and the use of RKHS covering-number-based penalties for selecting group partitions appear novel. The combination of these ideas with consistency guarantees is a meaningful contribution beyond prior OKGT and additive/interaction frameworks.

Significance
If made practical at scale and benchmarked against strong baselines, the approach could become a useful tool for interpretable nonparametric modeling with interactions in small groups. As it stands, the significance is promising but under-demonstrated empirically.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential
- Clarify the structural penalty:
  - Provide a consistent, unambiguous form; reconcile α|u| vs α|u|+1; explain α’s dependence on ln(r/ε), how ε is chosen, and how kernel bandwidths affect the bound.
  - Discuss the constants and assumptions (e.g., convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transforms) and their practical implications.
- State all assumptions for Theorems 2–4 explicitly (fixed p, boundedness/sub-Gaussian noise, independence/correlation of X, choice/rate of λn and µn) and provide guidance on selecting λ, µ in practice.
- Add baseline comparisons on synthetic and real data (GAM/SpAM, functional ANOVA, SALSA, OKGT, sparse interaction models), reporting both structure recovery and prediction accuracy across varying p, group sizes, correlation, and noise.
- Expand Algorithm 2 details and provide a runtime/scalability analysis; consider multiple-split steps or beam search to reduce greediness.

Optional
- Explore alternative penalties (e.g., information criteria, MDL, HSIC-based surrogates) and integrate kernel bandwidth selection jointly with structure selection.
- Evaluate robustness when the true function is not group-additive; report approximation quality.
- Provide an open-source implementation and additional real datasets.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptual and theoretical novelty with a clean minimality notion and consistency guarantees.
- Sound RKHS-based framework with a principled link between model capacity and structure selection.
- Potential for interpretable structure discovery with flexible nonparametrics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of comparisons to established baselines.
- Limited scalability and no guarantees for the proposed heuristic search.
- Clarity gaps in the practical form and tuning of the penalty.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit from clearer penalty specification and stronger, comparative empirical evidence, along with improved algorithmic practicality for larger p.
