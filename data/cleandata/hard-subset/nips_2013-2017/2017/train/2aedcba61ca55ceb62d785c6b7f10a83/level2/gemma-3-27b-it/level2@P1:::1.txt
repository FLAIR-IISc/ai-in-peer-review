This paper tackles the problem of identifying the group additive structure in nonparametric regression, a generalization of the standard additive model that allows for interactions between variables within groups. The authors address two key issues: the non-uniqueness of group structures and the lack of a systematic approach for their identification. They propose a structure-penalized kernel method, deriving a novel complexity measure for group additive structures and proving consistency of their method in identifying the intrinsic group additive structure. The work is well-motivated, builds upon existing literature (e.g., [11], [4], [6]), and includes both theoretical results and empirical validation.

**Strengths:**

* **Well-defined Problem:** The paper clearly defines the problem of group additive structure identification and the concept of an “intrinsic” group structure, providing a solid foundation for their approach.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The derivation of a complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs is a significant contribution.  This provides a principled way to penalize model complexity and encourage simpler structures.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The consistency proof for identifying the intrinsic group structure is a strong theoretical result, demonstrating the method's reliability under appropriate conditions.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough evaluation of their method, including simulations with different model settings and a real-data application. The inclusion of both an exhaustive search and a backward stepwise algorithm is beneficial.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The discussion connecting their work to graphical models ([2], [7], [9]) is insightful and helps position the contribution within the broader field.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the technical details accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the backward stepwise algorithm is introduced to address the computational cost of exhaustive search, the paper doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of the algorithm's efficiency or scalability to very high-dimensional data.  It would be helpful to include computational complexity analysis or benchmarks.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The method relies on tuning parameters (λ, µ, α) which can be challenging in practice. The authors mention using a validation set, but a more detailed discussion of parameter selection strategies would be useful. The connection between these parameters and the theoretical results could be clarified.
* **Kernel Choice:** The performance of the method is sensitive to the choice of kernel. The paper mentions using universal kernels, but does not explore the impact of different kernel choices or provide guidance on selecting an appropriate kernel for a given dataset.
* **Limited Real-Data Analysis:** The real-data analysis, while illustrative, is relatively limited.  Applying the method to more complex datasets and comparing its performance to other relevant methods (e.g., graphical model-based approaches) would strengthen the results.
* **Complexity Measure Intuition:** While the mathematical formulation of the complexity measure is clear, the intuition behind it could be further elaborated. A more intuitive explanation of why covering numbers capture the “complexity” of a group structure would be helpful.
* **Stepwise Algorithm Performance:** The results for Model 4 in the simulation section using the stepwise algorithm are described as "not satisfying." This warrants further investigation and discussion. Why does the stepwise approach fail in this specific scenario?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The problem is well-defined, the proposed method is theoretically sound, and the empirical results are promising.  While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper’s focus on identifying the *intrinsic* group structure is a novel and important aspect.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with consistency guarantees.
* Novel and well-motivated approach to group additive structure identification.
* Comprehensive experimental evaluation.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.
* Significant contribution to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost could be a limitation for very high-dimensional data.
* Parameter tuning can be challenging.
* Limited exploration of kernel choices.
* Real-data analysis could be more extensive.



**Rating:**  Strong Accept (7/10)

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the backward stepwise algorithm and explore techniques for improving its scalability.
* Discuss parameter selection strategies in more detail and explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of parameters.
* Investigate the impact of different kernel choices on the performance of the method.
* Apply the method to more complex real-world datasets and compare its performance to other relevant approaches.
* Provide a more intuitive explanation of the complexity measure and its connection to the theoretical results.
* Investigate why the stepwise algorithm performs poorly on Model 4 and suggest potential remedies.
