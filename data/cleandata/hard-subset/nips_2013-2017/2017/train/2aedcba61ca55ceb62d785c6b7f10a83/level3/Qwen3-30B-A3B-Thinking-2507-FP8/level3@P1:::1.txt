

This paper proposes a novel approach for high-dimensional nonparametric regression by introducing the concept of "group additive structure" and developing methods to identify the intrinsic group structure. The authors define a minimal group additive structure that captures all relevant interactions while maintaining estimation efficiency, addressing limitations of both standard additive models (which ignore interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from combinatorial explosion). The key theoretical contribution is a complexity measure for group structures derived from RKHS covering numbers, which is used as a penalty term in a structure-penalized kernel method. The paper proves consistency of the proposed method under certain conditions and develops two algorithms for structure identification: an exhaustive search approach and a backward stepwise algorithm.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by formally defining the intrinsic group additive structure and establishing its uniqueness. The authors rigorously prove that the proposed estimator is consistent for identifying this structure, which addresses a fundamental gap in the literature regarding the non-uniqueness of group additive structures. The complexity measure derived from covering numbers provides a principled way to penalize overly complex group structures, and the theoretical analysis is technically sound. The paper also connects to graphical models but clearly distinguishes their approach from conditional dependence modeling.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the related work coverage is inadequate. The paper fails to properly engage with structured sparsity literature (e.g., group Lasso, sparse additive models), which directly addresses feature grouping in regression. The connection to graphical models is superficial, and the paper misses key references on functional ANOVA and interaction modeling (e.g., [4], [6] are cited but not properly contrasted with the proposed approach). The authors do not acknowledge how their complexity measure relates to other existing complexity measures in high-dimensional statistics.

The experimental evaluation is notably thin. The authors present results on only five synthetic models and the Boston Housing dataset, with no comparison to alternative methods for group structure identification. The lack of baselines makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed method. For instance, how does it compare to group Lasso-based approaches or other structure learning methods? The paper also fails to report computational complexity or scalability analysis, despite proposing a stepwise algorithm for large-scale problems.

The optimization approach is problematic. The authors acknowledge that their complexity measure is not easily optimizable and resort to computationally expensive exhaustive search (which scales exponentially with the number of features) or a greedy backward stepwise algorithm. The paper does not explore more efficient optimization strategies or provide evidence that the greedy approach reliably identifies the correct structure. The paper mentions "alternative complexity measures" but provides no substantive analysis or comparison of these alternatives.

The paper's contribution is somewhat incremental. While the formal definition of intrinsic group additive structure is valuable, the proposed method does not significantly advance beyond the OKGT method [11] that the authors cite as a starting point. The authors note that [11] did not address non-uniqueness or structure identification, but they don't convincingly demonstrate how their approach overcomes these limitations in a way that is substantially better than existing alternatives.

Regarding quality, the theoretical results are sound but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims. The paper is well-written but the experimental section lacks the necessary detail to assess the method's practical utility. The clarity of the theoretical arguments is good, but the connection to practical implementation is weak.

The significance of the work is limited by the narrow scope of the evaluation and the lack of comparison to relevant methods. While the group additive model concept is promising, the paper does not demonstrate that the proposed method provides a substantial improvement over existing techniques for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The application to Boston Housing is interesting but does not provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the problem of group structure identification in nonparametric regression. However, the weak related work coverage, inadequate experimental evaluation, and inefficient optimization approach significantly limit its impact. The authors should substantially strengthen the discussion of related work, provide more comprehensive experiments with appropriate baselines, and explore more efficient optimization strategies for the complexity measure. The current version falls short of the high standards expected for NIPS in terms of both theoretical contribution and empirical validation. While the problem is well-motivated, the proposed solution does not convincingly advance the state of the art. I recommend rejection.
