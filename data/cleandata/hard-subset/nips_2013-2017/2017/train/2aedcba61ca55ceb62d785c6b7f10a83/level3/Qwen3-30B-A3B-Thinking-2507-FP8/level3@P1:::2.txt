

This paper addresses a significant gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression by introducing a method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure, where the relationship between response and predictors is expressed as a sum of nonparametric functions over disjoint groups of predictors. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group partition that can represent the true underlying function, resolving the non-uniqueness issue present in previous group additive models (Pan et al., 2011). Their key innovation is a novel complexity measure for group structures derived from covering numbers of direct sum RKHSs, which they incorporate into a penalized kernel regression framework to simultaneously identify the group structure and estimate the nonparametric functions.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with Theorems 2-4 establishing that the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases. This consistency result is particularly valuable as it addresses the fundamental challenge of learning the group structure from data. The method is well-motivated and clearly distinguished from related work on functional ANOVA models and graphical models, with the authors correctly noting that their focus on group additive structure rather than conditional dependence structure represents a meaningful differentiator.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates the method's practical utility. The simulation studies show high recovery rates for the true group structures across diverse model settings, with the Boston Housing data application producing interpretable results that reveal meaningful patterns in the relationships between predictors and housing values. The two proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) provide practical solutions to the combinatorial optimization problem, with the stepwise approach offering a scalable solution for larger feature sets.

The paper's main strengths include its rigorous theoretical foundation, clear problem formulation, and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors effectively bridge theory and practice, providing both a principled approach to structure identification and practical algorithms for implementation. The method advances the state of the art by offering a computationally feasible way to incorporate structured interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that would benefit from revision. The writing contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "on RHS" instead of "on the RHS" on Line 145, "by compare" instead of "by comparing" on Line 251) that undermine the professionalism of the presentation. The technical detail on "almost surely" (Line 169) requires clarification, as it appears to refer to asymptotic behavior rather than probabilistic almost sure convergence. Equation (3) is missing a summation over i, which is a critical technical error. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why covering numbers provide a suitable complexity measure for group structures.

The most significant limitation is the computational complexity of the optimization problem, which becomes challenging when the number of predictors is large. While the backward stepwise algorithm partially addresses this, the authors acknowledge this remains a challenge, particularly for very high-dimensional problems. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of computational scalability and potential extensions for massive feature sets.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of high-dimensional nonparametric statistics. It provides a theoretically sound and practically useful approach to a fundamental problem, with clear advantages over both simple additive models (which ignore interactions) and full interaction models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). The method offers a compelling middle ground that maintains interpretability while accommodating structured interactions.

The writing would benefit from careful proofreading to address the numerous typos and grammatical issues. The technical details require more precise formulation, particularly regarding the "almost surely" usage and the missing summation in Equation (3). Despite these issues, the paper presents a strong theoretical foundation with promising practical applications, making it suitable for publication at NIPS.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are rigorous, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the method has clear practical value. The minor writing and technical issues are fixable with revision, and the paper advances the state of the art in high-dimensional nonparametric regression by providing a principled approach to learning structured interactions.
