This paper addresses the problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions by leveraging the concept of group additivity, where predictors are partitioned into groups and interactions are assumed to occur within groups but not across them. The central challenge lies in identifying the underlying group structure from the data, a task tackled by the authors with a novel structure-penalized kernel method. The key innovation is a complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), which is used as a penalty term in the optimization objective.  The paper presents theoretical results guaranteeing consistency of the estimated group structure and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach through simulations and a real-data application.

The work builds upon previous efforts to address the curse of dimensionality in nonparametric regression, such as additive models [5] and functional ANOVA [4, 13]. While these methods offer improvements over standard nonparametric regression, they either neglect interactions entirely (additive models) or quickly become computationally intractable as the number of predictors increases (functional ANOVA, [6]).  The group additive model [11] provides a middle ground, but prior work lacked a systematic approach for identifying the optimal group structure. This paper directly addresses this gap, providing a principled way to discover the intrinsic group additive structure, which the authors rigorously define as the minimal structure among all correct ones. The connection to graphical models [2, 7, 9] is noted, but the authors highlight that their approach directly targets the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The theoretical foundation is strong, with proofs of consistency provided in the supplementary material. The introduction of a complexity measure based on covering numbers is a significant contribution, offering a theoretically grounded way to penalize complex group structures. The empirical evaluation, while not exhaustive, supports the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying the true group structure in simulated data and demonstrates its applicability to a real-world dataset. The provision of two algorithms – exhaustive search and a stepwise approach – provides practical tools for implementation. The clarity of the exposition is generally good, making the core ideas accessible.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. A significant limitation is the computational cost of the optimization, particularly when dealing with a large number of predictor variables. While the stepwise algorithm attempts to mitigate this, its greedy nature may lead to suboptimal solutions, as acknowledged in the discussion. The writing contains a noticeable number of typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies, which detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.  While the theoretical results are compelling, the assumptions required for their validity should be more carefully discussed. The paper also lacks a more intuitive explanation of what constitutes an “interaction” in the context of group additivity, beyond the statement that predictors within a group interact, and those outside do not. It would be helpful to have a simple characterization of interaction between two variables that doesn't require invoking the full model in Equation (1). Furthermore, the use of "almost surely" in Line 169 is unclear and could be refined; is this intended to mean as n approaches infinity? Equation (3) also appears to be missing a summation over *i*.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with proofs of consistency.
*   Novel and principled approach to group structure identification.
*   Well-motivated complexity measure based on covering numbers.
*   Empirical results support the effectiveness of the method.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost can be prohibitive for large datasets.
*   Numerous typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies.
*   Limited discussion of assumptions and limitations.
*   Lack of a more intuitive explanation of interactions.



**Overall, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field.  While the weaknesses outlined above should be addressed, the strengths of the work – particularly the theoretical grounding and the novel complexity measure – outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the typos, clarify the assumptions, and provide a more intuitive explanation of interactions.**  I suggest the authors consider replacing "convolutional kernel" (Line 205) with "translation invariant kernel".
