## Review of “Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression”

This paper tackles the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by revisiting the group additive model, aiming to identify inherent feature groupings that capture correlations missed by standard additive models. The authors propose a structure-penalized kernel method, introducing a novel complexity measure for group additive structures derived from covering number theory. While the core idea of explicitly modeling feature interactions via grouping is appealing and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from several limitations in its current form.

The primary strength lies in the formal treatment of the group additive structure and the attempt to quantify its complexity. The derived complexity measure, favoring many small groups, is a theoretically interesting contribution. However, the practical utility is hampered by the intractability of optimizing this measure directly. The authors resort to exhaustive search (scaling poorly) or a greedy forward-selection approach, which feels like a missed opportunity given the theoretical work. Furthermore, the paper only briefly mentions alternative complexity measures without substantive exploration of their potential benefits or drawbacks. 

A significant weakness is the limited coverage of related work. The paper overlooks crucial connections to established fields like structured sparsity, graphical models (and Bayesian networks), and the broader feature selection literature. These areas offer alternative approaches to modeling feature interactions and could provide valuable insights for improving the proposed method. The experimental evaluation is also thin, relying on small synthetic datasets and the Boston Housing dataset without any baseline comparisons to existing methods. This makes it difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed approach. 

The post-rebuttal clarifies some points, but the core issues regarding insufficient experimentation and a weak related work section remain. While the theoretical foundation is interesting, the lack of empirical validation and connection to existing literature significantly limit the paper’s impact. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Formal treatment of group additive structure.
*   Novel complexity measure based on covering number theory.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Intractable complexity measure optimization.
*   Poor coverage of related work.
*   Limited and uncomparative experimental evaluation.
*   Scalability concerns with exhaustive search.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection**. The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, but its practical limitations and lack of thorough evaluation outweigh its strengths. A stronger connection to existing literature, more efficient optimization techniques, and a more comprehensive experimental analysis are needed to justify publication at a venue like NIPS.
