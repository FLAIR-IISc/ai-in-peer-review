This paper proposes a group additive model for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, which generalizes the traditional additive model by allowing interactions between predictor variables. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure and develop a structure-penalized kernel method for identifying this structure and estimating nonparametric functions. The method utilizes a novel complexity measure derived from the covering number of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs).

The main idea of the paper is to group features to model correlations ignored by standard additive models. The authors provide a formal treatment and propose ad hoc algorithms for finding optimal feature groupings. The theoretical contribution of the paper is the definition of a complexity measure that favors many small groups, which is derived as an upper bound on the covering number.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its theoretical foundation. However, the reviewer has some concerns regarding the optimizability of the complexity measure, the coverage of related work, and the experimental evaluation.

One of the main concerns is that the complexity measure is not easily optimizable, and the authors resort to exhaustive search or a greedy forward-selection approach. This may limit the scalability of the method to high-dimensional problems. The reviewer would have liked to see more efficient optimization methods or alternative measures that could be optimized more efficiently.

Another concern is the poor coverage of related work. The reviewer notes that the paper is missing links to structured sparsity, graphical models/Bayesian networks, and feature selection literature. The authors should provide a more comprehensive review of related work and discuss how their method relates to existing approaches.

The experimental evaluation is also thin, with only small synthetic cases and the Boston Housing dataset. The reviewer would have liked to see more extensive experiments, including comparisons to other methods and baselines. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

In the rebuttal, the authors address some of the concerns, but the reviewer still urges stronger related-work treatment and more extensive experimental evaluation.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the reviewer rates it as follows:

* Quality: 7/10
* Clarity: 8/10
* Originality: 8/10
* Significance: 7/10

The reviewer recommends accepting the paper but suggests that the authors should address the concerns regarding related work and experimental evaluation in the final version.
