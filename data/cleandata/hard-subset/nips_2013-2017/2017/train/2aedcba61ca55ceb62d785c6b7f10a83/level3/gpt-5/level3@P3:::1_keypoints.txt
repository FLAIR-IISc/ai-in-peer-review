- Paper studies group additive kernel regression: features partitioned into groups, learn multivariate nonlinear function per group, sum across groups; proposes structure-penalized RKHS estimator and two search procedures, with consistency guarantees.

- Positions work between classical additive models and full-interaction models (SS-ANOVA, SALSA/ESP kernels) and connects to structured sparsity, MKL, graphical models, and feature selection.

- Strength: formalizes partial order and intrinsic (minimal) group additive partition with clean existence/uniqueness result.

- Strength: principled RKHS estimator with a covering-number–derived penalty that penalizes larger groups to encourage parsimonious partitions.

- Strength: proves consistency for both prediction and structure recovery under universal kernels using capacity-control arguments.

- Strength: algorithms (exhaustive and greedy backward-splitting heuristic) are simple and reflect intended inductive bias.

- Weakness (penalty): complexity measure based on loose covering-number bounds with unknown constants and a hard-to-interpret/tune scale α, making the penalty not readily optimizable.

- Weakness (optimization): paper resorts to exhaustive search or a greedy heuristic that lacks algorithmic guarantees and may miss good partitions; no evidence of robustness.

- Weakness (theory): assumptions (fixed p, universal kernels, independence condition for strict inclusion) are strong and capacity bounds may be vacuous in practice; limited guidance on tuning λ, μ, α.

- Weakness (experiments): empirical evaluation is thin (small synthetics + Boston Housing) and lacks comparisons to key baselines (GAM/SS-ANOVA, SALSA/ESP, hierarchical MKL, OKGT, additive GPs, group-lasso on engineered features).

- Weakness (experiments): no ablation studies, runtime/scalability analysis, or stability across seeds.

- Clarity issue: derivation and role of α (from ln(r/ε)) and its setting from data are poorly explained; ambiguity whether penalty is ∑j α|uj| or ∑j α|uj|+1.

- Clarity issue: search algorithms are only sketched without specification of splitting criteria, tie-breaking, stopping conditions, or computational complexity.

- Originality: formalization of group additivity and intrinsic partition is novel, but searching over partitions with size-dependent penalties is closely related to existing structured-sparsity and hierarchical MKL work and distinctions/links are underdeveloped.

- Significance: potential to learn low-order interactions while preserving interpretability is valuable, but insufficient empirical evidence limits practical impact.

- Suggestion: expand related work to compare explicitly to SALSA, SS-ANOVA, structured sparsity/hierarchical MKL, graphical-model structure learning, SpAM, and additive GPs.

- Suggestion: include baselines (GAM, SS-ANOVA, SALSA, hierarchical MKL, OKGT, group-lasso on features) and report predictive error, group recovery accuracy, runtime, and stability.

- Suggestion: develop tractable surrogates to the covering-number penalty (concave-size penalties, group norms), consider soft partition relaxations, and use data-driven pruning (HSIC scores, graph clustering) to improve scalability and provide complexity analysis.

- Suggestion: clarify constants/choices in the penalty, analyze sensitivity to α, and relax or better justify conditions for Proposition 1’s strictness beyond independence.

- Post-rebuttal: authors clarified some definitions and tuning choices, partially alleviating α/μ concerns, but empirical and related-work issues remain.

- Arguments for acceptance: clean formalization of group additivity, theoretically grounded penalty, and consistency guarantees for structure recovery.

- Arguments against acceptance: optimization is ad hoc and non-scalable with no guarantees for greedy search; experiments insufficient and missing key related-work connections.

- Overall evaluation: Weak reject — interesting theory and modeling perspective but algorithmic and empirical components need substantial improvement for acceptance.
