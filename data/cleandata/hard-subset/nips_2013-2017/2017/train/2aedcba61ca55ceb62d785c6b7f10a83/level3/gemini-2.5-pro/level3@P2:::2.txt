This paper addresses the problem of identifying the structure of group additive models in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. Standard additive models are interpretable and avoid the curse of dimensionality but fail to capture interactions between predictor variables. This work generalizes the additive model by assuming the response function is a sum of functions, each depending on a sub-group of predictors. The key challenge, which is the focus of this paper, is to identify this unknown partition of variables from data.

The authors' main contribution is a novel structure-penalized kernel method. They first formalize the notion of an "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique, minimal partition that can represent the true function. To find this structure, they propose a penalized objective function that combines the standard kernel ridge regression loss (empirical error plus an RKHS norm penalty) with a new penalty on the complexity of the group structure itself. This structural penalty is cleverly derived from the capacity of the corresponding direct-sum RKHS, quantified by its covering number. This provides a principled way to compare different partitions. The paper proves that under suitable conditions, this method is consistent in identifying the intrinsic group structure. To solve the challenging combinatorial optimization problem over partitions, the authors propose an exhaustive search algorithm for a small number of predictors and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger problems. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on several synthetic datasets and the Boston Housing dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and challenging problem: learning interaction structure in nonparametric regression. The primary contribution—a complexity penalty for group structures derived from the covering number of the associated RKHS—is highly original and theoretically well-motivated. It provides an elegant, unified framework for simultaneously estimating the functions and learning the model structure. This work could have a substantial impact on the field by providing a new tool for building interpretable and efficient high-dimensional nonparametric models.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong. The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" brings clarity to the problem, and the consistency proof (Theorem 4) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The experimental evaluation on synthetic data, where the ground truth is known, convincingly demonstrates the method's ability to recover the correct structure.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-motivated. The introduction clearly lays out the limitations of existing models (additive, functional ANOVA) and positions the group additive model as a flexible compromise. The core idea of penalizing model structure via RKHS capacity is explained effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Scalability:** A major weakness is the computational cost of optimizing over the space of partitions. The number of partitions of p variables is given by the Bell number, which grows super-exponentially. The proposed exhaustive search is therefore only feasible for very small p. The backward stepwise algorithm for larger p is a reasonable heuristic, but it is greedy and offers no guarantee of finding the optimal structure, as the authors acknowledge in their discussion of the simulation results for Model 4. This practical limitation currently curtails the method's applicability to genuinely high-dimensional problems.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's quality is diminished by numerous typos and grammatical errors, which should be corrected in a final version. For example:
    *   Line 145: "on RHS" should be "on the RHS".
    *   Line 152: "a member of the direct sum function space defined as L2G(X) := ⊕u∈GL2u(X)." This sentence is grammatically awkward.
    *   Line 160: "induces the smallest function space which includes the model." This could be phrased more clearly.
    *   Algorithm 2, Line 1: "State with" should be "Start with".
    *   Equation (3) appears to be missing a summation over the samples `i=1` to `n` in the first term.
    *   The term "convolutional kernel" (Line 205) is non-standard in the machine learning literature; "translation-invariant kernel" is the more common term.
*   **Minor Theoretical Point:** On line 169, the statement "fG∗ = fG almost surely" is slightly confusing. Is this meant with respect to the data-generating distribution P(X), or in the context of an asymptotic limit (n → ∞)? Clarification would be helpful.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  The core of the model is that interacting variables should be in the same group. Is there a simpler, pairwise characterization of interaction that could be used to guide or initialize the search for G*? For example, could a statistical test for interaction between pairs (Xi, Xj) be used to prune the search space, making the method more scalable?
2.  The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript to correct the various typos and grammatical errors. Addressing the missing summation in Equation (3) is particularly important.
3.  Given the limitations of the greedy stepwise search, it would be valuable to discuss alternative optimization strategies. Could techniques from combinatorial optimization, such as stochastic search methods (e.g., MCMC) or more sophisticated greedy algorithms, offer better performance?

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and elegant solution to an important problem. The core idea of using RKHS covering numbers to define a structural penalty is a significant contribution. The work is supported by strong theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical results.
*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The computational scalability of the proposed search algorithms is a serious practical limitation. The manuscript also requires careful proofreading to fix several errors and typos.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant and original contribution. The theoretical foundation is sound and the central idea is elegant. While the computational challenges are substantial, they represent an opportunity for future work rather than a fatal flaw. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity issues and errors in the manuscript.
