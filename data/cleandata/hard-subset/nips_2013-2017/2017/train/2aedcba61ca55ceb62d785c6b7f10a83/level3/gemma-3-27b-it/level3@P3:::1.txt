This paper investigates high-dimensional kernel regression through a “group additive model,” aiming to capture feature correlations missed by standard additive models. The authors propose algorithms for identifying optimal feature groupings and introduce a complexity measure based on covering numbers to guide the grouping process. While the core idea is interesting and addresses a relevant problem, the paper suffers from several shortcomings in terms of theoretical development, experimental validation, and related work discussion.

The central contribution lies in formalizing the notion of an "intrinsic group additive structure" and proposing a penalized kernel method for simultaneously identifying this structure and estimating the nonparametric function. The derived complexity measure, intended to favor many small groups, is a theoretically sound attempt to regularize the grouping process. However, its practical usability is limited by the difficulty of optimization. The authors resort to exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy forward-selection approach. This is a significant weakness, as the effectiveness of the entire framework hinges on efficient structure identification. The paper acknowledges this limitation but offers little more than mentioning alternative measures exist without substantive exploration of their potential.

The theoretical results, specifically the consistency proof for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure, are valuable. However, the constant factors and practical implications of these theoretical guarantees are unclear. The conditions required for consistency seem strong and may not hold in many real-world scenarios. 

A substantial weakness is the inadequate coverage of related work. The authors briefly mention graphical models and sparse block structures but fail to fully connect their work to the extensive literature on structured sparsity, graphical models (and Bayesian networks specifically), and feature selection. For instance, there’s no discussion of lasso-type penalties promoting group sparsity or approaches from the causal inference literature that address identifying interaction effects. The connections to functional ANOVA models are also superficial, failing to acknowledge the broader context of interaction modeling. This omission leaves the reader wondering how this work distinguishes itself from existing approaches.

The experimental evaluation is also thin. The paper presents results on small synthetic datasets and the Boston Housing dataset. Critically, there are *no* baselines or comparisons to other methods. Without comparisons to standard additive models, functional ANOVA, or other relevant techniques, it is difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The synthetic experiments are limited in scope, and the real data analysis lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and model validation.

Post-rebuttal, the authors reportedly provided clarifications, but the fundamental issues regarding insufficient experimental validation and the limited discussion of related work remain. A stronger treatment of related work is crucial to contextualize the contribution and demonstrate its novelty.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem of capturing feature interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is important.
*   The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is a conceptually sound contribution.
*   The theoretical consistency result is valuable, although its practical implications require further investigation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The complexity measure is not efficiently optimizable, limiting the practical applicability of the method.
*   The related work discussion is poor and fails to adequately connect the work to existing literature.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak, lacking baselines and comprehensive comparisons.
*   The theoretical analysis lacks sufficient detail regarding practical constants and limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of being a strong contribution due to its limitations in optimization, experimental validation, and related work discussion. While the theoretical foundations are present, the practical utility and novelty of the approach are not convincingly demonstrated. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. The authors should prioritize developing more efficient optimization strategies, conducting a thorough literature review, and providing comprehensive experimental comparisons to established methods.
