- Introduces a group additive model that partitions features into groups to capture within-group interactions while keeping additivity across groups.  
- Defines an "intrinsic group additive structure" as the most parsimonious valid grouping and frames identification via penalized kernel regression.  
- Main theoretical contribution: a complexity measure for groupings derived as an upper bound on the RKHS covering number.  
- The complexity penalty naturally favors many small groups over fewer large groups.  
- Strength: deriving the complexity penalty from first principles (covering numbers) is principled and elegant.  
- Strength: formalization of the intrinsic structure provides a clear problem framing.  
- Weakness: theoretical measure is not amenable to efficient optimization, forcing exhaustive search or a simple greedy heuristic that scales poorly.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is critically limited—only small synthetic experiments and the Boston Housing dataset.  
- Weakness: no baseline comparisons or evaluations against existing methods, leaving practical utility unsubstantiated.  
- Weakness: related work survey is weak, missing connections to structured sparsity (e.g., group LASSO), graphical models, and interaction-detection literature.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors clarified points, but core concerns about inadequate experiments and superficial literature treatment remain.  
- Summary for PC — Pros: novel theoretical derivation and clear formalization; Cons: not efficiently optimizable, extremely thin empirical validation, and poor coverage of related work.
