This paper tackles the problem of group-additive nonparametric regression, a flexible modeling framework that sits between fully additive models and higher-order interaction models. The authors address a key challenge in this setting: identifying the underlying group structure from data. They propose a structure-penalized kernel method, utilizing a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The method is accompanied by theoretical guarantees of consistency and demonstrated through simulations and a real-data application.

The motivation for this work is well-articulated. While additive models are popular for high-dimensional nonparametric regression due to their efficiency and interpretability, they ignore potential interactions. Functional ANOVA and higher-order interaction models attempt to address this, but suffer from a rapid increase in the number of parameters to estimate. Group additive models offer a middle ground, and this paper focuses on the critical problem of identifying the “intrinsic” – minimal and most meaningful – group structure. This builds on the work of [11], addressing the non-identifiability and lack of a systematic identification approach present in that prior work. The connection to graphical models is also briefly discussed, appropriately noting the difference in focus (group additive structure versus conditional dependence).

The strengths of this paper are significant. The primary contribution – the complexity measure based on covering numbers – is a novel and theoretically sound approach to penalizing group structures. The theoretical analysis, including Theorems 2, 3, and 4, provides strong justification for the method’s consistency. The proposed algorithms, including the backward stepwise approach for larger datasets, offer practical solutions for implementation. The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, demonstrates the method's ability to recover the true group structure in synthetic data and provides a plausible analysis of a real dataset. The paper is generally well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical formulation and algorithmic details.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The computational complexity of the exhaustive search algorithm limits its applicability to smaller datasets. While the stepwise algorithm addresses this, its greedy nature raises concerns about potentially missing the optimal solution, as acknowledged in the discussion. The writing, while generally clear, suffers from numerous typos and grammatical errors which detract from the overall professionalism of the paper. The presentation of the empirical results could be strengthened by including quantitative measures of performance (e.g., prediction error) in addition to the qualitative assessment of group structure recovery. While the theory is strong, the assumptions required for the consistency guarantees may be restrictive in practice and could benefit from further discussion. Finally, the choice of kernel and the parameter λ are not explored in depth, and their impact on the results is not thoroughly investigated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to group structure identification.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees of consistency.
*   Practical algorithms for implementation.
*   Addresses an important problem in nonparametric regression.
*   Clear mathematical formulation and algorithmic details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational limitations of the exhaustive search algorithm.
*   Potential suboptimality of the stepwise algorithm.
*   Numerous typos and grammatical errors.
*   Limited empirical evaluation and lack of quantitative performance measures.
*   Restricted assumptions in the theoretical analysis.



**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The proposed method is theoretically sound and addresses an important practical challenge. While the writing and empirical evaluation could be improved, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe that with revisions addressing the identified concerns, this paper would be a suitable addition to NIPS.
