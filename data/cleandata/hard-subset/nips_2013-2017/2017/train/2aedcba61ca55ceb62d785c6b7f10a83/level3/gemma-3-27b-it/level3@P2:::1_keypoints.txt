- Proposes a method to identify group additive structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.  
- Defines a formal "intrinsic group additive structure" as the target for estimation.  
- Develops a structure-penalized kernel method to jointly select groups and estimate nonparametric functions.  
- Introduces a novel complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers.  
- Proves consistency results showing the method can recover the true structure under conditions.  
- Motivation is clear: group additive models balance additive simplicity and full interactions.  
- Correctly highlights that existing functional ANOVA approaches become computationally expensive in high dimensions.  
- Theoretical contributions (definition, complexity bound, consistency) are a major strength.  
- Practical optimization of the complexity measure is intractable; authors resort to exhaustive search and a greedy forward-selection heuristic.  
- The greedy algorithm lacks theoretical or empirical validation and may be suboptimal.  
- Paper fails to explore alternative, more tractable complexity measures despite mentioning related ideas.  
- Related-work discussion is limited, omitting connections to structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature-selection literature.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: limited simulations and a single real-data example (Boston Housing) with no baselines for comparison.  
- Lack of comparative experiments makes it hard to assess practical benefits versus computational cost.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a relevant problem, offers solid theoretical foundations, and provides a principled structure-selection approach.  
- Arguments for rejection: severe computational limitations, insufficient related-work integration, and inadequate empirical validation.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically interesting and sound but practically limited; needs improvements to impact.  
- Recommendation: Reject but encourage revision addressing optimization efficiency, broader related work, and stronger comparative experiments.
