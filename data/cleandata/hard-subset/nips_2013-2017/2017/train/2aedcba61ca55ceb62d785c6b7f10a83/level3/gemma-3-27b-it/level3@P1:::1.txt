This paper proposes a method for nonparametric regression in high dimensions based on a “group additive model,” aiming to balance the flexibility of fully nonparametric methods with the interpretability and efficiency of standard additive models. The core idea is to identify groups of features that interact, allowing for more complex relationships than a simple sum of individual feature effects, but avoiding the exponential growth in complexity seen in full interaction models like ANOVA. The authors rigorously define an "intrinsic group additive structure" and develop a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the underlying functions. A key contribution is a novel complexity measure for group additive structures, based on covering numbers, designed to penalize overly complex groupings.

This work builds on the well-established literature of additive models [5] and functional ANOVA models [4, 13]. The motivation for grouping features directly addresses a key limitation of traditional additive models – their inability to capture interactions. The paper acknowledges higher-order interaction models [6, 10] but argues these suffer from combinatorial explosion. The connection to the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) [11] is discussed, with the authors aiming to address the identifiability and automated structure identification issues present in that earlier work. The formulation also bears resemblance to sparse graphical models [2, 7, 9] which also seek to encode dependencies between variables, though the authors highlight a difference in scope.

The strengths of this paper lie in its formalization of the group additive structure and the derivation of a complexity measure, albeit one that appears challenging to work with in practice. The theoretical consistency results are valuable, establishing a foundation for the proposed method. The introduction of a penalty term based on the complexity of the grouping is a reasonable approach to controlling model complexity and preventing overfitting.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, the computational complexity of the proposed approach is a major concern. While the authors present two algorithms, exhaustive search scales poorly, and the stepwise approach, being greedy, is not guaranteed to find the optimal solution. More efficient optimization strategies are needed. Second, the related work section is surprisingly limited. While the authors mention some relevant papers, they miss crucial connections to a broader literature on structured sparsity, graphical models (beyond a brief mention), and feature selection techniques.  The omission of work on block-sparse methods, for example, is noticeable. Third, the experimental evaluation is weak. The synthetic experiments are relatively small-scale, and the real-data application to the Boston Housing dataset lacks a comparison to baseline methods. It is difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach without a thorough empirical evaluation. Fourth, the complexity measure, while theoretically grounded, appears difficult to interpret and may not perfectly capture the desired trade-off between model complexity and goodness-of-fit. Finally, the discussion around alternative complexity measures seems cursory; the authors mention other options but do not delve into their potential advantages or disadvantages.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   The formalization of the group additive structure and the derivation of a complexity measure are solid theoretical contributions.
*   The consistency results provide a theoretical guarantee for the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the algorithms is a significant limitation.
*   The related work section is incomplete and misses important connections to other fields.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak and lacks comparisons to baseline methods.
*   The complexity measure is difficult to interpret and may not be optimal.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile attempt to address the challenges of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by incorporating a notion of feature grouping. However, the weaknesses, particularly the limited experimental evaluation and the lack of thorough engagement with related work, outweigh the strengths. I recommend **rejection** with the encouragement of a major revision that addresses these concerns. Specifically, the authors should significantly expand the related work section, conduct a more thorough empirical evaluation with comparisons to relevant baselines, and investigate more efficient optimization strategies for the proposed method. A deeper discussion of the properties of the complexity measure and potential alternatives would also be beneficial.
