This paper introduces a method to identify feature interactions in high-dimensional kernel regression. It extends the standard additive model, which ignores such interactions, by proposing a "group additive model." This model partitions features into groups to capture correlations internally while maintaining an additive structure between the groups. The authors formally define an "intrinsic group additive structure" as the most parsimonious valid grouping and propose a penalized kernel regression framework to identify it. The main theoretical contribution is a novel complexity measure for these groupings, derived as an upper bound on the covering number of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), which naturally favors solutions with many small groups over fewer large ones.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strength is its theoretical contribution. Deriving a complexity penalty from first principles like covering numbers is an elegant and principled approach to the problem. The formalization of an "intrinsic" structure is also a clear and useful concept that helps frame the task.

However, the work has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution.
**Quality:** The work suffers from a disconnect between its theory and practical application. The proposed complexity measure is not amenable to efficient optimization, forcing the authors to rely on an exhaustive search, which scales poorly, or a simple greedy algorithm. I was disappointed that a more efficient optimization strategy was not developed. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation is critically insufficient to support the paper's claims. Experiments are confined to small synthetic cases and the Boston Housing dataset, and most importantly, they lack any baselines or comparisons to other methods. Without this context, the practical utility of the proposed method remains unsubstantiated.

**Originality and Significance:** The paper’s originality is hampered by a poor survey of related work. The authors miss crucial connections to the extensive literature on structured sparsity (e.g., group LASSO), graphical models for dependency structure learning, and feature interaction detection. This oversight makes it difficult to assess the novelty and significance of the contribution. While the problem is important, the lack of demonstrated empirical advantage and the impractical search algorithms limit the paper's potential impact.

**Post-rebuttal Comments**
While the authors' rebuttal provided some clarifications, the fundamental concerns regarding the inadequate experimental validation and the superficial treatment of related literature remain. For this work to be a meaningful contribution, it requires a much stronger positioning within the existing literature and a thorough empirical comparison.

**Summary for Program Committee**
*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel theoretical derivation of a complexity measure for group structures; formalizes the problem well.
*   **Arguments for Rejection:** Proposed measure is not efficiently optimizable; experimental evaluation is extremely thin with no baselines; poor coverage of highly relevant related work.
