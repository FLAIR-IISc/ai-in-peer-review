Summary and relation to prior work: This paper studies high-dimensional nonparametric regression via a group additive model (GAM-G), where features are partitioned into groups and the regression function is a sum of low-dimensional kernel components over groups. The authors formalize an intrinsic group additive structure using a partial order on partitions, propose a structure-penalized kernel method with a complexity measure derived from covering-number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs, and prove selection consistency of the ideal penalized estimator. They provide exhaustive search and a greedy backward stepwise heuristic to identify groups, and evaluate on small synthetic examples and Boston Housing. The work connects to additive/functional ANOVA models, OKGT, and kernel ridge regression. However, the related work overlooks substantial threads at NIPS and elsewhere: sparse additive models and SALSA (Ravikumar et al.; Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), hierarchical kernel learning and MKL (e.g., Bach 2009), structured sparsity/group lasso and overlapping group norms (Yuan & Lin 2006; Jacob et al. 2009; Bien et al. 2013), additive/structured Gaussian processes, and structure learning in graphical models/Bayesian networks (e.g., Markov blanket discovery; Meinshausen & Bühlmann; Chow–Liu), which offer relevant ideas for grouping and scalable surrogates.

Quality: The formalization of intrinsic group structure and the use of covering-number-based penalties are interesting, and the selection-consistency result (under appropriate tuning) is technically sound at the level of the population objective. Yet the complexity measure is difficult to optimize; the paper ultimately relies on exhaustive search (poor scaling with Bell numbers) or a greedy scheme without guarantees, creating a gap between theory and practical optimization. Experiments are thin, with no comparisons to strong baselines.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear in definitions and derivations, but the intuition behind the chosen penalty and its tightness is limited; alternative measures are mentioned but not developed. Important implementation details (kernel choice, hyperparameter selection, runtime) are under-specified.

Originality: The notion of intrinsic group structure and the specific RKHS capacity-based penalty are novel refinements of prior group-additive ideas. However, the conceptual space is close to existing structured-sparsity and hierarchical kernel literature.

Significance: Potentially impactful if it led to scalable, competitive methods. As presented, limited empirical validation and lack of baselines reduce confidence in practical relevance.

Pros:
- Crisp formalization of intrinsic group additive structure.
- RKHS-based complexity penalty and consistency analysis.

Cons:
- Optimization relies on exhaustive/greedy search; no scalability or guarantees.
- Sparse experiments; no baselines against additive models, SALSA, GP-additive, group-lasso, or OKGT.
- Related-work coverage misses key areas and NIPS-relevant threads.

Post-rebuttal: Clarifications helped, but experiments and literature discussion remain insufficient. I recommend rejection at this time; a strengthened version should add thorough baselines, larger datasets with structure-recovery metrics and runtime scaling, and deeper connections to structured sparsity, MKL/hierarchical kernels, GP-additive models, and graphical-model structure learning.
