

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors define a minimal group additive structure (the "intrinsic group additive structure") that generalizes the standard additive model by allowing predictor variables to be grouped together to capture interactions, while maintaining the interpretability and efficiency advantages of additive models. The paper introduces a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneously identifying this structure and estimating the nonparametric functions, using a complexity measure derived from covering numbers of RKHSs.

The paper builds upon previous work on additive models (e.g., Hastie & Tibshirani [5]) and functional ANOVA models ([4, 13]), but makes a clear distinction by focusing on the group structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure considered in graphical models ([2, 7]). The authors formally define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal structure for which the function can be expressed as a sum of functions each depending on a group of variables, addressing a key gap in the OKGT method from [11].

**Strengths:**
1. The formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure is a significant theoretical contribution, resolving the non-uniqueness problem noted in previous work.
2. The theoretical consistency results (Theorems 2-4) are rigorous and well-motivated, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
3. The paper clearly distinguishes the group additive model from other approaches (functional ANOVA, graphical models), highlighting its position as a middle ground between the standard additive model and higher-order interaction models.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited related work coverage:** The paper fails to adequately connect with structured sparsity literature (e.g., [1, 6, 9]) and feature selection methods that address similar grouping problems. The discussion of connections to graphical models is superficial, and the paper doesn't engage with how their approach compares to methods that directly estimate group structures from data.
   
2. **Insufficient experimental validation:** The experiments are limited to small synthetic datasets (with only 5 models) and the Boston Housing dataset, with no baseline comparisons to alternative methods. The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the method's performance under different data-generating processes, sample sizes, or when the group structure is not perfectly captured by the intrinsic structure. The absence of comparison to methods like those in [6, 9] or [11] is particularly concerning.

3. **Inefficient optimization approach:** The authors derive a complexity measure based on covering numbers, but their optimization approach relies on either exhaustive search (which scales poorly with the number of predictors) or a greedy backward stepwise algorithm (which may get stuck in local optima). The paper doesn't explore more efficient optimization strategies or discuss why alternative complexity measures (e.g., based on Rademacher complexity or VC-dimension) wouldn't work better. The claim that "the number of amiable group additive structures is finite" is misleading in high-dimensional settings where the number of possible groupings grows combinatorially.

4. **Lack of practical guidance:** The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on selecting the tuning parameters (µ and α) in practice, beyond a grid search. The computational cost of the exhaustive search approach makes it impractical for larger datasets, yet the paper doesn't acknowledge this limitation or suggest alternative strategies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The theoretical foundation is strong and addresses a clear gap in the literature.
- The concept of the intrinsic group additive structure is novel and well-defined.
- The paper provides a useful framework for understanding group structures in nonparametric regression.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical utility of the method.
- The related work is inadequately covered, missing key connections to structured sparsity and graphical models.
- The optimization approach is inefficient and not well-suited for high-dimensional data, which is the primary context the paper addresses.
- The paper lacks comparison to alternative methods that could demonstrate the advantages of their approach.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method through comprehensive experiments and meaningful comparisons to alternative approaches. The limited related work coverage and inefficient optimization strategy further weaken the paper's contribution. The authors should substantially expand their experimental validation, provide meaningful comparisons to existing methods, and strengthen their discussion of related work before the paper can be considered for publication at NIPS.
