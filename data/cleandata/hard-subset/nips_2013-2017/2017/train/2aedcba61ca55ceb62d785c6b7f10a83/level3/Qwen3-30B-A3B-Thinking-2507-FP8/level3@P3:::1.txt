

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a group additive model for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, where the predictor variables are partitioned into groups such that the regression function is additive across these groups rather than individual variables. The authors formally define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, addressing the non-uniqueness issue in previous work on group additive models (e.g., [11]). They derive a complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs to penalize group structures, enabling simultaneous structure identification and function estimation through a structure-penalized kernel method.

The paper builds on the functional ANOVA and additive model literature, positioning group additive models as a middle ground between standard additive models (which ignore all interactions) and functional ANOVA models (which consider all possible interactions). The authors establish theoretical consistency for their method, showing that the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases. They propose two algorithms: one using exhaustive search for small feature sets and another using a backward stepwise approach for larger problems.

**Strengths:**
1. The formal definition of intrinsic group additive structure is a valuable contribution that addresses a key limitation in previous work.
2. The theoretical consistency proof for the structure identification is sound and well-executed.
3. The derivation of the complexity measure from covering numbers is mathematically rigorous, providing a principled approach to structure selection.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Optimization limitations:** The proposed complexity measure leads to an optimization problem that cannot be efficiently solved. The authors resort to exhaustive search (which scales exponentially with the number of features) or a greedy backward stepwise approach with no theoretical guarantees. This severely limits the method's applicability to high-dimensional problems, which is precisely the domain where the authors claim the method is most valuable.

2. **Inadequate related work:** The paper fails to properly connect to substantial literatures. The authors mention connections to graphical models ([2], [7], [9]) but don't properly discuss how their approach differs from or relates to structured sparsity methods (e.g., [1, 6, 13]), which explicitly handle group structures in regression. The discussion of Bayesian networks and feature selection literature is notably absent, despite these being highly relevant to group structure identification.

3. **Insufficient experiments:** The experimental evaluation is limited to small synthetic examples (5 models with up to 13 features) and the Boston Housing dataset, with no comparisons to alternative methods. The lack of baselines makes it impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers practical advantages over existing approaches. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their experiments in the rebuttal but provide no additional comparisons or larger-scale validation.

4. **Complexity measure justification:** The authors don't adequately justify why their specific complexity measure (based on covering numbers) is preferable to alternative measures (e.g., VC-dimension, Rademacher complexity). The paper mentions but doesn't substantively engage with these alternatives, leaving readers to wonder if a more efficient measure might exist.

**Evaluation against criteria:**

*Quality:* The theoretical contributions are strong, but the practical limitations of the proposed algorithms and the lack of extensive experimental validation weaken the paper's quality. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach but fail to adequately address how to overcome them.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with precise mathematical definitions and proofs. The technical content is presented accessibly to experts in the field.

*Originality:* The paper makes a specific contribution by formally defining the intrinsic group additive structure and deriving a complexity measure for it. However, the lack of proper connection to structured sparsity literature (which has extensively studied group structures in regression) significantly reduces the perceived novelty.

*Significance:* The problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression is important, but the paper's limited experimental validation and the poor scaling of the proposed algorithms make it difficult to assess the method's practical significance. Without comparisons to existing methods or larger-scale experiments, it's unclear whether the proposed approach offers meaningful advantages.

**Pro and con acceptance arguments:**

*Pro:* The formal treatment of group additive structures and the consistency proof represent a valuable theoretical contribution to the literature on high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The paper provides a clear framework for understanding and identifying group structures that accommodate some interactions while maintaining interpretability.

*Con:* The practical limitations of the proposed algorithms (exponential scaling for exhaustive search, no guarantees for greedy approach) severely restrict the method's applicability. The inadequate experimental validation and insufficient related work coverage prevent readers from assessing the method's practical value and novelty relative to existing approaches.

**Conclusion:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression through the formal definition of intrinsic group additive structure. However, the lack of efficient optimization methods, insufficient experimental validation, and poor treatment of related work significantly limit its impact. While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method offers practical advantages over existing approaches or to adequately establish its novelty within the broader literature. The authors should address these issues through additional experiments, comparisons to relevant methods, and a more thorough discussion of related work before the paper can be considered for publication at NIPS.
