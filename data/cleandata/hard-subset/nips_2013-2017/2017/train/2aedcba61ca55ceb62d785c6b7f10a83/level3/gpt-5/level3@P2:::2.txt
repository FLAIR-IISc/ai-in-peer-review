Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression where predictors are partitioned into disjoint groups and the regression function is additive over groups. The authors (i) formalize the notion of an “intrinsic group additive structure” (the minimal partition under a natural refinement partial order), proving existence and uniqueness; (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly fits within-group functions in RKHSs and selects the group partition via a complexity penalty derived from covering numbers of direct-sum RKHS balls; (iii) provide theory showing the estimator consistently recovers the intrinsic group structure; and (iv) present an exhaustive-search and a backward stepwise algorithm, with empirical validation on synthetic data and Boston Housing.

Relative to prior work, this sits between additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) and functional ANOVA/sparse higher-order interaction RKHS models (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA; ESP/ANOVA kernels; SALSA, ICML’16). It also relates to graphical models but targets functional decomposition rather than conditional independence. The paper builds on classical RKHS capacity results (covering numbers for translation-invariant kernels, e.g., Gaussian) and kernel ridge regression.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of the intrinsic structure via a lattice of partitions and the monotonicity results are clean. The complexity penalty leverages covering-number upper bounds for direct-sum spaces (Proposition 3), yielding a tractable penalty ∑u α|u| (after absorbing constants) in the empirical objective. The selection consistency argument distinguishes amiable vs non-amiable structures and uses uniform risk bounds (Theorems 2–4).
- Assumptions/limitations: The strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2 (and the interpretability of “intrinsic”) rests on independence of X coordinates (Proposition 1). The penalty depends on an abstract α = ln(r/ε); in practice α is a hyperparameter with unclear relationship to bandwidths and sample size. The number of candidate partitions is super-exponential in p; the stepwise search is heuristic with no guarantees.
- Empirics: Simulations suggest successful recovery when p is modest and with stepwise for some settings; performance degrades in one model and for greedy search on Model 4. Real-data application is illustrative and interpretable, though limited. Comparisons to competing interaction-learning baselines are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured and readable, with motivation, definitions, and theory laid out. However, there are several typos and a few places where terminology and statements should be tightened (listed below). The role of “almost surely” (Section 2.3.1) and the practical meaning of α would benefit from clarification. A brief complexity analysis of the proposed algorithms would help.

Originality
Defining and proving the existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic group additive structure and connecting structure selection to covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior work has either fixed additive structure, modeled generic interactions, or optimized over subset selections rather than partitions. The explicit complexity penalty for partitions derived from RKHS capacity is an original and interesting angle.

Significance
If scalable, the approach provides an interpretable middle ground between purely additive and fully general interaction models, with consistency guarantees. The contribution could spur further work on principled penalties over partitions and on efficient search. Current scalability constraints and limited empirical comparisons temper immediate practical impact, but the conceptual contribution is solid.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, well-motivated formalization of intrinsic group structure with a clean partial order and existence/uniqueness.
- Principled structural penalty derived from RKHS capacity of direct-sum spaces; integrates smoothly with KRR.
- Nontrivial theory: uniform risk bounds and selection consistency distinguishing amiable vs non-amiable structures.
- Interpretable empirical results on synthetic and real data.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Search over partitions is combinatorial; proposed stepwise heuristic lacks guarantees and can miss true structures.
- Practical meaning and selection of α (from covering-number bounds) is opaque; penalty strength may be hard to tune.
- Dependence on independence assumptions for some structural results; unclear robustness when X is correlated.
- Empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to competitive baselines that learn interactions (e.g., functional ANOVA kernels, SALSA/additive with interactions, boosted trees/RF feature interaction measures), and lacks runtime/scalability studies.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Interaction characterization: Can you provide a simple criterion to declare that two variables interact (must be in the same group) without committing to the full model in Eq. (1)? For example, could a test based on HSIC or differences in additive vs joint fit be used to pre-screen pairs?
- Use of “almost surely” in Section 2.3.1 (around line 169): do you mean equality in L2(PX) (“in population”) or almost-sure equality of functions PX-a.s.? Please clarify the measure and limit (if any).
- Penalty parameter α: Since α = ln(r/ε) originates from covering-number bounds, how should α relate to kernel bandwidths and sample size in practice? Any guidance beyond CV, or sensitivity analysis?
- Theory: The uniform bounds involve |Ga| and maxG ln N(⋯, HG, d∞). Can you discuss how these scale with p, and whether your choice of penalty effectively controls the union bound in finite samples?
- Algorithms: Please provide computational complexity and wall-clock times; discuss initialization strategies and whether merges/splits or restarts improve the greedy search; any empirical stability analysis of recovered groups under resampling?

- Suggest “translation-invariant kernel” instead of “convolution kernel” when K(x, t) = k(x − t) (line ~205).
- Equation (3): ensure the empirical loss includes the summation over i (it appears to be missing in the current draft).
- Line 145: “on RHS” → “on the RHS”.
- Line 152: grammar fix needed.
- Line 160: grammar fix needed.
- Line 247: “turning” → “tuning”.
- Algorithm 2, line 1: “State with” → “Start with”.
- Line 251: “by compare” → “by comparing”.
- Notation: Be consistent about d = |G| and use |u| for group sizes; in Proposition 3, check constants inside ln N(⋯) and the dependence on |G| for clarity.

Suggestions for strengthening
- Add baselines: functional ANOVA kernels (incl. KRR with ANOVA/ESP kernels), SALSA (ICML’16), and tree-based models (report interaction recovery and predictive performance).
- Report runtime and scalability experiments; consider multiple restarts and split-merge heuristics; discuss potential relaxations (e.g., convex surrogates over partitions, structured sparsity across ANOVA kernels).
- Discuss robustness when covariates are correlated and when kernels are not universal; consider extensions to categorical variables and mixed data types.
- Provide code or pseudocode with complexity and implementation details; include sensitivity plots for μ, α, and λ.

Overall
A thoughtful and technically sound paper with a novel take on learning additive group structure via RKHS capacity control. I find the conceptual and theoretical contributions compelling. The main concerns are scalability and empirical comparisons. With clarifications and stronger experiments, this would be a solid contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can address the empirical and clarity issues.
