Summary and relation to prior work: This paper studies nonparametric regression with unknown group-additive structure, where predictors are partitioned into groups and interactions are allowed within groups but not across them. The authors formalize the intrinsic (minimal) group-additive structure and propose a structure-penalized kernel ridge regression that jointly estimates the partition and within-group functions. The key innovation is a penalty that quantifies the complexity of a candidate partition via covering numbers of direct-sum RKHS balls. Two approximate solvers—exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic—are presented. Theoretical results establish convergence of empirical to population risk and consistency of the estimated group structure. Empirical studies on synthetic data and Boston Housing indicate the method can recover true partitions. The work connects to additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), spline ANOVA (Gu), interaction kernels (e.g., ESP/SALSA), OKGT, and, conceptually, to graphical models, though it targets a different structural object.

Quality: The modeling and penalty are well-motivated; the use of covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs is elegant, and the structure-consistency result is a meaningful contribution. Proof sketches rely on standard capacity control; assumptions and rates are plausible. Experiments support the claims, though baselines against functional ANOVA/ESP kernels or hierarchical kernel learning would strengthen the case.

Clarity: Generally clear, but marred by numerous typos and some ambiguity around tuning the penalty (μ, α) and the practical meaning/setting of α from the covering-number bounds. Please also clarify the computational complexity of the stepwise method.

Originality: Quantifying group-structure complexity via covering numbers of the induced direct-sum RKHS appears novel and differentiates this work from OKGT, SALSA, and standard additive/ANOVA RKHS approaches.

Significance: If situated against the broader literature (e.g., multiple/hierarchical kernel learning for interactions), the contribution is potentially significant, offering a principled path to structure learning with consistency guarantees.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled structural penalty tied to RKHS capacity
- Clear formalization of intrinsic group structure with consistency
- Solid theoretical development and supportive experiments

Cons for acceptance:
- Optimization over partitions scales poorly; heuristics may miss true structures when p is large
- Limited empirical comparisons; sensitivity to tuning not fully analyzed
- Writing issues and a few technical clarifications needed
