Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies high-dimensional nonparametric regression with kernels when main-effect additivity is too restrictive. It formalizes a group additive model in which features are partitioned into groups; within each group, an arbitrary multivariate nonlinear function is learned, and the final predictor is the sum over groups. The authors (i) define a partial order over groupings and the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal correct partition), (ii) propose a structure-penalized RKHS estimator whose penalty is a complexity measure derived from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs, and (iii) give two search procedures over partitions (exhaustive and a greedy backward stepwise split heuristic). They prove consistency of both the function estimator and, under suitable tuning, recovery of the intrinsic structure.

This is positioned between classical additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) and full interaction models such as SS-ANOVA (Gu) and ESP kernels (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16 SALSA). It also connects to structured sparsity (e.g., group/overlapping group lasso and hierarchical kernels), multiple kernel learning, graphical models/Bayesian networks for dependency structure (Koller & Friedman), and feature selection methods (lasso/ARD). The paper cites some of these strands but the related-work section misses important links and contrasts (see below).

Strengths
- Clear formalization of group additivity and the intrinsic structure via a partial order; the existence/uniqueness result is clean and useful.
- A principled, RKHS-based estimator with a structure penalty derived from capacity control. The covering-number–based measure penalizes larger groups more heavily and thus encourages many small groups when supported by data.
- Consistency results for both prediction and structure identification under universal kernels; the proofs leverage standard capacity control and are generally sound.
- The algorithms are simple and easy to implement; the backward-splitting heuristic reflects the intended inductive bias.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The complexity measure is built from loose upper bounds on covering numbers, with unknown constants and a “scale” parameter α that is hard to interpret/tune. As a result, the penalty is not readily optimizable and the paper resorts to exhaustive search (exponential in p) or a greedy forward/backward procedure that can miss good partitions. There is no algorithmic guarantee for the heuristic nor evidence of robustness.
- The theory assumes fixed p and relies on capacity bounds that may be vacuous in practice; conditions such as the use of universal kernels and the independence requirement in Proposition 1 (for strict inclusion) are strong. Practical guidance on λ, μ, α is limited.
- Experiments are thin: small synthetic cases and Boston Housing only. There are no baselines against (i) GAM/SS-ANOVA, (ii) SALSA/ESP kernels, (iii) hierarchical multiple kernel learning and structured sparsity (group/overlapping group lasso on engineered interaction features), (iv) OKGT, or (v) additive Gaussian processes with learned groups. No ablations or runtime/scalability analysis are provided.

Clarity
- The writing is generally clear, but the derivation and role of α (coming from ln(r/ε)) and how it is set from data are not well explained; in places it is unclear whether the final penalty is ∑j α|uj| or ∑j α|uj|+1. Be precise and motivate the choice.
- The search algorithms are only sketched (e.g., splitting criterion, tie-breaking, stopping conditions) and the computational complexity is not quantified.

Originality
- Framing group additivity and proving existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic partition are nice contributions. However, searching over partitions with penalties that increase with group size is closely related to prior work on structured sparsity/hierarchical kernel learning and MKL, and the paper does not clearly differentiate from those lines or build on their optimization machinery.

Significance
- The potential is real—learning low-order interactions while keeping interpretability is valuable—but the current empirical evidence is insufficient to establish practical impact. Without baselines and scaling studies, it is hard to assess whether practitioners should adopt the method.

Suggestions for improvement
- Related work: discuss and compare to (a) SALSA and SS-ANOVA; (b) structured sparsity and hierarchical kernel learning (e.g., group/overlapping group lasso, tree-structured and hierarchical MKL); (c) graphical lasso and Bayesian network structure learning as alternative ways to propose groupings; (d) sparse additive models (SpAM) and additive GPs.
- Baselines: include GAM, SS-ANOVA with pairwise interactions, SALSA, hierarchical MKL with group penalties, OKGT, and a group-lasso on polynomial or random Fourier features. Report predictive error, group recovery accuracy, runtime, and stability across seeds.
- Optimization: consider tractable surrogates to the covering-number penalty (e.g., concave-in-size penalties, group-structured sparsity norms) that admit convex or difference-of-convex programs; or relax partitions to soft assignments with entropy regularization and alternate minimization. Use data-driven interaction scores (e.g., HSIC-based pairwise/triwise statistics) to prune the partition lattice before search; or adopt graph clustering (Louvain/spectral) on an interaction graph to seed the heuristic. Provide complexity analysis and scalability experiments (e.g., p=50–200).
- Clarify constants/choices in the penalty, sensitivity to α, and conditions (beyond independence) under which Proposition 1’s strictness holds.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal clarified some definitions and tuning choices, which alleviates part of the concern about α/μ. However, the lack of baselines and limited datasets remain, and the discussion of related structured-sparsity and kernel-learning literature needs to be substantially strengthened. I encourage the authors to address these points in a revision.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean formalization of group additive structure and a theoretically grounded penalty.
- Consistency guarantees for structure recovery with kernels.
- Addresses an important gap between additive models and full interaction models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Optimization of the proposed complexity is ad hoc and does not scale; no guarantees for the greedy search.
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient and lacks comparisons to strong, relevant baselines.
- Related work coverage is incomplete, missing key connections to structured sparsity, MKL, and graphical-model structure learning.
- Practical guidance and empirical validation of tuning and scalability are limited.

Overall evaluation: Weak reject. The paper has interesting theory and a promising modeling perspective, but the current algorithmic and empirical components do not yet meet the bar for NIPS.
