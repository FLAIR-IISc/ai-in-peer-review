- Proposal: structure-penalized kernel method to identify an intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression using RKHS covering-number complexity.  
- Key contribution: formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure resolving non-uniqueness in prior work.  
- Theoretical results: rigorous consistency theorems (Theorems 2–4) supporting the method.  
- Positioning: clearly distinguishes group additive models from functional ANOVA and graphical models as a middle ground between additive and high-order interaction models.  

- Strength: formal definition is a significant theoretical advance addressing a gap in OKGT-type methods.  
- Strength: theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.  
- Strength: conceptual framing of group additive models is clear and useful.  

- Weakness: limited related work coverage—fails to engage structured sparsity and feature-grouping literature and only superficially discusses graphical models.  
- Weakness: insufficient experimental validation—few small synthetic examples and Boston Housing, no baselines or comparisons to relevant methods (e.g., [6,9,11]).  
- Weakness: inefficient optimization—relies on exhaustive search or greedy backward stepwise, which do not scale and may get stuck in local optima.  
- Weakness: choice of complexity measure and lack of exploration of alternatives (e.g., Rademacher complexity, VC-dimension) is not justified.  
- Weakness: lack of practical guidance for tuning (µ, α) beyond grid search and no strategies for large datasets.  

- Argument for acceptance: solid theoretical foundation and a novel, well-defined concept that fills a literature gap.  
- Argument for acceptance: provides a useful theoretical framework for group structure in nonparametric regression.  
- Argument against acceptance: experiments do not demonstrate practical utility or robustness.  
- Argument against acceptance: related work omissions weaken the paper’s contextualization.  
- Argument against acceptance: optimization approach is not suitable for the high-dimensional settings the paper targets.  
- Argument against acceptance: missing empirical comparisons to alternative methods.  

- Recommendation: reject; needs substantially expanded experiments, stronger related-work connections, scalable optimization, and practical tuning guidance before resubmission.
