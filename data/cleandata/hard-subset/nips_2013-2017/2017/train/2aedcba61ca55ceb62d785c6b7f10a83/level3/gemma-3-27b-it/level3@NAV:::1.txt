## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper tackles the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by extending the standard additive model to a “group additive model,” aiming to capture feature correlations ignored by traditional approaches. The core idea – grouping features based on underlying relationships – is intuitively appealing and potentially valuable. The authors provide a formal treatment of this concept, defining an “intrinsic group additive structure” and proposing a penalized kernel method for simultaneous structure identification and function estimation. A novel complexity measure, derived as an upper bound on the covering number, is introduced to guide the grouping process.

The work builds upon existing literature in additive modeling and functional ANOVA, recognizing the limitations of assuming complete independence between predictors. However, the paper notably falls short in thoroughly contextualizing its contribution within related fields. The connection to graphical models, structured sparsity, and feature selection – areas dealing with dependency structures and variable grouping – is acknowledged briefly but lacks depth. Citations to relevant work in these areas (e.g., recent advances in block-sparse estimation, conditional independence testing) are missing, hindering a clear understanding of the novelty and positioning of this approach.

A significant weakness lies in the optimization of the proposed complexity measure. While theoretically sound, the authors resort to exhaustive search (intractable for larger datasets) or a greedy forward-selection approach. The limited exploration of alternative, more efficient optimization strategies is disappointing. Furthermore, the theoretical analysis, while establishing consistency, doesn’t fully address the practical challenges of selecting appropriate regularization parameters and the impact of the choice of kernel. 

The experimental evaluation is also a concern. The experiments are limited to small synthetic datasets and the Boston Housing dataset, without any comparison to established benchmarks or alternative methods. This makes it difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed approach. While the post-rebuttal clarifies some theoretical aspects, the lack of more comprehensive experiments and a more robust literature review remain major drawbacks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem of capturing feature interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is relevant and challenging.
*   The formalization of the “intrinsic group additive structure” provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited originality due to insufficient connection to related fields (graphical models, structured sparsity).
*   The proposed complexity measure is difficult to optimize efficiently.
*   Weak experimental validation with a lack of baselines and comprehensive evaluation.
*   Poor coverage of related work.



**Overall:** I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting idea and some theoretical results, the limitations in optimization, experimental evaluation, and related work coverage significantly detract from its contribution. A substantially revised version with more comprehensive experiments, a more thorough literature review, and a discussion of more efficient optimization strategies would be necessary for reconsideration.
