- Problem and contribution: Studies nonparametric regression with group-additive structure and formalizes the intrinsic (minimal) partition admitting the true function.
- Method: Proposes a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly estimates the regression function and the input partition by penalizing the capacity of direct-sum RKHSs.
- Penalty derivation: Derives covering-number–based complexity bounds that reduce to a practical penalty depending only on group sizes (penalty of the form sum_u α|u|).
- Theory: Proves selection consistency for recovering the intrinsic partition under universal kernels and appropriate tuning, distinguishing amiable vs non-amiable partitions.
- Algorithms and experiments: Provides exhaustive search (small p) and a backward stepwise split heuristic (moderate/large p), with simulations and a real-data example.
- Positioning: Clearly places the work between classical additive models and ANOVA/higher-order interaction models and clarifies differences from OKGT and graphical models.
- Strength — formalization: Rigorous, lattice-theoretic formalization of intrinsic group structure with L2/RKHS inclusion arguments.
- Strength — penalty: Novel, well-motivated RKHS-capacity penalty and elegant reduction from covering-number bounds to a simple group-size penalty.
- Strength — consistency: Careful consistency results for structure recovery under stated kernel assumptions.
- Strength — practicality: Offers practical algorithms for different p regimes and demonstrates empirical support.
- Strength — exposition: Good discussion relating the method to ANOVA kernels, SALSA, and graphical-model literature.
- Weakness — scalability: Partition optimization is combinatorial (Bell numbers); greedy stepwise can fail and does so in examples (Model 4).
- Weakness — search methods: More robust search strategies (merge–split, beam search, MCMC) or relaxations are needed for large p.
- Weakness — penalty calibration: The α parameter comes from bounds and lacks direct interpretability; sensitivity to α and λ is underexplored.
- Weakness — hyperparameter interactions: Interactions among kernel hyperparameters, λ, µ, and α require more discussion and empirical study.
- Weakness — assumptions: Some results depend on universal kernels, boundedness, and independence; these assumptions should be explicitly stated where used.
- Weakness — empirical baselines: Limited comparisons — should include GAM/SpAM, spline-ANOVA, SALSA/ESP, OKGT, and HKL for prediction and structure recovery.
- Weakness — writing: Generally clear but contains typos and a few technical imprecisions that need fixing.
- Quality summary: Methodology is technically sound with a well-motivated capacity penalty; consistency results are valuable though high-level; experiments supportive but could be stronger.
- Clarity summary: Paper mostly well organized; intrinsic structure and amiable definitions are clear, but the penalty derivation needs a compact roadmap and clearer assumptions.
- Originality: Penalizing partitions via RKHS capacity and identifying an intrinsic partition appear novel and conceptually useful.
- Significance: Potentially impactful if scalability and empirical comparisons are strengthened; theory reassures identifiability.
- Pros for acceptance: Clean formulation, principled penalty, solid consistency result, practical algorithms with positive experiments.
- Cons for acceptance: Scalability issues, greedy search failures, sparse empirical comparisons, tuning sensitivity, and some notation/typo problems.
- Requested clarifications: Provide practical interaction tests (e.g., compare bivariate vs sum of univariates via KRR/held-out risk), clarify “almost surely” meaning (L2(P_X) equality vs asymptotics).
- Specific technical/typo fixes: Fix empirical risk summation in Eq.(3), use “translation-invariant kernel,” correct multiple grammatical/wording typos, denote λ_n when sample-dependent, and state independence assumption in Prop.1.
- Suggestions for improvement: Add stronger baselines, explore scalable search (merge–split/beam/MCMC or convex surrogates), study kernel/bandwidth interactions and ARD within groups, and consider alternative capacity penalties (localized Rademacher/Gaussian complexities).
