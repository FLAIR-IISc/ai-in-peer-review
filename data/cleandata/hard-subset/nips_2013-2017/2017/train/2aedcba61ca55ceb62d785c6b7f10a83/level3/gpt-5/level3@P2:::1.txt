Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive model (GAM in groups). Instead of assuming additivity across single variables, the authors assume the regression function decomposes as a sum of functions over disjoint groups of covariates. They (i) formalize a notion of “intrinsic group additive structure” as the unique minimal partition (under a refinement partial order) that still represents the true regression function, (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly estimates the function and the group partition, and (iii) derive a complexity penalty over group structures from covering-number bounds of the associated direct-sum RKHS. They prove selection consistency for identifying the intrinsic group structure and provide two search strategies (exhaustive search and a greedy backward stepwise “splitting” approach). Experiments on small synthetic problems and the Boston Housing dataset illustrate the method qualitatively.

This work relates to additive models and functional ANOVA (Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu; Ramsay & Silverman), kernel ridge regression with ANOVA/ESP kernels, and the OKGT framework (Pan et al.). It also connects to sparse/structured additive modeling (e.g., SpAM, Ravikumar et al.; SALSA, Kandasamy & Yu) and to structure discovery problems in probabilistic graphical models. The proposed “group additive” approach sits between purely additive (no interactions) and full higher-order interactions, aiming to capture low-order interactions within small groups.

Strengths
- Clear and useful formalization of “intrinsic group additive structure” as the minimal partition consistent with the function, with a clean partial order and existence/uniqueness result.
- Consistency guarantees: the structure-penalized estimator identifies the intrinsic partition under suitable conditions, separating amiable vs. non-amiable structures.
- A principled route to a structure penalty via covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs; the resulting C(G) favors many small groups and penalizes large groups.
- Practical algorithms (exhaustive and a greedy backward splitting heuristic) and an interpretable real-data illustration.

Weaknesses
Quality (technical soundness, empirical support)
- The penalty C(G) is derived from upper bounds requiring convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transform (e.g., Gaussian/Laplace) and sup-norm covering numbers on a compact domain. The paper would benefit from stating the assumptions in theorems explicitly (domain compactness, kernel universality, independence where used), clarifying how they map to the experimental setups, and discussing constant factors and scaling with p.
- C(G) depends on a reparameterized α stemming from ln(r/ε); in practice α is tuned, so the link to covering numbers becomes somewhat ad hoc. A sensitivity analysis of (μ, α) and a discussion of how α relates to kernel bandwidth and sample size would strengthen the story.
- Optimization over partitions is combinatorial. The exhaustive search is only feasible for very small p; the proposed greedy backward stepwise method is heuristic and can miss the true structure (the authors observe this). There is no complexity/runtime analysis or comparison to alternative search/relaxation strategies.
- Experiments are thin: small synthetic cases and one classic UCI dataset, with no quantitative comparisons to baselines. Given direct related methods, the lack of baselines makes it hard to assess gains in accuracy or structure recovery.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. Some notational points could be tightened (e.g., clearly distinguish empirical vs. penalized risks across sections; explicitly state when independence assumptions are needed such as in Proposition 1).
- The role and interpretation of α would benefit from a more concrete discussion and practical guidance.
- The pseudocode for the stepwise procedure should more clearly indicate how μ and α are selected inside the search and how kernel hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth) are tuned.

Originality
- The definition of intrinsic group additive structure and its identification via a covering-number–based penalty over direct-sum RKHSs is original and interesting. At the same time, the idea of encouraging small groups via a combinatorial penalty is related in spirit to structured sparsity and hierarchical kernel learning.

Significance
- Potentially useful if it scales: discovering grouped interactions can improve interpretability and avoid the explosion of pairwise/higher-order terms. However, current algorithms do not scale, and the empirical section does not demonstrate advantages over strong baselines (e.g., SALSA; KRR with ANOVA/ESP kernels; SpAM and variants; hierarchical interaction models; OKGT).

Missing or underdeveloped related work
- Structured sparsity for grouped interactions and additive models: SpAM (Ravikumar et al., 2009), Meier et al. (group lasso for logistic), Jacob et al. (overlapping group lasso), Bach (hierarchical kernel learning; sparsity-inducing norms). These offer convex/relaxed formulations that may provide scalable surrogates to the proposed penalty.
- Interaction selection with hierarchy (e.g., Bien et al. 2013; strong hierarchy penalties).
- Broader feature grouping/partition learning and Bayesian partition models; scalable graph/partition search heuristics.
- While the paper mentions graphical models, the connection to conditional independence vs. group additivity could be sharpened, and related structure-learning literature more comprehensively covered.

Suggestions
- Add quantitative baselines: SALSA, OKGT, KRR with ANOVA/ESP kernels, SpAM/sparse GAMs, hierarchical interaction models. Report predictive error, structure recovery (e.g., ARI for partitions), and runtime.
- Provide scalability analysis with p and n; consider relaxations (e.g., convex surrogates approximating C(G)) or search strategies (merge-split heuristics, stochastic local search, MIP with cutting planes) and report improvements.
- Clarify assumptions for the theorems, sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth, and provide a systematic tuning protocol for (μ, α, λ).
- Explore correlated covariates beyond independence (used in Proposition 1) and assess robustness.
- Consider a Bayesian perspective for partitions (e.g., CRP over groups) as a complementary estimator.

Post-rebuttal note
The rebuttal clarified aspects of the theory and implementation, but the experimental evaluation and related-work coverage remain insufficient for NIPS standards. Strengthening these two aspects would substantially improve the paper.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean formalization of intrinsic group additive structure.
- Theoretically grounded penalty and selection consistency results.
- Potentially impactful direction for interpretable interaction discovery.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and no comparisons to closely related baselines.
- Scalability and optimization remain unresolved; current methods are ad hoc and may miss true structures.
- Related work on structured sparsity and hierarchical kernels is insufficiently covered and contrasted.

Overall recommendation
Interesting and potentially impactful theoretical contribution, but the current empirical and algorithmic components do not yet meet the bar. I encourage the authors to expand experiments with strong baselines, improve scalability, and strengthen the related-work discussion.
