

This paper proposes a group additive model for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, aiming to address the limitation of standard additive models that ignore feature interactions. The authors define an intrinsic group additive structure and develop a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous identification of this structure and function estimation. The key theoretical contribution is a complexity measure derived from covering number bounds that favors many small groups. The paper proves consistency for the proposed method and develops two algorithms for structure identification: an exhaustive search and a greedy forward-selection approach.

The paper's main strengths include a rigorous formal treatment of the problem and clear theoretical results demonstrating consistency under appropriate conditions. The definition of intrinsic group additive structure provides a valuable conceptual framework for understanding the minimal structure that captures feature interactions.

However, significant weaknesses limit the paper's contribution. The complexity measure is not efficiently optimizable, forcing reliance on computationally expensive exhaustive search or a greedy approach that may not find the optimal structure. The authors only briefly mention alternative complexity measures without substantive discussion or comparison. More critically, the related work coverage is inadequate, omitting key connections to structured sparsity (e.g., group lasso), graphical models, Bayesian networks, and feature selection literature that directly address similar problems. The experiments are limited to small synthetic datasets and Boston Housing without proper baselines or comparisons to competing methods, making it difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed approach.

While the problem is important and the theoretical foundation is sound, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how this work advances the state of the art beyond existing methods. The post-rebuttal clarifications addressed some concerns but did not substantially improve the experimental evaluation or related work discussion. Given these limitations, particularly the insufficient experimental validation and inadequate literature comparison, I recommend rejection. The paper would require significantly stronger empirical evaluation against relevant baselines and a more comprehensive treatment of related work to be considered for publication.
