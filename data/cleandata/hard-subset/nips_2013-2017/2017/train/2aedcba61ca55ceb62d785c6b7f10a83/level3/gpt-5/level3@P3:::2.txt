Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies nonparametric regression when predictors are partitioned into unknown groups and the regression function is additive across groups but fully nonparametric within each group. The authors formalize the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal partition under a partial order of refinements), and propose to learn both the partition and the within-group functions in an RKHS via a new structural penalty. The penalty quantifies the complexity of a candidate partition G through covering-number bounds of the direct-sum RKHS HG, leading to a joint optimization akin to kernel ridge regression (KRR) with an additional group-structure penalty. Two practical solvers are provided: exhaustive search with validation (small p) and a backward stepwise search (larger p). Theoretical results show uniform convergence of empirical to population risk under amiable partitions and, crucially, consistency of the estimated group structure. Experiments on synthetic and real data (Boston Housing) support the approach.

This work connects to additive models and functional ANOVA (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA), SALSA (Kandasamy and Yu, ICML’16) for low-order interactions, and to MKL/structured sparsity in RKHSs. Compared to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15), which assumed a group structure, this paper rigorously defines identifiability (intrinsic structure) and provides a principled selection mechanism using capacity control of direct-sum RKHSs. Unlike ANOVA-style models that consider all pairwise or higher-order interactions, the proposed framework targets a partition, reducing the number of functions to estimate when true interactions are localized within small groups.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of group-additive structure with a well-defined partial order and the intrinsic (minimal) structure; this resolves prior identifiability ambiguities.
- Novel and principled structural penalty: complexity of G is linked to the covering numbers of HG (direct sums), yielding an interpretable penalty that increases with group size and number of groups.
- Sound theory: bounds tying empirical and population risk via covering numbers, and consistency of the selected group structure (P(Ĝ = G∗) → 1).
- Practical algorithms and evidence: both exhaustive and backward stepwise implementations, with synthetic and real data showing meaningful discovered groups and functions.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Search over partitions scales poorly: the number of partitions grows super-exponentially (Bell numbers). The backward stepwise heuristic is greedy and can miss the true structure, as observed (e.g., Model 4).
- The penalty’s practical tuning (µ, α) is somewhat opaque; α folds in properties of the covering-number bound and acts similarly to a base of an exponential in group size, making selection non-intuitive. Guidance or sensitivity analyses would help.
- Theory relies on covering-number bounds that require conditions such as translation-invariant kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transforms (e.g., Gaussian/Laplace) and, for strict inclusion statements, independence of predictors. The scope of these assumptions and robustness to violations could be discussed more explicitly.
- Empirical evaluation would be stronger with competitive baselines: GAM/sparse additive models, ANOVA kernels, SALSA, and MKL-based structured penalties, including runtime comparisons.

Quality:
- Technically careful overall, with a clear path from capacity control to structure selection and consistency. The empirical study is supportive but could be broader and more comparative.
- Minor technical issues and typos (listed below) detract slightly from polish.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, but the manuscript contains numerous typos and a few points needing clarification (see below). Algorithmic descriptions are clear; however, more detail on computational complexity and convergence behavior of the stepwise method would help.

Originality:
- The key idea of quantifying the complexity of a partition via covering numbers of a direct-sum RKHS—and using this to penalize group structures—is original and compelling. The identification of the intrinsic group structure and consistency results represent a meaningful conceptual advance over prior work.

Significance:
- If scalable, the approach could be impactful for interpretable nonparametrics where interactions are localized within small groups. The theory provides confidence in model selection, and the framework is general.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Simple characterization of pairwise interaction: Could you provide a definition that does not require fitting the full model in (1)? For example, define that variables j1 and j2 interact if the projection of f onto H{j1,j2} is not equal (PX-a.s.) to the sum of its projections onto Hj1 and Hj2, equivalently, f is not representable as h1(Xj1) + h2(Xj2). A functional ANOVA or Sobol/Hoeffding decomposition-based criterion would also be acceptable. Please include a concrete operational test.
- Line 169 (“almost surely”): Do you mean equality PX-a.s. by definition when G is amiable (i.e., f ∈ L2G), or convergence a.s. as n → ∞? Please clarify the intended notion (it appears to be equality in population, not a statement about n).
- Computational complexity: Please provide big-O estimates for the exhaustive and stepwise procedures and discuss practical scaling (e.g., p ≈ 50–200). Any pruning, beam search, or warm-start strategies?

Suggestions:
- Use “translation-invariant kernel” rather than “convolutional kernel” (Line 205).
- Consider relating your penalty to MDL/codelength for partitions, or to group-sparsity penalties in direct-sum RKHSs (e.g., MKL), and discuss pros/cons.
- Provide ablations to illustrate the effect of µ and α, and guidance for setting α beyond grid search.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled structural penalty with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Clear formulation of intrinsic group structure and identifiability.
- Practical algorithms and positive empirical evidence.
- Topic fits NeurIPS and bridges statistical learning theory with interpretable nonparametrics.

Cons for acceptance:
- Scalability remains a challenge; heuristic search can fail.
- Limited comparisons to strong baselines; empirical section could be more comprehensive.
- A few technical/clarity issues and typos.

Overall assessment:
A comprehensive and well-motivated contribution that advances group-additive nonparametric modeling with a novel complexity-driven penalty and solid consistency theory. With strengthened experiments and a clearer discussion of scalability and assumptions, this could be a significant addition to the literature. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing clarity issues and expanding empirical comparisons.
