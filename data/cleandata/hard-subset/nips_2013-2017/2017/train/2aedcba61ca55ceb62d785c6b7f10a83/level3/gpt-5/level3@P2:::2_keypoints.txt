- Studies nonparametric regression with predictors partitioned into groups, defines an intrinsic group additive structure, and proves existence/uniqueness.
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel method that fits within-group RKHS functions and selects partitions via a complexity penalty from covering numbers.
- Derives a tractable penalty (effectively ∑u α|u| after constants) using covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs (Proposition 3).
- Provides consistency theory for recovering intrinsic group structure, distinguishing amiable vs non-amiable cases and using uniform risk bounds (Theorems 2–4).
- Presents exhaustive-search and backward stepwise algorithms and validates empirically on synthetic data and Boston Housing.
- Positions the work between additive models and functional ANOVA/sparse interaction RKHS models; relates to graphical models but focuses on functional decomposition.
- Technical formulation (partition lattice, monotonicity) is clean and technically sound.
- Selection consistency arguments are nontrivial and grounded in uniform risk/control of model complexity.
- Intrinsic-structure interpretation and some results rely on independence of X coordinates (Proposition 1).
- The penalty involves α = ln(r/ε) from theory but α’s practical relation to kernel bandwidths and sample size is unclear.
- Number of candidate partitions is super-exponential in p; the stepwise search is heuristic with no theoretical guarantees.
- Empirics: successful recovery for modest p and some settings, but performance degrades in certain models and for greedy search on Model 4.
- Real-data experiment is illustrative and interpretable but limited; empirical comparisons to interaction-learning baselines are missing.
- Clarity: generally readable with good structure, but contains typos, inconsistent terminology, and ambiguous uses of “almost surely” and α.
- Originality: novel formalization of intrinsic group additive structure and novel connection between partition selection and RKHS capacity.
- Significance: offers an interpretable middle ground with consistency guarantees, but scalability and empirical-comparison gaps limit immediate practical impact.
- Pros: principled formalization, RKHS-derived penalty integrated with KRR, solid theoretical guarantees, interpretable empirical results.
- Cons: combinatorial partition search, opaque tuning of α, dependence on independence assumptions, missing baselines and scalability/runtime analysis.
- Requests for clarification: simple pairwise interaction criteria, precise meaning of “almost surely” (L2 vs a.s.), guidance on α vs bandwidth/sample size, scaling of uniform bounds with p, and algorithmic complexity/stability details.
- Minor issues/typos: suggest “translation-invariant kernel,” fix missing summation in Eq.(3), several grammar/wording fixes, and notation consistency (|G| vs |u|, constants in Prop.3).
- Suggestions to strengthen: add baselines (ANOVA/ESP, SALSA, tree-based), report runtime/scalability, try restarts/split-merge heuristics, discuss correlated covariates and non-universal kernels, provide code/pseudocode and sensitivity analyses.
- Overall: thoughtful, technically solid, and novel; recommend acceptance conditional on addressing empirical, clarity, and scalability concerns.
