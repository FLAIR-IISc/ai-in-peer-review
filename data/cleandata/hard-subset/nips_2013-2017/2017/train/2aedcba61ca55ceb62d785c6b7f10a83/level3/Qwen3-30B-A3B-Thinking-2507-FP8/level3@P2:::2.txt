

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: identifying group additive structures where predictor variables are partitioned into groups, and the response depends additively on functions of these groups. The authors extend the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method by rigorously defining the *intrinsic group additive structure* (the minimal group structure that can represent the true function) and proposing a method to identify it while estimating the nonparametric functions.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it formally defines the intrinsic group additive structure, resolving the non-uniqueness issue in previous work. Second, it develops a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs, which is then incorporated into a structure-penalized kernel method. Third, the authors prove consistency of their method in identifying the intrinsic structure as the sample size grows. The method is implemented through two algorithms: an exhaustive search for small p and a backward stepwise approach for larger p. The approach is evaluated on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset, demonstrating its ability to recover true group structures and provide interpretable models.

The paper is technically sound with solid theoretical foundations. The consistency proofs in Section 3 are well-constructed, and the experimental results (Section 4-5) provide convincing empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness. The authors successfully address a key limitation of additive models (neglecting interactions) while maintaining interpretability and avoiding the curse of dimensionality. The connection to graphical models is well-explained, though the paper appropriately distinguishes its focus on group additive structures rather than conditional dependence structures.

The paper's main strengths include:
- A clear and rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure
- A novel complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs
- Comprehensive theoretical guarantees (consistency results)
- Practical algorithms with empirical validation on both synthetic and real data
- Clear exposition of the method's advantages over existing approaches

The paper has some notable weaknesses:
- The writing contains numerous typos and grammatical errors that distract from the technical content (e.g., "on RHS" instead of "on the RHS," "turning" instead of "tuning")
- Equation (3) is missing the summation over i in the empirical risk term
- The "almost surely" claim on Line 169 is confusing without clarification about the limiting behavior
- The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) may not find the optimal structure due to its greedy nature, as noted in the paper
- The computational complexity becomes prohibitive for very large p, which is acknowledged but not deeply addressed

The paper is well-motivated and makes a meaningful contribution to nonparametric regression. The problem is significant because group additive structures provide a useful middle ground between standard additive models and high-order interaction models. The proposed method offers a practical approach to identifying these structures, which could be valuable for practitioners working with high-dimensional data. The theoretical results are strong, and the empirical evaluation is thorough.

For acceptance considerations:
Pro: The paper presents a novel, well-motivated method that addresses an important limitation in nonparametric regression. The theoretical results are sound, the method is practical, and the empirical validation is convincing. The problem is highly relevant to NIPS and the approach represents a significant advance over previous work.

Con: The paper contains numerous distracting typos and grammatical errors that undermine the professionalism of the presentation. The computational limitations for large p are acknowledged but not adequately addressed, and the stepwise algorithm's limitations could affect real-world applicability.

The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript, fix the technical errors (particularly Equation 3), and clarify the "almost surely" statement. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational scalability and potential extensions to handle larger problems.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with clear significance for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The method is well-founded theoretically and empirically, and the presentation, while marred by some technical errors, conveys a compelling approach to an important problem. The paper makes a valuable addition to the NIPS literature and should be accepted.
