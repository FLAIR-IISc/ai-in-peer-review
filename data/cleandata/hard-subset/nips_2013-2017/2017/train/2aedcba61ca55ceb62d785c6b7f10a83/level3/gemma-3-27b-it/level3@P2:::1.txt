This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, aiming to bridge the gap between purely additive models and those incorporating full interactions. The authors rigorously define an "intrinsic group additive structure" and develop a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous identification of this structure and nonparametric function estimation. A novel complexity measure, based on covering numbers, is derived to penalize group structures, and consistency results are presented.

The motivation for exploring group additive models is well-articulated. Standard additive models, while addressing the curse of dimensionality, ignore potentially important feature interactions. Functional ANOVA models address this, but quickly become computationally expensive as the number of features grows. Group additive models offer a middle ground, and the authors rightly point out the need for a principled way to *identify* these groups, rather than relying on prior knowledge.

The theoretical contributions are a key strength. The formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure provides a clear target for the proposed method. Deriving a complexity measure for group structures – albeit as an upper bound via covering numbers – is a significant step towards a theoretically grounded approach to structure selection. The consistency results are encouraging, demonstrating that the method can, under certain conditions, recover the true underlying structure.

However, several weaknesses detract from the paper's overall impact. A primary concern is the practical optimization of the proposed complexity measure. The authors acknowledge this, resorting to exhaustive search (which is intractable for even moderately sized problems) and a greedy forward-selection algorithm. The theoretical justification for the complexity measure is strong, but its practical utility is limited by the difficulty of efficiently minimizing it. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t explore alternative, potentially more tractable, complexity measures, despite briefly mentioning related concepts.

A significant shortcoming is the limited discussion of related work. While the authors cite relevant papers on nonparametric regression and functional ANOVA, the connections to broader areas such as structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection are largely missing. The paper acknowledges a connection to graphical models, but frames it in a way that diminishes the relevance of existing literature. For example, the work on sparse block structure in graphical models ([9] cited) is highly relevant and deserves more detailed discussion. Similarly, the authors could benefit from acknowledging connections to feature selection methods that aim to identify relevant subsets of features, with the group structure representing a higher-level feature grouping. This lack of comprehensive related work weakens the originality claim and potentially overlooks alternative approaches.

The experimental evaluation is also weak. The simulation studies are limited in scope, and the real data application (Boston Housing) lacks a comparison to any baseline methods. It is difficult to assess the performance of the proposed method without knowing how it compares to existing approaches. Simply demonstrating that the method can recover the true structure in a limited set of synthetic examples and on a single dataset is insufficient. The lack of comparison to other techniques leaves the reader wondering whether the benefits of the proposed method outweigh its computational complexity.

**Strengths:**

*   Theoretically sound approach to identifying group additive structures.
*   Rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure.
*   Novel complexity measure (albeit with limitations).
*   Consistency results providing theoretical guarantees.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Computational intractability of the complexity measure optimization.
*   Limited discussion of related work, particularly in structured sparsity and graphical models.
*   Weak experimental evaluation with a lack of baselines.
*   Greedy algorithm may be sub-optimal.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   The theoretical contributions are valuable and provide a foundation for future research.
*   The proposed method offers a principled approach to structure selection.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The computational limitations of the method significantly reduce its practical utility.
*   The limited related work discussion and lack of comparative evaluation weaken the paper's impact.
*   The experiments are insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in real-world scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem. However, the practical limitations of the method, coupled with the weak experimental evaluation and insufficient related work discussion, raise concerns about its overall impact. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the paper falls short of demonstrating a significant advancement in the field.

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. Specifically, the authors should explore more efficient optimization strategies for the complexity measure, provide a more comprehensive discussion of related work, and conduct a thorough experimental evaluation with comparisons to relevant baseline methods.
