This paper studies high-dimensional nonparametric regression with interactions by grouping features into low-dimensional blocks. The authors formalize a “group additive” model f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables, and introduce the intrinsic group additive structure G*, the minimal (finest) partition among all partitions that can represent f. They construct a structure-penalized kernel estimator over the direct-sum RKHS HG and derive a complexity penalty C(G) from covering-number bounds of these RKHSs, yielding an objective that trades off fit, RKHS norm regularization, and group-structure complexity. They prove that, with suitable tuning, the selected partition Ĝ is consistent for G*. The paper proposes two search strategies over partitions (exhaustive and backward stepwise), and reports small-scale simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work: The work sits between classical additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), SS-ANOVA (Gu), and higher-order interaction RKHS expansions (e.g., ANOVA/ESP kernels). It is close in spirit to OKGT (Pan et al.), and to structured sparsity/MKL approaches where kernels or groups are selected/weighted (sparse additive models; group/overlapping group lasso; MKL for additive components). It also relates to interaction selection under heredity constraints (e.g., Bien et al., hierarchical lasso) and to structure learning in probabilistic graphical models and block-structured covariance estimation. The paper would benefit from deeper engagement with these literatures (see comments below).

Quality: 
- Theory: The notion of amiable structures and the existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic partition are clean and useful. The capacity-based penalty is motivated by covering number bounds for direct sum RKHSs, leading to C(G) ≈ ∑u α|u|+1. The selection consistency result (Theorem 4) is valuable, but rests on several choices and assumptions that need to be stated more explicitly: bounded/universal kernels; conditions on PX; noise assumptions; whether independence across covariates is required beyond Prop. 1; and the behavior of λn, µn. The role and practical meaning of α (tied to the approximation scale ε) is not fully transparent, and the transition from C(G)=∑u α|u|+1 to the simplified penalty in Eq. (6) is confusing (it looks like α|u| rather than α|u|+1). Clarifying constants, scaling, and what users actually tune would greatly improve reproducibility.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search scales exponentially in p; the backward stepwise procedure is heuristic with no approximation guarantees. There is no discussion of time complexity, pruning strategies, or certificates. In high dimensions these limitations are serious.
- Empirics: The simulations are small and do not compare against meaningful baselines (SS-ANOVA; SALSA; OKGT; hierarchical/interaction lasso; additive MKL; tree-based methods for interaction discovery). The Boston Housing example is illustrative but does not measure recovery or predictive gains. Sensitivity to λ, µ, α is only lightly explored; the stepwise failures (Model 4) highlight the fragility of the search.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, but Section 2.3.2 would benefit from a more concrete, example-driven exposition of the penalty, its units, and how α is chosen in practice (currently tied to ε in the covering number). Please unify notation around G ≤ G′ and HG vs L2G. State all assumptions up-front for Propositions/Theorems. Provide pseudo-code with complexities and clear hyperparameter selection protocols.

Originality:
- The definition of an intrinsic group additive structure and its identification via a capacity-based structural penalty is novel and interesting. However, the optimization approach (search over partitions with penalized risk) and the general idea of penalizing higher-order group interactions connect strongly to established structured sparsity and MKL literatures, which are not sufficiently discussed or contrasted.

Significance:
- If scalable and robust, identifying minimal interaction groups could be impactful for interpretability and efficiency. In its current form, the lack of scalable optimization and the limited experimental validation reduce practical significance.

Specific suggestions:
- Strengthen related work: structured sparsity (group/overlapping group lasso; tree/graph-guided lasso), sparse additive models, MKL for additive/interaction kernels, hierarchical interaction selection with heredity, and structure learning in graphical models.
- Optimization: Consider convex/continuous relaxations by placing sparse, group-structured penalties over a library of candidate group kernels (e.g., MKL with ℓ1/ℓp over groups; overlapping groups for all pairs/tuples up to size D), or use cardinality-based submodular penalties over partitions to enable greedy/divisive clustering with approximation guarantees. Alternative searches: agglomerative/beam search on the partition lattice with MDL/BIC-style criteria; stochastic search (e.g., MCMC) over partitions; mixed-integer formulations with cutting planes; or differentiable assignment relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) with annealing and rounding.
- Penalty calibration: Provide a data-driven scheme to map α to sample size, kernel bandwidth, and noise level; report sensitivity; justify the simplification in Eq. (6).
- Experiments: Add baselines (SS-ANOVA, SALSA, OKGT, hierarchical lasso, additive MKL, boosted trees/GBDT for interaction discovery). Include correlated covariates, higher p (≥50–200), misspecified scenarios, and recovery metrics (Rand index/VI over partitions), with runtime. Ablate search strategies and penalties.

Pros:
- Clear, useful formalization of intrinsic group additive structure.
- Capacity-based penalty with selection consistency.
- Conceptually appealing middle ground between additive models and full ANOVA expansions.

Cons:
- Optimization does not scale; greedy search has no guarantees.
- Empirical evaluation is thin and lacks baselines.
- Related work coverage omits key connections to structured sparsity and MKL.
- Practical guidance on tuning and penalty calibration is unclear.

Post-rebuttal note: While the clarifications helped with some theoretical details, the empirical comparisons and related-work discussion remain insufficient. I encourage a substantial revision addressing optimization scalability, stronger baselines, and a more complete positioning within structured sparsity/MKL/interaction-selection literatures.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The core idea is promising and, with stronger optimization, empirical validation, and contextualization, could become a solid contribution.
