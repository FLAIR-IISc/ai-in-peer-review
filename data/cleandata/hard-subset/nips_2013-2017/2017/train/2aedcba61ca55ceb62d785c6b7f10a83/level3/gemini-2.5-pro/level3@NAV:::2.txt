This paper addresses the important problem of group-additive nonparametric regression, where the goal is to simultaneously learn the underlying group structure of predictor variables and estimate the regression function. This extends classical additive models, which ignore interactions, and functional ANOVA models, which can become overly complex. The authors propose a novel structure-penalized kernel ridge regression method. The key innovation is a penalty on the group structure's complexity, elegantly derived from the covering numbers of the corresponding direct-sum Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The paper provides two optimization algorithms, a theoretical analysis establishing the consistency of the group structure identification, and experiments on synthetic and real data.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—quantifying the complexity of a group partition via the capacity of its direct-sum RKHS—is novel and theoretically well-grounded. This provides a principled way to navigate the model selection problem between underfitting (standard additive models) and overfitting (fully nonparametric models). If the approach proves scalable, it could become a significant tool for interpretable high-dimensional regression.
2.  **Quality:** The paper is a comprehensive piece of work. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the claims are supported by both strong theoretical results (consistency of the estimated group structure) and empirical evidence. The inclusion of both exhaustive and stepwise algorithms shows consideration for practical application.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Scalability:** The main limitation, which the authors acknowledge, is the computational difficulty of optimizing over the combinatorial space of group structures. The proposed backward stepwise algorithm is a pragmatic heuristic, but its greedy nature offers no guarantees. The method's applicability to problems with a large number of variables (`p`) remains a concern.
2.  **Clarity:** While the core ideas are generally clear, the paper is marred by a significant number of typos and grammatical errors that detract from its quality and readability. A thorough proofreading is required.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
*   Is there a simpler way to characterize the interaction between two variables (e.g., `X_i` and `X_j`) without fitting the full group additive model in Eq. (1)? This could be useful for diagnostics.
*   Line 169: Please clarify the use of "almost surely." Is this with respect to the data-generating distribution, or is it intended in an asymptotic sense as `n → ∞`?
*   Equation (3) appears to be missing a summation over the data points `i=1` to `n` in the first term.
*   Line 205: Consider using the more standard term "translation invariant kernel" instead of "convolutional kernel."
