

This paper addresses the important problem of identifying group-additive structures in nonparametric regression, where predictor variables are partitioned into groups and the group structure must be learned from data. The authors rigorously define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, resolving identifiability issues present in prior work. They propose a novel method that combines kernel ridge regression with a structural penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, providing a theoretically sound complexity measure for group structures. The paper develops two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) to solve the joint optimization problem and establishes consistency results showing the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure with probability approaching 1 as sample size increases.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with comprehensive consistency proofs and solid empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets. The key innovation—quantifying group structure complexity via covering numbers of direct-sum RKHS—represents a significant advancement over previous approaches that either assumed fixed group structures or used ad-hoc methods. The method effectively bridges the gap between standard additive models and full functional ANOVA models by allowing controlled interaction patterns.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The writing contains numerous typos and grammatical errors that undermine clarity (e.g., "on RHS" instead of "on the RHS," missing summation in Equation 3). The computational complexity becomes prohibitive for large numbers of variables, a significant limitation for high-dimensional applications. While the theoretical results are strong, the connection to recent NIPS work on similar problems (e.g., [6], [9]) is incomplete.

The paper excels in originality and significance, addressing a meaningful gap in nonparametric regression with a novel theoretical framework. The minor issues with writing and computational scalability do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution. With corrections to the typos and minor technical errors, this would be a strong NIPS paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
