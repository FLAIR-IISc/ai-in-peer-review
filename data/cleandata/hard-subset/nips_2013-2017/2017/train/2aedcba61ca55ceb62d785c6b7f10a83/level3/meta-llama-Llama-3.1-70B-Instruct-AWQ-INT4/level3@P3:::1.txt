This paper proposes a novel approach to high-dimensional kernel regression by introducing a group additive model that groups features to model correlations ignored by standard additive models. The authors provide a formal treatment of the problem and propose ad hoc algorithms for finding optimal feature groupings. The paper's main contribution is the definition of a complexity measure derived as an upper bound on the covering number, which is used as a penalty term in the kernel method.

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method through simulation studies and real data applications. The writing is clear, and the organization is logical, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the issue of non-uniqueness of the group additive structure, which is a common problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors propose a rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and develop a method to identify it.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The complexity measure proposed by the authors is not easily optimizable, and the authors resort to exhaustive search or a greedy forward-selection approach. This may not be practical for large-scale problems. Additionally, the paper's coverage of related work is poor, missing links to structured sparsity, graphical models/Bayesian networks, and feature selection literature.

The experimental results are also limited, with small synthetic cases and only one real data application (Boston Housing). There are no baselines or comparisons to other methods, making it difficult to assess the performance of the proposed method.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to high-dimensional kernel regression, but the idea of using a group additive model is not new. The paper's contribution lies in the formal treatment of the problem and the proposal of a complexity measure as a penalty term.

The significance of the paper is moderate, as it addresses a common problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. However, the paper's impact is limited by its lack of comparison to other methods and its limited experimental results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to high-dimensional kernel regression.
* The authors provide a formal treatment of the problem and propose ad hoc algorithms for finding optimal feature groupings.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method through simulation studies and real data applications.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The complexity measure proposed by the authors is not easily optimizable.
* The paper's coverage of related work is poor.
* The experimental results are limited, with small synthetic cases and only one real data application.
* There are no baselines or comparisons to other methods.

Recommendation:

The paper is a good contribution to the field of high-dimensional kernel regression, but it needs to be improved in terms of its experimental results and coverage of related work. The authors should consider adding more experimental results, including comparisons to other methods, and provide a more comprehensive review of related work. With these improvements, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Rating: 7/10
