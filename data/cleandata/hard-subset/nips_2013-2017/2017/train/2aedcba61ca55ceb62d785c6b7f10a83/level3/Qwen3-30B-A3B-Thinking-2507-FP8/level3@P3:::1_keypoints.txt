- Proposes a group additive model for high-dimensional nonparametric regression with predictors partitioned into groups and additivity across groups.

- Defines the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group decomposition representing the true function, addressing non-uniqueness.

- Derives a complexity penalty based on covering numbers of RKHSs to select group structures and estimate functions via a structure-penalized kernel method.

- Positions group additive models between standard additive models and functional ANOVA, building on that literature.

- Proves consistency: the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases.

- Proposes two algorithms: exhaustive search for small feature sets and a backward stepwise greedy approach for larger problems.

- Strength: formal definition of intrinsic group additive structure is a valuable theoretical contribution.

- Strength: theoretical consistency proof is sound and well-executed.

- Strength: complexity measure derivation from covering numbers is mathematically rigorous and principled.

- Weakness: resulting optimization is intractable in high dimensions—exhaustive search scales exponentially and greedy stepwise has no guarantees.

- Weakness: related work coverage is inadequate, notably lacking connections to structured sparsity methods, Bayesian networks, and feature selection literatures.

- Weakness: experiments are insufficient—limited to small synthetic cases and Boston Housing with no baseline comparisons or large-scale validation.

- Weakness: insufficient justification for using covering-number-based complexity over alternatives like VC-dimension or Rademacher complexity.

- Quality assessment: strong theoretical work but practical applicability is weakened by algorithmic and experimental limitations.

- Clarity assessment: paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically accessible to experts.

- Originality assessment: specific theoretical novelty exists, but perceived novelty is reduced by poor engagement with related structured-sparsity literature.

- Significance assessment: important problem, but unclear practical impact due to poor scalability and limited validation.

- Pro acceptance argument: offers a clear formal framework and consistency guarantees for group additive structure identification.

- Con acceptance argument: lacks scalable optimization, robust experiments, and comprehensive related-work comparison, limiting practical usefulness.

- Conclusion: theoretically solid contribution but needs efficient optimization methods, stronger experiments and comparisons, and better literature integration before being publication-ready.
