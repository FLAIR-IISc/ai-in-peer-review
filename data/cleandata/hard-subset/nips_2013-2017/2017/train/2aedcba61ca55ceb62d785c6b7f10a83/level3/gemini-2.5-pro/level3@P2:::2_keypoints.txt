- Problem addressed: identify unknown partition (group additive structure) in high-dimensional nonparametric regression to capture interactions.  
- Main contribution: a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly estimates functions and the partition.  
- Formalization: defines "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique minimal partition representing the true function.  
- Structural penalty: derived from covering numbers of the direct-sum RKHS to quantify partition complexity.  
- Theoretical result: proves consistency in recovering the intrinsic group structure under suitable conditions (Theorem 4).  
- Optimization: proposes exhaustive search for small p and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p to handle combinatorial partition search.  
- Experiments: synthetic datasets (with known ground truth) and Boston Housing data demonstrate empirical effectiveness.  
- Strength — originality/significance: novel, well-motivated penalty linking RKHS capacity to model structure; potentially high impact for interpretable nonparametric models.  
- Strength — technical quality: clear formalization and strong theoretical guarantees supporting the method.  
- Strength — clarity: generally well-written with a clear motivation and explanation of the core idea.  
- Weakness — computational scalability: search over partitions is infeasible for large p (Bell number growth); stepwise heuristic is greedy with no optimality guarantees.  
- Weakness — presentation: multiple typos, grammatical errors, and a likely missing summation in Equation (3) reduce manuscript quality.  
- Weakness — minor theoretical ambiguity: need to clarify the meaning of "fG* = fG almost surely" (distributional vs. asymptotic interpretation).  
- Suggestions: consider pairwise interaction tests or other pruning/initialization to reduce search space and improve scalability.  
- Suggestions: explore alternative combinatorial optimization methods (e.g., MCMC or more advanced greedy algorithms) for better search performance.  
- Recommendation summary: strong, original paper worth accepting if computational limitations and clarity/typos are addressed; overall strengths outweigh weaknesses.
