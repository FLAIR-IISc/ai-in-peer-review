This paper addresses the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression by proposing a "group additive model." This model serves as a compromise between standard additive models, which are efficient but ignore variable interactions, and fully nonparametric models, which are flexible but suffer from the curse ofdimensionality. The core idea is to partition the predictor variables into groups, assuming an additive structure across groups while allowing for arbitrary nonparametric interactions within each group.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold. First, it provides a rigorous theoretical formalization of the problem by defining a unique, minimal "intrinsic group additive structure" for any given function. Second, it proposes a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this intrinsic structure and estimate the regression function. The key element here is a novel complexity penalty for group structures, which is derived as an upper bound on the covering number of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). Third, the authors prove that their proposed method is consistent in identifying the true intrinsic group structure. For implementation, the paper suggests an exhaustive search for a small number of predictors and a greedy backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical contributions. The formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure is elegant, and the derivation of a complexity penalty from the capacity measure of an RKHS (specifically, its covering number) provides a principled foundation for the method. The proof of selection consistency is a solid technical result that supports the proposed framework.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in its practical methodology and empirical validation. The proposed complexity penalty results in a challenging combinatorial optimization problem. The authors' solutions—exhaustive search or a simple greedy stepwise algorithm—are not scalable and feel ad-hoc. It is disappointing that the authors did not explore whether their complexity measure could be relaxed or reformulated to allow for more efficient optimization, nor did they discuss alternative, potentially more tractable, complexity measures.

The experimental evaluation is insufficient. The synthetic experiments are small in scale, and the application to the Boston Housing dataset is a standard exercise. Critically, the experiments lack any baselines or comparisons to alternative methods. Without comparing against functional ANOVA models, sparse interaction models (e.g., SALSA [6]), or even simpler additive models, it is impossible to gauge the practical significance or superiority of the proposed approach. The claims of "effectiveness" are not substantiated by the evidence provided.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and the theoretical development in Section 2 is clear. However, the organization could be improved by providing a more thorough discussion of the broader context. The connection to graphical models is mentioned only in passing, which is a missed opportunity to clarify the method's relationship to a well-known area of structure learning.

**Originality:**
The idea of identifying groups of interacting variables is not entirely new, but the specific formalization of an "intrinsic structure" and the use of a covering number-based penalty is novel. The theoretical framework is original and interesting.

That said, the paper's originality is diminished by its poor engagement with related literature. There are strong connections to the vast literature on structured sparsity (e.g., group Lasso, overlapping group Lasso), which deals with learning structural assumptions from data. The problem is also closely related to structure learning in graphical models and Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not explored. This lack of context makes it difficult to assess how this work differs from and advances upon previous contributions in learning sparse, structured models.

**Significance:**
The problem addressed is of high significance for the NIPS community, as modeling interactions in high-dimensional data is a central challenge. The theoretical ideas presented are potentially valuable and could inspire future work.

However, the paper's current impact is limited by the weaknesses outlined above. The inefficient optimization algorithms and the lack of compelling empirical evidence mean that practitioners are unlikely to adopt this method in its current form. The insufficient discussion of related work also prevents researchers from easily building upon these ideas or understanding their place within the existing landscape of tools for high-dimensional regression. While subsequent clarifications from the authors might address some minor points, the fundamental shortcomings in the experimental design and literature review remain. A much stronger treatment of related work and a more rigorous experimental comparison against existing methods would be necessary to demonstrate a significant contribution.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of modeling variable interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure."
*   The proposed structure penalty is derived from first principles (RKHS covering numbers), and the authors provide a proof of consistency for structure selection.

**Con:**
*   The optimization procedure relies on exhaustive or greedy search, which scales poorly and limits the practical utility of the method.
*   The experimental evaluation is extremely thin and lacks any baselines, making it impossible to assess the method's performance relative to existing approaches.
*   The paper fails to connect its contributions to extensive and highly relevant bodies of work in structured sparsity, graphical model learning, and feature interaction selection, thus overstating its novelty and failing to properly contextualize the work.
