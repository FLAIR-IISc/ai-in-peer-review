This paper addresses the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression under a group-additive structure, where the predictor variables are partitioned into groups, and the regression function is a sum of functions applied to each group. The central challenge, which this paper tackles, is that this group structure is unknown and must be learned from the data. This problem sits in a useful middle ground between standard additive models, which forbid interactions, and fully nonparametric models or functional ANOVA models, which can be computationally and statistically intractable due to the curse of dimensionality.

The main contribution is a novel, principled method for simultaneously identifying the group structure and estimating the within-group functions. The authors formalize the notion of a unique, minimal "intrinsic group additive structure." The core of their method is a new penalty term, incorporated into a kernel ridge regression framework, that quantifies the complexity of a given group structure. This complexity measure is derived from the covering numbers of the direct-sum Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) associated with a particular partition of variables. The authors propose two algorithms to solve the resulting non-convex optimization problem: an exhaustive search for small problems and a greedy backward stepwise algorithm for larger ones.

The work is supported by a solid theoretical analysis, proving that under suitable conditions, the empirical risk converges to the true risk and, more importantly, that the proposed method is consistent—the probability of identifying the true intrinsic group structure converges to one as the sample size increases. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic datasets, where it successfully recovers known group structures, and on the Boston Housing dataset, yielding interpretable results.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The key innovation is the formulation of a complexity measure for group structures based on the covering numbers of the corresponding RKHS. This provides a theoretically grounded way to navigate the trade-off between model fit and structural simplicity, which is a significant contribution to the literature on structured nonparametric regression. The problem itself is important, offering a practical compromise between the inflexibility of additive models and the intractability of models allowing all interactions.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The definition of an "intrinsic group additive structure" clarifies the identifiability problem noted in prior work. The consistency result (Theorem 4) is strong and provides confidence that the method is well-behaved asymptotically.
3.  **Comprehensive Work:** The paper is a complete piece of research, presenting a clear problem definition, a novel methodological solution, practical algorithms, strong theoretical guarantees, and empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary limitation is the difficulty of optimizing over the space of partitions. The number of ways to partition `p` variables (the Bell number) grows super-exponentially. While acknowledged by the authors, the exhaustive search algorithm is only feasible for very small `p`. The proposed backward stepwise algorithm is a reasonable heuristic but is greedy and lacks guarantees of finding the optimal structure, a point that could be emphasized more strongly. The performance on Model 4 in the simulations suggests it can fail to find the true structure.
2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's quality is significantly diminished by numerous typos and grammatical errors. While the core ideas are understandable, these errors make the paper harder to read and suggest a lack of careful proofreading. This is a correctable but notable flaw.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions:**

*   **Minor Question:** The paper frames variable interaction in terms of appearing in the same group within the model of Equation (1). Is there a simpler, model-free characterization of interaction that this method captures? For instance, can the "intrinsic" structure be defined directly in terms of properties of the true function `f`, independent of the RKHS formulation?
*   **Clarification on L169:** The phrase "almost surely (in population)" is used when stating `f_G* = f_G`. Could you clarify the meaning here? Is this with respect to the data generating distribution `P_X`? Later in the theory section, you move to probabilistic statements about convergence as `n -> ∞`. The use of "almost surely" in a non-asymptotic context is slightly confusing.
*   **Technical Error:** Equation (3) appears to be missing a summation over the samples `i` from 1 to `n` in the first term, `(1/n) * sum_{i=1 to n} ...`. Please double-check this expression.
*   **Terminology Suggestion (L205):** The term "convolution kernel" is used. While technically correct for `K(x, t) = k(x-t)`, the more standard term in the kernel literature for such kernels is "translation-invariant" or "stationary" kernel. Using this standard terminology would improve clarity.
*   **Typos and Grammatical Fixes:** The manuscript would benefit greatly from a thorough proofread. Here are some examples I caught:
    *   L145: "on RHS" -> "on the RHS"
    *   L152: "the L2 distance between any function fG ∈ HG and the true function f will be bounded below." -> "...will be bounded away from zero." or "...will have a positive lower bound." (for clarity)
    *   L160: "...the intrinsic group additive structure G* can achieve the goodness of fit represented by the first two terms in (3) and the penalty on the structural complexity represented by the last term." This sentence is confusingly structured. Suggest rephrasing, e.g., "...G* is expected to perform well on the combined objective, as it achieves good model fit (first two terms) while having a low complexity penalty (last term)."
    *   L247: "turning parameters" -> "tuning parameters"
    *   Algorithm 2, Line 1: "State with" -> "Start with"
    *   L251: "by compare" -> "by comparing"

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to structured nonparametric regression. The method is well-motivated, and the theoretical and empirical support is solid. The main drawbacks are the practical scalability of the optimization and the large number of presentational errors. Assuming the typos and technical errors can be easily fixed, the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.
