This paper studies nonparametric regression with group-additive structure, where predictors are partitioned into disjoint groups and the regression function is a sum of multivariate components over groups. The authors (i) formalize the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure as the unique minimal partition (under a natural refinement partial order) among all partitions that admit the true regression function; (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly estimates the function and the group partition by adding a penalty on the capacity of the direct-sum RKHS induced by a partition; (iii) derive a tractable complexity measure via covering-number bounds for RKHS balls, leading to a penalty of the form sum_u α|u|; (iv) provide selection consistency for recovering the intrinsic partition; and (v) give two algorithms (exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise split heuristic), supported by simulations and a real-data application. The work is positioned between classical additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) that ignore interactions and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA; SALSA/ESP kernels, Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), and connects to (but is distinct from) OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15) and graphical models: it targets the partition structure directly rather than conditional dependence.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of group structure with a well-defined intrinsic (minimal) partition, supported by a neat lattice-theoretic argument and L2/RKHS inclusions.
- Novel complexity penalty tied to RKHS capacity of direct sums; the reduction from covering-number upper bounds to a simple penalty depending on group sizes is elegant and practically usable.
- Consistency for structure recovery under universal kernels and appropriate tuning, with careful separation of amiable vs non-amiable partitions.
- Practical algorithms covering small p (exhaustive) and moderate/large p (backward stepwise), with demonstrations on synthetic data and a real dataset.
- Good discussion of relation to ANOVA kernels/SALSA and graphical models, clarifying the problem setting and contribution.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scalability: optimizing over partitions is combinatorial (Bell numbers). The stepwise procedure is greedy and can miss the true structure (as seen in Model 4). More robust search (merge–split, beam search, MCMC over partitions) or relaxations would strengthen applicability.
- Penalty calibration: α arises from reparameterizing covering-number bounds and is not directly interpretable; sensitivity to α and λ is not fully explored. Interactions between kernel hyperparameters, λ, µ, and α deserve more discussion.
- Assumptions and conditions: some theoretical statements rely on universal kernels, boundedness, and independence (for strict inclusions). These should be made explicit where used (e.g., in Prop. 1, Theorems 2–4).
- Empirical evaluation: comparisons to strong baselines are limited. Including GAM/SpAM, spline-ANOVA, SALSA/ESP kernels, OKGT, and hierarchical kernel learning (HKL) would contextualize gains in both prediction and structure recovery.
- Writing: generally clear but there are several typos and a few technical imprecisions (listed below).

Quality
- The methodology is technically sound. The capacity-based penalty is well-motivated from covering-number bounds for translation-invariant kernels, and the direct-sum covering argument (Prop. 3) is reasonable. The selection consistency result is valuable, though high-level; a brief discussion of how rates depend on group sizes and kernel smoothness would help. Empirical results are supportive but could be stronger with broader baselines and larger p.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well organized. The intrinsic structure and amiable vs non-amiable definitions are clear. The derivation of the penalty would benefit from a compact roadmap (from (4) to C(G) and then to (6)). Please clarify the role and interpretation of α, and explicitly state the assumptions near each main result. Fix the typos listed below.

Originality
- The idea of penalizing group partitions via RKHS capacity of direct sums appears novel. While additive and ANOVA kernel decompositions are well studied, learning a partition of inputs with an RKHS capacity penalty is a fresh contribution. The identification of an intrinsic partition is also a useful conceptual advance.

Significance
- If the approach scales, it could be a useful addition for modeling interactions without exploding the number of terms. The theory provides reassurance about identifiability. With stronger baselines and improved large-p search, impact could be substantial.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually clean problem formulation (intrinsic partition).
- Novel, principled penalty; solid consistency result.
- Practical algorithms and positive experiments.

Cons:
- Limited scalability and occasional failure of the greedy search.
- Sparse empirical comparisons; sensitivity to tuning not fully assessed.
- Some notation/terminology imprecision and typos.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Interaction characterization: A simple criterion to decide whether two variables i and j “interact” without committing to a full partition is to test whether the best bivariate function over {i,j} significantly improves over the sum of best univariate functions: compare inf_{f_ij∈H_{ij}} R(f_ij) versus inf_{f_i∈H_i, f_j∈H_j} R(f_i+f_j). In practice, fit both with KRR and use a held-out risk difference or a permutation test (related to HSIC) to decide if they should be grouped.
- “Almost surely” (Line 169): do you mean equality in L2(P_X) (i.e., functions equal almost everywhere in population), not a statement as n→∞? Please clarify the measure-theoretic sense and separate it from asymptotics.

Specific technical/terminology issues and typos
- Equation (3): ensure the empirical risk includes the summation over i; some instances appear to omit it.
- Line 205: use “translation-invariant kernel” rather than “convolution kernel.”
- Line 145: “on RHS” → “on the RHS.”
- Line 152 and Line 160: fix grammar (subject–verb agreement).
- Line 247: “turning” → “tuning.”
- Algorithm 2, Line 1: “State with” → “Start with.”
- Line 251: “by compare” → “by comparing.”
- Notational clarity: denote the sample-size–dependent λ as λ_n where needed (e.g., Theorem 4’s condition λ_n·n → 0). Define all constants in the bounds and the dependence of C(G) on kernel parameters and input dimension.
- Assumptions in Prop. 1: explicitly state independence when invoking strict inclusion L^2_{G1} ⊂ L^2_{G2}.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines (GAM/SpAM, spline ANOVA, SALSA/ESP, OKGT, HKL) and report both structure recovery (e.g., VI distance between partitions) and prediction.
- Explore more scalable search (merge–split stochastic search, beam search, or convex surrogates for partitions) and report runtime vs p.
- Discuss how kernel choice and bandwidth interact with structure learning; consider automatic relevance determination within groups.
- Consider alternative capacity penalties (localized Rademacher, Gaussian complexities) and whether they yield better finite-sample behavior.

Overall, a well-motivated and theoretically grounded contribution; with strengthened experiments and improved large-p search, this would be a solid addition.
