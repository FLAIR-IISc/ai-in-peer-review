

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses a significant gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression by proposing a method to simultaneously identify group additive structures and estimate nonparametric functions. The authors build upon the group additive model introduced by Pan et al. (2011), which generalizes the standard additive model by allowing interactions within groups while maintaining interpretability. The key contribution is the rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal group structure that correctly represents the function) and a novel penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls to identify this structure.

The paper makes several important technical contributions. First, it formally defines the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proves its uniqueness, addressing a critical gap in previous work. Second, the authors derive a tractable complexity measure for group structures using covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs, which is then incorporated into a structure-penalized kernel ridge regression framework (Equation 6). Third, they prove consistency of the estimated group structure (Theorem 4), showing that the probability of identifying the true structure approaches 1 as sample size increases. Finally, they propose two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) to solve the combinatorial optimization problem.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations and empirical validation. The theory is well-motivated, with careful handling of the distinction between amiable and non-amiable group structures. The simulation studies (Section 4) and real data application (Section 5) provide compelling evidence that the method successfully identifies true group structures and produces interpretable models. The Boston Housing analysis is particularly compelling, with the group structure revealing meaningful patterns in the relationship between predictors and house values.

The paper's main innovation lies in quantifying group structure complexity via covering numbers of the direct-sum RKHS, which provides a principled way to balance model fit and structural complexity. This approach is conceptually elegant and addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

Several strengths make this paper stand out. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical development is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from related work in graphical models (Section 1), emphasizing that their method directly targets group additive structures rather than the more complex conditional dependence structures.

However, the paper has some notable limitations and issues. The main limitation is the computational complexity of the group structure search, which becomes prohibitive as the number of variables increases (Section 2.4). While the backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) addresses this to some extent, it remains a significant constraint for very large-scale problems.

The paper contains several technical errors and writing issues that undermine its quality. The most serious is the missing summation over i in Equation (3), which appears to be a critical typo. Additionally, the use of "convolutional kernel" (Line 205) is technically inaccurate; "translation invariant kernel" would be more precise. The phrase "almost surely" (Line 169) is ambiguous without clarification of whether it refers to n → ∞. The paper contains numerous other typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "on RHS" instead of "on the RHS" on Line 145).

The authors could have strengthened their work by addressing two minor but important points. First, they ask for a simple characterization of interaction between two variables that doesn't require the full model (Equation 1), which would be valuable for interpretability. Second, they could have more clearly defined the relationship between their group additive structure and the conditional dependence structure in graphical models.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The theoretical framework is sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear practical utility. Despite the writing issues and computational limitations, the paper advances the state of the art in group additive modeling. The authors have addressed the key theoretical gap of identifying the intrinsic group structure, which previous work (Pan et al., 2011) had not resolved.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with strong theoretical justification and empirical validation (Quality); it is well-organized with clear exposition (Clarity); it makes a novel contribution by formalizing the intrinsic group structure and deriving a tractable complexity measure (Originality); and it addresses an important problem in high-dimensional regression with potential for broad impact (Significance).

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical errors and writing issues. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field and would be a strong addition to NIPS 2017.
