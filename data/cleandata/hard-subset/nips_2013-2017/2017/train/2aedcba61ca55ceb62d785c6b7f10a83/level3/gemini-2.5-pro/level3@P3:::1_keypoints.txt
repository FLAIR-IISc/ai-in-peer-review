- Proposes identifying a "group additive structure" for high-dimensional nonparametric regression as a middle ground between additive and fully nonparametric models.
- Defines an "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique minimal partition representing the true function.
- Introduces a structure-penalized kernel method with penalty C(G) derived from an RKHS covering-number upper bound.
- Penalty C(G) favors many small groups, promoting parsimony.
- Optimization is done by exhaustive search (impractical) or a greedy backward stepwise algorithm.
- Provides theoretical consistency guarantees for the estimator of the group structure.
- Strength: tackles an important problem with a clean formalization of intrinsic group additive structure.
- Strength: principled derivation of a complexity penalty from function-space capacity (RKHS covering number).
- Weakness: major scalability gap—complexity measure is hard to optimize; exhaustive search infeasible and greedy search has no optimality guarantees and can fail.
- Weakness: empirical validation is insufficient—no baselines and experiments limited to small synthetic data and Boston Housing.
- Weakness: related-work discussion is shallow, missing connections to structured sparsity, graphical model structure learning, and interaction-detection methods like MARS.
- Concern: choice of covering-number-based C(G) seems arbitrary; alternative capacity measures might yield more tractable penalties.
- Concern: backward stepwise algorithm is underspecified and not reproducible (unclear move set and evaluation at each step).
- Concern: Boston Housing results are post-hoc without external validation or comparison, so interpretability is questionable.
- Conclusion: theoretically interesting but incomplete—needs scalable optimization methods, comprehensive empirical comparisons to baselines, and a much deeper literature contextualization.
