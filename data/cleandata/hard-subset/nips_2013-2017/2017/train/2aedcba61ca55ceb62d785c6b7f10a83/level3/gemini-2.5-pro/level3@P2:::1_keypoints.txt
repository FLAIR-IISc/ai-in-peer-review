- Problem: Introduces "group additive model" to trade off between additive models and fully nonparametric models by grouping predictors to allow within-group interactions.
- Core idea: Partition predictors into groups, assume additivity across groups, allow arbitrary nonparametric interactions within groups.
- Contribution 1: Defines a unique, minimal "intrinsic group additive structure" for any function.
- Contribution 2: Proposes a structure-penalized kernel method to jointly identify structure and estimate the regression function.
- Contribution 3: Derives a complexity penalty based on an upper bound on the RKHS covering number and proves selection consistency for the method.
- Implementation: Suggests exhaustive search for small p and a greedy backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems.
- Strength (theoretical): Solid theoretical formalization and principled complexity penalty; elegant derivations and consistency proof.
- Weakness (optimization): Resulting combinatorial optimization is challenging; exhaustive and greedy solutions are unscalable and ad-hoc.
- Weakness (penalty/design): Authors did not explore relaxing or reformulating the complexity measure for more tractable optimization.
- Weakness (experiments): Empirical evaluation is insufficient—small synthetic tests, only Boston Housing dataset, and no baseline comparisons.
- Weakness (comparisons): Lacks comparisons to functional ANOVA, sparse interaction models (e.g., SALSA), and simpler additive models, so practical efficacy is unproven.
- Clarity: Well-written theoretical sections but would benefit from broader contextual discussion; connection to graphical models is only briefly mentioned.
- Originality: Novel formalization of intrinsic structure and RKHS covering-number penalty, but idea of grouping interactions is not entirely new.
- Literature engagement: Poor engagement with related work on structured sparsity, overlapping group Lasso, and graphical model structure learning.
- Significance: Addresses an important problem with potentially valuable theory, but limited practical impact due to optimization and empirical weaknesses.
- Decision arguments — Pro: Tackles important problem, offers novel formalization and principled penalty with consistency proof.
- Decision arguments — Con: Intractable optimization, thin empirical validation without baselines, and insufficient contextualization in related literature.
