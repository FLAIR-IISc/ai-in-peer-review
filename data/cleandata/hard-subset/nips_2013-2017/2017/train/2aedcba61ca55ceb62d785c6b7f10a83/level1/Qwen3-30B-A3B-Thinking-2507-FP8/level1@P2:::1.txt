

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: the limitation of standard additive models in capturing variable interactions while avoiding the curse of dimensionality. The authors propose a novel framework for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure, defined as the minimal group partition that can represent a target function. This work builds on the group additive model framework introduced by Pan et al. (2011), but makes significant contributions by rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure (addressing the non-uniqueness issue) and developing a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous structure identification and function estimation.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for the intrinsic group additive structure, proving its uniqueness (Theorem 1) and developing a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers (Section 2.3.2). Their theoretical contribution is solid, with a careful proof of consistency (Theorem 4) that builds on Propositions 1-4. The method is well-motivated and the experiments (Section 4-5) provide convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the stepwise algorithm's greedy nature) and carefully distinguishes their approach from related work on graphical models (Section 1). The technical depth and mathematical soundness are impressive.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define all necessary concepts (Definitions 1-2), build the argument step by step, and provide intuitive explanations of complex ideas (e.g., the relationship between group structures and function spaces). The connection to previous work is well-explained, and the paper avoids unnecessary jargon. The two-step estimation procedure (Algorithm 1) and the backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) are clearly described. The supplementary material would likely provide additional clarity for implementation details. The paper meets the standard for reproducibility by providing sufficient mathematical details.

**Originality:** This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature. While group additive models have been previously explored (Pan et al., 2011), the authors address a critical gap: the non-uniqueness of group additive structures. Their definition of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is novel and provides a principled way to resolve this ambiguity. The complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers (C(G) = ∑α|uj|) is a creative adaptation of existing capacity measures for RKHSs. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work on functional ANOVA models (e.g., [4, 13]) and graphical models (e.g., [2, 7, 9]), making it clear how their approach differs in both problem formulation and methodology.

**Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression with significant practical implications. The method provides a general tool that can handle high-dimensional data while capturing interactions between variables, which is particularly valuable in fields like genomics, finance, and social sciences where variable interactions are common but high dimensionality is a major challenge. The paper demonstrates effectiveness through both simulation studies (Section 4) and a real-world application to the Boston Housing dataset (Section 5). The results show that the method successfully recovers true group structures in various scenarios, with the backward stepwise algorithm making it applicable to larger problems. The approach bridges the gap between standard additive models (which ignore interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from high dimensionality).

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous mathematical foundation for the intrinsic group additive structure
- Novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers
- Strong theoretical consistency results
- Clear distinction from related work
- Comprehensive experimental validation
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) shows limitations in Model 4 (Fig. 2), suggesting room for improvement in the algorithm design
- The paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the method to the choice of kernel or the specific form of the complexity measure
- While the theoretical results are strong, the paper doesn't provide a thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the exhaustive search approach for large p

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
1. The paper addresses a well-defined gap in the literature with a technically sound solution
2. The intrinsic group additive structure concept is novel and well-motivated
3. Theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported
4. Experimental results demonstrate clear effectiveness
5. The work has clear potential for impact in high-dimensional regression applications

*Con:*
1. The stepwise algorithm's limitations for certain models suggest the method might not be universally robust
2. The paper doesn't fully explore the relationship between the complexity measure and practical implementation choices
3. The computational complexity of the exhaustive search approach may limit applicability to very large problems

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the literature, provided a rigorous solution with strong theoretical foundations, and demonstrated the approach's effectiveness through comprehensive experiments. While the stepwise algorithm has some limitations, these are acknowledged and do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in statistical learning with a novel methodological contribution.

The paper meets all four review criteria exceptionally well. It is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a genuine contribution beyond prior work (Originality), and addresses an important problem with significant potential impact (Significance). I recommend acceptance.
