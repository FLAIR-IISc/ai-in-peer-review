This paper tackles the problem of group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression, a crucial task for improving interpretability and efficiency in high-dimensional settings. The authors address the non-uniqueness of group additive structures by defining an “intrinsic” structure and propose a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous identification and function estimation. The novelty lies in the definition of a complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers and the resulting penalized regression framework.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction of the problem, a thorough review of related work (additive models, functional ANOVA, OKGT), and a rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure. The method is presented logically, building from kernel methods with known structure to the penalized approach for unknown structure. The theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency is a significant strength. The simulations and real data application provide empirical support for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and motivate the need for identifying the intrinsic group structure.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The consistency proof is a substantial contribution, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The use of covering numbers to define a complexity measure for group structures is innovative and provides a principled way to penalize model complexity.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The simulations cover a range of models, and the real data application demonstrates the practical applicability of the method.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing literature on additive models and graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is computationally expensive and may not scale well to very high-dimensional datasets. The stepwise algorithm is presented as a solution, but its performance appears limited based on the simulation results (Model 4).  More detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The performance of the method is sensitive to the choice of tuning parameters (λ, μ, α). While cross-validation is used, a more in-depth discussion of strategies for selecting these parameters would be helpful.
* **Kernel Choice:** The paper mentions universal kernels but doesn’t provide much guidance on kernel selection.  The impact of different kernel choices on the performance of the method is not explored.
* **Limited Exploration of Stepwise Algorithm:** The simulation results suggest that the stepwise algorithm performs poorly on Model 4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the reasons for this, and potential improvements to the algorithm.
* **Clarity of Proposition 1:** While the proposition is technically correct, its intuitive understanding could be enhanced with an example showing L2G1 ⊂ L2G2.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in nonparametric regression with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The consistency result provides a strong guarantee on the performance of the method.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a range of datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm may limit the applicability of the method to high-dimensional datasets.
* The sensitivity to tuning parameters and the lack of detailed guidance on parameter selection could be a barrier to practical use.
* The stepwise algorithm's performance isn't robust across all tested models, indicating potential limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical results are strong, and the empirical evidence supports the effectiveness of the proposed method. While there are some limitations related to computational cost and parameter tuning, these do not outweigh the significant contributions of the work. The paper is of high quality and is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on providing more guidance on parameter selection, discussing the computational complexity in greater detail, and exploring potential improvements to the stepwise algorithm.
