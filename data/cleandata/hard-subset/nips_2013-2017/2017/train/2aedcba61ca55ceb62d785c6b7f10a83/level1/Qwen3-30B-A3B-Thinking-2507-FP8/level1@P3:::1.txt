

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of additive models in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: their inability to capture interactions between predictors. The authors propose a novel method to identify the intrinsic group additive structure, which represents the minimal partition of predictor variables into groups where interactions occur only within groups. This work builds on the group additive model framework introduced by Pan et al. (2015) but makes a critical theoretical advance by rigorously defining the intrinsic group structure and developing a method to identify it.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's most significant strength is the rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and the proof of consistency for the proposed method (Theorem 4). This addresses a fundamental gap in previous work on group additive models where the structure was assumed known rather than identified. The authors effectively demonstrate that the intrinsic structure minimizes both model complexity and estimation error.

2. **Novel Complexity Measure:** The derivation of the complexity measure C(G) = ∑α^{|u_j|} based on RKHS covering numbers (Section 2.3.2) is a valuable contribution. This measure properly captures the dimensionality trade-off between group sizes and model complexity, which is crucial for the penalization approach.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) that make the method implementable for both small and large p. The theoretical justification for these algorithms (Section 2.4) is sound.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The simulation study (Section 4) demonstrates the method's ability to correctly identify group structures across multiple models, and the real data application (Section 5) provides concrete evidence of the method's utility in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Scalability:** While the backward stepwise algorithm addresses computational concerns, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence of scalability for very high-dimensional settings (p > 100). The computational complexity of the exhaustive search approach (O(2^p)) is well-known to be intractable for large p, and the stepwise approach's theoretical guarantees for large p are not fully established.

2. **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity:** The method depends on two tuning parameters (μ and α), and while the paper uses cross-validation for selection, it doesn't thoroughly investigate how sensitive the results are to these choices. The simulation results show that parameter selection can significantly impact performance (e.g., Model 1's deterioration in Table 2).

3. **Limited Real Data Application:** The Boston Housing application is valuable but limited to a single dataset. More extensive real-world validation across multiple domains would strengthen the case for practical utility.

4. **Connection to Related Work:** The paper briefly notes the connection to graphical models (Section 1) but doesn't fully explore how their method relates to existing work on sparse graphical models or conditional dependence structures. This is a missed opportunity to situate the contribution more precisely within the broader literature on high-dimensional structure learning.

5. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The consistency proofs (Theorems 2-4) rely on several technical assumptions (e.g., the RKHS being dense in L2, the specific form of the covering number bound). The paper could better discuss the practical implications of these assumptions and how they might be violated in real data.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed theoretical and methodological contribution to the field of high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The paper makes a clear, important advance by providing a principled way to identify the group additive structure rather than assuming it is known. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation is convincing for the problem at hand.

The method addresses a genuine limitation of additive models and provides a practical approach for high-dimensional settings where interactions are present but not all possible interactions need to be modeled. The intrinsic group additive structure concept is elegant and has the potential to improve both interpretability and estimation efficiency.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. While the computational scalability for extremely high dimensions could be improved, the authors provide a reasonable approach (stepwise algorithm) and acknowledge this limitation. The method is novel, well-motivated, and well-supported by theory and experiments. It advances the state of the art in high-dimensional nonparametric regression and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Rigorous definition of intrinsic group additive structure
- Consistency proof for structure identification
- Novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers
- Practical algorithms with good empirical performance
- Clear connection to important limitations of existing additive models

**Con Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Computational scalability for very large p remains a concern
- Tuning parameter sensitivity requires careful selection
- Limited real-world validation beyond one dataset
- Some theoretical assumptions could be better motivated

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), makes a clear theoretical contribution (Originality), and addresses an important problem with potential impact (Significance). The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
