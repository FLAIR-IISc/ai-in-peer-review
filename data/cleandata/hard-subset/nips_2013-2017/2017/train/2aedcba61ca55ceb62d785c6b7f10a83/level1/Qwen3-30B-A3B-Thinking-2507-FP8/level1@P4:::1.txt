

**Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying group additive structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group additive structure among all correct structures for a given function, resolving the non-uniqueness issue left unaddressed in prior work (Pan et al., 2015). They propose a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies the intrinsic group structure and estimates nonparametric functions, using a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers.

The paper builds on additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1986) and functional ANOVA models (Stone, 1985), but extends them by introducing the intrinsic group additive structure concept. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009), noting their method directly targets group additive structure rather than conditional dependence structure.

Key contributions include:
1. A rigorous definition of intrinsic group additive structure
2. A novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on RKHS covering numbers
3. Theoretical consistency proofs for the proposed method
4. Two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) for implementation
5. Empirical validation through simulation studies and real data applications

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results (Propositions 1-4, Theorems 2-4) are well-motivated and rigorously proven, showing that the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases. The complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers is novel and well-justified. The simulation studies (Table 2) demonstrate successful recovery of true group structures across multiple models, and the real data application on Boston Housing data provides meaningful insights.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with logical progression from problem definition to theoretical results to empirical validation. The algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2), and the connection between the theoretical development and practical implementation is well-maintained.

The originality is significant. While previous work on group additive models (Pan et al., 2015) acknowledged the non-uniqueness issue, this paper provides the first rigorous definition of the intrinsic structure and a method for its identification. The complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers represents a novel contribution to the literature on structure identification in nonparametric regression.

The significance of the work is high. The group additive model provides a middle ground between standard additive models (which ignore interactions) and full interaction models (which suffer from high dimensionality). The method enables practitioners to identify meaningful group structures that balance model complexity with interpretability in high-dimensional settings. The real data application demonstrates practical utility.

Strengths:
- Resolves a well-defined gap in the literature (non-uniqueness of group additive structures)
- Provides rigorous theoretical foundation with consistent proofs
- Develops a novel complexity measure based on RKHS theory
- Demonstrates empirical effectiveness across multiple models
- Clear writing with well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
- The exhaustive search algorithm's computational complexity may limit scalability to extremely high dimensions (though the backward stepwise approach partially addresses this)
- Simulation studies cover only five models, leaving open questions about performance on more complex structures
- Limited comparison with alternative structure identification methods for nonparametric regression

The paper makes a significant contribution to nonparametric regression methodology. It is technically sound, with clear theoretical results and empirical validation. The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is novel and well-justified, and the complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers represents a meaningful methodological advance. The method addresses a practical challenge in high-dimensional statistical modeling, with potential applications across multiple domains.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is of high technical quality, clearly written, makes a novel contribution to the field, and addresses an important problem with significant potential impact. It represents a substantial advance over previous work on group additive models and provides a valuable tool for high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
