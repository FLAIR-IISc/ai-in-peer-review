- Paper proposes a novel structure-penalized kernel method that defines intrinsic group additive structure (GAS) to model interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.  
- Introduces an RKHS-based complexity measure for GAS and proves consistency in identifying the intrinsic GAS.  
- Develops two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) and validates them via simulations and real data (e.g., Boston Housing).  

Strengths:  
- Novelty: Introduces intrinsic GAS to balance interpretability and flexibility beyond standard additive models.  
- Theoretical rigor: Provides solid proofs and a new RKHS covering-number complexity measure.  
- Practical applicability: Algorithms make the method feasible across problem scales and include real-data demonstration.  
- Empirical validation: Extensive simulations show successful GAS identification for small-to-moderate p.  

Weaknesses:  
- Computational scalability: Unclear performance for very high-dimensional data; exhaustive search infeasible for large p.  
- Sensitivity to tuning: Multiple tuning parameters (λ, µ, α) with seemingly ad hoc selection.  
- Limited real-world applications: More diverse datasets would strengthen empirical claims.  
- Algorithmic greediness: Backward stepwise can fail to recover true GAS in some scenarios.  

Pro:  
- Significant theoretical and methodological contribution with supporting consistency results and empirical evidence.  

Con:  
- Practical concerns about scalability, tuning robustness, and inconsistent backward stepwise performance.  

Recommendation:  
- Accept with minor revisions to address scalability, parameter tuning, and broader empirical validation.
