

This paper proposes a novel method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in nonparametric regression, addressing a key limitation of previous group additive models that lacked a rigorous definition of minimal structure and systematic identification procedures. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal amiable group partition, which resolves the non-uniqueness problem inherent in prior work (e.g., Pan et al., 2015). They develop a structure-penalized kernel method using a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers, proving consistency of the proposed approach. The method bridges the gap between simple additive models and full interaction models by accommodating structured interactions while maintaining estimation efficiency.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with rigorous proofs of consistency (Theorems 2-4) and practical validation through comprehensive simulation studies and a real-world application to Boston Housing data. The technical contributions are significant: the formal definition of intrinsic structure and the RKHS-based complexity measure represent meaningful advances beyond related work on group additive models (e.g., Marlin & Murphy, 2009) and functional ANOVA (e.g., Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990). The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition and precise mathematical formulations. The method is clearly explained through two algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) with practical implementation details.

Strengths: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by rigorously defining the intrinsic structure and providing a consistent estimation method. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness across diverse scenarios. The connection to RKHS theory is well-motivated and properly contextualized.

Weaknesses: The computational scalability of the exhaustive search method is limited to moderate p, though the stepwise algorithm partially addresses this. The theoretical results assume independent predictors, which may limit applicability to correlated data settings common in practice.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical contribution, rigorous proofs, strong empirical validation, clear exposition, and significant practical relevance for high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited discussion of computational constraints for large-scale applications and the assumption of independent predictors may restrict applicability.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the field of nonparametric regression with a well-motivated, theoretically sound method that addresses a clear gap in the literature. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and should be accepted.
