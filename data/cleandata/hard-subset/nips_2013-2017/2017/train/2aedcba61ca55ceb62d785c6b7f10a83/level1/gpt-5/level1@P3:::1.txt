Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure (IGAS) for nonparametric regression: a unique minimal partition G* of predictors such that f(X) = ∑u∈G* fu(Xu), with each fu an unrestricted multivariate function over a small subset Xu. This sits between additive models (no interactions) and full ANOVA/high-order interaction models (many interactions). The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group structures (refinement), prove existence/uniqueness of a minimal amiable structure G* (intrinsic structure), (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel method that selects G while estimating f in a direct-sum RKHS HG, and (iii) establish consistency of recovering G* using a complexity penalty derived from covering number bounds of RKHSs. They provide an exhaustive search algorithm for small p and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic for larger p, with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

This connects to several strands: additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani], functional ANOVA and smoothing spline ANOVA [Gu], higher-order interaction kernels (e.g., ESP kernel, SALSA [Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16]), structured sparsity and MKL for additive/interaction selection (e.g., hierarchical kernel learning and group lasso in RKHS; Bach 2009; sparse additive models, Ravikumar et al. 2009), and to some extent graphical models (though their target structure is different).

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity: formalization of a poset over groupings and definition of an intrinsic minimal grouping is appealing and provides a target for identifiability.
- Methodological link: using direct-sum RKHSs to encode group additive structure is natural and flexible; the penalty leverages capacity control ideas (covering numbers) in a way reminiscent of SRM.
- Theoretical results: the distinction between amiable vs. non-amiable structures and consistency of selecting G* are established under the proposed penalty; the covering number bound for sums of RKHSs (Proposition 3) is plausible and useful.
- Practical motivation: aims to capture low-order interactions without the combinatorial explosion of full ANOVA, retaining interpretability.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality/technical soundness
- Assumptions and identifiability: The existence and uniqueness of the intrinsic structure (Theorem 1) hinges on properties of the set of amiable partitions under refinement. The paper asserts completeness and a unique minimal element, but key conditions on PX (e.g., independence or product structure) and f needed to guarantee uniqueness are not clearly stated. Proposition 1 uses independence to obtain strict inclusion of L2 spaces; without independence, multiple incomparable minimal structures can arise (e.g., symmetries or degeneracies like f(x1,x2)=g(x1+x2)). Please clarify the precise conditions ensuring a unique minimal partition and whether uniqueness can fail under dependence.
- Complexity penalty derivation: The path from ln N(ε, IK,r) ≤ C (ln(r/ε))p+1 to C(G)=∑u α|u|+1 and finally to the practical penalty µ∑u α|u| (Eq. 6) is inconsistent: the exponent drops from |u|+1 to |u| without justification. Moreover, α=ln(r/ε) is introduced as a reparameterization but then treated as an integer hyperparameter unrelated to the actual kernels or ε. This divorces the penalty from the true capacity of HG (kernel bandwidths, radii), turning it into an ad hoc function of group sizes. Either keep a principled, kernel- and ε-dependent penalty or explicitly justify the simplification and its impact on the theory.
- Rates and tuning: The consistency theorem (Theorem 4) requires “proper” choices of λn and µn but does not provide rates or a data-driven procedure guaranteeing the required scaling. Bounds depend on |Ga| and supG ln N(·,HG), which can be huge. Please specify admissible sequences for (λn, µn), and discuss the dependence on p and group sizes.
- Kernel specification: The method relies on universal kernels to avoid approximation bias, but practical performance depends heavily on kernel choices (e.g., Gaussian bandwidth) per group. The paper does not specify how these hyperparameters are set or selected across candidate partitions. Because HG changes with G, this is a critical implementation detail.
- Algorithmic scalability: Exhaustive search over partitions is intractable (Bell number growth). The backward stepwise procedure is greedy with no backtracking/merge operations, and empirical results show failures (Model 4). There is no complexity analysis, nor guarantees for the heuristic. Comparisons to convex relaxations (e.g., MKL with ℓ1/ℓp penalties over group kernels, hierarchical sparsity for interactions) are absent.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear, but some derivations (Proposition 3 to Eq. 6) and assumptions (on PX, uniqueness of G*) need stronger exposition. Provide a small illustrative example showing when multiple amiable structures exist and how G* is determined.
- Several proofs are deferred; key lemmas underlying Theorem 1 and 4 should be summarized in the main text with assumptions.

Originality
- The notion of an intrinsic minimal group partition and its selection via a capacity-based penalty is novel and interesting. However, the idea of selecting additive/interaction groups in RKHSs via complexity penalties is related to prior work on hierarchical kernel learning and structured sparsity; these connections and differences are under-cited and under-discussed (e.g., Bach 2009; Ravikumar et al. 2009; Bien et al. 2013; MKL for additive kernels).

Significance
- If made practical, the approach could provide an interpretable middle ground between additive and full interaction models. As presented, experimental evidence is limited: small synthetic setups, no comparisons against strong baselines (SALSA, ANOVA kernels, sparse additive/interaction methods), and a single small real dataset with qualitative analysis. The lack of quantitative predictive comparisons and scalability limits the current impact.

Empirical evaluation
- Include baselines: SALSA (ICML’16), ANOVA kernels/KRR, sparse additive models, hierarchical interaction selection (e.g., hierNet), MKL-based additive kernels. Report both structure recovery and predictive performance.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, µ, kernel bandwidths; provide ablations showing the role of the penalty.
- Discuss runtime and scalability; consider more efficient search (split-merge, beam search) or convex surrogates.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and potentially useful formalization of intrinsic group additive structure.
- Theoretically grounded selection criterion using RKHS capacity with consistency results.
- Conceptual contribution could stimulate further work on structured nonparametric model selection.

Cons for acceptance:
- Penalty derivation has inconsistencies; tuning lacks principled guidance.
- Assumptions for uniqueness/identifiability are unclear; dependence structure is largely ignored.
- Scalability and algorithms are insufficient; empirical evaluation is limited with no strong baselines.
- Practical kernel hyperparameter selection across varying groupings is unspecified.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The core idea is appealing and the theory promising, but the penalty formulation, assumptions, algorithmic practicality, and empirical validation need strengthening. I encourage the authors to (i) tighten the theory with explicit assumptions and tuning rates, (ii) ground the penalty in actual kernel complexities or adopt a data-dependent complexity (e.g., Rademacher), (iii) develop scalable search/convex relaxations with guarantees, and (iv) provide thorough comparisons to established baselines.
