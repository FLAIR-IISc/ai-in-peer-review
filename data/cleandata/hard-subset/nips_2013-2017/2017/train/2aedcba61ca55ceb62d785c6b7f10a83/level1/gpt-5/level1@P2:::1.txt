Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies nonparametric regression when f(X) admits a group-additive decomposition: f(X)=∑u∈G fu(Xu) where G is a partition of predictors. The authors (i) formalize the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal partition among all partitions for which f is representable; (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously estimates f and selects G; (iii) derive a complexity measure for a given group structure via covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs; and (iv) prove selection consistency under suitable conditions. Algorithms based on exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic are provided, with empirical demonstrations on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

The work builds on additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), smoothing spline ANOVA (Gu), and kernel ridge regression. It relates to higher-order interaction models such as SALSA/ESP kernels (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), but instead proposes grouping variables to capture within-group interactions while maintaining tractability. It also connects to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15) but addresses identifiability and provides a selection-consistent method. There are conceptual links to structured sparsity and MKL/hierarchical kernel learning, and to sparse additive models (e.g., SpAM, Ravikumar et al., NIPS 2007), which could be more fully discussed.

Quality
- Theory: The formalization of a partial order over partitions and the definition of an intrinsic (minimal) group structure are well motivated. The complexity penalty is derived from covering-number upper bounds for RKHSs, leading to a penalty of the form µ∑u∈G α|u|. Theorems establish: (a) concentration of empirical risk for amiable structures; (b) a positive risk gap for non-amiable ones; and (c) selection consistency with appropriate tuning (λn n→0 and µ chosen properly).
  • Strengths: Use of direct-sum RKHS covering numbers (Proposition 3) is neat; the amiable/non-amiable dichotomy clarifies identifiability; the consistency result is aligned with the penalty’s monotonicity in group size.
  • Concerns/open points: (1) The uniqueness/existence of a minimal amiable partition G∗: the set of partitions is finite and forms a lattice under refinement; however, the proof that the meet of amiable partitions remains amiable (needed for a unique minimal element) is not provided. Please clarify or add a proof/condition ensuring Ga is closed under refinement (e.g., identify conditions on PX and f that guarantee closure). (2) Proposition 1’s strict inclusion requires independence of Xj’s; elsewhere, dependence is allowed. What happens under general dependence? Does identifiability rely on independence? Please make assumptions explicit throughout. (3) The penalty’s α arises from ln(r/ε), where ε is a covering radius; in practice α is tuned, which decouples the penalty from the actual kernel capacity and constants in the covering number bound. The consistency theorem states “choose a proper µ,” but does it also require α to scale with data (n, kernel bandwidth, group dimension)? A more explicit prescription or robustness analysis would help. (4) The concentration bounds involve ln N(·,HG,·), but the penalty uses only group cardinalities; any mismatch between the true covering numbers and |u| may affect finite-sample selection.

- Algorithms/empirics: The exhaustive search is exponential; the backward stepwise heuristic scales but has no guarantees and sometimes misses the true structure (Model 4). Simulations report identification frequencies but lack comparisons to baselines such as functional ANOVA kernels, SALSA/ESP, OKGT, SpAM with pairwise interactions, hierarchical kernel learning, or HSIC-based interaction discovery. The real data example is qualitative; predictive metrics versus baselines are not reported. Guidance on computational cost, sensitivity to λ/µ/α, and kernel choice per group is limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized. The derivations around covering numbers could be streamlined, and the role/interpretation of α should be clarified. Assumptions (kernel universality, boundedness/sub-Gaussian noise, independence vs. general PX) should be stated prominently where used (Proposition 1, Theorem 4). Including proofs or sketches for Theorem 1 (uniqueness of G∗) and Proposition 3 would improve completeness.

Originality
The notion of “intrinsic” group additive structure and its selection via an RKHS capacity-based penalty is novel and interesting. The work is related to but distinct from prior additive/ANOVA kernels and OKGT. Connections to structured sparsity and hierarchical kernel learning (e.g., group/MKL penalties for interactions) could be better contextualized.

Significance
Potentially significant as a framework for discovering interpretable, low-order interactions via groupings in nonparametric regression. However, the current empirical evidence is limited, and the practical impact is tempered by scalability (exhaustive search) and the heuristic nature of the stepwise algorithm. Stronger empirical comparisons and larger-scale studies are needed to establish practical relevance.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and identifiability-oriented notion of intrinsic group structure.
- Sound use of RKHS capacity to design a structural penalty; selection consistency result.
- Interpretable modeling stance between purely additive and full interaction models.
- Codeable with standard KRR components; real-data visualization is intuitive.

Cons
- Potential gaps/ambiguities in the uniqueness proof of the intrinsic partition and assumptions (independence vs. general PX).
- Penalty parameterization via α is somewhat ad hoc relative to the covering-number theory; practical guidance is limited.
- Limited experiments: small-scale simulations, one real dataset, no baselines, no runtime/scalability study.
- Heuristic search without guarantees; failure cases observed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous proof (or additional assumptions) ensuring uniqueness of G∗ and closure of Ga under refinement; discuss identifiability under dependent covariates.
- Elaborate the link between the covering-number bounds and the chosen penalty; give practical guidelines for α and µ selection; analyze sensitivity.
- Compare against baselines (functional ANOVA kernels, SALSA/ESP KRR, OKGT, SpAM with interactions, hierarchical kernel learning, HSIC-based methods) on both identification accuracy and prediction error; include runtime and scalability.
- Explore stronger search strategies (e.g., beam search, split-merge proposals, stochastic search, or convex relaxations via MKL/structured sparsity) and, if possible, provide approximation guarantees.
- Clarify kernel choices per group and bandwidth selection; discuss universality requirements.

Overall assessment
Interesting and promising theoretical framework with clear motivation, but empirical validation and some theoretical details are not yet at NeurIPS standards. I would lean borderline reject, with the belief that addressing the above points could make this a strong submission.
