This paper addresses the problem of group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression, a natural extension of the standard additive model to account for interactions between predictors without the exponential complexity of full interaction models like ANOVA. The authors rigorously define the "intrinsic" group additive structure and propose a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. The penalty term leverages a novel complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs. The theoretical analysis demonstrates consistency, and empirical evaluation supports the method's effectiveness.

The paper builds upon existing work in additive modeling [5, 16], functional ANOVA [4, 13], and higher-order interaction models [6]. It differentiates itself by offering a middle ground between purely additive and fully interactive models, akin to [11] but addressing the limitations of non-identifiability and lacking a systematic identification approach. The connection to graphical models [2, 7, 9] is acknowledged, highlighting the difference in modeling perspectives (group structure vs. conditional dependence).  Compared to existing NIPS work on nonparametric regression, this paper presents a novel approach to structure learning that could be valuable for high-dimensional data.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formal definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and the RKHS-based complexity penalty are novel contributions. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for identifying a meaningful structure.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The consistency proof is a significant strength, providing confidence in the method's ability to identify the true structure in the limit.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of group structure identification and its relevance in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, bridging the gap between additive and fully interactive models.
* **Practical Algorithms:**  The proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) offer practical solutions for implementation, accommodating both small and large datasets.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and real data application demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in identifying the group structure and provide insights into its applicability.
* **Well-written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections (particularly the theory) are dense.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theory:** The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, relies heavily on covering number concepts which may be inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with that area.  A more intuitive explanation of the complexity measure would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally expensive for high-dimensional problems, limiting its scalability. While the stepwise algorithm addresses this to some extent, its greedy nature might lead to suboptimal solutions.  Exploring other efficient search strategies (e.g., genetic algorithms) could be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of µ and α, as evidenced by the simulation results. More guidance on selecting these parameters, beyond cross-validation, would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison:** The empirical evaluation primarily focuses on demonstrating the method's ability to identify the true structure. A more thorough comparison with existing methods (e.g., sparse interaction screening approaches) on benchmark datasets would strengthen the results.
* **Real Data Interpretation:** While the real data example is illustrative, the interpretation of the identified group structure is somewhat limited.  Connecting the group structure to domain knowledge or providing more in-depth analysis of the estimated functions would enhance the impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
* It presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to group structure identification.
* The consistency guarantee provides strong support for the method's reliability.
* The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in simulated and real-world scenarios.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is highly technical and may limit accessibility.
* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm can be prohibitive.
* The sensitivity to parameter tuning requires careful consideration.
* The lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods weakens the empirical evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a solid contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. While the theoretical complexity and computational limitations are valid concerns, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising empirical results outweigh these drawbacks. The paper addresses a significant problem with a well-motivated and thoroughly investigated method.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. I recommend accepting the paper with the following minor revisions:

1.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the complexity measure based on covering numbers, perhaps with a simple example.
2.  Discuss potential strategies for improving the scalability of the algorithm, beyond the stepwise approach.
3.  Provide more guidance on selecting the tuning parameters µ and α.
4.  Include a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods on benchmark datasets.
5.  Expand the interpretation of the real data results, connecting the group structure to domain knowledge.
