

# Peer Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

This paper addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: balancing model flexibility with interpretability and computational feasibility. The authors propose a novel approach to identify the "intrinsic group additive structure" for high-dimensional regression problems, which lies between the standard additive model (no interactions) and functional ANOVA models (all possible interactions). The key insight is that by grouping variables that interact with each other into the same group, one can maintain estimation efficiency while accommodating meaningful interactions.

The paper builds upon the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method by Pan et al. (2011), addressing two critical limitations: the non-uniqueness of group structures and the lack of a systematic method to identify the group structure. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that correctly represents the true function, proving its uniqueness. They then develop a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies this structure and estimates nonparametric functions, using a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with the authors proving consistency of their method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure. The complexity measure C(G) = ∑_j α^|u_j| is well-motivated through RKHS capacity theory and provides a principled way to penalize complex group structures. The paper also presents two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) for practical implementation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a clear mathematical framework for group additive structures, with well-defined concepts (amiable structures, intrinsic structure) and strong theoretical results (Theorems 1-4). The consistency proof is particularly compelling.

2. **Novel Complexity Measure:** The derivation of C(G) based on RKHS covering numbers is a significant contribution that addresses the core challenge of balancing model complexity with fit.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The proposed algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are well-described and the experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real data.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Related Work:** The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from functional ANOVA models and graphical models, highlighting that the group additive structure is a more appropriate target for nonparametric regression than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with Alternative Methods:** While the paper addresses limitations in Pan et al.'s work, it doesn't thoroughly compare with other approaches for group structure identification in nonparametric regression (e.g., methods based on sparsity or graphical models).

2. **Computational Scalability:** The exhaustive search approach (Algorithm 1) is computationally expensive for large p, and while the stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) addresses this, the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of its scalability or potential pitfalls when p is very large.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The method depends on tuning parameters (µ, α), and while the paper uses validation for parameter selection, it doesn't thoroughly investigate how sensitive the results are to these choices.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Complexity Analysis:** The paper doesn't provide theoretical analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, which is important for understanding their practical applicability.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims through theoretical proofs and empirical validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach. The experimental design is appropriate, with synthetic data to test identification performance and a real-world application to demonstrate practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Key concepts are clearly defined, and the flow from problem formulation to solution to experimental validation is logical. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" is novel and represents a significant theoretical advance. The complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers is also a novel contribution. While the group additive model concept has appeared in prior work (Pan et al., 2011), the rigorous treatment of structure identification is new.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression that has practical implications across many domains. The group additive model provides a valuable middle ground between simple additive models and complex interaction models. The ability to automatically identify the group structure is particularly valuable for practitioners who lack domain knowledge to specify it a priori.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for group additive structure identification
- Introduces a novel complexity measure based on RKHS theory
- Proves consistency of the proposed method
- Demonstrates practical effectiveness through experiments
- Addresses a significant limitation in prior work on group additive models

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with alternative methods for structure identification
- Computational scalability concerns for very high-dimensional problems
- Parameter sensitivity not thoroughly investigated

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by providing a principled approach to identify group structures that balance model flexibility, interpretability, and computational feasibility. The theoretical framework is sound, the empirical validation is appropriate, and the method has clear practical utility. While there are some limitations regarding computational scalability and parameter sensitivity, these are common challenges in the field and do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and addresses an important problem in a novel way.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017. This work represents a significant step forward in high-dimensional nonparametric regression methodology and should be of interest to the NIPS community.
