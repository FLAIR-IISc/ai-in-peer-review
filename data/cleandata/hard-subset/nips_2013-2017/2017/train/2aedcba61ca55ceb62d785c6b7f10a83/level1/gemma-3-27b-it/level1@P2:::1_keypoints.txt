- Addresses group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression in high dimensions, defining an “intrinsic” group additive structure.
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel method that jointly identifies group structure and estimates nonparametric functions.
- Introduces a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers to construct the penalty.
- Provides theoretical rigor with a consistency proof (in supplement).
- Develops two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) to handle different dimensionality regimes.
- Presents simulation studies and a Boston Housing real-data example demonstrating effectiveness and prediction performance.
- Well-motivated paper that clearly positions itself relative to additive models, functional ANOVA, and OKGT.
- Strong, clear presentation with consistent notation and reasonable mathematical derivations.

- Exhaustive search is computationally prohibitive in high dimensions; stepwise is faster but potentially suboptimal.
- Method performance appears sensitive to tuning parameters (λ, μ, α); parameter selection relies on validation without a systematic procedure.
- Impact of kernel choice is not thoroughly investigated; discussion of kernel selection and influence is limited.
- Real-data evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Boston Housing), reducing empirical generalizability.
- Discussion of connections to graphical models is superficial and could be expanded.

- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, theoretically sound, introduces a novel complexity measure, and offers practical algorithms with empirical support.
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost limits applicability, sensitivity to tuning and kernel choice needs care, and real-data validation is limited.

- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Requested minor revisions: discuss/justify computational efficiency or alternative search strategies; detail parameter tuning procedure and consider systematic selection techniques; discuss kernel choice and possibly test additional kernels; add results on another real dataset to demonstrate generalizability.
