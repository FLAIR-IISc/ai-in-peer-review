## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses the important problem of identifying group additive structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, a generalization of the standard additive model that allows for interactions within groups of predictors. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of purely additive models and the challenges of functional ANOVA approaches which can suffer from a rapid increase in model complexity. They propose a structure-penalized kernel method, introducing a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs, and demonstrate consistency of their method.

The paper builds upon existing work in additive modeling [5, 11], functional ANOVA [4, 13], and kernel methods [10]. It distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the non-uniqueness of group additive structures and providing a principled approach to identify the *intrinsic* structure – a minimal representation. The connection to graphical models [2, 7, 9] is acknowledged, but the authors rightly point out that their approach directly targets group structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation. The derivation of the complexity measure for group structures is a significant contribution, and the consistency proof provides strong theoretical guarantees. The inclusion of both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms offers practical flexibility. The simulation studies and real data application demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the visualizations of the real data results are insightful. The authors carefully acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the potential for the stepwise algorithm to get stuck in local optima.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical development is strong, the presentation could be more accessible. The notation is dense, and some definitions (e.g., Proposition 1) require careful reading. The discussion of related work, while present, could be more comprehensive. For example, recent advances in sparse additive models [e.g., Ravikumar et al., 2009] are not mentioned. The simulation studies, while demonstrating the method's effectiveness, could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing methods (e.g., OKGT [11]). The choice of parameters (λ, µ, α) is not fully explained, and the sensitivity of the results to these parameters could be further investigated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
*   Well-defined complexity measure for group additive structures.
*   Consistency guarantees and practical algorithms.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness through simulations and real data analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and potentially challenging presentation.
*   Limited comparison to existing methods in simulation studies.
*   Lack of detailed discussion on parameter selection.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. While the presentation could be improved, the technical soundness, theoretical guarantees, and empirical results justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the comparison to related work.
