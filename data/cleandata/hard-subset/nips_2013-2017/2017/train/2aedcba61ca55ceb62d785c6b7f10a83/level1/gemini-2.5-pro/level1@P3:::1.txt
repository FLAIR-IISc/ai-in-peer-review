This paper addresses the important problem of balancing model flexibility and interpretability in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. Standard additive models are interpretable but ignore variable interactions, while fully nonparametric models suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The authors focus on the group additive model, `f(X) = sum_j f_j(X_{u_j})`, as a compromise. This model class, where `{u_j}` is a partition of the predictor indices, has been explored before (e.g., Pan et al., 2015), but this paper makes two key contributions. First, it rigorously tackles the non-uniqueness of such decompositions by defining a partial order on group structures and proving the existence of a unique, minimal "intrinsic group additive structure". This provides a solid theoretical foundation for model identifiability. Second, it proposes a novel structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously estimate the functions `f_j` and identify this intrinsic structure. The core of this method is a principled complexity penalty for group structures, `C(G)`, derived from the capacity (specifically, the covering number) of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors prove the consistency of their structure selection procedure and demonstrate its effectiveness on synthetic and real data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Significance:** The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is a significant contribution. By defining a partial order and proving the existence and uniqueness of a minimal element in the set of all valid structures, the authors address a fundamental identifiability problem that has been largely overlooked in previous work. This is a clean and elegant theoretical result.

2.  **Principled Methodology:** The proposed method for identifying this structure is well-motivated. Instead of using an ad-hoc penalty, the authors derive the complexity term `C(G)` from the capacity of the function space `H_G` induced by the structure `G`. Linking the structural penalty to the covering numbers of the associated RKHS provides a strong theoretical justification for the approach. This is a major strength over more heuristic methods.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a consistency proof (Theorem 4), showing that the estimated group structure converges to the true intrinsic structure in probability as the sample size increases. This guarantee lends substantial credibility to the proposed method and distinguishes it from purely algorithmic approaches.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the notation is consistent, and the theoretical concepts (e.g., amiable structures, partial ordering) are well-defined and illustrated with examples. The overall narrative, from problem definition to theoretical solution and practical algorithm, is logical and compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Algorithmic Scalability and Robustness:** While the proposed framework is theoretically sound, its practical realization is less developed. The exhaustive search (Algorithm 1) is infeasible for all but the smallest `p`. The proposed backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a reasonable heuristic, but it is greedy and not guaranteed to find the global optimum. The authors honestly report its failure on one of their five simulation models, which highlights its potential fragility. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the optimization challenges, the limitations of the greedy approach, and potential alternatives (e.g., stochastic search, MCMC, or genetic algorithms). Furthermore, the description of Algorithm 2 seems imprecise for a backward search; a backward step would typically involve splitting an existing group, not adding a variable to an existing one, which sounds more like a forward-step component.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while supportive, could be more comprehensive.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The proposed GASI method is not compared against any competing methods. It would be highly informative to see how it performs against other techniques for interaction detection, such as COSSO (Lin & Zhang, 2006), sparse group Lasso, or even gradient boosting methods and random forests, which can implicitly model interactions and provide variable importance measures. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical state-of-the-art performance of the method.
    *   **Limited Scale:** The simulations are conducted on problems with a small number of predictors (`p`=5 or 6). A key motivation for this work is high-dimensional analysis, so demonstrating performance on problems with `p` in the dozens would make a much stronger case for the method's utility, particularly for the stepwise algorithm.
    *   **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity:** The method relies on three tuning parameters (`λ`, `µ`, `α`). The results for Model 1 in Table 2 show a significant performance drop when moving from the best-case parameter setting to the tuned one, suggesting potential sensitivity. A more detailed study on the impact of these parameters and the robustness of the cross-validation procedure would be valuable.

3.  **Clarity on Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on specific assumptions (e.g., properties of the kernel function needed for the covering number bounds). These assumptions are mentioned but could be stated more explicitly and prominently in the main text to clearly define the scope under which the consistency guarantees hold.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a valuable and well-founded contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure and the principled, RKHS-based penalty for identifying it are novel and significant. The work is supported by a strong consistency proof. However, the practical aspects of the paper, namely the optimization algorithm and the experimental evaluation, are less developed and could be substantially improved. The lack of comparison to baseline methods makes it difficult to judge the method's practical impact.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and significance of the theoretical framework are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces an important concept and a principled method that will likely inspire future work in this area. I would strongly encourage the authors to expand their experimental section and provide a more detailed discussion of the algorithmic challenges in the final version.
