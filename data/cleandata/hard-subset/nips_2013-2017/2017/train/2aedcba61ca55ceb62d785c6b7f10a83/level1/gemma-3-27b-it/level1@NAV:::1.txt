## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper tackles the problem of identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, a crucial step towards both improving estimation efficiency and interpretability. Building upon the group additive model introduced in [11], the authors address the issues of non-uniqueness and lack of a systematic identification method. They propose a structure-penalized kernel method, introducing a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs.  The paper rigorously analyzes the consistency of the proposed method and demonstrates its effectiveness through simulations and a real-data application. This work relates to a broad body of literature on dimensionality reduction in nonparametric regression, including additive models [5], functional ANOVA [4, 13], and higher-order interaction models [6].  The proposed method represents a valuable middle ground between these approaches, offering a balance between flexibility and interpretability. Connections to graphical models [2, 7, 9] are also appropriately noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the consistency of the proposed method under reasonable conditions. The derivation of the complexity measure for group additive structures is a significant contribution.
* **Novelty:** The proposed structural penalty based on RKHS complexity is a novel approach to structure identification in nonparametric regression.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The paper includes both simulation studies and a real-data application, showcasing the effectiveness of the method in different settings.  The inclusion of both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms demonstrates consideration for scalability.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The authors thoroughly relate their work to existing literature, providing appropriate citations and contextualizing their contribution. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** The theoretical framework, while rigorous, might lead to a complex implementation. The dependency on covering number calculations for the complexity measure could be computationally challenging in high dimensions.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The performance is sensitive to the choice of tuning parameters (λ, µ, α), and a robust method for selecting these parameters beyond cross-validation would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the simulations demonstrate the method's ability to identify the true structure, a more direct comparison to existing methods (e.g., those based on graphical models or functional ANOVA) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Real Data Interpretation:** The interpretation of the identified group structure on the Boston Housing dataset could be more in-depth and connect the findings more strongly to domain knowledge.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees for the consistency of the method.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of implementation and parameter tuning might limit the practical applicability of the method.
* A more thorough comparative analysis with existing methods would strengthen the paper.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While some practical considerations and comparative evaluations could be further improved, the novelty, rigor, and comprehensive evaluation justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance to NIPS, with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
