- Addresses high-dimensional nonparametric regression by identifying an intermediate "intrinsic group additive structure" that balances flexibility and interpretability.
- Proposes grouping interacting variables so estimation remains efficient while allowing meaningful interactions.
- Builds on OKGT (Pan et al., 2011) and tackles two limitations: non-unique group structures and lack of systematic identification.
- Rigorously defines the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal correct group representation and proves its uniqueness.
- Introduces a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies group structure and estimates nonparametric functions.
- Derives a novel complexity measure C(G) = ∑_j α^|u_j| from RKHS covering numbers to penalize complex group structures.
- Provides strong theoretical results, including consistency proofs for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorems 1–4).
- Presents two practical algorithms: exhaustive search and backward stepwise, for structure identification.
- Experimental results on synthetic and real data demonstrate the method’s effectiveness.
- Clearly differentiates the approach from functional ANOVA and graphical models, arguing group additive structure is a more suitable target for regression.
- Strength: high theoretical rigor with well-defined concepts (amiable, intrinsic) and compelling consistency proof.
- Strength: novel RKHS-based complexity measure that principledly balances fit and complexity.
- Strength: practical implementation and demonstrated empirical performance.
- Weakness: limited comparisons with other structure-identification methods (e.g., sparsity-based or graphical-model approaches).
- Weakness: computational scalability concerns—exhaustive search is expensive and stepwise method’s behavior for very large p is insufficiently analyzed.
- Weakness: sensitivity to tuning parameters (µ, α) not thoroughly investigated.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis of computational complexity for the proposed algorithms.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written, original, significant contribution with practical utility; reviewer recommends acceptance despite noted limitations.
