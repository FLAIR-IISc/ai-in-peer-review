- Summary: Studies nonparametric regression with group-additive decomposition, formalizes intrinsic minimal partition, proposes a structure-penalized kernel method, derives RKHS-based complexity measures, and proves selection consistency with algorithms and experiments.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on additive models, smoothing spline ANOVA, KRR; relates to SALSA/ESP, OKGT, structured sparsity, MKL, and sparse additive models but claims distinct identifiability and selection guarantees.

- Theoretical strengths: Formal partial order over partitions, neat direct-sum RKHS covering-number analysis, amiable/non-amiable identifiability dichotomy, and consistency results aligned with penalty monotonicity.

- Theoretical concerns: Unproven uniqueness/existence of minimal amiable partition (closure under refinement), unclear role of covariate dependence vs. independence, and lack of explicit scaling/choice guidance for α and µ relative to covering-number constants.

- Penalty mismatch: Theoretical penalty derived from covering numbers uses α from ln(r/ε) but in practice α is tuned, decoupling theory from implementation and raising finite-sample concerns.

- Algorithmic issues: Exhaustive search is exponential; backward stepwise heuristic lacks guarantees and can miss true structure; computational cost and scalability guidance are limited.

- Empirical limitations: Experiments are small-scale, report identification frequencies without comparisons to relevant baselines (ANOVA kernels, SALSA/ESP, OKGT, SpAM, HSIC), and real-data results lack predictive metrics and runtime analysis.

- Clarity: Paper is readable but should streamline covering-number derivations, clarify interpretation of α, and state key assumptions (kernel universality, noise, independence) prominently.

- Originality: The intrinsic group-additive concept and RKHS capacity-based selection are novel and interesting, but connections to structured sparsity and hierarchical kernel learning need fuller discussion.

- Significance: Promising framework for interpretable interaction discovery, but practical impact is limited by empirical evidence and scalability concerns.

- Pros: Clear problem formulation and identifiability focus; principled RKHS-based structural penalty with consistency; interpretable intermediate model class; implementable with standard KRR components.

- Cons: Gaps/ambiguities in uniqueness and identifiability under dependence; penalty parameterization is somewhat ad hoc; limited experiments and baselines; heuristic search without guarantees and observed failure cases.

- Suggested improvements: Prove or state conditions for uniqueness/closure of amiable partitions; connect covering-number theory to practical α/µ selection and sensitivity; compare to multiple baselines and report runtimes; explore better search strategies (beam/split-merge/stochastic/convex relaxations); clarify kernel and bandwidth choices.

- Overall assessment: Interesting theoretical contribution but needs stronger theoretical detail and substantially improved empirical validation; reviewer leans borderline reject pending revisions.
