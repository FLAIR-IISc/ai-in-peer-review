Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies high-dimensional nonparametric regression under a group additive structure. Instead of assuming fully additive effects or allowing all higher-order interactions (as in functional ANOVA or ESP-kernel-based KRR), the authors consider partitions of variables into small groups and model f(X) as a sum over nonparametric functions on the groups. They formalize a partial order over group structures (refinement), define amiable structures (those that can express f), and prove the existence and uniqueness of an intrinsic group additive structure: the minimal amiable partition G*. They propose a structure-penalized kernel method that simultaneously estimates f and selects G, with a penalty derived from covering-number capacity bounds of direct-sum RKHSs. They provide selection consistency for recovering G*, design an exhaustive-search and a backward stepwise algorithm, and present simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

This sits between generalized additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) and functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA (Gu) and the recent SALSA approach (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16) and ESP-kernel KRR (higher-order interactions). It is related to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15), and tangentially to graphical models.

Quality:
– Theory: The formalization of group-structure partial order and the existence of a unique minimal (intrinsic) structure is useful. The structural penalty is motivated by covering-number bounds for RKHS balls and an inequality for the covering numbers of direct-sum spaces. Theorems provide: (i) uniform convergence of empirical risks for amiable G, (ii) separation for non-amiable G, and (iii) consistency of the selected Ĝ.

However, several key technical points are underspecified:
• Conditions: Many assumptions are implicit (bounded/universal kernels, compact domain, sub-Gaussian noise, tuning λn, µn schedules). Proposition 1 requires independence to guarantee strict inclusion of function spaces; dependence is common in practice and used in the real-data example—how does this affect identifiability?
• Penalty derivation: The transition from ln N(ε,·) bounds to a penalty C(G)=Σ α|u| (Equation (6)) is not cleanly justified. α is introduced via α=(ln(r/ε)), but then treated as a discrete hyperparameter independent of n, r, or ε. The role of ε (covering scale) in finite-sample model selection and its coupling with µ is unclear.
• Rates/choices: The consistency theorem requires λn·n→0 and a “proper” µ, yet no explicit guidance on µ’s scaling with n or on how α is set from data. The bounds feature large constants; practical implications are not discussed.
• Algorithms: Exhaustive search is exponential; the backward stepwise heuristic lacks any approximation or consistency guarantees and can stall in local minima. No complexity analysis is given.

– Experiments: Synthetic results report frequency of exact recovery for selected (µ,α) and a CV-tuned variant; stepwise works on 4/5 models but fails on one. There are no comparisons to strong baselines (SALSA, ESP-kernel KRR with order control, sparse additive models, smoothing spline ANOVA, OKGT). Real-data analysis is qualitative; no predictive metrics versus baselines. Kernel choices, bandwidths, and λ selection are not fully specified, limiting reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable, with helpful motivating examples. That said:
• The “amiable” terminology is unusual; “compatible/admissible” would be more standard.
• The complexity penalty section is confusing: ε and r disappear, α becomes a free integer, and exponents change from |u|+1 to |u| in (6). Please reconcile and clearly state the final penalty with its theoretical justification and default settings.
• Assumptions (compact X, kernel boundedness/universality, noise) should be centralized. Notation occasionally has typos and formatting issues.
• Algorithm 2’s split/merge rules are insufficiently specified; details of kernel choice and λ, µ, α tuning are needed for reproducibility.

Originality:
Defining and proving uniqueness of the intrinsic group additive structure within the partial order is novel and useful. Using RKHS covering-number-based penalties to score group partitions and proving structure-selection consistency is a fresh angle. The work is distinct from SALSA/ESP approaches that enumerate interaction orders, and from OKGT which lacked identifiability and selection theory.

Significance:
If robustly validated, a consistent and practical method for discovering low-order interacting groups would be impactful for interpretable nonparametric modeling. However, the current empirical evaluation and algorithmic scalability leave questions about practical utility on modern high-dimensional problems. The theoretical contribution is promising; the methodological bridge to practice needs tightening.

Pros for acceptance:
• Clear and well-motivated problem; sits between additive and full interaction models.
• Formalization of intrinsic group additive structure with a uniqueness result.
• RKHS capacity–based structural penalty and selection-consistency theory.
• Interpretable structure discovery; illustrative synthetic and real examples.

Cons for acceptance:
• Ambiguity in the penalty derivation (role of ε, r, α), and lack of guidance on µ/α scaling.
• Assumptions (e.g., independence) not aligned with practice; conditions for theorems not consolidated.
• Heuristic search without guarantees; scalability concerns.
• Limited experiments: no comparisons to strong baselines; qualitative real-data results only.
• Reproducibility gaps (kernel/bandwidth/λ choices).

Suggestions for improvement:
1) Clarify and tighten the penalty: explicitly relate α and µ to ε and n; provide a principled default (e.g., set ε as a function of n; absorb constants into µn) and state conditions guaranteeing consistency. Reconcile the exponent from |u|+1 to |u|.
2) State all assumptions upfront (kernel universality, boundedness, compact X, noise), discuss dependence among covariates, and indicate which results require independence.
3) Provide algorithmic refinements and analysis: e.g., beam search with splitting/merging, stochastic search with restarts, or relaxations (submodular surrogates) with approximation guarantees; discuss computational complexity empirically.
4) Expand experiments: compare against SALSA (ICML’16), ESP-kernel KRR for interactions up to D, sparse additive models (e.g., Ravikumar et al., JMLR 2010), smoothing spline ANOVA, and OKGT, on both recovery and prediction metrics; include runtime.
5) Detail implementation: kernel family and bandwidth selection, λ tuning, cross-validation protocol, and provide code.
6) Improve terminology and notation consistency; add a brief example illustrating G* vs amiable superstructures under correlated X.

Overall assessment:
Promising theoretical contribution with interesting ideas on identifiability and RKHS-capacity penalties, but the empirical validation and clarity around the penalty and assumptions are not yet at NIPS standards. I would lean toward a borderline reject, with encouragement to revise along the lines above.
