Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies nonparametric regression when the regression function f(X) admits a group additive structure: the predictors are partitioned into disjoint groups u1,…,ud, and f(X) = ∑j fuj(Xuj). This model lies between standard additive models (no interactions) and high-order ANOVA/interaction models (many interactions), aiming to capture within-group interactions while keeping estimation and interpretation tractable when groups are small.

The authors (i) formalize a partial order on group structures and define the intrinsic group additive structure G*, the unique minimal element among all “amiable” group structures (those for which some fG in the corresponding function space equals f). (ii) Propose a structure-penalized kernel method that, for a candidate group structure G, estimates f in the direct-sum RKHS HG = ⊕u∈GHu via kernel ridge regression and penalizes the structural complexity C(G). (iii) Derive C(G) from covering-number upper bounds of direct-sum RKHSs, leading to a practical penalty proportional to ∑u∈G α|u| (after reparameterization). (iv) Prove selection consistency of the estimated structure under fixed p, appropriate tuning, and universal kernels. (v) Provide an exhaustive-search algorithm and a backward stepwise heuristic, and present simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

This work connects to: additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), SS-ANOVA (Gu), higher-order interaction kernels (e.g., ESP kernel, Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), and earlier group-based nonparametric modeling (OKGT; Pan et al., KDD’15). It also touches on ideas from additive RKHS/multiple kernel learning and structured sparsity for additive/interaction selection (e.g., sparse additive models; Ravikumar et al., NIPS’09; hierarchical interaction selection; Bien et al., Annals of Appl. Stat., 2013), which are not cited but are relevant comparisons.

Strengths:
- A clear formalization of the partial order on group structures and the existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic minimal structure. This conceptual contribution helps define the target of structure recovery.
- A principled link between structural complexity and RKHS capacity via covering numbers of direct-sum spaces; Proposition 3 is natural and useful.
- A consistent selection result (Ĝ → G*) under universal kernels and appropriate tuning, with probability bounds leveraging covering numbers.
- The formulation is general across kernels and accommodates within-group interactions without enumerating all pairwise/higher-order terms.
- Empirical demonstrations show the approach can recover true structures in several synthetic settings; the Boston data example illustrates interpretability.

Weaknesses:
- Practical penalty specification is coarse. The progression from covering-number bounds to C(G) ≈ ∑ α|u| discards kernel- and geometry-dependent constants and the radius/accuracy parameters, making the penalty largely a function of group sizes and count. It is unclear whether this surrogate aligns with the true capacity differences across competing structures, especially when different kernels or data scales are used.
- Algorithmic scalability and guarantees are limited. Exhaustive search is exponential in p; the backward stepwise heuristic has no approximation guarantees and empirically fails in some settings. No complexity analysis or more principled search (e.g., submodular surrogates, branch-and-bound, or convex relaxations via MKL/structured sparsity) is provided.
- Theory is for fixed p and assumes access to universal kernels and tuning sequences (λn, µ) with unknown constants; there is no analysis for p → ∞ or for high-dimensional regimes. The identification results rely on population notions (amiability) and capacity bounds but do not address statistical rates for f nor robustness to correlated covariates (independence is used in Proposition 1 for strict inclusion).
- Empirical evaluation lacks baselines. There is no comparison to SALSA (ICML’16), SS-ANOVA, sparse additive models (Ravikumar et al.), hierarchical interaction methods (e.g., hierNet), or MKL-based additive/interaction approaches. Predictive performance, structure recovery rates, and runtime comparisons would strengthen significance claims.
- Some clarity/notation issues: the transition from the covering-number bound ln N(·) to the penalty with parameters (µ, α) is abrupt, with α absorbing ln(r/ε) and kernel-dependent constants. The dependence on ε is not operationalized, and the simplification to α|u| (vs α|u|+1 earlier) is confusing. Details on kernel choices per group, λ selection, and validation protocol are sparse.

Quality:
- The conceptual framing and several propositions are technically sound and consistent with RKHS capacity theory. The selection consistency theorem is plausible given the bounds, though it depends on unspecified constants and fixed p. The algorithmic part is correct but not theoretically justified beyond consistency under exhaustive search. Empirical results are indicative but limited.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized with a coherent narrative from definitions to method to theory. Some notation and parameter reparameterization (C(G), α, ε) should be tightened. More details on kernel choices, hyperparameter tuning, and implementation would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and the use of direct-sum RKHS covering numbers to penalize group structures appear novel. However, the general idea of penalizing structural complexity to select additive/interaction components has precedents in additive RKHS, MKL, and structured sparsity literatures; clearer differentiation and comparisons are needed.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for discovering interacting variable groups with nonparametric flexibility. The current empirical and algorithmic limitations reduce immediate impact. Demonstrating scalability and superiority (or complementary value) against established baselines would enhance significance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide rigorous justification for the specific penalty form used in practice, or explore data-dependent penalties (e.g., Rademacher complexities) that retain kernel dependence.
- Compare against SALSA, SS-ANOVA, sparse additive models, hierNet, and MKL-based formulations, including structure recovery and prediction metrics.
- Develop and analyze more scalable search strategies with approximation guarantees, or convex relaxations linking to group-sparsity over additive/interaction kernels.
- Clarify assumptions (e.g., independence) and discuss extensions to correlated covariates; consider theory for growing p.
- Improve exposition around the covering-number bound to penalty mapping; specify kernel choices and hyperparameter tuning details.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear and well-motivated problem formulation with a novel, rigorous notion of intrinsic group structure.
- Principled capacity-based structural penalty and corresponding selection consistency result.
- Addresses an important gap between additive and high-order interaction models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical penalty is oversimplified and may not reflect true capacity; theoretical constants not linked to implementation.
- Limited scalability and no guarantees for the heuristic search; weak empirical evaluation without baselines.
- Fixed-p theory and reliance on strong assumptions limit applicability; clarity issues in the penalty derivation.

Overall, the paper offers interesting theoretical framing and a promising direction but requires stronger empirical validation, clearer penalty justification, and improved algorithms to reach NeurIPS standards.
