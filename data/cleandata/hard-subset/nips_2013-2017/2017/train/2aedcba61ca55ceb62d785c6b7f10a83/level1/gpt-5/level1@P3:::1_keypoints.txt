- Paper proposes "intrinsic group additive structure" (IGAS): a unique minimal partition G* with f(X)=∑u∈G* fu(Xu) lying between additive and full interaction models.  
- Authors formalize a refinement partial order on groupings, prove existence/uniqueness of a minimal amiable partition, propose a structure-penalized kernel method in direct-sum RKHS HG, and give consistency results for G* recovery.  
- Connections drawn to additive models, functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA, higher-order interaction kernels (e.g., ESP, SALSA), structured sparsity/MKL, and graphical models.  
- Strength: clear conceptual formalization of a poset over groupings and a target intrinsic minimal grouping for identifiability.  
- Strength: natural use of direct-sum RKHSs to encode group-additive structure and a capacity-based penalty reminiscent of SRM.  
- Strength: theoretical distinction between amiable/non-amiable structures, consistency results, and a covering-number bound for sums of RKHSs.  
- Strength: practical motivation to capture low-order interactions while avoiding combinatorial explosion and preserving interpretability.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1’s uniqueness relies on unstated conditions (e.g., independence of PX); identifiability conditions and failure modes under dependence are not clearly specified.  
- Weakness: Derivation from covering-number bounds to the practical penalty is inconsistent—the exponent drops and α is reparameterized and treated ad hoc, disconnecting the penalty from true kernel capacity.  
- Weakness: Theorem 4 requires “proper” λn, µn but gives no rates or data-driven tuning guidance; dependence on p and group sizes is not quantified.  
- Weakness: Kernel choice/hyperparameter selection (e.g., bandwidths/radii) across differing HG is unspecified, yet crucial for practical performance.  
- Weakness: Exhaustive search is intractable; the backward stepwise heuristic is greedy, lacks guarantees, fails on examples, and scalability/computational complexity are not analyzed.  
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to convex/structured-sparsity baselines (e.g., MKL, hierarchical kernel learning, SALSA, hierNet) and related literature is under-cited.  
- Clarity: Some derivations (Proposition 3→Eq.6) and key assumptions are under-explained; illustrative examples of non-uniqueness should be added and key proofs summarized.  
- Originality: The intrinsic minimal partition idea is novel, but selection via RKHS complexity penalties overlaps existing hierarchical kernel and structured sparsity methods.  
- Significance: Potentially useful if made practical, but current empirical evidence is limited to small simulations and a single small real dataset without strong baselines.  
- Empirical gaps: Missing baselines (SALSA, ANOVA KRR, sparse additive/interactions, MKL), no sensitivity analyses for λ/µ and kernel bandwidths, and insufficient runtime/scalability reporting.  
- Recommended improvements: tighten theory with explicit assumptions and tuning rates, ground penalty in kernel-dependent complexity or data-dependent measures, and develop scalable search/convex surrogates.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear formalization, theoretically grounded selection criterion, and conceptually stimulating contribution.  
- Cons for acceptance: inconsistent penalty derivation, unclear identifiability assumptions, poor scalability/algorithmic guarantees, and weak empirical validation.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak reject—idea promising but needs stronger theory, principled penalty/tuning, scalable algorithms, and thorough empirical comparisons.
