This paper addresses the problem of group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression, a crucial task in high-dimensional settings where traditional additive models may be too restrictive and full interaction models suffer from dimensionality issues. The authors rigorously define the “intrinsic” group additive structure and develop a structure-penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. The penalty term leverages a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches and positioning their work as a valuable contribution. The introduction effectively summarizes the relevant background (additive models, functional ANOVA, OKGT) and highlights the key challenges – non-uniqueness of group structures and lack of systematic identification methods. The proposed approach is theoretically sound, with a consistency proof provided in the supplementary material. The exploration of two algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) demonstrates practical consideration for different dimensionality scenarios. The presentation of simulation results and a real data application further strengthens the paper’s evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the problem, defining the intrinsic group additive structure and establishing consistency results for the proposed method. This is a significant strength, demonstrating the theoretical validity of the approach.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The introduction of a complexity measure based on covering numbers is a novel contribution and provides a principled way to penalize the complexity of group structures.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The development and comparison of two algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) make the method more practical for application to real-world datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear flow of ideas. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are reasonably presented.
* **Empirical Validation:** Simulation studies and a real-data example demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in identifying group structures and achieving good prediction performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm may be computationally prohibitive for high-dimensional datasets. While the stepwise approach addresses this, its greedy nature may lead to suboptimal solutions. Further exploration of more efficient search strategies could be beneficial. This is acknowledged by the authors but doesn't have a concrete solution.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method appears sensitive to the choice of tuning parameters (λ, μ, α). The paper discusses using a validation set for parameter selection, but a more systematic approach to parameter tuning could be explored.
* **Kernel Choice:** The impact of the kernel choice on the performance of the method is not thoroughly investigated. While the authors mention the use of universal kernels, a more detailed discussion of kernel selection and its influence on the results would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Real Data Application:** The real data application focuses on a single dataset (Boston Housing). While this provides a proof of concept, applying the method to a wider range of datasets would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Connection to Graphical Models - Superficial:** While the connection to graphical models is acknowledged, the discussion feels somewhat superficial. Expanding on the similarities and differences, and potentially exploring how insights from graphical models could be incorporated into the GASI framework, would be valuable.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
* The proposed method is theoretically sound and supported by empirical evidence.
* The novelty of the complexity measure and the development of practical algorithms contribute to the field.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm may limit its applicability.
* The sensitivity to tuning parameters and kernel choice requires careful consideration.
* The real data application is somewhat limited in scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical foundations are strong, the proposed method is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising. While there are some limitations, particularly regarding computational cost and parameter sensitivity, these are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the following:

1.  **Computational Efficiency:** Briefly discuss potential strategies for improving the computational efficiency of the stepwise algorithm or exploring alternative search methods.
2.  **Parameter Tuning:** Provide more details on the parameter tuning procedure used in the experiments, and consider exploring more systematic parameter selection techniques.
3.  **Kernel Sensitivity:** Discuss the impact of kernel choice and potentially explore the performance of different kernels.
4.  **Real Data Expansion:** If possible, include results on another real dataset to demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
