

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for studying query complexity in clustering with side information, addressing a fundamental gap between theoretical bounds and practical heuristics in interactive clustering. The authors tackle the problem of clustering n points into k unknown clusters with the help of an oracle (answering same-cluster queries) and side information in the form of a similarity matrix. Their key insight is that side information, generated from data similarity, can drastically reduce query complexity from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+‖f-))), where H is the Hellinger divergence between within-cluster and across-cluster similarity distributions.

**Originality and Novelty:** This work is highly original and addresses a significant gap in the literature. While previous work on interactive clustering focused on query complexity without side information (yielding Ω(nk) bounds), the authors demonstrate that side information - a critical component in practical applications like entity resolution - enables a dramatic reduction in query complexity. The paper generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- for within-cluster and across-cluster similarities, rather than restricting to Bernoulli distributions as in SBM. This is a bold theoretical generalization that opens up new research directions. The authors also develop a novel framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which is particularly impressive given the technical challenges of adaptive query algorithms.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. The paper directly addresses a critical problem in modern machine learning: how to reduce the cost of human-in-the-loop learning. By theoretically demonstrating that side information can reduce query complexity from linear in n to potentially sublinear, the work has immediate practical implications for applications like entity resolution, where crowdsourcing is expensive. The authors' experimental results on the Cora dataset validate their theoretical findings, showing that even with approximate similarity distributions, their greedy algorithm achieves performance close to the optimal strategy.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The authors provide tight upper and lower bounds that match within logarithmic factors, and their algorithms are computationally efficient and parameter-free (working without knowledge of f+, f-, or k). The paper's main technical contribution is the lower bound framework, which overcomes significant challenges in proving information-theoretic bounds for adaptive algorithms. The experimental validation, while limited to one dataset, is well-conducted and provides valuable insights into the practical implications of their theoretical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is compelling, the problem statement is clear, and the technical contributions are presented logically. The authors effectively connect their work to the broader literature on stochastic block models and entity resolution, and they clearly articulate how their work differs from previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:** The main limitation is the assumption of a perfect oracle, which doesn't reflect real-world crowdsourcing where human workers make errors. While the authors acknowledge this in a companion paper, they could have more thoroughly discussed this limitation in the main paper. Additionally, while the theoretical results are strong, the experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (Cora), which would be strengthened by additional validation on diverse datasets.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The paper clearly distinguishes itself from prior work. Unlike previous interactive clustering approaches that focus on pair-wise queries without side information, this work demonstrates the critical role of side information. The connection to SBM is particularly valuable, as it shows how the query complexity framework generalizes and extends existing results in community detection.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that bridges the gap between theory and practice in interactive clustering. The novelty of the problem formulation, the technical depth of the analysis, and the clear practical implications make this work stand out. The authors' bold approach of generalizing the stochastic block model to arbitrary distributions while providing tight query complexity bounds is exactly the kind of innovative work NIPS should prioritize.

While the assumption of a perfect oracle is a limitation, this is acknowledged by the authors and addressed in companion work. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The work opens up numerous promising directions for future research, including extending the framework to handle noisy queries and exploring applications in sparse SBM regimes.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant theoretical advance with clear practical relevance, and its novelty and bold approach align perfectly with NIPS's mission to publish groundbreaking work in machine learning. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and is likely to influence future research in interactive learning and clustering.
