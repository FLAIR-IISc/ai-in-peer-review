- Central result: Side information reduces oracle queries from Θ(nk) to ~Θ(k^2/H^2) (up to logs), where H^2 is squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−.

- Lower bounds: Any adaptive algorithm requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) expected queries (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for probability-1 success), even if k, f± known.

- Algorithms: Parameter-free Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms (not knowing k or f±) achieve query complexity within log n of the lower bound using estimated Hellinger-based statistics.

- Monte Carlo claim: O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries with high probability (as stated, but typesetting inconsistent).

- Las Vegas claim: O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries (typesetting inconsistent in the paper).

- Proof techniques: Novel adaptive lower-bound framework leveraging Hellinger divergence, product structure, and a “weak cluster” argument; upper bounds use Sanov-type analysis and iterative threshold updates.

- Model generality: Strict generalization of SBM—unknown k, arbitrary f± (finite support; lower bounds extend to continuous), adversarial cluster sizes allowed.

- Empirical motivation: Results help explain empirical gains in entity-resolution workflows using side information.

- Strength—conceptual clarity: Formalizes trade-off between side information and interactive query complexity, reconciling theory and practice.

- Strength—technical novelty: New adaptive lower-bound approach and parameter-free algorithm that estimates effective divergence.

- Strength—generality: Results hold for very general distributions and unknown/adversarial cluster parameters.

- Weakness—notation/typos: Repeated confusions between H and H^2, missing division symbols, and inconsistent statement of upper bounds impede comparison to lower bounds.

- Weakness—side-information assumptions: Analysis assumes i.i.d. entries from a single pair f±; conditional independence is crucial and heterogeneity is not rigorously handled.

- Weakness—proof sketch gaps: Key lemmas (anti-concentration, threshold convergence, finite-sample Hellinger control) are deferred to appendix with insufficient main-text overview.

- Weakness—limited experiments: Only one dataset (Cora), one side-info pipeline, no comparisons to existing ER/active clustering heuristics, no oracle-with-known-f± baseline, and limited scalability (time O(n^2)).

- Weakness—scope of recovery: Focuses on exact clustering; lacks discussion of approximate recovery or query/error trade-offs relevant to ER.

- Quality assessment: Technically solid and insightful use of Hellinger, but notation/typo issues hinder verification and experiments are too limited to show practical impact.

- Clarity issues: Paper is organized but marred by notational inconsistencies; recommends a notation table and a figure for iterative updates.

- Originality: High—both the adaptive lower-bound framework with side information and the parameter-free iterative algorithm are novel.

- Significance: High for theory of interactive/active clustering and entity resolution; extends SBM insights and may spur further work.

- Suggested fixes: Standardize H^2 notation and big-Oh bounds, provide stronger main-text proof overviews, and fix typesetting errors.

- Suggested extensions: Consider accelerated/anytime threshold updates given coarse priors, robustness theorems for mild heterogeneity, and empirical comparisons to standard ER heuristics.

- Practical clarification request: State whether computing the full similarity matrix W is assumed free and address its computational cost.

- Pros summary: Near-tight theoretical bounds, parameter-free adaptive algorithms, and conceptual bridge to SBM.

- Cons summary: Notational/typographical errors, deferred proof details, limited experiments, and strong i.i.d. side-information assumption.

- Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on fixing notation/clarity and, if possible, modestly strengthening experiments.
