

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the study of interactive clustering with side information. The authors investigate the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to a similarity matrix (side information) and can query an oracle about whether pairs of points belong to the same cluster. The key theoretical contribution is establishing tight bounds on query complexity that depend on the Hellinger divergence between the within-cluster and between-cluster similarity distributions. The work generalizes the stochastic block model and provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of this problem.

The paper builds upon recent work in entity resolution and interactive clustering [18, 40, 42, 43], but makes a crucial theoretical contribution by addressing the gap between practical heuristics (which use far fewer queries than O(nk)) and the theoretical lower bound. The authors show that with side information, the query complexity can be reduced to O(k log n / H²(f+‖f-)), which is a dramatic improvement over the O(nk) bound without side information.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound results with rigorous proofs for both the lower and upper bounds. The lower bound proof is particularly impressive, developing a novel framework for information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings. The algorithmic contributions (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) are computationally efficient (O(n²) time) and parameter-free, working without knowledge of f+, f-, or k. The experimental results on the Cora dataset support the theoretical claims, though more comprehensive experiments would strengthen the practical impact.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The connection to entity resolution provides strong motivation, and the technical content is presented logically. The introduction of the Hellinger divergence as a key metric is well-justified, and the algorithm description is clear. The proofs are thorough, though some technical details in the lower bound proof might challenge readers unfamiliar with information theory.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. It fills a significant gap in the literature by providing the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information. The model generalizes the stochastic block model by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f-, and the parameter-free algorithms represent a major advance over previous work that required knowledge of these parameters. The lower bound technique is novel and could be applicable to other interactive learning problems.

**Significance:** The results have substantial significance for both theory and practice. The dramatic reduction in query complexity (from O(nk) to O(k log n / H²(f+‖f-))) has direct implications for real-world applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering, where reducing the number of queries can significantly lower costs. The work also opens important theoretical directions for studying how query complexity changes with side information in community detection models.

**Strengths:**
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of interactive clustering with side information
- Tight information-theoretic lower bounds matching the upper bounds within a logarithmic factor
- Novel parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f+, f-, or k
- Strong connection to the stochastic block model and community detection literature
- Clear, well-organized presentation of complex theoretical material

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental evaluation (only one dataset, Cora)
- Could better articulate the practical impact of the theoretical results in real-world applications
- Doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach when side information is imperfect
- The connection to practical implementation could be strengthened

**Arguments for acceptance:** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a practically important problem. The results are technically sound, novel, and have clear implications for real-world applications. The paper fills a crucial gap in the literature and establishes tight bounds that will guide future work in interactive clustering.

**Arguments against acceptance:** While theoretically strong, the practical impact could be better demonstrated with more comprehensive experiments. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how the results translate to real-world scenarios.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper meets the standards of NIPS in terms of technical depth, novelty, and significance. The minor limitations regarding experimental evaluation and practical translation are outweighed by the strength of the theoretical results. I recommend acceptance with the suggestion that the authors expand the experimental section to include more datasets and provide a clearer discussion of practical implementation considerations.

The work represents a substantial advance in understanding the theoretical limits of interactive clustering with side information, and the techniques developed could be applicable to other problems in interactive learning. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning.
