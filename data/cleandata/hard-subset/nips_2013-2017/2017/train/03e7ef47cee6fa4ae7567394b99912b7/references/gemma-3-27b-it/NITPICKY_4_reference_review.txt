This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix. The authors establish both lower and upper bounds, demonstrating a reduction in query complexity when side information is available compared to clustering without it. They present a Monte Carlo algorithm that nearly matches the lower bound and a Las Vegas algorithm offering a stronger guarantee. The work builds upon and generalizes concepts from stochastic block models (SBM) and draws connections to entity resolution.

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical treatment of the problem. The attempt to provide information-theoretic lower bounds is commendable, and the generalization to arbitrary distributions f+ and f− is a noteworthy contribution. The connection to SBM is clearly articulated, and the authors position their work within the existing literature effectively. The iterative update algorithm proposed is a potentially valuable technique for developing parameter-free algorithms, which is highlighted as a significant benefit.

However, the paper is marred by an overwhelming number of minor, yet distracting, flaws. The writing style is often convoluted and repetitive. The introduction is lengthy and meanders, losing focus on the core problem statement. For instance, the extended discussion of entity resolution, while motivating, feels overly detailed and disrupts the flow. Several sentences are grammatically awkward and require careful parsing. Throughout the paper, mathematical notation is inconsistently used, with variables sometimes defined and sometimes not. The Hellinger divergence notation, while ultimately defined, is introduced mid-sentence without prior context.

The technical details are presented with a level of precision that borders on pedantry, but this precision often comes at the expense of clarity. The proof sketches lack sufficient intuition, making it difficult to follow the logic without meticulously working through the details. The proofs themselves, while likely correct, are dense and heavily reliant on established results without sufficient explanation of *why* those results apply in this specific context. The experimental section feels tacked on and lacks depth. The choice of dataset (cora) is reasonable, but the analysis is superficial. The comparisons to “best” and “greedy” strategies aren’t fully justified; it’s unclear what constitutes the “best” strategy and the rationale behind the greedy approach could be expanded. The claim of "close" performance with perturbed distributions needs more rigorous quantitative backing – a table showing precise F1 scores would be far more convincing.

The constant emphasis on parameter-freeness, while a strength, is occasionally overstated. While avoiding parameter tuning is desirable, the paper doesn't adequately discuss the practical implications of *not* having access to estimates of f+ and f−. In many real-world scenarios, some prior knowledge about the underlying distributions is available, and the paper doesn’t explore how that information could be incorporated.

**Specific criticisms:**

*   The sentence "However, lack of an ideal similarity function to compare objects makes ER an extremely challenging task" is a platitude.
*   The phrase “barring the Ω(nk) theoretical lower bound” is awkward.
*   The justification for choosing ‘dC log n’ as the initial cluster size in the algorithm is weak.
*   The “Discussion” section reads more like a future work proposal than a conclusion.
*   Numerous minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the text. (e.g., "fi,js" instead of f+, "fmfs" instead of pmfs).
*   The use of “e.g.” is often inappropriate; many instances should be “i.e.”

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis and novel lower bounds.
*   Generalization of SBM and potential relevance to entity resolution.
*   Development of a parameter-free algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Poor writing quality and lack of clarity.
*   Overly detailed and pedantic presentation.
*   Superficial experimental evaluation.
*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.
*   The sheer volume of minor issues detracts significantly from the paper’s overall quality.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the theoretical results, the pervasive flaws in presentation and clarity outweigh the contributions. The paper feels unnecessarily difficult to understand, and the numerous minor issues create a distracting reading experience. While the authors demonstrate technical competence, their ability to communicate that competence effectively is severely lacking. I recommend **rejection**. A major revision addressing the clarity, writing style, and presentation issues would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should focus on streamlining the presentation, providing more intuition behind the technical details, and conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation.
