Summary
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when the learner has access to (i) a same-cluster oracle that can be queried on pairs and (ii) a side-information matrix W whose entries are drawn from f+ if two points are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. Crucially, the algorithm does not know k, f+, or f−. The authors give:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive (possibly randomized) algorithms, showing any algorithm that succeeds with constant probability must use Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries, and for success with probability 1, Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries, even if k, f+, f− are known.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms: a Monte Carlo algorithm and a Las Vegas variant whose query complexities nearly match the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors; the dependence on f+, f− appears via the Hellinger divergence H^2(f+‖f−). The algorithms only use W and pairwise queries, and do not require knowledge of k, f+, f−.
- A generic lower-bound framework for interactive (adaptive) settings, leveraging Hellinger distances on product distributions, which may be of independent interest, and a connection to SBM as a special case.
- Limited experiments on a bibliographic dataset (CORA) illustrating query–accuracy tradeoffs; a greedy strategy informed by side information performs close to an oracle-best strategy.

Relation to prior work
The model generalizes stochastic block models (SBM) from Bernoulli edge probabilities to arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− over a finite alphabet (with discussion of extensions). Prior work on clustering with same-cluster queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al.) does not incorporate side information and typically yields Θ(nk) query complexity; heuristic crowdsourcing systems use side information but lack guarantees and often assume known parameters or specific distributions. The paper’s lower bounds address adaptive querying—where Fano-type arguments are tricky—by constructing “weakly queried” clusters and bounding error via Hellinger distances, which is novel in this context. Parameter-free recovery in general SBM settings is comparatively recent and typically assumes known k or balanced clusters; this work targets arbitrary partitions and unknown parameters.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: crisp characterization of how side information (via H^2(f+‖f−)) reduces query complexity, and a framework for lower bounds in adaptive interactive settings.
- Near-matching upper/lower bounds with only logarithmic gaps; algorithms are parameter-free and computationally efficient (O(n^2) time).
- Use of Hellinger divergence as the right measure both in lower and upper bounds, and an iterative-update scheme to estimate p+, p− and adapt thresholds—an appealing recipe for parameter-free design.
- The connection to SBM and discussion of how queries shift recovery thresholds is timely and opens a clear path for follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/consistency: there are inconsistent statements of the algorithmic query complexity (e.g., O(k/(log n H^2)) vs O(k^2 log n/H^2)); the final theorems suggest the latter matches the lower bound up to log n, but the text needs to be unified and unambiguous.
- Experimental evaluation is minimal: a single dataset, no comparison to strong baselines from [40, 43, 18], limited exploration of scaling in n, k, and H^2. For a theory paper this is not fatal, but a small synthetic study varying H^2, k, and cluster balance would strengthen the narrative substantially.
- Assumptions: the side information entries are independent conditioned on clusters; this can be violated in practice. The paper briefly mentions continuous distributions but focuses on finite alphabets; formal statements for continuous cases are deferred.
- Some technical steps in the lower bound are loose (e.g., Markov-based steps, constants, the H^2 ≤ 1/18 restriction), which may be improvable; nonetheless the rates appear right.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Strong on the theory side. The lower-bound construction for adaptive queries and the parameter-free algorithms are well-motivated. Proof sketches are sensible; full proofs are referenced to appendices. Main technical caveat is clarity of exact query complexities and the stated conditions.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable but marred by typos, notational inconsistencies, and formatting issues (missing years, footnote markers, ambiguous big-O expressions). The algorithm would benefit from cleaner pseudocode and a single theorem that clearly states success probability and query bounds.
- Originality: High. The adaptive lower-bound framework and parameter-free algorithms for general, unknown f+, f− with near-optimal query complexity are new and nontrivial. The Hellinger-based analysis is insightful.
- Significance: High theoretical significance with a plausible path to impacting practice (guiding active clustering and crowdsourcing). The SBM perspective and “how queries shift thresholds” question are valuable directions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify and correct all statements of query complexity for both algorithms; specify success probabilities (e.g., 1 − 1/n vs 1 − o(1)) and dependence on n, k, H^2 in a single theorem box.
- Provide concise pseudocode for the Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms in the main text.
- Clarify assumptions: finite alphabet vs continuous cases; independence of W; handling of highly unbalanced clusters; what happens when H^2 is very small.
- Include a synthetic study varying H^2, k, and cluster size distributions; add comparisons with representative heuristics (e.g., from [40, 43, 18]).
- Discuss memory/runtime when n is large; can the algorithm subsample W or operate in streaming/semi-streaming regimes?
- Tighten the constants and, if possible, relax the H^2 ≤ 1/18 condition; or explain its role.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel theoretical contribution with near-matching bounds and a general framework for adaptive lower bounds.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms that align with practice.
- Bridges two active areas (active clustering/crowdsourcing and SBM), opening new research directions.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation issues and inconsistent complexity claims need fixing.
- Empirical evaluation is weak and does not demonstrate scaling or comparisons to prior heuristics.
- Some assumptions on W may limit direct applicability.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new conceptual framework and near-optimal results that meaningfully advance our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the presentation and empirical limitations, which appear fixable.
