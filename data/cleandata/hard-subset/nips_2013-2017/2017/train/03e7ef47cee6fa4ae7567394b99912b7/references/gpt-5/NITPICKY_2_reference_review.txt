Summary
The paper studies interactive clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries in the presence of “side information” W: a noisy similarity matrix whose entries are drawn from f+ or f− depending on whether a pair is in the same cluster. The key contributions are: (i) information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries required to exactly recover the clustering; (ii) parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that achieve query complexity within a logarithmic factor of the lower bounds while not knowing k, f+, f−; (iii) a discussion of connections to SBM. The lower bound scales as Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) (plus an Ω(n) term for probability-1 recovery), and the algorithms aim for O(min{nk, k log n / H^2}) (Monte Carlo) and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}) (Las Vegas). A small experimental section on Cora illustrates query-accuracy tradeoffs under a greedy querying heuristic.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant problem: interactive clustering with oracle queries is widely used in ER and crowdsourcing.
- Formalization with side information that generalizes SBM and captures practitioner heuristics.
- Nontrivial information-theoretic lower bound in an adaptive setting; the constructive proof approach (reducing assignment of one point to k-hypothesis testing and controlling adaptivity) is interesting.
- Parameter-free algorithms are practically appealing and computationally efficient (claimed O(n^2) time).
- The dependence on Hellinger divergence is natural and yields clean, interpretable tradeoffs.
- The paper’s connection to SBM and discussion of how queries might shift recovery thresholds is thought-provoking.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Notation inconsistencies and potential errors:
  - Hellinger usage fluctuates between H and H2; sometimes “Hellinger divergence” denotes the non-squared metric, sometimes the squared version. Theorems mix H and H^2 without clear, consistent constants. E.g., Theorem 1 uses H2 in the statement but the algorithm’s thresholds use H(p+||p−) and H(p+||p−)^2 in confusing ways.
  - In Lemma 1 the displayed “probability of error is at least” expression appears negative in many regimes and even flips signs between the lemma statement and later derivations (e.g., “− 2/k (…) − … − …” vs “≤ 2/k (…) + … + …” in Step 4). As written, this cannot be a valid lower bound. This needs careful correction (constants, signs, and whether H or H^2 is used).
  - Step 2 defines β ambiguously (“β ≡ 1 / + √Q/(ak)” in the PDF text) and later uses β to derive cardinalities of J′ and J′′; the algebra is opaque and likely has missing factors of k. The line “(k − |J′|) · 2T/k(1−β) ≤ T” vs “2Tk(1−β)” suggests inconsistencies.
  - The inequality linking TV and Hellinger is misstated. Standard bounds are TV(P,Q) ≤ √2 H(P,Q) (where H is the Hellinger distance), or in terms of H^2 with appropriate constants. The paper writes “≤ √H(P||Q)” without specifying squared vs non-squared and constants.
  - Product distribution Hellinger bound: the text gives “≤ √2 · 2a H2(f+||f−)” leading to 2√a H(…), which mixes H and H^2. Please fix constants and present a clean lemma with proof or citation (1−∏(1−H_i^2) ≤ ∑ H_i^2).
- Algorithmic details and edge cases:
  - The algorithm assumes a “grown” cluster of size Θ(log n / H^2) will emerge via random querying in Phase 1/2. In adversarial clusterings (allowed by the model), there may be no cluster of size ≥ C log n (e.g., k≈n). What is the stopping rule and query complexity in that regime? How do you recover the O(nk) bound in worst case? This is not clearly spelled out.
  - The constant C is used both as a fixed algorithmic constant and as a cluster variable C, leading to confusion (e.g., “Membership(v, C) ≥ −( 4H(p+||p−)C − …)”).
  - Membership thresholds in Phase 3 have dimensionally odd forms with “± 2 H(p+||p−)^2 / (C √log n)”. Clarify the derivation, constants, and whether the thresholds scale with |C| or with log n/H^2.
  - The Monte Carlo algorithm’s success probability is stated as 1−o(1/n); this is extremely strong but the analysis sketch is insufficient. The Las Vegas guarantee says “recovers clusters with probability 1” but then gives a high-probability query complexity; please specify whether the complexity bound is in expectation or w.h.p., and separate correctness (probability 1) from runtime tail bounds.
  - The algorithm relies on discrete side information with fixed support size q. You claim extensions to larger/continuous spaces, but the algorithm (empirical histograms, Sanov) depends on discreteness. Please state the precise assumptions and give at least a lemma for continuous/discretized cases or clarify that the algorithms apply only to finite alphabets.
- Lower bound assumptions:
  - The assumption H2(f+||f−) ≤ 1/18 is idiosyncratic. Is this needed only for constants or for a particular inequality? Please justify and indicate how bounds behave outside this range.
- Relation to prior work:
  - The claim “first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information” should be moderated and positioned carefully relative to works on active learning for SBM, semi-supervised “must-link/cannot-link” clustering, and adaptive ER frameworks. Some relevant literature is missing or under-cited.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - Very limited: a single dataset, no baselines (e.g., [40, 43] heuristics) or comparisons to non-interactive SBM methods; no scaling studies vs k or H^2; no standard errors. The “best/worst/greedy” strategies are not directly tied to the proposed algorithm’s theory (and “best” is not implementable). There is no evaluation of the parameter-free iterative thresholds or their convergence.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., “st Conference on NIPS 2017,” stray footnote “1,” “therem,” inconsistent equation numbering, incomplete references without years/pages). The appendix-dependent core proofs are not summarized sufficiently in the main text.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practically motivated question with a clean theoretical framework.
- Provides near-matching upper/lower bounds with intuitive dependence on Hellinger divergence.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient approach is attractive.

Cons for acceptance
- Multiple technical inconsistencies and likely errors in the lower bound derivation and notation.
- Algorithmic description has unclear thresholds/edge cases; proofs are mostly deferred and the main text lacks rigor needed to verify claims.
- Empirical evaluation is too thin and not aligned with theory; no baseline comparisons.
- Presentation quality (typos, formatting, incomplete references) falls below NIPS standards.

Questions for authors
1. Fix the Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 statements with correct constants/signs and a consistent definition of H vs H^2 throughout. Provide a clean, self-contained lemma for product Hellinger on independent variables with exact constants used.
2. Clarify Phase 1/2 behavior when no cluster reaches Θ(log n/H^2) (e.g., k≈n). Provide a worst-case query bound and termination guarantee.
3. Precisely state the success probability and the type of query complexity bounds (expected vs w.h.p.) for both algorithms.
4. Specify assumptions on the side information alphabet; provide extensions or limitations for continuous f+, f−.
5. Expand experiments: include synthetic studies varying H^2 and k to validate the predicted scalings, and compare against strong baselines from [40, 43, 18].

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The core idea is strong and potentially impactful, but the current draft has too many technical/notation errors, underspecified algorithmic details in adversarial cases, and inadequate experiments. With corrected proofs, clearer exposition, and stronger empirical validation, this could become a solid contribution.
