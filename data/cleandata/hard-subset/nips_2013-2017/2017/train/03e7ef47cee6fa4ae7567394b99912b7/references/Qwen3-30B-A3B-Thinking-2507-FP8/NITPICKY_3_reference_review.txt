

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper addresses an important problem in interactive clustering: determining the minimum number of queries to an oracle needed to cluster data points when side information (a similarity matrix) is available. The authors develop a theoretical framework for analyzing query complexity, establish matching lower and upper bounds, and design efficient algorithms that work without knowledge of the underlying distributions f+ and f-.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering with side information. The connection to the stochastic block model is well-motivated and provides a natural bridge to existing literature. The authors demonstrate a dramatic reduction in query complexity from O(nk) to O(k / (log n · H²(f+||f-))), which is a substantial theoretical advance. The algorithms are computationally efficient and parameter-free, which is practically important. The experimental validation on the Cora dataset provides concrete evidence of the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

## Weaknesses

Despite the strong theoretical contributions, the paper contains numerous minor but significant technical inconsistencies that undermine the precision of the presentation. These issues, while individually small, collectively indicate a lack of meticulous attention to detail expected at NIPS.

1. **Mathematical notation inconsistencies**: The paper inconsistently uses "H2(f+‖f−)" and "H(f+‖f−)" to refer to the squared Hellinger divergence. The abstract states "O( k logn H2(f+‖f−) )" which should be "O(k / (log n · H²(f+||f-)))" for clarity. In Theorem 2, the condition "H2(f+‖f−) ≤ 118" is clearly a typo that should be "H2(f+‖f−) ≤ 1/18".

2. **Algorithmic description flaws**: The algorithm description uses a constant "C" without specification, contradicting the claim that the algorithm is "parameter free." The phrase "C logn H(p+,x‖p−,x)2" in the algorithm description is confusing and should be clarified as "C log n / H²(p+,x||p-,x)".

3. **Terminology inconsistencies**: The paper uses both "Hellinger divergence" and "Hellinger square divergence" interchangeably, though it defines H² as the squared Hellinger divergence. In the experimental section, they state "Hellinger square divergence between the two distributions is 0.6" which is redundant since H² is already the squared measure.

4. **Technical inaccuracies**: In Lemma 1, the authors state "a = nk" when describing equally sized clusters (n = ka), which should be "a = n/k" for consistency with their notation. The paper claims f+ and f- can be continuous distributions, but the Hellinger divergence is typically defined for discrete distributions, creating a minor theoretical inconsistency.

5. **Minor typographical errors**: The paper contains inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "O( k logn H2(f+‖f−) )" should have spaces around the operators), inconsistent use of "+" and "-" as superscripts, and a typo in "f+ and f− can be arbitrary probability mass functions (pmfs)" where "pmfs" should be "pmfs."

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information, the numerous technical inconsistencies in mathematical notation and presentation are concerning. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading to ensure consistent notation and precise mathematical statements. These minor issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility as a rigorous theoretical contribution.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is strong, but the presentation issues are significant enough to warrant rejection. The authors have made a valuable theoretical contribution, but the inconsistent presentation makes it difficult to fully trust the technical claims. The field of interactive clustering would benefit from a more polished version of this work.

## Recommendation

Reject. While the theoretical contribution is significant, the numerous technical inconsistencies in notation and presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors should address these minor but pervasive issues before resubmitting to NIPS or another venue. The paper would be substantially stronger with a more consistent and precise mathematical presentation.
