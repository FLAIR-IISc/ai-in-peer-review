This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, framing it as an interactive problem with an oracle. The central question – how side information reduces the number of queries needed compared to the theoretical lower bound of O(nk) – is compelling and directly relevant to practical applications like entity resolution where human-in-the-loop approaches are common. The work establishes a strong connection to the stochastic block model (SBM), generalizing it significantly by allowing arbitrary, unknown distributions for within- and between-cluster similarity. The authors provide both information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms that nearly match these bounds, demonstrating a potentially significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem formulation, connecting interactive clustering with oracle queries and side information, is a fresh perspective. The generalization of the SBM to arbitrary distributions *f+* and *f-* is particularly noteworthy. The development of information-theoretic lower bounds in the interactive setting, a notoriously difficult area, is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper offers a rigorous theoretical analysis, including lower bounds and matching upper bounds on query complexity. The use of Hellinger divergence as a measure of distributional difference is well-justified. The adaptation of Sanov’s theorem is a clever move.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivating example of entity resolution grounds the work in a real-world problem. The potential for reducing the number of queries in practical systems is significant.
* **Algorithmic Contribution:** The proposed Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms are parameter-free and efficient, offering a practical solution to the problem. The iterative update strategy shows promise for developing further parameter-free algorithms.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper thoughtfully positions the work within the context of existing research on clustering, community detection, and active learning. The discussion of related work is comprehensive and highlights the paper's unique contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Validation Limited:** The experimental evaluation on the Cora dataset is somewhat limited. While it demonstrates the performance of the greedy strategy, it lacks a more thorough comparison to existing entity resolution heuristics and a sensitivity analysis to the choice of the similarity function.
* **Lower Bound Assumptions:** The lower bound relies on knowing *k*, *f+*, and *f-* which is unrealistic in many scenarios. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a significant limitation. The scaling of the lower bound with respect to *k* needs more discussion in relation to the algorithm's performance.
* **Constant Factors:** The logarithmic factors in the upper bounds might hide substantial constant factors, impacting practical performance. A more detailed analysis of these factors would be beneficial.
* **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** While the algorithmic outline is provided, a more detailed pseudocode or implementation description would enhance reproducibility. The convergence analysis could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a fundamentally important and previously unaddressed question in clustering.
* The theoretical contributions are strong and provide valuable insights into the power of side information.
* The algorithms are well-motivated and potentially impactful for real-world applications.
* The connection to the SBM provides a solid foundation for future research.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms.
* The assumptions required for the lower bound are restrictive.
* The constant factors in the upper bounds could be significant.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper with significant novelty and theoretical depth. While the experimental validation is limited and the lower bound assumptions are restrictive, the core contributions are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces a compelling new research direction and provides a solid foundation for future work in interactive clustering with side information. The value of the novel theoretical results outweighs the minor limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the experimental limitations in a future version. I encourage the authors to consider a more extensive experimental evaluation and provide more detailed pseudocode to improve reproducibility.
