Summary and contributions:
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when, in addition to access to a same-cluster oracle, the algorithm has access to side information in the form of a noisy pairwise similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster). The main contributions are:
- An information-theoretic lower bound showing that, even when f+, f−, and k are known, any (possibly adaptive) algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) expected queries to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1, where H^2 is squared Hellinger divergence.
- Two parameter-free, efficient algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not assume knowledge of k, f+, f−. The Monte Carlo algorithm purportedly achieves O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries with success probability 1 − o(1/n), and the Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries with probability 1.
- A general lower-bound framework for interactive settings that handles adaptivity by combining an under-queried-element argument with product Hellinger distance bounds.
- A parameter-free iterative-update method that estimates p+ and p− from the current clustering and uses Hellinger-based tests to decide when side information suffices versus when to query.
- A small experiment on the Cora ER dataset demonstrating that a greedy query strategy guided by side information attains good F1 with far fewer than nk queries, and is robust to small perturbations of f+, f−.

Relation to prior work:
The paper connects to and substantially generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary, unknown f+ and f− and adversarial cluster sizes. It also relates to query-based clustering work (e.g., same-cluster queries; interactive feedback; crowdsourced ER), and to active learning for SBM. Compared to [Ashtiani et al.] on k-means with separation and metric assumptions, this work provides information-theoretic lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms for a different oracle model and broader side-information distributions. The lower-bound framework is related in spirit to information-constrained proofs using Fano-type inequalities, but the adaptive query aspect necessitates a different construction.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important practical gap: why heuristic systems need far fewer than nk queries. The analysis identifies H^2(f+||f−) as the right information-theoretic quantity governing query savings.
- Lower bounds that handle adaptive querying and unknown parameters are nontrivial and of independent interest.
- Parameter-free algorithms are valuable in practice; the iterative-update idea that adapts thresholds based on empirical p+, p− is elegant and potentially reusable.
- The Las Vegas algorithm’s query complexity matches the lower bound up to a log n factor, which is tight up to logs under broad assumptions.
- Clear conceptual connection to SBM and recovery thresholds, and interesting conjecture on how oracle queries might shift SBM thresholds.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Major clarity/inconsistency: The paper states different upper bounds for the Monte Carlo algorithm in different places. Early text says O(k/(log n·H^2)); Theorem 1 says O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}); the lower bound is Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}). As written, the Monte Carlo bound appears to contradict the lower bound by a factor of k (super-constant), not just a log factor. I suspect typos, and that the intended Monte Carlo complexity may be O(min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}), aligning with the lower bound up to logs, or that the lower bound’s k^2 term has a different dependence (e.g., on success probability). The authors must reconcile these statements precisely and consistently, including the placement of log n (numerator vs denominator).
- Notational confusion around Hellinger: H^2 is defined as squared Hellinger divergence, but later steps use inequalities like ||P−Q||TV ≤ sqrt(H(P||Q)), which seems dimensionally inconsistent unless H denotes squared Hellinger and the inequality uses TV ≤ sqrt(2)·Hellinger. Please standardize notation and constants (e.g., TV ≤ Hellinger ≤ sqrt(KL/2), etc.).
- Lower bound proof sketch: While the high-level idea is plausible, several steps are delicate:
  - The construction of the “weak” clusters set and the choice of β, T should be made precise; constants and inequalities currently look loose and hard to verify.
  - The transition from total variation to Hellinger for product distributions needs explicit justification (use tensorization and subadditivity of squared Hellinger for independent coordinates).
  - The step using Markov’s inequality on E2 (≥ k−1 queries) largely drives the k^2 term; a clearer argument or tighter bound would strengthen confidence.
- Assumptions on f+, f−: The algorithms are analyzed for discrete distributions with fixed support size; the extension to larger finite alphabets is only sketched. Claims that the lower bound holds for continuous distributions are asserted without detail—some care is needed in defining H^2 and Sanov-type arguments beyond finite alphabets.
- Algorithmic details: Several thresholds (constants C, B) and the precise stopping/convergence criteria rely on Sanov-type bounds. The analysis relies on Hoeffding/Sanov; providing precise concentration statements for Hellinger-based empirical estimates would help reproducibility.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: only one dataset, no comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., popular ER heuristics or query-efficient clustering methods), no synthetic sweeps varying k, imbalance, and H^2. The evaluation measures F1 vs. number of queries but does not report exact recovery rates or how often side information alone suffices, nor scaling with k and H^2 as predicted.

Quality:
The technical direction is strong, and the lower/upper bound program is compelling. However, the inconsistencies in the stated bounds and notational ambiguities hinder verification. The proof sketches provided are promising but need tightening and consistent constants. The empirical evidence is suggestive but insufficient.

Clarity:
Generally well-motivated and well-organized, but the paper suffers from several typographical and internal inconsistencies (k vs k^2, placement of log n, H vs H^2). The algorithm description could be streamlined, and assumptions (finite alphabet, independence, oracle correctness) should be stated prominently. Please fix numerous minor typos and incomplete references.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first general interactive clustering analysis with arbitrary unknown f+, f− establishing information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching parameter-free algorithms.

Significance:
High, if the inconsistencies are resolved. The results cleanly quantify when and how side information reduces query complexity, with implications for active ER/crowdsourcing and connections to SBM thresholds.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and relevant problem formulation; clear separation between with/without side information.
- Strong lower-bound framework for adaptive querying.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms with near-optimal query complexity.
- Bridges theory and practice in interactive clustering.

Cons for acceptance:
- Apparent contradiction between Monte Carlo upper bound and lower bound; unclear log/k factors.
- Notational and constant-factor confusion in proofs.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of baselines.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept, conditional on clarifying and correcting the upper-bound statements (especially the k vs k^2 and log n placement) and tightening the notation and proofs. Strengthening the experiments with synthetic sweeps and baseline comparisons would further solidify the paper.
