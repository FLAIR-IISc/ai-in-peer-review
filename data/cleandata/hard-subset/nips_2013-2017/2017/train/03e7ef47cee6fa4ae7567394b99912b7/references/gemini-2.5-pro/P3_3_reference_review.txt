This paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information, aiming to explain the discrepancy between the theoretical Ω(nk) query complexity lower bound and the much better performance of practical heuristics. The central idea is that these heuristics implicitly use side information (e.g., a similarity matrix) to guide their queries. The authors formalize this by modeling the side information as a matrix where entries are drawn from one of two distributions, f+ or f-, depending on whether the corresponding pair of points lies in the same or different clusters.

The main contributions are twofold. First, the paper establishes a strong information-theoretic lower bound on the query complexity, showing that Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) queries are necessary for exact recovery, where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f-. This elegantly connects the problem's hardness to the statistical distinguishability of the side information. Second, the authors present nearly matching upper bounds via two efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that achieve a query complexity of O(min{nk, (k² log n)/H²(f+||f-)}). A key aspect of these algorithms is their ability to work without prior knowledge of the number of clusters (k) or the distributions f+ and f-. The authors also correctly situate their work as a significant generalization of the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Excellent Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap between theory and practice in interactive clustering. The model of side information is general, well-motivated by applications like entity resolution, and provides a powerful framework for analysis.
2.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The information-theoretic lower bound (Theorem 2) is the paper's strongest result. Proving such a bound for an adaptive querying model is non-trivial, as standard techniques like Fano's inequality do not directly apply. The developed proof technique, which identifies elements that are infrequently queried and analyzes the multi-hypothesis testing problem for them, is novel and could be of independent interest.
3.  **Near-Optimal and Practical Algorithms:** The development of parameter-free algorithms that nearly match the lower bound is a major achievement. In theoretical work, algorithms often require knowledge of underlying model parameters, limiting their practical use. The iterative-update scheme in the Monte Carlo algorithm, which adaptively estimates the distributions and determines the required cluster size for confident classification, is a clever and practically relevant technique.
4.  **Generality and Connection to SBM:** The framework significantly generalizes the SBM by allowing arbitrary (and unknown) distributions f+ and f-, arbitrary cluster sizes, and an adversarial partition. This connection firmly places the work within a major stream of research in machine learning and statistics while pushing its boundaries. The discussion on how queries might alter the SBM recovery threshold is insightful and opens up exciting avenues for future work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of the Lower Bound Proof Sketch:** While the high-level idea of the lower bound proof is conveyed, the sketch in Section 2 is very dense. The introduction of `β ≡ 1 + sqrt(Q/ak)` and the subsequent steps in partitioning the clusters into `J'` and `J''` lack sufficient intuition. A few sentences explaining why these specific forms and thresholds are chosen would greatly enhance the readability and accessibility of this core technical contribution.
2.  **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section feels more like a proof-of-concept than a thorough validation. While it demonstrates that the algorithm works on a real dataset, it could be made much stronger.
    *   **Baselines:** The paper is motivated by the superior performance of existing heuristics ([42, 43, 40]). A direct comparison against one or two of these state-of-the-art heuristics on the same dataset would be far more compelling and would directly substantiate the paper's central claim.
    *   **Algorithm Mapping:** It is not entirely clear how the "best", "worst", and "greedy" strategies tested relate to the algorithm described in Section 3. The algorithm specifies a clear procedure; the evaluation should ideally test *that* algorithm, rather than separate query strategies.
3.  **The log n Gap:** There remains a logarithmic gap between the upper and lower bounds. While this is common in the field, it would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss whether they believe this gap is an artifact of their analysis (e.g., due to the use of concentration inequalities) or if it might be fundamental.
4.  **Assumptions on Distributions:** The upper bound assumes discrete distributions with a fixed support cardinality. While the authors note the results extend, a brief discussion on how the complexity scales with the support size in the main text would be helpful. Similarly, the lower bound requires H²(f+||f-) ≤ 1/18. While this is a technical condition for a region where the problem is non-trivial, it appears somewhat arbitrary. A short justification for this choice would be welcome.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. The work is technically deep, addressing a well-motivated problem with strong theoretical results (nearly tight bounds) and a novel, practical algorithmic approach. The connection to and generalization of the SBM is a major plus. Despite some weaknesses in the experimental section and the density of the proof sketches, the core theoretical contributions are outstanding. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and advances the state of the art. It is very likely to be influential and to inspire follow-up work at the intersection of active learning, clustering, and community detection. I strongly recommend acceptance.
