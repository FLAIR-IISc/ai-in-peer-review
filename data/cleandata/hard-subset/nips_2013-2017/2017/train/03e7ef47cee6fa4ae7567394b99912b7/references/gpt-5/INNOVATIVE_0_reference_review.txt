Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm can ask same-cluster queries to an oracle and has access to side information: a noisy similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) or f− (across-cluster). The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds for any (possibly adaptive, randomized) algorithm with knowledge of k, f+, f−: Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}), and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1, where H denotes Hellinger divergence (squared).
- Two efficient, parameter-free algorithms (k, f+, f− unknown). A Monte Carlo algorithm achieves O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries with high probability; a Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries almost surely. Both run in O(n^2) time (dominated by reading W).
- A general lower-bound technique for interactive, adaptive settings (beyond non-interactive Fano-type), and an iterative-update design that estimates H(f+‖f−) on the fly using Hellinger on empirical “intra/inter” distributions to set sample thresholds.
- Connections to SBM are discussed; the model strictly generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary unknown f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes. Experiments on a bibliographic dataset (CORA) illustrate strong query efficiency using a practical greedy strategy.

Quality
- Theory: The lower-bound approach—selecting a lightly queried element, constructing “weak” clusters, and bounding correct-assignment probability via total variation–Hellinger relations—appears sound and novel for adaptive query settings. The upper bounds, using an iterative estimation of p+ and p− and Hellinger-based membership tests with Sanov-type concentration, plausibly deliver the stated complexities. That said, several technical steps (e.g., the adaptation of Sanov from KL to Hellinger bounds, anti-concentration for Waiting(C)) are deferred to the appendix; a clearer proof sketch in the main text would improve readability and verification.
- Algorithms: The parameter-free iterative-update idea is elegant and practical. The use of Hellinger as the central measure on both sides (lower and upper bounds) is conceptually clean and likely useful elsewhere.
- Experiments: Empirical validation is limited: one real dataset, no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., [40, 43, 18] or recent active SBM methods), and no synthetic experiments to validate scaling with k and H^2 as predicted. The CORA results are encouraging but do not fully substantiate the claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with helpful context and connections to SBM and ER. However, there are notational inconsistencies and typos that impede clarity:
  - The query complexities sometimes appear as O(k log n H^2) instead of O((k log n)/H^2). Please standardize and use explicit fractions.
  - H vs H^2 is sometimes mixed; define once and use consistently.
  - Precisely state discrete vs continuous assumptions where needed; Theorem 1 assumes discrete fixed support, while lower bounds allow continuous—clarify how discretization/estimation impacts guarantees.
- Providing concise pseudocode for the algorithms and a figure illustrating the iterative-update loop would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- High. The paper offers a new interactive learning lower-bound framework that handles adaptivity, quantifies the benefit of side information in terms of Hellinger divergence, and delivers nearly tight parameter-free algorithms for unknown k and unknown f+, f− across arbitrary cluster size distributions. The unification around Hellinger is novel and insightful.

Significance
- The results give a clean, near-tight characterization of how side information reduces query complexity, which is highly relevant to ER/crowdsourcing and active community detection. The lower-bound technique and the parameter-free iterative estimation scheme are likely to influence follow-up work in interactive learning and active inference for structured problems. Empirical significance would be clearer with broader experiments and comparisons.

Pros
- Conceptually bold and novel framework; quantifies value of side information.
- Nearly matching upper/lower bounds, parameter-free, and supports arbitrary unknown f+, f−.
- Generalization and connection to SBM with insightful discussion of thresholds.
- Practical algorithmic idea (iterative-update with Hellinger) that may transfer to other problems.

Cons
- Limited empirical evaluation: one dataset, no baselines, no synthetic studies to validate scaling with k and H^2, and no runtime/memory analysis for large n.
- Some proofs and crucial constants deferred; adaptation from KL-based Sanov to Hellinger-based bounds should be highlighted more clearly in the main text.
- Notational inconsistencies (e.g., missing division by H^2) and a few typos hinder clarity.
- Assumes noiseless oracle; a brief robustness discussion or small experiment would be valuable, even if the companion paper addresses noise.

Questions for the authors
- Please standardize the query complexity expressions and clarify whether they are O((k log n)/H^2) and O((k^2 log n)/H^2).
- Can you add synthetic experiments to validate the predicted scaling in k and H^2, and compare against heuristics [40, 43, 18] and active SBM [19]?
- How sensitive is the algorithm to discretization choices when f+, f− are continuous?
- Could you briefly outline in the main text how Sanov-style arguments are adapted to Hellinger-based deviations?
- Any practical guidance on memory/time for building and using W at scale, and on parallelization?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new direction with strong, general lower bounds and nearly matching parameter-free algorithms. Despite the modest experiments and some clarity issues, the conceptual novelty and potential impact are high.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
