Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm can make adaptive same-cluster queries to an oracle and also has access to a noisy similarity matrix W (“side information”). The side information is generated by an unknown pair of distributions f+ (for within-cluster pairs) and f− (for cross-cluster pairs), with no assumptions on k or cluster sizes. The main results are:

- Lower bounds: An information-theoretic framework yields that any (possibly randomized) algorithm, even with full knowledge of k, f+, f−, requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) expected queries (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1), in the regime H^2(f+‖f−) ≤ 1/18. The proof handles adaptivity by identifying “weakly queried” clusters and using a Hellinger-based change-of-measure argument.

- Upper bounds: Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that do not know k, f+, f−. A Monte Carlo algorithm recovers the clustering with high probability and uses O(·) queries scaling polylogarithmically in n and inversely with H^2(f+‖f−), and a Las Vegas variant achieves success probability 1 with O(n log n + ·) queries. The algorithm iteratively estimates empirical intra/inter distributions from W, computes thresholds proportional to log n/H^2, and assigns points to “grown” clusters either via side information (when statistically reliable) or a small number of queries for borderline cases. Experiments on Cora illustrate that a greedy query strategy guided by side information achieves high F1 with far fewer queries than nk.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bound technique is careful and novel for adaptive querying, reducing to a k-hypothesis test for a “lightly queried” point and controlling total variation via Hellinger divergence for product distributions. The upper bound analysis uses large deviations (Sanov-type) to control deviations between empirical and true distributions and justifies the log n/H^2 thresholds. The parameter-free design is nontrivial and practically relevant.

- Rigor/support: Most proof details are in the appendices, but the main ideas are conveyed. However, there are inconsistencies in stated bounds that must be corrected: at several places the Monte Carlo query complexity alternates between O(k log n/H^2) and O(k^2 log n/H^2), while the lower bound has k^2/H^2. The Las Vegas bound is stated as O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}), which matches the lower bound up to a log factor; the Monte Carlo bound should be clarified so it does not appear to contradict the lower bound. The regime assumptions (e.g., H^2 ≤ 1/18) are explicit.

- Empirics: The experiment is illustrative but limited (single dataset, modest ablations). It supports the practical viability of side information but does not stress-test the claimed scalings with k or H^2, or compare against strong baselines on multiple datasets.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-motivated, with clear problem setup and connections to entity resolution and SBM. The iterative-update algorithm is described at a high level; pseudocode would help reproducibility.

- Notational consistency needs attention: H vs H^2 is sometimes conflated; several theorem statements contain likely typos (k/log n/H^2 vs k log n/H^2; k vs k^2 dependences). References have formatting issues.

Originality
- The paper addresses an important and less-explored question: the effect of side information on the query complexity of exact clustering, with unknown parameters and adaptive queries. The information-theoretic lower bound framework for adaptive interactive learning is novel and potentially reusable. The parameter-free algorithmic design that leverages iterative estimation of f+ and f− and Hellinger-based thresholds is also new. The work goes substantially beyond standard SBM (unknown parameters, arbitrary cluster sizes, general discrete/continuous distributions).

Significance
- The results sharpen our understanding of when and how side information can reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) to near O(log n/H^2) per “degree of freedom,” nearly matching the lower bound. This has implications for active clustering/crowdsourcing and for active extensions of SBM. The lower-bound methodology may transfer to other interactive inference problems. On the practical side, the O(n^2) time/space reliance on W and the discrete support assumption may limit immediate deployment, but the approach is a strong theoretical contribution.

Pros
- Novel adaptive-information lower bound technique; handles unknown k, f+, f−.
- Parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal query complexity (log factor gap).
- General model subsumes SBM; clear connection to community detection thresholds.
- Useful use of Hellinger distance as the right measure for both bounds and algorithms.
- Practical intuition validated on a real dataset; greedy strategy close to best.

Cons
- Inconsistencies/typos in the main query-complexity statements (k vs k^2, missing parentheses, H vs H^2), which obscure the true guarantees.
- Empirical evaluation is limited (single dataset, no comparisons to strong baselines beyond variants of the proposed strategy, no stress tests vs k and H^2).
- Assumption of discrete W; continuous-case handling is asserted for lower bounds but not fully addressed algorithmically (quantization?).
- Clarity can be improved (pseudocode, cleaner constants, tighter exposition of Phase 2 convergence, explicit dependence on unknown k).

Questions for the authors
1. Please reconcile the stated bounds: What is the exact query complexity of the Monte Carlo algorithm—O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) or O(min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2})? As written, parts of the paper appear to contradict the lower bound.
2. How essential is the discrete-support assumption algorithmically? Can you quantize continuous W with provable impact on H^2 and the thresholds?
3. Can you provide pseudocode and a precise accounting showing why |Waiting(C)| is O(1) (independent of n and k) with high probability?
4. Could you add experiments varying k and the separation between f+ and f− (synthetic or semi-synthetic) to verify the predicted log n/H^2 and k- or k^2-scaling?

Recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with novel lower bounds and near-matching parameter-free algorithms, addressing an important question with clear connections to SBM and active clustering. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on fixing the inconsistencies in the main theorems and improving clarity. Expanded experiments would further strengthen the submission but are secondary to the core theory.
