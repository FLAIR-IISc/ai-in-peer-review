This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, framing it as an interactive problem with an oracle. The authors establish both lower and upper bounds, demonstrating a reduction in query complexity when side information (a similarity matrix) is available. They connect their work to the stochastic block model (SBM) and propose algorithms designed to be parameter-free, making them practically appealing.

The core idea – leveraging side information to reduce the number of oracle queries needed for clustering – is compelling and directly addresses a crucial gap in the theoretical understanding of practical clustering heuristics. The connection to SBM is well-motivated, and the extension to arbitrary distributions *f+* and *f-* is a valuable generalization. However, the presentation and technical rigor, while aiming for perfection, ultimately hinder the overall impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The theoretical investigation of query complexity with side information is a novel contribution, bridging a gap between theoretical analysis and practical algorithms.
*   **Generality:** The work extends beyond the typical SBM assumptions, handling arbitrary distributions *f+* and *f-* and without assuming knowledge of *k*.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** Developing algorithms that don't require knowledge of *f+*, *f-* or *k* is a significant advantage for real-world applicability.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The authors attempt a rigorous theoretical analysis, including information-theoretic lower bounds.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Detailed and Dense Introduction:** The introduction is excessively long and packed with citations. It jumps between motivations, related work, and contributions without a clear, concise narrative. The constant referencing feels like padding rather than genuine contextualization.
*   **Technical Inconsistencies/Ambiguities:** Several technical aspects lack clarity. The definitions of *f+* and *f-* are initially stated as probability mass functions (pmfs), but later appear as distributions over a fixed set, causing confusion. The notation is inconsistent – for instance, using both *f+* and *H(f+‖f−)* without consistently defining the relationship.
*   **Lower Bound Proof – Lack of Detail:** The lower bound proof, while claiming generality, relies on significant simplification and lacks sufficient detail to fully assess its correctness. The jump from Lemma 1 to Theorem 2 is not fully justified, and the assertion about continuous distributions is unsubstantiated. The reference to Fano's inequality being inapplicable isn't elaborated upon sufficiently – what specific challenge arises?
*   **Algorithm Description – Ambiguity & Lack of Clarity:** The algorithm description, particularly Phase 2, is vague. Terms like "sufficiently large" and "constant" are used without quantification, making it difficult to understand the precise behavior of the algorithm. What is ‘dC’ in the initialization phase?
*   **Experimental Evaluation – Limited Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Cora) and a single similarity function. It lacks a more comprehensive assessment of the algorithm's performance on diverse datasets and under different conditions. The discussion of the results is also somewhat superficial.
*   **Formatting & Style:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, improper use of symbols). While seemingly trivial, these details detract from the overall professionalism of the manuscript. The sheer number of citations in a single paragraph is often distracting.
*   **Unnecessary Claims & Repetition:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the “parameter-free” nature of their algorithms, even when it doesn’t add significant value. Some statements feel redundant and contribute to the overall length of the paper.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The paper is excessively long and dense, making it difficult to follow the technical details.
*   The technical presentation lacks clarity and contains inconsistencies.
*   The lower bound proof and algorithm description are insufficiently detailed.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and lacks depth.
*   Numerous minor formatting and stylistic issues detract from the overall quality of the manuscript.

**For:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to current research in clustering.
*   The authors establish a theoretical foundation for understanding the benefits of side information.
*   The proposed algorithms are parameter-free, which is a significant advantage in practice.
*   The connection to the SBM is well-motivated and provides valuable context.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novel contributions and potentially significant results, I recommend **rejection**. The paper suffers from a lack of clarity, technical inconsistencies, and an overly detailed presentation. The lower bound and algorithm descriptions require significant refinement and expansion before they can be confidently accepted. While the core ideas are promising, the current manuscript falls short of the standards expected for NIPS due to a relentless pursuit of detail that obscures rather than illuminates the core contributions. A thorough revision, focusing on clarity, conciseness, and technical rigor, is necessary before this work can be considered for publication.
