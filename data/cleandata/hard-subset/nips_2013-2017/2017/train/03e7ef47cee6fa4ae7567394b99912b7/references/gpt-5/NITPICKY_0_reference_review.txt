Summary
This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information: given n items partitioned into k unknown clusters, an oracle answers same-cluster queries, and a noisy similarity matrix W is provided where within- and across-cluster entries are drawn from unknown distributions f+ and f−. The authors (i) derive information-theoretic lower bounds that account for adaptive querying, (ii) propose parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) whose query complexities depend on the Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−, and (iii) provide a brief empirical study on a Cora-based ER dataset. The work generalizes beyond SBM by allowing arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes, and argues that side information can reduce queries from Θ(nk) to roughly Θ(k log n / H^2(f+||f−)) in some regimes. The lower bound claims Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries (plus Ω(n) for success prob. 1).

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely: formalizes interactive clustering with side information, a setting motivated by entity resolution and active crowdsourcing.
- Information-theoretic lower bound in an adaptive querying setting is nontrivial and of independent interest.
- Parameter-free algorithms that estimate f+, f− on the fly; the iterative-update idea and use of Hellinger distance are interesting and potentially reusable.
- Clear connection drawn to SBM, including discussion of how queries might shift recovery thresholds.
- Claimed computational cost bounded by O(n^2), i.e., the size of W.

Major weaknesses and concerns
1) Inconsistency between upper and lower bounds. The lower bound (Theorem 2) states any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) expected queries (for nontrivial success probability), whereas Theorem 1 claims a Monte Carlo algorithm with query complexity O(min{nk, k log n / H^2}). For parameter regimes where k^2/H^2 dominates, this would contradict the lower bound. The text later says the Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}), which matches the lower bound up to log n, but then the Monte Carlo bound cannot hold as stated. This is a core technical inconsistency that must be resolved unambiguously.

2) Proof presentation issues and possible errors. The lower bound section has several problematic details:
- Lemma 1 statement appears malformed: it reports a lower bound on error probability as a sum of negative terms without the leading “1 − (…)” that the subsequent derivation suggests. As written, it can be negative.
- The definition of β in Step 2 is garbled; β is used inside (1−β) but appears >1 as printed, making expressions like 2Tk(1−β) negative. This is not a cosmetic issue; it undermines the logic selecting “weak” clusters.
- Several inequalities involving total variation and Hellinger are asserted with constants that should be stated precisely; the cited relation [38, Eq. (3)] should be reconciled with the exact Hellinger variant used here.
- There are jumps in switching between H and H^2; ensure consistent use throughout proofs and statements.

3) Model/assumptions mismatch across results. The algorithms explicitly require discrete f+, f− with fixed finite support (for empirical distribution estimates and the Sanov-style arguments). The lower bound claims to cover discrete or continuous. Please clarify:
- Are the algorithmic guarantees only for discrete support? If so, state this prominently; if continuous is allowed via discretization, quantify the induced bias and its effect on query and error guarantees.

4) Algorithmic details and worst-case validity. The Monte Carlo algorithm’s Phase 1 requires growing at least one cluster to Θ(log n) size before relying on side information. Under the stated adversarial partition model (no assumptions on cluster sizes), this may be impossible or require many queries if clusters are tiny. The paper needs:
- A clear bound on queries needed for Phase 1 under the worst-case cluster size distribution, or an explicit minimum-cluster-size assumption.
- A proof that the overall O(·) bounds include all phases, including the cost of bootstrapping to the reliability threshold.

5) Experimental evaluation is weak relative to strong theoretical claims.
- Only one real dataset; no synthetic experiments that vary k, n, and H^2 to validate predicted scaling and the thresholds.
- Theoretical results claim exact recovery with high probability; experiments report ~80% precision and ~90% recall, not exact, with no success-probability analysis.
- The “best/worst/greedy” strategies are not baselines against existing methods [40, 43, 18] and are partly unrealistic (e.g., “best” assumes knowledge of the true cluster to minimize queries).
- The side-information distributions are learned using ground truth (then “perturbed”) rather than estimated online within the algorithm as claimed; please evaluate the fully parameter-free pipeline.

6) Clarity/notation/formatting.
- Many typos and LaTeX artifacts: “st Conference on NIPS”, dangling footnotes (e.g., “1” in text), “discrete2”, “3 For simplicity…”, inconsistent spacing and punctuation, inconsistent use of H/H^2/H2.
- Several references are incomplete (missing years, venues), figure references without the figures in the main paper, and switching between p+, p− and f+, f− ambiguously.
- The write-up is long and dense; the main paper should carry clearer theorem statements with consistent notation and error terms; defer lengthy derivations to the appendix but ensure the statements are precise and self-contained.

Quality: The conceptual contributions are potentially strong, but the current draft has mathematical inconsistencies (upper vs lower bounds; lemma/proof typos that affect logic), unclear or missing assumptions for key algorithmic steps, and insufficient empirical support.

Clarity: Needs a thorough rewrite for notation consistency, removal of artifacts, precise definitions (especially Hellinger divergence vs squared divergence), and correction of proof statements.

Originality: The problem setting, lower bound framework for adaptive queries, and parameter-free approach appear novel and relevant.

Significance: If corrected and substantiated, this could be impactful for interactive clustering and active community detection. As is, the inconsistencies limit confidence.

Pros
- Important problem with practical motivation (ER).
- Lower bound framework for adaptive querying is interesting.
- Parameter-free, Hellinger-based iterative update is a nice idea.
- Connection to SBM and discussion of thresholds.

Cons
- Core inconsistency between MC upper bound and lower bound.
- Proof and notation errors (β definition, Lemma 1 statement).
- Algorithm relies on assumptions not stated or justified in worst case (bootstrapping cluster size).
- Limited experiments; no validation of theoretical scalings; no baseline comparisons.
- Numerous formatting and clarity issues.

Recommendation: Reject. The paper has promising ideas but requires substantial revision: fix the bound inconsistency, clean and verify proofs, clearly state assumptions (discrete vs continuous, cluster sizes), and provide stronger empirical validation (including synthetic scaling and baselines). I encourage resubmission after addressing these issues.
