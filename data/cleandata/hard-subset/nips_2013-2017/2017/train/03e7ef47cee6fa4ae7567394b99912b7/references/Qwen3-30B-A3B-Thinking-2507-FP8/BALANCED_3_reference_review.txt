

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in interactive clustering: determining the minimum number of pairwise queries needed to cluster n points into k unknown clusters, when the algorithm has access to side information (a similarity matrix) that provides probabilistic information about cluster membership. The authors provide the first rigorous theoretical analysis of this problem, establishing tight bounds on query complexity that significantly improve upon the standard O(nk) bound for clustering without side information. The work makes an important connection to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that their model generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- for intra- and inter-cluster similarities, without requiring knowledge of k, f+, or f-.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its theoretical contribution. The authors establish a strong information-theoretic lower bound (Theorem 2) that matches their upper bound within logarithmic factors, a significant achievement given the challenges of proving lower bounds in interactive settings. The paper demonstrates a novel technique for deriving such bounds, which could be valuable for other interactive learning problems. The Monte Carlo algorithm presented (Theorem 1) is particularly impressive for being parameter-free, which is a major practical advantage. The connection to SBM is insightful and well-motivated, showing how the query complexity changes with side information. The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide empirical support for the theoretical claims, showing that the greedy strategy closely approximates the optimal performance even when using estimated distributions.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper could be clearer about the distinction between "side information" and the standard clustering problem with an oracle. The problem statement describes the side information as a similarity matrix, but the oracle is still used for pairwise queries - it's not entirely clear how the side information is utilized beyond providing a similarity metric. This could confuse readers familiar with previous work.

The claim of being "parameter-free" requires careful examination. While the algorithm doesn't require knowledge of f+, f-, or k, it does depend on estimating Hellinger divergence between distributions, which in practice requires choosing a constant C in the algorithm. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how robust the algorithm is to different choices of C, or how this estimation might fail in practice. The experimental results use the exact distributions to compute the Hellinger divergence, but don't demonstrate performance with estimated distributions in the real-world scenario where the algorithm would have to learn f+ and f- from data.

The experimental section is somewhat limited in scope. The Cora dataset with only 1812 nodes is relatively small, and the paper doesn't discuss scalability to larger datasets. More comprehensive comparisons with existing heuristic algorithms would strengthen the practical significance of the results.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
1. Clarify the relationship between "side information" and the oracle queries. The paper could better distinguish how the side information is used to guide queries, rather than being an alternative to queries.

2. Expand the experimental section to include:
   - Performance on larger datasets (e.g., DBLP)
   - Comparison with more existing heuristics beyond the best/worst/greedy strategies
   - Analysis of how the choice of constant C affects performance

3. Provide more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the algorithm's dependence on Hellinger divergence estimation. How robust is the algorithm to poor estimation of H²(f+||f-)? What is the trade-off between query complexity and estimation accuracy?

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the algorithm would scale to very large datasets, particularly with respect to the computational complexity of maintaining and updating the empirical distributions.

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the study of interactive clustering with side information. The lower and upper bounds are tight within logarithmic factors, and the parameter-free nature of the algorithms is a major practical advantage. While the paper could benefit from greater clarity on the role of side information, more comprehensive experimental validation, and deeper exploration of the practical implications of the Hellinger divergence estimation, the theoretical foundation is strong and the work opens important new directions for future research.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses a problem with clear practical relevance in entity resolution and active learning, and provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity in this setting. The connection to SBM is particularly valuable, as it provides a bridge between this work and the well-studied community detection literature.

The main weakness is that the practical implementation details of the algorithm could be more thoroughly addressed. However, the theoretical results are strong enough to warrant acceptance, especially given the novelty of the approach and the importance of the problem.

**Decision:** Accept
