

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary
This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the goal is to determine the minimum number of pairwise queries needed to cluster n points into k clusters, given a similarity matrix W as side information. The authors claim to provide the first rigorous theoretical analysis of this problem, establishing information-theoretic lower bounds and proposing parameter-free algorithms that nearly match these bounds. They claim their algorithms reduce query complexity from O(nk) (without side information) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence between distributions f+ and f-.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the information-theoretic analysis of interactive clustering. The lower bound proof (Theorem 2) appears technically rigorous, and the paper correctly identifies an important gap between theoretical guarantees and practical heuristics in the literature. The connection to the stochastic block model is well-motivated, and the paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant theoretical background.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Lack of Comparison with Established Methods**: The paper claims to be "the first rigorous theoretical study" but fails to compare their algorithms with the heuristics they cite as motivating the work (e.g., [40, 42, 43]). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to evaluate whether the theoretical claims translate to practical advantages. The experimental section only compares different query strategies (best, worst, greedy) rather than their algorithm against existing methods. For instance, they report "With just 1136 queries, greedy achieves 80% precision," but don't state how many queries existing methods would require for the same performance.

2. **Overstated Claims about "Parameter-Free" Algorithms**: The paper repeatedly claims their algorithms are "parameter-free" (working without knowledge of f+, f-, or k), but the algorithms actually rely on estimating H²(f+||f-) from data. The convergence of this estimation process is not rigorously validated, and the paper doesn't address how sensitive the algorithm is to errors in this estimation. This is a significant practical limitation that undermines the "parameter-free" claim.

3. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experiments are confined to a single dataset (Cora) and focus on comparing query strategies rather than validating the core theoretical claims. The paper doesn't demonstrate that their algorithm achieves the claimed query complexity in practice. The reported results (e.g., 1136 queries for 80% precision) lack context regarding how this compares to the standard O(nk) baseline or existing heuristic approaches.

4. **Overstated Practical Significance**: The paper claims "the recent works of [18, 33] analyze popular heuristic algorithms... where the probability distributions are obtained from real datasets which show that these heuristics are significantly suboptimal." However, the paper doesn't provide evidence that their algorithm outperforms these heuristics in practice. The claim that "our algorithms are parameter free, that is they work without any knowledge of f+, f− or k" is misleading because the algorithm still requires estimating these distributions from data.

5. **Theoretical Claims Need More Context**: The paper states "our model can be allowed to have this as a special case when we have pmfs fi,js denoting the distributions of the corresponding random variables." This suggests the model is a generalization of SBM, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how their results advance the understanding of SBM beyond what's already known. The connection to SBM appears more like a motivation than a significant theoretical contribution.

## Specific Technical Concerns

- The proof of Lemma 1 (the key lower bound lemma) assumes equally sized clusters (n = ka), but the paper claims to handle arbitrary cluster sizes. This is a significant limitation that isn't adequately addressed.

- The Monte Carlo algorithm's query complexity is O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), but the paper doesn't clarify how H²(f+||f-) behaves in practical settings. If H²(f+||f-) is very small (as it often would be in practice), the query complexity could still be very large, contradicting the claim of "drastic reduction."

- The paper claims "our algorithms are computationally efficient - in fact, the time required is bounded by the size of the side information matrix, i.e., O(n²)." However, for large n (e.g., n > 10⁴), O(n²) is computationally infeasible, which undermines the practical relevance of their algorithm.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the information-theoretic analysis of clustering with side information. However, the lack of comparison with existing methods, limited experimental validation, and overstated claims about practical significance significantly weaken the paper's contribution. The "parameter-free" claim is misleading, and the experimental results don't demonstrate the practical advantages that the paper suggests.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it:
1. Compared their algorithm to existing heuristics from the literature
2. Provided a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of their algorithm to estimation errors in H²(f+||f-)
3. Demonstrated that their algorithm achieves the claimed query complexity in practice
4. Clarified the practical limitations of their approach (e.g., the O(n²) computational complexity)

As it stands, the paper's claims about practical significance are not sufficiently supported by evidence, and the experimental validation is inadequate. I recommend rejection, as the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that this theoretical framework translates to meaningful practical improvements over existing methods. The work is primarily theoretical with limited practical validation, which is not sufficient for NeurIPS given the high bar for empirical validation in this area.
