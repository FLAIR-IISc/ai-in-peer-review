Summary
The paper studies clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries (an oracle) when additional “side information” is available as a noisy similarity matrix. The side-information entries are drawn from unknown distributions f+ (same cluster) and f− (different clusters). The authors develop information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of queries needed for exact recovery and propose parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) whose query complexities are claimed to match the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors. The results generalize stochastic block models (SBM) to arbitrary (unknown) discrete f± and unknown k, and provide query complexity that depends on the Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−). A small experiment on the Cora dataset illustrates reductions in queries versus F1.

Strengths
- Ambitious and timely problem: understanding the value of side information in interactive clustering is important and under-theorized.
- Lower bound technique for adaptive queries is interesting and could be of broader use in interactive/active settings.
- Parameter-free algorithms that do not require knowledge of k or f± are practically appealing.
- Conceptual connection to SBM and discussion of how queries might shift thresholds is insightful.
- The use of Hellinger divergence to quantify separability is natural and distribution-agnostic.

Weaknesses
- Significant clarity and correctness issues in the statements of the main bounds (inconsistencies between k vs k^2 dependence, missing parentheses, sign errors), making it hard to verify the claimed near-optimality.
- Experimental evaluation is very limited: one dataset, no comparisons with established heuristics/algorithms, no synthetic scaling to validate dependence on k and H2(f+‖f−), and no runtime/memory analysis.
- Algorithmic description assumes discrete support for f±; extension to continuous distributions is asserted but not specified or evaluated.
- Proof sketches rely on heavy machinery (Sanov) with several steps deferred; the current write-up includes typos and ambiguous notation (e.g., use of “H” for different divergences), obstructing reproducibility and verification.
- Practical assumptions (perfect oracle, full O(n^2) side information) may limit applicability; noisy-oracle case is deferred to a companion paper.

Quality
- The lower bound approach (reducing to k-ary hypothesis testing for a lightly queried element and controlling TV via Hellinger) is promising; however, the key lemma and Theorem 2 contain formatting/sign/parameter inconsistencies. For instance, the Monte Carlo upper bound is variously written as O(k log n/H2) and O(k^2 log n/H2), while the lower bound is Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}). If the Monte Carlo bound is O(k log n/H2), it would contradict the stated lower bound; if it is O(k^2 log n/H2), the “within log factor” claim is plausible. This must be clarified.
- The algorithm relies on estimating p+ and p− via intra-/inter-cluster edges from a seed set and iteratively updating a threshold ME; the analysis uses Sanov with Hellinger. The sketch is plausible but too terse to judge rigorously; constants and event probabilities are not clearly tracked, and some inequalities appear mistyped.
- Experiments do not validate theoretical scaling or compare with baselines [Awasthi et al. 2014; Wang et al.; Firmani et al.] that motivated the problem.

Clarity
- Numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., missing years in references, broken equations, undefined constants, inconsistent notation for H vs H2, β definition) substantially impede understanding.
- The algorithm pseudocode is prose-only and mixes phases with unclear stopping criteria; the “Membership” subroutine is defined only for discrete support.
- The relationship between KL (in Sanov) and Hellinger is referenced but not carefully developed; which inequalities are used, and with what constants?

Originality
- The formulation with arbitrary, unknown f± and unknown k, together with adaptive lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms, is novel compared to prior SBM and crowdsourced ER literature. The lower bound framework for adaptive queries is likely of independent interest.

Significance
- Potentially significant if the bounds/algorithms are correct and clearly presented. However, the current experimental and expository weaknesses limit confidence and impact. Stronger empirical validation and a clean, consistent theoretical presentation are needed for NeurIPS.

Questions for the authors
1. Please resolve the discrepancy between O(k log n/H2) vs O(k^2 log n/H2) in the Monte Carlo upper bound, and align it with the lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}). Which is correct?
2. The lower bound lemma contains signs and constants that render the “probability of error at least …” expression negative. Please provide a corrected statement.
3. How exactly do you handle continuous-valued side information in the algorithm? Membership relies on discrete supports; do you discretize, and how does that affect the guarantees?
4. What sample sizes are needed to estimate p± to within the required Hellinger tolerance? Please provide finite-sample bounds and constants.
5. Can you compare against at least one or two strong baselines from [40, 43, 18, 33] on public datasets to demonstrate query savings and accuracy?
6. How sensitive is performance to imbalance in cluster sizes and to large k? Synthetic experiments varying H2, k, and n would be helpful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all theorem statements and notation; provide a clean, self-contained appendix with full proofs and explicit constants. Clearly distinguish KL and Hellinger throughout.
- Provide pseudocode for both algorithms, including continuous-data handling.
- Add synthetic experiments to test the predicted scaling with k and H2(f+‖f−); include comparisons to established heuristics and ablations (e.g., without side info).
- Report runtime and memory costs given O(n^2) side information and the cost of repeated divergence computations.
- Discuss robustness to oracle noise (even briefly), or summarize results from the companion paper as context.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a principled information-theoretic framework.
- Potentially strong lower bounds for adaptive interactive clustering.
- Parameter-free approach is practically attractive and conceptually interesting.
- Connection to SBM thresholds with queries could spur further work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Inconsistencies in the main results (k vs k^2) and numerous typos hinder verification.
- Experimental validation is minimal and lacks comparisons to prior methods.
- Algorithmic handling of continuous side information and finite-sample estimation is unclear.
- Clarity and presentation fall short of NeurIPS standards.

Recommendation
Lean reject in current form. With corrected, consistent theory, stronger experiments (including baselines and synthetic scaling), and substantially improved clarity, this work could become a solid contribution.
