This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining full pairwise comparisons is expensive and heuristics often outperform theoretical bounds. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and design algorithms that nearly match these bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available. The work generalizes the stochastic block model and provides a framework for analyzing interactive clustering.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction of the problem, motivation, and related work. The connection to entity resolution is effectively illustrated, and the gap between theoretical bounds and practical heuristics is convincingly highlighted. The formal problem definition and contributions are clearly stated. The lower bound result (Theorem 2) is a significant contribution, and the proposed algorithms aim to achieve the theoretical limits. The experimental validation, while limited in scope, provides some evidence of the algorithm’s performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The study of query complexity with side information is a novel and important direction, extending the classical clustering problem.  The generalization of the SBM is a noteworthy contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds, which are a substantial achievement.
*   **Algorithm Design:** The proposed algorithms aim to match the theoretical bounds, and the iterative-update approach seems promising.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The connection to related work is well-explained.
*   **Practical Motivation:** The application to entity resolution grounds the theoretical work in a real-world problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Algorithm Complexity Details:** The description of the algorithms is somewhat high-level.  More details on the implementation and practical considerations would be helpful. Specifically, the constants involved in the thresholds (like 'C' in Phase 1) and their impact on performance are not fully explored.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are limited. Evaluating on more datasets, especially larger ones, and comparing against existing heuristics more thoroughly would strengthen the paper. A more detailed analysis of the parameter sensitivity of the algorithms is needed.
*   **Assumptions on Distributions:** While the work generalizes beyond the SBM, the assumptions about the nature of *f+* and *f-* (e.g. fixed cardinality of support in the upper bound proof) are not fully discussed.  How sensitive are the results to violations of these assumptions?
*   **Comparison to SBM Recovery Thresholds:** The discussion of how the query complexity relates to the SBM recovery threshold is intriguing, but remains somewhat speculative. Further elaboration and perhaps a formal analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Proof Details:**  While the paper refers to Appendices for detailed proofs, the length and complexity of these proofs may make it difficult for readers to fully verify the results without significant effort.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in clustering, with strong theoretical results.
*   The lower bound establishes a fundamental limit on query complexity.
*   The proposed algorithms are a significant step towards achieving this limit.
*   The work has the potential to impact both theoretical and practical research in clustering and related fields.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of extensive experimental validation leaves some questions about the practical performance of the algorithms.
*   The high-level description of the algorithms and the complexity of the proofs may make it difficult to assess the full impact of the work.
*   The sensitivity analysis regarding assumptions on the distributions f+ and f- is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the experimental evaluation and algorithmic details could be improved, the novelty and rigor of the work justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly focusing on providing more details about the algorithms and expanding the experimental evaluation. A discussion on the implications of violating the assumptions concerning the distributions *f+* and *f-* would also be valuable.
