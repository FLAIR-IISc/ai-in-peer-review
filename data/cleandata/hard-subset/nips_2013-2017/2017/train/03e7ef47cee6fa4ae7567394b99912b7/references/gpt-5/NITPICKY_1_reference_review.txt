Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when given access to (i) a same-cluster oracle (binary queries) and (ii) a noisy similarity matrix W generated from two unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster). The central claims are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds: any (possibly adaptive, randomized) algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) expected queries for high-probability exact recovery; and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for probability-1 recovery (Las Vegas), when H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence and under H^2 ≤ 1/18.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms: a Monte Carlo algorithm and a Las Vegas algorithm that do not require k, f+, or f−. The claimed complexities are within a log n factor of the lower bounds, with time O(n^2).
- Connections to SBM and active learning, and a small empirical study on CORA showing reduced query counts with a greedy strategy informed by side information.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant problem: makes a rigorous step toward explaining empirical reductions in query counts when side information is available.
- Nontrivial lower bound under adaptivity: formulating and proving an information-theoretic lower bound for interactive, adaptive querying is significant and technically challenging.
- Parameter-free algorithmic design using an iterative update and concentration arguments (Sanov-based) is interesting and potentially useful elsewhere.
- The link to SBM and the discussion of thresholds with active querying are insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns
Major clarity/consistency issues:
- Repeated, critical ambiguities in query-complexity expressions. Throughout the paper, divisions are omitted and parentheses are unclear. Examples:
  - “O( k logn H2(f+‖f−) )” appears multiple times; context suggests O(k log n / H^2) or O(k^2 log n / H^2). The lower bound states Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}); to claim a log n gap, the Monte Carlo upper should be O(min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}). As written, the factors of k are inconsistent across sections.
  - The Las Vegas complexity is written “O(n log n + min (nk, k2 logn H2(f+‖f−) ))”; presumably O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}). Please standardize notation and use unambiguous fractions.
- Hellinger notation is inconsistent: H, H^2, √H are used interchangeably. E.g., Step 4 in Lemma 1 uses P`(E) ≤ Pt(E) + √H(P`‖Pt), but earlier H denotes the Hellinger divergence or its square; also standard TV ≤ √2·H holds—please fix constants and definitions.
- Lemma 1 statement appears to have a sign error: it claims the probability of error is at least a negative expression (“− 2/k ... − ... − ...”), which is nonsensical. The subsequent proof bounds the probability of correct assignment and then infers error ≥ 1 − (bound). Please correct the statement.
- Several threshold definitions and inequalities omit essential parentheses, e.g., ME_x, membership thresholds in Phase 3, etc., obscuring the logic.

Modeling/assumption gaps:
- Algorithms assume discrete f+, f− with fixed finite support and define Membership using empirical frequencies over {a1,…,aq}. Yet the lower bound explicitly claims to hold for continuous distributions as well, and the text asserts “extend to any finite sample space scaling linearly.” It is unclear how the parameter-free algorithm handles continuous or unknown support without pre-discretization and what the impact on guarantees is. This mismatch needs to be resolved.
- The constant condition H^2(f+‖f−) ≤ 1/18 in the lower bound is unexplained; a brief intuition and whether it is an artifact of proof techniques would help.

Algorithmic details:
- The iterative update (Phase 1–3) is appealing, but the analysis is only sketched. Critical steps hinge on accurate estimates p+ and p− aggregated over current clusters. While Phase 1 ensures purity by querying, a precise statement of invariants (that all maintained clusters are pure at each iteration) would improve rigor.
- Computational complexity: recomputing pooled distributions p+,x and p−,x every iteration naively is O(n^2) per iteration; the claim that total time is O(n^2) needs a clear argument (e.g., incremental maintenance). Pseudocode would help reproducibility.

Experiments:
- Very limited. Only a single real dataset, no synthetic experiments to probe scaling with k and H^2, which are the core theoretical parameters. No comparisons against prior active ER/clustering heuristics [40, 43, 18] (even though the paper positions itself against them). The “best/worst/greedy” protocols are not realistic baselines; they also do not demonstrate exact recovery, while the theory targets exact recovery.
- It is unclear how the oracle is instantiated (presumably ground-truth labels), and how the side information distributions are estimated in practice (binning choices, sensitivity).

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typos and formatting artifacts: “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)…” dangling text; inconsistent math typesetting; missing absolute bars/parentheses in denominators; “Hellinger square divergence” vs “squared”; |Ci|(|Ci − 1|) missing bracket. References are incomplete (many missing years/pages; inconsistent styles). These issues hamper readability.
- Related work: Some relevant recent results on adaptive community detection and parameter-free SBM recovery are cited, but the positioning could be tighter, and experimental comparisons are absent.

Quality: The lower-bound framework is promising, but the writeup suffers from pervasive notation/formatting errors that obscure the main results; key algorithmic proofs are deferred and only sketched; experiments are insufficient to support claims.

Clarity: Currently below the NeurIPS bar. The main theorems/complexities must be stated unambiguously and consistently; all notation for Hellinger distances should be standardized; algorithms need clearer, reproducible descriptions.

Originality: The adaptive lower-bound approach and parameter-free algorithm leveraging Hellinger-based membership are novel and interesting.

Significance: Potentially significant if clarified; the results could influence active clustering with side information and adaptive lower bounds. As written, the impact is limited by presentation and validation.

Pros
- Interesting, timely problem; clear motivation.
- Nontrivial adaptive information-theoretic lower bound.
- Parameter-free algorithmic idea with Hellinger-based tests and iterative thresholds.

Cons
- Ambiguous, inconsistent statements of main bounds; sign/notation errors in proofs.
- Mismatch between algorithmic assumptions (discrete support) and broader claims (continuous).
- Weak empirical section; no baselines; no scaling studies.
- Numerous formatting and citation issues.

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission after major revision). The core ideas are promising, but the paper needs substantial polishing: unify and correct all complexity expressions and Hellinger notation; fix proof statements; clarify assumptions (discrete vs continuous); provide complete, reproducible algorithm descriptions and tighter complexity analysis; and add thorough experiments (including synthetic to validate k/H^2 dependencies and comparisons to prior heuristics). With these improvements, this work could make a strong contribution.
