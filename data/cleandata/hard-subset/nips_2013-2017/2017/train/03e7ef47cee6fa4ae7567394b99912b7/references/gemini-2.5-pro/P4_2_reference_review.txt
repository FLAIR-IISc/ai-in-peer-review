This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with the help of a pairwise oracle, in a setting where the algorithm also has access to "side information." The problem considers clustering *n* points into an unknown number of *k* clusters. An oracle can answer whether any two points belong to the same cluster. The side information is provided as a similarity matrix *W*, where the entry *w_ij* is drawn from a distribution *f+* if points *i* and *j* are in the same cluster, and from *f-* otherwise. Crucially, the distributions *f+*, *f-*, and the number of clusters *k* are all unknown to the algorithm.

The work is motivated by the discrepancy between the theoretical worst-case query complexity of Ω(nk) for this problem without side information and the empirical success of practical heuristics (e.g., in entity resolution) that use far fewer queries, typically by leveraging some form of similarity scores. The authors formalize this setting and provide a rigorous analysis. This model is a significant generalization of the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM), where *f+* and *f-* are typically Bernoulli distributions.

The main contributions are twofold. First, the paper establishes a strong information-theoretic lower bound on the number of queries required. It shows that any algorithm, even if it knows *k*, *f+*, and *f-*, needs Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) expected queries to correctly recover the clustering with high probability. Here, H²(f+||f-) is the squared Hellinger divergence, which quantifies the distinguishability of the side information distributions. Second, the paper presents efficient, parameter-free algorithms whose query complexities nearly match this lower bound. A Monte Carlo algorithm is proposed that achieves a query complexity of O(k log n / H²(f+||f-)), and a Las Vegas algorithm is given that recovers the clustering with certainty.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   The derivation of the information-theoretic lower bound is a standout contribution. Proving lower bounds in an interactive, adaptive setting is notoriously difficult, as standard tools like Fano's inequality do not directly apply. The authors develop a novel proof technique that isolates elements that are queried infrequently and then applies a multi-hypothesis testing argument to bound the error probability. This framework is a valuable contribution and could potentially be applied to other interactive learning problems.
*   The analysis of the upper bounds is also solid. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms is a significant achievement, and its analysis, which relies on concentration results adapted from Sanov's theorem, is non-trivial and carefully executed.
*   The work is a complete piece of research, providing both strong lower bounds and nearly optimal algorithms that address the core questions posed.
*   A minor weakness is the experimental section, which is based on a single dataset. While the paper's focus is clearly theoretical, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation could have further strengthened the practical relevance of the proposed algorithms by, for instance, showing performance across datasets with varying characteristics or synthetically controlling the Hellinger distance between *f+* and *f-*.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized.
*   The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly explaining the gap between existing theory and practice, and situating the work within the broader context of active learning and SBMs.
*   The problem setup and main results (Theorems 1 and 2) are stated clearly and prominently at the beginning, providing a good roadmap for the reader.
*   The high-level intuition for both the lower bound proof and the algorithm design is communicated effectively.
*   Due to space limitations, many of the technical details and proofs are deferred to the appendix. While standard, this makes the main body somewhat dense. For instance, the sketch of the Monte Carlo algorithm in Section 3 is complex; a more detailed walkthrough of the three phases with a simple example could improve readability for a broader audience. The crucial role of Hellinger distance as a metric (as opposed to KL-divergence) is mentioned but not fully elaborated on in the main text, which would have been insightful.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original.
*   While clustering with queries has been studied, this is the first work to formally model and rigorously analyze the impact of probabilistic side information on query complexity. It provides a foundational theoretical understanding of a phenomenon previously observed only empirically.
*   The lower bound technique for adaptive querying is, to my knowledge, novel and a significant technical contribution.
*   The design of the parameter-free algorithms is also very creative. The iterative-update scheme, where the algorithm learns the unknown distributions from its own intermediate output and uses these estimates to adaptively set thresholds for querying versus using side information, is a sophisticated and novel approach.
*   The generalization of the SBM setting to arbitrary and unknown distributions is a substantial contribution that expands the applicability and theoretical reach of community detection models.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a lasting impact.
*   The paper provides a definitive answer to a fundamental question: by how much can side information reduce the query complexity of clustering? The tight characterization in terms of Hellinger divergence is an elegant and impactful result.
*   It bridges a notable gap between theory and practice in active clustering and entity resolution.
*   The proposed parameter-free algorithms offer a practical recipe for leveraging side information, which is valuable for practitioners who often lack knowledge of underlying data-generating distributions.
*   The work opens up numerous avenues for future research, including closing the logarithmic gap between the upper and lower bounds, extending the results to the sparse SBM regime (as noted by the authors), and considering settings with noisy oracles.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and well-motivated problem at the intersection of active learning, clustering, and community detection.
*   Provides strong, novel, and technically deep contributions in the form of a tight information-theoretic lower bound and nearly optimal, parameter-free algorithms.
*   The lower bound technique is a significant methodological contribution for analyzing interactive algorithms.
*   The results are significant, bridging a gap between theory and practice and opening new research directions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is limited and serves more as a proof of concept than a thorough empirical study.

Overall, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and far outweigh the minor weakness in the experimental section. The paper is well-executed, original, and significant, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
