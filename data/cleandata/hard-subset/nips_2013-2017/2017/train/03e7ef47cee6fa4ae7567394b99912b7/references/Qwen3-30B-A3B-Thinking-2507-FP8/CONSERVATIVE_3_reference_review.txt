

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to side information (a similarity matrix) and can query an oracle about whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors claim to establish information-theoretic lower bounds and develop algorithms that nearly match these bounds, with query complexity reduced from O(nk) to O(k/(log n H²(f⁺||f⁻))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by establishing information-theoretic lower bounds for a non-trivial variant of the clustering problem. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is well-motivated, and the authors correctly identify the gap between theoretical lower bounds and practical heuristics. The algorithmic approach, particularly the iterative-update method for parameter-free design, is novel and potentially useful for other problems. The proofs of the main theorems appear technically sound, and the paper provides a clear formalization of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is the insufficient experimental validation. The paper claims "extensive experiments" in the introduction but only presents results on a single dataset (Cora) with limited comparison to relevant baselines. The comparison to existing heuristic methods [40, 42, 43] is notably absent, which is critical given the authors' central claim that their method achieves "far less queries than nk" in practice. The experimental section shows only marginal improvements (12 additional queries) when using approximate distributions, but doesn't demonstrate the claimed "drastic reduction" in query complexity in a meaningful way.

The paper overstates its novelty claims. While the parameter-free aspect is interesting, the problem of clustering with side information has been studied before [40, 42, 43], and the authors don't adequately differentiate their approach from these existing works. The claim that "before this work, there existed no algorithm that works for arbitrary unknown distributions" appears exaggerated given the related literature.

The paper's practical significance is questionable. The time complexity is O(n²), which would be prohibitive for large-scale datasets, yet the paper doesn't address this limitation. The algorithm requires a constant C that isn't specified, and the paper doesn't discuss how to choose this parameter in practice. The experimental validation on a single dataset with limited comparison to baselines fails to demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

The theoretical contribution is somewhat limited by the logarithmic gap between the upper and lower bounds. While this gap is common in such problems, the paper doesn't clearly articulate why this gap is unavoidable or how it might be closed. The connection to SBM is interesting but doesn't provide concrete insights about how query complexity changes the recovery threshold in sparse SBM, despite the authors' claim that this would be "of independent interest."

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution, it falls short on the critical criterion of empirical validation. The claim that this approach "drastically reduces" query complexity is not convincingly supported by the limited experimental results. The paper fails to compare with existing heuristic methods that have been shown to work well in practice, which is essential for establishing significance in a field where practical applicability is paramount.

The paper's theoretical contribution would be stronger with a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly the existing heuristics that the authors claim to outperform. The experimental section should have included comparisons to established methods [40, 42, 43] on multiple datasets to demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.

The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the practical limitations of the approach, including computational complexity and the requirement for side information. Without these elements, the paper reads more like a theoretical exercise than a contribution with practical relevance to clustering problems in machine learning.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound theoretical result, the lack of meaningful experimental validation against relevant baselines and the overstatement of practical significance prevent it from meeting the standards expected for NIPS. The contribution would be significantly stronger if the authors provided more substantial empirical evidence of the algorithm's practical utility.
