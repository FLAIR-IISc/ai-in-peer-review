This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information. The standard setting considers an oracle that can answer pairwise queries ("do items u and v belong to the same cluster?"), for which the query complexity to perfectly recover `k` clusters of `n` items is known to be `Θ(nk)`. The authors address the gap between this theoretical bound and the much better performance of practical heuristics, hypothesizing that this is due to the use of "side information" (e.g., a similarity matrix).

The paper formalizes this by modeling the side information as a matrix `W`, where the entry `w_uv` is drawn from a distribution `f+` if `u,v` are in the same cluster, and from `f-` otherwise. The key challenge is that the algorithm has no prior knowledge of `k`, `f+`, or `f-`. The authors' main contributions are twofold: (1) An information-theoretic lower bound of `Ω(min(nk, k^2 / H^2(f+ || f-)))` on the number of queries required for exact recovery, where `H^2` is the squared Hellinger divergence. This bound holds even if `k, f+, f-` are known. (2) Two computationally efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) whose query complexities nearly match this lower bound, differing by a logarithmic factor. The paper also draws an insightful connection between this model and the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM).

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem and provides a convincing theoretical explanation for a widely observed phenomenon in practice, particularly in areas like entity resolution. Bridging the gap between the pessimistic `Θ(nk)` theoretical bound and the success of real-world systems is a significant and valuable contribution.
*   **Originality and Novelty:** The problem formulation itself—combining pairwise oracle queries with probabilistic side information from *unknown* distributions—is novel and well-posed. The development of parameter-free algorithms is a major strength, as this is crucial for practical applicability. The information-theoretic lower bound for an adaptive/interactive setting is technically challenging and represents a strong theoretical contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The lower bound proof sketch, which cleverly reduces the problem to a multi-hypothesis testing scenario and handles the adaptivity of queries by analyzing "weakly-queried" nodes, is elegant. The proposed algorithms are also technically sophisticated. The iterative-update scheme in the Monte Carlo algorithm, which gradually estimates the distributions and adaptively determines the required cluster size for making decisions from side information, is a creative and powerful idea for designing parameter-free methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and motivated. The introduction provides excellent context, clearly stating the problem, the gap in existing work, and a summary of the contributions. The connection to SBM is well-explained and adds valuable context for readers familiar with that line of work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** The description of the algorithm in Section 3 is rather high-level. While the appendix likely provides the full details, the main text would be significantly improved by including a more structured pseudocode block. The flow of control, particularly the alternation between Phase 2 (iterative updates) and Phase 3 (processing grown clusters), is not entirely clear. For instance, after a cluster `C` is "completely grown," the algorithm moves to the "next grown cluster." What happens if there are none? The text says "move back to Phase 2." A clearer flowchart or step-by-step description would greatly enhance readability and reproducibility.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section provides a proof of concept but could be substantially strengthened.
    1.  **Baselines:** A key motivation for the work is to explain and improve upon existing heuristics. It would be highly compelling to compare the proposed algorithm against some of the heuristic methods cited in the introduction (e.g., from [40, 42, 43]) on the same dataset.
    2.  **Synthetic Data:** The theoretical bounds depend critically on the Hellinger divergence `H^2(f+ || f-)`. An experiment on synthetic data where this quantity is varied would be an excellent way to empirically validate the theoretical findings.
    3.  **Clarity:** It is not immediately clear how the "best," "worst," and "greedy" strategies tested relate to the full three-phase algorithm presented. Is the greedy strategy, for instance, the one used in Phase 3B? Clarifying this would help connect the experiments more directly to the proposed algorithm.
*   **Gap Between Bounds:** There remains a `log n` factor gap between the upper and lower bounds. While this is common in this type of analysis, it would be helpful if the authors could briefly speculate on the source of this gap. Is it an artifact of using concentration inequalities like Sanov's theorem in the upper bound analysis, or is the lower bound construction not perfectly tight?
*   **Lower Bound Proof Presentation:** The proof sketch in Section 2 is very dense. While space is limited, adding a sentence or two of intuition for key steps (e.g., the definitions of `J'` and `J''` and why their intersection is large) would make the argument more accessible to a broader audience.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the theory of interactive clustering and active learning. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical results are novel and deep, and the proposed algorithms are practical due to their parameter-free nature. The weaknesses identified are primarily in the presentation of the algorithm and the scope of the experimental validation; they do not detract from the core theoretical achievements of the work. The paper is an excellent fit for NeurIPS and represents a substantial advance in our understanding of clustering with side information.

**Decision:** Accept.
