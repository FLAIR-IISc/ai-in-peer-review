

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to side information (a similarity matrix) and can query an oracle about whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors establish a tight information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(n + min{nk, k²/H²(f⁺||f⁻)}) and develop a parameter-free algorithm that achieves an upper bound of O(k log n / H²(f⁺||f⁻)) (Monte Carlo) and O(n log n + k log n / H²(f⁺||f⁻)) (Las Vegas), where H²(f⁺||f⁻) is the squared Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity values for intra-cluster and inter-cluster pairs. The work generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻, not just Bernoulli distributions as in SBM. The paper is well-motivated by practical applications like entity resolution, where side information from similarity matrices can significantly reduce the number of crowd-sourced queries needed.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper establishes a significant theoretical advance by providing the first tight bounds for interactive clustering with side information. The lower bound proof (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is particularly impressive, as it overcomes the challenge of proving information-theoretic bounds for adaptive algorithms where standard techniques like Fano's inequality don't apply.

2. **Novel Framework**: The authors develop a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering algorithms, which could be applicable to other problems in interactive learning. The connection to SBM is well-motivated and demonstrates the generality of their approach.

3. **Algorithm Design**: The iterative-update algorithm is clever and addresses the key challenge of designing a parameter-free algorithm. The use of Hellinger divergence (rather than KL divergence) as the metric for the algorithm's design is well-justified and appears to be crucial for the analysis.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly defines the problem, distinguishes it from previous work, and establishes the theoretical foundations for the analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experimental section (Section 4) is quite weak. The authors only evaluate their algorithm on one dataset (Cora) with a single perturbation of the similarity distributions. They don't compare against existing heuristics from the literature (e.g., [40, 42, 43]) that the paper cites as motivation. More comprehensive experiments on multiple datasets with varying properties would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

2. **Lack of Practical Context**: The paper doesn't clearly explain how the theoretical query complexity translates to practical time or cost savings in real-world applications. The experimental results don't provide sufficient context to understand the practical impact of the theoretical improvements.

3. **Ambiguous Claims About "Parameter-Free"**: While the authors claim their algorithms are "parameter-free," they don't fully justify this claim. The constants used in the threshold (e.g., the 4 in the threshold condition) appear arbitrary, and the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the algorithm is to these constants.

4. **Overstated Connection to SBM**: The connection to the stochastic block model is mentioned, but the paper doesn't clearly explain how their results improve upon or generalize existing SBM results. For sparse SBM, where f⁺ is Bernoulli(a' log n / n) and f⁻ is Bernoulli(b' log n / n), the paper's Lemma 1 is not tight, but they don't provide a more detailed analysis for this important special case.

5. **Proof Details**: The lower bound proof (Lemma 1) is quite dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation before diving into the formal proof. The paper would be more accessible if it included a high-level sketch of the proof strategy.

## Technical Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for the main theorems. The lower bound proof is particularly strong, though the algorithm analysis relies heavily on Sanov's theorem without sufficient justification for using Hellinger distance over KL divergence. The experimental section is weak but doesn't undermine the theoretical contributions.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some notational inconsistencies (e.g., H²(f⁺||f⁻) vs H(f⁺||f⁻)) could be improved. The main results are clearly stated, but the algorithm description in Section 3 could be more precise about implementation details.

**Originality**: The work is highly original in its formulation of the problem (generalizing SBM to arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻) and in its information-theoretic analysis. The iterative-update algorithm design is novel and clever. The paper distinguishes itself well from related work, particularly [5] which considers a different clustering objective.

**Significance**: The results provide a fundamental understanding of how side information can reduce query complexity in interactive clustering. This has direct implications for practical applications like entity resolution where reducing the number of crowd-sourced queries can significantly reduce costs. However, the limited experimental validation makes it harder to assess the practical significance.

## Recommendations

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The theoretical contributions are strong and significant, and the paper addresses an important problem with well-motivated methodology. The main areas for improvement are:

1. **Expand the experimental section** to include comparisons with existing heuristics (e.g., [40, 42, 43]) on multiple datasets with varying properties.

2. **Clarify the "parameter-free" claim** by discussing the sensitivity of the algorithm to the constants used in the threshold conditions.

3. **Strengthen the connection to SBM** by providing a more detailed analysis of how their results generalize or improve upon existing SBM work, especially for sparse SBM.

4. **Include a high-level explanation** of the lower bound proof before diving into the technical details.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The lower and upper bounds are well-motivated and the proofs are rigorous. While the experimental validation is limited, the theoretical results are strong enough to justify acceptance, especially given the importance of the problem and the lack of previous work on this specific formulation. The suggested revisions would strengthen the paper without fundamentally altering its contributions.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- First tight bounds for interactive clustering with side information
- Novel lower bound technique that overcomes challenges of adaptive algorithms
- Clever parameter-free algorithm design with practical implications
- Strong connection to stochastic block model and entity resolution applications
- Theoretical results provide fundamental understanding of query complexity

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation on a single dataset
- Overstated claims about "parameter-free" without sufficient justification
- Connection to SBM could be more clearly articulated
- Some technical details in proofs could be clarified for better accessibility

This paper represents a significant advance in the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information and would be valuable to the NIPS community. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the conference.
