

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper addresses a fundamental gap between theoretical lower bounds and practical heuristics in interactive clustering. The authors study clustering with side information, where an algorithm has access to a similarity matrix (with distributions f+ for points in the same cluster and f- for points in different clusters) and can query an oracle about pairwise cluster membership. The key insight is that side information dramatically reduces query complexity compared to the Ω(nk) lower bound without side information.

The main contribution is a precise characterization of how side information affects query complexity. The authors prove an information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(n + min(nk, k²/H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f-. They then develop algorithms matching this bound within logarithmic factors: a Monte Carlo algorithm with query complexity O(k log n / H²(f+||f-)) and a Las Vegas algorithm with O(n log n + min(nk, k² log n / H²(f+||f-))). The algorithms are parameter-free (work without knowing f+, f-, or k) and computationally efficient.

This work is highly novel in its approach. Previous theoretical work had Ω(nk) lower bounds without side information, but practical heuristics like those in [42, 43, 40] used significantly fewer queries. The paper explains this discrepancy by showing side information enables drastically reduced query complexity, with the precise reduction depending on the Hellinger divergence between the distributions. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is particularly insightful, as the authors demonstrate their model generalizes SBM while providing a more comprehensive framework for analyzing interactive clustering.

The technical contributions are strong. The lower bound proof requires a novel approach to handle the interactive nature of the problem, overcoming standard techniques that fail in adaptive settings. The upper bound algorithms are elegant, with the iterative-update technique providing a general recipe for developing parameter-free algorithms. The experimental results on the Cora dataset validate the theoretical claims, showing the greedy strategy achieves near-optimal performance with minimal queries.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental validation provides strong evidence for the practical relevance of the results. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and future directions, including the connection to sparse SBM and the potential for improving constants.

Strengths:
- Highly novel problem formulation and analysis
- Strong theoretical contributions with tight bounds
- Clear connection to SBM and other related work
- Practical relevance to entity resolution and crowdsourcing
- Parameter-free algorithms are extremely important in practice
- Well-executed experiments that validate theoretical claims

Weaknesses:
- Some technical details might be dense for non-experts
- The paper could better emphasize how side information is generated in practice (though this is acknowledged as future work)

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field. It provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why practical heuristics work better than theoretical lower bounds would suggest, and establishes a new framework for analyzing interactive clustering with side information. The results have clear implications for reducing the cost of crowdsourcing in clustering applications, and the framework could be extended to other interactive learning settings.

The paper is of high quality, with strong theoretical results that are well-supported by both analysis and experiments. It is clearly written and makes a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and significance of the work substantially outweigh any minor weaknesses.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. This paper presents a bold new approach that fundamentally changes our understanding of query complexity in clustering, and opens up many exciting directions for future work. The theoretical results are significant, the practical implications are clear, and the paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
