Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner has access to an oracle that answers same-cluster queries and side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W. The generative model assumes that each pairwise similarity is drawn from f+ if the pair is in the same cluster and from f− otherwise; crucially, k, f+, and f− are unknown to the algorithm. The authors give:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive algorithms (even with knowledge of k, f+, f−): any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) expected queries to recover with constant probability; and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) for success with probability 1. The bounds hold for discrete or continuous f+, f− and are proved via a novel argument that handles adaptivity, using Hellinger divergence.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that (i) a Monte Carlo procedure using empirical Hellinger thresholds, and (ii) a Las Vegas variant. The algorithm iteratively grows at least one cluster to a size ≈ Θ((log n)/H^2), estimates p+ and p− from W, and then classifies remaining points using a membership score based on Hellinger distance, querying only when a confidence interval is inconclusive. The claimed query complexity is near the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors.
- Empirical evidence on a real ER dataset (cora) demonstrating large reductions in queries with high F1, and robustness to imperfect estimates of f+, f−.

The work connects to and generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM): SBM corresponds to Bernoulli f+, f− without queries; this paper allows arbitrary (unknown) f+, f−, unknown k and adversarial cluster sizes, and studies how queries shift recovery thresholds.

Strengths
- Originality: Provides, to my knowledge, the first information-theoretic treatment of clustering with side information and adaptive same-cluster queries under very general f+, f− and unknown k. The lower bound technique for adaptive querying via Hellinger divergence is novel and potentially useful beyond this problem.
- Significance: Clarifies why heuristics leveraging side information can beat the Θ(nk) barrier, and quantifies the dependence on statistical separability via H^2(f+||f−). The results generalize SBM insights and could inspire new active community detection analyses.
- Technical quality: The lower bound is carefully constructed to handle adaptivity and does not rely on Fano in a black-box way. The upper-bound algorithm is parameter-free, conceptually clean, and computationally efficient (O(n^2) to read W).
- Clarity of the high-level ideas: The membership function and the iterative-update mechanism are well motivated. The role of Hellinger (metric properties, product distributions) is explained.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Inconsistency in query bounds: The main text states several different bounds for the Monte Carlo algorithm (e.g., “O(k/log n H^2)”, “O(min(nk, k log n/H^2))”), while the lower bound is Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}). The Las Vegas bound matches within a log factor (O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2})), but the Monte Carlo claim appears off by a factor k or has missing parentheses. Please clarify the exact Monte Carlo bound and reconcile it with Theorem 2; if there are different regimes or additional assumptions, state them precisely.
- Assumptions on distributions: The algorithm assumes discrete f+, f− with fixed support to define empirical histograms. The lower bound allows continuous distributions. How should the algorithm be instantiated for continuous W (e.g., Gaussian similarities)? A brief discussion of binning/plug-in estimators and how discretization affects Hellinger estimation and query complexity (constants/log factors) would strengthen the practicality claims.
- Rigour of concentration arguments: The analysis hinges on Sanov-type bounds for Hellinger divergence and anti-concentration ensuring small Waiting(C). While appendices reportedly contain details, the main text sometimes oscillates between H and H^2 notation and omits constants (e.g., TV ≤ sqrt(H) vs the standard TV ≤ sqrt(2) H relation depending on conventions). Tighten notation and constants to avoid ambiguity.
- Lower-bound constants/conditions: Theorem 2 assumes H^2(f+||f−) ≤ 1/18 and derives a constant 1/6 error probability. Is the small-separation assumption essential for the k^2/H^2 term, or just for constants? A short remark would help.
- Experimental evaluation: The single dataset experiment is illustrative but limited. There are no comparisons to prior heuristics [Wang et al. 2012; Vesdapunt et al. 2014; Firmani et al. 2016], no synthetic studies varying H^2 and k to validate the predicted scaling, and no runtime measurements. Given the primarily theoretical contribution, this is not fatal, but a minimal synthetic validation of the H^2 and k dependencies would significantly improve the empirical story.
- Presentation issues: There are numerous typographical glitches (e.g., “st Conference on NIPS 2017,” stray footnote markers, inconsistent equation formatting), incomplete citations (missing years/pages), and minor notational slips (H vs H^2). A careful proofreading pass is needed.

Quality
- The lower bounds and the algorithmic framework appear technically sound, with a clear path to proofs via appendices. The adaptivity-aware lower bound is a highlight. The main unresolved issue is the Monte Carlo query complexity inconsistency.

Clarity
- Generally understandable at a high level, but notation and formatting need polishing. Consider including succinct pseudocode for the algorithm in the main text and a precise statement of assumptions (discrete vs continuous W, support size, access model).

Originality
- High. The combination of general f+, f−, unknown k, adaptive-query lower bounds, and parameter-free algorithms is new.

Significance
- High for the theory/ML community working on active learning, clustering with side information, and SBM. The Hellinger-based characterization is likely to be reused.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix and unify the stated query complexities; add a theorem box with the exact Monte Carlo guarantee and its probability of success.
- Discuss continuous W and practical estimation of Hellinger divergence.
- Add a small synthetic study varying H^2 and k; optionally compare to a standard ER heuristic with side information to illustrate query savings.
- Clean up typos and references; standardize the use of Hellinger distance vs its square.

Pros
- Novel and broadly applicable lower-bound methodology for adaptive interactive learning.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithm with near-optimal query complexity.
- Clear conceptual connection to and generalization of SBM.

Cons
- Inconsistent statements about the Monte Carlo query bound vs the lower bound.
- Limited experimental validation and no comparison to prior heuristics.
- Some notation/clarity issues and minor technical gaps (discrete vs continuous W).

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are strong and timely; with clarifications and minor additions, this will be a valuable NIPS paper.
