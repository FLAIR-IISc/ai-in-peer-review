Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the query (oracle) complexity of clustering when the algorithm also has access to a noisy similarity matrix (“side information”). The central question is how many same-cluster queries are needed to exactly recover the ground-truth clustering when k, f+, and f− are unknown, where side information is generated by drawing pairwise similarities from f+ or f− depending on whether the pair is in the same or different clusters.

Main contributions:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering with side information, even allowing randomized, adaptive querying and even granting the algorithm knowledge of k, f+, f−. The bounds are expressed in terms of the Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−), and show a separation from the Ω(nk) worst-case bound without side information.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f− and whose query complexity matches the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors.
- A general lower-bound framework for interactive settings that overcomes the limitations of standard Fano-type approaches, and a parameter-free iterative-update method for learning effective thresholds from side information.
- Connection to (and generalization of) the stochastic block model (SBM): the side information model encompasses SBM as a special case; the work suggests how oracle queries shift recovery thresholds.

Relative to prior work, this paper advances beyond: (i) earlier same-cluster-query analyses without side information (e.g., O(nk) bounds) by exploiting side information quantitatively; (ii) heuristic ER/interactive clustering methods that empirically query far less than nk but lacked matching theory; and (iii) SBM recovery thresholds by introducing the role of oracle queries. The parameter-free design aligns with recent progress on SBM without known parameters, but in a more general side-information setting.

Quality:
The technical core is strong and largely convincing. The lower bounds account for adaptivity and use Hellinger divergence in a careful way. The parameter-free algorithmic design (iterative update that learns empirical p+ and p− and stops when a Hellinger-based size threshold is met) is elegant and practically appealing. The Monte Carlo and Las Vegas guarantees appear to be near-optimal within a log n factor, with complexity dominated by O(n^2) processing of the similarity matrix.

That said, there are clarity issues in the presentation of some bounds (see below), and the experimental validation is limited (one dataset, basic metrics), though this is primarily a theory paper.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable but suffers from notational and typographical inconsistencies that obscure the main results:
- The query complexity expressions sometimes omit slashes/exponents, making it ambiguous whether a term is O(k log n / H^2) or O(k^2 log n / H^2). Later statements suggest the Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}), matching the lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2}) up to log n; please unify this across the paper and explicitly state the Monte Carlo bound.
- Success probability statements fluctuate between 1 − 1/n and 1 − o(1/n); clarify the intended high-probability regime.
- The assumption “discrete distributions with fixed support” needs a precise dependence on the support size q in the analysis and runtime.

Originality:
High. The lower-bound technique for interactive, adaptive query models with side information is novel and valuable. The parameter-free iterative-update strategy that learns the effective threshold from side information via Hellinger distance is also a fresh and useful idea. The generalization of SBM and the explicit quantification of how side information reduces query complexity are significant advances.

Significance:
High. The work bridges a real gap between heuristic practice (entity resolution, crowdsourcing-based clustering) and theory by quantifying the benefit of side information. The results provide a principled, parameter-free recipe that practitioners could use when a similarity matrix is available. The lower-bound framework could influence other interactive/active learning problems.

Strengths:
- Conceptually bold and timely: formalizes and explains why heuristics need far fewer queries than nk when side information exists.
- Nearly tight query bounds in terms of Hellinger divergence; independence of k and unknown f+, f− is compelling.
- General, reusable lower-bound framework for interactive queries.
- Clear connection to SBM and thought-provoking conjecture about how queries shift thresholds.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms.

Weaknesses:
- Notational/typographical ambiguities in the main theorems and bounds; success probability inconsistencies.
- Assumptions: noiseless oracle; discrete f+, f− with fixed support (upper bounds), full n×n side information; these limit immediate applicability and should be discussed more candidly.
- Experiments are limited: one dataset, no comparisons to strong baselines, little exploration of scaling with k, H2, or cluster size imbalance.
- Some constants/conditions in the lower bound (e.g., H2 ≤ 1/18) are restrictive; discuss whether these can be relaxed.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Please reconcile the query complexity statements for Monte Carlo vs Las Vegas and explicitly express all bounds as functions of k, n, and H2 with clear division signs.
- Can the upper-bound techniques handle continuous f+, f− or large support with a clear dependence on q?
- How sensitive is performance to cluster size imbalance and large k (e.g., k growing with n)? Synthetic studies demonstrating the predicted scaling would strengthen the paper.
- Is the O(n log n) overhead in the Las Vegas algorithm necessary? Can it be reduced?
- Any extension to sparse side-information matrices or streaming settings?
- Consider including a brief proof sketch of the Sanov-with-Hellinger adaptation to aid readers.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theory that nearly closes a key gap; parameter-free algorithms; reusable techniques; important problem with clear practical motivation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Presentation issues and limited empirical validation; some assumptions narrow immediate applicability.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10). Confidence: 0.7.
