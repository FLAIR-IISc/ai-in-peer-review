This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically examining how access to a similarity matrix (representing side information) impacts the number of oracle queries needed to accurately cluster data. The authors establish a theoretical framework and provide both lower and upper bounds on query complexity, demonstrating a significant reduction in queries possible when side information is available compared to clustering without it. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is well-articulated, and the paper proposes parameter-free algorithms that nearly match the derived lower bounds.

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The derivation of lower bounds, particularly in the interactive setting where queries are adaptive, is a substantial contribution. Existing lower bounding techniques often fail in such scenarios, and the authors successfully develop a general framework applicable to interactive clustering. Similarly, the proposed algorithms are well-designed and achieve near-optimal query complexity.  The parameter-free nature of the algorithms is particularly valuable, addressing a practical limitation of many existing methods. The authors also do a commendable job of contextualizing their work within related areas, such as entity resolution, SBM, and active learning. The inclusion of experimental results, even if preliminary, helps to illustrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. The detailed organization and clear writing style further contribute to the paper’s overall quality.  The connection made to the stochastic block model and the discussion of how the query process affects the recovery threshold for SBM is a particularly insightful contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the experimental results are a positive addition, they feel somewhat limited in scope. The focus on a single dataset (Cora) and the limited comparison against existing heuristics restrict the ability to fully assess the practical performance of the proposed algorithms. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art entity resolution techniques or clustering heuristics on a variety of datasets with differing characteristics, would significantly strengthen the empirical validation.  

The analysis of the algorithms’ running time is somewhat cursory. While stated as O(n^2), the constant factors and the practical implications of this complexity for large datasets are not discussed. Furthermore, the iterative-update algorithm described in Section 3, while conceptually appealing, lacks a detailed runtime analysis of each phase. 

The paper occasionally suffers from a degree of technical density. While the mathematical rigor is appreciated, some of the derivations and explanations could be made more accessible to a broader audience. For example, the explanation of Sanov's Theorem and its application to the Hellinger distance would benefit from further elaboration. The notation, while consistent, is occasionally cumbersome and could be simplified.

Finally, the discussion of potential future work is somewhat brief. Expanding on the potential applications of the developed techniques to other areas, such as community detection in dynamic networks or noisy clustering scenarios, would enhance the paper’s impact.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees on the query complexity of clustering with side information, filling a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Novel Lower Bounds:** The development of a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in the interactive setting is a substantial achievement.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms are parameter-free, making them highly practical and applicable to a wide range of scenarios.
*   **Clear and Well-Organized:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.
*   **Connection to SBM:** Insightful connection to and generalization of the stochastic block model.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is limited in scope and lacks comprehensive comparisons to existing techniques.
*   **Runtime Analysis:** The runtime analysis of the algorithms is cursory and lacks detailed consideration of practical implications.
*   **Technical Density:** Some of the mathematical derivations and explanations could be made more accessible.
*   **Brief Future Work Discussion:** The discussion of potential future work is limited and could be expanded.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of clustering with side information. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and some aspects of the presentation could be improved, the rigor and novelty of the theoretical results outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the need for a more thorough experimental evaluation and a more detailed discussion of the algorithms’ runtime complexity. I encourage the authors to expand on their discussion of future work and consider making the technical explanations more accessible to a broader audience.
