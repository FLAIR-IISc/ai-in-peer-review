### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering *n* items into an unknown number of *k* clusters, with the help of an oracle that can answer pairwise "same-cluster" queries. The authors are motivated by a discrepancy between the established theoretical lower bound of Ω(nk) queries and the much better performance of practical heuristic algorithms, especially in applications like entity resolution. They hypothesize that this gap is explained by the use of "side information," which they formalize as a noisy similarity matrix *W*. In their model, the similarity value between two points is drawn from a distribution *f+* if they are in the same cluster, and from *f-* if they are in different clusters. The key challenge is that the distributions *f+*, *f-*, and the number of clusters *k* are all unknown to the algorithm.

The main contributions are twofold. First, the paper establishes a strong information-theoretic lower bound on the number of queries required for exact recovery, showing that Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) queries are necessary, where H² is the squared Hellinger distance. This bound holds even for randomized algorithms that know the model parameters, making it a powerful result. Second, the paper provides computationally efficient, parameter-free algorithms (both Monte Carlo and Las Vegas versions) whose query complexity nearly matches the lower bound, achieving an upper bound of O(min(nk, k² log n / H²(f+||f-))). A key technical innovation in the algorithm is an iterative-update scheme that learns approximations of the distributions on the fly and uses them to decide when the side information is reliable enough to avoid querying. The authors also draw a clear connection between their model and the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM), of which their framework is a significant generalization.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a fundamental and well-motivated problem. The gap between the Θ(nk) query complexity in the standard model and the performance of practical systems is a compelling starting point. By formalizing the notion of "side information," the authors provide the first rigorous framework to theoretically explain and analyze this phenomenon. The results are likely to be of high interest to researchers in active learning, clustering, and crowdsourcing.

2.  **Technical Depth and Originality:** The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and novel.
    *   The **lower bound** is particularly impressive. Proving information-theoretic bounds in an interactive, adaptive setting is notoriously difficult. The authors' proof technique, which identifies "weak" clusters that are not queried enough and analyzes the multi-hypothesis testing problem for a single element, is elegant and could be of independent interest for other problems in interactive learning.
    *   The **parameter-free algorithm** is a significant achievement. Designing algorithms that work without knowledge of the underlying model parameters (*k*, *f+*, *f-*) is crucial for practical applicability but poses major technical challenges. The iterative-update procedure, which dynamically estimates the Hellinger distance to determine a confidence threshold for using the side information, is a clever and powerful idea that could serve as a recipe for other parameter-free algorithm designs.

3.  **Completeness of the Theoretical Story:** The work provides a remarkably complete picture of the problem by establishing nearly tight lower and upper bounds. This characterizes the query complexity of the problem precisely in terms of the statistical distance between the underlying distributions, providing a clear and satisfying theoretical result.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, summarizing related work, and positioning the paper's contributions. The connection to SBM is explained well, adding valuable context.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the inclusion of experiments on the Cora dataset is appreciated, the evaluation feels somewhat preliminary and could be strengthened to better demonstrate the practical impact of the work.
    *   **Comparison to Heuristics:** The paper is motivated by heuristic algorithms used in entity resolution [40, 42, 43]. A direct experimental comparison of the proposed algorithm against one or more of these widely used heuristics would provide a much stronger case for its practical relevance.
    *   **Validation of Theoretical Scaling:** The theoretical results show a clear dependence on the Hellinger distance H²(f+||f-). It would be very insightful to include experiments on synthetic data where this distance can be systematically varied to empirically validate that the number of queries required by the algorithm scales as predicted by the theory. This would beautifully connect the theoretical and empirical parts of the paper.

2.  **Intuition for Algorithmic Thresholds:** The description of the algorithm in Section 3, particularly in Phase 3, presents complex-looking thresholds for deciding when to query. While these are presumably derived rigorously in the appendix, the main text would benefit from more intuition. A brief explanation of why these specific forms (involving H(p+||p-), constants, and log n) are the "right" ones for balancing confidence and query cost would enhance readability and understanding.

3.  **Scope of the Side Information Model:** The model assumes a single *f+* and a single *f-*. While this is a powerful generalization of SBM, the paper briefly mentions that SBM can have different edge probabilities between different pairs of communities (*q_ij*). A short discussion on the limitations of the two-distribution model and the challenges or possibilities of extending the results to a more general setting (e.g., with cluster-pair-dependent distributions *f_ij*) would be a valuable addition.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental and practically important problem in clustering and active learning.
    *   Provides the first rigorous theoretical characterization of the query complexity of clustering with side information, successfully bridging a known gap between theory and practice.
    *   The information-theoretic lower bound is technically deep, novel for an interactive setting, and provides a strong foundation for the work.
    *   The proposed parameter-free algorithm is highly innovative, computationally efficient, and its performance nearly matches the lower bound.
    *   The work establishes an interesting and fruitful connection to the Stochastic Block Model.

*   **Con:**
    *   The experimental section is limited and misses the opportunity to compare against the very heuristics that motivated the work.
    *   A small logarithmic gap remains between the upper and lower bounds.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical contributions. It tackles an important, well-motivated problem with novel and powerful techniques, delivering a comprehensive theoretical characterization. The strengths, particularly the strong lower bound and the clever parameter-free algorithm, are substantial. The weaknesses are primarily in the experimental section and are addressable; they do not detract from the core theoretical achievements of the paper. This work represents a clear advance in our understanding of interactive clustering and is an excellent fit for NeurIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
