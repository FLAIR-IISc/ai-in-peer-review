### Summary

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information. The problem setting involves partitioning *n* items into an unknown number of *k* clusters by adaptively making pairwise queries to an oracle that reveals whether two items are in the same cluster. The key feature is the availability of "side information" in the form of a noisy similarity matrix *W*, where the similarity value for a pair of items is drawn from one of two distributions, *f+* or *f-*, depending on whether the items belong to the same cluster or not. The algorithm does not know *k*, *f+*, or *f-*.

This work is motivated by the observed gap between the pessimistic Θ(nk) theoretical query complexity for clustering without side information and the much better performance of practical heuristics (e.g., for entity resolution) that leverage such similarity scores. The paper aims to formally characterize how side information reduces query complexity. The authors establish nearly-tight information-theoretic bounds. They prove a lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}), where H² is the squared Hellinger distance. Complementing this, they propose computationally efficient, parameter-free Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms with query complexities matching the lower bound up to logarithmic factors. This work provides a significant generalization of the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM) to a setting with arbitrary unknown distributions and an interactive querying mechanism.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a fundamental and practically relevant problem. The discrepancy between the theoretical complexity of interactive clustering and the performance of real-world, heuristic-based systems is a compelling motivation. By providing a rigorous theoretical model that explains this gap, the paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of active/interactive learning. The results are likely to be influential for researchers in active learning, clustering, and crowdsourcing.

2.  **Technical Depth and Originality:** The theoretical contributions are novel and substantial.
    *   The information-theoretic lower bound is particularly strong. The proof technique, which identifies elements that are infrequently queried and frames the problem as a multi-hypothesis test, is elegant and non-trivial, especially in handling the adaptive nature of the queries. This technique may be of independent interest for proving lower bounds in other interactive settings.
    *   The design of the parameter-free algorithms is a major strength. Handling unknown distributions *f+*, *f-*, and an unknown *k* is a significant challenge. The proposed iterative-update scheme, which dynamically estimates the Hellinger distance between the distributions and uses it to set a threshold for when to query versus when to trust the side information, is a clever and powerful idea.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction effectively sets the stage by clearly defining the problem, connecting it to prior work (like SBM and entity resolution), and crisply stating the main contributions. The formal problem statement and theorems are precise.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section, while providing a useful proof of concept, is somewhat limited. The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (Cora) and primarily demonstrate the algorithm's robustness to approximate distributions. A major part of the paper's motivation is the sub-optimality of existing heuristics [40, 42, 43]. A direct experimental comparison against one or two of these state-of-the-art heuristics would have powerfully substantiated the practical benefits of the proposed principled approach and closed the loop on the paper's motivating narrative.

2.  **Algorithmic Details in Main Paper:** Due to space constraints, the description of the algorithms and their analysis in the main paper is highly condensed. The core ideas are presented, but much of the intuition—for instance, why Hellinger distance is the natural metric for both the `Membership` score and the convergence threshold, or how the Sanov's theorem-based analysis works—is deferred to the appendix. This makes it challenging for a reader to fully appreciate the algorithmic contribution without a deep dive into the supplementary material.

3.  **Technical Assumptions:** The lower bound in Theorem 2 requires H²(f+||f-) ≤ 1/18. While this condition likely focuses the analysis on the interesting regime where the distributions are not easily separable (if they were, few queries would be needed), the origin and tightness of this specific constant are not discussed. It appears to be an artifact of the proof technique, and it would be helpful to clarify if it is believed to be necessary.

### Constructive Suggestions

*   **Improving Intuition for the Algorithm:** In Section 3, a few sentences could be added to provide more intuition. For example, briefly explaining that the Hellinger distance is used because it bounds the total variation distance, which in turn bounds the classification error probability between the two estimated distributions (*p+* and *p-*), would immediately clarify its central role in the algorithm's design.

*   **Strengthening Experimental Claims:** For a future version of this work (e.g., a journal submission), the authors should strongly consider adding experiments that compare their algorithm's performance (in terms of query count vs. accuracy) against the prominent heuristics mentioned in the introduction. This would provide a more direct and compelling validation of the paper's practical significance.

*   **Clarifying the Lower Bound Condition:** A short remark in Section 2 on the H²(f+||f-) ≤ 1/18 condition would be beneficial. For example, explaining that this ensures the distributions are sufficiently "confusable" to make the query problem non-trivial, and acknowledging that the constant is a product of the specific analysis, would add clarity for the reader.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental, well-motivated problem with significant practical implications.
    *   Provides the first nearly-tight theoretical characterization of the query complexity of clustering with this general side information model.
    *   Contributions are technically deep, especially the novel information-theoretic lower bound and the elegant parameter-free algorithms.
    *   The work is original and establishes interesting connections to the SBM literature.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The experimental validation is limited and misses the opportunity to compare against the very heuristics that motivate the paper.
    *   The algorithm's description in the main text is very high-level, which may obscure the novelty of the algorithmic contribution for some readers.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

The strengths of this paper, particularly its significant and novel theoretical contributions to an important problem, far outweigh its weaknesses. The paper bridges a critical gap between theory and practice in interactive clustering and provides a solid foundation for future work in this area. The technical quality is high, and the results are of clear interest to the NeurIPS community.
