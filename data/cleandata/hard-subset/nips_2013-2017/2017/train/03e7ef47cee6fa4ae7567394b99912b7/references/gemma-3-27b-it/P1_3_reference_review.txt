## NIPS 2017 Paper Review: Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when an oracle providing pairwise cluster membership information is available, *alongside* side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors address a gap in the theoretical understanding of clustering algorithms that leverage side information – common in practical applications like entity resolution – which often outperform theoretical bounds (O(nk)).  They establish both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly-matching upper bounds on the query complexity, demonstrating a significant reduction in queries possible with the presence of side information. The key contributions include a novel framework for proving lower bounds in interactive clustering, efficient algorithms that are parameter-free, and a connection to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself within the growing body of work on clustering with oracles (e.g., [42, 43, 40]) and draws heavily from the theory of query complexity and decision tree complexity [16, 4, 8].  It acknowledges the existing O(nk) bounds but highlights the empirical observation that practical algorithms often require far fewer queries. The authors explicitly connect their work to entity resolution [17, 20] and community detection via the SBM [28, 15, 14, 2, 1].  They differentiate their work from existing SBM analysis by considering arbitrary distributions *f+* and *f-* and avoiding assumptions about *k* or cluster size, thereby tackling a more general and challenging scenario. The paper correctly points out limitations of previous work like [5] focusing on different settings and lacking information-theoretic lower bounds.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The development of a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive clustering with side information is a significant contribution.  This framework extends beyond the specific settings considered in the paper.
* **Tight Bounds:** The paper provides near-matching upper and lower bounds on query complexity, demonstrating a clear understanding of the fundamental limits of the problem. The logarithmic gap between bounds is reasonable and represents a strong result.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require knowledge of the underlying distributions *f+* and *f-* or the number of clusters *k*, making them highly practical and adaptable.
* **Connection to SBM:**  Establishing the relationship between the problem and the SBM provides a valuable connection to a well-studied area and facilitates the understanding and potential application of existing SBM techniques.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on the Cora dataset provide some empirical support for the theoretical findings, demonstrating the performance of the proposed algorithms and their resilience to approximate side information.
* **Clear and Thorough Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written, clearly explaining the problem, the contributions, and the technical details. The organization is logical, and the proofs are outlined with appropriate references to the supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Cora) and a specific similarity function.  A more comprehensive experimental evaluation on diverse datasets and with different similarity measures would strengthen the conclusions. While the authors acknowledge this is a starting point, a larger empirical study would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Algorithms:** While parameter-free, the proposed algorithms, particularly the iterative update procedure, appear somewhat complex to implement. A clearer discussion of the practical challenges and potential optimizations would be helpful.
* **Constants and Logarithmic Factors:** While the authors achieve near-matching bounds, the constants and logarithmic factors involved can significantly impact practical performance. A more detailed analysis of these factors would provide a more nuanced understanding of the algorithm's efficiency.
* **Handling Noisy Oracles:**  The paper explicitly excludes the case of noisy oracle responses, which is a realistic concern in many applications (e.g., crowdsourcing). Addressing this limitation in future work would increase the paper’s applicability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a fundamental and practically relevant problem with a rigorous theoretical approach.
* The contributions are novel and significant, filling a gap in the existing literature.
* The results are well-supported by theoretical analysis and preliminary experimental evidence.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The complexity of the proposed algorithms could hinder their practical adoption.
* The constants and logarithmic factors in the bounds could be significant in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The novelty of the framework, the tightness of the bounds, and the parameter-free nature of the algorithms are particularly commendable. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, and the algorithms are somewhat complex, these weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's theoretical strengths. 

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should consider:

* Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and similarity measures.
* Providing a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges and potential optimizations for the proposed algorithms.
*  Briefly acknowledging the implications of the constants and logarithmic factors in the bounds.
