Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner can adaptively ask same-cluster queries from an oracle and also has access to side information in the form of a noisy pairwise similarity matrix W. The side information is generated by two unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster), which may be arbitrary (beyond SBM), and the number of clusters k is unknown. The central question is how many queries are needed to exactly recover the clustering.

Without side information, both upper and lower bounds are Θ(nk). The paper shows that side information can dramatically reduce query complexity. It provides:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds: Any (possibly randomized) algorithm that knows k and f± must use at least Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries (for small Hellinger distance H(f+‖f−)), and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1.
- Parameter-free algorithms (unknown k and f±): A Monte Carlo algorithm that, according to the paper, uses O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries and succeeds with probability 1 − o(1/n), and a Las Vegas algorithm with O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries.

The work is positioned relative to: (i) practical heuristics for entity resolution/crowdsourcing that use side information but lack guarantees; (ii) interactive clustering with split/merge feedback (Balcan & Blum; Awasthi et al.); (iii) same-cluster queries for metric k-means (Ashtiani et al.) which differs in assumptions and objectives; and (iv) SBM/community detection where parameters are often known and thresholds are characterized for p, q. This paper generalizes SBM to arbitrary f±, provides adaptive-query lower bounds, and designs parameter-free algorithms.

Strengths:
- Novel model and question: Query complexity with arbitrary, unknown f± and unknown k in an adaptive setting with side information is timely and relevant to active learning/crowdsourcing and ER.
- Lower bound technique: The information-theoretic framework for adaptive queries is interesting and nontrivial; using Hellinger divergence, product distributions, and a careful “weak cluster” argument provides a clean dependency on k and H(f+‖f−). The lower bound applies even when the algorithm knows k and f±, strengthening the result.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms: The iterative-update scheme that estimates p+ and p− on the fly and uses data-driven thresholds is elegant and practically appealing. Time O(n^2), matching the side-information size, is reasonable for this model.
- Clear connection to SBM: The paper articulates how queries could shift recovery thresholds and suggests conjectures for the sparse regime.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Apparent mismatch between lower and upper bounds: The lower bound states Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}), while the Monte Carlo upper bound claims O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}). In the regime where k^2/H^2 ≤ nk, the upper bound would beat the lower bound by a factor ≈ k/log n, which cannot be correct. The Las Vegas bound O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) matches the lower bound up to log n. I suspect a typo in the Monte Carlo rate (perhaps it should be k^2 log n/H^2), or additional conditions are missing; this needs clarification.
- Assumptions and scope: The algorithmic results explicitly assume discrete f± with fixed support, while the lower bound allows discrete or continuous. The extension claim to larger finite supports is plausible but should be stated precisely (how do constants and log factors scale?). Discussion of continuous f± via discretization would strengthen the contribution.
- Clarity and presentation: The paper has several typographical/notation issues (e.g., ambiguous O( k logn H2 ) formatting; inconsistent constants; missing years/pages in references; some equations with sign errors—Lemma 1’s bound as written has negative terms leading the “probability of error” expression to look malformed). Pseudocode for the algorithms would help. A succinct table contrasting regimes and bounds would improve readability.
- Experimental evaluation: Only one dataset, no comparison to baselines (e.g., popular ER heuristics or active SBM methods), and no synthetic studies to verify scaling with k and H(f+‖f−). The “best/worst/greedy” strategies are internal ablations rather than comparisons to other methods. More evidence (varying k, H, n; noise models; robustness to model misspecification) would substantiate practical relevance.
- Model idealizations: The oracle is noiseless; although the authors point to a companion paper for noisy queries, a brief discussion of robustness and how the techniques might extend would be valuable. The requirement to store/access the full O(n^2) W can be prohibitive; commenting on sampling/compression would help.

Quality:
Technically strong overall, with a compelling lower bound framework and a promising parameter-free algorithmic approach. The main quality concern is the unresolved discrepancy between the lower bound and the stated Monte Carlo upper bound.

Clarity:
Readable but with nontrivial presentation issues. Proof sketches are reasonable, but key technical steps (e.g., concentration via Sanov in the Hellinger metric, independence assumptions across pairwise similarities) should be stated more cleanly. Algorithm description would benefit from pseudocode and a clearer exposition of the stopping thresholds.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first to tie adaptive query complexity for clustering with arbitrary unknown side-information distributions via Hellinger divergence, with parameter-free algorithms and near-matching information-theoretic lower bounds.

Significance:
High potential impact for theory of interactive clustering/entity resolution and community detection with queries. The results sharpen understanding of when side information materially reduces query needs.

Constructive suggestions:
- Reconcile the k vs k^2 discrepancy. If the Monte Carlo bound is k^2 log n/H^2, correct it; if not, explain the assumptions under which k log n/H^2 is achievable without contradicting the lower bound.
- Provide algorithm pseudocode and a compact theorem summary table by regimes (dominant term, success probability, knowledge of f±/k).
- Clarify assumptions: independence of wi,j across unordered pairs, discrete vs continuous f±, support size dependence, and constants (e.g., H^2 ≤ 1/18).
- Expand experiments: add synthetic studies sweeping k and H; compare against ER heuristics [40, 43, 18] and active SBM [19]; include runtime/memory metrics and ablations on estimation thresholds.
- Discuss extension to noisy oracles (briefly), and practical considerations when W is sparse or sampled.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, novel lower bounds for adaptive interactive clustering with side information
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms with guarantees
- Broad generalization of SBM; clear theoretical contributions

Arguments against acceptance:
- Potential inconsistency between lower and Monte Carlo upper bounds
- Limited empirical validation and lack of baselines
- Clarity/presentation issues and some ambiguous notation

Recommendation: Borderline accept, contingent on clarifying/correcting the Monte Carlo query complexity claim and improving clarity. If the mismatch persists, I would lean to reject; otherwise, the theoretical contributions warrant acceptance.
