Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when one has access to (i) a pairwise same-cluster oracle (queried adaptively) and (ii) a noisy similarity matrix (“side information”) whose entries are drawn from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster). The central message is that side information can reduce the number of oracle queries from Θ(nk) (needed without side information) to nearly Θ(k^2/H^2) up to logarithmic factors, where H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−. The authors provide:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds that hold even when k, f+, f− are known, showing that any (possibly randomized) adaptive algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) expected queries to recover with nontrivial probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1.

- Efficient parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not know k, f+, f−, use Hellinger-divergence–based statistics estimated from the side information, and achieve query complexity within a log n factor of the lower bound. The Monte Carlo algorithm claims O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries with high probability; the Las Vegas algorithm claims O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}).

- A lower-bound proof technique tailored to adaptive queries that leverages Hellinger divergence, product structure, and a careful “weak cluster” argument; and an upper-bound analysis that adapts Sanov’s theorem and an iterative “threshold-update” scheme to make the algorithm parameter-free.

The model strictly generalizes SBM (unknown k, arbitrary f±, adversarial cluster sizes) and the results help explain empirical gains reported in entity-resolution workflows that use side information.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: convincingly formalizes how side information trades off with interactive query complexity and explains the empirical gap between nk worst-case theory and practical heuristics.

- Technical novelty: (i) a lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive learning using Hellinger divergence—nontrivial in the presence of adaptivity, and (ii) a parameter-free algorithm that iteratively estimates the effective divergence and sets data-dependent thresholds; the analysis uses Sanov-type tools but carefully switches to Hellinger for metric properties.

- Generality: Distributions f± can be arbitrary (finite support; lower bounds extend to continuous), k and cluster sizes are unknown and potentially adversarial.

- Bridging to SBM: The work situates itself relative to exact recovery thresholds and suggests how queries shift phase transitions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Notational and typographical inconsistencies: There are repeated confusions between H and H^2 and missing division symbols in several main claims. For example, the stated upper-bound sometimes appears as O(k/(log n H^2)) (which would be strictly smaller than O(k/H^2)) and elsewhere as O((k log n)/H^2). The Las Vegas bound is also inconsistently typeset. These must be fixed; otherwise the comparison to the lower bound is unclear.

- Assumptions on side information: The algorithmic analysis assumes a single pair of latent distributions f± from which all entries are drawn i.i.d. Conditional independence is crucial in both the lower and upper bounds. While the authors briefly discuss generalizations to heterogeneous fi,j, rigorous guarantees under such heterogeneity are not provided.

- Proof sketch gaps in the main text: Key lemmas (e.g., anti-concentration for Waiting(C), convergence of the iterative-update thresholds, finite-sample control of empirical Hellinger) are deferred. Given the novelty, a stronger main-text overview would help the reader validate the core ideas without relying entirely on the appendix.

- Practical evaluation is quite limited: One dataset (cora), a single side-information pipeline, and internal “best/worst/greedy” query strategies. There is no comparison to existing ER/active clustering heuristics (e.g., [Awasthi et al. 2014], [Vesdapunt et al. 2014], [Firmani et al. 2016]) in terms of queries vs accuracy, nor to an oracle with known f± to validate near-optimality in practice. Scalability beyond n≈2k is not demonstrated; time is O(n^2) due to side info, which is significant in ER settings.

- Scope of exact recovery: The results target exact clustering; in many ER applications approximate recovery is acceptable. It would be valuable to discuss whether one can get a smoother trade-off in queries vs error (e.g., akin to SBM weak recovery regimes) under side information.

Quality
Technically solid overall; the lower-bound construction and use of Hellinger are appropriate and insightful. The product Hellinger bound and the TV–Hellinger relationship are used correctly up to constants. However, the presentation has enough notation/typo issues to impede verification of the claimed matching factors. The experimental support is too limited to substantiate the practical impact.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized but marred by notational inconsistencies (H vs H^2, missing divisions, occasional sign errors). Definitions of p+, p−, Membership, and thresholds would benefit from a clean notation table. A concise figure illustrating the iterative-update schedule would help.

Originality
High. Both the adaptive lower-bound framework with side information and the parameter-free iterative algorithm are new to my knowledge, and the use of Hellinger to align lower/upper bounds is elegant.

Significance
High for theory of interactive/active clustering and for understanding ER with crowdsourced queries. The results generalize SBM arguments and should spur work on interactive information-theoretic limits.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please standardize notation: use H^2(·||·) for squared Hellinger, state all bounds in terms of H^2, and fix divisions. Explicitly state the final Monte Carlo and Las Vegas query bounds in big-Oh with the same H^2 convention, and restate the matching lower bound.

- Can the iterative threshold be accelerated if one has coarse priors on H^2 (e.g., from a small pilot)? Is there a simple anytime version?

- How sensitive are the results to mild heterogeneity (different f± across cluster pairs)? Can you state a robustness theorem under bounded f-divergence drift?

- Please compare empirically against standard ER query-selection heuristics on multiple datasets, and report query counts to a fixed F1 and runtime.

- Clarify whether accessing the full W is assumed free. In ER, computing all pairwise similarities is often prohibitive.

Pros
- Clear and novel theoretical framework; near-tight lower/upper bounds with side information.
- Parameter-free, adaptive algorithmic design grounded in information measures.
- Bridges to SBM and suggests how queries shift recovery thresholds.

Cons
- Significant notation/typo issues; some proofs deferred with minimal overview.
- Limited experimental validation and lack of comparisons to prior work.
- Assumes i.i.d. side information; robustness to heterogeneity not formalized.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core theoretical contributions are strong and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community. I encourage acceptance conditional on fixing notation/clarity and, if possible, modestly strengthening the experiments.
