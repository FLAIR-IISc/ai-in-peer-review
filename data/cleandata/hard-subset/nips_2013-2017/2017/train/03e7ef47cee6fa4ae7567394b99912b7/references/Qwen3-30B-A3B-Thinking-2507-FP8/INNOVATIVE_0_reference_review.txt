

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for interactive clustering with side information, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors study the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm can access a similarity matrix (side information) in addition to querying an oracle about whether pairs of points belong to the same cluster. Without side information, the query complexity is known to be Ω(nk), but the paper demonstrates that side information can drastically reduce this complexity, with the reduction depending on the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity scores for intra-cluster and inter-cluster pairs.

The main contributions are two-fold: (1) a strong information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(min(nk, k²/H²(f+||f-))), and (2) efficient parameter-free algorithms achieving O(k/log n / H²(f+||f-)) query complexity. The algorithms work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-, which is particularly valuable for practical applications. The authors connect their model to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that their framework generalizes SBM while providing a more comprehensive theoretical foundation.

This work is significant for several reasons. First, it resolves a key theoretical gap in interactive clustering literature, where prior work either assumed knowledge of parameters or only provided heuristic algorithms. Second, the parameter-free nature of the algorithms addresses a major practical challenge in real-world applications where such parameters are often unknown. Third, the connection to SBM opens new research directions for understanding how query complexity affects community detection thresholds.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical contributions. The lower bound proof is particularly impressive, as it overcomes the challenge of proving information-theoretic bounds in interactive settings where standard techniques like Fano's inequality fail. The authors develop a novel framework that could be applied to other interactive learning problems. The upper bound algorithms are cleverly designed, particularly the iterative-update method that enables parameter-free operation.

The paper's strengths are substantial: it introduces a genuinely novel theoretical framework, provides tight bounds matching within logarithmic factors, and connects to a well-studied model (SBM) in a meaningful way. The practical motivation from entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering is well-articulated, and the experimental results on the Cora dataset provide relevant validation.

The main weaknesses are somewhat limited experimental validation (only one dataset) and a lack of discussion on handling noisy side information (though the authors note this is addressed in companion work). The paper also could more thoroughly discuss the computational complexity of the algorithms beyond stating they are polynomial-time.

This paper stands out for its theoretical novelty and the bold approach to addressing a fundamental question in interactive clustering. While the experimental results are somewhat limited, the theoretical contributions are substantial and address a clear gap in the literature. The parameter-free algorithms are particularly noteworthy, as they directly address a major practical challenge in the field.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS, as it bridges theoretical computer science and machine learning with practical applications in crowdsourcing and entity resolution. It makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive learning and clustering.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical framework addressing a significant gap in the literature
- Tight bounds matching within logarithmic factors
- Parameter-free algorithms with strong practical relevance
- Connection to stochastic block model opens new research directions
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with novel techniques

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental validation (only one dataset)
- Some practical details about noisy side information could be elaborated
- The paper could better emphasize the practical implications of the theoretical results

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with significant novelty and practical relevance. The authors have made a clear and important advance in understanding the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information. The work is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem in machine learning. Given my preference for novel theoretical contributions over incremental improvements, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The results provide a solid foundation for future work in interactive clustering and could directly inform the design of more efficient crowdsourced clustering systems.
