Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner can adaptively ask pairwise same-cluster queries to an oracle and also has access to side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W. Each entry wu,v is drawn independently from f+ if u and v are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise; f+, f−, and k are unknown to the algorithm. The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds: Any algorithm (even knowing k, f+, f−) needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) expected queries to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1, where H^2 denotes squared Hellinger divergence H^2(f+||f−) (for the interesting regime H^2 ≤ 1/18).
- Parameter-free algorithms: A Monte Carlo algorithm that, without knowing k, f+, f−, achieves query complexity O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) and succeeds w.h.p.; and a Las Vegas algorithm with query complexity O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}). Both run in time O(n^2).
- Technique: A new lower-bound framework for interactive (adaptive-query) settings, and an iterative-update design for the algorithms that estimates H^2 from data using Hellinger divergence and large-deviations (Sanov) arguments.

The work generalizes beyond SBM by allowing arbitrary (unknown) discrete f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes, and it connects to community detection by quantifying how queries reduce the difficulty.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The lower bound is carefully argued for the interactive setting via a reduction to multi-hypothesis testing on lightly queried points and controlling total variation via Hellinger. This addresses a gap where standard Fano-type bounds fail under adaptive queries. Assumptions (independent entries of W conditioned on labels, small H^2 regime) are stated; the constants (e.g., 1/18) are not optimized but acceptable.
- Upper bounds: The parameter-free algorithms and analyses are nontrivial. The iterative-update technique that estimates H^2 and triggers side-information-only assignments at size thresholds ME ≈ Θ((log n)/H^2) is compelling. The use of Hellinger (a metric) instead of KL is appropriate for concentration and triangle-inequality arguments. Query complexities essentially match the lower bounds up to log n.
- Completeness: The main proofs are sketched; full details are deferred to appendices. One assumption in Theorem 1 is that f+, f− are discrete with fixed finite support; the text claims extensions to larger finite supports but defers details. The lower bound covers discrete and continuous distributions.
- Empirics: Experiments are limited to one ER dataset and primarily illustrate the feasibility of the greedy strategy guided by learned distributions. The gap to theory (e.g., scaling with k, H^2) is not stress-tested.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with a clear high-level story and structure (lower bound, algorithm, analysis). However, there are several typos and notational inconsistencies that could hinder understanding:
  - Query complexity expressions sometimes appear as O(k log n / H^2) and elsewhere as O(k log n H^2) or O(k/log n H^2); please standardize and confirm it is O(k log n / H^2).
  - The success probability is sometimes stated as 1 − 1/n and sometimes 1 − o(1/n).
  - The assumptions on the sample space of f+, f− (fixed support size q) vs. “arbitrary pmfs” should be tightened.
  - A few references are incomplete or misformatted.
- The motivation via ER and the connection to SBM are well presented. The Membership subroutine and thresholds could benefit from a concise, self-contained pseudocode with the constants summarized.

Originality
- The lower-bound framework for adaptive-query clustering with side information is novel and of independent interest.
- The parameter-free algorithm that adaptively estimates divergence to decide when side information suffices appears new.
- The explicit Hellinger-based characterization of query complexity sharply separates the with/without side-information regimes and generalizes beyond SBM.

Significance
- The results advance the theory of interactive clustering and active learning with side information, quantifying query savings in terms of H^2. This is a useful lens for both theory and practice.
- The lower-bound technique could influence related problems (interactive hypothesis testing, distribution testing, communication with adaptive reveals).
- Practical impact is tempered by the requirement of a full O(n^2) similarity matrix with independent entry noise and by limited empirical validation. Still, the parameter-free nature and near-optimal guarantees are valuable.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and rigorous bounds nearly matching up to log n.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms with careful analysis using Sanov and Hellinger geometry.
- Novel interactive lower-bound technique.
- Meaningful connection to SBM and crowdsourced ER.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity/notation issues and typos; some theorem statements rely on unstated constants.
- Assumption of complete, independent side-information matrix may be unrealistic in some applications; scalability in memory/time for very large n is a concern.
- Experimental section is minimal: one dataset, limited baselines, no systematic variation of k, H^2, or noise models.
- The lower bound assumes H^2 ≤ 1/18; discuss whether conclusions qualitatively extend beyond this regime.
- The algorithm assumes discrete distributions with fixed support; elaboration on continuous or growing-support settings is deferred.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify and standardize all query complexity expressions; confirm the intended dependence is O(k log n / H^2) and O(k^2 log n / H^2).
- How sensitive are the algorithms to model misspecification (e.g., correlated W entries, heteroskedastic noise, or different fij across cluster pairs)?
- Can the parameter-free approach be adapted when only a sparse subset of W is available?
- For continuous f+, f−, what changes in the estimation and thresholds are needed?
- Could you provide additional experiments varying k and synthetic f+, f− to illustrate the predicted 1/H^2 scaling?

Minor comments
- Clean up reference formatting and missing years.
- Define all constants (C, B) where they first appear; unify success probability notation.
- State the memory requirements explicitly.

Recommendation
- Accept (borderline strong). The theoretical contributions are solid, original, and significant, with near-tight bounds and interesting techniques. The paper would benefit from improved clarity and stronger experiments, but overall it meets NeurIPS criteria well.

Confidence
- High: I am familiar with interactive learning, information-theoretic bounds, and SBM literature.
