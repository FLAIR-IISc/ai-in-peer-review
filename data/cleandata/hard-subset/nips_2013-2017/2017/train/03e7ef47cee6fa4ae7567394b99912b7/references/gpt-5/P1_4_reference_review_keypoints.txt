- Problem: Studies query complexity of clustering with a noiseless pairwise oracle plus side-information W drawn from unknown f+ (same-cluster) and f− (different-cluster), with unknown k and arbitrary cluster sizes.
- Goal: Quantify value of side information in reducing queries and design parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal query complexity.
- Lower bound: Presents interactive adaptive information-theoretic lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) for constant success prob and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for certainty in regime H^2 ≤ 1/18.
- Lower-bound novelty: Extends beyond SBM to arbitrary unknown f+, f− (discrete/continuous) and adversarial cluster sizes via a k-hypothesis testing reduction overcoming adaptive-query challenges.
- Algorithms: Two parameter-free algorithms—Monte Carlo with O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries (success 1 − o(1/n)) and Las Vegas with O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries and always correct.
- Algorithmic technique: Iterative-update procedure that empirically learns intra/inter distributions and uses Hellinger divergence to decide when side-information suffices.
- Relation to SBM: Model generalizes SBM and discusses how queries alter SBM recovery thresholds.
- Technical correctness: Overall approach and matching orders are sound; adaptive lower bound is a genuine contribution and algorithms appear plausible.
- Notational issues: Several presentation errors—Lemma 1 missing leading “1 −”, inconsistent TV–Hellinger constants and H vs H^2 notation, and a likely missing division in definition of MEx.
- Success-probability clarity: Confusing statements about Las Vegas vs Monte Carlo guarantees (claims of “recover with probability 1” vs query complexity holding with high probability).
- Completeness: Algorithm descriptions are high-level; needs precise pseudocode, stopping criteria, and a formal lemma for the Sanov-based Hellinger concentration (conditions such as fixed finite support).
- Early-estimation robustness: Question whether initial small-cluster estimates can be biased by misassignments and whether there is backtracking/safeguard.
- Runtime/memory: Algorithm reads O(n^2) side information; feasibility for large n and whether sparse/sampled W suffices is not discussed.
- Experiments: Limited empirical evaluation—single real dataset (Cora), no comparisons to prior heuristics ([40,43,18]) and no synthetic validation across H^2, k, n.
- Clarity: Paper is generally readable but has many typos, notation inconsistencies, missing reference details, and needs explicit assumptions for Hellinger estimation accuracy.
- Originality: Interactive adaptive lower bound and parameter-free Hellinger-based iterative algorithm are novel and the generality beyond SBM is a strong point.
- Significance: Substantially advances theoretical understanding with near-tight bounds; techniques likely applicable to other interactive learning problems.
- Practical impact: Experimental evidence too limited to establish practical impact; stronger empirical validation needed.
- Suggested corrections: Fix Lemma 1 and TV–H constants, clarify H vs H^2 notation, correct MEx formula, and formalize Las Vegas/Monte Carlo success claims.
- Suggested theoretical clarifications: Specify Sanov-based concentration assumptions (support size q, minimal mass); discuss continuous f+, f− and growing q.
- Suggested robustness analysis: Discuss adaptation to oracle errors (or point to companion work) and whether majority voting can be incorporated at similar query cost.
- Suggested experiments: Add comparisons to strong heuristics, and synthetic studies varying H^2, k, n to validate scaling and Phase 3B gray zone.
- Pros: Strong, near-tight theoretical results; general model (unknown f+, f−, unknown k); novel techniques with broad applicability.
- Cons: Notational/typographical inaccuracies, minimal experiments and baselines, assumptions (finite support, H^2 ≤ 1/18) and constants/log factors not fully explored, and algorithm description needs formalization for reproducibility.
- Recommendation: Lean accept, contingent on fixing clarity/notation issues and providing stronger empirical validation.
