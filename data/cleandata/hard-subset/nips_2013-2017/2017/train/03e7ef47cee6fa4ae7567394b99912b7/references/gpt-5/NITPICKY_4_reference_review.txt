Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when one has access to pairwise “same-cluster?” queries to an oracle and side information in the form of a similarity matrix W. Side information is generated by two unknown distributions f+ and f− over edge weights, depending on whether a pair is within the same cluster or across clusters. The paper claims (i) an information-theoretic lower bound for the number of adaptive pairwise queries needed for exact recovery, expressed in terms of Hellinger divergence H^2(f+||f−), and (ii) parameter-free, polynomial-time Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms whose query complexities purportedly match the lower bound up to a log n factor. It relates the model to SBM (as a special case) and briefly reports results on Cora.

Strengths
- Problem importance and motivation: Active clustering with side information is practically relevant (ER/crowdsourcing) and theoretically meaningful; the side-information lens helps explain lower query counts observed empirically.
- Conceptual contribution: A lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive algorithms that (purports to) handle unknown parameters, with a clean dependence on divergence between f+ and f−. This direction is valuable and potentially broadly applicable.
- Parameter-free algorithmic angle: Iterative, data-driven estimation of in-/out-distributions and stopping thresholds without knowing f+, f−, or k is appealing.
- Connection to SBM: Generalizes beyond Bernoulli weights and raises interesting questions about recovery thresholds under querying.

Major weaknesses and concerns
1) Inconsistent and possibly incorrect bounds:
- The dependence on k in the upper bounds is inconsistent across the abstract, Theorem 1, and surrounding text. Early on the paper states a Monte Carlo bound O(k log n / H^2), while later the Las Vegas bound is O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}), and the lower bound is Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2}). If the lower bound is k^2/H^2 (up to logs), a Monte Carlo upper bound of k log n / H^2 cannot “nearly match” it. This discrepancy is central and must be resolved precisely.
- The expressions often miss division slashes or parentheses (e.g., O( k logn H2(·) ) vs O(k log n / H^2(·)), “k2 logn H2” vs “k^2 log n / H^2”), making it impossible to verify the claims.

2) Hellinger divergence notation and inequalities:
- The paper alternates between H and H^2, calling H “Hellinger divergence” and defining H^2 = (1/2)∑(√f − √g)^2. In the proof of Lemma 1 it uses ||P−Q||TV ≤ √H(P||Q) (without constants or consistent definition). Standard relations are TV ≤ H ≤ √2·TV, with H commonly the (non-squared) Hellinger distance; if H^2 is used, constants differ. This inconsistency undermines the proof steps that hinge on quantitative bounds.
- Sanov’s theorem is KL-based; replacing KL by Hellinger in the large deviations argument requires careful inequalities with explicit constants. The main text asserts such replacements but details are deferred; given the already inconsistent use of H vs H^2, this is a red flag.

3) Proof technicalities and clarity:
- In Lemma 1, the definition and use of β appear malformed and lead to terms like (1−β) in denominators; the typesetting seems broken (“β ≡ 1 / + √Q/(ak)?”), and the set thresholds (e.g., 2T/k(1−β)) are difficult to parse or verify.
- The “weak clusters” argument and the characterization of events E, E1, E2 are not rigorous enough in the main text to instill confidence; constants and conditions (e.g., when Pt(E2) ≤ T/(k−1)) need precise derivations.
- The lower-bound regime requires H^2(f+||f−) ≤ 1/18, an odd-looking constant without justification; explain why this assumption is needed.

4) Algorithmic specification issues:
- The algorithm assumes discrete support for f+, f−. How is Membership defined and analyzed for continuous weights or large alphabets? The claim that results extend to any finite sample space “scaling linearly with its size” is not substantiated; binning/quantization effects on H^2 and guarantees are not discussed.
- Definitions of p+,x and p−,x and their denominators are error-prone in the text (e.g., |Ci|(|Ci − 1|) and typos), making it unclear how they are computed in practice and whether symmetry/double-counting is handled.
- The threshold MEx is written as “C log n H(p+,x||p−,x)^2” in one place and implied to be C log n / H^2 elsewhere. This must be fixed; the algorithm’s correctness depends on the exact threshold.
- Complexity claims say time O(n^2) bounded by reading W. But the algorithm repeatedly recomputes empirical distributions and runs iterative membership calculations; a more careful runtime and memory analysis is warranted, especially if W is dense and large.

5) Empirical evaluation is insufficient:
- Only one real dataset (Cora), no synthetic stress tests to validate dependence on H^2, k, or cluster-size imbalance, and no comparison to baselines (e.g., representative heuristics [40, 42, 43], or recent active SBM methods).
- The goal is exact recovery with high probability, but reported precision/recall are ~80–90%, not exact recovery. The link between the theory (exact recovery) and experiments (approximate recovery) is not addressed.
- The “best/worst/greedy” query strategies are internal to this work and not meaningful baselines; moreover, the “best” strategy assumes knowledge that the algorithm would not have.
- The “approximate distributions” exercise does not convincingly validate the parameter-free method under unknown f+, f−.

6) Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues (“therem,” “we can be allowed,” “st Conference on NIPS 2017”), inconsistent notation, and many incomplete or malformed references (missing years/pages, placeholders). This hinders readability and confidence.

Questions for the authors
- Please reconcile the dependence on k in the upper bounds with the lower bound. What is the correct Monte Carlo query complexity: O(k log n / H^2) or O(k^2 log n / H^2)? If the latter, where did the former come from?
- Provide a consistent definition of Hellinger distance/divergence used throughout, and correct the TV–Hellinger inequality constants in the proofs.
- Clarify the role and exact definition of β in Lemma 1 and fix all malformed expressions; provide a clean, self-contained derivation in the main paper of the crucial steps.
- Explain how the algorithm handles continuous-valued W or large alphabets. If discretization is required, how does it affect H^2 and guarantees?
- Expand the runtime/memory analysis beyond O(n^2), accounting for iterative updates.
- Strengthen experiments: include comparisons to established baselines, synthetic studies sweeping H^2 and k, and report exact-recovery rates where applicable.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Timely and important problem with a principled information-theoretic perspective.
- Potentially useful lower-bound framework for adaptive querying.
- Parameter-free algorithmic approach is conceptually attractive.

Cons:
- Critical inconsistencies in the main theoretical claims (dependence on k; Hellinger notation/inequalities).
- Proof sketch lacks rigor and contains likely errors.
- Algorithm specification has ambiguous thresholds/definitions and unclear handling of continuous W.
- Weak empirical validation and no meaningful baselines.
- Numerous presentation and reference issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles an important question and contains promising ideas, but the current version has too many technical inconsistencies, unclear or possibly incorrect bounds, and insufficient empirical support. A careful revision that fixes the bounds/notation, shores up the proofs, clarifies the algorithm, and adds solid experiments could make this a strong contribution in the future.
