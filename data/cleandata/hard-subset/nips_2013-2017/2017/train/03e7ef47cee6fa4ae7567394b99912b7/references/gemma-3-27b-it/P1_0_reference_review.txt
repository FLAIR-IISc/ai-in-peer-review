## Peer Review for "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with access to an oracle, specifically focusing on the impact of “side information” in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and design efficient algorithms that nearly match these bounds when side information is available. They demonstrate a significant reduction in query complexity compared to the case *without* side information (O(nk)), achieving complexities potentially sublinear in *n* depending on the quality of the side information and the number of clusters. The work builds on connections to the stochastic block model (SBM) and provides both a Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithm with accompanying proofs. The paper is well-motivated by practical applications like entity resolution, where leveraging human feedback via interactive querying is common.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself within a growing body of work on interactive clustering and active learning. It correctly identifies the gap between theoretical lower bounds (O(nk)) and the observed performance of heuristics in practice. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work by focusing on *information-theoretic* lower bounds and *parameter-free* algorithms, a notable distinction from works like [5] which rely on specific objective functions and parameter knowledge.  The connection to the SBM is well-placed, but the authors emphasize the generalization of their model through arbitrary distributions f+ and f-, and the lack of assumptions on cluster size/k.  The authors appropriately cite a comprehensive range of relevant literature, including works on entity resolution, stochastic block models, and active learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper’s core contribution lies in its rigorous information-theoretic analysis. Establishing lower bounds in the interactive setting is a significant achievement, overcoming the challenges posed by adaptive querying.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivation from entity resolution and crowdsourcing provides a compelling justification for the problem being addressed.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require knowledge of the underlying distributions or the number of clusters is a major strength, enhancing their applicability in real-world scenarios.
* **Clear Presentation of Results:** The theorems and algorithmic descriptions are clearly stated.  The connection between theoretical results and algorithm design is well-explained.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** While not a primary focus, the experiments on the Cora dataset demonstrate the practical performance of the proposed algorithms and validate the benefits of using side information.
* **Detailed Proofs:**  The extensive proofs (deferred to the appendix) demonstrate a thorough understanding of the underlying mathematical principles.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Proofs:**  The proofs, while rigorous, are dense and difficult to follow without substantial background in information theory.  The appendix is essential for understanding the details, and reviewers may struggle to fully assess the validity of the arguments without dedicating significant time.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset (Cora). While this demonstrates proof of concept, a broader evaluation on diverse datasets with varying characteristics (density, noise, cluster separation) would strengthen the claims.
* **Constant Factors:** The paper focuses on asymptotic complexity (O() notation). The impact of constant factors in the algorithms’ runtime and memory usage is not discussed.  In practice, these constants can be crucial for performance.
* **Hellinger Divergence Focus:** While a natural choice, the authors heavily lean on Hellinger divergence. Exploring other divergence measures or distance metrics might provide additional insights or alternative algorithmic approaches.
* **Discussion of Practical Challenges:** The paper assumes a perfect oracle. A brief discussion of the impact of noisy oracle responses (as mentioned in the related work section and a companion paper [34]) would acknowledge a practical limitation.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Excellent. The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work with well-supported claims.  The authors demonstrate careful analysis and honesty in acknowledging the assumptions and limitations.
* **Clarity:** Good. The main body of the paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard, but the proofs are demanding.
* **Originality:**  High. The combination of information-theoretic lower bounds, parameter-free algorithms, and the focus on side information represents a novel contribution to the field.
* **Significance:**  High. The results are important and likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on clustering, active learning, and entity resolution. The development of efficient algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees has the potential to impact real-world applications.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of query complexity in clustering.
* Novel and parameter-free algorithms with provable guarantees.
* Strong connection to relevant prior work and practical applications.
* Rigorous mathematical analysis and detailed proofs.

**Against:**

*  Proofs are dense and require significant expertise to fully verify.
* Limited experimental evaluation.
* Lack of discussion regarding constant factors and practical challenges.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** 

Despite the challenging proofs and limited experimental scope, this paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of interactive clustering. The rigorous analysis, parameter-free algorithms, and clear connection to practical applications make it a valuable contribution to NIPS. The authors have addressed a fundamental problem with a novel and insightful approach. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions:

1. **Expand Discussion of Practical Considerations:** Briefly acknowledge the impact of noisy oracle responses and discuss potential strategies for mitigating their effects.
2. **Clarify Constant Factors:** Provide a qualitative discussion of the constant factors involved in the algorithm’s runtime and memory usage.
3. **Consider Additional Experiments:** While not mandatory, exploring additional datasets would strengthen the empirical validation.
