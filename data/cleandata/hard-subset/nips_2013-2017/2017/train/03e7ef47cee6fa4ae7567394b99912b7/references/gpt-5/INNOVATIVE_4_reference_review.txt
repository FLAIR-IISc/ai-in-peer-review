Summary
The paper studies the fundamental query complexity of clustering when the learner has access to side information in the form of a noisy pairwise similarity matrix, but does not know the generating distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster), nor the number of clusters k. The authors:

- Develop an information-theoretic lower bound framework for adaptive (interactive) clustering with side information, showing that any (possibly randomized) algorithm needs at least Ω(min{nk, …}) queries, with the dependence on the separation between f+ and f− captured via the Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−).
- Design two efficient, parameter-free algorithms:
  - A Monte Carlo algorithm that recovers the clustering w.h.p. with query complexity scaling as O(min{nk, (k log n)/H2(f+‖f−)}), using an iterative-update mechanism that learns empirical intra/inter distributions and dynamically decides when side information is reliable enough to replace queries.
  - A Las Vegas algorithm that guarantees correctness with query complexity O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2(f+‖f−)}).
- Provide a generalization lens to SBM (Bernoulli(p), Bernoulli(q)) and discuss how querying could shift known recovery thresholds.
- Offer a small experimental case study on an entity-resolution dataset illustrating that a greedy query strategy closely matches a best-case query bound.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to interactive clustering/crowdsourcing (e.g., Awasthi et al., Balcan & Blum; heuristics for ER), active learning on SBM, and information-theoretic limits (Fano-type). It goes beyond typical SBM settings by handling arbitrary, unknown f+ and f− and adversarial cluster sizes, and by giving adaptive lower bounds where standard Fano does not directly apply. It also contrasts with works that assume metric structure or parameter knowledge.

Strengths
- Novelty and ambition: A clean, general model of side information with unknown f+ and f−, and parameter-free algorithms that nearly match information-theoretic lower bounds, is a substantial conceptual advance. The interactive lower bound framework for adaptive algorithms is particularly bold and of potential independent interest.
- Technical depth: The lower bound develops a careful argument handling adaptivity (via “weak clusters” and TV–Hellinger relations). The upper bound leverages an iterative-update method, with convergence analyses based on Sanov’s theorem while measuring distances via Hellinger—a nontrivial choice that enables metric control.
- Clarity of contribution: The paper explains the gap between nk-style worst-case theory and the much smaller query counts observed in ER heuristics, attributing the gap to exploitable side information and quantifying its effect via H2(f+‖f−).
- Practical relevance: Parameter-free design and the iterative-update recipe are attractive for practitioners, as exact knowledge of f+ and f− is unrealistic.

Weaknesses
- Notational/typing inconsistencies: There are several ambiguous big-O expressions where division vs multiplication is unclear (e.g., “k logn H2(f+‖f−)” versus “k log n / H2(f+‖f−)”; “k 2 H2(·)” could mean k^2/H2 or k^2·H2). This matters because the gap between lower and upper bounds depends critically on whether the dependence is k or k^2. The Monte Carlo upper bound and the stated lower bound appear inconsistent unless these are corrected; as written, the MC algorithm would seemingly beat the lower bound, which cannot be right. This must be clarified.
- Assumption/coverage gaps: The main development focuses on discrete distributions with fixed support for upper bounds; the extension to continuous distributions is only stated for lower bounds. A brief discussion or appendix on how to discretize or handle continuous f+, f− for the algorithm would strengthen the story.
- Experiments are minimal: Only one dataset, no comparisons to strong baselines or to prior ER heuristics under the same protocol, and no scaling or robustness studies. Given the theory-centric nature, this is not fatal, but a bit more empirical validation would help.
- Constants and practicality: The O(n^2) time due to processing the full side information matrix and log factors may be acceptable, but some discussion of memory/streaming variants or sparsity would help practitioners.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful use of Hellinger geometry and large deviations; however, the ambiguity in asymptotic expressions needs fixing, and a short proof sketch for some key lemmas (Sanov-based bounds specialized to Hellinger) would improve reproducibility. The lower bound argument for adaptivity is interesting and appears correct, albeit with conservative constants.

Clarity
The high-level narrative is clear, but presentation issues reduce readability: ambiguous formulas, footnotes/formatting glitches, and dense algorithm description without pseudocode. A figure or pseudocode block for the iterative-update phases (threshold setting, membership tests, Waiting(C)) would help.

Originality
High. A general, parameter-free treatment with side information, interactive lower bounds for adaptive querying, and the explicit Hellinger-driven characterization are genuinely new, to my knowledge.

Significance
High. The results help explain and formalize why ER-style systems can succeed with far fewer queries than nk, and the lower bound framework could influence other interactive learning problems. The SBM connection and conjectured threshold shifts open an exciting direction.

Questions for authors
1) Please disambiguate all query-complexity expressions. What are the exact dependences on k and H2 for MC and LV algorithms, and for the lower bounds (constant or failure probability 1/6 vs w.h.p.)? If the LV upper bound is O(n log n + (k^2 log n)/H2), is the lower bound Ω(n + k^2/H2), and how does the MC algorithm avoid contradicting it?
2) Can the algorithm handle continuous f+, f− by binning? What are the induced penalties?
3) Can you provide pseudocode for the iterative-update phases and explicit constants for ME?
4) Any guidance on computational/memory footprint and handling sparse side-information graphs?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel theory for an important problem with clear ML relevance.
- Parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal guarantees.
- Adaptive lower bound framework of independent interest.
- Bridges practice (ER, crowdsourcing) and theory.

Cons against acceptance
- Ambiguities in key asymptotic statements (k vs k^2) that must be resolved.
- Limited experimental validation and comparisons.
- Some presentation/notation issues reduce accessibility.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The conceptual advance and breadth outweigh the presentation glitches and modest experiments. Please fix the notational ambiguities, add concise pseudocode, and clarify the MC vs LV vs LB relationships before publication. My enthusiasm is driven by the novelty and potential impact of the lower-bound framework and parameter-free design.
