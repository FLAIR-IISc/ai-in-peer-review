Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when, in addition to a same-cluster oracle, the algorithm has access to a noisy pairwise similarity matrix (side information). Side information is generated from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster pairs) and f− (across-cluster pairs), with no assumptions on k or cluster sizes. The authors:

- Prove information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive algorithms (even with known k, f+, f−), showing that any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1.
- Give two efficient, parameter-free clustering algorithms: a Monte Carlo algorithm with query complexity O(min{nk, k/(H^2 log n)}) and a Las Vegas algorithm with O(n log n + min{nk, k^2/(H^2 log n)}), both using the Hellinger divergence between empirical distributions. These match the lower bounds up to a log n factor.
- Connect the model to SBM and discuss how queries could shift recovery thresholds.
- Provide a small experiment on the Cora dataset indicating that a greedy querying strategy guided by side information can use far fewer queries than nk while maintaining good F1.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and timely problem: understanding why heuristic ER/interactive clustering methods can succeed with far fewer queries than nk when guided by side information.
- Lower bounds for interactive (adaptive) algorithms are nontrivial; the framework using Hellinger distance and careful conditioning appears novel and of independent interest.
- Upper bounds are nearly tight and “parameter-free,” not assuming access to k, f+, f−. The iterative-update idea for estimating p+ and p− and adapting thresholds is interesting.
- The formulation unifies and generalizes SBM, and the paper clearly positions the work within that literature.
- Algorithms are polynomial time (O(n^2)), which is reasonable given the need to read the side-information matrix.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and practicality: The model assumes access to the full n×n side-information matrix with i.i.d. entries conditioned on cluster labels. In many ER/real settings, computing/storing all pairwise similarities is infeasible; blocking is essential. The paper does not discuss how the results extend to partial/blocked side information.
- Discrete vs continuous: The algorithm and analysis rely on discrete supports (fixed q) to form empirical distributions. The text claims continuous distributions are covered for the lower bound, but it is unclear how the parameter-free algorithm operates with continuous-valued similarities (binning? bandwidth selection?) and whether guarantees persist.
- Parameter-free claim: The method appears to need a fixed finite alphabet size and uses a constant C in thresholds (e.g., ME = C log n / H^2). How C is chosen, and sensitivity to this choice, are not clearly specified. This weakens the “parameter-free” claim in practice.
- Clarity and rigor: The presentation has numerous typos/formatting issues and some imprecise statements (e.g., switching between H and H^2, inconsistent constants). Several critical pieces are deferred to the appendix (Sanov-based bounds, anti-concentration for Waiting(C)), making it difficult to verify key steps. The lower bound assumes H^2 ≤ 1/18; the necessity of this regime and its effect on tightness are not discussed.
- Empirical validation is very limited: a single dataset, no synthetic experiments to validate the predicted scaling in k and H^2, no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., query-efficient correlation clustering or SBM-active methods), no ablations (e.g., impact of errors in p+ and p− estimation, varying support size q, or continuous noise). For a claim of general, parameter-free practicality, much more evidence is needed.
- Practical complexity: Time O(n^2) and reliance on a full W may be prohibitive. There is no discussion of memory/streaming variants, or how to integrate blocking.

Quality
The theoretical direction is strong and potentially impactful. However, some proofs are sketched and depend on substantial appendices; constants, regimes, and assumptions could be articulated more precisely. The empirical section does not substantiate the practical claims or test the theory’s predicted dependencies.

Clarity
The paper needs significant editing for clarity and polish. Notation around Hellinger distance is inconsistent. The algorithm description would benefit from precise pseudocode, explicit handling of ties, and a clearer exposition of threshold updates and stopping conditions. Several references are incomplete or missing years; there are obvious formatting issues.

Originality
High on the lower-bound framework for interactive clustering with side information and on parameter-free algorithms that adapt to unknown f+, f−, k. The explicit linkage to SBM is useful.

Significance
Theoretical significance is promising: tight (up to log n) bounds and a novel lower-bound approach for adaptive settings. Practical significance is less clear given the strong assumptions about side information, limited experiments, and lack of discussion on scalability constraints common in ER.

Questions for the authors
- How does the algorithm handle continuous-valued W? Do you require binning; if so, how are bins chosen without parameters, and how does this affect guarantees?
- What is the role and selection of constant C in ME = C log n / H^2? How sensitive is performance to C?
- Do your guarantees hold under blocking/partial W, or if only O(n log n) similarities are available?
- Can you provide explicit finite-sample constants in the Sanov-based bounds and in the anti-concentration (Waiting(C)) result?
- Can the lower bound be extended beyond H^2 ≤ 1/18, or argue this is the practically relevant regime?
- What are the failure modes with highly unbalanced/very small clusters?

Pros
- Nontrivial, near-tight lower and upper bounds in an adaptive setting.
- Parameter-free approach that does not assume k, f+, f−.
- Connections to SBM and query-augmented recovery thresholds.

Cons
- Strong assumptions on availability and form of side information.
- Limited empirical validation; no scaling or robustness studies.
- Clarity/rigor issues, with key details deferred and some notational inconsistencies.
- Practicality concerns (full O(n^2) W, no blocking).

Recommendation
Weak Reject. The theoretical contributions are interesting and potentially significant, but the paper in its current form lacks clarity in key proofs/assumptions and provides insufficient empirical validation for the practical claims. Strengthening the exposition, clarifying assumptions (especially regarding continuous W and true parameter-free operation), and adding controlled experiments that validate the predicted dependencies would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
