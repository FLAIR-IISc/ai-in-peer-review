Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when, in addition to access to a same-cluster oracle (pairwise queries answered by an oracle that knows the true partition), the algorithm also has side information: a similarity matrix W whose entries are generated i.i.d. from f+ if the pair is within a cluster and from f− otherwise. Crucially, the algorithm does not know k, f+, or f−. The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of adaptive pairwise queries required to exactly recover the clustering (even when k, f+, f− are known to the algorithm), expressed in terms of the squared Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−).
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that recover the clustering exactly with high probability, using O(log n / H2(f+‖f−)) “seed” samples per cluster to leverage side information and reduce the number of oracle queries to near the lower bound (up to log factors).
- A general technique for lower bounds in interactive settings by casting node assignment as a k-ary hypothesis test and controlling the total variation between distributions under different hypotheses via Hellinger distance.

The work generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) community detection setting (e.g., Abbe et al., Mossel et al.) by allowing arbitrary, unknown f+, f− (beyond Bernoulli), unknown k, and adversarial cluster sizes. It connects to prior heuristics for crowdsourced entity resolution and interactive clustering (e.g., Wang et al., Gokhale et al., Awasthi et al., Ashtiani et al.), and explains why, in practice, side information can dramatically reduce the number of human queries compared to the Ω(nk) lower bound without side information.

Strengths:
- Novelty: Formulates and analyzes clustering with side information and pairwise queries for unknown k and unknown f+, f−; provides general lower bounds for adaptive algorithms. This appears new relative to prior SBM thresholds and interactive clustering works.
- Technical soundness (lower bound): The proof strategy—selecting a lightly queried element, expressing assignment as multi-hypothesis testing, and bounding error probability via total variation/Hellinger relationships—is principled and robust to adaptivity. The construction carefully separates “weakly queried” clusters and uses independence of W to control divergences.
- Technical soundness (upper bound): The parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm uses an iterative update to estimate p+ and p− from currently grown clusters and a membership score based on Hellinger divergence between intra- and inter-distributions. The stopping threshold ME ≈ C log n / H2 aligns with large deviations intuition (Sanov-type) and yields near-optimal query complexity, with a Las Vegas variant adding an O(n log n) overhead.
- Clarity of high-level ideas: The motivation and connection to entity resolution and SBM are explained well; the role of Hellinger distance is consistent across lower and upper bounds.
- Significance: Establishes a clean dependence of query complexity on statistical separability H2(f+‖f−), offering a theoretical explanation for empirical reductions in queries seen in practice. The parameter-free nature is attractive for deployment.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Notational/typographical ambiguity: Several big-O and Ω bounds appear with missing parentheses or unclear exponents (e.g., “O( k logn H2 )” could be O(k log n / H2); Theorem 2 states a term “k 2 H2” whose intended form (k/H2 vs k2/H2) must be clarified. Since the text claims matching within a log n factor, the precise dependence on k should be made consistent for all statements (Monte Carlo, Las Vegas, and lower bound).
- Assumptions on distributions: The upper bounds assume discrete f+, f− over a fixed, bounded alphabet (q = O(1)); extensions to larger finite alphabets or continuous distributions are mentioned but not developed for the algorithm. It would help to state clearly the exact assumptions per theorem and the dependence on q if q grows.
- Proof details deferred: The main algorithmic analysis relies on an adaptation of Sanov’s theorem but the core concentration and anti-concentration lemmas (e.g., Lemma 3, Lemma 6) are only sketched. While acceptable due to space, some readers may find it difficult to verify the finite-sample constants and the exact success probability (stated as 1 − o(1/n) in places).
- Experimental evaluation: The cora experiment is limited in scope. It assesses best/worst/greedy query ordering strategies relative to the learned (or perturbed) f+, f−, but does not compare against existing active clustering/crowdsourcing heuristics [40, 43, etc.], nor does it report end-to-end exact recovery rates or performance without an oracle. Stronger empirical validation (more datasets, baselines, sensitivity to k and H2, runtime) would bolster the practical significance.
- Computational considerations: The algorithm requires O(n^2) time and access to the full similarity matrix W. Discussion of scalability and memory for large n would be useful.

Quality:
- Lower bound methods are sound and general; constants are not optimized, and some steps use coarse inequalities (e.g., Markov), but that is acceptable for order-wise results.
- Upper bound methods are plausible and internally consistent with their thresholds; the reliance on Hellinger (a metric) is appropriate for triangle-like inequalities and product distributions.

Clarity:
- The narrative is clear, but the paper would benefit from cleaning typographical issues, unifying notation for H vs H2, and explicitly stating each theorem’s assumptions and exact asymptotic form.
- Including a concise algorithm box and a small worked example would help readability.

Originality:
- High. The interactive lower bound framework with side information and the parameter-free iterative update algorithm using Hellinger-based membership appear novel.

Significance:
- The results are potentially impactful for active clustering and entity resolution, offering near-optimal query bounds and a unifying perspective on the role of side information. The connection to SBM thresholds with queries is an interesting direction.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a timely and practically motivated problem with strong theory.
- General lower bounds for adaptive querying with side information.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with near-matching bounds.
- Clear connections to and generalizations of SBM.

Cons for acceptance:
- Some ambiguity/inconsistency in stated bounds (especially k-dependence) and notation.
- Algorithmic assumptions (discrete bounded alphabet) could be restrictive; extensions not fully formalized.
- Limited experiments and lack of comparison to strong baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify all big-O/Ω expressions with unambiguous parentheses and exponents; ensure consistency across abstract, theorems, and discussion.
- State assumptions on f+, f− explicitly per theorem; discuss dependence on alphabet size and extensions to continuous distributions for the algorithm.
- Expand empirical section with standard baselines and larger datasets; report exact recovery rates and query budgets vs H2 and k.
- Discuss computational/memory scalability for large n and possible sampling/compression of W.

Overall recommendation: Accept if the notational issues are corrected and the evaluation is modestly strengthened; the theoretical contributions are substantial, technically sound, and of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.
