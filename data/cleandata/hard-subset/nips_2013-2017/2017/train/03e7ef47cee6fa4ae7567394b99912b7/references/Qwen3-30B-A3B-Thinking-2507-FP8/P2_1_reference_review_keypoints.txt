- Paper studies query complexity for clustering with side information (similarity matrix W) and oracle same-cluster queries.
- Key result: query complexity reduced from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H^2(f+||f-))) using side information (H^2 = squared Hellinger divergence).
- Authors provide both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds (within logarithmic factors).
- Lower bound (Theorem 2) is information-theoretic and holds for randomized interactive algorithms.
- Upper bound algorithms (Theorem 1) are computationally efficient (O(n^2)) and parameter-free (do not require f+, f-, or k).
- Framework connects the model to the stochastic block model (SBM) and analyzes recovery thresholds with queries.
- Proof techniques are sophisticated, including an extension of Sanov’s theorem and linking algorithm performance to Hellinger divergence.
- Paper acknowledges limitations such as not handling erroneous oracle answers (addressed in companion work).
- Experimental validation uses the Cora dataset and shows greedy strategy nearly matches best strategy with slightly more queries.
- Presentation is well-organized and clear; problem statement and algorithm phases are detailed and understandable.
- Lower bound proofs are dense and may be challenging for readers without strong information-theory background; some notation needs clarification.
- Originality: first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information and novel framework for interactive lower bounds.
- Practical significance: substantial reduction in human-labeled queries for applications like entity resolution; parameter-free algorithms are practitioner-friendly.
- Connection to SBM is insightful but could be developed further theoretically.
- Experimental section is limited (single dataset, one similarity function) and lacks broader comparisons to heuristics.
- Weaknesses also include limited practical comparisons and potentially hard-to-verify technical proofs.
- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, technically sound contribution with practical relevance despite limited experiments.
