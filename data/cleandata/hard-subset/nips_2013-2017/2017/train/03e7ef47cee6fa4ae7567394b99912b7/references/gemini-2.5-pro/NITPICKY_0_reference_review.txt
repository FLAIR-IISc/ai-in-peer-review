**Summary**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with the help of a "same-cluster" oracle, motivated by the observation that practical heuristics often outperform the worst-case theoretical lower bound of Ω(nk). The authors hypothesize that this is due to the use of "side information," which they model as a noisy similarity matrix where values are drawn from one of two distributions, *f+* (intra-cluster) or *f-* (inter-cluster). The key contributions are threefold: (1) an information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) on the number of queries required, which holds even for randomized algorithms with full knowledge of the problem parameters; (2) a parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm that nearly matches this bound up to a logarithmic factor, succeeding with high probability; and (3) a corresponding Las Vegas algorithm. The model is presented as a significant generalization of the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM). Experiments on the Cora dataset are provided to support the proposed methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important discrepancy between the theory and practice of interactive clustering. The core idea of formally modeling the "side information" to explain this gap is novel and provides a solid theoretical foundation for a previously heuristic-driven area. The problem formulation is a natural and powerful generalization of the SBM.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contributions:** The paper presents a comprehensive theoretical study, providing both lower and upper bounds. The information-theoretic lower bound is particularly strong, as it handles adaptive queries and holds even if the algorithm knows the distributions *f+, f-* and the number of clusters *k*. The result elegantly quantifies the value of side information in terms of the Hellinger divergence between the distributions.

3.  **Advanced Algorithmic Design:** The proposed algorithms, especially the Monte Carlo version, are sophisticated. The iterative-update scheme to estimate the distributions on the fly and determine a confidence threshold for using the side information is a clever technique for designing a parameter-free algorithm. This is a non-trivial achievement, as the algorithm must operate without knowledge of *k, f+,* or *f-*.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, while containing promising ideas, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and methodological rigor, which collectively undermine its contribution.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Numerous Errors:** The manuscript is replete with typographical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and ambiguous statements that severely impede readability and technical assessment.
    *   The introductory paragraph contains a malformed reference: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)".
    *   There is a major and misleading typo in the summary of the lower bound in the "Contributions" section. It states the bound is Ω(min{nk, k²H²(f+||f-)}), which incorrectly suggests that more distinguishable distributions (larger H²) require *more* queries. The correct bound, as stated in Theorem 2, should be proportional to k²/H². This is a critical error in the summary of the paper's main result.
    *   There is an inconsistency in the query complexity of the Monte Carlo algorithm. The "Upper Bound" paragraph claims O(k log n / H²(...)), while Theorem 1 claims O(k² log n / H²(...)). This discrepancy regarding a key parameter *k* is confusing.
    *   The proof sketch of Theorem 2 is difficult to follow and appears to contain an error. The final step claims `1− ok(1)− 1/6 − 2/3 = 1/6 −O(1/√k)`, which is arithmetically unsound and conflates different terms without justification. Similarly, the formatting for the choice of `a` is broken: `a = b 1 9H2(f+‖f−)c`.
    *   The bibliography is inconsistently formatted (e.g., compare [4], [38], [42]).

2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail and Unverified Claims:** The paper makes strong claims that are not adequately supported in the main text.
    *   The core analysis of the algorithms, including the proof of convergence for the iterative-update scheme and the crucial anti-concentration result for the size of `Waiting(C)` (Lemma 6), are relegated entirely to an appendix. As the appendix was not provided, the central claims of the upper bound are unverifiable. A paper's core technical contributions must be assessable.
    *   The algorithm is claimed to be "parameter-free," but its description involves a constant `C` (e.g., `dC log ne` and `MEx`). The choice and sensitivity of this hyperparameter are not discussed, which contradicts the "parameter-free" claim.
    *   Footnote 3 makes a strong simplifying assumption that the sample space has a constant size. The claim that the results extend by "scaling linearly with its size" is a significant detail that is stated without proof or explanation of its impact on the complexity bounds.

3.  **Weak Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is cursory and unconvincing.
    *   The paper refers to Figures 1 and 2, but they are not included in the manuscript, making it impossible to evaluate the experimental results.
    *   The description states that perturbing the distributions to have a smaller Hellinger divergence (0.4587 vs 0.6, meaning the side information is *less* useful) resulted in a negligible increase in queries (1136 vs 1148). This result seems to contradict the theoretical bounds, which show a strong `1/H²` dependency. This discrepancy is not explained or even acknowledged. The section lacks the depth needed to properly validate the proposed algorithms.

4.  **Limited Assumptions in Analysis:** The lower bound in Theorem 2 requires the condition H²(f+||f-) ≤ 1/18. While such conditions are common in information-theoretic proofs, this specific, somewhat small constant limits the stated applicability of the result without a clear discussion of why this threshold arises from the proof technique.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper tackles an important and interesting problem with a novel approach and presents potentially significant theoretical results, the execution is deeply flawed. The high density of typographical and notational errors, a questionable calculation in a key proof sketch, the complete reliance on an unavailable appendix for the verification of the main algorithmic claims, and a weak experimental section prevent me from recommending acceptance. The work shows promise, but the manuscript does not meet the standards of quality, clarity, and completeness required for publication at NIPS. A major revision addressing these numerous and substantial issues would be necessary before the work could be fairly evaluated.
