Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner can ask same-cluster queries to an oracle and also has access to side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W. The side information is generated from unknown distributions f+ and f−: for pairs within a cluster, entries are drawn from f+, and across clusters from f−. The setting strictly generalizes stochastic block models (SBM), allowing arbitrary discrete (and, for the lower bound, continuous) distributions, unknown k, and adversarial cluster sizes. The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds on the expected number of pairwise queries required for exact recovery, in terms of the Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−): Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}) for success probability bounded away from 0, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H2}) for success probability 1.
- Two efficient parameter-free algorithms (no knowledge of k, f+, f−): a Monte Carlo algorithm that reportedly uses O(min{nk, (k log n)/H2}) queries and succeeds with probability 1 − o(1/n), and a Las Vegas algorithm with query complexity O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2}) and success probability 1.
- A proof framework for adaptive lower bounds that leverages product-structure Hellinger divergence, and an iterative-update design for parameter-free algorithms that estimates intra/inter distributions from side information and adapts thresholds until a cluster is “grown”.
- A small empirical study on a bibliographic entity-resolution dataset showing that a greedy query-ordering strategy performs close to a best-case oracle.

Relation to prior work
The model with side information generalizes SBM (e.g., Abbe et al., Mossel et al.), moving beyond Bernoulli edge models and constant/known k, and placing emphasis on interactive querying. It complements crowdsourcing/ER heuristics that use side information to select queries (e.g., Wang et al., Vesdapunt et al., Firmani et al.) by providing tight query-complexity characterizations. The lower-bound technique goes beyond non-interactive Fano-type arguments by handling adaptive queries. The algorithms are parameter-free, contrasting with recent SBM results that assume parameter knowledge for exact recovery. The paper also contrasts with interactive clustering for k-means under metric/separation assumptions (Ashtiani et al.), noting different objectives and bounds.

Strengths
- Novel and significant theoretical contribution: An adaptive, information-theoretic lower bound in terms of Hellinger divergence that applies even when f+, f−, and k are known to the algorithm. The Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}) form crisply quantifies the value of side information.
- Nearly matching upper bounds with efficient, parameter-free algorithms. The Las Vegas algorithm’s query complexity matches the lower bound up to a log n factor and an additive n log n term.
- Clean generalization of SBM and connection to recovery thresholds; the paper opens a clear path to studying how active queries shift SBM thresholds.
- Clear high-level proof ideas: reduction to k-ary hypothesis testing for a lightly queried element, identification of “weak” clusters, and use of Hellinger divergence for product distributions.
- Practical motivation (entity resolution) and a simple experiment demonstrating that an adaptive greedy rule can get close to a best-case querying strategy.

Weaknesses
- Potential inconsistency between lower and upper bounds: The Monte Carlo algorithm is claimed to succeed with high probability using O((k log n)/H2) queries, which is asymptotically smaller than the lower bound Ω(k^2/H2) for success probability bounded away from 0. Either the Monte Carlo bound is misstated, the success probability notion differs (e.g., conditional/median vs expected queries), or there is a missing factor of k. This discrepancy must be reconciled explicitly.
- Presentation/notation issues hinder clarity:
  - Several big-O expressions are ambiguous without parentheses (e.g., O(k logn H2) vs O((k log n)/H2)).
  - Inconsistent use of H and H2; thresholds sometimes written in terms of H, sometimes H2.
  - Proof sketches rely on lemmas relegated to the appendix; the main paper could better convey the key concentration steps (e.g., how Sanov’s theorem is adapted via relations between KL and Hellinger).
  - Some references are incomplete (missing years, venues) and there are noticeable typos and formatting glitches.
- Experimental validation is limited:
  - One dataset, no comparison against established heuristics for crowdsourced ER/interactive clustering.
  - No stress tests varying k, cluster imbalance, or Hellinger divergence to corroborate theory.
  - The oracle/noise model used in the experiment (and how queries are answered) is not fully specified.
- Assumptions and constants:
  - Lower bound requires H2(f+‖f−) ≤ 1/18; discuss necessity/sharpness and what happens outside this regime.
  - Anti-concentration and “Waiting(C) is constant” are crucial to the query bound; more intuition and a brief proof sketch in the main text would help.

Quality
- The core ideas and techniques are sound and well-motivated. The lower-bound methodology for adaptive querying is a notable technical achievement. However, the Monte Carlo vs lower-bound discrepancy needs resolution, and several derivations are only outlined. The experimental section is not sufficient to substantiate practical performance beyond a demonstration.

Clarity
- The paper is generally organized and readable at a high level, but suffers from ambiguities in notation, missing parentheses, and heavy reliance on appendices. Improving exposition around thresholds, divergence choices, and the iterative-update mechanism would significantly help.

Originality
- High. The adaptive lower-bound framework, the parameter-free iterative-update algorithm using Hellinger-based membership, and the general side-information model are novel and impactful.

Significance
- High for the theory/learning community: quantifies the benefit of side information in interactive clustering and bridges a recognized gap between heuristics and worst-case nk bounds. The techniques could influence future work in active learning with side information, adaptive lower bounds, and SBM with queries.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify the query complexity of the Monte Carlo algorithm and reconcile it with Theorem 2. If the bound should be O((k^2 log n)/H2), please correct; otherwise, explain the probability/expectation regimes to avoid apparent contradiction.
- Standardize notation for Hellinger: consistently use H2 for squared divergence; ensure all thresholds and sample complexities use the same power.
- Briefly outline in the main text how Sanov (KL-based) is converted to Hellinger-based confidence intervals (via inequalities between divergences), and why Hellinger’s metric properties are essential.
- Provide more intuition on Lemma 6 (anti-concentration) and why Waiting(C) is constant in expectation/high probability.
- Expand experiments: compare to practical baselines (e.g., [40], [43]), vary k and synthetic f+, f− to verify scaling with H2, and report wall-clock costs.
- Discuss cluster-size imbalance adversarially (your lower bounds allow it); how do the algorithms behave when many tiny clusters exist?
- Minor: clean up citations, fix typos, and add missing years/venues.

Pros
- Strong, general, and nearly tight theory for interactive clustering with side information.
- New adaptive lower-bound technique using Hellinger divergence.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms; Las Vegas bound matches lower bound up to log n.
- Meaningful generalization of SBM and clear future directions.

Cons
- Possible inconsistency in Monte Carlo complexity vs lower bound.
- Clarity and notation issues.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons.

Recommendation
- Lean accept, contingent on clarifying the Monte Carlo bound and improving exposition. The theoretical contributions are significant and of clear interest to NeurIPS.

Confidence
- High on the assessment of the lower-bound framework and significance; moderate on exact algorithmic query bounds due to the noted discrepancy.
