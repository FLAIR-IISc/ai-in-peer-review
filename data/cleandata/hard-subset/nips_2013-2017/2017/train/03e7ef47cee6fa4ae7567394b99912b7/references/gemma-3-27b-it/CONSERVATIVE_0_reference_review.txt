This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on scenarios where an oracle provides pairwise cluster membership information. The authors establish a connection to entity resolution, motivating the study with practical applications where obtaining labeled data is costly. They present information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms that nearly match these bounds, demonstrating a reduction in query complexity when side information is available. The work builds on and generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM), making a contribution to understanding the interplay between side information and clustering efficiency.

The core idea – that side information can significantly reduce the query complexity of clustering – is intuitive and potentially valuable. The theoretical framework established by the authors is commendable, and the attempt to provide matching upper and lower bounds is ambitious. However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that significantly temper my enthusiasm.

Firstly, the presentation is dense and challenging to follow. While the authors demonstrate technical prowess, the exposition lacks clarity. The numerous theorems and lemmas, particularly in the lower bound proof (Section 2), are presented without sufficient intuition or explanation, making it difficult to grasp the core arguments. The connection to SBM, while mentioned, isn’t fully leveraged to provide context for readers familiar with that literature. The jump between the motivating application and the formal problem statement feels abrupt.

Secondly, the experimental validation (Section 4) feels perfunctory and underwhelming. The results on the Cora dataset are presented with minimal analysis. While demonstrating the algorithm’s performance, they don't convincingly showcase the *practical* impact of the theoretical results. The comparison to "best," "worst," and "greedy" strategies feels ad-hoc and lacks a rigorous comparison against established clustering heuristics, *without* an oracle. This is a critical omission, given the stated goal of understanding the deviation between theory and practice. Simply showing that the proposed algorithm works well isn't enough; it needs to be demonstrated that it outperforms existing methods, or at least provides a valuable trade-off between query complexity and accuracy.

Thirdly, the assumption of a perfect oracle throughout the theoretical analysis feels unrealistic. While the authors acknowledge the possibility of noisy oracle answers and mention a companion work addressing this, the core contribution is built on a simplified, idealized scenario. This limits the direct applicability of the results to real-world applications, where oracle errors are inevitable.

Finally, the authors frequently emphasize the "parameter-free" nature of their algorithms. However, the reliance on estimating distributions (f+ and f-) from data, even in the initial phases, introduces a practical parameter – the size of the initial cluster needed for reliable estimation. While not explicitly stated, this introduces a tuning parameter that could impact performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical framework for analyzing query complexity with side information.
*   Tight information-theoretic bounds demonstrating the benefits of side information.
*   Generalization of the stochastic block model.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity and accessibility in the presentation.
*   Weak experimental validation and lack of comparison to established heuristics.
*   Unrealistic assumption of a perfect oracle.
*   Overemphasis on “parameter-free” nature while implicitly introducing tuning parameters.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a technically sound contribution, but its impact is limited by the aforementioned weaknesses. The theoretical results are interesting, but the lack of compelling experimental evidence and the convoluted presentation hinder its accessibility and practical relevance. Given these concerns, I recommend **rejection**. The paper would benefit significantly from a more focused experimental evaluation, a clearer and more intuitive presentation of the theoretical results, and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the idealized oracle assumption. The authors should also more thoroughly contextualize their work within the broader literature on clustering and entity resolution, referencing and comparing against state-of-the-art techniques beyond those relying on an oracle.
