Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner can ask an oracle same-cluster queries and also has access to side information in the form of a noisy pairwise similarity matrix W. Side information entries are drawn i.i.d. from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster). The main results are (i) information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of adaptive pairwise queries needed for exact recovery, even when k, f+, f− are known to the algorithm; and (ii) parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that, without knowing k, f+, f−, recover the true clustering with high probability using O((k log n)/H^2(f+||f−)) queries, where H^2 is squared Hellinger divergence, and O(n log n + (k^2 log n)/H^2) for a Las Vegas variant. The lower bound shows any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) expected queries to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1, up to constants, thereby matching the upper bounds within logarithmic factors.

The work generalizes and connects to the stochastic block model (SBM): when queries are disallowed, the setting reduces to community detection with general within/across distributions. Unlike much of the SBM literature, the paper allows unknown k, arbitrary cluster sizes (adversarial), and unknown f+, f−. It contrasts with prior interactive clustering and crowdsourcing heuristics (e.g., Awasthi et al., Wang et al., Gokhale et al.) that use side information but lack query-complexity guarantees, and with Ashtiani et al. on same-cluster queries under k-means/separation assumptions. It also relates to decision tree/query complexity and Fano-type bounds but develops new arguments to handle adaptivity.

Strengths:
- Quality/technical soundness: The lower bound develops a careful reduction of adaptive clustering to a multi-hypothesis testing instance for a lightly queried element, combining a query-budget averaging argument with Hellinger-based bounds on total variation (and a product-distribution argument). This avoids reliance on standard Fano inequalities that do not directly handle adaptivity. The upper bound algorithm is parameter-free, iteratively bootstraps estimates of f+, f− from small seeded clusters, and uses Hellinger divergence, with analysis via a Sanov-style large-deviations bound. The upper and lower bounds align in their dependence on k and H^2 up to log n factors, indicating near-optimality.
- Originality: The formulation of query complexity with arbitrary unknown side-information distributions and the corresponding information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive querying appear novel. The iterative-update, parameter-free design that infers “how much side information is enough” on the fly using Hellinger divergence is also original and practically appealing.
- Significance: The results explain the empirical gap between worst-case nk query bounds and the efficiency of heuristics that leverage side information, quantifying the role of distributional separability via H^2. The framework is relevant to ER/crowdsourcing and suggests future directions for active community detection and interactive learning with side information.
- Clarity of high-level exposition: The problem, contributions, intuition for the lower bound, and algorithmic phases are clearly laid out, and the SBM connection is well-motivated.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Clarity/notation issues: Several key formulas appear with ambiguous missing division symbols (e.g., O(k log n / H^2) is sometimes written as O(k log n H^2) or O(k / (log n H^2)). The same ambiguity occurs in the lower bound (k^2/H^2 vs k^2·H^2). Consistency between H (Hellinger) and H^2 is also occasionally unclear. The success probability is variously stated as 1 − 1/n and 1 − o(1/n); standard “with high probability” statements (1 − 1/poly(n)) would be preferable. References have missing years. These should be fixed for readability and to avoid misinterpretation.
- Assumptions: Upper bounds assume discrete f+, f− with fixed support size; the authors state extensions to larger finite supports scale linearly with support size, but this is not proved in the main text. The lower bound permits continuous distributions, but the upper bound does not address them beyond a remark; clarifying how discretization affects guarantees would strengthen the contribution.
- Dependence on side information access: The algorithm and analysis require O(n^2) time to process W. In ER-scale problems, W may be implicit/sparse. A discussion of computational/IO costs or adaptations for sparse W would improve practical relevance.
- Experimental evaluation: The empirical section uses one dataset (CORA), evaluates only internal strategy variants of the proposed method, and does not compare against prior heuristic baselines that also leverage side information and queries (e.g., Wang et al., Gokhale et al., Vesdapunt et al.). Experiments do not explore how query counts scale with k, H^2, imbalance, or noise model mismatch. Adding synthetic studies that vary H^2 and k and comparisons to existing methods would substantiate the theoretical claims.
- Constants and thresholds: The lower bound assumes H^2(f+||f−) ≤ 1/18, and constants in Lemma 1 yield a somewhat opaque 1/6 − O(1/√k) error floor; a simplified presentation with clearer constant regimes would aid understanding. For the algorithm, sensitivity to the initialization constant C and to early estimation errors could be further discussed.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: Strong on theory; the lower-bound technique for adaptive querying is technically interesting. Some proofs are deferred; constants/notation need cleanup. Empirics are limited.
- Clarity: Generally well-structured but marred by notation/typo issues; improve probability statements, divisions, and reference metadata.
- Originality: High; first near-tight query-complexity characterization with arbitrary unknown side-information distributions and parameter-free algorithms.
- Significance: Theoretical impact is clear; practical impact would be bolstered by broader experiments and discussion of computational aspects.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Near-tight info-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive queries with side information.
- Parameter-free algorithms matching bounds up to log factors, with clean Hellinger-based analysis.
- Generalization and unification with SBM; opens new questions on “queries vs. thresholds.”
- Addresses an important setting motivated by ER/crowdsourcing.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Ambiguities and typos in core formulas and notation; some assumptions not fully justified in the main text.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to prior methods.
- Upper bounds focus on discrete f+, f−; continuous-case handling left implicit.
- O(n^2) time may limit applicability; no discussion of scalability beyond n≈2k.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept). The theoretical contributions and near-tight characterization are substantial and of interest to the NIPS community. With improved clarity and stronger empirical comparisons, the paper would be even more compelling.

Reviewer confidence: High on theory; medium on empirical/practical aspects.
