

**Summary:**  
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether two points belong to the same cluster and has access to a similarity matrix (side information). The authors establish tight bounds on the number of queries needed for exact clustering, showing that side information reduces query complexity from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence between distributions of similarity scores within and between clusters. The work generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- without requiring knowledge of k or the distributions, addressing a significant gap in the literature where previous heuristic approaches lacked theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with complete proofs for both lower and upper bounds. The lower bound holds even when the algorithm knows f+, f-, and k, making it strong. The algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) are parameter-free and nearly match the lower bound within a logarithmic factor. The experimental results on the Cora dataset validate the theoretical findings, showing the greedy strategy achieves near-optimal performance with few queries.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized with clear exposition of the problem, theoretical results, and algorithms. The main contributions are clearly stated upfront, and the appendix provides detailed proofs for experts. The connection to SBM is well-explained, and the paper avoids unnecessary technical jargon.

*Originality:* This is the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information. It generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- without knowledge of k or the distributions, which previous work did not address. The information-theoretic lower bound framework is novel and could be useful for other interactive learning problems.

*Significance:* The results have theoretical significance in advancing understanding of query complexity in clustering. They also have practical significance for applications like entity resolution, where the algorithm can significantly reduce the number of expensive human queries needed. The paper establishes a tight theoretical foundation that could guide future algorithm design.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quality:* The lower bound has a logarithmic gap with the upper bound (the upper bound is O(k / (log n * H²)) while the lower bound is Ω(k / H²)). While this is common in theoretical work, closing the gap would strengthen the contribution.

*Clarity:* The iterative-update algorithm in Section 3 could be explained more intuitively. Notation for Hellinger divergence (H² vs H2) is inconsistent in places, which might confuse readers.

*Originality:* The connection to SBM is well-established, and the paper could better emphasize how their results specifically advance the SBM literature (e.g., how query complexity changes the recovery threshold).

*Significance:* The practical impact is limited to specific applications like entity resolution. More discussion of real-world implementation challenges would strengthen the practical significance.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**  

*Pro:*  
- First rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information  
- Tight bounds within logarithmic factor for query complexity  
- Parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f+, f-, or k  
- Strong theoretical foundation with complete proofs  
- Validated by experiments on real-world Cora dataset  
- Generalizes previous work on SBM  

*Con:*  
- Logarithmic gap between lower and upper bounds  
- Some notation inconsistencies  
- Limited discussion of practical implementation details  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding query complexity in clustering with side information. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and address an important gap in the literature. The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first rigorous theoretical analysis with tight bounds and parameter-free algorithms. While the logarithmic gap between bounds is a limitation, it is common in such theoretical work, and the results remain highly significant. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and includes strong experimental validation. Given the importance of the problem and the quality of the theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance.
