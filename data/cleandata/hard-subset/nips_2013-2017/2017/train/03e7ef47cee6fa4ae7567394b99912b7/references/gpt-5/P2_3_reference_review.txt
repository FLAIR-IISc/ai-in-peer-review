Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies clustering with access to a noiseless same-cluster oracle and a noisy pairwise similarity matrix (“side information”). The side information is generated from two unknown distributions f+ and f− depending on whether a pair is intra- or inter-cluster. The central question is the minimum number of pairwise oracle queries needed to exactly recover the clusters, when queries can be adaptive. The authors give:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds on the query complexity that hold even when k, f+, and f− are known to the algorithm. The bounds scale as Ω(min{n k, k^2 / H^2(f+‖f−)}) in expectation (and Ω(n + min{n k, k^2 / H^2}) for success with probability 1), where H denotes Hellinger distance. This quantifies the value of side information relative to the known Ω(nk) bound without side information.
- Efficient parameter-free algorithms (no knowledge of k, f+, f−) whose query complexities are O(min{n k, k log n / H^2}) for a Monte Carlo variant (whp exact recovery), and O(n log n + min{n k, k^2 log n / H^2}) for a Las Vegas variant (probability 1). The latter matches the lower bound up to a log n factor.

The model strictly generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM), which corresponds to Bernoulli f+, f−, and the results therefore inform the question “how do a few active same-cluster queries change SBM recovery thresholds?” The paper also connects to crowdsourced entity resolution heuristics, providing the first formal query-complexity guarantees that explain why side information can drastically reduce human labeling.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The lower bound technique is careful about adaptivity: it isolates an element queried few times and reduces to a k-hypothesis test; it controls total variation via Hellinger distance and uses product structure to compose divergences. The explicit constant-success lower bound and the Ω(n) term for probability-1 recovery are appropriate. The upper bounds are supported by a clear high-level analysis using Sanov’s theorem and Hellinger distance (a metric) for concentration; the iterative-update thresholding idea is well-motivated. The Las Vegas algorithm essentially matches the lower bound up to log n, which is a strong result.
- Assumptions and scope: The lower bound allows discrete or continuous f+, f−; the algorithms currently require discrete distributions with fixed finite support (though the authors argue the approach extends). The oracle is noiseless; noisy queries are deferred to a companion work. The adversarial cluster-size setting is ambitious and strengthens the results.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments on Cora are suggestive but limited: a single dataset, no comparison against prior interactive/crowdsourcing heuristics, and no ablation versus k or H^2. The “best/greedy/worst” strategies illustrate the algorithm’s behavior, but a stronger empirical section would benchmark against [40, 43, 18] and report query savings and accuracy across settings.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable with good intuition for why Hellinger distance is the right choice. The iterative-update algorithm is described at a high level; pseudocode would help. There are several typesetting glitches (e.g., “st Conference…”, footnote placement, ambiguous “k 2 H2” vs “k^2/H^2”), and some references lack years or complete details. The distinction between the Monte Carlo O(k log n / H^2) and Las Vegas O(k^2 log n / H^2) regimes should be made explicit earlier, together with where each is optimal relative to the lower bound.

Originality:
- To my knowledge, this is the first information-theoretic lower bound for adaptive clustering with side information that covers arbitrary unknown f+, f− and adversarial partitions, and the first parameter-free algorithm achieving near-optimal query complexity in this model. The use of Hellinger distance throughout, and the iterative-update scheme that estimates a usable separation proxy online, are novel and conceptually clean. The connection and conjecture about shifting SBM thresholds with queries is insightful.

Significance:
- The results give a principled explanation for why entity-resolution systems with side information can beat the Ω(nk) barrier, and they quantify the tradeoff between statistical separability (Hellinger) and query budget. This should inform both theory (active community detection, interactive learning lower bounds) and practice (design of crowdsourcing pipelines). The near-tight lower/upper bounds make this a solid contribution for NeurIPS.

Pros:
- Strong and general lower bounds handling adaptivity.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms; Las Vegas variant nearly matches the lower bound (log n gap).
- Conceptually unifying use of Hellinger divergence; careful proof ideas (Sanov + metric properties).
- Clear generalization beyond SBM; relevant to active learning/crowdsourcing.

Cons and suggestions:
- Empirics are thin and lack comparisons to established heuristics; add baselines and multiple datasets, and report scaling with k, H^2, and n.
- Clarify notation throughout; explicitly write k^2/H^2 and avoid ambiguous spacing; provide pseudocode.
- Discuss continuous-valued similarities for the algorithm more concretely (e.g., discretization schemes and impact on guarantees).
- Briefly address robustness to oracle noise in the main text, even if full results are in the companion work.
- Clean up typos, footnotes, and incomplete references.

Overall recommendation:
- I lean toward acceptance. The paper is technically strong, original, and significant for the theory of interactive clustering with side information, with clear potential impact on community detection and ER. Strengthening the empirical section and polishing presentation would further improve it, but the theoretical contributions on their own meet the NeurIPS bar.

Reviewer confidence: High on theory; moderate on empirical assessment due to limited experiments.
