This paper tackles a fundamental question in clustering: how much labeled data (or oracle queries) are *really* needed, especially when some side information is available? The authors rigorously analyze the query complexity of clustering with an oracle, motivated by applications like entity resolution where human-in-the-loop labeling is common. The core idea – that side information (a similarity matrix) can dramatically reduce query complexity – is compelling and represents a significant shift in thinking about this problem. The connection to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) is well-articulated and provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The theoretical investigation of query complexity *with* side information is genuinely novel. Existing work largely focuses on worst-case scenarios *without* side information, or heuristic approaches lacking theoretical guarantees. This paper fills a critical gap.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds. The development of a general framework for lower bounds is a significant accomplishment.
* **Generality:** The work makes minimal assumptions about the underlying distributions (f+ and f-) or the number of clusters (k), making the results broadly applicable. The connection to SBM further highlights the generality.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivation from entity resolution and active learning is compelling, and the potential for reducing labeling costs is substantial.
* **Well-Written (mostly):** The introduction clearly establishes the problem and motivation. The connection to SBM is particularly well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experimental results on the Cora dataset are encouraging, they are limited. It would be substantially stronger to demonstrate the algorithms’ performance on a wider range of datasets with varying characteristics (density, cluster sizes, noise levels in the similarity matrix). The choice of datasets and comparison to existing heuristics is insufficient to conclusively demonstrate the proposed method's practical superiority.
* **Algorithm Complexity:** The description of the iterative-update Monte Carlo algorithm (Phase 2) feels somewhat hand-wavy. More detail on the choice of the threshold parameters and convergence criteria would be helpful. The analysis, while present in the appendix, needs to be more tightly integrated into the main text for clarity.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation is occasionally dense, and some of the mathematical arguments could benefit from further explanation. The transition between different sections (e.g., from lower bound proof to algorithm description) sometimes feels abrupt.
* **Hellinger Divergence Focus:** While a natural choice, the exclusive focus on Hellinger divergence as a measure of separability might limit the applicability of the results. Exploring other divergence measures could be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamentally important question in clustering with significant theoretical depth.
* The novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical results are impressive.
* The generality of the model and results is a major strength.
* The connection to SBM provides a strong foundation and opens avenues for further research.
* Even with minor weaknesses, the potential impact of this work is high.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
* The algorithmic description could be more detailed and the analysis better integrated.
* The reliance on a single divergence measure (Hellinger) might limit the scope of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper with a truly novel contribution. The theoretical results are strong and the connection to SBM is well-established. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and the algorithmic description more detailed, the significance of the findings outweighs these weaknesses.  I lean *strongly* towards acceptance. The authors have identified a crucial gap in our understanding of clustering with side information, and this paper lays a solid foundation for future research in this area. The potential for practical impact, particularly in applications involving expensive labeling, is significant.  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding experimental validation and algorithm clarity in a revised version. I believe the authors have significantly advanced the state of the art, and this work is suitable for NIPS.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
