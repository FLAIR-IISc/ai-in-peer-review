Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering with pairwise same-cluster/different-cluster oracle queries when the algorithm also has access to “side information”: a noisy similarity matrix whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ if two items are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. Crucially, k, f+, and f− are unknown. The authors:
- Prove information-theoretic lower bounds on the expected number of (adaptive) queries needed to recover the ground-truth clustering, showing Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) for recovery with constant probability and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for recovery with probability 1 (for small Hellinger divergence H^2).
- Design parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that, using only W and the oracle, recover the clustering and match the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors. The algorithms iteratively learn empirical inter/intra distributions and use a Hellinger-distance-based membership test; they only query when the side information is insufficiently decisive. Runtime is O(n^2) to process W.
- Connect the model to stochastic block models (SBM) as a special case, and discuss how queries shift recovery thresholds.

Relation to prior work: Without side information, O(nk) queries are known necessary and sufficient. Heuristic ER/crowdsourcing methods leverage side information but lack guarantees. Prior theory on clustering with queries often targets k-means under metric/separation assumptions or non-adaptive querying; SBM works assume known parameters or balanced clusters. This paper provides (i) a general, parameter-free treatment of side information via unknown f+, f−; (ii) adaptive lower bounds in an interactive setting; and (iii) algorithms nearly matching those bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: cleanly characterizes how side information reduces query complexity, with matching lower/upper bounds up to logs.
- General model: unknown k, unknown f+, f−, arbitrary cluster sizes (upper bounds), and results stated in terms of Hellinger divergence, not tied to a specific distribution family.
- Technical novelty: an information-theoretic framework for adaptive-query lower bounds (not just non-interactive Fano), and a parameter-free iterative algorithm that learns the relevant divergence on the fly using Sanov-type arguments.
- Relevance: unifies practice in ER/crowdsourcing with rigorous guarantees, and suggests implications for SBM thresholds with active querying.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/consistency: There are apparent inconsistencies in the big-O expressions (e.g., Theorem 1’s O(k/(log n·H^2)) vs the lower bound’s Ω(k^2/H^2) and the Las Vegas O(n log n + min(nk, k^2 log n/H^2)). This likely reflects typos, but currently obscures the main message; the intended statement seems to be that upper bounds match Ω(k^2/H^2) up to log n.
- Scope of assumptions: Upper bounds assume discrete f+, f− with fixed support (continuous case discussed for lower bounds). Practical side information may be continuous or violate i.i.d. assumptions; robustness is discussed informally but not analyzed.
- Experimental validation: Only one dataset (cora), no comparison with strong baselines beyond synthetic “strategies,” and no ablation versus existing ER/crowdsourcing heuristics. The results illustrate the approach but do not thoroughly validate practical impact.
- Proof accessibility: Key proofs and the Las Vegas algorithm are deferred to the appendix; the main text sketches are dense, and some steps (e.g., “weak clusters” selection, constants in Lemma 1) could use clearer exposition.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong on the theoretical side. The lower bound technique for adaptive queries and the parameter-free upper bounds are carefully argued conceptually, though some details and constants are opaque in the main text. The experimental section is modest. Claims about matching lower/upper bounds are largely convincing, pending correction of the k vs k^2/log n inconsistencies. Overall: high quality theory, limited empirical validation.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured (intro, lower bound, algorithm), but several notational/typo issues hamper readability, especially on the exact dependence on k, log n, and H^2. The algorithm description is clear enough to implement at a high level; more guidance on constants and thresholds would help. Overall: medium clarity; improvable with careful editing.
- Originality: High. The combination of a general side-information model, adaptive lower bounds framework, and parameter-free algorithms appears novel relative to prior NIPS/SBM/ER literature.
- Significance: High. The results give a near-complete characterization (up to logs) of query complexity with side information and open interesting directions (active SBM thresholds, robustness, continuous distributions). Likely to influence both theory and the design of interactive clustering systems.

Questions and suggestions
- Please reconcile the apparent mismatch between Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 (k vs k^2 factors and log n placement). A consolidated theorem box summarizing all regimes would help.
- Can the upper bounds be extended to continuous f+, f− (e.g., via discretization with quantization error control)? Any finite-sample robustness guarantees under mild model misspecification?
- Provide comparisons to existing ER/crowdsourcing heuristics (e.g., [40, 42, 43]) on public datasets, measuring queries vs recovery accuracy.
- Clarify handling of highly imbalanced cluster sizes; the algorithm relies on “grown” clusters—what happens if many clusters remain small?
- Minor: avoid defining Membership as a negative value; using +Hellinger similarity might be clearer.

Pros
- Near-tight theory for interactive clustering with side information.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms using learned Hellinger tests.
- General model; clear connection to SBM and active learning.
- New lower-bound technique for adaptive queries.

Cons
- Inconsistent big-O dependences/typos; some proofs hard to parse.
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of baselines.
- Discrete-support assumption for upper bounds; robustness not quantified.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are significant and original, with strong potential impact. With clearer statements (fixing k/log factors), an expanded empirical section, and a brief robustness discussion, this would be a solid accept.
