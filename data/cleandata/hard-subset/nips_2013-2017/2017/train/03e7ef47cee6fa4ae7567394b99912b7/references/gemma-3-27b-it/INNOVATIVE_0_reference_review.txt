This paper tackles the query complexity of clustering with side information, motivated by practical applications like entity resolution where oracles (e.g., human annotators) are expensive. The core idea – that side information can dramatically reduce the number of queries needed – is compelling and, importantly, *novel*. While prior work has observed this empirically, this paper attempts a rigorous theoretical grounding, connecting the problem to stochastic block models (SBM) and deriving both lower and upper bounds on query complexity. This is a bold undertaking, especially given the challenging interactive setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of query complexity with side information is, to the best of my knowledge, a novel contribution. The connection to SBM is insightful, framing the problem within a well-studied context while simultaneously generalizing it significantly.
* **Significance:** If proven correct, the bounds established in this paper have significant implications for the design of efficient clustering algorithms leveraging side information. The potential for sub-linear query complexity is particularly exciting. The motivation via entity resolution is practical and well-articulated.
* **Technical Approach:** The attempt to establish information-theoretic lower bounds in an *interactive* setting is commendable. The development of a general framework for these bounds is a substantial effort. The algorithms proposed appear tailored to match the derived bounds.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a very thorough and detailed review of related work, spanning clustering, SBM, active learning, and crowdsourcing. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity & Clarity:** While the *ideas* behind the proofs are sketched, the actual proofs themselves, relegated to the appendix, are likely to be difficult to fully verify within a review timeframe. The presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key steps. More details in the main text about the technical subtleties would be helpful.
* **Practicality of Algorithms:** The described algorithms, while theoretically sound, may have practical limitations. The reliance on accurately estimating distributions *without prior knowledge* seems challenging in real-world scenarios. The constant factors hidden within the asymptotic notation could be large, rendering the algorithms less appealing in practice.
* **Experimental Validation:** While experiments are presented, they are limited in scope. More extensive evaluations on diverse datasets with varying levels of side information quality would strengthen the empirical support for the theoretical claims. Specifically, the impact of noisy side information deserves more attention.
* **Tightness of Bounds:** While the bounds are close, the logarithmic factor separating the upper and lower bounds suggests potential for further refinement. It’s not clear how easily this gap can be closed.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
*  The connection to SBM provides a valuable framework for understanding the problem.
*  The established bounds, even with a logarithmic gap, are a significant step forward.
*  The algorithmic proposals, while potentially impractical, demonstrate the achievability of the theoretical bounds.

**Con:**

*  The technical details of the proofs are dense and difficult to fully assess in a review.
*  The algorithms’ practical usability is questionable without further analysis.
*  The experimental validation is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some concerns about the technical complexity and practical limitations, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper tackles a challenging theoretical problem with a bold approach and delivers meaningful results. The paper’s value lies in opening up new avenues for research in interactive clustering and providing a rigorous framework for understanding the role of side information. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address reviewer concerns about clarity and provide additional experimental validation in a final version. A more detailed discussion of the algorithmic practicalities in the camera-ready version would also be beneficial. The slight imperfections in the validation and proof readability are acceptable given the boldness and novelty of the contribution.
