

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether two points belong to the same cluster, and also has access to a similarity matrix as side information. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient algorithms that nearly match these bounds, showing that the query complexity can be reduced from O(nk) to O(k / (log n H²(f₊‖f₋))) with side information.

## Summary
The paper establishes a theoretical framework for interactive clustering with side information, where the similarity matrix is generated from unknown distributions f₊ and f₋. The main contributions are information-theoretic lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms that nearly match these bounds. The connection to the stochastic block model is well-explained, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important problem in interactive clustering with side information, with clear practical relevance to entity resolution and crowdsourcing.
2. The information-theoretic lower bound is a significant contribution, providing a general framework for proving lower bounds in interactive settings.
3. The algorithms are parameter-free, which is valuable for practical applications where distribution parameters are unknown.
4. The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses
1. **Major Technical Error in Upper Bound**: Theorem 1 states the upper bound as O(k / (log n H²(f₊‖f₋))), but the lower bound in Theorem 2 is Ω(k² / H²(f₊‖f₋)). This means the upper bound and lower bound differ by a factor of k, not a logarithmic factor as claimed in the paper ("the lower bound therefore matches the query complexity upper bound within a logarithmic factor"). The upper bound should likely be O(k² / (log n H²(f₊‖f₋))) to match the lower bound. This is a fundamental error that undermines the main contribution of the paper.

2. **Probability of Error in Lemma 1**: In Lemma 1, the probability of error bound is written as "− 2k(1 + √(4Q/ak))² − 4Q/ak(k−1) − 2√aH(f₊‖f₋)". The negative sign at the beginning is incorrect as probability of error must be positive. This appears to be a typo, but it makes the proof difficult to follow and raises questions about the correctness of the bound.

3. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for Hellinger divergence. Sometimes they write H(p‖q) for the Hellinger divergence, and sometimes they write H²(p‖q) for the squared Hellinger divergence, but in the algorithm description they write H(p₊,x‖p₋,x)², which could be misinterpreted as [H(p₊,x‖p₋,x)]² (which is correct) but is confusing.

4. **Experimental Discrepancy**: The paper states "a popular bibliographic dataset cora consisting of 1879 nodes, 191 clusters" but later claims "the final number of vertices that we classify is 1812 with 124 clusters." This discrepancy is not explained.

5. **Formatting Errors**: The text contains several formatting errors, such as "discrete2" and "f₋3" in the definition of the similarity matrix. The reference to [3, 24] in the related work section is incorrectly formatted as "For SBM, parameter free algorithms are known relatively recently for constant number of linear sized clusters [3, 24]." It should be "For SBM, parameter free algorithms are known relatively recently for constant number of linear sized clusters [3, 24]." (with the citation numbers in the correct format).

6. **Ambiguity in Algorithm Description**: In Phase 1 of the algorithm, the paper states "query the oracle with it by choosing exactly one element from each of the clusters formed so far." This is ambiguous - does this mean making one query per existing cluster (which would be O(k) queries per new element) or just one query total? This ambiguity could lead to confusion in implementing the algorithm.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and has some strong theoretical contributions, the major technical error in the upper bound (which differs from the lower bound by a factor of k rather than a logarithmic factor) is a fundamental flaw. The probability of error bound in Lemma 1 having a negative sign is another serious error. These issues undermine the main contribution of the paper and make it difficult to trust the results.

The paper would benefit from a thorough re-examination of the upper bound and lower bound relationship, and a careful proofreading to fix the notation and formatting errors. Without these corrections, the paper does not make a convincing case for the nearly matching bounds it claims.

## List of Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The problem is well-motivated with practical applications in entity resolution and crowdsourcing.
- The information-theoretic lower bound is a significant theoretical contribution.
- The algorithms are parameter-free, which is valuable for practical applications.
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The upper bound and lower bound differ by a factor of k, not a logarithmic factor as claimed.
- The probability of error bound in Lemma 1 has a negative sign, which is incorrect.
- Multiple notational inconsistencies and formatting errors.
- Discrepancy in experimental cluster counts (191 vs 124) without explanation.
- Ambiguity in the algorithm description that could lead to implementation errors.
