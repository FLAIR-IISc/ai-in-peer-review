This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on scenarios where an oracle can provide pairwise cluster membership information. The authors establish a connection to entity resolution, motivating the problem with practical applications where obtaining labeled data is costly. They present a theoretical framework to analyze the query complexity, demonstrating a reduction in the number of queries required when side information (a similarity matrix) is available, compared to clustering without such information. The core contributions are information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on query complexity, along with algorithms that operate without prior knowledge of the underlying cluster structure or similarity distributions.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem at the intersection of theory and practice. The connection to entity resolution is clearly articulated, and the motivation for studying query complexity—reducing the cost of obtaining labeled data—is compelling. The authors correctly identify the gap between theoretical lower bounds (O(nk)) and the performance of heuristics used in practice, which prompted the investigation into the role of side information. The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) to accommodate arbitrary similarity distributions is a significant step, and the presented results provide valuable theoretical insights. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem of clustering with an oracle and side information is clearly defined, and the motivation is well-explained.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper employs information-theoretic techniques to derive rigorous lower bounds and upper bounds on query complexity.
*   **Generalization of SBM:** Extending the SBM to handle arbitrary similarity distributions represents a significant contribution.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require prior knowledge of cluster parameters (k, f+, f-), making them more applicable in real-world scenarios.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on the CORA dataset demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of the existing literature, including both theoretical and practical approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, while rigorous, are quite dense and challenging to follow without a strong background in information theory. Some key steps could benefit from further clarification and simplification. The extensive reliance on supplementary material adds to this challenge.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (CORA). While this demonstrates the algorithm’s performance in one setting, it would be beneficial to evaluate it on a broader range of datasets with varying characteristics.
*   **Constant Factors:** The upper bounds contain logarithmic factors (O(k log n)). While theoretically significant, the practical impact of these factors could be substantial, particularly for large datasets. A discussion of the constants involved and their impact on performance would be valuable.
*   **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper does not delve into the practical considerations of implementing the proposed algorithms. Details such as computational cost and memory requirements could be discussed. The runtime of O(n^2) may be limiting for large datasets.
*   **Connection to SBM - Discussion Depth:** While the connection to SBM is made, the discussion of how the query complexity affects the SBM recovery threshold (mentioned towards the end) feels somewhat cursory.  A more in-depth exploration of this connection would strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of a challenging problem.
*   The results provide valuable insights into the trade-offs between query complexity and side information.
*   The algorithms proposed are parameter-free and have strong theoretical guarantees.
*   The work has the potential to impact both the theoretical understanding and practical application of clustering algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complexity of the proofs and reliance on supplementary materials may make it difficult for a broad audience to fully grasp the technical details.
*   The limited experimental evaluation reduces confidence in the generalizability of the results.
*   The presence of logarithmic factors in the upper bounds could limit practical applicability.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the complexity of the proofs and limited experimental evaluation are weaknesses, the novel insights and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  **Simplify Proofs:**  Where possible, simplify the proofs and provide more intuitive explanations for key steps.
2.  **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Evaluate the algorithms on a wider range of datasets to demonstrate their robustness and generalizability.
3.  **Discuss Constant Factors:** Discuss the impact of the logarithmic factors in the upper bounds and explore potential techniques for mitigating them.
4.  **Clarify Implementation Considerations:** Provide a brief discussion of the practical considerations of implementing the proposed algorithms (computational cost, memory requirements).
5.  **Expand SBM Connection:** Elaborate on the relationship between the query complexity results and the recovery thresholds in the SBM, particularly in the sparse regime.



The work represents a significant step forward in understanding the theoretical limits of interactive clustering and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
