

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the algorithm has access to a noisy similarity matrix (side information) and must determine the minimum number of pairwise oracle queries (asking whether two points are in the same cluster) needed to recover the true clustering. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and propose efficient parameter-free algorithms that nearly match these bounds. The model generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) and provides a theoretical foundation for recent heuristic approaches in entity resolution and clustering with crowdsourcing. This work represents the first rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity for this problem, addressing a gap between theoretical lower bounds (Ω(nk)) and practical heuristic performance (which often uses far fewer queries).

## Strengths

**Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first information-theoretic lower bounds for clustering with side information. The separation between theoretical bounds (Ω(nk)) and practical heuristics (which use far fewer queries) is well-motivated by the entity resolution application, and the paper successfully explains this through the lens of side information. The work generalizes the SBM and opens up new directions for analyzing how query complexity affects recovery thresholds in community detection. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms is particularly significant for practical applications where distributions f+ and f- are unknown.

**Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and well-supported. The lower bound proof is novel and addresses the challenge of proving information-theoretic bounds in interactive settings (where standard techniques like Fano's inequality don't apply). The authors establish a tight connection between Hellinger divergence and query complexity, which is both theoretically elegant and practically useful. The algorithms are computationally efficient (O(n²) time) and parameter-free, matching the theoretical lower bound within logarithmic factors. The experimental results on the Cora dataset validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to algorithms. The main contributions are clearly articulated, and the connection to related work (especially SBM) is well-explained. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for experts to follow the proofs.

## Weaknesses

**Oracle Assumption:** The paper assumes a perfect oracle (no errors), which is a significant limitation for real-world applications. While the authors mention this as future work, it's a major gap since most practical applications (e.g., crowdsourcing) involve error-prone oracles. The companion work [34] on noisy queries is mentioned but not sufficiently integrated into the current paper's narrative.

**Experimental Analysis:** The experimental section is somewhat limited. While the results support the theoretical claims, the paper could provide more detailed analysis of algorithm convergence, the impact of different Hellinger divergence values, or comparisons with additional baselines. The discussion of the "gray region" in Phase 3 of the algorithm could be strengthened with more empirical evidence.

**Technical Accessibility:** The paper assumes a strong background in information theory (particularly Hellinger divergence and Sanov's theorem), which might limit accessibility for some readers. While the supplementary materials provide proofs, a brief explanation of why Hellinger divergence is used instead of KL divergence (as noted in the paper) would be helpful for broader readership.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Pro Acceptance:**
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity with side information, addressing a significant gap between theory and practice
- Strong theoretical results with tight bounds (within log n factor)
- Parameter-free algorithms with practical significance
- Clear connections to SBM and applications in entity resolution
- Well-written with clear organization and strong technical content

**Con Acceptance:**
- Oracle assumption (no errors) limits immediate practical applicability
- Experimental section could be more comprehensive
- Some technical depth might be challenging for readers without strong information theory background

## Recommendations

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a practically important problem. The results are novel, well-supported, and have clear implications for real-world applications like entity resolution. The minor weaknesses (oracle assumption, limited experimental analysis) are outweighed by the strength of the theoretical contributions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. In the revision, briefly discuss how the algorithm could be extended to handle noisy oracles (even if only as a future direction), citing the companion work [34] more prominently.
2. Expand the experimental section to include more detailed analysis of the convergence properties of the algorithms and the sensitivity to different Hellinger divergence values.
3. Add a brief explanation in the introduction of why Hellinger divergence is the right measure for this problem (rather than KL divergence), which would improve accessibility without adding much length.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. It meets NIPS standards for quality, originality, and significance, and would be a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
