## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with access to an oracle providing pairwise similarity information, and crucially, with the addition of "side information" in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors establish both information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms that nearly match those bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available compared to the classical Ω(nk) bound for clustering without side information. They connect their work to the stochastic block model (SBM) and present a novel iterative-update algorithm along with experimental validation on a bibliographic dataset.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental question at the intersection of clustering, active learning, and information theory. The demonstration of reduced query complexity with side information is a significant contribution, especially given the practical prevalence of heuristics that implicitly leverage such information (e.g., in entity resolution).  Connecting this to the well-studied SBM provides a strong theoretical foundation and opens avenues for further research.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds that are demonstrably tight (within a logarithmic factor) for the proposed algorithms.  The development of a general framework for proving these lower bounds is a key achievement.
* **Algorithm Design:** The proposed algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) are parameter-free, a significant practical advantage.  The iterative update strategy described for the Monte Carlo algorithm is a clever approach to handling the unknown distributions f+ and f-.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments, though on a single dataset, provide empirical support for the theoretical results, showcasing the benefits of the proposed approach and the robustness to approximate side information.
* **Clear Connection to Applications:**  The motivation through the entity resolution example is compelling and effectively illustrates the practical relevance of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Cora).  While the results are encouraging, a more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets with varying characteristics (e.g., different cluster structures, noise levels in the side information) would significantly strengthen the claims.
* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and technically demanding. While thoroughness is appreciated, the presentation could be streamlined to improve readability.  For example, some definitions are repeated unnecessarily, and the connections between different parts of the paper are not always immediately clear.
* **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical guarantees. A more in-depth discussion of the practical challenges in estimating the distributions f+ and f- from real-world data, and the sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to these estimations, would be valuable.
* **Logarithmic Factor:** While the upper bounds match the lower bounds within a logarithmic factor, a reduction of this factor would be a significant improvement. It would be good to discuss potential avenues for achieving that.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to point out the limitations of their analysis and identify areas for future work. This is a complete and polished piece of work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is challenging to read due to its density and technical complexity. However, it is well-organized and logically structured. Improving the clarity of explanations, especially for the iterative update algorithm, would be beneficial.
* **Originality:** The problem of clustering with side information and the associated query complexity analysis are novel. The connection to the SBM and the development of parameter-free algorithms demonstrate a creative combination of existing techniques.  The authors thoroughly review related work.
* **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact both theoretical understanding and practical applications of clustering. The reduction in query complexity is a demonstrable advance, and the parameter-free nature of the algorithms makes them particularly appealing for real-world use cases.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contribution with tight bounds.
* Novel and important problem formulation.
* Parameter-free algorithms with strong guarantees.
* Clear connection to practical applications.

**Con:**

* Limited experimental evaluation.
* Density and technical complexity may hinder accessibility.
* Discussion of practical challenges could be more in-depth.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are rigorous and the algorithms are well-designed. While the experimental evaluation is limited and the paper is challenging to read, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation. Specifically, I recommend adding results on additional datasets and providing a more detailed discussion of the algorithm’s sensitivity to the estimation of f+ and f-.
