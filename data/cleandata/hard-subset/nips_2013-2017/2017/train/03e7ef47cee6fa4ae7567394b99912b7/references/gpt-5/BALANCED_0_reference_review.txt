Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when provided with side information in the form of a noisy pairwise similarity matrix W. Each pairwise similarity is drawn from an unknown distribution f+ if the pair is in the same cluster and f− otherwise. The learner may adaptively query an oracle that reveals whether a pair is in the same cluster. The core questions are: how many queries are necessary and sufficient (as a function of n, k, and the discrepancy between f+ and f−) to exactly recover the clustering, and how does side information reduce query complexity compared to the nk baseline?

The authors present:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds (for randomized adaptive algorithms, even when f+, f−, and k are known) showing that when H2(f+‖f−) is small, one needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2(f+‖f−)}) queries (and Ω(n + min{…}) for success probability 1).
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not assume knowledge of f+, f−, or k. They use Hellinger divergence between empirical intra- and inter-cluster distributions to guide querying.
- An upper bound that is within logarithmic factors of the lower bound (claimed), with time O(n^2) (to read W).

This setting generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary (unknown) f+, f−, adversarial cluster sizes, and unknown k. It also connects to interactive/crowdsourced entity resolution heuristics, for which this work provides formal query-complexity guarantees. The work contrasts with prior interactive clustering (e.g., split/merge queries), k-means with separation assumptions, and active learning for SBM, and complements non-interactive SBM threshold results.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual contribution: side information can drastically reduce query complexity vs. the Ω(nk) barrier without side info.
- Strong and general lower bound technique for adaptive querying, using Hellinger divergence and a careful reduction to multi-hypothesis testing; applicable beyond clustering.
- Parameter-free algorithms that adaptively estimate the key separability via empirical Hellinger divergence and use an iterative-update scheme that may be useful more broadly.
- Near-optimal rates (up to log factors) claimed, bridging theory and the behavior of ER heuristics.
- Broader impact and connections to SBM recovery thresholds; opens interesting directions.
- Practical relevance and a small empirical demonstration.

Weaknesses:
- Inconsistency/possible typo in the stated upper bounds: The Monte Carlo query complexity is variously written as O(k log n / H2) and earlier as O(k / (log n H2)), whereas the lower bound is Ω(k^2 / H2). As written, O(k log n / H2) would contradict the lower bound for large k. The Las Vegas bound matches the lower bound up to log n (O(k^2 log n / H2)), suggesting the Monte Carlo bound should likely be O(k^2 log n / H2) as well. This must be clarified.
- Assumptions for upper bounds require discrete f+, f− with fixed support; extensions to continuous or large-support discrete distributions are not fully developed (lower bounds allow continuous). Practical guidance for discretization is not discussed.
- The perfect oracle assumption is strong for crowdsourcing; while a companion paper addresses noisy queries, a brief robustness discussion here would help.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: one real dataset, no comparisons to established ER baselines [40, 42, 43] or to heuristics analyzed in [18, 33], and no synthetic stress tests that systematically vary H2(f+‖f−), k, or cluster-size imbalance to validate the predicted scaling.
- Some clarity issues: heavy notation, several steps deferred to appendices; the rationale for Hellinger over KL is only sketched; membership definition and negative sign are initially unintuitive; constants (e.g., C) and stopping thresholds could be more concretely specified.
- Lower bound Lemma 1 handles equal-sized clusters or a specific construction; while the paper claims adversarial partitions are allowed, the path from the lemma to general instances relies on constructions that could be explained more clearly.

Quality:
- The technical core is strong and (subject to the noted inconsistency) appears sound. The lower bound framework for adaptive querying is a noteworthy contribution. The algorithmic analysis via Sanov’s theorem and Hellinger concentration is interesting and well-motivated. However, the mismatch in big-O expressions for the Monte Carlo bound must be resolved.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written for a theory paper, but some derivations are dense. The paper would benefit from a precise, consolidated statement of upper bounds (with all dependencies and success probabilities in one place), an explicit pseudocode listing, and a table summarizing regimes and complexities. Fixing the bound inconsistency is essential. A brief intuition figure for the iterative-update thresholding would help.

Originality:
- High. The combination of adaptive-query lower bounds in this setting, parameter-free algorithms for unknown f+, f−, k, and the use of Hellinger-driven thresholds appears novel. The connection to SBM with active queries is timely.

Significance:
- High theoretical significance: provides the first near-tight characterization of query complexity with side information for clustering in a very general model. Practical significance is promising but under-demonstrated empirically; stronger experiments would enhance impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- New, general lower bound framework for interactive clustering with side info.
- Parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal query complexity.
- Conceptual clarity on the role of side information (via Hellinger divergence) in reducing queries.
- Bridges gaps between ER heuristics and theory; broad relevance to active learning and SBM.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Apparent inconsistency in the stated Monte Carlo query bound relative to the lower bound.
- Limited empirical evaluation and no comparison to baselines.
- Upper bounds require discrete fixed-support side information; practical discretization not evaluated.
- Clarity gaps and reliance on appendices for key proofs.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Resolve and clearly state the correct query complexities for both algorithms, including all parameters (n, k, H2, success probability), and unify notation (H vs. H2).
- Add pseudocode with constants and thresholds, and a concise proof sketch for key lemmas in the main text.
- Discuss extensions to continuous or large-support discrete side information (e.g., via binning), and sensitivity to misspecification.
- Include experiments on synthetic data (SBM with varying k, H2, sparsity) to validate scaling, and comparisons to ER baselines and recent active SBM methods.
- Provide a brief robustness discussion to noisy oracles, even if only heuristic (and point to the companion work).

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers substantial theoretical contributions and a compelling perspective on interactive clustering with side information. Addressing the bound inconsistency and strengthening the empirical section would make it a strong accept.
