

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when an oracle can answer whether two points belong to the same cluster, with the additional side information of a similarity matrix. The authors claim to provide the first rigorous theoretical analysis of this problem, showing that with side information (a similarity matrix generated from unknown distributions f+ and f-), the query complexity can be reduced from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H^2(f+||f-))), where H is the Hellinger divergence. They present both Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-, and prove matching lower bounds.

The paper is well-organized and technically sound in its theoretical contributions. The authors develop a novel framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which is an interesting technical contribution. The connection to stochastic block models (SBM) is well-motivated, and the theoretical results are rigorously proven.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's practical relevance and the assumptions underlying the model. The authors assume an oracle that always returns correct answers, which is completely unrealistic for crowdsourcing applications where errors are the norm. While they acknowledge this limitation ("we do not consider the more complex scenarios of handling erroneous oracle answers"), they dismiss it as outside their main contribution. This is a major oversight, as the primary motivation for the problem (entity resolution with crowdsourcing) inherently involves error-prone oracles. The companion work [34] mentioned in the paper addresses this issue, but it's not properly integrated into the main contribution, making the current paper's results largely theoretical without practical applicability.

The experimental section is notably weak. The authors only test on one dataset (Cora) with limited strategies, and crucially, they don't compare their method against existing heuristic approaches that have been successfully used in entity resolution applications [40, 42, 43]. The results show that their greedy strategy performs close to the "best" strategy, but this is expected since they're using the same side information. Without comparison to established methods, it's impossible to gauge whether the claimed query complexity reduction actually translates to practical benefits.

The paper's main claim of being "the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information" appears questionable. Several related works [5, 19, 22, 40] have studied similar interactive clustering frameworks, though perhaps not with the exact theoretical guarantees claimed here. The authors cite these works but don't adequately distinguish their approach from prior efforts.

While the theoretical results are mathematically interesting, I'm skeptical about their practical significance. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms is appealing, but the requirement for the side information to be generated from unknown distributions f+ and f- is highly artificial. In practice, these distributions are rarely known, and the algorithms would need to estimate them from limited data, which isn't addressed in the paper.

The lower bound proof is technically sound but relies on assumptions that don't match real-world scenarios. For instance, Lemma 1 assumes equally sized clusters, which is uncommon in practice. The paper also doesn't address how the theoretical guarantees translate to real-world applications with noisy data.

The paper's significance is limited by these issues. While the theoretical framework for lower bounds could be useful for future work, the practical contribution is minimal given the unrealistic assumptions. The claimed query complexity reduction is impressive on paper, but without validation against realistic scenarios and comparison to existing methods, it's difficult to assess its value.

In summary, while the paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution to the query complexity of clustering with side information, its practical relevance is severely undermined by unrealistic assumptions and a lack of meaningful empirical validation. The authors' focus on theoretical elegance without sufficient consideration of practical constraints and comparisons to existing approaches is a significant weakness.

I would recommend rejection due to the lack of practical significance and insufficient empirical validation. The paper presents an interesting theoretical result but fails to demonstrate how this result translates to meaningful improvements in real-world clustering applications, which is the primary motivation for the work. The field would benefit more from papers that bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical applications, rather than those that focus solely on theoretical aspects with minimal connection to real-world use cases.
