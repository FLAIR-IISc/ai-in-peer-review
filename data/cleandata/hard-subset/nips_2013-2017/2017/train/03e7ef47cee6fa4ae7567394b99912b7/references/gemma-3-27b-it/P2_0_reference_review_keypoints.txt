- Summary: Analyzes query complexity of clustering with an oracle plus similarity matrix, giving lower/upper bounds and near-optimal Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms parameterized by Hellinger divergence; connects to SBM.

- Strong theoretical foundation: Provides rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds and proofs.

- Novelty: Formally studies clustering with side information, filling a gap between heuristics and theory.

- Practical relevance: Motivated by entity resolution and crowdsourcing; aims to reduce expensive oracle queries.

- Parameter-free algorithms: Proposed methods do not require knowledge of cluster distributions or k.

- Clear presentation (mostly): Introduction and contributions are well-motivated and clearly stated.

- Experimental validation: Includes real-world Cora dataset experiments supporting theoretical claims.

- Technical density: Paper is very dense; proofs may be hard to follow for readers without information-theory background.

- Algorithm presentation: Monte Carlo algorithm description is high-level; needs pseudocode and clearer rationale for design choices (e.g., 'dC').

- Experimental scope: Evaluation is limited to one dataset and lacks comparisons to existing heuristics.

- Minor clarity issues: Some sentences and explanations (e.g., Phase 3 thresholds) are convoluted and need simplification.

- SBM connection could be tighter: Implications of general f+ and f- vs SBM assumptions are not fully explored.

- Quality (qualitative): Strong; technically sound and comprehensive problem formulation.

- Clarity (qualitative): Good overall but technical details are challenging and need more intuition.

- Originality (qualitative): Excellent; novel problem formulation and theoretical analysis.

- Significance (qualitative): High; relevant to clustering, active learning, information theory, and entity resolution.

- Pro acceptance: Rigorous analysis, novel formulation, practical relevance, parameter-free algorithms, connection to SBM.

- Con acceptance: High technical density, limited experiments, and insufficient algorithmic detail.

- Overall assessment: Strong paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions.

- Recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions.

- Suggested revisions: Add intuitive explanations, provide Monte Carlo pseudocode/step-by-step details, expand experiments and heuristic comparisons, fix minor clarity issues, and tighten SBM discussion.
