Summary
The paper studies interactive clustering with an oracle that answers same-cluster queries, in the presence of side information given as a similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ or f− depending on whether a pair belongs to the same cluster or not. The number of clusters k and the distributions f+, f− are unknown. The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds on the expected number of adaptive pairwise queries needed for exact recovery. The bounds scale as Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}), up to constants (for H^2 ≤ 1/18), and Ω(n + …) for success probability 1.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that achieve query complexities O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}), respectively, and thus match the lower bounds up to a log n factor.
- An iterative-update design that estimates the separation between f+ and f− via empirical Hellinger divergence and uses it to decide when side information is reliable enough to avoid queries.
- A brief experiment on the Cora ER dataset showing reduced queries for a greedy strategy guided by the learned distributions.

Relation to prior work: The model generalizes SBM (f+, f− Bernoulli) and connects to active learning/crowdsourcing heuristics that leverage side information. The paper positions itself as the first to give parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal query complexity for arbitrary (unknown) f+, f−, and to provide an information-theoretic treatment of adaptively chosen queries.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-motivated question (query complexity gap between theory and practice when side info is available).
- A nontrivial lower-bound framework for adaptive querying; the reduction to multi-hypothesis testing and the Hellinger-based TV control are technically interesting.
- Near-matching upper and lower bounds (up to log n), with parameter-free algorithms that do not assume k or f+, f−.
- The use of Hellinger distance (a metric) in both bounds and analysis is coherent and may be more robust than KL in this setting.
- Clear connections to SBM and ER; potential to spur follow-up work on active querying thresholds in SBM.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and correctness issues:
  - Lemma 1 appears misstated: the “probability of error is at least” a negative expression; the proof bounds probability of correct assignment from above, so the lemma likely needs a “1 − …” form. This needs correction.
  - Inconsistencies in query complexity notation: O(k/(log n H^2)) vs O(k log n/H^2). Please standardize (likely the latter).
  - Success probabilities are inconsistently stated (e.g., 1 − 1/n vs 1 − o(1/n)).
  - Numerous typos, formatting glitches, and incomplete references hinder readability.
- Assumptions vs practicality:
  - The side-information model assumes i.i.d. pairwise draws conditioned on the partition, which is strong and often violated in ER/graphs.
  - The algorithm and analysis assume discrete distributions with fixed support; the claim that results extend to continuous/large support is not substantiated in detail (what discretization, what impact on H^2 and rates?).
  - Exact oracle answers only; the noisy-oracle case is deferred to a companion paper, limiting practical relevance here.
- Algorithmic details:
  - The iterative-update procedure and thresholds (Phases 2/3) are intricate; pseudo-code and tighter guarantees in the main text would help. Several steps rely on extended Sanov’s theorem; however, the deviation bounds and constants are only sketched.
  - Time and memory O(n^2) due to dense W may be prohibitive in ER-scale problems; this limitation is acknowledged but not addressed.
- Evaluation:
  - Experiments are minimal (one dataset), with no comparisons to established heuristics ([40, 43], etc.), no synthetic tests to validate scaling with H^2 and k, and no robustness to model misspecification (non-i.i.d. W, continuous/noisy distributions).
  - The “best/worst/greedy” query strategies are somewhat artificial; “best” assumes identifying the correct cluster with one query, which is unrealistic.
- Scope of lower bounds:
  - The main lower bound is stated for H^2 ≤ 1/18; constants are loose; the SBM sparse-threshold discussion remains a conjecture. More precise/tighter statements would strengthen the contribution.

Quality
- The theoretical direction is promising, but the current presentation has correctness/clarity issues (e.g., Lemma 1 sign, notational inconsistencies) and omits important technical details from the main paper.
- The experimental evidence is too limited to substantiate practical impact or robustness.

Clarity
- The paper needs careful proofreading, consistent notation for Hellinger divergence (squared vs not), and a consolidated, unambiguous statement of the main theorems with assumptions.
- Provide pseudo-code and a clearer description of the thresholds and stopping conditions.

Originality
- The lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive clustering with side information, and the parameter-free near-optimal algorithms based on Hellinger-guided thresholds, appear novel.

Significance
- Potentially significant theoretically, given the near-tight bounds and generality in f+, f−. Practical significance is under-demonstrated due to limited experiments and strong modeling assumptions.

Questions for authors
1) Please correct and restate Lemma 1 precisely (probability of correct vs error) and unify the query complexity expressions across the paper.
2) How exactly do the results extend to continuous/large-support distributions? What discretization scheme preserves the H^2 scaling and guarantees?
3) Can you provide pseudo-code and a self-contained proof sketch for the convergence and correctness of the iterative-update thresholds in the main text?
4) How sensitive is the algorithm to violations of the i.i.d. W assumption? Any robustness guarantees?
5) Can you add synthetic experiments varying H^2 and k to validate the predicted 1/H^2 and k, k^2 regimes, and comparisons to standard heuristics?

Pros
- Strong, near-matching theory for an important question.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient in terms of W.
- Clear link to SBM and active learning.

Cons
- Clarity/correctness issues in statements.
- Strong assumptions on W; no noisy-oracle treatment here.
- Very limited empirical validation; no baseline comparisons.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The theory is promising, but the paper needs substantial polishing for correctness and clarity, and stronger empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage a revision addressing the issues above. Confidence: High.
