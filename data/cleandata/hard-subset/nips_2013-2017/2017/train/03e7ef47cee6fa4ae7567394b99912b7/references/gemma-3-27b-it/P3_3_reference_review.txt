## NIPS 2017 Review: Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the query complexity of clustering when auxiliary information (a similarity matrix) is available. The work establishes a clear separation in complexity between clustering with and without side information, provides tight information-theoretic lower bounds, and designs efficient algorithms that nearly match those bounds. While the presentation is dense and could benefit from improved clarity in certain sections, the technical depth and theoretical importance of the results are high. I believe this paper is a strong submission suitable for acceptance to NIPS.

**Summary of Ideas:**

The paper investigates the fundamental question of how much interaction (oracle queries) is necessary to cluster a set of points when an oracle can confirm cluster membership, and when a similarity matrix between all point pairs (side information) is also available. It builds upon the well-studied problem of clustering with an oracle (which has a known lower bound of Ω(nk)), and aims to characterize the benefit provided by the side information. The authors demonstrate that side information can drastically reduce the query complexity, potentially to sublinear levels, and rigorously quantify this reduction.  A core concept is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions governing pairwise similarities within and between clusters, which serves as a key parameter influencing the achievable query complexity. The work also establishes a connection to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), demonstrating that this problem is a generalization of it, and generalizes existing results to arbitrary unknown distributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is deeply rooted in information theory.  The derivation of the lower bounds is particularly impressive, tackling the challenges of adaptivity and the continuous nature of the side information. The use of a generalized Fano's inequality is commendable.
* **Tight Bounds:** The derived upper and lower bounds match (within a logarithmic factor), which is a hallmark of strong theoretical results. This demonstrates a fundamental understanding of the problem.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivating application of entity resolution is well-chosen and highlights the practical importance of reducing query complexity in interactive data analysis. The experimental results on the Cora dataset, while limited, support the theoretical findings.
* **Generality:** The work considers very general settings: unknown number of clusters, arbitrary distributions governing the similarity matrix, and no assumptions on cluster size distributions. This broad applicability significantly enhances the paper's impact.
* **Connection to SBM:** Establishing the connection to the Stochastic Block Model and highlighting the generalization broadens the relevance of the work and positions it within a well-established research area.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require knowledge of the underlying distributions or the number of clusters is a significant achievement.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Readability:** The paper is dense and requires considerable effort to follow. The notation is heavy, and the presentation of some arguments could be more intuitive. For example, the explanation of the iterative update algorithm could be improved.  More intermediate steps and explanations would enhance understanding.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are limited to a single dataset (Cora) and primarily serve to demonstrate the algorithm's feasibility.  A more comprehensive evaluation with diverse datasets and comparison to existing heuristics (e.g., those cited in the introduction) would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Constant Factors:** While the bounds are tight in terms of asymptotic complexity, the presence of potentially large constant factors (especially in the Hellinger divergence term) could limit practical performance in certain scenarios. A discussion of these factors would be helpful.
* **Assumption of Correct Oracle:** While acknowledged, the impact of a potentially noisy oracle is not explored in detail.  The authors mention a companion work addressing this, but a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to oracle errors would be valuable.
* **Length and Detail:** The paper is very long, and some details are relegated to the appendix. While understandable, it adds to the difficulty of grasping the core ideas.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a rigorous and original theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem in clustering.
* The results are significant and have the potential to impact both theoretical and practical research in data analysis.
* The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature and position their work effectively within the existing body of knowledge.
* The connection to SBM and the generalization of previous results are noteworthy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and lack of clarity may make it inaccessible to a broader audience.
* The limited experimental evaluation weakens the empirical validation of the theoretical findings.




**Overall Recommendation:** **Accept.**

Despite the weaknesses in clarity and experimental evaluation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and justify acceptance to NIPS. The rigorous analysis, tight bounds, and practical relevance make it a valuable addition to the field.  I recommend that the authors address the clarity issues and consider expanding the experimental evaluation in a revised version.
