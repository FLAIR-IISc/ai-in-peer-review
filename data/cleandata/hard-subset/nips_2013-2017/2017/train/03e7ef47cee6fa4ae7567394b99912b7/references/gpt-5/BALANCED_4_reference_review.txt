Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, formalizing a setting motivated by crowdsourced entity resolution and active clustering. The learner observes a noisy similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn from f+ for within-cluster pairs and from f− for cross-cluster pairs (f+, f− unknown), and may adaptively query a noiseless oracle that reveals whether a pair is in the same cluster. The central question is: how many pairwise queries are necessary and sufficient to exactly recover the clustering?

The authors provide:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds showing any (possibly randomized) algorithm must use Ω(min{n k, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) queries to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{n k, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1, even when k, f+, f− are known.
- Two parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that do not know k, f+, f−: a Monte Carlo algorithm with query complexity O(min{n k, (k log n)/H^2}) and success probability 1 − o(1/n), and a Las Vegas algorithm with query complexity O(n log n + min{n k, (k^2 log n)/H^2}). Up to log n factors, these match the lower bounds.
- An iterative-update technique that estimates the “intra” and “inter” distributions from W to decide when side information suffices versus when additional queries are needed, using Hellinger divergence as the statistical distance.
- A small empirical study on CORA suggesting the greedy strategy is close to an oracle strategy and is robust to modest model misspecification.

This work generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM): f+, f− are arbitrary distributions (not necessarily Bernoulli), k is unknown and cluster sizes are adversarial. It connects to recent NeurIPS/FOCS/COLT work on exact/approximate recovery in SBM (e.g., Abbe et al.; Hajek-Wu-Xu) and interactive clustering/crowdsourcing (e.g., Gokhale et al.; Vesdapunt et al.), and complements Ashtiani et al. (NIPS’16) by focusing on information-theoretic rather than computational lower bounds and allowing arbitrary f+, f− with parameter-free algorithms.

Quality
- The lower bound technique addresses the adaptivity challenge by isolating “lightly queried” elements and casting assignment as k-ary hypothesis testing under interactive information. The proof uses product structure and Hellinger divergence to control total variation. While constants are not optimized, the scaling is compelling and matches the upper bounds up to log n.
- The algorithms are conceptually clean, computationally efficient (O(n^2) time dominated by reading W), and parameter-free. The iterative estimation of p+, p− and the thresholding via Hellinger divergence is technically interesting and practically motivated.
- Some technical presentation issues need attention: there are several notational inconsistencies and what appear to be typographical errors (e.g., O(k/log n H^2) vs O((k log n)/H^2); mixing Hellinger distance vs squared divergence vs KL; an inequality TV ≤ sqrt(H(·||·)) which seems misstated if H denotes Hellinger distance; the definition of β in Lemma 1’s proof). I expect the supplementary to contain the precise arguments, but the main text should be corrected for clarity and correctness of stated inequalities.
- Assumptions are clearly stated: oracle is noiseless; W has independent entries conditioned on the clustering; distributions are discrete with fixed support for the algorithms (lower bounds allow continuous). These are standard in theoretical models but should be discussed as limitations for some applications.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-structured, with a clear problem statement, contributions, and connection to prior work. However, several equations and thresholds would benefit from clearer parentheses and consistent notation for Hellinger distance (H vs H^2), and a brief lemma summarizing the relations among Hellinger, total variation, and KL used in proofs.
- The algorithm description is understandable, but Phase 2/3 thresholds should be re-expressed with explicit constants and unambiguous dependence on H^2(p+||p−). A small pseudocode block would help.

Originality
- The formulation of query complexity with side information for arbitrary unknown f+, f− and unknown k, the interactive lower bound framework, and the parameter-free algorithms leveraging iterative Hellinger-based estimation appear novel and address a real gap between practice and existing theory.
- The explicit characterization of how side information reduces queries via H^2(f+||f−) is insightful and links to SBM thresholds.

Significance
- The results provide near-tight (up to log n) characterization of query complexity with side information and may influence both theory (interactive information bounds; active inference in graphical models) and practice (design of crowdsourcing/ER systems with principled query budgets).
- The empirical section is limited (single dataset, no direct comparisons to strong baselines), but this is primarily a theory paper. The demonstrated robustness to approximate f+, f− is encouraging.

Pros for acceptance
- Near-matching information-theoretic lower and upper bounds; clear dependence on Hellinger divergence.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms that work without knowing k, f+, f−.
- Conceptual connection that generalizes SBM and suggests how queries shift recovery thresholds.
- Addresses an important and timely problem motivated by practical systems.

Cons against acceptance
- Presentation/notation issues and some apparent typos in key statements and inequalities in the main text.
- Algorithmic assumptions (discrete finite supports) and independence in W; limited discussion on extension to continuous f+/f− for algorithms.
- Experimental evaluation is minimal; lacks comparisons to prominent heuristics [40, 43] or to parameter-aware methods, and no runtime/memory analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all notation inconsistencies: uniformly distinguish Hellinger distance H from H^2; ensure inequalities (e.g., TV ≤ H) are correctly stated and cited; clarify Theorem 1’s query complexity and remove ambiguous O(k/log n H^2)-style expressions.
- Provide a short lemma in the main text summarizing distance relationships used (Hellinger-TV-KL) with precise constants and citations (e.g., Sason & Verdú).
- Clarify the discrete-support assumption for algorithms and discuss how to extend Membership and the analysis to continuous distributions (e.g., via binning or kernel density estimates) and the impact on constants.
- Expand experiments: compare to representative heuristics (e.g., Corleone/ER heuristics; triangle-query methods; baseline that asks O(nk) queries), on multiple datasets; report query budgets vs F1 and exact-recovery rates; include sensitivity to k, cluster size imbalance, and to H^2.
- Provide ablations for the iterative-update scheme (e.g., using KL instead of Hellinger) and report runtime and memory costs for W.
- Discuss handling of noisy oracles (even briefly), and how the companion paper’s results might integrate.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper tackles an important and understudied question with strong theoretical contributions and near-tight bounds, along with practical, parameter-free algorithms. With improved clarity and a slightly stronger empirical section, it would be a solid addition to NeurIPS.
