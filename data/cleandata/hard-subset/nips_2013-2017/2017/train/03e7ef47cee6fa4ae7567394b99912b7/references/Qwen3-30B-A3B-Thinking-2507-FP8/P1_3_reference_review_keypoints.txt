- Summary: Paper studies query complexity for clustering with side information (similarity matrix) and oracle queries, giving near-matching information-theoretic lower and upper bounds.  
- Main theoretical result: Establishes strong lower bounds (Theorem 2) that hold for randomized algorithms even with full knowledge of f+, f-, and k.  
- Query complexity improvement: Shows reduction from O(nk) (no side info) to roughly O(k / (log n · H^2(f+||f-))) using Hellinger divergence.  
- Generalization of SBM: Extends the stochastic block model to arbitrary f+ and f-, providing a more realistic model for practice.  
- Algorithmic contribution: Presents parameter-free, iterative-update Monte Carlo algorithms that estimate when to trust side information versus querying.  
- Handling unknown distributions: Uses side information to estimate Hellinger divergence and adaptively decide query behavior without prior knowledge of f+, f-, or k.  
- Practical relevance: Connects results to entity resolution and crowdsourcing, with experiments on the Cora dataset showing strong empirical performance and query savings.  
- Rigorous analysis: Proofs (e.g., Lemma 1, Theorem 2) are technically sound and limitations are acknowledged.  
- Weakness — practical constants: Paper lacks discussion of constant factors in the asymptotic bound, which may affect practical significance.  
- Weakness — sparse SBM: Mentions sparse SBM as future work but does not analyze how results extend to sparse regimes relevant in practice.  
- Weakness — perfect oracle assumption: Assumes noiseless oracle answers; effects of noisy queries are deferred to a companion paper and not analyzed here.  
- Weakness — experimental scope: Experiments are limited to a single dataset (Cora) and lack comparisons to recent heuristics for entity resolution.  
- Overall assessment: Strong theoretical and practical contribution; technically sound and well-written, with weaknesses deemed minor relative to contributions.  
- Recommendation: Accept — merits publication due to tight bounds, parameter-free algorithms, and clear practical relevance.
