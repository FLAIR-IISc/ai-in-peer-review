### **Summary**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with pairwise oracle queries when the algorithm has access to "side information" in the form of a noisy similarity matrix. The problem is motivated by the discrepancy between the well-known Ω(nk) worst-case lower bound for clustering *n* items into *k* clusters and the much better empirical performance of heuristic algorithms used in applications like entity resolution. The authors hypothesize that these heuristics implicitly leverage a similarity matrix, which they formalize as side information.

The core model assumes that for any pair of points (i, j), the corresponding entry *w_ij* in the similarity matrix is drawn from a distribution *f+* if i and j are in the same cluster, and from *f-* otherwise. Crucially, the number of clusters *k* and the distributions *f+* and *f-* are all unknown to the algorithm. This model generalizes the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM).

The main contributions are twofold:
1.  An information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k² / H²(f+||f-)}), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence. This bound quantifies the fundamental trade-off between the number of oracle queries and the distinguishability of the side information distributions.
2.  Two efficient, parameter-free algorithms (one Monte Carlo, one Las Vegas) whose query complexities nearly match the lower bound, differing only by a logarithmic factor. A key feature is their ability to operate without knowledge of *k*, *f+*, or *f-*.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs. The lower bound proof is a notable contribution, as it successfully navigates the challenges of an interactive setting where an adaptive algorithm can gain deterministic information from queries. Standard information-theoretic bounds like Fano's inequality are not directly applicable, and the authors develop a clever argument by identifying a subset of elements that are queried infrequently and analyzing the multi-hypothesis testing problem for placing these elements. The upper bound analysis, which uses tools from large deviation theory (Sanov's Theorem) in conjunction with the Hellinger metric, also appears sound and well-suited to the problem. The work is a complete piece, presenting both lower bounds and nearly optimal algorithms.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by connecting a practical observation (good performance of heuristics) to a theoretical question. The relationship to prior work, particularly SBM and active clustering, is clearly articulated. The main theorems are stated precisely, and the high-level ideas behind the proofs and algorithms are conveyed. However, due to space constraints, the description of the Monte Carlo algorithm in Section 3 is dense, and the detailed proofs are relegated to the appendix. While standard practice, this makes a full assessment of the technical details more challenging from the main text alone. The experimental section is also quite brief.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first paper to formally model and analyze the query complexity of active clustering with this type of generative side information. The problem formulation itself, which provides a theoretical lens for the success of many practical systems, is a key contribution. The proof technique for the interactive lower bound is novel and could be of independent interest for other problems in interactive learning. The design of parameter-free algorithms that adaptively learn the necessary statistics (*f+*, *f-*) to achieve near-optimal performance is a significant algorithmic contribution. While related to SBM, the generalization to arbitrary unknown distributions and the focus on query complexity clearly distinguishes this work from the existing SBM literature.

**Significance:** The results are significant for both theoretical and practical communities. For theoreticians, it provides a new, general framework for analyzing interactive learning and opens up questions about the interplay between active queries and passive observation in other statistical tasks. The proposed lower-bound technique could prove useful in other adaptive settings. For practitioners in areas like entity resolution and crowdsourcing, the paper provides a formal justification for using similarity scores to guide querying and offers insights into how the quality of these scores (captured by H²(f+||f-)) impacts the cost of achieving accurate results. The development of parameter-free algorithms is particularly important, as model parameters are rarely known in real-world applications.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is limited. While it provides a proof-of-concept on the Cora dataset, a more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the paper. Comparing the proposed algorithm against some of the heuristic methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., from [40, 42, 43]) would more directly validate the paper's central claim that its formal approach captures and improves upon what is done in practice.

2.  **Gap in Bounds:** There remains a logarithmic factor between the upper and lower bounds. While this is a small gap, it would be valuable to discuss whether this is an artifact of the analysis (e.g., from concentration inequalities) or if it might be fundamental.

3.  **Lower Bound Condition:** The lower bound in Theorem 2 requires H²(f+||f-) ≤ 1/18. It would be helpful to briefly discuss the role of this constant and whether the bound can be extended to cases where the distributions are further apart, or if the problem becomes trivial in that regime.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper formalizes an important and previously unstudied problem that bridges the gap between the theory and practice of active clustering.
*   **Strong Theoretical Contributions:** It provides a novel information-theoretic lower bound for an interactive setting and nearly matching, computationally efficient upper bounds.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require knowledge of the underlying model parameters (*k*, *f+*, *f-*), making them highly relevant for practical applications.
*   **Significant Implications:** The results provide fundamental insights into the value of side information in interactive learning and have clear connections to important areas like entity resolution and community detection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is brief and does not include comparisons to existing state-of-the-art heuristic methods.
*   **Clarity of Algorithm:** The algorithm's description in the main paper is dense, and a full understanding requires reading the appendix.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its originality and theoretical significance, are compelling. It addresses a fundamental problem with novel techniques and provides strong results. The weaknesses are minor in comparison and primarily relate to the scope of the experimental validation.
