Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm can ask same-cluster queries to an oracle and also has access to side information: a noisy similarity matrix W in which each pairwise weight is drawn from f+ if the pair is co-clustered and from f− otherwise. The number of clusters k, as well as f+ and f−, are unknown. The main results are:

- Lower bounds: An information-theoretic framework for interactive settings with adaptive queries, yielding that any algorithm (even knowing k, f+, f−) needs Ω(min{n k, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) expected queries to achieve small error, and Ω(n + min{n k, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1 (under H^2 ≤ 1/18). This generalizes and strengthens classical Ω(nk) bounds without side info.

- Upper bounds: Two efficient, parameter-free algorithms. A Monte Carlo algorithm achieves O(min{n k, (k log n)/H^2}) queries with success probability 1−o(1/n). A Las Vegas variant succeeds with probability 1 and uses O(n log n + min{n k, (k log n)/H^2}) queries. Both use an iterative-update scheme that estimates the intra/inter distributions from W, uses Hellinger divergence to set a reliability threshold on cluster size, and relies on side information whenever the current cluster is “grown” enough; otherwise the algorithm issues a small number of queries focused on ambiguous (“gray zone”) points.

The work connects to and significantly generalizes stochastic block models (SBM), where f+ and f− are Bernoulli(p), Bernoulli(q). Unlike much SBM work, this paper allows arbitrary and unknown f+, f−, unknown k, and adversarial cluster sizes. It also tackles adaptive queries, where classical Fano-type arguments are insufficient. Compared to prior interactive clustering and crowdsourced ER heuristics [Awasthi et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2012; Vesdapunt et al. 2014; Firmani et al. 2016], the paper provides the first near-tight query complexity characterization leveraging side information. It is also distinct from Ashtiani et al. (NIPS’16), which studies k-means under metric/separation assumptions with computational lower bounds and parameter knowledge; here the bounds are information-theoretic, distributions are unknown, and no metric or separation is assumed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bound technique is carefully constructed for the adaptive setting: select an element with few queries, identify many “weak” clusters that are under-queried and seldom chosen via side information, and bound success probability via total variation controlled by the Hellinger divergence between product distributions. Assumptions and constants (e.g., H^2 ≤ 1/18) are clearly stated, with equal-size and crafted instances for the reduction. For upper bounds, the iterative estimation of p+ and p− and the use of Hellinger (a metric) allow clean concentration and Sanov-type arguments; the gray-zone analysis bounds the number of necessary queries.

- Completeness: Proof sketches are given; full proofs are in appendices. The analysis addresses unknown k and unknown f+ and f−, with computational cost O(n^2) to read W. The Monte Carlo and Las Vegas guarantees are explicit.

- Experiments: A small empirical study on a CORA entity-resolution dataset illustrates query–accuracy trade-offs and robustness to approximate distributions. However, experiments are limited: only one dataset, no comparisons to prior heuristics, and no synthetic stress tests varying k, cluster imbalance, or H^2.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with an informative introduction and concrete algorithmic phases. Some notation is hard to parse due to missing fractions (e.g., “k log n H^2” reads ambiguously; it should be k log n divided by H^2). Similarly for “k^2 H^2” in lower bounds; clarify as k^2/H^2. Define constants (C, B) in-line where used, and consider including compact pseudocode for the algorithms in the main text. Typos and minor grammar issues appear throughout and should be cleaned. Assumptions about discrete support size q vs. “arbitrary distributions” merit a clearer statement on how to handle continuous or growing support.

Originality
- Strong. The interactive lower bound framework for adaptive queries with side information and the near-matching, parameter-free upper bounds depending on H^2(f+‖f−) are novel. Using Hellinger (rather than KL) is a thoughtful choice that enables metric-based concentration and clean product-distribution bounds.

Significance
- The work provides a principled explanation for the empirical observation that side information can drastically reduce query complexity in crowdsourced clustering/ER. The dependence on H^2(f+‖f−) offers a crisp characterization: as side information becomes more discriminative, fewer queries are needed. The results should influence both theory (active community detection beyond SBM) and practice (design of query-efficient ER systems). The experimental section is weaker than the theory; nonetheless, the theoretical contribution is substantial.

Pros
- Near-tight information-theoretic lower bounds in an adaptive, interactive setting with side information.
- Efficient, parameter-free algorithms whose query complexity matches the lower bounds up to log n.
- General model: unknown k, unknown f+ and f−, arbitrary cluster sizes; connection to and extension of SBM.
- Clear insight: H^2 divergence as the right measure linking side info quality to label query complexity.

Cons
- Notational ambiguity (missing fractions), typos, and some proof details deferred.
- Experimental evaluation is limited (single dataset, no baselines, no systematic variation of k, H^2, or imbalance).
- Some assumptions (fixed discrete support q, H^2 ≤ 1/18 in LB) deserve clearer scope and discussion.
- Practical guidance on constants, thresholds, and computational overhead could be elaborated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify all asymptotic expressions with explicit fractions; unify the statements of Theorems 1 and 2 and the Las Vegas bound (is it k^2/H^2 or k/H^2?).
- Provide concise pseudocode for the Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms in the main text and explicitly state runtime and memory.
- Expand experiments: include synthetic tests varying H^2, k, and cluster size imbalance; compare to standard ER heuristics [40, 43] and to an oracle-free SBM estimator.
- Clarify handling of continuous-valued W (binning vs. kernel density) and how “fixed support” assumptions impact guarantees.
- Discuss robustness to mild oracle errors, even if full analysis is deferred to the companion paper.
- Tighten constants or at least provide practical parameter choices (e.g., C) and sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a strong and timely theoretical contribution with novel lower bounds and near-matching, parameter-free algorithms in a general model. While clarity and experimental evaluation can be improved, the core ideas are solid and of clear interest to the NIPS community.
