This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on how the availability of a similarity matrix (representing side information) impacts the number of oracle queries needed to accurately cluster data. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and design algorithms that nearly match these bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity compared to clustering without side information. The work is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution where human-in-the-loop approaches are common, and the connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing both lower and upper bounds on query complexity. The use of information theory and Sanov’s theorem is appropriate and skillfully applied.
* **Novel Contribution:** The study of clustering with side information from a query complexity perspective is a novel and valuable contribution. It bridges a gap between theoretical lower bounds (O(nk) without side information) and the observed performance of practical heuristics.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms are parameter-free, making them practical and adaptable to various scenarios without requiring prior knowledge of the underlying distributions.
* **Connection to SBM:**  The clear connection to the well-studied stochastic block model provides context and highlights the generalization of the presented work.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The empirical evaluation on the Cora dataset demonstrates the effectiveness of the algorithms and validates the theoretical findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solutions, and analysis. The organization is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:** The technical details of the proofs, particularly those involving Sanov’s theorem and Hellinger distance, are dense and might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with these tools to follow without significant effort.  While the arguments are sound, a more intuitive explanation of the key steps would improve accessibility.
* **Dataset Choice & Scalability:** The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (Cora).  While useful for demonstrating the concepts, it doesn’t fully showcase the scalability of the algorithms to larger, more complex datasets. The paper acknowledges this limitation but further exploration would be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper largely focuses on the theoretical aspects. A more detailed discussion of practical considerations, such as the computational cost of calculating the similarity matrix and handling noisy oracle responses (mentioned briefly in the introduction), would be valuable.
* **Hellinger Divergence Focus:** While the use of Hellinger divergence is justified, the paper relies heavily on it. A brief discussion of why Hellinger divergence is particularly well-suited for this problem compared to other divergence measures (e.g., KL divergence) could strengthen the argument.
* **Figure Resolution:** The figures in the submitted manuscript are somewhat blurry making it difficult to understand the plots and comparisons. 



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information.
* The results are novel, well-motivated, and have the potential to impact the design of more efficient clustering algorithms, particularly in interactive settings.
* The algorithms proposed are parameter-free, making them practical for real-world applications.
* The connection to the stochastic block model provides a strong foundation for further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity of the proofs might limit the accessibility of the paper to a wider audience.
* The experimental validation is limited and doesn’t fully demonstrate the scalability of the algorithms.
* The lack of a detailed discussion of practical considerations could hinder the immediate applicability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of clustering. The rigorous analysis, novel insights, and parameter-free algorithms are commendable. While the technical complexity and limited experimental validation are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall value of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions.  Specifically:

1. **Improve Figure Resolution:** Ensure all figures are clear and easily readable.
2. **Clarify Proofs:**  Provide more intuitive explanations of the key steps in the proofs, potentially with simplified examples.
3. **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Consider including results on additional datasets to demonstrate scalability.
4. **Discuss Practical Considerations:**  Expand the discussion of practical considerations, such as similarity matrix computation and noisy oracle responses.
5. **Justification of Hellinger Divergence:** Provide a brief justification for choosing Hellinger divergence over other divergence measures.
