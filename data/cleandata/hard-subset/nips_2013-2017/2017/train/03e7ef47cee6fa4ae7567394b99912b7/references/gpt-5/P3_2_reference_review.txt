Summary
The paper studies interactive clustering with access to (i) a perfect same-cluster oracle for pairwise queries and (ii) side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn from f+ or f− depending on whether a pair are in the same cluster or not. The key question is the minimal number of adaptive queries needed for exact recovery when k and the distributions f+, f− are unknown. The authors:

- Prove information-theoretic lower bounds on the expected query complexity that incorporate the statistical quality of side information via the Hellinger divergence H2(f+||f−): any (possibly randomized) algorithm requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}) queries (under H2≤1/18), and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H2}) for success with probability 1.
- Design parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas). The algorithm iteratively grows at least one “reliable” cluster to size Θ((log n)/H2) using the oracle, estimates empirical “intra” and “inter” distributions p+ and p− from currently certified clusters, and then assigns the remaining points by testing whether their side-information profile to a grown cluster looks like f+ or f− using Hellinger divergence. This leads to claimed query complexity on the order of O(min{nk, (k log n)/H2}) for the Monte Carlo algorithm and O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2}) for the Las Vegas algorithm.
- Connect this model to the stochastic block model (SBM) as a strict generalization (unknown k, adversarial cluster sizes, arbitrary unknown f+, f−), discuss how queries may shift SBM recovery thresholds, and show preliminary empirical results on a CORA-based ER dataset.

Quality and correctness
- Lower bound: The core technique is insightful. The reduction to a k-ary hypothesis test for a “lightly queried” element x, construction of many “weak” clusters, and control of the correct-assignment probability via TV–Hellinger inequalities are sound at a high level. The adaptive nature of queries is handled by bounding how often each cluster was queried under a fixed hypothesis and then invoking information distances for the side information. However, there are several issues needing clarification/correction:
  - The statement of Lemma 1 has sign/formatting errors (the lower bound on error probability is written with negative terms and inconsistent scaling factors). The definition of β and thresholds for J′ also seem mangled (2Tk(1−β) vs 2T/k(1−β)). Please fix and provide a clean derivation with correct constants.
  - The TV–Hellinger step uses P`(E) ≤ Pt(E) + ||P`−Pt||TV ≤ Pt(E) + sqrt(H(P`||Pt)), but the standard relation is ||P−Q||TV ≤ sqrt(2)·H(P,Q) for H the Hellinger distance (not its square). Please standardize notation (H vs H2) and constants.
  - The lower bound assumes H2 ≤ 1/18; discussion of behavior beyond this regime is limited (authors note that “no queries are required” as H2→1). Make explicit which statements hold uniformly and which are restricted to small H2.

- Upper bounds/algorithms: The iterative-update idea—growing a reliable cluster to use as a statistical anchor and estimating p+, p− on-the-fly—is elegant and practically meaningful. The use of Hellinger distance (a metric) simplifies testing and concentration arguments (via a Sanov-style analysis). Nonetheless:
  - There is a major inconsistency between the claimed Monte Carlo query complexity and the lower bound. Theorem 2 gives Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}). The Las Vegas upper bound O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2}) matches within a log factor. But the paper also claims a Monte Carlo bound O(min{nk, (k log n)/H2}), which would contradict the lower bound for large k. This is likely a typo; please clarify the correct dependence on k for the Monte Carlo algorithm and ensure consistency across abstract, main text, and Theorem 1.
  - Phase 1 requires at least one true cluster of size Ω((log n)/H2) to “grow.” Under adversarial cluster sizes, this may be impossible (e.g., all clusters smaller than that). The theorem statements promise O(min{nk, …}) queries; please spell out the fallback behavior and stopping criteria when no cluster can reach the threshold, and prove that the overall query bound remains O(nk) in worst case.
  - The analysis mentions extending Sanov’s theorem to Hellinger; more precisely, you appear to combine Sanov with f-divergence inequalities. Please provide a clean lemma with assumptions (finite alphabet size q, bounds on probabilities) and explicit constants.

Originality
- The work is original in formalizing the effect of side information on query complexity for clustering, providing adaptive lower bounds, and giving parameter-free algorithms that do not assume knowledge of k or f+, f−. The lower-bound framework for interactive settings is notably novel, going beyond standard Fano-based arguments.

Significance
- This is a timely and significant contribution. It explains why practical ER/clustering heuristics can query far fewer than nk pairs by leveraging side information, and it provides nearly tight (within log factors) information-theoretic limits and constructive algorithms. The connections and conjectures about query-augmented SBM thresholds could motivate follow-up work.

Clarity
- The paper’s organization is reasonable, but the presentation has many typographical and notational issues that impede understanding:
  - Inconsistent use of H vs H2, and several broken equations (β definition, thresholds, probability bounds).
  - Inconsistent query bounds for the Monte Carlo algorithm across sections.
  - Some algorithmic details are hard to follow without pseudocode (e.g., precise conditions for adding to Waiting(C), termination logic when no cluster is “grown”). Please add clear pseudocode and a flowchart-like description.
  - Assumptions on the support size of f+, f− (fixed q) and implications for continuous/discretized similarities should be centralized and discussed.

Empirical evaluation
- The experiments are limited: one real dataset, no comparison to prior query-selection heuristics [40, 43] or recent theory-driven methods; no synthetic studies varying k, cluster size imbalance, and H2 to validate the predicted scaling; and no error bars. The qualitative result that greedy performs near-best is encouraging, but a stronger experimental section would substantially improve the paper.

Pros
- Important problem with clear practical motivation; rigorous formulation.
- Adaptive lower bound technique for interactive clustering with side info.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms with near-matching bounds.
- Generalization of SBM; thoughtful discussion of implications.

Cons
- Major inconsistency in the claimed Monte Carlo query complexity vs lower bound.
- Numerous typos/notation issues in key proofs (Lemma 1).
- Termination and correctness under adversarial tiny clusters not clearly handled.
- Limited experiments; lack of baselines and synthetic validation.

Questions for authors
1) Please resolve the k vs k^2 dependence in the Monte Carlo bound and align Theorem 1 with the lower bound.
2) What guarantees hold if no cluster has size Ω((log n)/H2)? Do you fall back to O(nk) queries, and how is this detected?
3) Can your analysis handle continuous similarities via discretization? How does the support size q affect constants and query bounds?
4) Could you provide explicit constants in the Sanov/Hellinger concentration lemma and the TV–Hellinger step?
5) How sensitive is performance to mis-specification in p+, p− early on? Any safeguards against early contamination?

Minor comments
- Clean up broken equations (β, J′ threshold, probabilities in Lemma 1).
- Standardize H vs H2 and include the precise TV–Hellinger inequality with constants.
- Provide pseudocode and complexity analysis per phase (time and queries).
- Add comparisons to [40, 43] and a synthetic study varying k and H2.

Recommendation
Borderline accept, contingent on fixing the Monte Carlo bound inconsistency and clarifying the above points. The theoretical contributions are strong and, if corrected, would merit inclusion; the paper would benefit from improved clarity and stronger experiments.

Reviewer confidence: High.
