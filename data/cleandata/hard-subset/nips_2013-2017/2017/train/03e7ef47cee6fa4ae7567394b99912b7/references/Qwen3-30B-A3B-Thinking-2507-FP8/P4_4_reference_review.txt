

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when side information (a similarity matrix) is available. The authors consider a model where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether two points belong to the same cluster, with the goal of minimizing the number of queries needed to recover the exact clustering. The key innovation is the formalization of side information as a noisy similarity matrix where intra-cluster pairs follow distribution f+ and inter-cluster pairs follow f- (both unknown to the algorithm). The paper provides both information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient algorithms for this problem.

The work significantly extends the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. The authors relate their model to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that their framework generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- without requiring assumptions about cluster sizes or k (number of clusters). This contrasts with prior SBM work that often assumes balanced clusters or known k. The paper also connects to practical applications like entity resolution, where recent heuristic approaches [40, 42, 43] have shown reduced query complexity in practice but lack theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper provides rigorous theoretical results. The lower bound proof (Theorem 2) is particularly impressive, developing a novel framework for information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which is challenging due to the adaptive nature of queries. The upper bound algorithms (Theorem 1) are computationally efficient (O(n^2) time), parameter-free (work without knowledge of f+, f-, or k), and nearly match the lower bound within logarithmic factors. The experimental validation on the Cora dataset supports the theoretical claims, showing that the greedy strategy achieves near-optimal performance with only modestly more queries than the best-case scenario.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem definition is precise, and the connection to related work (particularly SBM) is well-articulated. The algorithm description (Section 3) provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though some aspects (like the iterative-update procedure) might benefit from more intuitive explanation. The paper carefully distinguishes its contributions from related work, particularly addressing the limitations of [5] (which considers a different setting with k-means objective and deterministic separation properties).

*Originality:* The paper makes several novel contributions. The formalization of side information as a general similarity matrix with arbitrary distributions f+ and f- is a significant generalization of SBM. The information-theoretic lower bound framework for interactive clustering is new, as the authors note that standard Fano-type inequalities do not apply to adaptive query settings. The parameter-free algorithms that nearly match the lower bound are particularly valuable for practical applications.

*Significance:* The results have clear practical implications for applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering, where minimizing human queries is crucial. The reduction from O(nk) to O(k/(log n * H^2(f+||f-))) query complexity represents a substantial improvement, especially when the similarity matrix provides meaningful side information (i.e., when H^2(f+||f-) is small). The work opens new theoretical directions for studying how side information affects query complexity in community detection models.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper does not address the case of noisy oracle answers, though the authors note this is handled in a companion work [34]. While this is reasonable, the current paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the proposed algorithms might be extended to handle noise, given that noisy oracles are common in practical applications like crowdsourcing.

The experimental results, while supporting the theoretical claims, are limited to a single dataset (Cora). More extensive experiments across multiple datasets with varying similarity distributions would strengthen the practical relevance of the findings.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of interactive clustering with side information
- Develops novel information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering
- Proposes parameter-free algorithms that nearly match the lower bound within logarithmic factors
- Has clear practical relevance to entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications
- Demonstrates strong theoretical results with clear connections to existing work (particularly SBM)

*Con:*
- Limited experimental validation (only one dataset)
- Does not address noisy oracle answers (though noted as handled in companion work)
- The iterative-update algorithm, while effective, is somewhat complex and might be challenging to implement in practice

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of interactive clustering. It successfully bridges the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical heuristics by providing rigorous bounds and efficient algorithms that match the lower bound within logarithmic factors. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem with clear practical implications. The novelty is significant, as the paper generalizes the stochastic block model and provides the first information-theoretic analysis of query complexity with side information. While the experimental validation could be strengthened with more datasets, the theoretical contribution is substantial and meets the high standards of NIPS.

The paper is technically rigorous, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It addresses a fundamental question about the power of side information in clustering and provides definitive theoretical answers. The results have the potential to guide future algorithm design in interactive clustering and community detection. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
