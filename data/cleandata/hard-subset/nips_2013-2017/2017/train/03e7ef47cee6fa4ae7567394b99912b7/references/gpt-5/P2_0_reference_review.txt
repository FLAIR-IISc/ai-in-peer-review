Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when the algorithm has access to side information: a similarity matrix whose entries are drawn from f+ if two points are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The oracle answers pairwise same-cluster queries. The key contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds (allowing adaptive, randomized algorithms, and even assuming knowledge of k, f+, f−) that show any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries in expectation to succeed with nontrivial probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1 (stated for H^2 ≤ 1/18).
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not assume knowledge of k, f+, f−, and achieve query complexity O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2(f+‖f−)}) up to logarithmic factors, with runtime O(n^2).
- A general lower-bound technique for interactive (adaptive) settings leveraging Hellinger distance, plus an iterative-update method that estimates f+, f− from the side information and uses thresholds proportional to log n / H^2 to decide when side information suffices vs. when to query.
- Conceptual links to stochastic block models (SBM) and how queries affect recovery thresholds; small-scale experiments on the Cora ER dataset illustrating the algorithmic behavior.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower-bound framework for adaptive querying is a significant and nontrivial contribution; the reduction to per-element multi-hypothesis testing and the use of Hellinger distance to control total variation between distributions under different hypotheses are well-motivated. The upper-bound algorithmic strategy (iteratively learn p+, p−; use Hellinger thresholds to decide when side information is reliable; fall back to queries otherwise) is sensible and matches the lower bounds up to log factors.
- Rigor and completeness: Some steps are deferred to appendices (Sanov-based analysis for Hellinger; Las Vegas algorithm). For a theory paper, that is acceptable, but the main text contains notational ambiguities and a few apparent sign/formatting errors that impede verification. For example, several expressions involving k^2/H^2 vs. k^2·H^2 are ambiguously typeset; Lemma 1’s error bound appears with a negative right-hand side (likely missing a “1 − ...”); the stated Las Vegas complexity alternately uses k or k^2 factors. These should be corrected. The assumption H^2 ≤ 1/18 for the lower bound is reasonable (small-divergence regime) but should be explicitly discussed as to necessity vs. convenience.
- Empirics: Experiments are minimal and only compare strategies within the proposed framework, not against prior heuristics or baselines. Given the strong theoretical nature, this is not fatal but limits empirical support.

Clarity
- Organization is generally good, with a clear problem formulation, motivation (ER), and relation to SBM. However, there are several clarity issues:
  - Inconsistent/ambiguous notation for query complexity terms (is it k log n / H^2 or k / (log n · H^2)?), and for lower bounds (min{nk, k^2/H^2} vs. min{nk, k^2·H^2}).
  - Lemma 1 statement likely has sign mistakes; Theorem 2 later clarifies the intended regime but the inconsistency is distracting.
  - The algorithmic description would benefit from concise pseudo-code or a figure; terms like “grown cluster,” “Membership,” and ME threshold are defined but scattered.
  - Some references to “Appendix C/D” in the main text are fine, but a short high-level proof sketch for the Las Vegas algorithm would help.
- The writing otherwise conveys the main ideas well, and the choice of Hellinger (metric and product-friendly) is clearly justified.

Originality
- High. Prior work either did not analyze query complexity with side information in generality or focused on specific objectives/assumptions (e.g., k-means with separation, SBM with known parameters). This paper:
  - Provides interactive lower bounds under adaptive queries using information-theoretic tools in a general side-information model.
  - Gives parameter-free algorithms for unknown k, f+, f− that adaptively learn and exploit side information.
  - Extends beyond SBM by allowing arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes.
This combination is novel and advances understanding of when and how side information can reduce query complexity.

Significance
- The results sharpen the theoretical landscape for interactive clustering with side information, explaining the empirical gap between nk worst-case query bounds and much smaller practical query usage. The lower-bound framework and the iterative-update design pattern are likely reusable in related interactive/active-learning problems. The connections to SBM thresholds with queries open compelling directions. Practical impact is plausible in ER and crowdsourcing settings, though more empirical validation would strengthen the case.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Strong, broadly applicable lower-bound method for adaptive querying; uses appropriate divergences and handles unknown parameters in the upper bounds.
- Parameter-free algorithms with near-matching complexities; conceptually clean and computationally efficient.
- Clear motivation and positioning relative to ER and SBM; fills a gap between practice and theory.
- Opens several concrete future directions; likely to influence subsequent work.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Clarity/notation issues and apparent typographical errors in key theorems/lemmas that must be fixed to avoid misinterpretation.
- Experimental section is small-scale and lacks comparisons to existing heuristic methods widely used in ER/crowdsourcing; empirical support of parameter-free behavior is limited.
- Some assumptions (noiseless oracle; discrete finite support in algorithm) could be discussed more thoroughly; the lower-bound regime H^2 ≤ 1/18 may appear restrictive without context.

Suggestions for improvement
- Disambiguate all occurrences of “k^2 H2(·)” vs. “k^2/H2(·)” and “k log n/H^2(·)” vs. “k/(log n H^2(·))”; fix Lemma 1 sign issue and ensure consistency between Monte Carlo and Las Vegas complexities.
- Add compact pseudo-code for the algorithm, explicitly state stopping criteria, and summarize the key concentration lemma (Sanov-to-Hellinger) in the main text.
- Expand experiments: include at least one baseline (e.g., prior ER heuristics guided by similarities; pure transitivity-based algorithms), ablations (with/without iterative updates), and sensitivity to mis-specified support.
- Briefly discuss extension to continuous side-information distributions and to noisy oracles (even if in companion work), to help readers situate applicability.

Overall evaluation
- Quality: strong theory, but clarity needs attention; limited experiments.
- Clarity: good high-level exposition but marred by notational/typographical issues.
- Originality: high.
- Significance: high for theory; moderate immediate practical impact pending more empirical evidence.

My inclination is to accept, contingent on fixing the clarity/notation issues; the theoretical contributions are substantial and timely.
