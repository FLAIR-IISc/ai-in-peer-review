

# Peer Review: Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information

## Summary
This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when the algorithm has access to side information (a similarity matrix) and can query an oracle about whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient algorithms that nearly match these bounds. They show that with side information, the query complexity can be reduced from O(nk) (without side information) to O(k / (log n * H^2(f+||f-))), where H is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity scores within clusters and between clusters. The paper connects their model to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing it as a significant generalization, and provides experimental validation on the Cora dataset. This work addresses a theoretically important problem with direct practical relevance to entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution and Novelty:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing tight bounds for interactive clustering with side information. The lower bound (Theorem 2) is particularly impressive as it holds for randomized algorithms and even with full knowledge of f+, f-, and k, which makes it stronger than many existing results. The paper's generalization of the SBM to arbitrary distributions f+ and f- is significant and provides a more realistic model for practical applications.

**Algorithmic Innovation:** The parameter-free algorithms presented are highly valuable for practice. The iterative-update technique for designing the Monte Carlo algorithm is particularly clever and could be applied to other parameter-free learning problems. The paper demonstrates how to handle the challenge of not knowing f+ and f- by using the side information to estimate the Hellinger divergence and adaptively determine when to trust the side information versus making queries.

**Practical Relevance:** The connection to entity resolution and crowdsourcing is well-motivated, and the experimental results on the Cora dataset provide compelling evidence that the theoretical findings translate to practical applications. The paper shows that the greedy strategy (using side information) achieves performance very close to the best-case scenario with significantly fewer queries.

**Rigorous Analysis:** The paper carefully addresses both theoretical and practical aspects. The proof of Lemma 1 and Theorem 2 is technically sound, and the authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., assuming a perfect oracle, which they address in a companion paper).

## Weaknesses

**Limited Discussion of Practical Constants:** While the theoretical query complexity is significantly improved, the paper doesn't adequately address the constant factors in the O(k / (log n * H^2(f+||f-))) bound. For many practical applications, the constant factor could be large, making the theoretical reduction less impactful than it appears. The authors should provide more intuition about when this reduction becomes meaningful in practice.

**Limited Connection to Sparse SBM:** The paper mentions the connection to sparse SBM as a future direction but doesn't explore it in depth. The sparse SBM is particularly relevant for real-world applications, and the authors could have made a stronger case for their results by analyzing how their query complexity results change the recovery thresholds in the sparse SBM regime.

**Assumption of Perfect Oracle:** The paper assumes the oracle always returns correct answers, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications. While they mention a companion paper on noisy queries, they don't discuss how their results would change under noisy oracle responses. This is a critical limitation for practical applications where crowdsourced oracle responses are often noisy.

**Experimental Scope:** The experimental results are limited to one dataset (Cora). More extensive experiments across multiple datasets would strengthen the practical claims. The authors should also compare their approach to more recent heuristic algorithms for entity resolution.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of interactive clustering with side information. The lower and upper bounds nearly match, the algorithms are parameter-free, and the work bridges theoretical computer science with practical applications in entity resolution. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with clear practical relevance.

The main limitations are the lack of discussion about practical constant factors, the limited exploration of the sparse SBM connection, and the assumption of a perfect oracle. However, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's substantial theoretical contribution.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to a problem with clear practical relevance. The results are well-supported, the algorithms are innovative and parameter-free, and the connection to entity resolution and SBM is well-motivated. While the practical constants and oracle noise are limitations, they don't undermine the fundamental contribution of the paper.

## Pro/Con Arguments

**Pro:**
- Establishes tight theoretical bounds for a practically important problem
- Provides parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f+, f-, or k
- Demonstrates significant query complexity reduction from O(nk) to nearly O(k / (log n * H^2(f+||f-)))
- Connects to real-world applications (entity resolution) and the stochastic block model
- The iterative-update technique could inspire further parameter-free algorithm design

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of practical constant factors in the query complexity bound
- Assumption of a perfect oracle (no noise in queries) is a significant limitation for real applications
- Experimental results limited to one dataset without comparison to recent heuristics
- Sparse SBM connection is mentioned but not deeply explored

This paper represents a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information and has clear potential for practical impact. The strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses, making it suitable for publication at NIPS.
