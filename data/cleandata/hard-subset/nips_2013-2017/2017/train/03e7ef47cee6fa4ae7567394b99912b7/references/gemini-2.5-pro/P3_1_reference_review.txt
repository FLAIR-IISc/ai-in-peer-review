This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with an oracle when side information, in the form of a noisy similarity matrix, is available. The authors aim to bridge the gap between the pessimistic theoretical lower bound of Ω(nk) queries (without side information) and the empirical success of practical heuristics that often use far fewer queries by leveraging such side information. The core problem is to recover a latent partition of n items into an unknown number of k clusters using pairwise same-cluster oracle queries. The side information is modeled as a matrix W where each entry `w_ij` is drawn from a distribution `f+` if items i and j are in the same cluster, and from `f-` otherwise.

The main contributions are a tight, information-theoretic characterization of the problem's query complexity. The authors prove a lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) queries for any algorithm to succeed with constant probability, where H²(f+||f-) is the squared Hellinger divergence between the two distributions. They complement this with two computationally efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) whose query complexities nearly match this lower bound up to logarithmic factors. A key strength of the algorithms is that they do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f-. This work significantly generalizes the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM) by allowing for arbitrary, unknown distributions and provides a framework for analyzing the impact of active queries in such models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important disconnect between theory and practice in interactive clustering. By formalizing the role of side information, it provides a rigorous explanation for the empirical success of heuristics used in applications like entity resolution. The results are significant as they precisely quantify the value of side information, showing that the query complexity is governed by the statistical distinguishability of the intra- and inter-cluster similarity distributions, as measured by Hellinger divergence.

2.  **Novel and Strong Technical Contributions:**
    *   The information-theoretic lower bound is a major contribution. Proving lower bounds in an adaptive query setting is notoriously difficult, as standard tools like Fano's inequality do not readily apply. The authors' technique of identifying elements and clusters that are "weakly queried" and analyzing the hypothesis testing problem for these specific elements is novel and powerful. This framework could be of independent interest for other interactive learning problems.
    *   The design of parameter-free algorithms is a standout feature. In practice, parameters like k and the noise distributions are rarely known. The proposed iterative-update scheme, where the algorithm refines its estimate of H²(f+||f-) and adjusts its own sample size threshold `ME_x` accordingly, is a very clever and practical approach. This provides a valuable recipe for designing other parameter-free algorithms.

3.  **Theoretical Rigor and Tightness:** The paper is technically sound. The upper bounds for the proposed Las Vegas algorithm match the lower bound up to a `log n` factor, providing a near-complete characterization of the query complexity. The use of Hellinger distance is well-justified and proves to be the "right" metric for both the upper and lower bound analyses.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on the Monte Carlo Bound:** There appears to be a gap between the Monte Carlo upper bound and the general lower bound that is not fully addressed. Theorem 1 states a Monte Carlo query complexity of O(k log n / H²(f+||f-)), whereas Theorem 2 provides a lower bound of Ω(k² / H²(f+||f-)). This is a gap of a factor of `k`. Is the Monte Carlo algorithm suboptimal by this factor, or does the `k^2` in the lower bound only apply to exact recovery (Las Vegas)? The paper would be strengthened by explicitly discussing this gap. If it is not a typo, explaining the intuition behind why a high-probability algorithm can save a factor of `k` over an exact one would be valuable.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the inclusion of experiments on the Cora dataset is appreciated, the evaluation could be more comprehensive. The main motivation of the paper is to explain the performance of existing heuristics [40, 42, 43]. A direct experimental comparison against one or two of these state-of-the-art heuristic methods would make a much stronger case for the practical utility of the proposed algorithm. Showing that a theoretically-grounded, parameter-free algorithm is competitive with or superior to tuned heuristics would be a very compelling result. The current baselines ("best", "worst") are internal and synthetic, making it hard to judge performance in a broader context.

3.  **Presentation of the Lower Bound Proof:** The proof of the lower bound in Section 2 is quite dense. While the high-level steps are outlined, the technical details, such as the choice of `β` and the probabilistic arguments in "Step 2: Finding 'weak' clusters," could benefit from more intuition. Expanding this section slightly to walk the reader through the reasoning would improve its accessibility without sacrificing rigor.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes fundamental contributions to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. It successfully bridges a gap between theory and practice, introduces novel techniques for both lower bounds and algorithm design in an interactive setting, and provides nearly tight results. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms is a significant practical advantage. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarifying a specific theoretical gap and expanding the experimental section, and they do not detract from the core strength and importance of the work. The paper is well-written, original, and addresses a significant problem. It is highly likely to inspire follow-up work at the intersection of active learning, clustering, and community detection. I strongly recommend acceptance.
