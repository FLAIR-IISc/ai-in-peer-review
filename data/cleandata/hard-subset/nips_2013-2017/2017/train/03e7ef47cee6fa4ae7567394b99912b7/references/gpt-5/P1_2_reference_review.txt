Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm has access to a noiseless same-cluster oracle and to side information: a pairwise similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f‚ąí (across-cluster). The number of clusters k is unknown and clusters may be adversarially sized. The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds on the expected number of queries any (possibly randomized, adaptive) algorithm must ask to exactly recover the clustering, even when f+, f‚ąí, and k are known to the algorithm. The bound scales as ‚Ą¶(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‚ÄĖf‚ąí)}) in the regime H^2(f+‚ÄĖf‚ąí) ‚Č§ 1/18, where H is the Hellinger divergence; and ‚Ą¶(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to achieve success probability 1.
- Two parameter-free algorithms (no knowledge of k, f+, f‚ąí): a Monte Carlo algorithm that uses O(min{nk, k/(log n ¬∑ H^2)}) queries and succeeds with probability 1 ‚ąí o(1/n), and a Las Vegas algorithm with O(n log n + min{nk, k^2/(log n ¬∑ H^2)}) queries and success probability 1. Both run in O(n^2) time due to processing W.
- A general lower-bound technique for interactive settings that handles adaptive queries and uses Hellinger divergence; and an iterative-update design for parameter-free algorithms that estimate f+, f‚ąí on the fly, using side information to reduce queries.
- Connections to SBM as a special case and discussion of how queries might shift exact-recovery thresholds.
- Limited experiments on an entity-resolution dataset illustrating the query/accuracy tradeoff and robustness to approximate f+, f‚ąí.

Relation to prior work: The paper generalizes SBM-based recovery (e.g., Abbe et al., Mossel et al.) by allowing arbitrary unknown f+, f‚ąí and adversarial cluster sizes. It contrasts with interactive clustering with same-cluster queries without side information (Ashtiani et al.) and with crowdsourced ER heuristics that leverage similarities but lack guarantees. It also discusses related active-query models for SBM and crowdsourcing error models, clarifying scope.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level arguments for both the lower bound and the algorithms are plausible and thoughtfully constructed. The lower bound handles adaptivity by identifying low-query ‚Äúweak‚ÄĚ clusters and reducing to a multi-hypothesis test, controlling distances via Hellinger and total-variation bounds. The upper bounds rely on iterative estimation of f+, f‚ąí using Sanov-type arguments and an affinity score based on Hellinger distance.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized (intro, lower bound, algorithm sketch, experiments), but has numerous typographical and notation issues that impede readability: missing fractions/powers (H2 vs H^2, ‚Äúk 2 H2(...)‚ÄĚ), ‚Äúon(1)‚ÄĚ/‚Äúok(1)‚ÄĚ instead of o(1), incomplete references (years, venues), dangling footnotes, and some awkward sentences. Provide explicit pseudocode for the algorithms, define all constants (C, B), and be consistent about ordered vs unordered pairs in empirical distributions.
- Explain why Hellinger (vs KL) is the right choice across both lower and upper bounds, and how Sanov is adapted when using a metric divergence.
- Clarify what ‚Äúfixed cardinality of support‚ÄĚ for f+, f‚ąí means in the upper bound and whether/how results extend to continuous/large alphabets.

Originality
- The formulation of query complexity with side information and unknown f+, f‚ąí, k is new and practically motivated. The lower-bound technique for adaptive interactive algorithms using Hellinger distances is a notable conceptual contribution. The parameter-free iterative update for estimating f+, f‚ąí to set data-driven thresholds is also novel.

Significance
- Provides a near-tight characterization (up to log factors) of how side information reduces query complexity, in a general setting that strictly extends SBM. The techniques are likely reusable (interactive lower bounds, parameter-free design with Hellinger). This is valuable for theory of crowdsourced clustering/ER and active learning with similarity side info.

Pros
- Clear, important problem with strong practical motivation (ER).
- General model (unknown k, arbitrary f+, f‚ąí), adversarial cluster sizes.
- New information-theoretic lower-bound methodology for interactive settings.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms with provable guarantees, nearly matching LB up to log factors.
- Thoughtful connections to SBM thresholds and future directions.

Cons
- Notational/typo issues in core theorems (especially Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) that should be fixed.
- Apparent discrepancy between LB ‚Ą¶(k^2/H^2) and Monte Carlo UB O(k/(log n ¬∑ H^2)) needs explicit reconciliation.
- Experimental section is thin: single dataset, no baselines, no variance/error bars.
- Some assumptions and steps (e.g., Sanov with Hellinger, constants, support size) are under-specified in the main text.

Overall: Strong theoretical contribution with high potential impact but needs clarifications and polish. I lean toward acceptance if the stated issues (especially the bound statements and Monte Carlo vs LB reconciliation) are addressed.
