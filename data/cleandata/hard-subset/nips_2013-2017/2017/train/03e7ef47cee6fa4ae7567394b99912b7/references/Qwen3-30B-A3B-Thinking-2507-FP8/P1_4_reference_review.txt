

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary
This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to side information (a similarity matrix) in addition to an oracle that answers whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that side information can drastically reduce the number of queries needed for clustering from O(nk) to O(k / (log n · H²(f⁺||f⁻))), where H is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity scores within and between clusters. They establish matching lower bounds and develop parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f⁺, f⁻, or k. The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) as a special case and addresses a gap between theoretical guarantees and practical heuristics that often use fewer queries than the theoretical O(nk) bound.

The paper makes significant contributions by: (1) providing the first information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering with side information; (2) designing efficient parameter-free algorithms that nearly match these bounds; and (3) establishing a general framework for analyzing such problems that could extend to other interactive learning scenarios.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of both upper and lower bounds that match within a logarithmic factor. The authors carefully analyze the information-theoretic limits of the problem, including a novel framework for lower bounds in interactive settings that overcomes challenges with adaptive querying. The Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms are computationally efficient (O(n²) time) and parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage. The experimental results on the Cora dataset support the theoretical claims, showing that the greedy strategy performs nearly as well as the optimal strategy with minimal queries.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized, with clear problem statements and logical flow from motivation to theoretical results to algorithms. The connection to practical applications like entity resolution is well-explained. The technical details are presented accessibly, though some readers might find the lower bound proof (Lemma 1) challenging. The supplementary material provides the necessary details for reproducibility.

**Originality (High):** This work significantly extends previous research on interactive clustering. While prior work studied clustering with oracle queries without side information, or analyzed specific heuristics for clustering with side information, this is the first paper to provide a general theoretical analysis for arbitrary f⁺ and f⁻ distributions. The connection to SBM as a special case and the generalization of the model to arbitrary distributions is a novel contribution. The techniques for proving lower bounds in interactive settings are particularly original.

**Significance (High):** The results have important implications for practical applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering where minimizing human effort (queries) is crucial. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms makes them highly applicable in real-world scenarios where f⁺ and f⁻ are unknown. The work bridges the gap between theory and practice in interactive clustering, addressing a long-standing question about why heuristic methods often perform better than theoretical O(nk) bounds.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Experimental Validation:** While the Cora dataset experiments support the theoretical claims, the paper lacks experiments on additional datasets or synthetic data to demonstrate the robustness of the algorithm across different settings. More comprehensive experimental validation would strengthen the practical claims.

**Dependence on Hellinger Divergence:** The paper focuses on the case where H²(f⁺||f⁻) is small (the interesting regime), but it doesn't discuss what happens when this divergence is large (i.e., when side information is poor). It would be valuable to analyze the algorithm's performance in this regime.

**Parameter-Free Claim:** The paper claims the algorithms are "parameter-free," but they actually depend on a constant C in the threshold calculations. The paper doesn't explain how to choose C optimally, which could affect performance in practice.

**Comparison to Existing Algorithms:** The paper compares to "best" and "worst" strategies but doesn't compare to other existing algorithms for clustering with side information. A comparison to state-of-the-art heuristics would better demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information, with clear practical implications. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important gap between theory and practice in the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the experimental section to include additional datasets or synthetic experiments to validate the algorithm's performance across different settings.

2. Discuss the algorithm's behavior when H²(f⁺||f⁻) is large (not small), and analyze the trade-offs between side information quality and query complexity.

3. Clarify the choice of the constant C in the threshold calculations and discuss how it might be optimized in practice.

4. Include a comparison to other existing algorithms for clustering with side information to better demonstrate the practical advantages.

5. Briefly explain why Hellinger divergence was chosen over KL divergence, especially since KL is more common in information theory, and note how the choice affects the theoretical guarantees.

## Conclusion
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem in interactive clustering. The paper's rigorous analysis, novel lower bound framework, and practical parameter-free algorithms make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The work has clear significance for both theory and practice, and with minor improvements to the experimental section and clarifications, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
