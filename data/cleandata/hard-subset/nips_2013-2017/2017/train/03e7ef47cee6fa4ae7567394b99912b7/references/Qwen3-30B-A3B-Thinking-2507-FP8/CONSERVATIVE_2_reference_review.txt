

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework for analyzing the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the algorithm has access to a similarity matrix in addition to an oracle that answers whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors claim to provide the first rigorous theoretical study of this problem, showing that the query complexity can be reduced from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity scores within clusters (f+) and between clusters (f-).

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution in establishing information-theoretic lower bounds that match the proposed upper bounds within logarithmic factors. The main technical contribution is a general framework for proving lower bounds in the interactive clustering setting, which appears to be novel. The authors also develop parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f+, f-, or k, which is theoretically interesting.

However, I have substantial concerns about the paper's practical relevance and the strength of its theoretical claims. The model assumptions are highly restrictive and unrealistic for real-world clustering applications. The paper assumes a "noisy weighted upper-triangular similarity matrix" where similarity scores are drawn from f+ if points belong to the same cluster and from f- otherwise. This assumes that the similarity matrix is generated from a clean probabilistic model, which is rarely the case in practice. In real-world applications, similarity matrices are often generated from noisy, imperfect features or heuristics, not from clean distributions f+ and f-.

The paper claims that "recent works of [18, 33] analyze popular heuristic algorithms... where the probability distributions are obtained from real datasets which show that these heuristics are significantly suboptimal even for very simple distributions." However, the authors fail to adequately address why these heuristics work well in practice despite the Ω(nk) theoretical lower bound. The paper states that "the recent works of [18, 33]... show that these heuristics are significantly suboptimal," but doesn't provide evidence that these heuristics actually perform poorly in real-world settings.

The experimental validation is severely limited to a single dataset (Cora) with a small number of clusters (124 clusters for 1812 nodes). The results show that the greedy strategy achieves performance close to the best strategy, but this doesn't demonstrate the practical utility of the theoretical results. The paper doesn't compare against established clustering methods with side information or demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach on a broader range of datasets.

The paper also overstates the novelty of its contribution. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is noted, but the paper doesn't clearly explain how this generalization is practically useful. The authors claim that "f+ and f- can be arbitrary probability mass functions (pmfs)" but then assume they have fixed cardinality of support, which is a significant limitation.

Most critically, the paper's main theoretical result depends on knowing the Hellinger divergence H²(f+||f-), which the authors claim is unknown to the algorithm. However, the algorithm requires an estimate of this divergence to function properly, which is not realistic in practice. The paper doesn't address how this estimate would be obtained or how errors in this estimate would affect performance.

The lower bound proof assumes "k equally sized clusters of size a each," which is a highly artificial setup that doesn't reflect real-world clustering problems where cluster sizes are typically highly imbalanced. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't address how their results would change in more realistic settings.

In summary, while the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the query complexity of clustering with side information, the model assumptions are too strong, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the practical relevance is questionable. The paper would benefit from a more realistic model of side information, broader experimental validation, and a clearer explanation of how the theoretical results translate to practical applications.

The paper's main claim—that side information can drastically reduce query complexity—relies on a model that doesn't align with real-world clustering scenarios. The authors fail to demonstrate that their theoretical results provide meaningful improvements in practical clustering applications. Without addressing these limitations, the paper's contribution appears to be primarily of theoretical interest rather than practical significance.

**Quality**: The theoretical results are technically sound, but the practical relevance is questionable due to unrealistic model assumptions. The experimental validation is insufficient.

**Clarity**: Well-written and well-organized, with clear statements of theorems and algorithms.

**Originality**: The theoretical framework for lower bounds in interactive clustering is novel, but the main result depends on unrealistic assumptions.

**Significance**: The theoretical contribution is interesting but lacks practical significance due to the restrictive model assumptions.

**Recommendation**: Weak reject. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the paper's practical relevance is questionable due to unrealistic assumptions and insufficient experimental validation. The authors should address these limitations before considering this work for publication at NIPS.
