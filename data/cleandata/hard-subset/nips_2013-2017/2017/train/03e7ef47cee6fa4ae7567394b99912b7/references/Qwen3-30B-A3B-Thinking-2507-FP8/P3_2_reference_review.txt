

# NIPS Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to side information (a similarity matrix) in addition to an oracle that can answer whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that side information can dramatically reduce query complexity from Ω(nk) (without side information) to O(k / (log n · H²(f⁺‖f⁻))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity scores for points in the same cluster (f⁺) and points in different clusters (f⁻). The paper develops both information-theoretic lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that nearly match these bounds. The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) as a special case, showing how query complexity affects recovery thresholds in SBM.

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a gap in the literature: while practical heuristics for clustering with side information use far fewer queries than the Ω(nk) theoretical bound, there was no rigorous theoretical understanding of how side information affects query complexity. The authors provide the first such analysis for arbitrary unknown distributions f⁺ and f⁻.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides strong theoretical results with well-constructed proofs for both lower and upper bounds. The lower bound proof is particularly impressive, as it overcomes the challenge of proving information-theoretic bounds in the interactive setting where standard techniques like Fano's inequality don't apply.

2. **Novel Technique for Lower Bounds**: The authors develop a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which could be applicable to other problems in active learning and classification.

3. **Parameter-Free Algorithms**: The proposed Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms are completely parameter-free (work without knowing k, f⁺, or f⁻), which is crucial for practical applications where these parameters are unknown.

4. **Connection to Stochastic Block Model**: The paper makes a clear connection to SBM, showing how their results generalize and extend the understanding of recovery thresholds in SBM when queries are allowed.

5. **Clear Contribution Statement**: The paper precisely characterizes how side information affects query complexity, resolving the theoretical gap between the Ω(nk) bound for no side information and the practical heuristics that use far fewer queries.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experimental section is very brief and lacks sufficient depth. The paper only reports results on a single dataset (Cora) with limited parameter variations. A more thorough experimental analysis across different datasets and parameter regimes would strengthen the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

2. **Ambiguity in Distribution Assumptions**: The paper claims the lower bound holds for continuous distributions, but the proof is developed for discrete distributions. The extension to continuous distributions isn't clearly explained, which weakens the theoretical claim.

3. **Overreliance on Hellinger Distance**: While the authors justify using Hellinger distance because it's a metric, they don't sufficiently explain why it's the right measure for both lower and upper bounds. The connection between Hellinger distance and Sanov's theorem (used in the upper bound analysis) isn't fully justified. The authors mention they could have used KL divergence (which appears in Sanov's theorem), but don't explain why Hellinger is superior in this context.

4. **Algorithm Complexity**: The algorithms are described as "computationally efficient" with time complexity O(n²), but the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of how the algorithm avoids the O(n²) side information matrix size in practice, which could be prohibitive for large datasets.

5. **Limited Comparison with Baselines**: The experimental section only compares with the "best" and "worst" strategies, but doesn't compare with existing heuristic algorithms from the literature (e.g., those from [42, 43, 40]), which would better demonstrate the practical advantage of the proposed approach.

## Specific Technical Comments

1. **Lower Bound Proof**: The proof of Lemma 1 is complex but well-structured. However, the transition from Lemma 1 to Theorem 2 is somewhat abrupt. The authors should clarify why the two cases (nk < k²/H²(f⁺‖f⁻) and nk ≥ k²/H²(f⁺‖f⁻)) are sufficient to cover all possible parameter regimes.

2. **Upper Bound Analysis**: The key insight in the algorithm design is the iterative-update procedure to learn the Hellinger divergence between f⁺ and f⁻ without prior knowledge. This is a clever approach, but the paper could better explain why the specific threshold τₓ = C log n / H(p⁺,ₓ‖p⁻,ₓ)² is chosen. The connection between this threshold and the theoretical lower bound could be made more explicit.

3. **Sanov's Theorem Application**: The use of Sanov's theorem to bound the error probability is innovative, but the paper doesn't clearly explain why Hellinger distance is the appropriate metric for this application when Sanov's theorem is typically formulated with KL divergence. This is a critical point that needs better justification.

4. **Experimental Setup**: The paper states that the Hellinger square divergence between the two distributions is 0.6, but doesn't explain how they computed this from the similarity scores. This is important for reproducibility.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of query complexity in clustering with side information. The results are theoretically sound, and the connection to practical applications (like entity resolution) is well-motivated. The parameter-free algorithms are particularly valuable for real-world applications where the underlying distributions are unknown.

However, the paper's practical significance is somewhat limited by the brief experimental section and the lack of comparison with existing heuristic algorithms. The theoretical results are strong, but the paper would be more compelling with more thorough experimental validation.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. While the experimental validation is limited, the theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and address an important gap in the literature. The paper would benefit from more thorough experiments and better explanation of the Hellinger distance justification, but these are not sufficient to warrant rejection.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with existing heuristic algorithms from the literature (e.g., [42, 43, 40]) on multiple datasets.

2. Clarify the extension of the lower bound to continuous distributions and provide a more detailed justification for using Hellinger distance instead of KL divergence.

3. Provide a more detailed explanation of how the algorithm can be implemented efficiently without explicitly constructing the full n×n side information matrix, which would be prohibitive for large n.

4. Include a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to deviations from the assumed model (e.g., if f⁺ and f⁻ are not well-separated).

5. Add a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm in terms of the size of the side information matrix, and how it scales with n.

## Final Note

This is a well-written paper with strong theoretical contributions that addresses an important gap in the literature. While the experimental validation could be strengthened, the theoretical results are significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. The paper represents a clear advancement in the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information.
