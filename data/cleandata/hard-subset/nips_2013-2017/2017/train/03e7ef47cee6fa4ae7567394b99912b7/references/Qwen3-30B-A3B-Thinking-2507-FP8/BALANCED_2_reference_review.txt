

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when side information (a similarity matrix) is available, providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of how side information reduces the number of queries needed to cluster points. The authors establish that with side information, query complexity can be reduced from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence between within-cluster and between-cluster similarity distributions. They prove matching lower and upper bounds and develop parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-.

The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM), generalizing it by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- without assumptions on k or cluster sizes. This generalization is significant as it encompasses real-world scenarios where similarity distributions are unknown and complex. The paper also relates to practical applications like entity resolution, where human input is expensive, and the authors provide experimental validation on the Cora dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with a novel information-theoretic lower bound framework for interactive clustering algorithms, which is challenging due to the adaptive nature of query selection. The lower bound matches the upper bound within logarithmic factors, establishing tight results.

2. **Practical Relevance:** The parameter-free algorithms are particularly valuable for real-world applications where parameters like k, f+, and f- are unknown. The Monte Carlo algorithm reduces query complexity from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), which could significantly reduce human effort in applications like entity resolution.

3. **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides comprehensive theoretical analysis with careful treatment of the challenging regime where f+ and f- are close (small Hellinger divergence). The proof of Lemma 1 is particularly impressive for handling the interactive nature of the problem.

4. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on the Cora dataset demonstrate that the algorithm performs well in practice, with the greedy strategy closely matching the best-case performance (only 12 additional queries when using approximate distributions).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental section is based on a single dataset (Cora), which limits the generalizability of the practical findings. More diverse experiments across different data distributions would strengthen the practical relevance claims.

2. **Technical Accessibility:** The lower bound proof (Section 2) is highly technical and would benefit from additional intuitive explanations. For instance, the authors could include a brief example illustrating why the Hellinger divergence is the appropriate measure for their problem rather than KL divergence, which is more commonly used in information theory.

3. **Connection to SBM:** While the paper mentions the connection to SBM, it doesn't fully explore how query complexity affects the recovery thresholds in SBM. The discussion in Section 4 about sparse SBM recovery is promising but would be stronger with more concrete examples or a more detailed analysis of this specific connection.

4. **Regime of Interest:** The paper doesn't sufficiently address the most interesting regime where H²(f+||f-) is very small (close to 0), which is where the query complexity reduction would be most dramatic. The authors could provide more intuition about how the query complexity behaves in this regime.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of interactive clustering with side information, with strong results that are both theoretically rigorous and practically relevant. The main strengths are the tight bounds, the parameter-free algorithms, and the clear connection to real-world applications. The weaknesses are primarily in the experimental validation scope and the accessibility of the technical content.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper is of high quality and makes a clear contribution to the field. To improve it further, the authors should:

1. Expand the experimental section to include at least one additional dataset to better validate the practical performance across different data distributions.
2. Add a brief intuitive explanation of why Hellinger divergence is the appropriate measure for this problem (as opposed to KL divergence) to improve accessibility.
3. Deepen the discussion of the connection between their results and SBM recovery thresholds, particularly for sparse SBM cases.
4. Include a more detailed analysis of the most interesting regime where H²(f+||f-) is very small.

The paper's contribution is novel and significant, and the results will likely be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on clustering, active learning, and interactive machine learning. The theoretical framework developed here could also be applicable to other interactive learning problems, adding to the paper's broader impact.
