## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with access to an oracle that can determine if two points belong to the same cluster, *and* the availability of side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors establish a theoretical framework for analyzing this problem, proving information-theoretic lower bounds and designing algorithms that nearly match these bounds. A key contribution is showing how side information can drastically reduce the required number of queries compared to the classic O(nk) complexity when no side information is available. The work connects to and generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM), and presents results applicable to arbitrary, unknown distributions governing the side information.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including both lower and upper bounds on query complexity. The lower bound is particularly noteworthy as it establishes a fundamental limit on interactive clustering with side information.
* **Novelty:** The problem itself – precisely characterizing the impact of side information on clustering query complexity – is novel.  The generalization of the SBM and the handling of arbitrary, unknown distributions for the side information are also significant contributions.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The motivating application of entity resolution, and the observation that practical heuristics outperform theoretical bounds, make this work highly relevant to real-world problems.  The proposed algorithms offer a potential path to more efficient interactive clustering.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that *do not* require knowledge of the underlying distributions (f+ and f-) or the number of clusters (k) is a major strength and boosts practicality.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper thoroughly situates itself within the broader landscape of clustering, active learning, and community detection, referencing relevant literature extensively.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results on a real dataset (Cora) provides some validation of the theoretical findings and demonstrates the practical performance of the proposed algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** While the paper is theoretically sound, it is dense and the mathematical notation can be challenging to follow without significant background in information theory.  More intuitive explanations of key concepts (e.g., Hellinger divergence) would be helpful.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset and a relatively narrow set of parameters.  More comprehensive experiments with varying dataset characteristics and side information qualities would strengthen the practical claims.  A comparison to existing state-of-the-art heuristics (beyond simply noting their performance) would also be valuable.
* **Logarithmic Factors:** The algorithm’s upper bound still contains a logarithmic factor. While this is common in theoretical analyses, it's not explicitly discussed whether this factor poses a practical limitation.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of a fixed cardinality of support for f+ and f- might be limiting. While stated as a simplification, it's unclear how sensitive the results are to this assumption.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound and provides strong theoretical results. The proofs appear rigorous, and the analysis is well-motivated.  However, the density of the presentation slightly detracts from the overall quality.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is difficult to follow at times due to the technicality of the subject matter and the density of notation. While the structure is logical, a more accessible explanation of the core ideas would significantly improve clarity.
* **Originality (9/10):** The problem and the approach are highly original. The generalization of the SBM and the development of parameter-free algorithms are particularly novel.
* **Significance (8/10):**  The results are important because they provide a fundamental understanding of the role of side information in clustering. This has implications for a wide range of applications, including entity resolution and active learning.  The algorithms offer a potential path to more efficient clustering in practical settings.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in our understanding of interactive clustering.
* The results are novel, well-supported, and have potential practical implications.
* The parameter-free algorithms are a valuable contribution.
* The connection to the SBM provides a link to a well-studied area of research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and challenging to read.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The logarithmic factor in the upper bound could be a practical limitation.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. While the paper could benefit from improved clarity and more extensive experimental validation, the theoretical contributions are substantial and the work addresses a challenging and important problem.  I suggest the authors focus on simplifying the presentation, providing more intuitive explanations of key concepts, and expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and comparisons to existing heuristics. Specifically, a discussion of the practical implications of the logarithmic factor would be beneficial. The overall quality and originality of the work justify acceptance despite these minor shortcomings.
