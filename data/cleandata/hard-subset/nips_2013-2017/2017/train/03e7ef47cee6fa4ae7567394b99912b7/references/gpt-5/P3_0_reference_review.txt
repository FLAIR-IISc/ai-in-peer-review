Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner has access to side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W, where each pairwise weight is drawn from f+ if the pair is in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. Both the number of clusters k and the distributions f+, f− are unknown to the algorithm. The learner can adaptively issue “same-cluster?” queries to an oracle (assumed correct). The paper provides:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds: Any (possibly randomized) algorithm that succeeds with nontrivial probability must use Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1), under H^2(f+‖f−) ≤ 1/18. The proof develops a framework for adaptive, interactive settings, leveraging Hellinger divergence and a careful reduction to multi-hypothesis testing.

- Algorithms: Two parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that do not require k, f+, or f−. A Monte Carlo algorithm achieves query complexity O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) with success probability 1 − o(1/n). A Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}). The key ideas are (i) using Hellinger divergence as a membership score between an element and a candidate cluster (comparing “inter” vs “intra” empirical distributions), and (ii) an iterative-update scheme that estimates p+ and p− on the fly and adjusts a size threshold before relying purely on side information. The analysis uses Sanov’s theorem and properties of Hellinger distance.

- Connections to SBM: The model generalizes SBM (Bernoulli p/q is a special case), and the lower bound suggests how active queries can shift recovery thresholds.

- Experiments: A small study on the Cora ER dataset shows that a greedy strategy guided by learned distributions achieves high precision/recall with ~10^3 queries, and is robust to approximate f+, f−.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant: Explains the gap between naive Ω(nk) query lower bounds and the empirical success of query-efficient, side-information–guided heuristics in ER/crowdsourcing.
- General model and parameter-free methods: Works for unknown k and unknown f+, f−; uses only W and pairwise queries.
- New lower-bound framework for adaptive querying: Goes beyond standard non-interactive Fano-based arguments; may be of independent interest.
- Use of Hellinger divergence is well-motivated: It is a metric (enabling triangle-type arguments) and yields clean dependence H−2(f+‖f−).
- Algorithms are conceptually simple and polynomial-time; complexity depends only logarithmically on n.
- Connections to SBM and a plausible conjecture about how queries shift sparse-regime thresholds.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Potential inconsistency between upper and lower bounds: The stated Monte Carlo upper bound O((k log n)/H^2) appears to beat the lower bound Ω(k^2/H^2) when nk is not the bottleneck, contradicting the claim of near-tightness (log factor). The Las Vegas bound O(n log n + (k^2 log n)/H^2) matches the lower bound up to a log n factor, but the Monte Carlo bound as written seems strictly better by a factor k. Please reconcile: is there a missing factor of k in the Monte Carlo bound, a different success criterion, or additional assumptions?
- Assumptions on f+, f−: Upper bounds assume discrete distributions with fixed support (or finite support scaling “linearly”), whereas lower bounds allow discrete/continuous. The algorithm relies on histogram-based estimation and Hellinger between empirical distributions. Please clarify precisely the allowable support size, minimal mass assumptions, and how the algorithm extends to continuous or large-alphabet settings (e.g., via binning) with explicit dependence on alphabet size.
- Proof sketching and constants: The lower bound proof employs TV ≤ sqrt(H(·‖·)) (with H used for Hellinger divergence), and then manipulates √H2 across products to get 2√a H. The presentation mixes Hellinger distance and squared Hellinger divergence, and constant factors are elided. While constants need not be optimal, the argument would benefit from a careful, consistent statement of which form (distance vs squared divergence) is used where.
- Robustness to early mistakes: The iterative-update algorithm estimates p+, p− from currently formed clusters; if early assignments are wrong, the estimates can be biased. While the analysis claims high-probability guarantees, the paper should make explicit how errors in Phase 1/2 are controlled and how error propagation is prevented.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: Only one dataset, no comparison with strong baselines (e.g., [40, 43] and follow-ups), and no ablation on the effect of distribution mismatch or k. Since practicality is a key motivation (ER), stronger empirical validation would help.
- Practical costs and scope: The method assumes access to the full n×n side-information matrix and runs in O(n^2) time, which may be prohibitive in large-scale ER. A discussion of blocking/sampling or sparse W would broaden applicability.
- Oracle noise is deferred: While the companion work is cited, a brief discussion of how the present techniques adapt to noisy oracle answers would improve the narrative.
- Clarity/typos: Many minor issues (e.g., “st Conference”, missing years/pages in references, inconsistent notation H vs H2, footnotes misplaced, ambiguous big-Oh expressions lacking parentheses). Algorithmic thresholds (e.g., the inequalities in Phase 3) are hard to parse.

Questions for the authors
1) Please reconcile the Monte Carlo upper bound vs the Ω(k^2/H^2) lower bound. Which success probability/expectation regime is each bound stated in? Is there a missing factor in the MC bound?
2) What precise conditions on f+, f− (alphabet size, minimal probabilities) are needed for the empirical Hellinger concentration and the Sanov-based arguments? How does the query complexity scale with alphabet size?
3) How do you guard against early misclusterings contaminating p+, p− estimates? Is there a formal invariant ensuring that the “grown” clusters used for estimation are sufficiently pure?
4) Can the approach handle sparse or on-the-fly side information (e.g., sampled W) without materializing O(n^2) entries?

Pros
- Novel and general theoretical framework; interesting use of Hellinger.
- Parameter-free, adaptive algorithms with nontrivial guarantees.
- Clear connection to SBM and active learning in ER.
- Potential to influence both theory and practice of interactive clustering.

Cons
- Apparent mismatch between one upper bound and lower bound.
- Assumptions and concentration statements need tightening.
- Limited empirical validation and clarity issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the Monte Carlo bound versus the lower bound and tightening assumptions/notation. The core contributions are significant and original, and the lower-bound framework for adaptive querying is valuable. My confidence is high.
