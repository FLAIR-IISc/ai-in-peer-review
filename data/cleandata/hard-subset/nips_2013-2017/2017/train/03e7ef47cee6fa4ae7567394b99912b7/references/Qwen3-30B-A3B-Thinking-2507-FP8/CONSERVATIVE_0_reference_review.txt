

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether pairs of points belong to the same cluster. The authors claim to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of this problem, establishing information-theoretic lower bounds and designing algorithms that nearly match these bounds.

The paper builds on previous work on interactive clustering (e.g., [40, 13, 42, 43]), but claims to be the first to provide a rigorous theoretical study with side information. The main contribution appears to be a framework for proving lower bounds and algorithms that reduce query complexity from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H^2(f+||f-))), where H is Hellinger divergence. The authors claim their algorithms are parameter-free (work without knowing f+, f-, or k).

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound in parts, but contains significant gaps. The lower bound proof (Section 2) relies on Lemma 1, which is claimed to prove the main result. However, the proof of Lemma 1 is problematic. The argument that "there exist at least ak/2 elements that are queried at most T times" assumes a uniform distribution of queries across elements, which is not necessarily true for adaptive algorithms. The subsequent analysis of "weak clusters" and the application of Hellinger distance to the joint distributions is not properly justified. The authors claim their algorithms match the lower bounds within a logarithmic factor, but the experimental results only show modest improvements (12 additional queries with approximate distributions), which is far from the theoretical claim of "drastic reduction."

**Clarity:** The paper is reasonably well-organized, but the theoretical sections are dense and difficult to follow. The proof of Lemma 1 is particularly opaque, with multiple jumps in logic that would require significant effort to verify. The experimental section is brief and lacks sufficient detail to assess the practical significance of the claims.

**Originality:** The problem of interactive clustering has been studied before, and the authors correctly cite relevant work. However, the claim that this is "the first rigorous theoretical study" with side information is overstated. Previous work on the stochastic block model (SBM) has addressed related problems, and the connection to SBM is well-established in the literature (e.g., [2, 15, 28]). While the authors generalize SBM to arbitrary distributions, this generalization doesn't appear to provide substantial new insights. The "iterative-update" algorithm is presented as novel, but it bears significant resemblance to existing techniques for parameter-free clustering.

**Significance:** The problem is relevant to applications like entity resolution, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate practical significance. The experimental results on the Cora dataset show only modest improvements (1136 vs. 962 queries for the greedy vs. best strategy), and the theoretical improvements don't translate to meaningful practical gains. The claim that the algorithms "drastically reduce" query complexity is misleading given the experimental results. Furthermore, the paper doesn't address how their approach compares to state-of-the-art heuristics in terms of both query complexity and clustering accuracy.

**Major Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical lower bound proof contains logical gaps that undermine the main contribution.
2. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claimed improvements.
3. The paper overstates the novelty of its contributions compared to existing work on SBM and interactive clustering.
4. The connection to practical applications is tenuous, as the experimental results don't demonstrate meaningful improvements over existing approaches.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem formulation is clear and well-motivated by applications like entity resolution.
2. The theoretical framework for analyzing query complexity with side information is conceptually interesting.
3. The paper correctly references relevant prior work.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting theoretical problem, but the claims of novelty and significance are not supported by the technical content or experimental results. The theoretical contributions are not sufficiently rigorous, and the practical implications are not convincingly demonstrated. While the problem is important, this paper fails to make a compelling case for its contribution to the field.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:** The problem is well-motivated, and the theoretical framework is conceptually appealing. The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering.

**Con Acceptance Arguments:** The theoretical claims are not rigorously supported, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the novelty is overstated. The paper does not demonstrate meaningful improvements over existing approaches.

Overall, while the problem is interesting, the paper falls short of NIPS standards for theoretical rigor and practical significance. The theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel or well-justified, and the experimental results do not validate the theoretical claims. I recommend rejection.
