This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on the benefit a similarity matrix provides when querying an oracle for cluster assignments. The problem is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labels is expensive, and the authors correctly point to the discrepancy between theoretical lower bounds (Ω(nk)) and empirical performance of heuristics. The paper aims to establish tighter theoretical bounds when side information is available and develops algorithms that approach these bounds.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical focus and attempt to rigorously analyze the impact of side information. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is noted, and the authors rightfully emphasize the generalization of their model beyond standard SBM assumptions. The proposed algorithms, both Monte Carlo and Las Vegas, are presented with complexity claims and attempt to match the derived lower bounds. The experimental evaluation on the Cora dataset, while limited, demonstrates performance consistent with the theoretical analysis.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses, primarily stemming from a lack of thorough empirical validation and a somewhat convoluted presentation. The theoretical analysis, while mathematically rigorous, feels detached from practical considerations. The assumption of arbitrary, unknown distributions f+ and f−, while providing generality, makes concrete evaluation challenging. The experimental section only presents results on a single dataset (Cora) and the comparison is primarily against itself – different configurations of the proposed algorithm. A crucial missing element is a comparison against established clustering algorithms *without* an oracle, and a demonstration of how much query reduction is achieved compared to a naive oracle-querying strategy (e.g., simply querying all pairs). The claim of significant query reduction is made without substantial evidence. The authors state that previous work "often ask significantly less queries than nk," but don't convincingly demonstrate *why* their approach achieves the same, or better, efficiency.

Furthermore, the presentation is dense and difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the algorithmic descriptions lack sufficient detail for easy implementation. The lengthy discussion of related work, while comprehensive, feels somewhat disconnected from the core contributions. The connection to SBM is mentioned but not deeply explored in terms of practical implications. The jump between theoretical results and practical application is weak. The Monte Carlo algorithm's iterative update procedure, while described, isn’t intuitively clear how it performs in various scenarios. The detailed explanation of the proofs in the appendix are appreciated, but reinforce the impression that the primary focus is on theoretical completeness rather than practical utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical investigation of query complexity with side information is a valuable contribution.
*   The formulation of the problem and the development of information-theoretic lower bounds are significant achievements.
*   The algorithms, despite limitations, provide a starting point for more efficient interactive clustering.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comprehensive empirical validation, particularly comparison to baseline clustering methods *without* an oracle, is a major weakness.
*   The paper is overly theoretical and lacks a strong connection to practical applications.
*   The presentation is dense and difficult to follow, hindering reproducibility.
*   The single dataset experiment provides insufficient evidence to support the claims of significant query reduction.
*   The algorithmic descriptions are lacking in detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution, but falls short of NIPS standards due to insufficient empirical validation and a lack of practical focus. The theoretical results are valuable, but the paper needs significantly more work to demonstrate their real-world impact. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation with comparisons to standard clustering algorithms, exploring a wider range of datasets, and simplifying the presentation to improve clarity. A more compelling demonstration of the practical benefits of the proposed approach is essential for consideration in future submissions.
