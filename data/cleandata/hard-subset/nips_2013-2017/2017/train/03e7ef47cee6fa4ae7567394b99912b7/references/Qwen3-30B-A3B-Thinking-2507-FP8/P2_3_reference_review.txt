

# Review: Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when provided with side information (a similarity matrix), which is a significant generalization of the standard clustering with an oracle model. The authors establish tight information-theoretic bounds on the number of queries required to perform exact clustering, demonstrating a dramatic reduction from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f₊||f₋))), where H is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions generating the side information for same-cluster and different-cluster pairs. The paper makes two major contributions: (1) a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering with side information, and (2) efficient parameter-free algorithms that nearly match these bounds.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for both the lower and upper bounds. The lower bound proof (Theorem 2) is particularly impressive, as it overcomes significant technical challenges in proving information-theoretic bounds for interactive learning problems. The authors cleverly adapt Sanov's theorem to the Hellinger divergence framework, which is crucial for their analysis. The algorithm design (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) is elegant and parameter-free, working without knowledge of f₊, f₋, or k. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate the practical relevance of their theoretical findings, showing that the greedy strategy closely matches the best possible performance with minimal queries. The paper properly acknowledges limitations (e.g., not considering noisy oracle answers, which is covered in a companion work) and provides adequate theoretical grounding for their claims.

## Clarity

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong introduction that effectively motivates the problem through the entity resolution application. The technical contributions are well-explained, though the lower bound proof may require careful reading due to its complexity. The algorithm description is detailed, though the iterative-update mechanism could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. The experimental section is clear and well-supported, with appropriate visualizations of the results. The paper adequately informs the reader about the problem setup, theoretical contributions, and practical implications.

## Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution by extending the clustering with oracle model to incorporate side information, which is a major step toward bridging the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical heuristics. The paper's main novelty lies in characterizing the precise effect of side information on query complexity, showing that the Hellinger divergence between f₊ and f₋ is the right measure for this effect. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) provides a meaningful theoretical bridge, and the authors demonstrate how their results generalize and extend previous SBM work. This is not just a minor variation of prior work but a substantial theoretical advance in the area of interactive clustering.

## Significance

The problem studied has direct relevance to practical applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced data labeling, where reducing the number of queries (i.e., human labeling efforts) is crucial. The paper provides theoretical justification for why heuristic methods that use side information (e.g., [40, 43]) perform significantly better than the O(nk) theoretical bound would suggest. The query complexity reduction from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f₊||f₋))) is substantial, especially for large n and small H²(f₊||f₋). The parameter-free nature of their algorithms makes them particularly valuable for real-world applications where the underlying distributions are unknown. The paper opens up new theoretical directions for studying query complexity in the presence of side information.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity with side information
- Strong information-theoretic lower bounds that match upper bounds within a logarithmic factor
- Parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f₊, f₋, or k
- Clear connection to practical applications (entity resolution) and theoretical models (SBM)
- Well-executed experimental results validating the theoretical claims

**Weaknesses:**
- Experimental results limited to a single dataset (Cora), though the results are compelling
- The paper assumes a generative model for side information that might not always hold in practice
- The connection to SBM could be explored in more depth (e.g., how their results change the known recovery thresholds)

## Recommendation

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information, with clear implications for both theory and practice. The technical quality is excellent, the results are significant, and the paper is well-written. While the experimental validation could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper successfully bridges a gap between theoretical guarantees and practical heuristics, demonstrating why side information enables dramatically reduced query complexity in real-world applications.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It represents a significant advance in the field of interactive learning, with theoretical depth that will be valuable to researchers and practical implications that will benefit practitioners working on entity resolution and crowdsourced data labeling. The paper's parameter-free algorithms are particularly valuable for real-world applications where the underlying distributions are unknown.
