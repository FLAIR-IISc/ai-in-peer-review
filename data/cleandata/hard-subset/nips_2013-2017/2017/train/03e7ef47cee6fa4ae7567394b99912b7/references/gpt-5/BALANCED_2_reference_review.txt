Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when the learner has access to pairwise “same-cluster?” queries to an oracle and a noisy similarity matrix W (“side information”). Entries of W are drawn i.i.d. from f+ if two points are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise; both f+ and f− (and k) are unknown. The authors ask: how many queries are necessary and sufficient to exactly recover the clustering, and how does side information change the answer?

Without side information, Θ(nk) queries are known to be necessary and sufficient, even adaptively. The paper shows that side information can dramatically reduce this number. The main contributions are:
- A general information-theoretic lower bound for adaptive algorithms (even with knowledge of k, f+, f−), scaling as Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}), and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success probability 1. The analysis introduces a framework for handling adaptive queries in this setting.
- Two parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that do not know k, f+, or f−: a Monte Carlo algorithm (exact recovery w.h.p.) and a Las Vegas algorithm (success prob. 1). They use an iterative estimation of the intra- and inter-distributions via Hellinger distance and a membership subroutine. The stated query complexities aim to match the lower bound up to log factors.
- A connection to SBM: their model strictly generalizes SBMs (and even some degree-corrected variants via arbitrary discrete f+, f−). They discuss how queries might shift SBM recovery thresholds.

The paper relates to interactive clustering and ER heuristics that use side information and crowdsourcing [Awasthi et al., Balcan & Blum, Vinayak & Hassibi; Firmani et al.; Wang et al.], to active learning for SBMs, and to the large literature on SBM thresholds and parameter-free recovery [Abbe et al.; Mossel et al.]. Unlike most prior work, the paper provides query-complexity results with general, unknown f+, f− and adversarial partitions.

Strengths:
- Novel lower-bound technique for adaptive querying with side information. Handling adaptivity cleanly is nontrivial; the reduction to a multi-hypothesis testing argument with Hellinger control is interesting and potentially reusable.
- Parameter-free algorithms that adaptively estimate f+, f− from side information and use an iterative thresholding scheme are appealing in practice and theoretically motivated by Sanov-type reasoning.
- The use of Hellinger distance (rather than KL), consistently in both bounds and algorithms, provides clean dependence on separation and supports parameter-free design.
- The model generalizes SBM and formalizes a practically relevant setting (ER with noisy similarities plus a human oracle).
- Claimed bounds suggest substantial savings over Θ(nk) when H^2(f+‖f−) is small but positive and/or when k is modest.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Notational and potential correctness inconsistencies in the main claims. The paper alternates between H and H^2 and has conflicting query-complexity dependences on k:
  • Upper bounds: in several places the Monte Carlo complexity is stated as O(k log n / H^2), but elsewhere it looks like O(k log n · H^2) or O(k/(log n · H^2)). The Las Vegas bound is given as O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}), which aligns with the lower bound.
  • Lower bound: Theorem 2 states Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}). If the Monte Carlo upper bound is O(k log n / H^2), the gap is a factor Θ(k), not a logarithmic factor. If instead the intended upper bound is O(k^2 log n / H^2), then it matches (up to log n) but contradicts earlier text. This inconsistency must be clarified, as it is central to the contribution.
- Assumptions on f+, f−. The algorithm and analysis assume discrete distributions with fixed support size q. The paper claims extensions to continuous or growing supports, but it is unclear how the algorithm (which relies on empirical histograms and Hellinger on a finite alphabet) adapts without binning or smoothing; finite-sample error bounds in those settings are not provided.
- Model idealization. The side-information entries are assumed i.i.d. conditional on the (possibly adversarial) partition, which may be a poor fit for many real similarity matrices that violate independence. Some discussion of robustness to model misspecification would help.
- Experimental evaluation is quite limited. One dataset (cora), one figure, and no comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., practical ER heuristics that use queries, Ashtiani et al.’s same-cluster queries without side info, or simple query strategies using W). There is no systematic study of how queries scale with k, H^2, or cluster-size imbalance, nor ablations of the iterative estimation. As a NeurIPS paper, stronger empirical support or synthetic experiments varying H^2 and k would bolster the claims.
- Clarity/presentation. There are numerous typos and equation formatting issues (e.g., missing parentheses around log n/H^2, switching between H and H^2, “Hellinger divergence” vs “square divergence”), some proofs are sketched with omitted constants, and several references are incomplete. The algorithm description would benefit from clear pseudocode, complexity accounting, and a crisp statement of the success and query bounds including all conditions.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: The lower-bound framework and the iterative, parameter-free algorithmic idea are technically interesting. However, the central complexity claims need to be made consistent and fully proved, and assumptions and finite-sample effects need clearer treatment.
- Clarity: The paper is overall readable but hampered by notation inconsistencies and missing details. Sections 3 and Theorem statements require careful editing to resolve contradictions.
- Originality: High. Interactive clustering with general side information, adaptive lower bounds, and parameter-free algorithms together seem new.
- Significance: Potentially significant, especially as a bridge between SBM theory, active querying, and practical ER. If the k-dependence is clarified and tight, the results could be influential.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Resolve the k vs k^2 discrepancy and standardize the use of H vs H^2 throughout. State the exact Monte Carlo and Las Vegas bounds cleanly (with min{·} forms, log factors, and success probabilities).
- Clearly specify the assumptions on f+, f− (support size, discreteness) in both theorems and the algorithm, and either restrict the claims accordingly or add an explicit extension (e.g., finite partitioning of the range, KDE) with finite-sample error bounds.
- Provide full pseudocode for the iterative-update algorithm and a precise accounting of the number of queries and runtime as a function of n, k, and H^2.
- Expand experiments: include synthetic studies sweeping H^2 and k, comparisons to baselines (query-all-against-current-clusters; heuristics from ER literature), and sensitivity to mis-specification of f+, f−.
- Discuss robustness to mild oracle errors (even if full treatment is deferred to the companion paper), and to deviations from the independence assumption for W.
- Improve presentation and references; fix typos and incomplete citations.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper tackles an important and timely problem with promising ideas and a potentially impactful lower-bound framework. However, the inconsistency in the main complexity claims, limited empirical support, and clarity issues make it premature for acceptance. With clarified bounds, tightened analysis, and stronger experiments, this could become a strong submission.

Confidence: Moderate.
