

# Review for "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering problems with the help of an oracle, where the goal is to determine the minimum number of pairwise queries needed to cluster n points into k clusters. The key novelty is the introduction of "side information" – a similarity matrix generated from the data (e.g., via Jaccard similarity), which the clustering algorithm can use to guide its queries. The authors show that with side information, the query complexity can be drastically reduced from the Ω(nk) lower bound without side information to O(k / (log n H²(f₊‖f₋))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarities within clusters (f₊) and between clusters (f₋).

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering algorithms, and (2) efficient parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that nearly match these lower bounds. The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that it's a special case of their model, and opens up new directions for studying how query complexity affects SBM recovery thresholds.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis with clearly stated theorems and proofs. The lower bound (Theorem 2) is particularly impressive, as it requires developing a new framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in the interactive setting – a challenging problem since standard techniques like Fano's inequality don't apply to adaptive query algorithms.

The upper bound algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) are described in detail, with a clear explanation of the iterative-update method that allows them to work without knowing f₊, f₋, or k. The analysis of the algorithms (Section 3 and Appendix A) carefully establishes the query complexity bounds.

The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide empirical validation of the theoretical claims, showing that the greedy strategy (which would be used in practice) performs very close to the optimal strategy. However, the experimental section is somewhat limited, only using one dataset with 124 clusters out of 1812 nodes, and could have included more detailed analysis of how the Hellinger divergence affects performance.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, explains the connection to entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications, and situates the work within the broader literature. The technical sections are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor.

However, the paper could benefit from some improvements in clarity:
- Some technical terms (like the Hellinger divergence) could be briefly explained when first introduced.
- The lower bound proof (Section 2) is quite dense and may be challenging for readers without strong information theory background.
- The experimental section could provide more details on parameter choices and how they relate to the theoretical guarantees.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on interactive clustering. Previous work on clustering with oracles (e.g., [42, 43, 40]) focused on heuristic algorithms without theoretical guarantees. The authors are the first to provide rigorous theoretical bounds for clustering with side information, showing a clear separation in query complexity between with and without side information.

The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is particularly novel – the authors show that their model generalizes SBM, allowing for arbitrary distributions f₊ and f₋ (not just Bernoulli), and without requiring assumptions about k or cluster size distribution. The framework for proving lower bounds in the interactive setting is also a novel technical contribution that could be applied to other interactive learning problems.

This work is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on clustering with side information, which mostly focused on heuristics without theoretical guarantees.

## Significance

The problem addressed has significant practical importance in modern machine learning applications where obtaining labeled data is expensive, such as entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering. The theoretical results provide a precise characterization of how side information can reduce the number of queries needed, which directly translates to cost savings in real-world applications.

The paper's connection to the stochastic block model and its implications for understanding how query complexity affects recovery thresholds in SBM is of broad theoretical interest. The authors' framework for proving lower bounds could also be applied to other interactive learning problems, making the paper's contributions potentially far-reaching.

The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing that the greedy strategy (which would be used in practice) performs very close to optimal. This demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

## Strengths

- Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of clustering with side information, showing a significant separation in query complexity compared to no side information.
- Develops a novel framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering algorithms.
- Creates parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f₊, f₋, or k, which is crucial for practical applications.
- Connects to the well-studied stochastic block model, generalizing it to a more flexible framework.
- Demonstrates practical relevance through experimental results on a real-world dataset.

## Weaknesses

- The experimental section is somewhat limited, using only one dataset with a relatively small number of clusters.
- The lower bound proof is quite technical and dense, which may make it challenging for some readers to follow without substantial background in information theory.
- The paper doesn't provide a concrete example of how to compute the Hellinger divergence H²(f₊‖f₋) in practice, which would help readers understand how to apply the theoretical results.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of interactive clustering. The authors provide rigorous bounds that are both novel and practically relevant, addressing a problem of clear importance in modern machine learning applications. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and connects to important existing work in the stochastic block model literature.

While the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to make this a valuable addition to the field. The paper meets the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, and I believe it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and significant, and the paper is well-written with clear contributions that advance the state of the art in interactive clustering. The connection to the stochastic block model and the development of a new lower bound framework are particularly noteworthy contributions that will likely be of interest to the NIPS community.
