

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the clustering algorithm has access to a similarity matrix W that provides noisy side information about whether pairs of points belong to the same cluster. The key innovation is the formalization of this problem in a general setting where the similarity distributions f+ (for same-cluster pairs) and f- (for different-cluster pairs) are arbitrary and unknown. The authors establish a clean theoretical framework that characterizes how side information reduces the query complexity of clustering from the standard O(nk) (without side information) to O(k/(log n H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence.

The paper makes important connections to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that their framework generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- (rather than just Bernoulli distributions) and without assuming knowledge of k or cluster size distributions. The authors provide both strong information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorem 2) and matching upper bounds (Theorem 1) for their problem, demonstrating a near-optimal algorithm that is completely parameter-free (works without knowing f+, f-, or k).

This work extends previous studies of clustering with interactive queries (e.g., [7, 6, 5]) by incorporating side information, which is a critical practical consideration in real-world applications like entity resolution. The paper addresses a gap in the literature where theoretical guarantees were lacking for heuristic approaches that use side information to reduce query complexity.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis of the problem, establishing tight information-theoretic lower bounds that match the upper bounds within a logarithmic factor. This is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering.

2. **Novel Framework**: The authors introduce a general framework for analyzing clustering with side information that goes beyond the stochastic block model. Their model's generality (allowing arbitrary f+ and f- without assumptions on k or cluster sizes) makes it applicable to a wide range of practical scenarios.

3. **Parameter-Free Algorithms**: The proposed algorithms are completely parameter-free (work without knowing f+, f-, or k), which is a major practical advantage. This is particularly valuable in real-world applications where these parameters are often unknown.

4. **Rigorous Analysis**: The proof of the lower bound (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is technically sophisticated, overcoming challenges in the interactive setting by developing a new framework for information-theoretic lower bounds. The proof technique, which extends Sanov's theorem to the Hellinger distance, is elegant and could be useful in other contexts.

5. **Experiments**: The experimental results on the Cora dataset validate the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithms, particularly showing that approximate distributions (rather than exact ones) are sufficient for good performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Comparison**: While the experiments on the Cora dataset are relevant, they don't compare the proposed algorithms with the heuristic approaches mentioned in the introduction (e.g., [42, 43, 40]). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper states that the algorithms are computationally efficient with time complexity O(n²), but it doesn't provide details about how the algorithms would scale to larger datasets. For instance, the time complexity of the iterative update phase could potentially be improved.

3. **Assumption on Side Information**: The paper assumes the side information matrix W is given as input, but doesn't discuss how to generate this side information in practice. The paper references [18, 33] for the generation of side information, but these references aren't thoroughly explained in the context of the paper.

4. **Constants in the Bounds**: The paper mentions that the constants in the lower and upper bounds aren't optimized. While this is acknowledged, it would be helpful to provide a discussion of how the constants might affect the practical applicability of the results.

5. **Connection to SBM**: The connection to the stochastic block model is promising, but the paper doesn't fully explore how their results change the understanding of the SBM recovery threshold. The conjecture in the discussion section about how the recovery threshold changes with queries is interesting but not proven.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The claims are well-supported by both the lower and upper bound proofs, and the experimental results provide empirical validation. The authors are careful to discuss both the strengths and limitations of their approach (e.g., noting that the constants in the bounds aren't optimized).

The paper is a complete piece of work, not merely a position paper. It addresses a well-defined problem with a clear theoretical contribution and practical validation. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work (e.g., the assumption of a noiseless oracle, the focus on side information without error handling), which demonstrates honesty in their evaluation.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and explains the significance of the side information. The technical sections are logically structured, with the proof of the lower bound and the algorithm design presented in a clear sequence.

However, some parts of the paper could be improved for clarity:

1. The explanation of the "weak clusters" in the lower bound proof (Step 2) is somewhat dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation or a small illustrative example.

2. The description of the Membership subroutine could be clearer with a simple example showing how the Hellinger divergence is computed and used.

3. The experimental section would be clearer with a more detailed description of the "best," "worst," and "greedy" strategies.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a significant original contribution by formalizing the query complexity of clustering with side information in a general framework. While there have been previous works on clustering with interactive queries (e.g., [5, 7, 6]), this paper is the first to establish tight bounds for the general case with side information. The connection to SBM is a novel way to understand the problem's relationship to existing literature.

The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous contributions by emphasizing the generality of their model (arbitrary f+ and f-, unknown k) and the parameter-free nature of their algorithms. The related work section is comprehensive and properly references the relevant literature.

## Significance Assessment

The results have significant theoretical and practical importance. Theoretically, they provide a clean characterization of how side information affects query complexity, which is a fundamental question in interactive learning. Practically, the results explain why heuristic approaches that use side information (like those in [42, 43, 40]) perform better than the theoretical O(nk) bound without side information.

The work has the potential to influence future research in interactive clustering, active learning, and crowdsourcing. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms makes them particularly valuable for practitioners who don't have access to the exact distributions f+ and f-.

The results advance the state of the art in clustering with interactive queries by providing tight theoretical guarantees that match practical observations. The paper provides a unique theoretical approach to understanding the role of side information in interactive clustering.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is strong, the results are well-supported, and the work addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The paper's main weakness is the limited empirical comparison with existing heuristic approaches, but this doesn't detract from the theoretical significance of the work. The experimental results on the Cora dataset are sufficient to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to a problem with clear practical applications in machine learning and data mining.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a more detailed comparison with heuristic approaches (e.g., [42, 43, 40]) in the experimental section to better demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms.

2. Clarify the relationship between the side information generation (how W is obtained) and the practical implementation of the algorithms.

3. Provide a more detailed discussion of the constants in the bounds and their practical implications.

4. Consider adding a small illustrative example to explain the Membership subroutine and the Hellinger divergence calculation.

5. In the discussion section, elaborate on how the results change the understanding of the SBM recovery threshold, perhaps with a more concrete example.

## Final Summary

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of interactive clustering with side information. It establishes tight bounds on query complexity and provides efficient parameter-free algorithms that match these bounds within a logarithmic factor. The work is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, as it combines theoretical rigor with practical insights for a problem with significant applications in machine learning and data mining.
