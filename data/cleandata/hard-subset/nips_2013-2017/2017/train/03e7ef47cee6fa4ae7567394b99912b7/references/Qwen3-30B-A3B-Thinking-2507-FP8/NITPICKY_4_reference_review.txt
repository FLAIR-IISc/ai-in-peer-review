

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary
This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, where an oracle provides pairwise cluster membership information, and a similarity matrix serves as side information. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and propose parameter-free algorithms that nearly match these bounds. The key contribution is showing that side information can drastically reduce query complexity from O(nk) to O(k/(log n H^2(f+||f-))), where H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence. The paper connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) and claims to be the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem at the intersection of active learning and clustering. The theoretical results are novel and provide a clean separation between clustering with and without side information. The connection to SBM is well-motivated, and the proposed algorithms are computationally efficient. The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide empirical validation of the approach.

## Weaknesses
Despite the significant theoretical contributions, the paper contains numerous critical formatting, notational, and technical issues that undermine its scientific quality:

1. **Critical Mathematical Typo**: The paper repeatedly states "H^2(f+||f-) ≤ 118" (e.g., in Theorem 2, Proof of Theorem 2), which is clearly a typo for "H^2(f+||f-) ≤ 1/18." This is a severe error that would confuse readers and invalidate the lower bound analysis.

2. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for mathematical expressions. For example, "H2(f+‖f−)" should be "H^2(f+||f-)" or "H_2(f+||f-)" to indicate squared Hellinger divergence. The paper also writes "k 2 H2(f+‖f−)" instead of "k^2 H^2(f+||f-)".

3. **Formatting and Typos**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors, including "st Conference" (should be "The 31st Conference"), missing spaces before "however" after em dashes, and inconsistent use of "Hellinger divergence" vs. "Hellinger distance."

4. **Experimental Inconsistency**: The paper states "1879 nodes, 191 clusters" but later claims "the final number of vertices that we classify is 1812 with 124 clusters" without explaining the discrepancy. This inconsistency undermines the experimental results.

5. **Incomplete Proof**: The lower bound proof (Lemma 1) has a critical flaw in the calculation: "there exist at least ak/2 elements that are queried at most T < 4Q/ak times." The math here is incorrect; the average number of queries per element is 2Q/n = 2Q/(ka), so the calculation of the number of elements with few queries is flawed.

6. **Algorithmic Details**: The paper claims algorithms are "parameter-free" but uses a constant C that must be chosen, with no guidance on how to select it. The convergence analysis is also incomplete, claiming "the process converges" without providing a formal proof.

7. **Missing Figure**: The experimental section refers to "Figure 2" but the figure is not provided in the text.

8. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "Hellinger square divergence" when it should say "squared Hellinger divergence" or "Hellinger divergence squared."

9. **Theoretical Gap**: The paper claims to handle "arbitrary" f+ and f- but doesn't address the case where f+ = f- (in which case no queries are needed), which is a fundamental edge case.

10. **Reference to Missing Appendix**: The paper states "We have analyzed this case in Appendix C" but Appendix C is not included in the provided text.

## Recommendation
While the paper's theoretical contribution is potentially significant, the numerous formatting errors, mathematical typos, and incomplete proofs make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The critical typo "H^2(f+||f-) ≤ 118" (should be 1/18) is particularly problematic as it fundamentally affects the lower bound analysis. Additionally, the experimental results section contains inconsistencies that undermine the empirical validation.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I must note that these are not minor issues but rather fundamental errors that would require substantial revision. The paper's quality as a scientific contribution is significantly compromised by these issues, and I recommend rejection.

## Pro/Con Acceptance
**Pro**: The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical results are novel, and the connection to SBM is strong.

**Con**: Critical mathematical errors (notably the H^2 ≤ 118 typo), inconsistent notation, experimental inconsistencies, incomplete proofs, and missing figures. The paper does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.

The paper has significant potential but requires substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it can be considered for publication.
