This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on scenarios where an oracle provides pairwise cluster membership. The authors establish both lower and upper bounds on the number of queries needed, demonstrating a significant reduction in complexity when side information (a similarity matrix) is available compared to clustering without it. The work is motivated by practical applications like entity resolution, where human-in-the-loop approaches benefit from minimizing the number of queries to experts.

The paper builds upon the classical query complexity model and connects it to the stochastic block model (SBM), framing side information as a generalization of SBM's connectivity patterns.  The authors highlight a gap between theoretical bounds (O(nk)) and the observed performance of heuristics in practice, motivating their investigation. They develop algorithms with complexity O(k log n H2(f+||f−)), where H2 is the Hellinger divergence between distributions governing intra- and inter-cluster similarities, and prove a matching lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k²H2(f+||f−)}).  The contribution lies in providing rigorous theoretical guarantees for interactive clustering *with* side information, a setting previously lacking such analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a sound theoretical foundation for understanding the impact of side information on query complexity.  The lower bound is particularly strong, and the matching upper bound demonstrates the tightness of the results.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem of clustering with an oracle and side information is well-defined, and its relevance to practical applications is convincingly argued.
* **Connection to SBM:** Establishing a connection to the well-studied SBM provides context and allows for leveraging existing techniques and insights.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The algorithms developed do not require knowledge of the underlying distributions or the number of clusters, making them potentially more practical.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments on the Cora dataset demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach, and the performance comparison with different strategies is informative.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and technically challenging.  While thorough, the numerous lemmas and theorems can make it difficult to follow the main line of argument.  More intuitive explanations of the key ideas could improve accessibility.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Cora).  Results on diverse datasets with varying characteristics would strengthen the empirical validation.  A comparison with existing heuristics beyond the strategies the authors themselves tested would also be beneficial.
* **Hellinger Divergence Assumption:** While the Hellinger divergence is a natural measure of distributional difference, its impact on the query complexity is not always intuitive. A more detailed discussion on the practical implications of different Hellinger divergence values would be helpful.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical guarantees. A discussion of the computational cost of calculating the Hellinger divergence and implementing the algorithms in practice would be valuable.
* **Minor Typographical Errors:** There are a few minor typos throughout the paper that distract from the overall presentation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in clustering with significant theoretical and practical implications.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and provide valuable insights into the role of side information.
* The algorithms developed are parameter-free and achieve near-optimal query complexity.
* The connection to the SBM provides a strong foundation for further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and technical complexity may limit its accessibility to a wider audience.
* The experimental evaluation is limited, and a more comprehensive assessment of practical performance is needed.
* While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications of the Hellinger divergence parameter are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of clustering with side information. The rigorous analysis and the development of near-optimal algorithms are commendable. While the paper could benefit from improved accessibility and more extensive experimental evaluation, its theoretical novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental results.
