This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining full pairwise comparisons is expensive and leveraging existing similarity measures can reduce the number of necessary oracle queries. The authors establish both lower and upper bounds on the query complexity, demonstrating a significant reduction in queries achievable with side information compared to clustering without it. The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) and provides parameter-free algorithms.

**Summary of Contributions:** The core contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating that side information (a similarity matrix) can drastically reduce the query complexity of clustering.  They provide information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, establishing a clear relationship between the quality of the side information (measured by Hellinger divergence) and the required number of queries. They also develop practical, parameter-free algorithms that achieve these bounds.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper offers a solid theoretical framework for analyzing the query complexity of clustering with side information.  The lower bound is particularly noteworthy as it rigorously demonstrates the potential benefits of using side information.
* **Connection to SBM:**  The framing of the problem as a generalization of the stochastic block model provides a valuable connection to a well-studied area, and allows for leveraging existing insights and techniques.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do *not* require knowledge of the underlying distributions or the number of clusters is a significant practical advantage. This makes the algorithms more widely applicable in real-world scenarios.
* **Clear Motivation:**  The motivation from entity resolution is clearly articulated and effectively highlights the practical relevance of the research.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results on a real dataset provides empirical support for the theoretical findings and demonstrates the performance of the proposed algorithms.  Showing that performance remains consistent with approximate distributions is a useful addition.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear definitions and explanations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, particularly for the lower bound, appear quite complex and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with information theory to fully grasp. While the outline is provided, the detail in the main body is limited, pushing much of the rigorous detail into the appendix.
* **Assumptions of the Generative Model:** The assumption of a noisy weighted upper-triangular similarity matrix, while reasonable, is still a simplification. Real-world similarity measures are often more complex and may not perfectly fit this model. Exploring the robustness of the results to deviations from this model would be valuable.
* **Practical Considerations:** The algorithms, while theoretically sound, may have practical limitations in terms of computational cost, especially for very large datasets.  Discussing these limitations and potential optimizations would strengthen the paper.  The running time of O(n^2) is mentioned, but a more detailed complexity analysis with respect to the size of the support (q) of the distributions would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Existing Heuristics:** While the introduction mentions existing heuristics used in entity resolution, the experimental comparison is focused primarily on validating the theoretical results. A more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art heuristics on a wider range of datasets would provide a stronger assessment of the practical benefits of the proposed algorithms.
* **Missing Related Work:** The paper mentions some related work but might benefit from a more comprehensive review of the literature on active learning and crowdsourcing for clustering, specifically discussing how this work builds upon or differs from existing approaches in these areas.  For example, connection to the work of [5] is a bit brief given the similarities.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of a practically relevant problem.
* The results provide significant insights into the potential benefits of using side information for clustering.
* The proposed algorithms are parameter-free and achieve near-optimal query complexity.
* The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proofs may make the paper inaccessible to some readers.
* The assumptions of the generative model may limit the applicability of the results.
* The practical limitations of the algorithms are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of clustering with side information.  The rigorous analysis, novel results, and practical algorithms make it a valuable addition to the literature. While the complexity of the proofs and potential practical limitations are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**  Accept with minor revisions.  The authors should consider:

1.  **Providing more intuition behind the proofs:**  Adding more high-level explanations of the key ideas in the proofs, perhaps in the main body of the paper, would make the results more accessible.
2. **Discussing practical limitations:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the algorithms and potential optimization strategies would be helpful.
3. **Expanding the experimental evaluation:**  A more comprehensive comparison to existing heuristics on a wider range of datasets would strengthen the empirical validation.
4. **Elaborating on related work:** A more thorough review of related work on active learning and crowdsourcing would provide valuable context.
