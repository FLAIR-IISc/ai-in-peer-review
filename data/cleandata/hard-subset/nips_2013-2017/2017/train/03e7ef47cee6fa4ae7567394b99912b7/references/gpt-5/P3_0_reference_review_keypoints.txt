- Paper studies query complexity of exact clustering with noisy pairwise side-info W, unknown k and f+/f−, and adaptive same-cluster queries to a perfect oracle.

- Main lower bounds: any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for certainty), proved via an adaptive, interactive Hellinger-divergence reduction.

- Algorithms: two parameter-free polynomial-time methods — Monte Carlo: O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries w.h.p.; Las Vegas: O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) expected — using Hellinger-based membership scores and iterative estimation.

- Key technical tools: Hellinger divergence for membership scoring, Sanov’s theorem for concentration, and on-the-fly estimation of p+/p− with iterative thresholding.

- Model generalizes SBM (Bernoulli p/q case) and suggests how active queries shift recovery thresholds in sparse regimes.

- Small experiments on Cora ER show a greedy, distribution-guided strategy attains high precision/recall with ~10^3 queries and is robust to approximate f+/f−.

- Strength: timely and relevant explanation bridging naive Ω(nk) bounds and practical query-efficient heuristics in ER/crowdsourcing.

- Strength: general, parameter-free methods that do not require knowing k or f+/f− and use only W plus queries.

- Strength: novel lower-bound framework for adaptive querying that goes beyond non-interactive Fano arguments.

- Strength: well-motivated use of Hellinger distance yielding clean H−2 dependence.

- Strength: algorithms are conceptually simple, polynomial-time, and logarithmic in n dependence.

- Strength: clear connections to SBM and plausible conjectures about query effects in sparse regimes.

- Weakness: apparent inconsistency — Monte Carlo upper bound O((k log n)/H^2) seems to contradict lower bound Ω(k^2/H^2) when nk not dominant; needs reconciliation.

- Weakness: upper bounds assume discrete/fixed finite support for f+/f− while lower bounds allow continuous; need precise support-size and minimal-mass assumptions and dependence on alphabet size.

- Weakness: presentation mixes Hellinger vs squared Hellinger and elides constants; clarity needed on which form is used where.

- Weakness: robustness concerns — iterative estimation may be biased by early misclusterings; more explicit control of error propagation and purity invariants required.

- Weakness: limited experiments — single dataset, no strong baselines or ablations on distribution mismatch or varying k.

- Weakness: practical costs — assumes full n×n W and O(n^2) time; discussion of blocking/sampling or sparse W needed for large-scale ER.

- Weakness: oracle noise deferred to companion work; brief treatment of adaptation to noisy oracle answers would improve the paper.

- Weakness: clarity and typos — inconsistent notation (H vs H2), misplaced footnotes, ambiguous big-Oh expressions, hard-to-parse algorithmic thresholds.

- Key question 1: reconcile Monte Carlo upper bound vs Ω(k^2/H^2) lower bound — differences in success criteria, missing factors, or assumptions?

- Key question 2: specify precise conditions on f+/f− (alphabet size, minimal probabilities) and how query complexity scales with alphabet/support size.

- Key question 3: how are early misclusterings prevented from contaminating p+/p− estimates — is there a formal purity invariant?

- Key question 4: can the approach handle sparse or on-the-fly side information (sampled W) without materializing O(n^2) entries?

- Pros: novel theoretical framework, parameter-free adaptive algorithms, strong Hellinger-based analysis, relevance to SBM and active ER.

- Cons: mismatch between one upper bound and lower bound, unclear distributional assumptions/concentration bounds, limited empirical validation and clarity issues.

- Recommendation: weak accept conditioned on clarifying the Monte Carlo vs lower bound discrepancy and tightening assumptions/notation; reviewer confident in core contributions.
