Summary
The paper studies the fundamental query complexity of clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries in the presence of side information: a noisy similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ if two items share a cluster and from f− otherwise. The algorithm does not know k, f+, or f−. The main contributions are (i) an information-theoretic lower bound that captures the effect of side information via the squared Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−), and (ii) parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) whose query complexity nearly matches the lower bound up to logarithmic factors. The work connects to stochastic block models (SBM) as a special case and claims to generalize several prior heuristic approaches used in entity resolution.

Strengths
- Novel and timely formalization of interactive clustering with side information, bridging a gap between heuristic practice and worst-case theory.
- A clear quantification of how side information reduces query complexity via H2(f+‖f−); the qualitative dependence is compelling and aligns with intuition.
- Lower bound technique addresses adaptivity of queries, which is notoriously challenging for Fano-type arguments; the construction around “lightly queried” elements and weak clusters is interesting and potentially reusable.
- Parameter-free algorithms that do not require k, f+, or f− are practically attractive; the iterative-update strategy to learn effective thresholds from data is a nice idea.
- Connection to SBM and discussion of how active queries might shift recovery thresholds is thought-provoking.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Apparent inconsistencies in the stated bounds, especially the dependence on k:
  - Early text: O(k/(log n H2)) vs Theorem 1: O(min{nk, (k log n)/H2}) vs Las Vegas: O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2}) vs lower bound: Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}). As written, the Monte Carlo bound O((k log n)/H2) contradicts the lower bound Ω(k^2/H2) for high-probability recovery when k is not tiny. My guess is that Theorem 1 should also read min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2} (or there is an unstated regime assumption), but this must be clarified. The current mismatch undermines the “matches within a log n factor” claim.
  - There are also typographical ambiguities like O(k/(log n H2(·))) vs O((k log n)/H2(·)).
- The lower bound proof sketch uses several loose steps (e.g., Markov bound for E2, somewhat ad hoc selection of β, rough constants). This is acceptable for readability, but given the centrality of the result, please make the statement and regimes precise, including the role of a, k, and H2, and ensure that the extension from equal-size clusters to arbitrary partitions is formalized.
- Sanov’s theorem is a KL-based large deviations result; the analysis claims to “extend” it and then measures deviations in Hellinger because it is a metric. The paper should clearly state how KL-based rate control is converted to uniform Hellinger deviations in the specific finite-alphabet setting, with precise constants (currently deferred and hard to verify).
- Modeling assumptions: W’s entries are independent given the clustering. This is stronger than many real-world similarity constructions (correlations across pairs sharing an item). Some discussion of robustness to violations (or a counterexample) would help.
- Experimental evaluation is minimal:
  - One dataset (cora), no comparison to prior active/crowd ER heuristics beyond the artificial “best/worst/greedy” strategies, and no ablations on k, H2, or size. The greedy strategy essentially operationalizes the proposed idea, but the lack of baselines and broader datasets weakens the empirical case.
  - The paper seems to “compute f+ and f−” from the dataset—how are these obtained without knowing the true clustering (which is the target)? If ground-truth labels are used to estimate f±, is there risk of data leakage in evaluating clustering performance on the same dataset?
- Clarity and presentation issues:
  - Several typos and missing/ambiguous parentheses in bounds; some references are incomplete.
  - Switching between H and H2 leads to confusion; please standardize on H2 throughout and keep track of constants.
  - The algorithm description is dense; a high-level pseudocode, a figure illustrating the iterative-update phases, and a summary of the main invariants would improve readability.

Quality
The technical direction is strong and the results are plausible and valuable. The lower bound approach to handle adaptivity, and the parameter-free iterative algorithmic design, are meaningful contributions. However, the paper must resolve the k vs k^2 discrepancy in the bounds and provide a cleaner, unambiguous statement of all theorems with consistent notation. The experimental section is not commensurate with the theoretical ambition.

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first to give information-theoretic lower bounds and matching (up to logs) parameter-free algorithms for interactive clustering with side information under unknown f± and k, and to tie the complexity to Hellinger divergence. The treatment of adaptivity in lower bounds is novel in this context.

Significance
High theoretical significance; if clarified, the results could become a reference point for active clustering and ER with side information, and stimulate work on active SBM. Practical impact will depend on robustness of assumptions and scalability beyond O(n^2) access to W.

Questions for the authors
1. Please reconcile Theorem 1 and Theorem 2: what is the correct dependence on k in the upper bound? Does the Monte Carlo algorithm achieve min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H2} or min{nk, (k log n)/H2}? In which regimes?
2. How exactly do you leverage Sanov (KL) to obtain Hellinger-based confidence intervals? A brief lemma translating KL to Hellinger in your finite-support setting would help.
3. Do your algorithmic guarantees extend to continuous f±? The lower bound says yes; for the algorithm, what replaces histogram-based estimates p+, p−?
4. How sensitive is the method to dependence in W (e.g., correlations across pairs sharing a node)?
5. In experiments, how are f± estimated and evaluated without leaking label information? Can you add comparisons to prior crowd/active ER methods?

Minor comments
- Clean up notation and parentheses in all O(·) expressions; standardize on H2.
- Provide pseudocode and a complexity summary table.
- Several references miss years/pages; please complete.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical contributions are compelling and appear to be new and significant, but the paper has presentation issues and inconsistencies in the stated bounds that must be resolved. Strengthening clarity and adding a more thorough empirical comparison would greatly improve the paper.
