Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to (i) a noiseless pairwise oracle indicating whether two items are in the same cluster, and (ii) “side information” in the form of a similarity matrix W whose entries are generated from f+ if two items are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The number of clusters k and the distributions f+, f− are unknown. The main goals are to quantify the value of side information in reducing queries and to design parameter-free algorithms with nearly optimal query complexity.

Contributions:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds: An interactive, adaptive lower bound that applies even to randomized algorithms with knowledge of k, f+, f−. The paper shows any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) queries to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success probability 1, in the regime H^2 ≤ 1/18. This extends beyond SBM to arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− (discrete or continuous) and adversarial cluster sizes. The proof develops a reduction to a k-hypothesis testing problem and carefully controls what information can be gained adaptively.
- Upper bounds (algorithms): Two efficient parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not require k, f+, or f−. The Monte Carlo algorithm achieves query complexity O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2(f+||f−)}) and succeeds with probability 1 − o(1/n). The Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries and is always correct (with the stated query complexity holding with high probability). Both use an iterative-update procedure that learns empirical “intra” and “inter” distributions and employs Hellinger divergence to decide whether side information is sufficiently reliable to avoid further queries.
- Relation to SBM: The side-information model strictly generalizes SBM; the paper discusses how queries might shift SBM recovery thresholds.

The paper connects to prior heuristic work on crowdsourced entity resolution and interactive clustering [40, 42, 43] and to theoretical work on SBM thresholds [1,2,36], active querying in SBM [19], and crowdsourcing reliability [29, 39], while providing the first near-tight query complexity guarantees with unknown f+, f−.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The overall approach is sound and the orders of the lower and upper bounds match up to logarithmic factors. The lower bound argument for adaptive querying is a genuine contribution, and the algorithmic framework appears plausible. However, there are several notational and presentation issues that should be fixed:
  - Lemma 1’s stated “probability of error is at least − … − …” is clearly missing a leading “1 − (…);” later use in Theorem 2 confirms that the intended bound is 1 minus those terms.
  - The inequality relating total variation and Hellinger divergences in Step 4 should include the correct constant (e.g., ||P − Q||TV ≤ √2 H(P,Q) depending on the exact definition), whereas the text uses √H(P||Q) without constants; please reconcile definitions (H vs H^2) and constants.
  - The definition MEx = C log n H(p+,x||p−,x)^2 appears to be missing a division; later analysis and the claimed thresholds imply MEx should be C log n / H^2(p+,x||p−,x).
  - Success probability statements for Las Vegas vs Monte Carlo should be made precise: “Las Vegas” usually guarantees correctness with probability 1 and random runtime; the paper states the algorithm “recovers with probability 1” and has the stated query complexity with probability 1 − o(1/n).
- Completeness: The algorithm description is high-level; more precise pseudo-code and stopping criteria would help reproducibility. The adaptation of Sanov’s theorem to control Hellinger-distance estimation is only sketched; while plausible, a careful lemma with conditions (e.g., fixed finite support size) would strengthen the presentation.
- Experiments: Limited. One real dataset (cora) is used; results compare “best/worst/greedy” strategies rather than against existing baselines [40, 43, 18]. No synthetic experiments to validate scaling with H^2, k, and n, or to probe robustness.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear motivation (ER), problem statement, and the high-level ideas behind both the lower bound and the iterative-update algorithm.
- Nonetheless, there are many typographical and notation inconsistencies (examples above; also occasional missing years in references, and minor grammar). Given the theoretical nature, crisp definitions (H vs H^2, TV–H constants, conditions on support size q) and a self-contained algorithm box would substantially improve clarity.
- The “parameter-free” claim is compelling; please state explicitly the assumptions under which the empirical Hellinger estimates are accurate (e.g., fixed q, bounded away from zero probabilities), and discuss behavior if q grows or distributions have continuous support.

Originality
- The interactive lower bound framework for clustering with side information appears novel and addresses the key obstacle that adaptive queries complicate standard Fano-type arguments.
- The parameter-free, Hellinger-based iterative-update design is also original and of potential independent interest.
- The generality beyond SBM (unknown f+, f−; arbitrary cluster sizes; unknown k) is a strength.

Significance
- The work substantially advances our theoretical understanding of the query complexity of clustering with side information, nearly closing the gap with matching upper/lower bounds up to log factors.
- The techniques (both lower bound framework and algorithmic recipe) seem broadly applicable to other interactive learning problems with side information.
- On the practical side, the current experimental evidence is too limited to firmly establish impact; adding stronger empirical comparisons would help, given the motivation in ER.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
1. Please correct Lemma 1’s statement (missing “1 −”) and the TV–Hellinger inequality constants; clarify whether H denotes the squared or unsquared Hellinger divergence at each use.
2. Specify assumptions for the Sanov-based concentration of Hellinger divergence estimates (discrete support q fixed? minimal mass conditions?). What changes if f+, f− are continuous?
3. The algorithm learns p+, p− from initially small clusters; how do you ensure early estimates are not biased by mis-assignments? Is there a safeguard or backtracking?
4. Runtime/memory: You read/use O(n^2) side information; discuss feasibility for large n, and whether a sparse or sampled W suffices.
5. Experiments: Please compare against at least one strong heuristic (e.g., [40, 43]) and add synthetic studies varying H^2, k, and n to validate the predicted scaling and the small “gray zone” in Phase 3B.
6. Discuss sensitivity to oracle errors (pointer to companion paper is fine), and whether the algorithm can be adapted with majority voting at similar query complexity.

Pros
- Strong theoretical results: near-tight adaptive lower bounds and matching parameter-free algorithms.
- General model (unknown f+, f−, unknown k, arbitrary cluster sizes).
- Novel and potentially reusable techniques (interactive lower bounds; iterative-update using Hellinger).
- Clear connection and generalization of SBM; insightful discussion of thresholds.

Cons
- Several technical/notation inaccuracies and typos that should be corrected.
- Experimental evaluation is minimal and lacks comparisons to prior heuristics.
- Some assumptions (fixed finite support; H^2 ≤ 1/18) and constants/log factors are not fully explored.
- Algorithm description could be more formal for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The theoretical contributions are significant and original, addressing an important gap (interactive query complexity with side information) and providing broadly useful techniques. With corrections to clarity/notation and stronger empirical validation, this would be a strong NeurIPS paper.
