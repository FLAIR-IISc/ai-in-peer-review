Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when an oracle can answer same-cluster queries and the algorithm is given “side information” in the form of a noisy similarity matrix. Pairs from the same cluster are drawn i.i.d. from f+, across clusters from f−, both unknown. The authors provide:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds showing that any algorithm (even with knowledge of k, f+, f−) needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries in expectation to recover with nontrivial probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1, for small Hellinger divergence.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms with (claimed) query complexity O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) (Monte Carlo, success prob. 1 − o(1/n)) and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}) (Las Vegas).
- A technical framework using Hellinger divergence and a Sanov-type analysis to estimate f+, f− from side information and adaptively decide when the side information suffices to avoid queries.
- A brief empirical study on a single ER-style dataset (cora) illustrating that few queries can give good F1, and that approximate side-information distributions perform close to exact.

Relation to prior work is positioned w.r.t. classic nk upper/lower bounds without side information, stochastic block models (SBM), crowdsourced ER heuristics, and recent interactive clustering models.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practically motivated gap: theory that explains why side information can drastically reduce query complexity.
- Lower bounds for adaptive querying with side information are nontrivial; the proof structure (weakly queried clusters + TV/Hellinger control + Markov-type bounds) is of independent interest.
- Nearly matching upper bounds (within log n) that are parameter-free is appealing; the iterative-update approach guided by empirical Hellinger is reasonable and could be reusable.
- Clear conceptual connection to SBM, highlighting how queries might shift recovery thresholds.
- The use of Hellinger (a metric with convenient product properties) is technically sound for both upper and lower bounds.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Consistency/clarity issues in key statements:
  - The query complexity for the Monte Carlo algorithm alternates between O(k/(log n·H^2)) and O(k log n/H^2) in different parts of the paper; Theorem 1 uses the latter. Given ME ∝ log n/H^2 in the algorithm, O(k log n/H^2) seems correct. Please fix all occurrences and ensure a coherent statement.
  - Lemma 1 states the error lower bound as a negative sum; from the proof, it should be “≥ 1 − [terms]”. As written, the lemma is incorrect. This must be corrected.
  - There are other typographical issues (e.g., stray “st Conference…”, footnotes, incomplete references) that impede readability.
- Model/assumptions vs. claims:
  - Upper-bound algorithms and analysis assume discrete support of f+, f− over a fixed q. The manuscript claims lower bounds cover continuous distributions, but it is not clear how the parameter-free algorithm would be adapted to continuous (binning? kernels? choice of q?), and what effect this has on constants and log factors. The practical import of “parameter-free” becomes murky outside the discrete setting.
  - The algorithm aggregates empirical p+, p− across currently grown clusters. In adversarial partitions or highly imbalanced cluster sizes, it is not obvious that these aggregates are well-behaved early enough to guarantee the claimed thresholds. The main text handwaves to appendices; some intuition or partial guarantees in the main body would help.
- Empirical evaluation is very limited:
  - Only one real dataset and no comparison against established baselines (e.g., [40, 43] and successors) that motivate the work. The “best/worst/greedy” strategies are internal variants rather than comparisons to existing methods, and the “best” strategy is unrealistic.
  - No systematic scaling studies (vary k, H^2, cluster size distribution) on synthetic data (SBM or generalized models) to verify the k log n/H^2 dependence and the near-tightness to the lower bound.
  - No assessment of failure probability (theoretical 1 − o(1/n)) in practice, nor of the Las Vegas variant.
  - The experiment narrative sometimes assumes access to “exact” f+, f−, although the algorithm is supposed to be parameter-free. Please clarify precisely which quantities are estimated vs. assumed.
- Overclaiming: The statement that the lower-bound framework directly “provides a general framework for fundamental problems of classification, hypothesis testing, distribution testing, etc. in the interactive setting” is aspirational. The present proof exploits problem-specific structure (product distributions over side information, same/different cluster decomposition). It would be better to tone this down or delineate the conditions under which the approach can generalize.
- Presentation: The main paper pushes many crucial proofs and algorithmic details to appendices while the main text contains several formula-level inconsistencies. For a theory paper, precision in the main statements is essential.

Quality
Technically promising, but the current presentation includes significant inconsistencies and lacks adequate empirical validation. The lower-bound technique appears sound, but crucial statements need correction. The upper-bound algorithm is plausible; however, its practicality and robustness are under-demonstrated.

Clarity
Mixed. The high-level narrative is clear and well-motivated, but the mathematical statements contain inconsistencies and typos. Algorithmic thresholds and conditions (Phase 3A/3B) are hard to parse. References are incomplete. The paper needs a careful proofreading and a consolidation of notations and bounds.

Originality
The formulation with side information and adaptive queries, the Hellinger-based analysis, and the near-tight lower and upper bounds are novel and interesting relative to prior work on oracle-aided clustering and SBM.

Significance
Potentially significant for theory of interactive clustering and active learning with side information, and for understanding when crowdsourced ER can be query-efficient. The impact would be strengthened by clearer, consistent statements and a more compelling empirical validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all complexity expressions and the sign/error in Lemma 1; ensure Theorem 1/2 statements are self-contained and consistent.
- Clarify the discrete-support assumption and discuss extensions to continuous f+, f− (including how to choose/learn binning, effect on rates).
- Provide synthetic experiments varying k, H^2, and cluster imbalance to validate query complexity scaling; compare against representative baselines from [40, 43, 18].
- Report failure probabilities and the empirical behavior of the Las Vegas algorithm.
- Improve exposition of the iterative-update method, thresholds, and the anti-concentration lemma; move at least the main proof ideas into the paper.
- Clean up references and typos.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical direction is strong and close to publishable, but the current manuscript suffers from key inconsistencies and insufficient empirical validation against established baselines. With corrected statements and stronger experiments, this could be a solid NeurIPS contribution. Confidence: medium.
