Summary
The paper studies interactive clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries in the presence of “side information” W: a noisy similarity matrix whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ or f− depending on whether a pair is in the same cluster. The number of clusters k and the distributions f+, f− are unknown to the algorithm. The authors present:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds on query complexity in terms of k, n, and the Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−. Roughly, any algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries (plus Ω(n) for success prob. 1).
- A parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm with query complexity claimed to be O(min{nk, ?}) and a Las Vegas algorithm with O(n log n + min{nk, ?}) queries, where the “?” is stated as some function of H^2(f+‖f−) and log n. The algorithm iteratively estimates p+ and p− from W, sets a size threshold inversely proportional to the estimated squared Hellinger divergence, and then uses a membership test based on Hellinger distance to place points with few additional queries.
- An experiment on one entity-resolution dataset (cora) showing query–accuracy tradeoffs under a greedy query-ordering heuristic, with f+ and f− estimated (and perturbed).

Connection to prior work includes classic SBM and active/crowdsourced ER; the model generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes.

Quality
- Lower bound: The core idea—reducing assignment of a lightly queried point to a k-hypothesis test and leveraging Hellinger divergence via product distributions—is sound and interesting. However, the statement and proof presentation contain nontrivial issues:
  - Lemma 1 appears to miss a leading “1 −” in the error lower bound; as written, the bound can be negative. Later arguments implicitly use error ≥ 1 − P`(E).
  - The inequality between total variation and Hellinger is misstated: the paper writes ||P−Q||TV ≤ sqrt(H(P‖Q)), but the standard relation is ||P−Q||TV ≤ H(P,Q) (Hellinger distance), or ||P−Q||TV ≤ sqrt(2)·H, depending on normalization. The current form introduces an extra square root and mixes H vs H^2 inconsistently.
  - Notation for Hellinger is inconsistent throughout: H, H^2, H2 are used interchangeably, sometimes with a square root outside. This makes the constants/log factors impossible to verify.
  - The step that H^2 of the product distribution is at most the sum of component H^2 is plausible via Hellinger affinity factorization but should be stated precisely (using 1 − ∏ A_i ≤ ∑(1 − A_i)); currently it’s asserted without proof.
- Upper bounds/algorithms: The iterative, parameter-free idea is appealing, but the specification is underspecified and internally inconsistent:
  - The query complexity expressions are ambiguously typeset: “O( k logn H2(f+‖f−) )” could mean O(k log n / H^2) or O(k/(log n·H^2)); both appear plausible in different places. Similarly, the Las Vegas bound includes “k2 logn H2(·)” with the same ambiguity.
  - A Las Vegas algorithm is claimed to recover “with probability 1,” but the theorem then states “with probability 1 − o(1/n) has query complexity O(...).” These two statements conflict (Las Vegas typically guarantees success prob. 1 and bounds on expected queries).
  - The algorithm assumes discrete f+, f− over a fixed support size q and claims extension to finite supports “scaling linearly with its size,” but the dependence on q is not reflected in the bounds or runtime. What happens for continuous distributions?
  - Phase 1/2 can create erroneous clusters before estimates of p+ and p− stabilize; the analysis sketch relies on Sanov-type concentration but details are deferred to an appendix. Since correctness hinges on these estimates, a clearer invariant (fraction of misassigned points tolerated before convergence; how often estimates are refreshed; what constants C, B must be) is needed in the main text.
  - The success probability is variously stated as 1 − 1/n and 1 − o(1/n); please fix to a standard 1 − n^−c and ensure it propagates through union bounds.
- Experiments: Limited and misaligned with theory.
  - Single dataset; no synthetic experiments exploring how queries scale with H^2(f+‖f−), k, or cluster imbalance (central to the theory).
  - “Best” and “worst” strategies are unrealistic or oracle-aided; the greedy heuristic is reasonable but lacks comparison to strong baselines (e.g., existing ER active querying heuristics, or the nk trivial strategy).
  - The theory claims exact recovery with high probability, but the experiments report F1; no exact recovery rates or error analyses are shown. No runtime or sensitivity to the constants is reported.

Clarity
- Numerous formatting/citation errors: “st Conference on...”, missing years/page numbers for many references, dangling footnotes (1,2,3), inconsistent notation (k2 vs k^2), and missing division slashes in asymptotic expressions make it hard to parse claims.
- Several math statements are ambiguously typeset, especially the core bounds. Define H (distance) and H^2 (squared divergence) once, stick to one symbol, and remove extraneous square roots.
- The “Membership” rule uses negative H^2 as affinity; this is counterintuitive. Consider using +H^2 with “smaller is better,” or rescale to a similarity measure.

Originality
- The problem—query complexity of clustering with side information under unknown f+, f−—is a natural and timely generalization of SBM and prior interactive clustering. The information-theoretic lower bound technique for adaptive querying appears novel and valuable.
- Parameter-free algorithmic approach using iterative estimation and Hellinger thresholds is interesting. However, given the issues above, it’s hard to assess the precise innovation without clearer statements and proofs.

Significance
- If corrected and solidified, the separation between nk (no side info) and roughly k·polylog(n)/H^2 queries with side info would be significant, with implications for active ER and community detection.
- The experimental section in its current form does not substantiate the theoretical claims or guide practitioners.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap: theoretical query complexity with side information, unknown k, unknown f+, f−.
- Lower bound framework for adaptive querying is promising and may generalize.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient approach is appealing.

Cons
- Multiple technical inaccuracies and notational inconsistencies in core theorems and proofs.
- Ambiguous/contradictory complexity statements and Las Vegas claim.
- Experimental evaluation is weak and not aligned with theory.
- Numerous formatting and citation issues.

Recommendation: Reject
The paper is close to a strong contribution, but the accumulation of technical and presentation issues (misstated inequalities, ambiguous bounds, contradictory probability/complexity claims, and weak experiments) prevents reliable validation of the main results. I encourage the authors to:
- Rigorously fix all inequalities and notation (TV vs Hellinger, H vs H^2), correct Lemma 1 and Las Vegas statements, and unambiguously state all complexity bounds with explicit division and k-exponents.
- Bring key proof elements (concentration for p± estimates, convergence of thresholds, size of Waiting(C)) into the main text with clear constants.
- Clarify assumptions on support size q or continuous distributions and reflect q in bounds.
- Substantially expand experiments: synthetic SBM-like data varying H^2, k, sizes; exact recovery rates; comparisons to baselines; runtime.
- Thoroughly proofread and fix formatting/citation issues.

With these corrections, the work could be impactful.
