This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, a problem motivated by applications like entity resolution where oracle queries (e.g., to human annotators) are expensive. The authors establish both information-theoretic lower bounds and algorithms that nearly match these bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information (a similarity matrix) is available compared to clustering without it. The work generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) and aims to bridge the gap between theoretical lower bounds (Ω(nk)) and the performance of practical heuristics.

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical approach, its generalization of existing models like SBM, and the development of parameter-free algorithms – a valuable contribution for practical applicability. The connection to entity resolution provides a compelling application context. The detailed proofs (available in the supplementary material) demonstrate a strong commitment to technical correctness. The experimental results on the Cora dataset, while limited, offer initial support for the theoretical findings.

However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor yet pervasive issues that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality. These range from stylistic inconsistencies and imprecise language to questionable choices in notation and presentation, ultimately hindering clarity and making it difficult to fully assess the contribution.

First, the writing is often convoluted. For example, the introduction repeats the motivation unnecessarily. The phrasing “...a rigorous theoretical study of clustering with the help of an oracle…” is awkward. Better would be “...a rigorous theoretical study of clustering *using* an oracle…” Throughout the paper, passive voice is overused. Sentences are often overly long and complex.

Second, the notation is inconsistent and sometimes unclear. The definition of *W* as a “noisy weighted upper-triangular similarity matrix” is vague. The use of *f+* and *f−* without defining their support (finite or continuous) initially creates ambiguity. The explanation of H2(f‖g) is presented *after* it's used in the upper bound theorem, disrupting the flow.  The meaning of 'i 6= j' in the definition of *W* is redundant as an upper-triangular matrix already implies this.

Third, numerous minor technical issues abound.  The claim that the heuristics developed in practice often ask significantly less queries than *nk* is supported only by citations; a brief explanation of *why* these heuristics perform better would be useful. The discussion of the motivating application (entity resolution) feels somewhat disconnected from the subsequent theoretical development. The use of "o(1/n)" for probability is unconventional and unclear; typically, we express this with '1 - 1/n'. The comparison of the proposed approach with [5] feels incomplete – the authors only mention the computational vs. information-theoretic nature of the lower bounds but don’t fully contrast the algorithmic approaches.  There's an assertion that the proposed algorithms are computationally efficient (O(n^2)), but this is stated without justification; a brief explanation of the dominant computational cost would be beneficial.

Fourth, the presentation of the experimental results is lacking. The choice of a single dataset (Cora) is limiting. The figures are small and difficult to read. The analysis focuses primarily on query counts without any discussion of clustering accuracy (e.g., using standard metrics like NMI or ARI).  The comparison to existing heuristics is absent. The claim that “the performance of greedy strategy is very close to that of best” needs more quantitative evidence.

Fifth, the appendix references are not consistent (e.g., mentioning appendices A & B in the main text, but a potentially larger number of appendices exists based on section 4).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical contribution: The lower bounds and matching algorithms represent a significant advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information.
*   Generalization of SBM: The work extends the well-studied SBM model, providing a broader framework for analysis.
*   Parameter-free algorithms: The algorithms are valuable from a practical perspective.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Pervasive stylistic and notational issues: The paper is difficult to read and understand due to numerous minor flaws.
*   Limited experimental validation: The results are based on a single dataset and lack a comprehensive comparison to existing approaches.
*   Lack of clarity: The explanation of several concepts is unclear or incomplete.
*   Minor technical inaccuracies and inconsistencies.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the theoretical significance of the work, the numerous flaws in presentation and clarity significantly undermine its impact. While the core ideas are valuable, the paper requires substantial revisions to meet NIPS standards. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the writing, standardizing the notation, providing a more thorough experimental evaluation, and addressing the minor technical issues outlined above. A second round of review after these revisions may be warranted.
