

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the fundamental problem of determining the query complexity of clustering when side information (in the form of a noisy similarity matrix) is available. The authors consider a setting where an algorithm can query an oracle to determine whether two points belong to the same cluster, and they analyze how much the side information can reduce the number of queries needed to exactly recover the true clustering.

The main contributions are:
1. A general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering with side information
2. Efficient parameter-free algorithms that nearly match the lower bound
3. A dramatic reduction in query complexity from O(nk) (without side information) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the Hellinger divergence

The paper is motivated by practical applications like entity resolution, where human input (crowdsourcing) is expensive, and the authors show that the side information explains the discrepancy between the theoretical Ω(nk) bound and the much lower query counts used in practical heuristics.

This work extends the classical clustering with oracle queries problem by incorporating side information, and it generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- for intra-cluster and inter-cluster similarities. The authors establish tight bounds that characterize the effect of side information on query complexity.

## Relation to Previous Work

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work on clustering with oracle queries (without side information) which established Ω(nk) query complexity [13, 40]. It also relates to the stochastic block model literature [1, 2, 15, 28], but significantly generalizes it by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- without assuming knowledge of k or cluster sizes.

The authors note that recent heuristic algorithms [40, 42, 43] use far fewer queries than the theoretical Ω(nk) bound, and they hypothesize that side information is the reason for this discrepancy. The paper is the first to provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for this problem, filling a significant gap in the literature.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorem 2) and matching upper bounds (Theorem 1), establishing a tight characterization of query complexity with side information. The proofs are technically sound, with careful attention to the interactive nature of the problem.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The work has clear practical implications for entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications where minimizing human input is crucial. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms is particularly important for real-world deployment.

3. **Novel Framework**: The authors develop a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which is a significant contribution that could be applied to other problems beyond clustering.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate that the algorithm performs well in practice, with results very close to the theoretical predictions. The comparison between exact and approximate distributions shows robustness to learning errors.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from motivation to problem statement, theoretical results, algorithms, and experimental validation. The technical content is presented in a clear and accessible manner.

## Weaknesses

1. **Error Probability in Lower Bound**: The main lower bound (Theorem 2) guarantees an error probability of at least 1/6 - O(1/√k), which is not a very strong error guarantee. While the authors address the probability 1 recovery case, the main result doesn't provide high-probability guarantees. For practical applications, a higher probability of success would be desirable.

2. **Motivation for Hellinger Divergence**: The paper states that Hellinger divergence is used because it's a metric, but doesn't provide sufficient intuition for why it's the appropriate measure for this problem. A more detailed explanation of why Hellinger divergence was chosen over other divergences (like KL divergence) would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

3. **Experimental Section**: The experimental section is somewhat brief and lacks detail on how the side information was generated and how the algorithms were tuned. More information on the specific implementation choices would help readers reproduce the results.

4. **Connection to SBM**: While the paper mentions the connection to stochastic block models, it doesn't fully explore how their results generalize or improve upon existing SBM results, especially in the sparse regime.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-supported claims. The authors carefully evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The experimental results provide good validation of the theoretical claims. This is a complete piece of work, not a position paper, and the authors are careful about their claims.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement is precise, and the contributions are clearly articulated. The main technical concepts (like Hellinger divergence) are properly defined. The paper would benefit from slightly more detail in the experimental section and a bit more intuitive explanation of why Hellinger divergence is the right measure for this problem.

## Originality

The problem of clustering with side information and query complexity is novel in the way the authors formalize it. The framework for proving lower bounds in interactive settings is new and significant. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work on clustering with oracle queries (without side information) and from stochastic block models. The authors appropriately reference related work, including the recent "Clustering with Noisy Queries" paper by Mazumdar and Saha (NIPS 2017), which doesn't consider side information.

## Significance

The results are highly significant as they provide the first theoretical guarantees for interactive clustering with side information. The reduction in query complexity from O(nk) to O(k / (log n * H²(f+||f-))) is substantial, especially when H²(f+||f-) is small. This work could guide the design of more efficient interactive clustering algorithms in practice, particularly for applications like entity resolution where human input is expensive.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a strong theoretical contribution that is both novel and practically relevant. The results are rigorous, the algorithms are efficient and parameter-free, and the experimental results validate the theoretical claims.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro (Arguments for Acceptance):**
1. First rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information, filling a significant gap in the literature
2. Provides tight bounds (within logarithmic factors) for query complexity with side information
3. Develops a novel framework for proving lower bounds in interactive settings
4. Algorithms are parameter-free and computationally efficient
5. Experimental results on real-world data validate the theoretical findings
6. Strong connection to practical applications like entity resolution

**Con (Arguments for Rejection):**
1. The error probability in the lower bound is not very strong (1/6 - O(1/√k))
2. More explanation needed on why Hellinger divergence was chosen over other divergences
3. Experimental section could be more detailed

## Final Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The results are theoretically sound, the algorithms are practical, and the experimental validation is appropriate. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I believe this paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and should be accepted.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the lower bound proof, provide a reference for the relationship between total variation distance and Hellinger divergence that the authors use.

2. In the introduction, clarify why the specific normalization of Hellinger divergence (H²(f||g) = 1/2 ∑(√f(i) - √g(i))²) is used rather than other common normalizations.

3. Expand the experimental section to include more details about the side information generation process and algorithm tuning.

4. Provide a more detailed explanation of how the results generalize or improve upon existing SBM results, especially in the sparse regime.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the results might be extended to handle noisy oracle answers, as mentioned in the paper as a direction for future work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
