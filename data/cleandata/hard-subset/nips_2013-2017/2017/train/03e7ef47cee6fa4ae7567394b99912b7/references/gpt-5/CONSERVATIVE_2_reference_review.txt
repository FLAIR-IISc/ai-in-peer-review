Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm has access to side information in the form of a pairwise similarity matrix W whose entries are drawn from unknown distributions f+ (same-cluster pairs) and f− (different-cluster pairs). The learner may adaptively query an oracle that returns whether a pair belongs to the same cluster. The main claims are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds that quantify how side information (via the Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−)) reduces query complexity, even allowing randomized algorithms that know k, f+, f−.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that, without knowing k, f+, f−, achieve query complexity within a logarithmic factor of the lower bound. The Monte Carlo algorithm relies on iterative estimation of the divergence using side information and only queries when needed; the Las Vegas variant guarantees correctness with probability 1.
- A connection to stochastic block models (SBM), interpreting the no-query case as an SBM recovery problem, and raising questions about how active queries shift SBM thresholds.
- Limited experiments on a small ER dataset (cora), suggesting that a greedy strategy guided by learned distributions needs far fewer queries than nk and is robust to approximate distributions.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and timely question: how side information affects the minimal number of oracle queries required for exact clustering, a setting relevant to crowdsourcing and active learning.
- Attempts a general treatment with arbitrary unknown f+, f− (discrete) and unknown k, aiming for parameter-free procedures. This generality is attractive.
- Proposes an information-theoretic framework for interactive lower bounds under adaptivity, which is nontrivial and could be of independent interest.
- Conceptual links to SBM are clearly drawn and may spur further work on active community detection thresholds.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Inconsistencies and potential errors in key statements:
- The claimed upper bounds fluctuate between O(k log n/H2), O(k/(log n H2)), and O(k2 log n/H2) across the abstract/theorem statements/algorithm discussion. The Las Vegas bound is stated as O(n log n + min(nk, k2 log n/H2)), while the Monte Carlo bound is sometimes written with k (not k2). Given the lower bound Ω(min{nk, k2/H2}), a Monte Carlo upper bound of O(k log n/H2) would contradict the lower bound. These inconsistencies must be resolved and undermine confidence in the results.
- Lemma 1 as stated gives a lower bound on error probability that appears to be negative due to leading minus signs. Later derivations suggest it should read “≥ 1 − (terms)”. The presentation needs correction and careful restatement.

2) Clarity and presentation issues:
- The manuscript contains many typos, broken references, footnote artifacts (“st Conference on NIPS…”, “1” inline), and notational slips (e.g., pmfs fi,js). The proofs mix TV, Hellinger, and Sanov/KL without enough detail in the main text to verify the key steps; the claim that Sanov (KL-based) is adapted to Hellinger is asserted but not substantiated here.
- The algorithmic description is high-level and difficult to reproduce. Pseudocode and a precise specification of Membership, thresholds, stopping conditions, and the exact query policy are needed in the main text.

3) Lower bound under adaptivity:
- The lower bound approach (picking an element x with few queries, constructing “weak” clusters, and bounding P`(E) via Hellinger) is interesting but the handling of adaptivity is delicate. The current write-up does not convincingly rule out adaptive strategies that concentrate queries on ambiguous points and exploit side information in more sophisticated ways. At minimum, a clearer formalization of the interactive model and a careful justification of the product-form Hellinger bound under adaptivity are needed.

4) Assumptions and scope:
- The side information model assumes independent draws from fixed-support discrete f+, f−; extensions to continuous or growing support are only sketched. The algorithm reads O(n2) side information, which may dwarf query savings in realistic large-scale settings.
- The oracle is noiseless; while a companion work is mentioned, the practical relevance of exact oracles is limited. It would help to state precisely how the results extend when the oracle is noisy.
- The adversarial partition with generative W is unusual: the cluster assignment is arbitrary, yet the pairwise similarities are generated conditionally on that assignment. Some discussion of when this modeling choice is appropriate (beyond SBM) would be helpful.

5) Empirical validation:
- Experiments are minimal (one dataset), do not implement the full parameter-free algorithm, and lack comparisons to established baselines (e.g., heuristic ER/query selection methods cited). The “best/worst/greedy” strategies do not map cleanly to the proposed algorithms. Strong theoretical claims coupled with such limited empirical evidence make it hard to assess practical impact.

Quality: Mixed. The direction and claimed results are potentially strong, but the presentation contains inconsistencies in the main theorems and insufficient detail to verify correctness. The experimental section does not substantiate practical claims.

Clarity: Below the NIPS bar. The paper needs substantial editing, consistent notation and bounds, clearer algorithmic specification, and more self-contained proofs of key steps.

Originality: Moderate to high. Interactive lower bounds with side information and parameter-free algorithms in this generality appear novel, though there is related work in SBM and active ER.

Significance: Potentially high, if the results are correct and clearly presented. As is, the impact is limited by lack of clarity and validation.

Questions for the authors
- Please reconcile the discrepancies among the stated upper bounds (k vs k2 factors, with/without log n) and the lower bound. What are the exact Monte Carlo and Las Vegas query complexities?
- Can you provide a precise, stand-alone proof sketch for the central step adapting Sanov (KL) to Hellinger in your convergence analysis?
- How does the algorithm and analysis extend to continuous f+, f− or finite supports that grow with n? What are the explicit dependencies?
- Can you implement the full parameter-free algorithm and compare against strong baselines on multiple datasets? How often does Phase 2 converge, and what is the empirical Waiting(C) size?
- How sensitive are your results to slight oracle noise?

Pros
- Addresses an important gap; clean conceptual question
- Attempts general lower bound machinery for adaptive querying
- Parameter-free angle is appealing
- Connections to SBM and recovery thresholds

Cons
- Inconsistent key bounds; possible contradiction with the lower bound
- Many presentation/notation errors; hard to verify correctness
- Limited algorithmic detail; no full implementation
- Minimal, unconvincing experiments; no baselines
- Assumptions (noisy-free oracle, O(n2) side info) limit practical relevance

Recommendation: Reject
The paper has promise, but the inconsistencies and lack of clarity in core results, combined with thin empirical validation, preclude acceptance at this time. I encourage the authors to fix the bounds, strengthen and clarify the proofs, provide precise algorithms, and include substantive experiments. Confidence: High.
