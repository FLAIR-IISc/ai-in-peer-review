This paper tackles a fascinating and important problem: understanding the query complexity of clustering when aided by an oracle and, crucially, informed by side information. The core idea – that side information can drastically reduce the number of queries needed – is compelling and intuitively aligns with observations in fields like entity resolution. The connection drawn to the stochastic block model (SBM) is well-articulated, positioning the work as a significant generalization and opening avenues for cross-pollination of ideas. I find the theoretical framework presented to be bold and the attempt to provide information-theoretic lower bounds commendable.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The investigation of query complexity *with* side information is a genuinely novel contribution. Existing work largely focuses on worst-case scenarios *without* such guidance. The generalization of the SBM is also a strong point.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a commendable commitment to theoretical analysis, attempting to establish lower bounds and provide matching upper bounds.  The introduction of a new framework for proving these bounds is ambitious and valuable.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivating application in entity resolution grounds the work in a real-world problem, highlighting the potential impact of reducing query complexity for human-in-the-loop systems.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that don't require knowledge of the underlying distributions (f+ and f-) is a major strength, increasing the practicality of the approach.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors do a good job of situating their work within the context of clustering, active learning, and community detection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Validation:** While the paper mentions experimental results on the Cora dataset, the presentation is somewhat cursory. The claims of near-optimal performance and the benefits of the greedy strategy need more substantial justification with detailed comparisons to existing heuristics.  More datasets and ablations are required.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, especially for the lower bound, appear quite complex and dense. While rigorous, they might be inaccessible to a broad audience. A more intuitive explanation of the key ideas behind the lower bound would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions & Generality:** The assumption of fixed cardinality of support for f+ and f- feels restrictive and could be clarified. How sensitive are the results to variations in this assumption?  Exploring continuous distributions more thoroughly would strengthen the work.
* **Logarithmic Gap:**  The logarithmic gap between the upper and lower bounds, while not uncommon, is a point of potential improvement. Further research to close this gap could yield tighter bounds.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel theoretical framework for understanding query complexity in an important setting.
* The proposed algorithms are parameter-free and offer the potential for significant efficiency gains in real-world applications.
* The connection to the SBM provides a valuable bridge between theoretical computer science and statistical physics.
* The authors demonstrate a strong commitment to theoretical rigor.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn’t fully substantiate the claims of near-optimal performance.
* The complexity of the proofs may hinder widespread adoption of the ideas.
* The presence of a logarithmic gap between upper and lower bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The core idea is compelling, the theoretical framework is ambitious, and the potential impact is substantial.  While the experimental validation could be more thorough and the proofs more accessible, the novelty and boldness of the approach outweigh these concerns. I lean towards **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the feedback regarding experimental validation and proof clarity in a final revision. The potential for this work to shape future research on interactive clustering and community detection is considerable.
