- Paper develops a theoretical framework for clustering with an oracle plus side information (similarity matrix), focusing on query complexity.
- Shows side information can substantially reduce query complexity versus the O(nk) bound without side information.
- Proves matching lower and upper bounds on query complexity that depend on Hellinger divergence between within- and between-cluster similarity distributions.
- Presents parameter-free algorithms that achieve near-optimal query complexity without knowing f+, f−, or k.
- Draws connections to and generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) to arbitrary unknown distributions and no cluster-size assumptions.
- Builds on prior work in clustering, active learning, and community detection, and contrasts with heuristic methods lacking guarantees.
- Distinguishes its focus on side information from prior work that primarily studies noisy oracle responses.
- Provides a useful comparison to k-means with pairwise queries, emphasizing information-theoretic rather than computational lower bounds.
- Theoretical rigor: strong development of lower and upper bounds and solid analytical contributions.
- Generality: minimal assumptions about distributions, cluster sizes, and number of clusters.
- Novelty: relatively unexplored problem setting and insightful SBM generalization.
- Parameter-free algorithms: practically relevant since they do not require tuning to unknown parameters.
- Clear motivation: entity resolution is a well-explained real-world application.
- Experimental validation: real-world dataset experiments support theoretical findings, albeit limited in scope.
- Presentation is dense and mathematically heavy, which may hinder accessibility for non-experts.
- Logarithmic and constant factors in the bounds (e.g., O(k log n H2(...))) might be large in practice and need discussion.
- Experimental methodology could be stronger with more detail on parameter choices and comparisons to additional baselines.
- Limited discussion on how to obtain and represent side information effectively in diverse real-world settings.
- Quality score 9/10, clarity 7/10, originality 9/10, significance 8/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, novel setting, parameter-free algorithms, rigorous analysis, convincing experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility concerns, potential practical impact of log/constant factors, and experimental comparisons could be improved.
- Overall recommendation: Strong Accept (with minor revisions for clarity and discussion of constants).
