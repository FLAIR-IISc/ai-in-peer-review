This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data (oracle queries) is expensive. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and design algorithms that nearly match these bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity compared to clustering without side information (Ω(nk)). The work generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) and proposes parameter-free algorithms, a key advantage in practical scenarios.

**Summary of Contributions:** The authors formally define the “Query-Cluster” problem with an oracle and similarity matrix (side information). They present lower bounds of Ω(min{nk, k²H²(f+||f−)}) and algorithms with a complexity of O(k log n H²(f+||f−)), where f+ and f− represent the distributions of similarity scores within and between clusters respectively. The algorithms are parameter-free and computationally efficient. Experimental results on a bibliographic dataset validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper tackles a crucial problem in clustering—reducing query complexity using side information—with a rigorous theoretical treatment.  The generalization of the SBM and the focus on unknown distributions f+ and f− are significant contributions.  The parameter-free aspect of the algorithms is a notable strength.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The lower bound proof, utilizing Fano's inequality-inspired arguments adapted for interactive settings, appears sound. The algorithmic design, based on iterative updates and Hellinger divergence, is well-motivated and analyzed. The link to Sanov’s Theorem in the analysis is appropriate.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivates the problem. The connection to entity resolution is helpful.  The notation is generally standard and the key ideas are presented logically.
*   **Significance:** The results are important because they provide theoretical guarantees on the efficiency of clustering algorithms that leverage side information—a common scenario in real-world applications.  Reducing the number of oracle queries has direct practical implications.
* **Experimental Validation:** Inclusion of experimental results adds to the credibility and demonstrates the practical benefits of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lower Bound Tightness:** While the lower bound is a significant achievement, the logarithmic factor between the lower and upper bounds (O(log n)) remains.  Addressing this gap would further strengthen the theoretical results. The discussion of the potential for tighter bounds in the sparse SBM case is good, but leaving it as a future direction.
*   **Assumptions on Side Information:** While the work handles unknown f+ and f−, the assumption of a weighted upper-triangular similarity matrix may limit the applicability to certain scenarios. Exploring other forms of side information could broaden the scope.
*   **Experimental Details:** The experimental section could benefit from more detailed descriptions of the dataset, the computation of f+ and f-, and a comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for entity resolution (beyond simply showing performance). More in-depth analysis on the sensitivity to parameters (though parameter-free) would be helpful.
* **Proof Length:** While understandable given the complexity of the results, the length of the proofs and their location in the appendix may make it challenging for readers to fully grasp the technical details.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in clustering.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees with novel lower bounds and matching algorithms.
*   Generalizes existing models (SBM) and offers a parameter-free solution.
*   Demonstrates experimental validation.
*   High-quality and clear presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Logarithmic gap between lower and upper bounds.
*   Limited scope of side information assumptions.
*   Experimental section could be more comprehensive.
*   Proof complexity could hinder understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions.  The authors have rigorously analyzed the query complexity of clustering with side information and developed algorithms that achieve near-optimal performance. While some minor weaknesses exist, the novelty, soundness, and significance of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, a discussion of the difficulties in closing the logarithmic gap in the lower bound and a more detailed experimental analysis would enhance the paper's impact.
