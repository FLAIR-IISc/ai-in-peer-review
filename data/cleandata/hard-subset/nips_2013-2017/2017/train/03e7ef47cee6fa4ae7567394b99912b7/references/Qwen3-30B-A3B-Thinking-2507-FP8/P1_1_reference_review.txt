

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when the algorithm has access to side information in the form of a similarity matrix, along with the ability to query an oracle about whether pairs of points belong to the same cluster. The authors establish a significant theoretical separation between clustering with and without side information. Without side information, the query complexity is Θ(nk), but with side information (where similarity values within clusters are drawn from f⁺ and between clusters from f⁻), they show that the query complexity can be reduced to O(k / (log n · H²(f⁺‖f⁻))), where H is the Hellinger divergence. They provide matching lower bounds and develop parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of f⁺, f⁻, or k.

The paper connects to several relevant areas: the standard clustering with oracle problem (with O(nk) query complexity), stochastic block models (SBM) as a special case of their side information model, and practical applications like entity resolution where minimizing crowdworker interactions is crucial. The authors address a key gap in the literature by showing how side information can explain the discrepancy between theoretical Ω(nk) lower bounds and practical heuristics that use far fewer queries.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous information-theoretic analysis of clustering with side information, establishing tight bounds that match within logarithmic factors. This is a significant advancement over previous work that lacked theoretical guarantees.

2. **Novel Approach**: The authors introduce a novel framework for proving lower bounds in the interactive setting, which is particularly challenging due to adaptive query selection. Their technique using Hellinger divergence (rather than KL divergence) is well-motivated and proves crucial for their analysis.

3. **Parameter-Free Algorithms**: The proposed algorithms are completely parameter-free, working without knowledge of f⁺, f⁻, or k. This is a major practical advantage for real-world applications where these parameters are typically unknown.

4. **Clear Motivation**: The paper provides a strong practical motivation through entity resolution, a fundamental problem in data mining where the discrepancy between theoretical bounds and practical heuristics is well-documented.

5. **Experimental Validation**: The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate the practical relevance of their theoretical findings, showing that the algorithm works well with approximate distributions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (Cora). While the results are promising, more extensive experiments on diverse datasets would strengthen the practical relevance claims.

2. **No Handling of Noisy Queries**: The paper assumes the oracle always returns correct answers, though they mention this is addressed in a companion work. For practical applications like crowdsourcing, handling noisy responses is crucial, and this limitation should be acknowledged more explicitly.

3. **Constant Factors**: The theoretical bounds have large constant factors (e.g., the 4 in the Hellinger divergence threshold). While the paper acknowledges this, it's worth noting that these constants could impact practical performance.

4. **Discrete Distribution Assumption**: The paper primarily focuses on discrete distributions, though they mention extensions to continuous distributions. The main results are stated for discrete distributions, which might limit applicability to some real-world scenarios.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for both the lower and upper bounds. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the analysis, and the algorithm design is carefully constructed. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, not merely a position paper. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and place their work in context with related research.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the beginning, and the logical flow from motivation to theoretical results to algorithms to experiments is excellent. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The supplementary material (referenced in the paper) appears to contain the detailed proofs for the main results, which is appropriate for a conference paper.

## Originality

This work significantly advances the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. While previous work has studied clustering with oracle queries or side information separately, this paper is the first to rigorously analyze the query complexity of clustering with side information and establish tight bounds. The connection to the stochastic block model as a special case is well-articulated, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (e.g., [5] which considers a different clustering objective with metric distance matrices).

## Significance

The results are highly significant for practical applications where minimizing human interaction (e.g., crowdworker queries) is critical, such as entity resolution. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for why practical heuristics work better than the standard Ω(nk) lower bound, which has been a long-standing mystery in the field. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms makes them particularly valuable for real-world applications. This work opens up many new directions for future research in interactive clustering and community detection.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental question in interactive clustering with clear practical relevance. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant advancement over previous work. The results are likely to be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS, including researchers working on theoretical machine learning, active learning, and practical data mining applications.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information, providing tight bounds and parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of the underlying distributions.
