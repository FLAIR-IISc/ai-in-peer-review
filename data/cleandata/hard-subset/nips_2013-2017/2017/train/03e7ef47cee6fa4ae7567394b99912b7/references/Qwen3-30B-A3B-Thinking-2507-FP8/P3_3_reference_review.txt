

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in interactive clustering: determining the minimum number of queries required to exactly recover clusters when given a similarity matrix (side information) and an oracle that answers whether two points belong to the same cluster. The authors establish a theoretical framework that characterizes the query complexity as a function of the Hellinger divergence between the similarity distributions within and between clusters. The work builds upon the classical clustering query complexity problem (with Θ(nk) complexity) and extends it to incorporate side information, which is highly relevant to modern applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering. The paper makes a significant connection to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that their model generalizes SBM while relaxing key assumptions (no need to know k, cluster sizes, or distributions).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contributions**: The paper makes strong theoretical contributions with both tight upper and lower bounds for the query complexity. The lower bound (Theorem 2) is particularly impressive as it handles the challenging interactive setting and provides a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in adaptive learning scenarios.

2. **Novel Methodology**: The use of Hellinger divergence as the key measure for the side information quality is novel and well-motivated. The iterative-update algorithm for parameter-free learning represents a significant methodological contribution with potential applications beyond this specific problem.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The work directly addresses a gap between theory and practice in clustering with side information. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate that the algorithm performs nearly as well as an oracle with perfect knowledge, validating the theoretical claims.

4. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem, connecting to related work in SBM and information theory. The extension of Sanov's theorem to Hellinger distance is a valuable theoretical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experimental section relies on a single dataset (Cora) and does not demonstrate the algorithm's performance across diverse data distributions or under varying levels of side information quality. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation would strengthen the practical claims.

2. **Practical Assumptions**: The paper assumes the side information (similarity matrix) is perfect, which is unrealistic in practice. While the authors mention handling noisy oracles in companion work, this limitation significantly affects the practical applicability of the theoretical results.

3. **Algorithmic Complexity**: The paper claims O(n²) time complexity, but this may be prohibitive for large-scale applications. The authors should discuss how the algorithm scales with n and whether practical optimizations (e.g., sampling) could improve scalability.

4. **Comparison with Heuristics**: The paper mentions that heuristic algorithms use fewer queries than nk but doesn't provide a rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods. A more detailed comparison would better establish the algorithm's advantages.

5. **Theoretical Tightness**: While the lower bound matches the upper bound within a logarithmic factor, the paper doesn't address whether this gap is inherent to the problem or could be closed with different techniques.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical results are strong and novel, and the connection to SBM provides valuable context. The main weaknesses are the limited empirical validation and the assumption of perfect side information, which are important for practical applications.

The paper satisfies all four review criteria:
- **Quality**: The theoretical results are sound, with rigorous proofs for both upper and lower bounds. The experimental results provide reasonable validation.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some sections (like the lower bound proof) may require careful reading.
- **Originality**: The work is highly original, filling a significant gap in the literature with novel theoretical insights and a new algorithmic framework.
- **Significance**: The results have clear implications for practical applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering, and open up important new research directions.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The results have both theoretical importance and practical relevance to important real-world problems. While the experimental validation could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant publication at NeurIPS.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Providing more comprehensive experimental results across multiple datasets
2. Discussing the practical limitations of the side information assumption
3. Including a more detailed comparison with existing heuristic algorithms

However, these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws, and the paper's core contributions are significant enough to merit publication. The work represents an important step forward in the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering and will likely influence future research in this area.
