**Summary**

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with the aid of an oracle that can answer same-cluster queries for pairs of elements. The authors address the discrepancy between the established Ω(nk) lower bound for this problem and the superior performance of practical heuristics. They posit that this gap is explained by the use of "side information," which they formalize as a similarity matrix where weights are drawn from one of two distributions (f+ or f-) depending on whether the pair is in the same or different clusters.

The main contributions are theoretical. First, the paper establishes an information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) on the number of queries required for exact recovery, where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f-. Second, it presents a parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm with a nearly matching upper bound on query complexity, along with a Las Vegas variant. The proposed model is shown to be a generalization of the well-known Stochastic Block Model (SBM), and the authors discuss the potential implications of their results for SBM recovery thresholds under active querying. A small experimental evaluation is provided on the Cora dataset.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound and addresses a well-posed theoretical question. The problem formulation, which aims to formally explain the performance of practical active clustering systems, is a significant strength. Grounding the new model in the established theory of the Stochastic Block Model is a good choice, lending it credibility. The information-theoretic lower bound appears solid and provides a valuable benchmark for the problem. The core idea of the algorithm—iteratively estimating the unknown distributions and using this estimate to decide whether to query or rely on the side information—is clever and well-reasoned.

However, the work is weakened by its empirical validation, which is not thorough enough to substantiate the claims of practical relevance. The experiments are limited to a single dataset and, more importantly, fail to compare the proposed algorithm against the very heuristics [40, 42, 43] that motivate the entire investigation. The paper claims these heuristics are suboptimal but provides no direct evidence that the proposed algorithm outperforms them. The evaluation only compares different query-selection strategies within the proposed algorithm itself, which does not constitute a comparison against established baselines. This is a significant omission for a paper that positions itself as bridging theory and practice.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, and the introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem. The high-level ideas are communicated effectively.

However, there are some clarity issues. Most critically, there is a discrepancy in the statement of the main upper bound. The text preceding Theorem 1 claims a query complexity of O(k logn / H²(f+‖f−)), while Theorem 1 itself states O(k² logn / H²(f+‖f−)). This difference of a factor of k is substantial and must be clarified and corrected. Additionally, the description of the algorithm in Section 3 is quite dense. While space is limited, the three-phase structure is complex, and its intuition could be explained more clearly. As is common in theory papers, many of the technical details are deferred to the appendix, which makes it challenging to fully vet the claims from the main text.

**Originality:**
The paper's primary contribution is novel and important. While active clustering and the SBM are both well-studied, this work is the first, to my knowledge, to formally synthesize them to study the query complexity of clustering *with* side information. The central question—how much can side information reduce the number of required queries?—is fundamental, and the authors provide the first quantitative answer. Developing an information-theoretic lower bound for an adaptive querying process is a non-trivial contribution. Furthermore, designing algorithms that are parameter-free (i.e., do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f-) is a significant and practically relevant achievement.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding and designing active clustering algorithms that leverage noisy similarity scores. The results offer a principled explanation for why practical systems can be so much more efficient than the worst-case Ω(nk) bound suggests. The parameter-free nature of the proposed algorithms makes them promising candidates for real-world application, where such parameters are rarely known in advance.

The practical significance, however, remains unproven due to the limited experimental evaluation. Without demonstrating that the proposed method is competitive with or superior to existing, widely used heuristics, the impact on practitioners is muted. The theoretical results are a strong starting point, but the loop is not closed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a clear and important gap between the theory and practice of interactive clustering.
*   Provides the first formal, information-theoretic analysis of query complexity with side information.
*   The lower and upper bounds are nearly matching, representing a complete theoretical result.
*   The proposed algorithms are parameter-free, a crucial feature for practical utility.
*   Establishes a sound and interesting connection to the Stochastic Block Model.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental validation is insufficient. It lacks comparisons to the established baseline methods that are used to motivate the paper's central research question.
*   A critical inconsistency exists in the stated upper bound (O(k log n / H²) vs. O(k² log n / H²)), which undermines confidence in the presentation.
*   The practical advantages of the proposed algorithm over existing systems are not demonstrated.

**Conclusion:**
This is a strong theoretical paper that tackles an important and well-motivated problem. The formalization of clustering with side information and the derivation of nearly tight bounds are significant contributions. However, the paper's claims of practical relevance are not sufficiently supported by the experimental section, which lacks crucial comparisons to established baselines. The inconsistency in the main theorem statement is also a concern that needs to be addressed. While I value rigorous theoretical work, the lack of thorough validation against existing methods makes it difficult to endorse this work without reservation. It feels like an incomplete piece of work in its current state. I am therefore borderline on this paper but lean towards acceptance, with a strong recommendation that the authors substantially revise the experimental section for the final version to include the missing baseline comparisons.
