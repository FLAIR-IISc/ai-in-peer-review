Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner has access to (i) a same-cluster oracle for pairwise queries and (ii) a similarity matrix as side information generated from unknown distributions f+ (same-cluster) and f− (different-cluster). The authors show a sharp separation between clustering with and without side information. Without side information, Θ(nk) queries are necessary and sufficient. With side information, they develop:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds (for adaptive, possibly randomized algorithms even knowing k, f+, f−): Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) queries for success with constant probability; and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1 (assuming H^2 ≤ 1/18).
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not know k, f+, f− and whose query complexity matches the lower bounds up to a log n factor.

A key technical idea is to use Hellinger divergence as the governing measure and to design an iterative update scheme that learns empirical “intra” and “inter” distributions from a small seed of queries, then uses them to gate when side information alone is reliable. For lower bounds, the paper introduces a framework to handle adaptive querying via a reduction to multi-hypothesis testing and Hellinger-based control of total variation, which the authors argue circumvents limitations of standard Fano-type arguments.

The model generalizes stochastic block models (SBM): Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) is a special case; here f+, f− are arbitrary (unknown) pmfs, the number of clusters k and sizes are adversarial, and the algorithm is parameter free. The work connects to active learning/crowdsourcing for entity resolution and to active community detection in SBM.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A clean, general formulation of “clustering with side information” and a nearly tight characterization of query complexity that explains the empirical gap with Θ(nk).
- Lower-bound technique for interactive/adaptive settings that appears broadly reusable (classification/hypothesis testing with side information and adaptive queries).
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms that adaptively learn f+, f− proxies and automatically decide when side information suffices.
- Coherent choice of Hellinger divergence that aligns upper and lower bounds and facilitates metric-based concentration (via Sanov-style arguments).
- Practical relevance: directly motivated by crowdsourced ER; the algorithmic scheme is simple enough to be implementable.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity/consistency issues around query complexity expressions. The paper alternately states O(k/(log n H^2)) and O(k^2 log n/H^2) in different places, while the lower bound is Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}). Please unify the statements and parenthesization and clearly distinguish squared vs non-squared Hellinger. As written, some claims are dimensionally inconsistent.
- Lemma 1 statement appears to have a sign error (“probability of error at least, − 2/k(…) − …”), which must be “1 − …”. Later, Theorem 2 yields 1/6 − O(1/√k), indicating the lemma’s bound should be of the form 1 − (…) − 2√(a)H(…). Please correct.
- Assumption on discrete f+, f− with constant-size support for the algorithms: While the authors claim extensions to finite support growing linearly, the algorithm and analysis sections lean heavily on fixed q and Sanov; more details on how complexity and bounds scale with |support| would strengthen the contribution. Any guidance for continuous side-information (binned estimates) would also be helpful.
- Experimental evaluation is thin: one dataset, no comparison to strong baselines (e.g., popular heuristics [40, 43] or recent active SBM methods), no synthetic studies to stress the k and H^2 dependence, no Las Vegas variant results, and no ablations for the iterative thresholds. Given the bold theoretical claims, a more convincing empirical section would help.
- Practical costs: The algorithm is O(n^2) time due to processing the side-information matrix; some discussion on scalability, memory constraints, and approximate implementations (e.g., sketching) would be useful.
- Oracle assumption: perfect oracle answers are assumed; while a companion paper is cited, a brief discussion of robustness or how the current method composes with majority-vote oracles would increase practical relevance.
- Notational density and minor typos make parts hard to parse (e.g., phases 3A/3B thresholds, beta definition, use of H vs H^2). A careful editorial pass is recommended.

Quality
Technically solid overall with meaningful contributions on both lower and upper bounds; some proofs are deferred and a few presentation issues obscure the main results. The lower-bound framework for adaptive settings is a highlight.

Clarity
Generally understandable but hurt by notation inconsistencies and occasional typos/sign errors. The exposition of the iterative-update algorithm is promising; adding a high-level pseudocode and a clean theorem summarizing the final complexities would improve readability.

Originality
High. The parameter-free treatment with arbitrary unknown f+, f−, and the adaptive lower-bound technique using Hellinger are novel and go beyond prior SBM and ER heuristics.

Significance
High. This work provides a principled theory explaining when and how side information can dramatically reduce query complexity, aligning with active learning/crowdsourcing practice, and likely to influence future work on interactive inference.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify and clearly state the main bounds (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) next to the lower bounds with consistent use of H^2 and log factors.
- Fix Lemma 1 sign/formatting and streamline Step 2’s “weak cluster” argument.
- Expand experiments: synthetic sweeps over k, H^2, imbalance; comparisons to prior heuristics; report actual query counts vs theory; include Las Vegas variant; discuss runtime/memory.
- Briefly outline how to handle larger/continuous alphabets (e.g., binning) and the impact on constants.
- Add a paragraph on robustness to noisy oracles and how the current method could integrate denoising.

Pros
- Strong, general lower bounds for adaptive querying with side information.
- Parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal query complexity and practical design.
- Clear and meaningful use of Hellinger divergence tying lower and upper bounds.
- Bridges theory and practice in ER/crowdsourcing and connects to SBM.

Cons
- Inconsistencies/typos in key statements; some proofs deferred.
- Limited empirical validation and no baseline comparisons.
- Assumptions on discrete support not thoroughly explored for broader settings.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The paper advances a significant new direction with compelling theory and a plausible algorithmic framework. With improved clarity and expanded experiments, it can have substantial impact.

Confidence
High.
