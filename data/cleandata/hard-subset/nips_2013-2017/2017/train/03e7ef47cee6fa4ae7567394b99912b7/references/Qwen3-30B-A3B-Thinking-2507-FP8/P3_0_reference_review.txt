

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when the algorithm has access to side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors consider a model where an oracle answers whether two points belong to the same cluster, and the algorithm has access to a similarity matrix whose entries are drawn from distributions f+ (same cluster) or f- (different clusters), with both f+ and f- unknown to the algorithm. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the query complexity of this problem, establishing both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds for algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-.

The main contribution is showing that the query complexity can be reduced from the standard O(nk) (without side information) to O(k / (log n · H²(f+||f-))), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f-. The authors develop a novel framework for proving lower bounds in the interactive setting, which is particularly challenging because standard techniques based on Fano-type inequalities do not apply. The algorithms they propose are parameter-free and computationally efficient (O(n²) time).

The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) as a special case where f+ and f- are Bernoulli distributions, but generalizes it significantly by allowing arbitrary distributions, unknown parameters, and no assumptions about cluster sizes. The authors also provide experimental results on the Cora dataset to validate their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing tight query complexity bounds for clustering with side information. The lower bound proof (Lemma 1 and Theorem 2) is particularly impressive, as it provides a framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive learning settings where standard techniques fail. The use of Hellinger divergence as the key measure is well-justified and leads to clean results.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses a practical problem in entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering, where obtaining labeled data (via oracle queries) is expensive. The theoretical results directly translate to practical benefits, as the authors demonstrate on the Cora dataset that their algorithms can achieve high accuracy with far fewer queries than naive approaches.

3. **Parameter-Free Algorithms**: The development of parameter-free algorithms is a major practical contribution. Many existing algorithms require knowledge of k or the distributions f+ and f-, which is often unavailable in practice. The authors' iterative-update approach for designing parameter-free algorithms is novel and could be applied to other problems.

4. **Comprehensive Analysis**: The paper thoroughly analyzes the problem from multiple angles, including connections to SBM, discussion of the sparse region of SBM, and implications for future research. The experimental results provide good validation of the theoretical claims.

5. **Clear Contribution Statement**: The paper clearly differentiates its contributions from previous work, particularly in Section 1 where they discuss the gap between theory and practice in existing heuristic algorithms.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The experimental section is quite limited, focusing only on the Cora dataset. To strengthen the practical relevance, the authors should include more diverse datasets (e.g., from different domains) and compare against more baseline algorithms. The comparison with "best strategy" and "worst strategy" is helpful but could be more extensive.

2. **Assumption of Perfect Oracle**: The paper assumes the oracle always returns correct answers, which is unrealistic in practice. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and mention a companion work on noisy queries, they don't discuss how the algorithms would need to change to handle noisy responses in this paper. For a practical application like entity resolution, this is a significant limitation.

3. **Technical Complexity of Lower Bound Proof**: The lower bound proof (Section 2) is quite technical and dense. While the results are strong, the proof might be difficult for many readers to follow without more detailed explanations or intuition. The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the key insights behind the lower bound.

4. **Connection to SBM Could Be Stronger**: While the paper mentions the connection to SBM, it doesn't fully explore how their results change the understanding of recovery thresholds in SBM. The conjecture about sparse SBM in Section 4 is interesting but not rigorously proven, and it would strengthen the paper to have a more concrete connection.

5. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation**: The paper doesn't discuss how the algorithms would scale to very large datasets (beyond the Cora dataset), which is a practical concern for entity resolution applications. The O(n²) time complexity might be prohibitive for very large n.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes significant progress on an important problem in interactive clustering. The paper establishes tight query complexity bounds for clustering with side information and provides efficient algorithms that nearly match these bounds. The theoretical results are rigorous and the practical implications are clear.

The main weaknesses are the limited experimental validation and the assumption of a perfect oracle. However, these do not significantly undermine the theoretical contributions, which are substantial and novel.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, the results are well-supported, and the paper makes a clear contribution to the field. The limitations noted are important to acknowledge but do not detract from the main contributions.

## Specific Comments

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper's main theoretical contribution - the lower bound framework and the matching upper bounds - is the strongest aspect of the paper. The use of Hellinger divergence as the key measure is particularly well-justified and leads to clean results.

2. **Experimental Section**: I recommend expanding the experimental section to include more datasets and more comprehensive comparisons with baseline algorithms. The current results on Cora are promising but need broader validation.

3. **Oracle Noise**: The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of how their algorithms would need to be modified to handle noisy oracle responses, even if they don't provide full analysis in this paper.

4. **Algorithm Details**: The description of the algorithms (Section 3) is clear but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the iterative-update approach works. A simple example illustrating the process would be helpful.

5. **Related Work**: The related work section is good but could be expanded to include more recent work on clustering with side information (e.g., more recent papers from the last 1-2 years).

## Final Thoughts

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The results have clear implications for practical applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering. While there are some limitations in the experimental validation and the assumption of a perfect oracle, these do not diminish the strength of the theoretical contributions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and represents a substantial advance in the field.

The work opens up many interesting directions for future research, particularly regarding the connection between query complexity and recovery thresholds in stochastic block models. I believe this paper will be of significant interest to the NIPS community and makes a strong contribution to the field.
