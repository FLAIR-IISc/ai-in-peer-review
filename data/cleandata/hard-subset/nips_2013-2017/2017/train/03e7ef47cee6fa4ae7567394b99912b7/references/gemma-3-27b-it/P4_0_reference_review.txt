This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on the trade-off between the number of queries needed to an oracle and the quality of the side information (similarity matrix). The authors establish both lower and upper bounds on query complexity, demonstrating that side information can significantly reduce the number of queries compared to clustering without it. They generalize the stochastic block model (SBM) and develop parameter-free algorithms, addressing a gap in the literature regarding theoretical guarantees for existing heuristics.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting the theoretical problem to practical applications like entity resolution where crowd-sourcing is used to improve clustering accuracy. The introduction clearly articulates the problem, relates it to previous work, and highlights the deviation between theoretical lower bounds (O(nk)) and the performance of practical heuristics.  The extension of SBM is also clearly explained, and the relevance of their work to that field is explicitly stated. The formal problem definition (Problem 1) is clear and concise. The presentation of the upper and lower bound results (Theorems 1 and 2) is well-structured.  The inclusion of experimental results is valuable, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis of a practically relevant problem. Establishing both lower and upper bounds with near-optimal performance is a significant achievement.
* **Generalization of SBM:** Extending the SBM to handle arbitrary distributions and unknown parameters broadens the applicability of the theoretical framework.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:**  The development of algorithms that do not require knowledge of the underlying distributions or the number of clusters is a practical advantage.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex theoretical arguments. The motivation is well-explained.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results, even if brief, adds credibility to the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The theoretical analysis is quite dense and may be challenging for readers not specialized in information theory and clustering.  More intuitive explanations of the key ideas (e.g., the proof of the lower bound) would be helpful.
* **Experimental Depth:** While the inclusion of experimental results is good, they are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to include more datasets and a wider range of parameter settings would strengthen the evaluation.  A comparison with existing heuristics beyond just the query count would be beneficial (e.g. clustering accuracy).
* **Logarithmic Factors:** The logarithmic factors in the bounds (e.g., O(k log n)) are not thoroughly discussed in terms of their practical impact.  A more detailed analysis of how these factors affect performance in different scenarios would be useful.
* **Connection to SBM Discussion:** While the connection to SBM is stated, the discussion feels slightly disjointed. A more cohesive integration of the SBM results into the overall narrative would improve clarity.
* **Missing Definitions:** Certain terms, such as `H2(f‖g)`, are defined in the theorems but not fully explained initially, potentially hindering understanding for readers unfamiliar with information theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of clustering with side information.
* The results are mathematically sound and contribute novel insights to the field.
* The generalization of the SBM and the development of parameter-free algorithms are valuable contributions.
* The experimental results, though limited, provide evidence of the practical relevance of the findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical density of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The logarithmic factors in the bounds require further discussion to assess their practical impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. While the technical density and limited experimental evaluation are weaknesses, the novelty and importance of the results outweigh these concerns.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on improving the clarity of the technical explanations and expanding the experimental evaluation. Specifically, adding more intuitive explanations of the key mathematical arguments and including a more detailed comparison with existing heuristics would significantly strengthen the paper.
