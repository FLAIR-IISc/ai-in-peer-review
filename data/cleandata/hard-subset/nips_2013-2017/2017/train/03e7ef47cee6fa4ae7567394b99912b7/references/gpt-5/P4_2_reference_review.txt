Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information. Given n items partitioned into an unknown number k of clusters, the learner can (i) access a pairwise similarity matrix W where each entry is drawn i.i.d. from f+ if the pair is within a cluster and from f− otherwise, and (ii) adaptively query an oracle that returns whether two items are in the same cluster. The central question is how many oracle queries are necessary and sufficient for exact recovery, as a function of n, k, and the separation between f+ and f−.

The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds for any (possibly randomized, adaptive) algorithm—even when k, f+, f− are known—showing that unless the expected number of queries is Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}), the probability of error is at least a positive constant (under a regime where the Hellinger distance H between f+ and f− is small). For zero-error recovery, Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries are necessary.
- Two parameter-free algorithms (no prior knowledge of k, f+, f−). A Monte Carlo algorithm recovers all clusters with high probability using O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries. A Las Vegas algorithm achieves O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries with probability 1, matching the lower bound up to logarithmic factors.
- A general framework for interactive lower bounds under side information, and an iterative-update design that learns the separability of f+ vs f− on the fly using Hellinger divergence, enabling near-optimal, parameter-free query strategies.

This formalizes and explains empirical observations from crowdsourced entity resolution and active clustering heuristics ([40, 42, 43, 18]) that benefit from auxiliary similarities. It generalizes stochastic block models (SBM) beyond Bernoulli edge models (e.g., [1–3, 24, 36]) by allowing arbitrary, unknown f+, f−, arbitrary k and cluster sizes, and shows how queries shift recovery requirements. The lower bound technique is distinct from Fano-style non-interactive arguments and addresses adaptivity.

Strengths:
- Quality/technical soundness: The lower bound reduces assignment of an under-queried element to a k-ary hypothesis test, bounding success via total-variation/Hellinger distance for product distributions; it carefully handles adaptivity by identifying “weakly queried” clusters. The upper bounds use an iterative estimation of intra vs inter distributions and Hellinger separation to decide when side information suffices versus when to query, with concentration arguments (via Sanov-type bounds) to control errors. The Las Vegas algorithm’s query complexity matches the lower bound up to log n, which is tight in this model.
- Originality: Provides, to my knowledge, the first general information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering with side information and parameter-free algorithms that nearly attain them. The use of Hellinger distance as the unifying measure for both bounds and algorithms is elegant and broadly applicable.
- Significance: Establishes a principled separation between clustering with and without side information; offers a reusable blueprint for interactive learning lower bounds; connects to and extends SBM theory by quantifying how queries can relax recovery thresholds.
- Clarity of ideas: The overall problem statement, model, and high-level algorithmic approach are well-motivated and positioned with respect to entity resolution and SBM literature.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Monte Carlo bound vs lower bound: The Monte Carlo algorithm is stated to use O(min{nk, (k log n)/H^2}) queries, while the lower bound for small H requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}). This appears to contradict the lower bound when k^2/H^2 ≤ nk (the upper bound would be smaller by a factor ≈ k/log n). The Las Vegas algorithm matches the lower bound up to log n, so the likely intent is that only the Las Vegas algorithm is tight. Please clarify the precise regimes, success probabilities, and whether the Monte Carlo guarantee is a typo (e.g., should it be O(min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2})) or if additional assumptions reconcile the bounds.
- Distributional assumptions for upper bounds: The algorithms assume discrete f+, f− over a fixed finite support (or finite support growing linearly) and i.i.d. W entries conditioned on the clustering. The lower bound allows continuous distributions. Please make explicit the dependence of constants and sample complexity on support size q, and discuss robustness when W is not i.i.d. (common in ER where features induce dependencies).
- Use of Sanov with Hellinger: The analysis adapts Sanov’s theorem (KL-based large deviations) to control Hellinger concentration. Providing a more explicit statement/derivation of the key concentration lemma (Lemma 3) and constants would improve reproducibility.
- Computational and data access model: Algorithms require O(n^2) time to process W and assume full access to all pairwise similarities. In ER-scale settings, computing/storing W may dominate querying costs. A discussion of sparse/blocked W or streaming access would broaden applicability.
- Experiments: The empirical evaluation is limited to one dataset, no comparison to state-of-the-art active ER/clustering baselines ([40, 43], and follow-ups), and no ablations on k, H, or robustness to model misspecification. While the paper is theory-centric, a stronger empirical section would better substantiate practical impact.
- Presentation: There are several typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., H vs H2, “Hellinger square divergence” terminology, missing parentheses in bounds, minor formatting issues). Providing pseudocode and clearly defining constants (e.g., C in thresholds) would aid clarity.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: Strong theoretical development; proofs appear sound, with one potential inconsistency to clarify regarding the Monte Carlo bound. Assumptions are clearly stated for the theoretical model.
- Clarity: Generally readable with a clear structure; some sections (algorithm phases, concentration arguments) would benefit from more explicit constants, pseudocode, and cleaned-up notation.
- Originality: High. New lower bound framework for adaptive interactive clustering with side information and parameter-free, near-optimal algorithms.
- Significance: High theoretical significance; practical significance promising but under-evaluated empirically.

Pros for acceptance:
- Near-tight information-theoretic bounds establishing the power of side information.
- Novel, parameter-free algorithms leveraging learned Hellinger separation.
- Generalization of SBM insights and a framework potentially useful in other interactive learning problems.
- Clear connection to impactful applications like entity resolution.

Cons against acceptance:
- Apparent mismatch between the Monte Carlo upper bound and the lower bound needs resolution.
- Upper-bound analysis relies on finite-support discrete distributions and i.i.d. side information; robustness not evaluated.
- Limited empirical validation and no baseline comparisons.
- Some presentation/notation issues that hinder accessibility.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the Monte Carlo query bound versus lower bound and specify regimes/success probabilities precisely.
- State the extension conditions to continuous distributions (for upper bounds) or discuss discretization effects.
- Add pseudocode, unify notation for Hellinger, and provide constants in threshold definitions.
- Expand experiments with additional datasets, comparisons to prior heuristics, and sensitivity to H and k; discuss computational cost of constructing/using W.

Overall, the paper offers substantial theoretical contributions on an important and timely problem and would be strengthened by clarifying the Monte Carlo bound and improving empirical and presentation aspects.
