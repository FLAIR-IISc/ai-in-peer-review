Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm has access to a pairwise “side information” matrix W whose entries are drawn from f+ if two points are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise, with f+, f− unknown. An oracle answers “same-cluster?” queries on pairs. The central questions are how the presence of W changes the number of queries needed, and what are tight (or near-tight) bounds.

Main contributions:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorem 2) for algorithms (even randomized) with knowledge of k, f+, f−: to correctly recover the clustering with constant success probability requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) expected queries, and for success with probability 1 requires Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries. This holds even for adaptive querying and general (discrete or continuous) f+, f− when H^2 is small.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms that do not know k, f+, f−. A Monte Carlo algorithm achieves recovery with high probability and a Las Vegas algorithm succeeds with probability 1. The algorithms leverage an iterative scheme to (i) bootstrap a “grown” cluster to a size threshold Θ((log n)/H^2), (ii) estimate empirical intra/inter distributions and their Hellinger divergence, and (iii) place remaining points using the Membership score based on Hellinger distances, resorting to queries only for a small “gray region.” The stated query complexities match the lower bound up to logarithmic factors (though see clarity concerns below).
- Conceptual connection to stochastic block models (SBM): the model generalizes SBM to arbitrary unknown f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes, and the lower-bound framework addresses the interactive (adaptive) setting where standard Fano-type arguments do not directly apply.

This work relates to and generalizes community detection results in SBM (e.g., [1,2,3,24,36]) by allowing general side-information distributions and interactive queries, and to practical heuristics for ER/crowdsourced clustering [40, 42, 43] by providing theoretical bounds and parameter-free algorithms. It also relates to clustering with same-cluster queries [5,13] but differs in objective (exact recovery with side information, unknown parameters) and in using information-theoretic lower bounds.

Quality:
- Technical soundness (lower bounds): The lower bound proof strategy is carefully set up as a k-hypothesis testing instance for a “lightly queried” element, combining (i) identification of “weak” clusters (low query probability) under a chosen hypothesis, (ii) a decomposition of success events, and (iii) bounding total variation via Hellinger divergence and product structure to quantify indistinguishability given few queries. The constants and conditions (H^2 ≤ 1/18) are reasonable for the “close” regime. This appears solid and addresses the adaptive nature of queries, which is nontrivial.
- Technical soundness (upper bounds): The algorithmic design is plausible: use empirical Hellinger distances between intra/inter distributions estimated from grown clusters and classification via Membership, with Sanov-type concentration adapted to Hellinger to justify thresholds and correctness outside a small uncertainty band. The analysis outline (convergence of the iterative-update, correctness of Phase 3A/3B decisions, and bounded size of the gray region) is coherent. Full proofs are deferred to appendices; the main lemmas stated align with known large deviations tools for discrete distributions. One limitation is that the algorithm and its analysis assume discrete distributions with fixed support; the lower bounds cover continuous distributions, yielding a gap in generality between lower and upper bounds.
- Experiments: A single dataset (cora) evaluation illustrates practical behavior and that approximate f+, f− suffice, and compares “best/worst/greedy” query selection strategies. However, the empirical section is limited: no comparisons to prior heuristics, no scaling study vs k, H^2, or n, and no verification of asymptotic query complexity predictions.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clear problem statement, motivation via ER, and a high-level algorithm description. The lower-bound proof sketch is readable.
- There are notable notation and typographical issues that hinder clarity:
  - Inconsistent use of H vs H^2 for Hellinger divergence; the paper defines H^2 but sometimes writes H(·‖·) in denominators or thresholds. Please standardize.
  - Big-O expressions lack parentheses or division symbols in multiple places, e.g., “O( k logn H2(f+‖f−) )” vs “O(k log n / H^2)”. Later, the Las Vegas bound is given as O(n log n + min(nk, k^2 log n / H^2)), consistent with the lower bound up to a log factor; the Monte Carlo bound appears inconsistent with Theorem 2 unless it is also k^2 log n / H^2. Clarify and correct all occurrences.
  - Some sentences are truncated or have formatting artifacts; careful proofreading is needed.

Originality:
- The lower bound framework for interactive clustering with side information appears novel and addresses adaptivity beyond classical Fano-style arguments.
- The parameter-free iterative-update algorithm that learns effective thresholds from side information, using Hellinger rather than KL to obtain metric properties in the analysis, is a meaningful methodological contribution.
- The explicit dependence of query complexity on H^2(f+‖f−) provides a clean measure that ties to side-information quality.

Significance:
- Establishing (near-)tight query complexity bounds quantifies the value of side information in interactive clustering, bridging a practical gap observed in ER/crowdsourcing.
- The techniques could influence related interactive/active settings (e.g., community detection with label queries, hypothesis testing with adaptive side information).
- The assumption gap (upper bounds for discrete fixed-support f+, f−) slightly limits generality; nonetheless, the results are broadly relevant.

Pros:
- Strong, conceptually clean information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive querying with side information.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with near-matching bounds; clear use of Hellinger divergence and iterative threshold estimation.
- Connections to SBM and crowdsourced ER; problem is well-motivated and within NeurIPS scope.

Cons:
- Notational inconsistencies and likely typos in key complexity expressions; potential apparent mismatch between the Monte Carlo upper bound and the lower bound needs clarification.
- Upper-bound assumptions (discrete fixed support) vs lower-bound generality.
- Limited experimental evaluation; no comparisons to prior heuristics and no scaling studies.
- Some proofs deferred; stronger main-text intuition for key lemmas (e.g., anti-concentration for Waiting(C)) would aid comprehension.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Unify notation for Hellinger divergence and correct all big-O expressions; explicitly reconcile the Monte Carlo query complexity with Theorem 2.
- State clearly the support-size assumptions for f+, f− in the algorithmic results and discuss extensions (e.g., discretization or kernels) to continuous settings.
- Add comparisons to existing interactive ER/clustering heuristics and include ablations showing query complexity vs k and H^2 on synthetic data.
- Provide more intuition in the main text for why Hellinger (metric property) is essential and how the iterative-update converges.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with potential impact, pending clarification of the stated complexities and improved presentation.
