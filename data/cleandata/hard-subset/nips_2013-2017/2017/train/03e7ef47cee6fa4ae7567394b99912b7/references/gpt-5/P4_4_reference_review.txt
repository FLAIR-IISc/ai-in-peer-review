Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm can (i) adaptively query a noiseless same-cluster oracle on pairs and (ii) access side information W, a similarity matrix whose entries are sampled from f+ for within-cluster pairs and from f− for cross-cluster pairs. The key feature is that k, f+, and f− are unknown to the algorithm. The authors develop:

- Information-theoretic lower bounds on the number of (adaptive) queries needed to exactly recover the clustering, expressed in terms of k, n, and the statistical separation between f+ and f− measured via Hellinger divergence.
- Two parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that use W and same-cluster queries to achieve exact recovery with high probability or probability 1, respectively, with query complexity nearly matching the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors.

Without side information, the classical query complexity is Θ(nk) (e.g., [13, 40]). The paper explains why practical heuristics often use far fewer queries: they leverage side information, which can substantially reduce query complexity when f+ and f− are separable. The model strictly generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary (unknown) distributions for f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes and unknown k, connecting to a substantial literature on SBM recovery thresholds [1, 2, 24, 36] and recent active/interactive variants [19, 39–41]. The work is also positioned against interactive clustering and crowdsourcing heuristics [40, 42, 43] and theoretical studies of noisy comparisons and k-means with feedback [5].

Quality
- Lower bounds: The core technical result (Theorem 2, via Lemma 1) gives a lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) expected queries (and Ω(n + min{…}) for success probability 1), even when k and the distributions are known to the algorithm. The proof handles adaptivity by reducing assignment of a low-queried element to a k-ary hypothesis test, controlling the probability of correct assignment via total variation and Hellinger distance, and carefully accounting for “weakly queried” clusters. This addresses a nontrivial gap where standard Fano-type arguments are insufficient under adaptivity.
- Upper bounds: The Monte Carlo algorithm uses an iterative-update scheme to estimate p+ and p− from current clusters and uses Hellinger divergence to decide when side information is reliable enough to assign elements without queries; otherwise it issues a small number of queries to grow clusters until the estimates stabilize. The Las Vegas variant guarantees correctness and adds an O(n log n) term. The analysis uses Sanov’s theorem adapted to Hellinger geometry, showing convergence when a cluster reaches Θ(log n / H^2(f+‖f−)) size and bounding the number of ambiguous elements that need queries.

Technical concerns and requests for clarification:
- Apparent inconsistency in query complexities. The statement of Theorem 1 appears to claim a Monte Carlo query complexity O(min{nk, k log n / H^2(f+‖f−)}), whereas Theorem 2’s lower bound is Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2(f+‖f−)}). If the Monte Carlo bound is indeed O(k log n / H^2) in the regime where this dominates, it would contradict the lower bound for k ≫ log n. Later, the Las Vegas bound is given as O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}), which matches the lower bound up to a log n factor. Please clarify the precise dependence on k in Theorem 1 and ensure consistent use of H vs H^2 throughout. If Theorem 1 should read O(k^2 log n / H^2), state so explicitly.
- Divergence notation: The paper alternates between H and H^2 for Hellinger divergences in bounds and inequalities (e.g., TV vs Hellinger bounds). Please standardize the definition and track squared vs unsquared forms consistently in statements and proofs.
- Model assumptions: The upper-bound analysis assumes discrete distributions with fixed support size. Clarify how the algorithm and guarantees extend to (i) larger or growing finite supports, and (ii) continuous distributions (beyond the lower bound). If discretization is required, quantify its effect on query complexity and recovery guarantees.
- Proof details: In Lemma 1, the steps bounding total variation by Hellinger distance seem to omit constants (e.g., TV ≤ √2 · H). Please include exact constants and verify the dependence on a and H(f+‖f−) vs H^2. Similarly, the derivation of the “weak cluster” set and union bounds would benefit from a more explicit accounting of constants.
- Practicality: The algorithm’s time is O(n^2) to process W; this may be the bottleneck in realistic settings. Discuss whether sampling submatrices of W or streaming approximations could retain guarantees while reducing memory/time.
- Oracle noise: While a companion paper treats noisy queries, a brief discussion of how to incorporate majority voting or reliability estimation (and the resulting query inflation) would help practitioners.
- Experiments: The empirical section is limited to one dataset, no comparisons to prior heuristics [40, 42, 43, 18], and no synthetic sweeps over k, n, and H^2(f+‖f−). The reported H^2 ≈ 0.6 indicates fairly strong separability. To substantiate the claimed scaling, include experiments varying H^2, k, and n, and report query counts and accuracy, plus comparisons to baselines and to a no-side-information strategy.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear problem statement, intuitive motivation, and a good high-level description of the algorithms. However, several formulae suffer from typesetting ambiguities (k vs k^2 and H vs H^2), and some constants and conditions are hard to track. Consolidating notation, explicitly stating all assumptions (e.g., support size), and adding a short pseudocode block would improve readability.

Originality
- Introduces an interactive lower-bound framework for clustering with side information under adaptivity, which appears novel and of independent interest.
- Provides parameter-free algorithms that do not require k or f+, f−, achieving near-optimal query complexity governed by Hellinger separation. This is a new angle relative to prior SBMs and heuristic ER methods.

Significance
- Clarifies the role of side information in reducing query complexity, helping reconcile practice and theory in crowdsourced/active clustering.
- The techniques (Hellinger-based Sanov analysis; adaptive lower bounds) are likely reusable in related interactive inference settings.
- The connection to SBM and the conjectured shift in sparse recovery thresholds with queries is thought-provoking.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically motivated problem with a clean, general model.
- Strong theoretical contributions: adaptive lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms with near-matching guarantees.
- Methodological novelty (iterative-update; Hellinger-based analysis) of potential broader impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Possible inconsistency/typos in key complexity bounds (k vs k^2); needs clarification.
- Limited empirical validation and no comparisons to established baselines.
- Upper-bound assumptions (fixed discrete support, full W) may limit immediate applicability; extensions are not fully quantified.

Overall
Subject to clarifying the query complexity dependence on k and tightening notation, this is a solid theoretical contribution with clear novelty and potential impact. Strengthening the experimental section and resolving the noted clarity issues would considerably improve the paper.
