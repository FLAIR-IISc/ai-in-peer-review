## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with an oracle, focusing on the impact of side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The core problem is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data is expensive, and automated clustering based solely on similarity matrices often fails. The authors establish strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on the query complexity, demonstrating a significant reduction in queries needed when side information is available – from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)).

The work builds upon existing research in interactive clustering (Balcan et al., 2016), stochastic block models (Abbe et al., 2015, 2017), and query complexity (Dyer & Frieze, 1991). It distinguishes itself by providing a rigorous theoretical analysis in the interactive setting with potentially arbitrary similarity distributions (f+ and f−), a crucial departure from prior work often relying on specific distributional assumptions or heuristic algorithms. The connection drawn to the stochastic block model is insightful, framing the side information as a generalization of the planted partition model.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers tight bounds on query complexity, both lower and upper, providing a fundamental understanding of the problem.
* **Novelty:** The analysis of clustering with arbitrary side information distributions is a significant contribution.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f−, making them practically relevant.
* **Clear Motivation:** The application to entity resolution provides a compelling real-world context.
* **Connection to SBM:** The link to the well-studied SBM provides a valuable perspective and potential for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Presentation:** While technically sound, the paper is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key ideas, particularly the iterative update algorithm. The notation is also quite heavy.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are limited. While they demonstrate the algorithm's performance on a single dataset, a more comprehensive evaluation with varying parameters and datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Constant Factors:** The logarithmic factor in the upper bound, while theoretically acceptable, could be significant in practice. Further optimization to reduce constant factors would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical guarantees. A discussion of the practical challenges of estimating f+ and f− from real-world data would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of interactive clustering.
* The results are novel and contribute to a fundamental understanding of the role of side information.
* The parameter-free algorithms are a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and heavy notation may make it difficult for some readers to fully grasp the technical details.
* The limited experimental evaluation weakens the practical impact of the results.



**Overall:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and address an important problem. Addressing the clarity and experimental evaluation concerns would further enhance its impact. The authors should consider expanding the experimental section and providing more intuitive explanations of the algorithms.
