Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when an oracle answers same-cluster queries, and the learner also has access to side information in the form of a similarity matrix W. Under a generative model in which Wuv is drawn i.i.d. from f+ if u,v are co-cluster and from f− otherwise (with f+, f− unknown), the authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and design efficient, parameter-free algorithms with nearly matching upper bounds. The main message is that side information can drastically reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) (without W) to roughly Õ(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries, where H^2 = H^2(f+‖f−) is the squared Hellinger divergence; the remaining Õ hides logarithmic factors in n.

The lower bounds introduce a framework for adaptive, interactive settings where standard Fano-type arguments are insufficient. The upper bounds are achieved by a Monte Carlo algorithm and a Las Vegas variant. A core idea is to estimate intra- and inter-cluster distributions from queried seeds and then use Hellinger-based membership tests; an iterative-update scheme adaptively increases the reliability of these estimates until the side information suffices to place most remaining points without querying. The work generalizes and connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) (e.g., Bernoulli f+, f−) but allows arbitrary (unknown) f+, f−, unknown k, and adversarial cluster sizes. It relates to NIPS and neighboring literature on active learning/crowdsourcing (e.g., Karger et al. NIPS’11), interactive clustering heuristics, and information-theoretic limits for SBM (e.g., Abbe–Sandon).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The lower bound (Theorem 2) is carefully constructed for the adaptive setting via a reduction to k-way hypothesis testing on an element that has been queried only a few times, controlling total variation by Hellinger divergence and using product structure to bound divergence accumulation. Assumptions and steps are explicit. The need for new lower-bound tools in adaptive settings is compelling.
- Upper bounds: The parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm uses Sanov’s theorem and Hellinger as a metric to derive stopping thresholds ME ≈ C log n/H^2 and to argue that once a “grown” cluster exists, most remaining points can be placed using W with high probability; a Las Vegas version adds O(n log n) overhead. The analysis argues concentration of empirical intra/inter distributions and bounds the size of gray zones requiring queries. The overall logic is plausible and well motivated.
- Caveats: There is a notational inconsistency regarding the k vs k^2 dependence in the upper bounds (e.g., abstract and Theorem 1 differ). The Monte Carlo bound is claimed as O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) in one place and O(min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}) elsewhere; this should be reconciled. Assumptions for upper bounds include discrete f+, f− with fixed support; extensions to continuous domains are asserted for lower bounds but not algorithmically.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized; the problem setting, assumptions, and high-level ideas are clearly introduced. The iterative-update design is explained intuitively before formal statements.
- Notational/typographical issues (e.g., inconsistent k vs k^2, missing parentheses around H^2 terms, occasional formatting glitches) impede precision. Constants (C, B) that appear in thresholds could be summarized in a clean theorem statement. The membership test inequalities in Phase 3 would benefit from a simpler presentation and intuition.
- The proofs for algorithms are deferred; this is acceptable for NIPS, but the main paper could better highlight the key lemmas and their implications.

Originality:
- The interactive lower bound framework for side-information-driven clustering with adaptive queries and unknown parameters is novel and addresses a gap between practical heuristics and theory.
- The parameter-free algorithm that learns to calibrate the role of W via Hellinger-based empirical estimates and stopping thresholds is a neat idea and, to my knowledge, new in this setting.
- The explicit Hellinger-based characterization gives a clean, distribution-agnostic measure of side information quality, unifying and strictly generalizing SBM results.

Significance:
- The results sharply explain why practical systems that leverage a similarity matrix can beat Θ(nk) querying, quantifying improvements in terms of H^2(f+‖f−).
- The framework is likely to influence future work on active learning with side information, community detection with queries, and interactive lower bounds.
- Practical impact is promising but empirical validation is limited. Experiments are on a single ER dataset (cora) with simple strategies; there is no direct comparison to prior ER/querying heuristics. Still, the main contribution is theoretical.

Pros:
- Strong, well-motivated information-theoretic lower bounds in an adaptive setting.
- Nearly matching, efficient, parameter-free algorithms; clear dependence on H^2 and log n.
- Generalizes SBM; provides a principled lens on the power of side information.
- Clean conceptual tools (Hellinger metric, Sanov-based thresholds) of broader interest.

Cons:
- Inconsistency in the k vs k^2 factor across statements; needs clarification.
- Upper-bound analysis assumes discrete fixed-support f+, f−; practical similarity signals are often continuous or heavy-tailed.
- Independence assumptions on W given clusters may be unrealistic in ER; robustness to model misspecification is not explored.
- Experiments are limited in scope and do not benchmark against established heuristics; scalability beyond O(n^2) memory/time is not addressed.
- A remaining log n gap between lower and upper bounds.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile all query complexity statements and unify notation for Hellinger divergence (H vs H^2).
- State assumptions (discrete vs continuous, support size) prominently; discuss discretization or kernel estimators to handle continuous W.
- Add experiments varying H^2 on synthetic data to validate the predicted scaling; include comparisons to query-selection heuristics from [40, 43] and related works.
- Discuss robustness to dependent or biased similarity matrices and to oracle noise (even briefly, referencing the companion paper).
- Clarify constants and thresholds in Phase 3 and provide a schematic of the algorithm flow.

Overall recommendation:
A strong theoretical paper that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information, with novel lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms and a clean information-theoretic characterization. Despite clarity and empirical limitations, the contribution is substantial and well within NIPS scope. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on fixing inconsistencies and improving presentation.
