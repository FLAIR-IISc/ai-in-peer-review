

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information, where an algorithm can query an oracle about pairwise cluster membership while also having access to a similarity matrix representing noisy pairwise relationships. The authors establish both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on query complexity, demonstrating that side information can dramatically reduce the number of queries needed from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f⁺‖f⁻)), where H² denotes the squared Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity values within and across clusters. The paper connects this work to the stochastic block model (SBM), showing that their model generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻ without assumptions about cluster sizes or k.

The paper's main strengths are its technical depth and the novelty of its theoretical framework. The lower bound proof (Theorem 2) is particularly impressive, as it establishes a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which is challenging due to the adaptive nature of queries. The authors successfully handle the interactive aspect by carefully characterizing the set of elements that are not queried much by the algorithm, a significant technical achievement. The upper bound algorithms are also noteworthy for being parameter-free (they work without knowledge of k, f⁺, or f⁻), which is highly valuable for practical applications. The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide empirical validation of the theoretical findings, showing that the greedy strategy closely matches the best-case performance with only a small increase in queries.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature, distinguishing itself from previous work in several ways. Unlike [5] which considers a different clustering setting with metric distance matrices and computational lower bounds, this paper provides information-theoretic bounds for arbitrary distributions. It also generalizes SBM studies [1, 2, 28] by allowing arbitrary distributions and not requiring assumptions about cluster sizes or k. The paper's connection to entity resolution applications (discussed in Section 1) is particularly compelling, as it explains the gap between theoretical Ω(nk) bounds and the practical observation that heuristic algorithms use far fewer queries.

Some minor weaknesses include the lack of comparison with related work on clustering with noisy queries [34], which is mentioned as a companion work but not integrated into the main paper. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the results compare to recent work on active learning for community detection [19]. Additionally, while the paper states that the algorithms are computationally efficient (with time complexity O(n²)), it does not provide a thorough analysis of the practical scalability of these algorithms for very large datasets.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information
- Establishes tight information-theoretic bounds with a novel proof technique
- Develops parameter-free algorithms that are practically important
- Demonstrates strong connections to SBM and community detection
- Includes experimental validation on real-world data
- Offers significant potential for future work in interactive learning

Arguments against acceptance:
- The log n gap between lower and upper bounds is somewhat unsatisfying
- The paper would benefit from more detailed comparison with related work on noisy queries
- The practical scalability for extremely large n is not thoroughly addressed

The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level. The quality is excellent, with technically sound contributions that are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experiments. The clarity is good, with a well-structured presentation that makes the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The originality is strong, as the work significantly advances the understanding of interactive clustering with side information beyond previous heuristic approaches. The significance is substantial, as the results have both theoretical importance for information theory and practical relevance for applications like entity resolution where human input is expensive.

The paper's main contribution is the rigorous characterization of how side information reduces query complexity, which has been observed empirically but never proven theoretically before. This work provides a theoretical foundation for the common practice of using similarity matrices to guide query selection in interactive clustering. The connection to SBM also opens up new research directions for understanding how query complexity affects community detection thresholds.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and represents the highest quality of work expected at NIPS. The theoretical results are strong, the algorithms are practical, and the connections to both theory and applications are compelling. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions, and the work is clearly suitable for publication at NIPS 2017.
