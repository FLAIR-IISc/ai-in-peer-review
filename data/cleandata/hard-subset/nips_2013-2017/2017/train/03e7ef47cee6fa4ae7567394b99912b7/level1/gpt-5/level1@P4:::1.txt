Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when an oracle answers pairwise same-cluster queries and the algorithm has access to side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The side information is generated by an i.i.d. model: within-cluster pairs are drawn from an unknown distribution f+, across-cluster pairs from an unknown distribution f−. The main results are information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds for the number of oracle queries required to exactly recover the clusters.

Without side information, the known bounds are Θ(nk) queries even for randomized algorithms. With side information, the paper shows that the query complexity can be reduced dramatically to O(k log n / H2(f+‖f−)) for a Monte Carlo algorithm (succeeds w.h.p.) and O(n log n + k^2 log n / H2(f+‖f−)) for a Las Vegas algorithm (succeeds with probability 1), where H2 is squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−. The paper also gives lower bounds of Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H2(f+‖f−)}) (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2 / H2}) for success probability 1), holding even if k, f+, f− are known to the algorithm and even allowing adaptive queries. Thus the bounds match up to a log n factor. The algorithms are parameter-free (do not assume knowledge of k, f+, f−), and run in time O(n^2).

The work generalizes and connects to community detection in the stochastic block model (SBM), where f+, f− are Bernoulli(p), Bernoulli(q). It complements prior NIPS and related work on SBM thresholds and recovery (e.g., Abbe et al., Mossel et al.) by quantifying how access to pairwise queries changes the recovery requirements. It is also related to interactive clustering and crowdsourced entity resolution heuristics (Awasthi et al., Firmani et al., Wang et al.), and to prior theoretical work on same-cluster queries under metric separation assumptions (Ashtiani et al.), but differs by giving information-theoretic lower bounds and parameter-free algorithms for general, unknown f+, f−.

Strengths:
- Strong, general lower bounds: The interactive/adaptive nature of queries makes standard Fano-type arguments inadequate. The paper develops a careful reduction to a multi-hypothesis testing instance on a low-queried element and controls total variation via Hellinger divergence, yielding bounds that hold for randomized adaptive algorithms and even when k, f+, f− are known. This is technically nontrivial and broadly useful.
- Nearly matching upper bounds with parameter-free algorithms: The iterative-update Monte Carlo algorithm uses only the side information and a small number of queries to bootstrap empirical estimates of f+, f− and a data-driven threshold, relying on Sanov-type concentration for Hellinger distance. It achieves O(k log n / H2) queries and does not require k, f+, f−. The Las Vegas variant ensures correctness with probability 1 at the cost of an extra additive O(n log n) and a factor k.
- Clear quantification of the value of side information: The complexity improves from Θ(nk) to O(k polylog(n)/H2), with an explicit dependence on statistical separation H2(f+‖f−), matching the intuition that stronger side information reduces querying needs.
- Conceptual connection to SBM: The model includes SBM as a special case and suggests how active queries change recovery thresholds.
- Practical relevance: The membership scoring via Hellinger distance between empirical intra/inter distributions is simple, and the algorithm runs in O(n^2). A small experimental study on a real entity-resolution dataset demonstrates that a greedy strategy guided by learned distributions uses orders of magnitude fewer queries than nk while achieving high F1.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Assumptions on side information: The algorithmic analysis assumes f+, f− are discrete over a finite support (with fixed cardinality). While the lower bounds handle continuous distributions, the extension of the algorithm and its guarantees beyond discrete support is not fully developed.
- Independence model: The i.i.d. generation of pairwise similarities conditioned on cluster memberships may be unrealistic for many real datasets where similarities are correlated. Robustness to model misspecification is not analyzed.
- Noiseless oracle: The main results assume error-free queries; practical scenarios often have noisy crowd labels. The companion work addresses noisy queries, but integrating noise robustness here would increase completeness.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are limited to one dataset and do not include comparisons to existing active/crowdsourced clustering algorithms or entity resolution systems. Reported strategies (best/worst/greedy) are useful ablations, but broader evaluation would strengthen significance claims.
- Presentation issues: There are minor inconsistencies in the statement of bounds (e.g., O(k log n / H2) vs O(k^2 log n / H2) in different places), some typos, and reliance on appendices for algorithm details. Explicit pseudocode and clearer constants/thresholds would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical results appear sound, with careful probabilistic arguments (Sanov-based concentration for Hellinger divergence, TV–Hellinger relations, and adaptive lower bound construction). The bounds align and the algorithmic complexity and correctness guarantees are well supported. Some constants and details are deferred to the supplement, which is common for NIPS.
- Clarity: Generally well organized, with a clear problem statement, model, and main theorems. However, notation is occasionally inconsistent and some equations are difficult to parse. The algorithm would benefit from concise pseudocode and a more explicit discussion of the update/termination conditions.
- Originality: High. The formulation of interactive clustering with general unknown f+, f− side information, the parameter-free query strategy, and the adaptive information-theoretic lower bounds are novel relative to prior NIPS and related work.
- Significance: High. The results quantify a practically important regime (active clustering with side information) and provide broadly applicable techniques. The dependence on Hellinger divergence provides an interpretable measure of side information quality.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Unify the statements of query complexity (k vs k^2 factors) across abstract, theorems, and discussion.
- Provide succinct pseudocode for the Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms in the main text and specify constants (e.g., C, B) and thresholds more explicitly.
- Discuss memory/time considerations for large n (sparse similarity matrices, streaming computation).
- Add experiments on additional datasets and comparisons to baselines, including recent active entity resolution/clustering methods; evaluate robustness to deviations from the i.i.d. model and to approximate/continuous f+, f−.
- Briefly outline how the algorithm and its analysis extend to continuous distributions (e.g., via discretization or kernel density estimates).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, general lower bounds for adaptive interactive clustering.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with near-optimal query complexity and clear dependence on side information quality.
- Meaningful connection to SBM and active learning in networks.
- Addresses a practically motivated and timely problem.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Algorithmic assumptions (discrete i.i.d. side information, noiseless oracle) limit immediate applicability; robustness not analyzed.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Minor clarity/presentation issues and reliance on supplementary material for key details.

Overall, the paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to interactive clustering with side information, with techniques and bounds likely to be influential.
