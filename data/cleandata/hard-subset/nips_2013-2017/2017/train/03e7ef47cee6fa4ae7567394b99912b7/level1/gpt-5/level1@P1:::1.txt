Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm can ask pairwise same-cluster queries to an oracle, in the presence of a noisy similarity matrix W (side information). Entries of W are drawn i.i.d. from f+ if the pair is within a cluster and f− otherwise; both f+, f− and the number of clusters k are unknown to the algorithm. The main results are (i) information-theoretic lower bounds showing that any (possibly randomized) algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries in expectation (or Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1), where H^2 = H^2(f+||f−) is squared Hellinger divergence; and (ii) efficient, parameter-free algorithms whose query complexity nearly matches these bounds within logarithmic factors—Monte Carlo with O(k^2 log n/H^2) and Las Vegas with O(n log n + k^2 log n/H^2) queries (assuming the k^2 dependence; see clarity issues below). The paper develops a lower-bound technique for interactive settings and an iterative-update algorithm that estimates inter/intra distributions from side information to determine when to rely on queries versus W, with analysis via Sanov-type large deviations and Hellinger metrics.

This work generalizes and formalizes the role of side information beyond the stochastic block model (SBM), which is recovered as a special case (Bernoulli f+, f−). It complements active community detection (e.g., [19]) and significantly advances over prior heuristics for crowdsourced entity resolution that used W but lacked guarantees [40, 42, 43] and analyses of specific heuristics [18, 33]. It differs from [5], which studies k-means with metric/separation assumptions and parameter knowledge.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Clear and rigorous demonstration of how side information can reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) to near O(k^2/H^2), with matching lower/upper bounds up to log n.
- Generality: f+, f− can be arbitrary (discrete; lower bounds also handle continuous), k unknown, cluster sizes adversarial; algorithms are parameter-free.
- Lower-bound technique: A thoughtful interactive-information framework leveraging Hellinger distance and hypothesis testing, overcoming limitations of standard Fano bounds for adaptive queries; likely of independent interest.
- Algorithmic novelty: Iterative-update approach to learn p+ and p− on the fly and to decide when W is reliable; clean use of Hellinger as a metric enabling triangle-type arguments.
- Relation to SBM: Articulates links and poses natural questions about how queries shift SBM thresholds.
- Efficiency: Time O(n^2) dominated by reading W; query complexity polylog in n and near-optimal in k and divergence.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Inconsistency in bounds: The Monte Carlo query complexity is stated as O(k log n/H^2) in Theorem 1 but elsewhere (abstract, Las Vegas bound, and lower bound) the dependence is k^2/H^2. To match the Ω(k^2/H^2) lower bound within a log factor, the Monte Carlo upper bound should presumably be O(k^2 log n/H^2). Please reconcile and fix throughout.
- Assumptions on distributions: Algorithms assume discrete f+, f− over fixed support; extensions to continuous or growing-support settings are asserted but not developed (e.g., required binning, sensitivity to discretization). Practical guidance on handling real-valued similarities would strengthen the contribution.
- Technical conditions: The lower bound assumes H^2 ≤ 1/18 and starts from equal-size clusters in Lemma 1 before reductions; while reasonable, these conditions (and constants) are somewhat opaque. A high-level intuition for the k^2 factor and a discussion of how imbalance affects the bound would help readability.
- Experimental evaluation: Limited to one real dataset (cora) and to a stylized evaluation that presumes the true cluster has already been initialized and considers “best/worst/greedy” query ordering. There is no comparison to existing practical methods [40, 42, 43] or to a no-side-information baseline, and no synthetic scaling study varying H^2, k, n as suggested by the theory. The results demonstrate feasibility but do not stress-test the predicted dependencies.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation and constants (C, thresholds τx/ME, success probabilities) are dense; pseudo-code or a clearer algorithm box would help. A few typos and incomplete citations (years missing) are distracting. The relationship between Monte Carlo success probability (1 − 1/n) and Las Vegas guarantees (always correct; query bound holds w.h.p.) should be stated crisply.
- Modeling assumptions: W’s entries are i.i.d. conditional on labels; real ER similarities can be dependent. While standard in SBM-style analyses, a brief discussion of robustness to dependence or model misspecification would be valuable.
- Noisy oracle: Real crowds may be noisy; while addressed in a companion work [34], a short discussion of how the present techniques might extend would broaden applicability.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong; the lower bound proof strategy is careful, and the upper bound analysis uses appropriate concentration tools (Sanov) and metric properties of Hellinger. However, the k vs k^2 inconsistency must be resolved. Proof details reside in appendices; main text gives the right intuition.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable for a theory audience, but the algorithm description and constants could be streamlined. Fixing typos and notational inconsistencies is important.

Originality
- High. The interactive lower-bound framework and parameter-free near-optimal algorithms for clustering with side information represent a significant, novel step beyond prior NIPS/SBM/ER work.

Significance
- High potential impact: clarifies the role of side information in interactive clustering, provides nearly tight bounds, and offers tools likely to influence future work in active clustering and information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive learning.

Pros for acceptance
- Near-tight, general bounds and parameter-free, efficient algorithms.
- Innovative lower-bound methodology for adaptive querying.
- Bridges theory and practice by explaining observed query reductions when using side information.

Cons against acceptance
- Formula inconsistency (k vs k^2) is serious and must be corrected.
- Experiments are limited and do not validate scaling predictions or compare to baselines.
- Some assumptions (discrete support, independence) and constants are not fully discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reconcile and uniformly state the k^2/H^2 dependence; ensure the Monte Carlo bound matches the lower bound up to log n.
- Add synthetic experiments varying H^2, k, n to verify query scaling; include comparisons to practical heuristics.
- Provide a brief section on handling continuous similarities (discretization strategy) and robustness to mild dependence in W.
- Tighten presentation: add algorithm pseudo-code, clarify success and complexity guarantees, and polish references.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are substantial, original, and broadly relevant to NIPS. With corrected bounds and strengthened empirical evaluation, this would be a clear accept.
