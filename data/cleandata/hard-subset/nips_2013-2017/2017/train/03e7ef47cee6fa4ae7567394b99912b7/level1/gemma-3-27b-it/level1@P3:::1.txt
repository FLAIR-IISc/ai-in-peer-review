This paper tackles the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix. The core contribution is establishing tight (up to a log n factor) bounds on the number of queries needed to cluster n elements into k unknown clusters, given a similarity matrix where elements in the same cluster have similarity values drawn from distribution f+ and elements in different clusters from f−. The authors demonstrate a significant reduction in query complexity—from Θ(nk) without side information to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)) with it. The paper is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution and provides both lower and upper bounds, along with algorithms achieving the upper bound.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper offers a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing both information-theoretic lower and upper bounds. The connection to the stochastic block model is well-articulated.
* **Significant Result:** The reduction in query complexity achieved by leveraging side information is substantial and practically relevant.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:**  Developing algorithms that don’t require knowledge of *k*, *f+*, or *f−* is a significant achievement and enhances practical applicability.
* **Clear Motivation and Connection to Applications:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem with the entity resolution example, and the discussion clearly explains the deviation between theoretical lower bounds and empirical performance of heuristics.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of related work, including query complexity, clustering algorithms, stochastic block models, and crowdsourcing.  The citations are current and relevant.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results, even if relatively brief, helps to support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the performance of the algorithms.
* **Well-Organized & Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, although some sections are dense with theoretical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Technical Details:**  The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof of the lower bound, is quite complex and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with information theory to fully grasp. While detailed proofs are in the appendix, a more intuitive high-level explanation of key ideas would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions about Distributions:** The assumption that *f+* and *f−* have a fixed cardinality of support, while reasonable, is a limitation. The paper acknowledges this, but discussing the implications of relaxing this assumption (even briefly) would be valuable.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Cora).  While the results are encouraging, experiments on diverse datasets with different characteristics would strengthen the conclusions. It is unclear how sensitive the performance is to the specific choice of similarity function.
* **Practical Considerations:** While the algorithms are parameter-free, the computational cost of evaluating the Hellinger divergence and related calculations might be significant for large datasets. A discussion of the practical feasibility of the algorithms for massive datasets would be helpful.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Heuristics:** The paper mentions several heuristics used in practice (e.g., [22, 40, 43]) but does not provide a direct empirical comparison to these methods.  Demonstrating that the proposed algorithms outperform existing heuristics would significantly strengthen the contribution.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a substantial theoretical advancement in understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information.
* The results are theoretically sound, with tight bounds and parameter-free algorithms.
* The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed solutions have the potential to impact applications like entity resolution and crowdsourcing.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the contributions and related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity of the analysis might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The lack of comparison to existing practical heuristics is a missed opportunity.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and should be accepted. The theoretical results are significant and the paper addresses an important problem. While the technical details are complex and the experimental evaluation is limited, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, specifically:

1. **Provide a more intuitive explanation of the lower bound proof.**  A high-level overview of the key ideas would be helpful for readers less familiar with information theory.
2. **Discuss the potential impact of relaxing the assumption of fixed cardinality for *f+* and *f−*.**
3. **Consider adding a brief comparison to existing heuristics on the Cora dataset or another relevant dataset.**  This would demonstrate the practical value of the proposed algorithms.
4. **Briefly address the computational cost of the proposed algorithms for large datasets.**
