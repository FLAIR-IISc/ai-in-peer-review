This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, demonstrating that side information drastically reduces the query complexity compared to clustering without it. The contribution is significant, building upon existing work in interactive clustering and relating it to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM).

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper tackles the problem of clustering *n* elements into *k* unknown clusters using pairwise queries to an oracle, with the goal of minimizing the number of queries. The key insight is the impact of a similarity matrix (side information) representing pairwise relationships between elements.  The authors establish that the query complexity reduces from Θ(*nk*) (without side information) to O(*k*<sup>2</sup> log *n* H(f+||f−)) with side information, where H(f+||f−) is the Hellinger divergence between distributions representing similar and dissimilar pairs. They develop algorithms achieving this complexity and demonstrate matching lower bounds. The work connects this problem to community detection, particularly the SBM, and highlights the potential for parameter-free algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges earlier work on interactive clustering (e.g., [7, 6, 42, 43]) and the lack of theoretical guarantees. It correctly identifies the O(*nk*) upper and lower bounds without side information ([13, 40]).  Crucially, it distinguishes its contribution from recent work focused on specific clustering objectives or computational lower bounds ([5]) and emphasizes the information-theoretic nature of its results. The connection to SBM ([28, 15, 14, 2, 1, 25, 24, 11, 36]) is well-explained and motivates the study.  The authors appropriately cite relevant works on query complexity and information theory ([16, 4, 8, 9, 31, 38]).

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing both upper and lower bounds on query complexity.  The use of Hellinger divergence as a key parameter is insightful.
*   **Novel Contribution:** The result demonstrating the significant reduction in query complexity with side information is novel and valuable.  Connecting this to the SBM framework adds further significance.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require knowledge of *k*, f+, or f− is a practical strength.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem formulation is well-defined and clearly motivated by real-world applications like entity resolution.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature and accurately position their work within the field.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results on the cora dataset adds credibility to the findings and demonstrates the practical performance of the algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, particularly the lower bound, appear quite intricate and might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with advanced information theory to fully grasp. More intuition behind the key steps would be helpful.
*   **Dataset Limitations:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (cora).  Results on a wider range of datasets with varying characteristics would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Practical Considerations:** While the algorithms are parameter-free, the practical runtime could be a concern for extremely large datasets, even though it's polynomial. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Notational Issues:** Some notation could be clarified (e.g., the consistent use of pmfs f+ and f−).




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Theoretical Advance:** The paper presents a fundamental contribution to the understanding of interactive clustering.
*   **Strong Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results have the potential to influence the design of more efficient interactive clustering algorithms.
*   **Novelty:** The work introduces a new perspective on the interplay between side information and query complexity.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Proof Complexity:** The detailed proofs may hinder accessibility for some researchers.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The lack of a more diverse experimental evaluation raises some questions about the generalizability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of interactive clustering. The analysis is rigorous, the results are impactful, and the connection to the SBM provides valuable context. While the proofs are complex and the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, these weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially expand the experimental evaluation. The authors should consider adding more intuitive explanations of the core ideas, especially in the proof of the lower bound. A brief discussion on the practical limitations and potential optimizations of the algorithms would also be valuable.
