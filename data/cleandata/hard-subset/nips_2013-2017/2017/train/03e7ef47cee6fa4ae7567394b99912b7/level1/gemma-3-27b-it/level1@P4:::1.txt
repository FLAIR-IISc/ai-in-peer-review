This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix. The core contribution lies in demonstrating how the availability of a similarity matrix drastically reduces the number of queries required to achieve optimal clustering, from Θ(nk) without side information to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)) with it, where H denotes the Hellinger divergence between distributions defining the similarity matrix.  The authors also provide information-theoretic lower bounds matching their upper bounds within a logarithmic factor. They connect their work to the stochastic block model (SBM) and develop parameter-free algorithms.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in clustering where similarity functions are imperfect and interactive labeling via oracles (e.g., human experts or crowdsourcing) is used to improve accuracy. It builds on a growing body of work using interactive clustering, citing relevant heuristics (e.g., [22, 40, 13, 42, 43, 18, 39, 12, 21, 29]) and places the current work in the context of existing theoretical bounds for query complexity ([16, 4, 8]). The connection to the SBM is highlighted, positioning the work within a broader theoretical framework.  The authors correctly identify the gap between theoretical bounds and the observed performance of heuristics.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing both upper and lower bounds on query complexity. The use of Hellinger divergence to quantify the information provided by the similarity matrix is a sound choice.
*   **Novelty:** The core result – the dramatic reduction in query complexity with side information – is novel and significant. The matching lower bound strengthens the contribution.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** Developing algorithms that do not require knowledge of `k`, `f+`, and `f−` is a substantial practical advantage.
*   **Connection to SBM:** The link to the well-studied SBM provides a broader context and potential for further research.
*   **Clear Presentation:** While dense, the paper is generally clearly written. The motivation is well-explained, and the contributions are clearly stated.
* **Experimental validation**: The inclusion of experimental results on a real-world dataset is a definite strength.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density and Technicality:** The paper is quite dense and technically challenging. A less mathematically inclined reader might struggle to fully grasp the details.  More intuitive explanations of the key concepts could be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of discrete distributions for f+ and f−, while stated, might limit the applicability of the results.  Discussing the implications of continuous distributions more thoroughly would be valuable.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset and a single similarity function.  Exploring other datasets and similarity measures would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Algorithm Details in Appendix:** Crucial details of the algorithm are relegated to the appendix, making it harder to quickly assess the practicality and computational cost.
*   **Some Notation is Dense**: While the overall presentation is clear, certain notations (e.g., in Lemma 1) could be simplified for better readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem with a significant practical impact.
*   The theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and well-supported.
*   The development of parameter-free algorithms is a valuable contribution.
*   The connection to the SBM provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The experiments support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is highly technical and may be inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   The experimental validation is limited in scope.
*   The reliance on the discrete distribution assumption.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of clustering and interactive learning.  The theoretical results are significant, and the parameter-free algorithms are a practical advantage. While the paper is dense and could benefit from more extensive experimental validation, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the points raised above. Specifically, a slightly more intuitive explanation of the key algorithms and a discussion of the implications of different distribution assumptions would be beneficial. A suggestion would also be to move some of the more crucial algorithm details from the appendix into the main body of the paper.
