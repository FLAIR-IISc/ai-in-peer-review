This paper investigates the query complexity of interactive clustering, where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether two elements belong to the same cluster. The authors situate their work by noting the significant gap between the established Θ(nk) query complexity lower bound and the much better performance of practical heuristics, which often leverage a similarity matrix. The paper’s core contribution is to formalize this similarity matrix as “side information” and rigorously analyze its impact. The authors model the similarity values as being drawn from one of two distinct distributions, f+ or f−, depending on whether a pair is in the same or different clusters, respectively. This model generalizes the well-studied Stochastic Block Model. The paper presents nearly-tight upper and lower bounds, showing that side information can drastically reduce the query complexity to be proportional to k²/H²(f+‖f−), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between the distributions.

The paper has numerous strengths.
**Quality:** The work is technically sound, presenting a complete theoretical analysis with strong information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient algorithmic upper bounds to match. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs, particularly the novel lower bound technique for the adaptive query setting.
**Originality:** The primary contribution—formally quantifying the benefit of side information in interactive clustering—is highly original. While related to active learning and community detection, the paper’s specific problem formulation and analysis provide a new and insightful perspective. The proposed iterative-update method for a parameter-free algorithm is also a creative approach.
**Significance:** The results are highly significant as they provide a theoretical foundation for the success of many real-world systems in entity resolution and crowdsourcing that implicitly use side information. The proposed algorithms are not only efficient but also parameter-free (independent of k, f+, and f−), making them directly applicable. This work successfully bridges an important gap between theory and practice and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively sets up the problem, and the main theorems are stated upfront, providing a clear roadmap for the reader.

A minor weakness is that the experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset. While the paper’s main contributions are theoretical, a more extensive empirical study would further strengthen its practical claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and nearly-tight theoretical characterization of a fundamental problem.
*   Bridges a significant and long-standing gap between theory and practice.
*   The proposed parameter-free algorithms have high practical value.
*   The lower-bound proof technique is a strong contribution of independent interest.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Experimental validation could be more comprehensive.

The paper's strengths far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a significant contribution to our understanding of interactive learning and clustering. I strongly recommend acceptance.
