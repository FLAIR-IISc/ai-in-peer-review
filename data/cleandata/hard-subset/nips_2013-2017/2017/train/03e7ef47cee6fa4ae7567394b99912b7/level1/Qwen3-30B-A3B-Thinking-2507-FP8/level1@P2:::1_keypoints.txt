- Provides the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information, giving both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on query complexity.  
- Shows side information can reduce queries from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f⁺‖f⁻)), where H² is squared Hellinger divergence between within- and across-cluster similarity distributions.  
- Generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻ and not requiring assumptions on cluster sizes or k.  
- Introduces a novel, technically strong lower-bound framework (Theorem 2) that handles adaptivity in interactive query settings.  
- Presents parameter-free upper-bound algorithms that do not require knowledge of k, f⁺, or f⁻.  
- Upper and lower bounds are nearly tight (up to a log n factor), demonstrating strong theoretical characterization of query complexity.  
- Empirical results on the Cora dataset show the greedy strategy performs close to best-case with only a small increase in queries.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior work (e.g., metric-based clustering and computational lower bounds) by providing information-theoretic bounds for arbitrary distributions.  
- Connects results to entity resolution and community detection, explaining why heuristics use far fewer queries in practice.  
- Minor weaknesses: lacks detailed comparison to related work on clustering with noisy queries and active community detection, and does not fully address practical scalability for very large n despite O(n²) time.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel theoretical framework, tight bounds, parameter-free practical algorithms, empirical validation, and significant relevance to theory and applications.  
- Rejection concerns: remaining log n gap, missing related-work comparisons, and limited discussion of scalability for extremely large datasets.
