

# Peer Review: "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of interactive clustering with side information, a problem motivated by practical applications like entity resolution where domain experts or crowdsourced workers are used to improve clustering accuracy. The authors establish the query complexity of clustering when given a similarity matrix (side information), showing a dramatic reduction from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k² log n / H²(f+‖f-)), where H is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity values for same-cluster (f+) and different-cluster (f-) pairs. The work builds on recent heuristic approaches [40, 43] but provides the first theoretical guarantees for arbitrary unknown distributions f+ and f-, while connecting to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM) as a special case. Previous work on clustering with oracle queries [7, 6, 5] focused on different query models or computational lower bounds, while this paper establishes information-theoretic bounds that hold even for randomized algorithms.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for both lower and upper bounds. The lower bound framework (Section 2) is particularly impressive as it overcomes the challenge of proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings where standard techniques like Fano's inequality don't apply. The upper bound algorithms (Section 3) are computationally efficient, parameter-free (work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-), and nearly match the lower bound. The experimental validation on the Cora dataset (Section 4) supports the theoretical claims.

**Originality:** This is the first rigorous theoretical study of query complexity in clustering with side information. The connection to SBM as a special case and the development of a general lower bound framework for interactive learning are novel contributions that significantly advance the field beyond previous heuristic approaches. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work [5, 18, 33] by providing provable guarantees for arbitrary distributions without requiring knowledge of parameters.

**Significance:** The results have substantial practical implications for applications like entity resolution where querying experts is expensive. The dramatic reduction in query complexity (from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+‖f-))) could significantly reduce costs in real-world applications. The parameter-free nature of the algorithms makes them immediately applicable in practice. The work opens up important new directions for research in both theoretical and practical clustering.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to results. The main technical contributions are well-explained, though the lower bound proof (Section 2) is quite technical and may require familiarity with information theory. The algorithm descriptions (Section 3) are precise and accompanied by pseudocode.

## Weaknesses

**Limited experimental validation:** The experimental results are limited to a single dataset (Cora) with a specific similarity function. More extensive experiments on diverse datasets would strengthen the practical relevance claims.

**No handling of noisy oracles:** The paper assumes the oracle always returns correct answers, but in practice, crowdsourced oracles often provide noisy responses. While the authors mention this briefly and note it's addressed in companion work [34], the current paper would benefit from at least a brief discussion of how the results might extend to noisy settings.

**Assumption of similarity matrix model:** The generative model for the similarity matrix assumes f+ for same-cluster and f- for different-cluster pairs. In practice, similarity matrices might not perfectly follow this model, and it would be valuable to understand how robust the results are to model misspecification.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For acceptance:**
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity in clustering with side information
- Significant reduction in query complexity with strong theoretical guarantees
- Novel lower bound framework applicable to interactive learning problems
- Parameter-free algorithms with practical relevance
- Clear connection to stochastic block models and community detection

**Against acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation on a single dataset
- No consideration of noisy oracle answers (though noted in companion work)
- Assumption of specific similarity matrix model might be restrictive
- Technical complexity of lower bound proof may limit accessibility

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The paper's main contribution is establishing the fundamental query complexity of this problem, which has been lacking in the literature despite the proliferation of heuristic algorithms. The results are both theoretically interesting and practically relevant, with clear implications for applications like entity resolution.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents a novel theoretical framework (Originality), and has significant implications for both theory and practice (Significance). The main weaknesses are limited experimental validation and the assumption of noiseless oracles, but these do not undermine the core theoretical contributions.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited experimental validation (perhaps by adding a brief discussion of how the results might generalize to other datasets) and to more explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the noiseless oracle assumption.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS 2017 proceedings.
