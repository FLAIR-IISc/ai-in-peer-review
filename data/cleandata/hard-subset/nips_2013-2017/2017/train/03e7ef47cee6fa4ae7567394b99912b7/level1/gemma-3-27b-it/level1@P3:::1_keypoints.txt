- Addresses query complexity of clustering with similarity-matrix side information and gives tight (up to log n) bounds on queries needed to cluster n items into k unknown clusters.  
- Main theoretical result: query complexity reduces from Θ(nk) without side information to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)) with side information.  
- Provides both information-theoretic lower and upper bounds and algorithms that achieve the upper bound.  
- Connects problem formulation to the stochastic block model and articulates that connection clearly.  
- Develops parameter-free algorithms that do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f−.  
- Strong motivation and clear application framing (e.g., entity resolution).  
- Comprehensive and up-to-date related work survey across query complexity, clustering, SBM, and crowdsourcing.  
- Includes experimental validation (on Cora) that supports theoretical claims, though brief.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, despite dense theoretical sections.  
- Lower-bound proofs and technical analysis are complex and may be hard to follow for non-experts in information theory.  
- Assumes f+ and f− have fixed support cardinality; relaxing this assumption is not fully explored.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to a single dataset (Cora), leaving sensitivity to similarity choice unclear.  
- Practical concerns: computational cost of computing Hellinger divergence and related quantities may be high for very large datasets.  
- Lacks empirical comparison to existing practical heuristics cited in the paper.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, tight bounds, parameter-free algorithms, and good motivation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility due to technical complexity, limited experiments, and missing heuristic comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: add an intuitive high-level explanation of the lower bound proof.  
- Requested revisions: discuss implications of relaxing the fixed-support assumption for f+ and f−.  
- Requested revisions: include a brief empirical comparison to existing heuristics/datasets (e.g., Cora) and address computational feasibility for large-scale data.
