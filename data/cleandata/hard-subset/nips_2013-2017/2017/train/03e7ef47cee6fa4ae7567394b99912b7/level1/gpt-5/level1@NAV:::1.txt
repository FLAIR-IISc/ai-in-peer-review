This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner can ask same-cluster queries and also has access to side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix. The main contributions are twofold: (1) an information-theoretic lower bound showing that side information can reduce the number of adaptive pairwise queries from Θ(nk) (without side information) to roughly Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) up to constants, where H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence between the within- and across-cluster similarity distributions; and (2) efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that achieve Õ(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) or Õ(n + k^2/H^2) queries, nearly matching the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors. The work connects to and generalizes stochastic block models (SBMs), relating recovery thresholds with and without queries; it also ties to prior NIPS work on clustering with queries (Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16), community detection (Abbe & Sandon, NIPS’15), and crowdsourcing/active learning for entity resolution.

Quality: The lower bound technique—casting adaptive assignment as a multi-hypothesis test and controlling total variation via Hellinger divergence—appears sound and novel for interactive settings where standard Fano tools falter. The upper bound uses an iterative, parameter-free estimator of H^2 via empirical inter/intra distributions with Sanov-type control and a membership test. Some technical assumptions (independence of W entries; discrete support with fixed cardinality) are clearly stated for algorithms, while lower bounds handle continuous distributions. There are minor inconsistencies in the dependence on k (k vs k^2) across abstract/theorems that should be reconciled. Experiments, while suggestive, are limited.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized but dense; several typos and equation formatting issues (e.g., k vs k^2, log factors) hinder readability. The parameter-free protocol could be summarized more concisely, and assumptions (discrete vs continuous W, support size) should be centralized.

Originality: High. The formulation of query complexity with side information, the lower-bound framework for adaptive queries using Hellinger divergence, and parameter-free algorithms that work without k, f+, f− are novel and go beyond SBM-specific analyses.

Significance: High. The results formalize why practical systems with similarity matrices need far fewer queries and provide near-optimal guarantees. The framework is likely to influence active clustering, ER, and interactive learning lower bounds.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, nearly tight lower/upper bounds; parameter-free efficient algorithms
- Clear generalization and connection to SBM with insights on thresholds
- Conceptually novel lower-bound technique for adaptive interactive settings
- Practical relevance to crowdsourced entity resolution

Arguments against acceptance:
- Notational/statement inconsistencies on k and log factors; clarity issues
- Algorithmic guarantees rely on discrete fixed-support W; empirical scope limited
- Assumes perfect oracle; robustness deferred to companion work
- Experimental evaluation lacks broader synthetic/SBM comparisons and baselines
