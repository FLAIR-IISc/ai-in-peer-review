

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of interactive clustering with side information. The authors study the query complexity of clustering when an oracle answers pairwise same-cluster queries, and the algorithm has access to a similarity matrix (side information) generated from an underlying distribution f⁺ for same-cluster pairs and f⁻ for different-cluster pairs. They establish a tight information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(n + min{nk, k²/H²(f⁺‖f⁻)}) and provide nearly matching upper bounds of O(k² log n / H²(f⁺‖f⁻)) for parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f⁺, or f⁻. This represents a dramatic reduction from the Θ(nk) query complexity without side information.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical results are technically sound with rigorous proofs, and the connection to stochastic block models (SBM) provides valuable context for the problem. The parameter-free algorithms are particularly significant for practical applications, as they eliminate the need for tuning parameters that are often unknown in real-world scenarios. The paper builds appropriately on prior work on query complexity (e.g., [13, 40]) and clustering with side information (e.g., [18, 33]), but makes a clear departure by providing the first information-theoretic analysis of how side information affects query complexity.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though the lower bound proof is highly technical. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate practical relevance, though more extensive validation would strengthen the empirical claims. The limitation of not addressing noisy oracle responses (noted by the authors as future work) is a minor weakness but doesn't undermine the core theoretical contribution.

The work scores highly on all four criteria: Quality (rigorous proofs), Clarity (well-organized with clear contributions), Originality (novel bounds and parameter-free approach), and Significance (provides theoretical foundation for practical interactive clustering applications). The paper represents a substantial advance over previous heuristic approaches and fills a critical theoretical gap in the literature.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution with tight bounds, novel connection to SBM, practical parameter-free algorithms, and significant implications for interactive learning applications.  
Arguments against acceptance: Limited experimental validation and omission of noisy oracle handling, though these are secondary to the main theoretical contribution.  

This work is an excellent fit for NIPS and represents a meaningful contribution to the field of interactive learning.
