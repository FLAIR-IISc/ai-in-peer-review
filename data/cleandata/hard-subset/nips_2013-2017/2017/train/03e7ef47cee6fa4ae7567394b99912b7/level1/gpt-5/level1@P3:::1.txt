Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the algorithm can adaptively ask same-cluster queries to an oracle and has access to a noisy similarity matrix (side information). Side information is modeled by independent pairwise similarities drawn from f+ for same-cluster pairs and from f− for different-cluster pairs, with f+, f− unknown to the algorithm. The main claims are (i) information-theoretic lower bounds showing that side information can dramatically reduce the number of queries from Θ(nk) (no side info) to Θ(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}), up to constants, and (ii) nearly matching upper bounds via efficient parameter-free algorithms that do not require knowledge of k or the distributions. A Monte Carlo algorithm achieves query complexity roughly O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) with success probability 1 − o(1/n), and a Las Vegas variant achieves O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}). The analysis uses Hellinger divergence for both lower and upper bounds and leverages a Sanov-type large deviations argument in a parameter-free iterative procedure. The lower bound introduces a technique for adaptively queried settings where classical Fano-type arguments do not directly apply.

This fills a gap between practice in interactive clustering/entity resolution (ER)—where heuristics guided by side information often use far fewer than nk queries—and theory, which previously gave Ω(nk) lower bounds without side information. It generalizes stochastic block models (SBM) by allowing arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− and arbitrary, potentially adversarial partitions, unlike many SBM results that assume known parameters and balanced clusters. Related prior work includes: same-cluster queries for k-means under metric separation (Ashtiani et al., NIPS’16) with computational lower bounds and parameter-dependent algorithms; active learning on SBM (Gadde et al.); and numerous ER heuristics and crowdsourcing systems that exploit similarity matrices. Parameter-free SBM recovery for a constant number of linear-sized clusters has been studied (e.g., Abbe & Sandon), but without query-adaptive analysis. The paper is well positioned within NIPS’s interest in active learning, clustering theory, and information-theoretic limits.

Strengths
- Clear and important conceptual message: side information can provably reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) to near Θ(k^2/H^2), with tightness up to log factors.
- Novel lower-bound technique for adaptive interactive settings that circumvents direct use of Fano’s inequality; the argument via “weak” clusters and Hellinger-TV control is elegant and may be reusable.
- Parameter-free, computationally efficient algorithms with high-probability correctness; the iterative-update procedure that estimates f+, f− on the fly is practically appealing.
- Unifies upper/lower bounds through the same divergence measure (Hellinger), with a transparent thresholding condition scaling as log n/H^2.
- Interesting connection to SBM and a conjecture on how queries shift the sparse-regime threshold.

Weaknesses and questions
- Inconsistencies in stated bounds: different parts claim O(k log n/H^2) vs O(k^2 log n/H^2) for the Monte Carlo algorithm; Theorem 1 states O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}), while later text/abstract also mention k^2. This must be clarified precisely for both algorithms.
- Assumptions on f+, f−: proofs for the algorithm assume discrete distributions with fixed support; the lower bound claims to hold also for continuous distributions. The extension of the algorithmic guarantees to continuous or large-support cases is deferred to a brief remark; more detail on how sample complexity scales with support size would help.
- Lower bound regime requires H^2(f+||f−) ≤ 1/18 and uses equal cluster sizes (then generalized). While the small-H regime is the most relevant, the constants/conditions are somewhat ad hoc; a discussion of behavior for larger H and for highly unbalanced partitions would strengthen the message.
- Adaptivity in the lower bound: the argument singles out elements with few queries and converts assignment into a k-ary hypothesis test. While persuasive, a more explicit account of the coupling between global error probability and local misassignment (e.g., union bounds or a sharp reduction showing a single error implies global failure) would increase rigor.
- Algorithmic analysis: the membership test is shown correct outside a “gray zone” whose size is argued to be constant (anti-concentration). This is central to achieving the stated query bounds. Making the size and its dependence on H explicit, and clarifying how contamination is avoided when estimating p+, p− from partially grown clusters, would improve readability.
- Experiments are limited (single dataset, no comparison to prior interactive/ER baselines, no ablations on k, H, or support size). The “best/worst/greedy” strategies are informative but idealized; empirical comparisons to strong heuristics and sensitivity to H would bolster significance.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Theoretical development appears technically sound overall, with meaningful new lower-bound techniques and a careful algorithmic analysis using Sanov-type tools. However, clarity issues (k vs k^2 factors) and some proof steps that are only sketched (e.g., convergence/contamination handling) should be tightened.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with a clear storyline, but the aforementioned inconsistencies, some notation typos, and occasional mixing of H and H^2 hinder readability. The paper would benefit from a consolidated theorem summarizing all regimes and dependencies with consistent notation.
- Originality: High. The adaptive lower bound framework and the parameter-free clustering with side information under unknown f+, f− seem new. The use of Hellinger divergence throughout is clean and nonstandard compared to KL-centric analyses.
- Significance: High. The work explains a practical phenomenon (side information slashes query needs), provides near-optimal bounds, and may influence future interactive learning theory. Practical impact is promising given the parameter-free design, though stronger empirical validation would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify and correct the query-complexity statements for both algorithms; specify precisely when k vs k^2 appears and why (e.g., due to the gray-zone queries).
- Explicitly state assumptions on distribution support size and provide a short lemma on how guarantees scale with support size or extend to continuous f+, f−.
- Expand the discussion on highly unbalanced clusters and whether the thresholds and guarantees depend on min-cluster size.
- Provide a more detailed outline (or appendix pointer) on contamination control when estimating p+, p− from grown clusters.
- Strengthen experiments: add comparisons to existing interactive/ER methods; vary H artificially; report runtime and robustness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contributions with nearly matching lower/upper bounds in a timely and practically relevant setting.
- Novel adaptive lower-bound methodology potentially useful beyond clustering.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms and clean reliance on Hellinger divergence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation issues and inconsistencies in main bounds could confuse readers.
- Experimental section is thin and does not fully demonstrate practical superiority against baselines.
- Some technical conditions and constants are specialized and could be better justified.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The core theoretical advances are significant and relevant to the NIPS community, and the paper would make a valuable contribution after addressing clarity issues and better substantiating empirical claims.
