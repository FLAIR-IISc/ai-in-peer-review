This paper tackles the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix, in an interactive setting with an oracle. It establishes information-theoretic lower and upper bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available compared to the case without it. The work is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data via human experts is crucial.

**Summary of Related Work:** The paper places its contribution within the context of interactive clustering, contrasting it with earlier work focusing on purely similarity-based methods and heuristics. It effectively connects the problem to the stochastic block model (SBM) and discusses related work on active learning, crowdsourcing, and community detection. The authors correctly identify the gap in theoretical understanding of the interplay between similarity matrices and query complexity. They position their work as filling this gap and establishing a more rigorous foundation for the heuristics commonly used in practice.  They also acknowledge existing work on clustering with noisy queries (referencing their companion work) and highlight the differences.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the problem, establishing both lower and upper bounds on query complexity. The nearly matching bounds are a significant achievement.
* **Novelty:** The investigation of the impact of side information (similarity matrix) on interactive clustering query complexity is novel and represents a significant contribution. The connection to the SBM is insightful.
* **Generality:** The model is quite general, allowing for arbitrary similarity distributions (f+ and f-) and an unknown number of clusters (k). The parameter-free algorithms are particularly appealing.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivation from entity resolution and the potential impact on improving clustering accuracy with limited labeled data are significant. The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide empirical support.
* **Clear Presentation of Contributions:** The paper clearly articulates its contributions and lays out a roadmap for the technical development.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's performance on a single dataset (Cora), the evaluation is somewhat limited. Comparing the algorithm against existing heuristics on more diverse datasets would strengthen the empirical validation. The experiments primarily focus on validating the effectiveness of the approach rather than a detailed analysis of the runtime or scaling behavior.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs are complex and largely relegated to the appendices. While understandable for a theoretical paper, this can hinder accessibility for readers less familiar with the technical details.  Providing more intuitive explanations of key proof steps would be helpful.
* **Constant Factors:** The upper bounds have a logarithmic factor. While this is often acceptable, a closer examination of the constant factors and potential for optimization could be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not explicitly address practical challenges such as the computational cost of estimating the distributions f+ and f- from the similarity matrix, especially for large datasets.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the authors mention potential future directions, a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the model (e.g., assumptions about the oracle's accuracy) would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of interactive clustering.
* The established bounds are nearly matching and demonstrate the power of side information.
* The parameter-free algorithms are a valuable contribution.
* The work is well-motivated and has clear connections to practical applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited.
* The complexity of the proofs may make the paper inaccessible to some readers.
* The practical implications and potential challenges of applying the algorithms to large datasets are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The novelty of the problem investigated, the rigor of the analysis, and the generality of the results make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical findings are compelling. I believe the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest expanding the experimental evaluation with additional datasets and a more detailed discussion of runtime performance and scaling.  Consider adding a section dedicated to discussing the practical challenges and limitations of the approach.
