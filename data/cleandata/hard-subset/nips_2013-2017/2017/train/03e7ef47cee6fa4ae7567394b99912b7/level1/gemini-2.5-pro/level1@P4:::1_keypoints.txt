- Studies interactive clustering with pairwise oracle queries plus noisy side information (similarity matrix from distributions f+ and f-).  
- Shows without side info query complexity is Θ(nk), and formalizes how side information reduces this cost.  
- Proves an information-theoretic lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2(f+||f-)}) for expected queries, even for randomized algorithms with full parameter knowledge.  
- Presents two efficient, nearly-matching algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) with query complexities O(k^2 log n / H^2(f+||f-)) and O(n log n + k^2 log n / H^2(f+||f-)).  
- Algorithms are parameter-free, operating without knowing k, f+, or f-.  
- Connects the model to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) as a special case, explaining practical clustering heuristics.  
- Technical novelty: adapts new hypothesis-testing arguments to derive lower bounds in an adaptive query setting where Fano’s inequality doesn't apply.  
- Algorithmic novelty: iterative on-the-fly estimation of f+ and f- to decide when to query versus use side information.  
- Analysis relies on Sanov’s theorem and concentration inequalities and appears rigorous.  
- Minor gap: a log n factor separates upper and lower bounds; unclear if fundamental or an artifact of analysis.  
- Paper is well-written, with a clear problem statement and precise theorems.  
- Main algorithm description is dense; some threshold-intuition and convergence details are deferred to the appendix.  
- Originality: first rigorous information-theoretic characterization of interactive clustering with arbitrary unknown f+, f-.  
- Significance: provides theoretical foundation explaining why similarity-based heuristics reduce human labeling and offers potentially adaptable lower-bounding techniques for other interactive problems.  
- Practical relevance: parameter-free algorithms are appealing, but experiments are limited and lack comparisons to established heuristics.  
- Acceptance arguments: fundamental contribution, strong technical depth, high significance, and clear motivation; rejection concerns: limited experiments and the remaining log n gap.
