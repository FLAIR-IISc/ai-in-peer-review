## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when provided with side information in the form of a similarity matrix, modeling the similarity between pairs of elements as drawn from distributions *f+* if they belong to the same cluster and *f-* otherwise. The authors establish a significant result: incorporating this side information can dramatically reduce the query complexity from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k log n / H²(f+ || f-)), where H denotes the Hellinger distance. This upper bound is shown to be nearly tight, matching a corresponding lower bound up to a log n factor.

The work is well-motivated by the practical problem of entity resolution and interactive clustering, where obtaining labeled data is costly. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is clearly articulated, positioning the work within a well-established theoretical framework while addressing a novel question – how do queries impact recovery thresholds? The authors highlight the importance of the Hellinger distance as a principled way to assess the quality of the similarity matrix, providing a valuable insight. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Result:** The core result demonstrating the reduction in query complexity with side information is significant and rigorously proven. The near-tightness of the bound further strengthens the contribution.
* **Novel Perspective:** The paper tackles a crucial question – how does side information affect the *query complexity* of clustering, rather than just the clustering accuracy. This is a valuable and often overlooked aspect.
* **Clear Connection to SBM:** The relationship to the SBM literature provides context and allows for comparison with existing results.
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivation from entity resolution and interactive clustering makes the work highly relevant to real-world applications.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require knowledge of *k*, *f+*, or *f-* is a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** While the authors present results on the Cora dataset, the experimental section feels somewhat limited. A more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with varying levels of noise in the similarity matrix would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, while rigorous, are dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the technical details. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could improve accessibility.
* **Assumptions on Distributions:** The assumption of fixed cardinality of support for *f+* and *f-* might be restrictive. Exploring the implications of relaxing this assumption could be a valuable direction for future work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of clustering.
* The results are rigorously proven and have strong practical implications.
* The connection to the SBM literature provides valuable context and establishes the work's importance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited.
* The technical details and proofs may be challenging for some readers.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the suggestions for improvement, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation, would further enhance the paper's impact.
