This paper tackles the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix representing pairwise relationships. The authors demonstrate a significant reduction in query complexity – from Θ(nk) without side information to O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f-)) with it, where H is the Hellinger distance between the distributions governing similarity within and between clusters. This result is nearly tight, matching a lower bound up to a log n factor, and provides a principled way to assess similarity matrix quality via the Hellinger distance. The work is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution and connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) literature, offering insights into how queries alter recovery thresholds.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, presenting both upper and lower bounds on query complexity. The proofs, while potentially dense, seem logically structured (with detailed proofs relegated to the supplementary material). The authors acknowledge the limitations of their analysis and suggest directions for future work, demonstrating intellectual honesty. The algorithms proposed are efficient, and the parameter-free nature is a significant strength. The connection to SBM is thoughtfully explored, and the paper provides a valuable contribution to understanding the interplay between side information and query complexity.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, though dense in places, particularly in the theoretical sections. The motivation is clear, and the problem is well-defined. The notation is consistent, but some familiarity with information theory concepts (Hellinger distance, Fano’s inequality) is assumed. The experimental section is concise but provides some validation of the theoretical results. More detailed explanations of the algorithms and experimental setup would enhance clarity. The organization is logical, with a clear progression from problem statement to contributions and related work.

**Originality:** The problem of clustering with side information is not entirely novel, as evidenced by the related work section. However, the *theoretical* treatment of query complexity in this setting is a significant contribution. Existing work focuses primarily on heuristics, lacking rigorous guarantees. This paper fills that gap by providing information-theoretic bounds and demonstrating the dramatic impact of side information. The connection to SBM, framed in terms of query complexity, is also a novel perspective. The development of parameter-free algorithms is also a noteworthy achievement.

**Significance:** This work is highly significant. The result of drastically reduced query complexity with good side information has practical implications for numerous applications, including entity resolution, active learning, and crowdsourcing. The Hellinger distance provides a quantifiable metric for assessing the quality of similarity matrices, which is valuable in real-world scenarios. The theoretical framework developed can potentially be extended to other interactive learning problems. The lower bound is a valuable contribution in itself, providing a fundamental limit on the achievable query complexity.



**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** Provides rigorous upper and lower bounds on query complexity.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:**  The algorithms do not require knowledge of underlying distributions or the number of clusters.
*   **Principled Similarity Matrix Evaluation:** Offers a method (Hellinger distance) to assess the quality of side information.
*   **Connection to SBM:** Provides a novel perspective on the relationship between SBM recovery and query complexity.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  Applicable to real-world problems like entity resolution and active learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density of Theoretical Sections:**  The mathematical arguments are complex and may be difficult for readers without a strong background in information theory to follow.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are relatively limited and could be expanded to demonstrate the algorithm's performance on a wider range of datasets and scenarios.
*   **Constant Factors:** While asymptotically tight, the logarithmic factor in the upper bound could be significant in practice. Exploring techniques to reduce this factor is a potential direction for future work.
*   **Assumptions:** While the generality of f+ and f- is a strength, the assumption of fixed cardinality of support might be restrictive in some applications.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the understanding of clustering with side information.
*   The results are theoretically sound and have practical implications.
*   The work is well-motivated and clearly written (despite some density).
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical complexity may limit accessibility for some readers.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited.
*   The logarithmic factor in the upper bound, while acceptable, might warrant further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. Despite minor weaknesses, the theoretical contributions are significant, the work is well-motivated, and the results have practical relevance. The paper pushes the boundaries of our understanding of interactive clustering and provides a valuable framework for future research. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, making this a strong candidate for publication at NIPS.
