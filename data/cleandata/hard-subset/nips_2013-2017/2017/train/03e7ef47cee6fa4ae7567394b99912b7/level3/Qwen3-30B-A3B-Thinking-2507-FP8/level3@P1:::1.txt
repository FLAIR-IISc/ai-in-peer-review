

This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information, addressing a fundamental gap between theoretical bounds and practical heuristics in the field. The authors consider a setting where a clustering algorithm can query an oracle to determine if two elements belong to the same cluster, and additionally has access to a similarity matrix W where W_ij ~ f+ if i,j are in the same cluster, and W_ij ~ f- otherwise. The key theoretical contribution is establishing that with side information, the query complexity drops from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H is the Hellinger divergence. The authors prove this bound is tight within an O(log n) factor via information-theoretic lower bounds, and develop parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field by providing the first rigorous theoretical analysis of how side information affects query complexity. Previous work on interactive clustering (e.g., [13, 40]) established Θ(nk) query complexity without side information, but the authors correctly identify that practical heuristics [42, 43, 40] consistently use far fewer queries, and attribute this to the use of similarity matrices as side information. The paper's connection to stochastic block models (SBM) is particularly insightful: while SBM typically assumes known distributions and cluster sizes, the authors generalize this to arbitrary unknown distributions f+ and f- without cluster size assumptions. This generalization is significant because it reveals how query complexity affects the recovery thresholds in SBM, a key question in community detection.

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical rigor. The authors provide tight information-theoretic bounds that hold even for randomized algorithms, which is non-trivial given the interactive nature of the problem. The lower bound technique, which generalizes Fano's inequality to the interactive setting, is novel and could be of independent interest for other classification problems. The upper bounds are also impressive: the algorithms are computationally efficient (O(n²) time), parameter-free (work without knowing k, f+, or f-), and achieve nearly optimal query complexity. The Monte Carlo algorithm achieves O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)) queries with high probability, while the Las Vegas variant guarantees exact recovery with probability 1.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough comparison to related work. The connection to practical applications like entity resolution (a motivating example from the paper) is compelling and demonstrates the relevance of the theoretical results. The experimental section on the Cora dataset provides evidence that the algorithm works well in practice, with the greedy strategy achieving near-optimal performance using just 1136 queries to reach 80% precision and 90% recall.

However, there are some limitations. The paper does not address the case where the oracle provides noisy answers, though the authors note this is covered in a companion work. While the theoretical results are strong, the experimental validation is limited to a single dataset, which could be strengthened with more comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and similarity measures. The log n factor in the query complexity bound might be improvable in specific cases, though the authors correctly note this is the best possible for their general framework.

The paper excels in originality, significance, and quality. It is the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information, and it establishes a principled way to assess similarity matrix quality via Hellinger divergence. This work has clear implications for practical applications like entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering, where reducing the number of queries is crucial for cost efficiency. The connection to SBM opens up many promising research directions for future work.

Arguments for acceptance: 
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of interactive clustering with side information
- Tight bounds matching within log n factor
- Parameter-free algorithms with practical relevance
- Strong connection to stochastic block models with new insights
- Well-written with clear technical contributions

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental validation (only one dataset)
- Does not address noisy oracle responses (though noted in companion work)
- Log n factor in bound might be improvable

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering and provides a principled framework for leveraging side information. The results are theoretically sound, the writing is clear, and the significance is well-justified. The minor limitations do not detract from the paper's strong theoretical contributions, which are likely to be highly influential in both theoretical and applied machine learning communities. I recommend acceptance.
