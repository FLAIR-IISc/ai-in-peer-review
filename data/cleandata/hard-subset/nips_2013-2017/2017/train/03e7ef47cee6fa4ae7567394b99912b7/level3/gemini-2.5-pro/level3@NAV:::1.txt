This paper addresses the query complexity of interactive clustering when provided with side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix. The authors model this side information by assuming similarity values for same-cluster pairs are drawn from a distribution f+, and different-cluster pairs from f-. This work provides a formal theoretical explanation for why many practical clustering heuristics, which use such side information, significantly outperform the standard Θ(nk) query complexity lower bound. The paper connects this problem to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM), but poses a novel question: how do active queries affect the information-theoretic recovery thresholds?

The primary strength of this work lies in its significant and nearly-tight theoretical results. The authors make a crucial contribution by identifying the squared Hellinger distance, H²(f+ || f-), as the correct measure for the quality of the side information. They prove that with such information, the query complexity can be drastically reduced to O(k log n / H²(f+ || f-)), and provide a matching information-theoretic lower bound up to a log n factor. This is a fundamentally important result that elegantly quantifies the trade-off between the quality of prior information and the cost of active labeling. Furthermore, the proposed Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms are computationally efficient and, critically, parameter-free—they do not require prior knowledge of the number of clusters (k) or the distributions f+ and f-, which is a major advantage for practical applications.

The paper is technically sound and clearly written. One minor point for improvement would be to broaden the experimental evaluation. While the results on the Cora dataset are illustrative, experiments on synthetic data where H²(f+ || f-) can be precisely controlled would more directly validate the theoretical scaling laws derived in the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Significance:** The paper solves an important open problem, formally bridging the gap between the theory and practice of interactive clustering. The results are likely to influence future work in active learning and clustering.
*   **Originality:** The problem formulation is novel, and the use of Hellinger distance to characterize the quality of side information is a key insight.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing nearly tight upper and lower bounds. The parameter-free nature of the proposed algorithms adds significant practical value.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental section, while positive, is limited to a single real-world dataset.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a complete and impactful piece of research. The strengths far outweigh the minor limitations, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
