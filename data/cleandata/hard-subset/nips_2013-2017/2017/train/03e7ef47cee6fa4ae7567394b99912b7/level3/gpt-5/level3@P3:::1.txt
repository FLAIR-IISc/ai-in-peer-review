Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when, in addition to access to a noiseless same-cluster oracle, we are given a noisy similarity matrix W. The model assumes Wij is drawn i.i.d. from f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise, with f+, f− unknown to the algorithm. Without side information, the classic Θ(nk) query bound is both necessary and sufficient. The main positive result shows that side information can reduce the number of oracle queries to O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f−)), where H^2 is the squared Hellinger distance. The algorithm is efficient and parameter-free (no knowledge of k, f+, f−), and a Las Vegas variant achieves correctness with probability 1 with O(n log n + k log n / H^2) queries. On the lower-bound side, the paper develops an information-theoretic minimax framework for interactive algorithms and proves a nearly matching Ω(min{nk, k / H^2(f+ || f−)}) lower bound (up to a log n factor), even granting the algorithm knowledge of k, f+, f−.

The model strictly generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM): SBM corresponds to Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) choices for f+, f−, but here f+, f− are arbitrary (unknown) distributions; cluster sizes and k can be adversarial. The paper thus complements the rich SBM literature on recovery thresholds by asking how adaptive pairwise queries shift those thresholds. The results cleanly expose that the “quality” of side information is captured by H^2(f+, f−): as H^2 grows, fewer queries are needed; in the extreme of perfect side information (H^2 ≈ 1), the clustering may be fully encoded and need no queries.

Technical contributions
- Upper bounds: A parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm that iteratively learns empirical “intra” and “inter” distributions from small seed clusters, estimates H(p+ || p−), and uses this to decide when side information is reliable enough to assign new points without querying. It uses an adaptive size threshold of O(log n / H^2) for a “grown” cluster and then assigns most remaining points via a membership score based on Hellinger distance; only a small gray zone requires queries. A Las Vegas variant adds O(n log n) overhead and ensures correctness w.p. 1.
- Lower bounds: A general interactive lower-bound method that reduces assignment of a low-queried element to a k-hypothesis test, controls total variation via Hellinger on product distributions, and shows that unless the algorithm invests Ω(k / H^2) queries (or Ω(nk) in the no-side-information regime), it cannot achieve low error.
- Conceptual: Positions Hellinger distance as the right measure for side-information quality. In the Bernoulli SBM, this translates to scaling like 1/(√p − √q)^2, aligning with known thresholds.
- Practical relevance: Motivated by entity resolution; small experiments on CORA illustrate that a greedy strategy guided by the learned Hellinger gap attains good F1 with far fewer queries than Θ(nk).

Quality
The theory is solid and the techniques are well-chosen. The use of Hellinger (a metric) is key for both concentration and product-distribution comparisons, and the lower-bound framework carefully handles adaptivity, which defeats standard Fano-style arguments. The algorithms are clear in high level and plausibly efficient (O(n^2) time to process W). Some constants/log factors likely are not optimized. The empirical section is small but consistent with the theory.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, with a clear problem statement and a helpful discussion of connections to SBM. The iterative-update algorithm is intuitively motivated. However:
- There are occasional notational/typographical inconsistencies (e.g., k vs k^2 in some bounds; division signs around H^2; H vs H^2). Please unify the notation for Hellinger throughout and clearly separate Monte Carlo vs Las Vegas complexities.
- Present the algorithm in concise pseudo-code. Spell out assumptions on the support size of f+, f− for the upper bounds and how extensions to larger finite alphabets are handled.
- Briefly summarize Sanov’s-style derivations in the main text to improve readability.

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first principled analysis of query complexity for clustering with a generic similarity matrix under unknown f+, f−, providing nearly tight bounds and parameter-free algorithms. The lower-bound technique for interactive settings is novel and potentially reusable. The SBM connection, and the explicit quantification of the impact of side information via Hellinger, offer fresh insights beyond existing interactive clustering/crowdsourcing heuristics and beyond standard SBM thresholds.

Significance
High. The work addresses a central practical question—how much can noisy side information reduce querying?—and answers it with a clean quantity (H^2). It generalizes beyond SBM and could influence both theory (interactive lower bounds, active querying in networks) and practice (assessing similarity-matrix quality; designing query policies that adapt to estimated H^2). Even the message that side information can collapse query needs from Θ(nk) to O(k log n / H^2) is impactful.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Oracle assumed noiseless; although a companion paper handles noisy queries, a brief discussion of robustness here would help.
- Upper bounds assume discrete f+, f− with fixed support; state precisely how results extend to general finite alphabets or continuous distributions (the lower bound already does).
- The independence model for W may be violated in real similarity matrices derived from shared features; some robustness analysis would strengthen the practical message.
- Experiments are limited (single dataset, small ablation). Synthetic studies varying H^2 and k, and comparisons to pure-query and pure-similarity baselines, would better validate the predicted scaling.
- Runtime is at least O(n^2) to process W; note memory/streaming considerations for large n.
- There remains a log n gap in the main bound; discuss whether it is fundamental.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the k vs k^2 discrepancy in the lower bounds and the exact scaling of the Las Vegas algorithm; ensure consistent statements across abstract, theorems, and discussion.
- Provide pseudo-code and a brief complexity analysis in the main text; move some technical lemmas from the appendix if space permits.
- Add synthetic experiments sweeping H^2(f+, f−), k, and cluster-size imbalance; report query counts vs. accuracy.
- Discuss robustness to mild model misspecification (e.g., dependent edges, continuous similarities) and practical preprocessing for W.

Pros
- Clear, important question; crisp answer with interpretable dependence on H^2.
- Nearly tight lower/upper bounds; new interactive lower-bound technique.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms; elegant use of Hellinger and Sanov.
- Strong connection to and generalization of SBM; actionable insight for practitioners.

Cons
- Some presentation/notation issues; need to reconcile bound statements.
- Empirical validation is limited.
- Assumptions (noiseless oracle, i.i.d. W) may be strong for some applications.
- Logarithmic gap remains; constants likely loose.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a substantial, well-founded theoretical contribution with practical implications, and it opens a promising line on how queries shift recovery thresholds in block models and beyond. Strengthening clarity and expanding experiments would further increase its impact.
