This paper presents an investigation into the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix representing noisy pairwise relationships. The authors establish upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to recover the optimal clustering, demonstrating a significant reduction in complexity when side information is available. The core contribution lies in characterizing this reduction and proposing algorithms that nearly achieve the theoretical limits, even without knowledge of the underlying parameters.

The work builds upon the classical interactive clustering problem, addressing a critical gap in theoretical understanding highlighted by the discrepancy between theoretical bounds (Ω(nk)) and the performance of practical heuristics that leverage similarity matrices. This is particularly relevant to applications like entity resolution, where obtaining labeled data is costly and side information is readily available. The connection drawn to stochastic block models (SBM) provides a valuable theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The problem of query complexity for clustering with side information is a relevant and important one, bridging the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical performance.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The establishment of both upper and lower bounds, even if requiring careful verification, is a significant achievement. The result demonstrating the dramatic reduction in query complexity with side information is noteworthy.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms, operating without prior knowledge of the parameters (k, f+, f−), are a valuable feature, enhancing their practical applicability.
* **Connection to SBM:** Recognizing the connection to the well-studied SBM provides a solid theoretical grounding and opens avenues for leveraging existing techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Writing:** The paper suffers significantly from poor writing quality. Numerous grammatical errors, punctuation issues, and incomplete sentences hinder understanding and make verifying the technical details challenging. Many arguments feel dense and require substantial effort to parse.
* **Overstated Novelty:** The claims of novelty (“first work”, “initiates study”, “significantly generalizes”) are overstated and require careful revision.  Related work exists that is not adequately acknowledged.
* **Missing Related Work:** Several relevant papers are missing from the related work section. Notably, “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) is directly relevant to the querying models considered, and prior work on labeled or weighted SBMs should be discussed.
* **Model Specificity & Theorem 2:** The formal model is not sufficiently clear. The paper lacks explicit statements regarding the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes. Theorem 2, as stated, is potentially incorrect or incomplete. The conditions under which it holds (e.g., the relationship between *k* and the bound) are not explicitly stated and may contradict known results for small *k*. The assumption of *k* tending to infinity should be clearly stated if that is a requirement.
* **Weak Experiments:** The experimental results are weak and unconvincing. They only compare against trivial baselines and lack a benchmark against existing, relevant methods.  This limits the ability to assess the practical impact of the proposed algorithms.
* **Technical Depth & Verification:** The proofs are dense and difficult to verify without significant effort. The reliance on complex notation and the lack of clear explanations make it hard to assess the correctness of the technical arguments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contributions, if correct, represent a substantial advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering.
* The parameter-free nature of the proposed algorithms is a significant practical advantage.
* The problem addressed is important and timely.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The poor writing quality and lack of clarity severely hamper understanding and verification.
* The overstated claims of novelty and missing related work raise concerns about the thoroughness of the literature review.
* The potentially flawed Theorem 2 and weak experiments weaken the overall impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field, but its current form is significantly hindered by issues of clarity, writing quality, and completeness.  The core theoretical results are promising, but require more rigorous presentation and justification.  I initially leaned towards rejection due to the severity of the writing issues and concerns about the technical correctness. However, given the importance of the problem and the potential value of the contributions, I now recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. 

The authors *must* address the following:

1. **Thoroughly revise the writing** to improve clarity, grammar, and punctuation. This is non-negotiable.
2. **Carefully re-examine and clarify the statement of Theorem 2**, explicitly stating the assumptions (e.g., large *k*) and ensuring its correctness.
3. **Expand the related work section** to include relevant papers, particularly those on active learning in SBMs and weighted/labeled SBMs.
4. **Provide more compelling experimental results** by benchmarking against existing methods and demonstrating the performance of the algorithms on a wider range of datasets.
5. **Clarify the formal model** and explicitly state any restrictions on cluster sizes or other parameters.
6. **Reword claims of novelty** to be more accurate and less overstated.



Addressing these issues is crucial to unlocking the full potential of this work and ensuring its impact on the research community.
