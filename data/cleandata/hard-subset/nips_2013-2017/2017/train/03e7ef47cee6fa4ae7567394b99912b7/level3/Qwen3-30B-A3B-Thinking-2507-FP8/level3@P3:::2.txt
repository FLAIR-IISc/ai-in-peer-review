

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the goal is to recover clusters using a minimum number of pairwise queries to an oracle, augmented by a similarity matrix representing side information. The authors claim that with side information (where similarity values follow distributions f+ for same-cluster pairs and f- for different-cluster pairs), the query complexity drops from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence. The paper provides matching upper and lower bounds and develops efficient algorithms.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on the interactive clustering framework where an oracle answers whether two elements belong to the same cluster. While recent work has proposed heuristics for this problem (e.g., [42, 43, 40]), the authors claim their work provides the first rigorous theoretical study. They connect their model to the stochastic block model (SBM), arguing that their framework significantly generalizes existing work by allowing arbitrary distributions f+, f- without assumptions on cluster sizes or k.

However, the authors overlook several key prior works. Most notably, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) directly addresses querying models related to their work but is not cited. Additionally, numerous papers on labeled/weighted stochastic block models (e.g., [2, 1, 25, 24, 11, 36]) that characterize recovery thresholds for SBM are not properly contextualized. The authors' claim that their work "initiates the study" of clustering with side information is inaccurate, as several heuristic algorithms with theoretical analysis already exist.

## Strengths

The paper's main contribution—showing that side information drastically reduces query complexity—appears valuable if correct. The connection to SBM is interesting, and the algorithms are computationally efficient and parameter-free, which is practically significant. The lower bound framework could be of independent interest for classification problems in interactive settings.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity and Writing Quality (Major Weakness):** The paper suffers from poor writing quality. The formal model is unclear, particularly regarding the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes (e.g., the paper doesn't explicitly state whether it assumes balanced clusters or allows for extreme imbalance). Many sentences are confusing or incomplete, suggesting missing text or unclear arguments. The paper contains numerous grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting errors that require thorough proofreading.

**Theorem 2 (Critical Flaw):** Theorem 2 as stated is likely incorrect or incomplete. It appears to implicitly assume k → ∞ but does not state this condition, and may contradict known k = O(1) cases where Q = 0 suffices (e.g., when f+ and f- are perfectly distinguishable). The proof of Theorem 2 (in Section 2) contains technical gaps that make verification difficult.

**Overstated Novelty:** Claims of novelty ("first work," "significantly generalizes") are overstated. The connection to SBM is not as novel as claimed, and prior work (including Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) has already explored similar querying models.

**Experimental Evaluation (Weakness):** The experimental results are weak—they compare only to trivial baselines and do not benchmark against existing methods. The evaluation on the Cora dataset is limited and does not demonstrate the algorithm's superiority over state-of-the-art clustering approaches.

**Minor Issues:** The abstract is too long and delays the presentation of contributions. The use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" jargon is unclear without proper qualification (e.g., missing "average" for Monte Carlo). Footnote notation is suspicious, and phrasing implies non-adaptive algorithms when the work is fundamentally adaptive. Small brackets in the appendix are inconsistent.

## Quality Assessment

The technical content appears promising but is undermined by unclear writing and potential gaps in the proofs. The lower bound in Theorem 2 requires careful re-examination, particularly regarding the k → ∞ assumption. The upper bound algorithms are well-motivated but their analysis is dense and difficult to verify.

## Significance

If the theoretical claims are correct, this work would have significant theoretical and practical implications for interactive clustering. However, the current presentation does not sufficiently establish this significance due to the issues noted above. The paper opens interesting directions for future work, but the current results are not yet robustly established.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper has valuable contributions that could be impactful if the theoretical claims are verified and the writing is significantly improved. The authors must:

1. Correct the overstatement of novelty by properly citing relevant prior work (especially Gadde et al., ISIT 2016)
2. Precisely state all assumptions (e.g., k → ∞ for Theorem 2)
3. Improve writing quality and clarity throughout
4. Provide a more rigorous verification of Theorem 2
5. Strengthen the experimental evaluation by benchmarking against existing methods
6. Fix all grammatical, punctuation, and formatting errors

The authors have demonstrated a strong understanding of the problem, but the current manuscript requires careful revision to address the critical issues noted above. With these revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.
