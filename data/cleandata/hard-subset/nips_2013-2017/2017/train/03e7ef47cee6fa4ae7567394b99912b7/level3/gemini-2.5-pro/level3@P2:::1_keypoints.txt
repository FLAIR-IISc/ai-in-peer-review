- Problem setting: interactive clustering of n items into k unknown clusters using adaptive same-cluster queries plus a similarity matrix W with pairwise similarities drawn from f+ (same cluster) or f- (different clusters).  
- Main theoretical contribution: precise characterization of query complexity reduction using side information, with lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2(f+||f-)}).  
- Algorithms: two efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) whose query complexities nearly match the lower bound up to logarithmic factors.  
- SBM connection: generalizes and connects to the stochastic block model (f+/f- as Bernoulli), showing how active queries alter recovery thresholds.  
- Technical quality: high—complete theoretical study with strong, nearly tight upper and lower bounds and a notable information-theoretic lower bound for interactive settings.  
- Algorithmic strengths: computationally efficient and parameter-free (do not require k, f+, or f-), which is non-trivial and practically valuable.  
- Originality: novel framing of side-information quality via Hellinger distance H^2(f+||f-), formally linking similarity quality to information-theoretic query cost.  
- Significance: opens new research directions at the intersection of active learning and latent-structure models and provides practitioners a concrete measure for similarity utility.  
- Clarity: well-written and motivated, with an accessible introduction and clear articulation of the SBM relationship.  
- Weakness: need explicit discussion of the gap between the Monte Carlo algorithm (O(k log n / H^2)) and the lower bound (Ω(k^2 / H^2))—is the lower bound inapplicable to Monte Carlo or is analysis loose?  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation (Cora) should more explicitly relate the dataset-derived f+ and f- to the theoretical model and its assumptions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem, delivers strong nearly-tight results, introduces a novel conceptual tool (Hellinger), links to extensive SBM literature, and provides practical algorithms.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reviewer lists no strong objections—only minor clarifications suggested.  
- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance; the paper is an excellent, foundational contribution with high potential impact.
