This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a noisy similarity matrix. The authors provide information-theoretic lower and upper bounds on the number of queries needed to recover an optimal clustering when an oracle can answer pairwise comparison queries. The key contribution is demonstrating how side information dramatically reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)), where H denotes the Hellinger divergence. Algorithms are proposed that achieve this complexity, and a general framework for proving lower bounds in this interactive setting is presented.

The work builds upon a recent surge in heuristics for interactive clustering, motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data from experts or crowdsourcing is crucial. The authors correctly identify the lack of theoretical guarantees for these heuristics and attempt to address this gap. Their approach is commendable in seeking rigorous bounds, contrasting with the empirical nature of much existing work in this area.  The connection drawn to the stochastic block model (SBM) is also valuable, framing the problem within a well-studied theoretical context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity with side information is a significant contribution. While interactive clustering has been explored, the explicit consideration of similarity matrices and the derived bounds represent a novel advance.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The attempt to provide information-theoretic lower and upper bounds is commendable.  If the proofs are sound (see weaknesses), this provides a fundamental understanding of the problem’s limits.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem is motivated by important real-world applications, particularly entity resolution and crowdsourcing, where minimizing query cost is essential.
* **Connection to SBM:** The link to the well-established stochastic block model provides a valuable context and suggests avenues for further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Writing Quality:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. Many sentences are convoluted, arguments are difficult to follow, and the overall writing style is poor. Numerous grammatical and formatting errors further detract from readability. This makes it challenging to fully assess the correctness of the technical claims.
* **Incomplete/Imprecise Statements:** Theorem 2, in its current form, is problematic. It appears to make claims that depend on the number of clusters *k* being very large, but this condition isn’t explicitly stated. The theorem, and potentially others, require clearer assumptions. The formal model, especially regarding cluster size distributions, is also not fully specified.
* **Missing Related Work:** The authors omit several relevant papers. Notably, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) is directly relevant and should be cited. Furthermore, work on labeled or weighted SBMs, which already explores the impact of side information, is missing.  The paper also overstates its novelty, claiming to be the "first" in areas where related work exists.
* **Proof Verification Difficulty:** The proofs are dense and complex. While not a guarantee of incorrectness, this density hinders thorough verification within a reasonable review timeframe. 
* **Weak Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is limited. Comparing against only trivial baselines does not adequately demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. Benchmarking against existing entity resolution or clustering heuristics is necessary.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contributions, if correct, are significant and address a gap in the literature.
* The problem is well-motivated and relevant to important applications.
* The connection to the SBM provides a valuable theoretical foundation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The poor writing quality and lack of clarity significantly hinder understanding and verification.
* The potentially flawed or incomplete statements of key theorems raise concerns about correctness.
* The omission of relevant related work weakens the paper's positioning within the field.
* The weak experimental evaluation fails to demonstrate practical impact.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of interactive clustering. However, its significant clarity and presentation issues, coupled with concerns regarding the completeness of the theoretical statements, make it currently unsuitable for acceptance without major revisions.  The authors need to substantially rewrite the paper to improve clarity, address the identified weaknesses in related work, and rigorously verify the correctness of their theorems. A thorough proofreading pass is also essential.

**Recommendation:**

Reject with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. If the authors address the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding clarity, completeness, and related work, the revised paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to NIPS.
