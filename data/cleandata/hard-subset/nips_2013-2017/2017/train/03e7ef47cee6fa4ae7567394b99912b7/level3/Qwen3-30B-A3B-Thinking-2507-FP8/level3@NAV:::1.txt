

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when side information is available in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors establish that with side information (where pairwise similarities follow distributions f+ for same-cluster pairs and f- for different-cluster pairs), the query complexity reduces from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k log n / H²(f+ || f-)), where H is the Hellinger distance. They provide matching lower bounds within a logarithmic factor, demonstrating that side information can drastically reduce the number of queries needed for accurate clustering. The work connects to the stochastic block model literature by generalizing it to arbitrary distributions and showing how side information affects recovery thresholds.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous upper and lower bounds that match within a logarithmic factor. The Hellinger distance provides a principled framework for assessing similarity-matrix quality, and the algorithms are parameter-free, working without knowledge of k, f+, or f-. The connection to SBM is insightful and opens new research avenues. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a complete theoretical treatment.

While the theoretical results are compelling, the experimental validation is somewhat limited to a single dataset (Cora) and could benefit from more comprehensive evaluation across diverse settings. The authors acknowledge that noisy oracle responses are an important direction for future work, which suggests a natural limitation of the current model.

The work excels in all four evaluation criteria: it's technically sound with well-supported claims (Quality), clearly presented with adequate detail for reproducibility (Clarity), introduces a novel theoretical framework for assessing side information (Originality), and provides fundamental insights into how side information affects clustering efficiency with potential applications in entity resolution and active learning (Significance).

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The work is clearly written, makes a significant contribution to the field, and provides a principled framework for assessing similarity matrices. It represents a notable advance over previous heuristic approaches and should be of interest to the NIPS community.
