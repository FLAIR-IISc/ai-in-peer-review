The paper "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information" presents a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, including strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds.

The paper begins by introducing the problem of clustering with side information, where the clustering algorithm has access to a similarity matrix. The authors assume a generative model of side information, where the similarity values are drawn from a probability distribution f+ if the underlying pair of elements belong to the same cluster, and from a distribution f- otherwise. The algorithm designer is given only the similarity matrix without any information on f+ and f-.

The main contribution of the paper is to show the dramatic power of side information in reducing the query complexity of clustering. The authors provide a generic framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering using side information and design efficient algorithms for arbitrary f+ and f- that nearly match the lower bound.

The paper also establishes a connection to popular community detection models, such as the stochastic block model (SBM). The authors note that their model is a direct and significant generalization of the planted partition model, also known as the stochastic block model (SBM). They also provide a lower bound that matches the query complexity upper bound within a logarithmic factor.

The authors propose two algorithms, a Monte Carlo algorithm and a Las Vegas algorithm, both of which are completely parameter-free. The Monte Carlo algorithm reduces the number of queries from O(nk) (no side information) to O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f-)) and recovers the clusters exactly with probability at least 1 - o(1/n). The Las Vegas algorithm recovers the clusters with probability 1 (and not just with high probability) with query complexity O(n log n + k log n / H^2(f+ || f-)).

The paper also presents experimental results on a popular bibliographic dataset, cora, consisting of 1879 nodes, 191 clusters, and 1699612 edges. The authors report that their algorithm achieves 80% precision and close to 90% recall with just 1136 queries.
