This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when, in addition to an oracle answering same-cluster pairwise queries, one has access to a noisy similarity matrix W. Under a generative model where each pairwise similarity is drawn i.i.d. from f+ if two points share a cluster and from f− otherwise, the authors develop an information-theoretic lower bound framework for adaptive querying and give efficient, parameter-free algorithms with nearly matching upper bounds. The key insight is that the quality of side information can be summarized by the squared Hellinger divergence H^2(f+‖f−), which governs how many queries are necessary on top of W to recover the ground-truth partition.

Relation to prior work: Without side information, the optimal query complexity is Θ(nk), matching previous upper and lower bounds for same-cluster queries (e.g., [13, 40]). The side-information model strictly generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary (unknown) f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes; it connects to thresholds for exact/approximate recovery in SBM [1,2,3,36] and recent work on active queries in SBM [19]. It also complements interactive clustering frameworks with feedback [6,7] and same-cluster queries for metric k-means [5], offering information-theoretic guarantees rather than computational ones. The work is also relevant to practice in crowdsourced entity resolution [40,42,43], where W is routinely used to guide queries.

Main technical results: 
- Lower bounds: Any (possibly randomized) adaptive algorithm, even when given k, f+, f−, needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries to recover with constant success probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success probability 1. The proof develops a novel interactive lower bound technique handling adaptivity (standard Fano-type arguments alone are insufficient), reducing assignment of a lightly queried point to a multi-hypothesis test and relating error to Hellinger distances.
- Upper bounds: An efficient parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm achieves O(min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2(f+‖f−)}) queries with high probability; a Las Vegas variant achieves O(n log n + min{nk, (k^2 log n)/H^2}) queries with probability 1. The algorithm iteratively learns empirical “intra/inter” distributions from currently grown clusters and uses Hellinger-based membership tests to decide when W alone is reliable enough; convergence analysis leverages Sanov’s theorem adapted to Hellinger distance. 
- Experiments: On a bibliographic dataset (cora), a greedy query strategy guided by the learned distributions approaches the performance of an idealized “best” strategy with a small number of queries; results are robust to moderate perturbations of f+, f−.

Quality:
- Strengths: The lower bound framework for adaptive querying is technically solid and addresses a genuine gap. The parameter-free algorithm is carefully designed to bootstrap from small clusters to reliable use of W, and its analysis matches the lower bound up to a log n factor. The use of Hellinger divergence is principled and yields clean dependences.
- Weaknesses: (i) The algorithmic analysis assumes discrete f+, f− over a fixed finite alphabet; while the lower bounds cover continuous distributions, extensions of the algorithm and proofs to large/continuous supports are not fully developed. (ii) The i.i.d. sampling assumption for entries of W is strong; real similarity matrices often violate independence, potentially affecting both bounds and algorithms. (iii) The perfect oracle assumption is deferred to a companion paper; a brief robustness discussion or a unified bound would increase practical relevance. (iv) Experimental evaluation is limited (one dataset, no synthetic scaling tests, and no comparison to prior heuristics [40,43] under controlled H^2). (v) The paper sometimes alternates between H and H^2; consistent notation and a single statement of the main bound would aid readability.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and the high-level ideas are clearly conveyed. The organization places detailed proofs in the appendix; this is fine, but the main text could benefit from a succinct pseudocode for the iterative-update procedure and a clearer statement of assumptions (finite alphabet, independence). Unifying the notation for Hellinger distance and explicitly aligning upper/lower bound statements would prevent confusion.

Originality:
High. The interactive lower bound technique for adaptive queries in the presence of side information appears novel and broadly useful. The parameter-free, Hellinger-driven algorithmic framework is also original and addresses a practically important setting where k, f+, f− are unknown.

Significance:
High. The results quantify, in an information-theoretic way, how side information lowers query complexity via H^2(f+‖f−). This provides a principled yardstick for the quality of similarity matrices and bridges theory and practice in interactive clustering and ER. The connection to SBM suggests further work on how queries shift recovery thresholds.

Empirical evaluation:
The cora experiment is encouraging but limited. To strengthen the case: (i) include synthetic SBM-style experiments varying H^2 systematically and k to validate O((k^2 log n)/H^2) scaling; (ii) compare against established heuristics [40,43] and recent analysis [18,33]; (iii) report wall-clock and memory costs (reading W is O(n^2)); (iv) explore robustness to dependence and to oracle noise (even if only via simple majority voting).

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a clean, quantitative answer.
- Novel adaptive lower bound technique of potential independent interest.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with bounds matching lower bounds up to log n.
- Clear conceptual contribution: Hellinger divergence as the right measure of side-information quality.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong modeling assumptions (i.i.d. W, discrete support) and limited discussion of violations.
- Empirical section is thin and lacks comparisons and scaling validation.
- Some notation/statement inconsistencies may confuse readers.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Can the algorithm and analysis be extended to continuous/large-alphabet f+, f− (e.g., via kernel density or discretization) with explicit dependence on alphabet size/discretization error?
- How sensitive are the bounds to mild dependence in W (e.g., attribute-induced correlations)? Any partial robustness results?
- Can you provide synthetic experiments varying H^2 and k to empirically validate the predicted scaling?
- Clarify the exact query complexities in Theorem 1 vs Theorem 2 (k vs k^2 factors) and ensure consistent H vs H^2 notation.
- Any memory-efficient variants that avoid storing the full W when n is large?

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are strong and broadly relevant; addressing the experimental and clarity issues would make the paper even more compelling.
