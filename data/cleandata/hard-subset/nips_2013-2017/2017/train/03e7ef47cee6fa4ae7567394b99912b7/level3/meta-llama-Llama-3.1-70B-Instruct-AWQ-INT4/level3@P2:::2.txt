The paper "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information" presents a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity of clustering with side information. The authors provide strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on the query complexity of clustering with side information, demonstrating the power of side information in reducing the query complexity.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the areas of clustering, community detection, and active learning. The authors build upon the stochastic block model (SBM) and extend it to allow for arbitrary probability distributions, making their model a significant generalization of SBM.

The strengths of the paper include its novel contributions to the field of clustering with side information. The authors develop a generic framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering using side information and design efficient algorithms for arbitrary distributions that nearly match the lower bound. The paper also opens up many avenues for interesting future research, including the study of query complexity of interactive clustering with side information and the connection to popular community detection models.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The writing quality is poor, making it difficult to follow the authors' arguments and verify the proofs. The paper omits several relevant prior works, including labeled/weighted stochastic block model papers. The authors' claims of novelty are overstated and should be reworded. The formal model is unclear, and Theorem 2 as stated is likely incorrect or incomplete.

The paper's contributions are valuable if correct, but the writing quality is poor enough to make the recommendation borderline. The authors need to address the issues mentioned above, including rewriting the paper to improve clarity, adding missing citations, and revising the theorem statements to include precise conditions.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on originality, as it presents a novel study of the query complexity of clustering with side information. However, it scores poorly on clarity, due to the poor writing quality and the lack of clear explanations of the formal model and the proofs. The paper scores well on significance, as it opens up many avenues for interesting future research and has the potential to impact the field of clustering and community detection.

In conclusion, while the paper makes valuable contributions to the field of clustering with side information, its poor writing quality and lack of clarity make it difficult to recommend for publication in its current form. The authors need to address the issues mentioned above and revise the paper to improve its clarity and accuracy.
