- Paper studies interactive clustering with noisy similarity matrix W and shows side information cuts queries from Θ(nk) to O(k log n / H2(f+‖f−)) with nearly matching lower bounds and gives two parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that do not require k, f+, or f−.

- Model generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM); similarities are i.i.d. conditional on ground truth and the work connects to community detection, interactive clustering, and crowdsourced ER literature.

- Key conceptual advance: isolates value of side information via squared Hellinger divergence H2 and provides tight (up to log n) query bounds.

- Novel lower-bound framework for interactive, adaptive querying that handles randomized algorithms and known-parameter settings, going beyond standard Fano-type tools.

- Provides efficient, parameter-free algorithms with an iterative-update scheme that learns thresholds from data and theoretical guarantees matching lower bounds within O(log n).

- Runtime is O(n^2), dominated by reading W, and small empirical study on CORA shows feasibility and robustness to approximate f+, f−.

- Main modeling weakness: W entries assumed i.i.d. given clustering, which may fail in real data with dependencies and graph transitivity, potentially undermining concentration arguments.

- Algorithmic development assumes discrete support for f+, f− (restrictive); continuous-case guarantees are only sketched via Sanov-type arguments.

- Presentation issues: inconsistent notation and ambiguity about whether bounds involve k or k^2; needs consistent statements across abstract, theorems, and discussion.

- Empirical evaluation is limited: only one dataset, no comparisons to strong baselines, and no synthetic experiments varying k, H2, or cluster size distributions to validate scalings.

- Theoretical independence from k is good, but the paper does not quantify potential gains if k were known in practice.

- Practical concerns: O(n^2) runtime/memory may be heavy for large n and implementation/data-structure considerations are not discussed.

- Theoretical quality is strong: lower-bound argument and parameter-free algorithm analysis are technically sound though some proofs are deferred and exact k dependence needs clarification.

- Clarity is generally good with a clear problem formulation and iterative-update idea, but notation mix-ups (H vs H2, k vs k^2) and missing concise summary of bounds hinder readability.

- Originality is high: introducing H2 into query-complexity and interactive lower bounds that handle adaptivity is new; the parameter-free iterative thresholding is novel in this context.

- Significance is high: bridges theory and practice for interactive clustering/ER, gives an interpretable measure (H2) for side-information quality, and the lower-bound framework may have broader use.

- Requests for clarification: reconcile k vs k^2 factors in bounds; provide a formal continuous-distribution algorithmic statement (e.g., via discretization and its binning dependence); analyze sensitivity to cluster-size imbalance and small clusters; discuss relaxing independence assumptions (e.g., exchangeable arrays/graphons).

- Pros for acceptance: strong novel theory, parameter-free efficient algorithms, unification with SBM literature, and identifying H2 as the governing quantity.

- Cons for acceptance: ambiguity in k dependence and presentation, limited empirical validation and baseline comparisons, and potentially restrictive W assumptions.

- Suggestions for improvement: standardize asymptotic statements and include a summary table of bounds; add synthetic experiments varying k, H2, and cluster balance and compare to ER heuristics; discuss practical implementation (sampling W, memory/time tradeoffs); and outline formal extensions to continuous f+, f− and noisy oracles.

- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper with clear novelty and impact that would benefit from clarified statements and stronger experiments.

- Reviewer confidence: high in theory/positioning assessment; medium regarding empirical significance.
