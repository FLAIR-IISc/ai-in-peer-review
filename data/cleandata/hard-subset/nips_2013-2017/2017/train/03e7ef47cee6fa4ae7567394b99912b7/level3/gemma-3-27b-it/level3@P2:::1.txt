This paper presents a theoretical investigation into the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix. The authors demonstrate that incorporating such side information—where similarity values are drawn from distributions f+ and f- depending on cluster membership—can dramatically reduce the number of queries needed to recover an optimal clustering, from Θ(nk) without side information to O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f-)) with it, where H denotes the Hellinger divergence. They provide both upper and lower bounds, establishing near-optimality of their results, and connect their work to the stochastic block model (SBM) literature.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in clustering where obtaining accurate similarity functions is challenging. The increasing use of interactive clustering with human oracles (e.g., crowd-sourcing) underscores the practical relevance of understanding the theoretical limits of query complexity. The authors effectively highlight the gap between existing heuristic algorithms and theoretical guarantees, and position their work as filling this void. The connection to the SBM is also thoughtfully drawn, illustrating how their model generalizes established concepts and raising interesting questions about the interplay between querying and standard SBM recovery thresholds.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The core contribution – the O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f-)) query complexity – is a significant advancement. The nearly matching lower bound demonstrates the tightness of the result and strengthens its theoretical foundation.
* **Novel Framework:** The paper provides a rigorous framework for analyzing the impact of side information on clustering complexity, quantified by the Hellinger divergence between f+ and f-. This offers a principled way to assess the quality of similarity matrices.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f-, making them practically appealing.
* **Connection to SBM:** The established link to the SBM provides a valuable context and opens avenues for future research, particularly regarding the impact of queries on SBM recovery.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear introduction outlining the problem and motivations. The contributions are well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Evaluation Limited:** While the authors include experimental results, they are relatively limited in scope and primarily serve to illustrate the algorithm's performance on a single dataset. A more comprehensive evaluation, comparing against existing heuristics on a wider range of datasets and varying Hellinger divergence values, would strengthen the practical impact.
* **Constant Factors:** The logarithmic factors in the upper bound (and the constant factors generally) are not fully optimized. While asymptotically tight, the practical performance might be affected by these constants.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of a fixed support cardinality for f+ and f- could be a limitation. While acknowledged, it might be worthwhile to discuss the potential impact of relaxing this assumption.
* **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, while rigorous, are lengthy and relegated to the appendix. While standard for NIPS, accessibility could be improved by providing more intuition within the main text.
* **Related Work Gaps:** The paper references [5] regarding clustering with same-cluster queries but doesn’t fully elaborate on the differences between their work and the current paper’s scope and assumptions, specifically the NP-hardness and metric requirements of [5]. Similarly, the discussion of [19] and its active label-query approach could benefit from further contextualization.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in clustering with a rigorous theoretical approach.
* The results are technically sound, with strong upper and lower bounds demonstrating near-optimality.
* The parameter-free algorithms are practically relevant.
* The connection to the SBM and the insights into the role of side information are valuable contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited.
* The constant factors in the upper bound may affect practical performance.
* The complex proofs may hinder accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the understanding of interactive clustering. The results are impactful, and the proposed framework offers a valuable perspective on the role of side information. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and the proofs are dense, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference and recommend acceptance.

**Confidence:** 8/10.
