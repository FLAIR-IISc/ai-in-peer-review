

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information" (NIPS 2017)

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the side information is provided as a similarity matrix W with entries drawn from distributions f+ (same cluster) and f- (different clusters). The main contribution is showing that side information can dramatically reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H is the Hellinger distance, with matching lower bounds within a log n factor. The algorithms are efficient and parameter-free (work without knowing k, f+, or f-).

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of clustering with side information, addressing a gap in the literature where most previous work focused on heuristics. Second, it establishes a connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) literature, showing how side information changes recovery thresholds in SBM. Third, it introduces a principled way to assess similarity-matrix quality via Hellinger distance, which is more natural for this problem than KL divergence. Finally, it develops a novel framework for proving lower bounds in interactive learning settings, which could be of independent interest.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs for both upper and lower bounds. The lower bound proof is particularly impressive, as it overcomes the challenge of proving information-theoretic bounds in an interactive setting where standard Fano-type inequalities don't apply. The authors cleverly use a generalized version of Fano's inequality combined with a careful construction to establish their lower bound. The upper bound algorithms are efficient, parameter-free, and demonstrate significant improvements over the Θ(nk) baseline.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to results to experiments. The connection to the entity resolution application is well-motivated and provides a strong practical context. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate that the greedy strategy closely matches the best-case performance with significantly fewer queries, supporting the theoretical claims.

The work is highly original in several respects. While previous work on clustering with queries (e.g., [5, 18, 33]) focused on specific heuristics or special cases, this paper provides a general framework for arbitrary distributions f+ and f-. The use of Hellinger distance as a measure of similarity quality is novel in this context and provides a more natural metric for the problem than KL divergence. The paper also makes an important contribution by showing how side information can dramatically reduce query complexity, even encoding full clustering in extreme cases.

The significance of this work is substantial. The query complexity reduction from Θ(nk) to O(k log n / H²(f+||f-)) represents a dramatic improvement, especially when the similarity matrix is good (H² is not too small). This has direct implications for practical applications like entity resolution, where reducing the number of crowd-sourced queries can significantly reduce costs. The paper also opens up many avenues for future research, including extending the results to sparse SBM settings and exploring how to learn f+ and f- from data.

Some minor weaknesses include the somewhat complex algorithm description, which might make the paper less accessible to practitioners. The experimental results, while supportive, are limited to one dataset (Cora) and could benefit from more extensive validation. The paper also doesn't fully explore the trade-off between query complexity and computational complexity.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a significant gap in the literature. The paper provides rigorous, general results that are both theoretically interesting and practically relevant. The connection to SBM and the development of a new lower bounding framework are particularly noteworthy. The work meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS papers: it is technically sound, well-written, highly original, and significant for both theory and practice.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- First rigorous theoretical study of clustering with side information
- Dramatic query complexity reduction with tight bounds
- Novel framework for lower bounds in interactive learning
- Parameter-free algorithms with practical relevance
- Strong connection to SBM and entity resolution applications
- Clear theoretical contribution with significant implications

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Algorithm description is somewhat complex
- Limited experimental validation beyond Cora dataset
- Some technical details in proofs could be clarified

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of interactive learning and clustering. It provides a principled theoretical foundation for understanding how side information affects query complexity, with implications for practical applications like entity resolution and crowdsourcing. The results are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the work opens up many interesting avenues for future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
