This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries in the presence of side information: a noisy similarity matrix whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ for within-cluster pairs and f− for cross-cluster pairs. The main results are (i) information-theoretic lower bounds showing that, in a regime where f+ and f− are close, any algorithm needs on the order of min{nk, k^2/H^2} queries (up to constants), where H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−; and (ii) efficient, parameter-free algorithms achieving O(k log n/H^2) queries with high probability (Monte Carlo) and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}) queries with probability 1 (Las Vegas), nearly matching the lower bounds up to log n factors. The side information reduces the worst-case Θ(nk) query complexity dramatically, and the work connects the model to SBM (as the special case of Bernoulli side information). The lower bound is proved via a careful interactive-information argument that avoids standard Fano barriers; the upper bounds use an iterative, parameter-free estimation of f+ and f− via Hellinger projections to decide when queries can be replaced by side information.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on and contrasts with interactive clustering heuristics using constraint queries (e.g., Awasthi–Balcan–Voevodski, Ashtiani–Kushagra–Ben-David), and with SBM exact/weak recovery thresholds (Abbe–Bandeira–Hall; Mossel–Neeman–Sly; Abbe–Sandon). It is close in spirit to active label queries for SBM (e.g., Gadde et al., ISIT’16), and to parameter-free SBM recovery (Abbe–Sandon; Chin–Rao–Vu). It is also related to crowdsourced ER systems that guide queries by similarities. The general lower-bound framework for interactive settings is potentially useful beyond clustering.

Quality:
- Strengths: The formalization of query complexity with side information is clear in intent and addresses an important gap. The lower-bound technique for adaptive querying is novel and potentially reusable. The parameter-free iterative procedure is interesting and leverages Hellinger geometry in a natural way. The upper/lower bounds nearly match in their dependence on k, n, and H^2.
- Weaknesses: Key assumptions and regimes are not stated precisely enough to validate the theorems. Theorem 2 appears to require additional asymptotic conditions (e.g., behavior of k, cluster sizes, and H^2), but these are not explicitly stated; as written, it risks contradicting known cases with k = O(1) where Q = 0 suffices for exact recovery under SBM separations. The upper bounds assume discrete fixed-support f+, f−, whereas the lower bound allows continuous distributions; the implications of this mismatch are not discussed. Proofs are dense and hard to verify, with several missing steps and confusing phrasing. There is an inconsistency between statements of query complexity in different sections (e.g., O(k log n/H^2) vs O(k^2 log n/H^2)); these need to be reconciled and constants/log factors made explicit.

Clarity:
- The writing needs substantial revision. Definitions (e.g., minimax vs average-case formulation; whether cluster sizes are adversarial, balanced, or random; whether k is fixed, growing, or unknown) should appear up front. The conditions under which Theorem 2 holds (bounds on H^2, scaling of k, constraints on cluster sizes) must be precise in the theorem statement. Clarify the meaning of “Monte Carlo” (small failure probability for correctness) and “Las Vegas” (always correct; random query complexity) consistently; currently, the Las Vegas guarantee mixes probability-1 correctness with a high-probability bound on queries. Numerous grammatical, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting errors (including footnotes and bracket mismatches) impede readability.

Originality:
- The problem formulation and the explicit dependence of query complexity on Hellinger divergence are interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this level of generality. However, the novelty claims are overstated. The connection to SBM, active querying in SBM (e.g., Gadde et al., 2016), and parameter-free recovery in SBM should be discussed carefully. Related work on labeled/weighted SBMs and recovery thresholds for general block models should be cited and contrasted.

Significance:
- If the results are correct and clarified, the paper would provide a clean and influential characterization of how side information reduces query complexity, with a reusable lower-bound framework and practical, parameter-free algorithms. The current experimental evidence is too thin to support practical claims, but the theory alone is potentially valuable.

Empirical evaluation:
- Experiments are limited to a single ER dataset with trivial baselines. At minimum: (i) compare against existing interactive clustering heuristics and query strategies; (ii) simulate labeled SBMs (varying k, n, H^2) to empirically validate the O(k log n/H^2) scaling and the transition from needing Θ(nk) queries to sublinear; (iii) ablate the parameter-free estimation to show its benefit; (iv) report exact recovery probability vs query budget and error bars. Clarify whether the similarity distributions are known/learned and how robust the algorithm is to model misspecification.

Specific issues and suggestions:
- Precisely state the model: Are clusters arbitrary (minimax), or sampled from a distribution? Are extremely imbalanced clusterings allowed? What is the failure probability and over which randomness (oracle/algorithm/side information)?
- Theorem 2: Add explicit conditions on k, a lower bound on cluster sizes, and the parameter regime H^2 ≤ c (as used). Explain compatibility with regimes where Q = 0 is sufficient (e.g., high-signal SBM).
- Unify query-complexity statements across abstract, theorems, and algorithms; remove contradictions between O(k log n/H^2) and O(k^2 log n/H^2).
- Discuss the discrete-support assumption for the upper bound and whether/how the algorithm extends to continuous f+, f−.
- Tone down claims such as “first work,” “initiates,” and “significantly generalizes”; cite active querying in SBM (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) and relevant weighted/label SBM works.
- Improve algorithm description (pseudocode), define Membership clearly, and justify thresholds with explicit concentration inequalities. Ensure “parameter-free” is used precisely (no knowledge of k, f+, f−, but potentially needs finite support).
- Thorough proofreading to fix grammar/formatting and remove ambiguous phrases.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and timely question; clear theoretical advance if correct.
- Nearly matching upper/lower bounds with explicit dependence on H^2.
- Novel lower-bound machinery for adaptive queries.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with a plausible analysis path.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theorem statements and assumptions are currently imprecise; potential inconsistency with known regimes.
- Proofs are difficult to follow; writing quality is below conference standards.
- Experimental evaluation is weak and lacks comparisons.
- Overstated novelty and incomplete related work.

Post-rebuttal stance: I am inclined to accept provided the authors (i) precisely state all conditions in theorems (including dependence on k, cluster sizes, and H^2), (ii) reconcile all query-complexity claims, (iii) add missing related work and temper novelty claims, (iv) substantially improve clarity and correctness of proofs, and (v) strengthen experiments with competitive baselines and synthetic validations. A careful revision is essential.
