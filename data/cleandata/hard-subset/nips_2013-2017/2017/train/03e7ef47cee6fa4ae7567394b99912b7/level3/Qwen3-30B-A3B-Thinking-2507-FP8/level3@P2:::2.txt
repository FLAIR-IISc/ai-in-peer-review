

# Review for "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering when an oracle provides pair-wise "same cluster" answers, augmented with a side information matrix representing noisy pairwise similarities between elements. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, showing that side information dramatically reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f₊‖f₋)), where H denotes Hellinger divergence. The work connects to community detection models like the stochastic block model (SBM), and proposes parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f₊, or f₋.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in interactive learning where clustering accuracy can be improved by incorporating side information (similarity matrix) and querying an oracle. While the basic query complexity problem without side information has been studied (e.g., Ω(nk) lower bound), this work provides the first rigorous analysis of how side information reduces query complexity. The paper relates to SBM (a special case of their model) and prior work on interactive clustering heuristics [40, 42, 43], but notably omits several relevant theoretical works. For instance, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) study active learning for community detection in SBM, and Abbe et al. (FOCS 2015, NIPS 2015) analyze recovery thresholds in general stochastic block models. The authors' claim that "this is the first rigorous theoretical study" is overstated given these prior works.

## Strengths

**Originality & Significance (7/10):** The paper makes a valuable contribution by precisely characterizing how side information reduces query complexity. The connection to SBM and the demonstration that Hellinger divergence is the right measure for both upper and lower bounds is insightful. The results could guide the design of more efficient interactive clustering algorithms in practice.

**Quality (6/10):** The technical results appear promising if the proofs are correct. The authors establish both information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient algorithms that nearly match these bounds. The Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms are parameter-free, which is practically important. The experimental results on the Cora dataset provide some empirical validation, though they are limited.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity (4/10):** The writing is poor, with numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent notation, and unclear explanations. The formal model is ambiguous—restrictions on cluster sizes (e.g., whether clusters must be balanced) are not explicitly stated. Theorems lack precise conditions (e.g., Theorem 2 likely assumes k → ∞ but doesn't state this, contradicting known results for constant k where Q=0 suffices). The distinction between Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms is unclear without "average" qualifiers. The appendix contains suspicious footnote notation and small brackets that suggest incomplete text.

**Originality (5/10):** The novelty claims are overstated. The work generalizes SBM but doesn't significantly advance beyond existing theoretical results on SBM recovery thresholds. The omission of key related work (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) undermines the novelty claim. The "first work" assertion is inaccurate given the prior theoretical literature on related problems.

**Significance (6/10):** The results have potential practical significance for interactive clustering applications like entity resolution, but the experimental evaluation is weak. The authors only compare against trivial baselines rather than benchmarking against existing methods. The claimed "dramatic power" of side information isn't sufficiently demonstrated against realistic alternatives.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First precise characterization of how side information reduces query complexity in interactive clustering
- Demonstrates the importance of Hellinger divergence in both upper and lower bounds
- Proposes parameter-free algorithms with practical value
- Connects to fundamental community detection models like SBM
- Contains theoretically sound results if the proofs are verified

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Major omissions of key related work (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016; Abbe et al., FOCS 2015)
- Theorem 2 likely contains errors or missing conditions (k → ∞ assumption)
- Poor writing quality makes verification difficult
- Experimental results are weak and unconvincing
- Overstated novelty claims undermine credibility

## Recommendation

This paper addresses an important problem with potentially valuable results. However, the writing quality is so poor that it makes verification difficult, and the novelty claims are overstated. The omission of key related work is particularly problematic. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the following:

1. Carefully rewording novelty claims to accurately reflect prior work
2. Clarifying the formal model and adding precise conditions to theorems
3. Correcting numerous grammatical and formatting errors
4. Strengthening the experimental evaluation to benchmark against existing methods
5. Including the missing citation to Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016)

With these revisions, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, the weaknesses in clarity and originality make it borderline. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, as the core technical contributions appear sound and valuable if properly presented.
