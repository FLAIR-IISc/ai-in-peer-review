## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with access to a similarity matrix, representing side information about pairwise relationships between elements. The authors establish a significant result: incorporating this side information drastically reduces the query complexity from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k log n / H<sup>2</sup>(f+ || f-)), where H is the Hellinger distance between the distributions governing similarities within and between clusters. This bound is nearly tight, matching a corresponding lower bound up to a log n factor. 

The work is well-motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data interactively is crucial. The contribution builds on the stochastic block model (SBM) literature but uniquely focuses on how side information affects the *number of queries* needed for recovery, rather than simply characterizing recovery thresholds. This distinction is a key insight.  The authors also provide a principled way to assess the quality of the similarity matrix via the Hellinger distance, offering a valuable practical tool.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Result:** The core result demonstrating the reduction in query complexity with side information is significant and well-supported by theoretical analysis.  The near-tightness of the bound further strengthens this contribution.
* **Novel Perspective:** Framing the problem in terms of query complexity, and relating it to the SBM while highlighting the impact of side information on query thresholds, provides a fresh perspective.
* **Parameter-Free Algorithms:**  The proposed algorithms operate without needing to know k, f+, or f-, making them potentially practical.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, although dense. The motivation and connection to existing work are clearly established.
* **Lower Bound:** Establishing a matching (up to a log n factor) lower bound adds significant weight to the upper bound results and validates the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proofs:** While the main results are clearly stated, the proofs (detailed in the supplementary material) appear quite technical and may be difficult to fully grasp without significant background.
* **Practical Considerations:** While parameter-free, the practical applicability of the algorithm might be limited by the computational cost of estimating the Hellinger distance and the iterative update process in very large datasets. Further investigation of this is needed.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results, while demonstrating the algorithm's performance on a specific dataset, are somewhat limited.  More extensive evaluation with varying dataset characteristics and comparison to existing heuristics would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of interactive clustering.
* The results are well-motivated, clearly stated, and rigorously proven.
* The work opens up new avenues for research in the intersection of clustering, active learning, and information theory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity of the proofs may limit accessibility.
* The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.



**Overall:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. Despite some weaknesses, the significant theoretical contribution, novel perspective, and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and the research addresses an important problem with a rigorous and insightful approach. Addressing the practical considerations and expanding the empirical evaluation could further strengthen the work in future revisions.
