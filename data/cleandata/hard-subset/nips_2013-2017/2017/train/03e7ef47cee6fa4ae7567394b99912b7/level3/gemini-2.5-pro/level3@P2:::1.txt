This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the query complexity of interactive clustering when provided with noisy side information. The problem setting involves clustering n elements into an unknown number of k clusters by adaptively querying an oracle that can determine if any two elements belong to the same cluster. The key novelty is the introduction of a similarity matrix W, where the similarity value for a pair of elements is drawn from a distribution f+ if they are in the same cluster, and from f- otherwise.

The paper's main contribution is to precisely characterize the dramatic reduction in query complexity enabled by this side information. While clustering without side information requires Θ(nk) queries, the authors show that with it, the complexity can be significantly reduced. They establish an information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2(f+||f-)}), where H^2(f+||f-) is the squared Hellinger divergence between the two distributions. This result is complemented by two efficient, parameter-free algorithms (a Monte Carlo and a Las Vegas variant) whose query complexities nearly match this lower bound, differing only by a logarithmic factor. This work connects to and generalizes the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM), where f+ and f- are Bernoulli distributions, and provides a new perspective on how active queries can alter recovery thresholds.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. It presents a complete theoretical study with strong, nearly tight upper and lower bounds on the query complexity. The information-theoretic lower bound proof for an interactive setting is particularly notable and could be of independent interest for proving bounds in other adaptive learning scenarios. The algorithms are computationally efficient and, importantly, parameter-free (not requiring knowledge of k, f+, or f-), which is a non-trivial achievement.

*   **Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While active clustering and SBM are established fields, this work is the first to formally quantify the relationship between the quality of a similarity matrix and the information-theoretic cost of interactive clustering. Framing the quality of side information through the Hellinger distance between f+ and f- provides a novel and powerful conceptual tool. It moves beyond heuristics common in practice (e.g., in entity resolution) by providing a principled theoretical foundation.

*   **Significance:** The results are very significant for both researchers and practitioners. For researchers, the paper opens up new avenues for studying the interplay between active learning and latent structure models like SBM, particularly how queries can push fundamental recovery thresholds. For practitioners in areas like crowdsourcing and entity resolution, it provides a clear and interpretable answer to the question of "how valuable is my similarity function?". The Hellinger distance serves as a concrete measure of information that can guide the decision of whether to rely on automated scores or invest in expensive human queries.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and motivated. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem using the practical example of entity resolution, making the theoretical work accessible and demonstrating its relevance. The connection to SBM is also clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The clarity could be slightly improved regarding the two algorithmic upper bounds presented. Theorem 1 gives a Monte Carlo algorithm with complexity O(k log n / H^2) and a Las Vegas algorithm with complexity O(k^2 log n / H^2). The lower bound is Ω(k^2 / H^2). This suggests the Las Vegas algorithm is tight up to a log factor, but there is a gap of a factor of k for the Monte Carlo algorithm. It would be helpful for the authors to explicitly discuss this gap. Is the lower bound not tight for Monte Carlo (error-prone) algorithms, or is the analysis of the algorithm not tight? A brief clarification would strengthen the paper's narrative.

*   The experimental evaluation on the Cora dataset is a welcome addition. However, the connection between the experiment and the theoretical model could be made more explicit. For instance, do the empirically derived f+ and f- distributions from the dataset satisfy the model's assumptions? A short discussion on how the generative model relates to real-world similarity scores would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem at the intersection of active learning, clustering, and community detection.
2.  It delivers strong and nearly-tight theoretical results, providing a complete characterization of the query complexity.
3.  The core conceptual contribution—quantifying the value of side information via Hellinger distance—is novel, insightful, and likely to be influential.
4.  The work establishes an interesting and timely connection to the extensive literature on the stochastic block model.
5.  The proposed algorithms are efficient and parameter-free, which is a significant strength and enhances their practical relevance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
I do not have strong arguments against acceptance. The minor points mentioned above are for improvement and do not detract from the paper's core contributions.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that provides a foundational theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information. The results are strong, the insights are novel, and the work has high potential for impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.
