The paper "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information" presents a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity of clustering with side information, where the clustering algorithm has access to a similarity matrix. The authors provide strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on the query complexity of clustering with side information. They also develop efficient algorithms for arbitrary discrete distributions that nearly match the lower bound.

The main contributions of the paper are:

1. A general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering using side information, which holds even for randomized algorithms and with full knowledge of the distributions and the number of clusters.
2. A Monte Carlo randomized algorithm that reduces the number of queries from O(nk) to O(k^2 logn H^2(f+||f-)), where H^2(f+||f-) is the squared Hellinger divergence between the probability distributions f+ and f-, and recovers the clusters accurately with high probability.
3. A Las Vegas algorithm that recovers the clusters with probability 1 and has query complexity O(n log n + k^2 logn H^2(f+||f-)).

The paper also explores the connection to popular community detection models, such as the stochastic block model, and opens up many avenues for interesting future research.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity of clustering with side information, which is a fundamental problem in machine learning and data mining.
* The authors develop efficient algorithms that nearly match the lower bound, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The paper explores the connection to popular community detection models, which is an interesting direction for future research.

Weaknesses:

* The writing quality is poor, making it difficult to follow the arguments and proofs.
* The paper omits several relevant prior works, including labeled/weighted stochastic block model papers.
* The author claims of novelty are overstated and should be reworded.
* The formal model is unclear, and the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes are not explicitly stated.
* Theorem 2 as stated is likely incorrect or incomplete, and may contradict known k = O(1) cases where Q = 0 suffices.
* The numerical experiments are weak, comparing only to trivial baselines and not benchmarking against existing methods.

Recommendation:

* The paper has valuable contributions, but the writing quality is poor enough to make the recommendation borderline.
* The authors should carefully revise the paper to address the writing, precise theorem statements, and grammatical concerns.
* The authors should also provide a clear and explicit statement of the formal model, including the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes.
* The authors should reword their claims of novelty to be more accurate and modest.
* The authors should strengthen their numerical experiments by comparing to existing methods and providing more robust results.
