This paper studies the problem of interactive clustering, where an algorithm can query an oracle to determine if two elements belong to the same cluster. The primary contribution is a theoretical analysis of how access to a noisy similarity matrix (termed "side information") affects the query complexity required to perfectly recover the underlying clustering. The authors model the side information as a matrix where entries for same-cluster pairs are drawn from a distribution f+, and entries for different-cluster pairs are drawn from f−.

The main results establish information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on the query complexity. The authors show that side information can dramatically reduce the number of required queries from Θ(nk) (in the absence of side information) to O(k^2 log n / H^2(f+||f−)), where H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence between the two distributions. This work connects the problem to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM), as SBM is a special case of their side information model. The paper also presents efficient, parameter-free algorithms (both Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) that achieve the upper bounds.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem is well-motivated, theoretically interesting, and practically relevant. Quantifying the precise value of noisy side information in reducing human annotation effort (queries) is an important contribution. The connection to the SBM and the generalization of it to arbitrary distributions f+ and f− is a strong point.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical treatment, including information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching algorithmic upper bounds. If technically sound, these results represent a significant advance in our understanding of interactive clustering.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require prior knowledge of the number of clusters (k) or the underlying distributions (f+, f−) is a valuable practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's significant potential is unfortunately undermined by major issues in clarity, technical precision, and experimental evaluation.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very difficult to read and verify. The writing is dense and contains numerous grammatical, punctuation, and formatting errors. Many sentences are confusingly structured or incomplete, which severely hinders the reader's ability to follow the technical arguments, particularly in the proofs. The abstract is overly long and delays the statement of the core contributions. This lack of clarity is the most critical weakness and makes the paper borderline for acceptance.

*   **Technical Precision and Soundness:**
    1.  **Unclear Formal Model:** The problem statement is not fully specified. It is unclear if the bounds are for the worst-case (minimax) over all possible partitions or for some assumed generative model of partitions. The behavior of the bounds for pathological cases, like highly imbalanced clusters, is not discussed.
    2.  **Potentially Incomplete Lower Bound (Theorem 2):** The statement of Theorem 2 appears incomplete or potentially incorrect as written. The stated lower bound of Ω(k^2 / H^2) seems to implicitly assume k grows with n. For constant k, it is known that in certain SBM regimes, perfect recovery is possible with zero queries (Q=0), which would contradict a non-trivial lower bound. The theorem statement must be precise about all assumptions, including any dependence on k.
    3.  **Use of Jargon:** The distinction between "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" algorithms is non-standard here. Typically, a Las Vegas algorithm for this problem would always be correct but have random complexity, while a Monte Carlo algorithm would have deterministic complexity but a probability of error. The descriptions provided in the paper do not perfectly align with these standard definitions.

*   **Related Work:** The authors have missed several relevant lines of work.
    1.  The literature on the stochastic block model with node labels or weighted edges is highly relevant and should be discussed.
    2.  Specifically, the paper "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" by Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) is directly related as it also considers active querying in SBMs.
    3.  The claims of novelty (e.g., "first rigorous theoretical study") are overstated and should be toned down to more accurately reflect the state of the field.

*   **Weak Experimental Results:** The experiments are not convincing. The proposed algorithm is only compared against trivial "best" and "worst" case query strategies, rather than being benchmarked against existing heuristic methods for interactive clustering or entity resolution that also use similarity scores (e.g., [40, 43, 18]). This makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed algorithm.

**Recommendation:**
This paper tackles an important problem and the high-level theoretical results appear valuable. However, the poor quality of the writing, the lack of precision in the theoretical statements, and the weak experimental section make it fall short of the acceptance bar for NIPS in its current form.

I would be willing to raise my score if the authors can substantially revise the paper to address these concerns. Specifically, a successful revision must include:
1.  A thorough and careful proofreading of the entire manuscript to fix the numerous grammatical and stylistic errors.
2.  A precise formal definition of the problem setting (e.g., minimax over what class of partitions?).
3.  A revised statement of Theorem 2 that explicitly states all assumptions, especially regarding the regime of k, and clarifies how it relates to known SBM recovery thresholds.
4.  An expanded related work section that includes the missing citations and moderates the claims of novelty.
5.  A strengthened experimental section with comparisons to relevant existing algorithms.

Given the depth of these issues, the paper is currently borderline, but with a clear path to acceptance if they are addressed.
