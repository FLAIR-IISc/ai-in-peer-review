This paper studies the problem of interactive clustering, where the goal is to perfectly recover a latent partition of `n` items into `k` clusters by making pairwise oracle queries. The central question is how the query complexity changes with the availability of "side information" in the form of a similarity matrix `W`. The paper models `W` as being generated from two distinct, but unknown, distributions: `f+` for pairs within the same cluster and `f-` for pairs in different clusters. This model is a significant generalization of the well-known Stochastic Block Model (SBM), where `f+` and `f-` are Bernoulli.

Without side information, the query complexity for exact recovery is known to be Θ(nk). The main contribution of this work is to provide a tight, information-theoretic characterization of how good side information reduces this complexity. The paper shows that O(k² log n / H²(f+ || f-)) queries are sufficient, where H²(f+ || f-) is the squared Hellinger distance between the two distributions. It complements this with a nearly matching lower bound of Ω(k² / H²(f+ || f-)), demonstrating that the Hellinger distance is precisely the right measure for the quality of the side information. A key strength is that the proposed algorithms are efficient and parameter-free, operating without knowledge of `k`, `f+`, or `f-`.

The paper connects this query complexity problem to the SBM literature, reframing the question from one of passive recovery thresholds to one of how active queries can alter these thresholds.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are strong and well-supported by rigorous proofs.
*   The primary strength is the tightness of the results. Providing matching upper and lower bounds (up to a logarithmic factor) for a problem of this nature is a significant achievement and makes for a complete theoretical contribution.
*   The lower bound proof technique is particularly noteworthy. Standard information-theoretic tools are often difficult to apply in settings with adaptive queries. The paper's approach of identifying a subset of elements that are queried infrequently and then analyzing the multi-hypothesis testing problem for these elements is clever and potentially applicable to other interactive learning problems.
*   The algorithms are not only proven to be near-optimal in terms of query complexity but are also computationally efficient and, importantly, parameter-free. The iterative-update scheme in the Monte Carlo algorithm, which gradually refines its estimate of H(f+||f-) to determine how much to trust the side information, is an elegant solution to the challenge of unknown distributions.

A minor weakness is the experimental section. While it serves as a good proof-of-concept on the CORA dataset, it is limited. A more thorough evaluation comparing the proposed algorithm against the practical heuristics mentioned in the introduction (e.g., from [40, 42, 43]) would have provided a clearer picture of its empirical performance and strengthened the paper's connection to the motivating applications like entity resolution.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.
*   The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the gap between theory (Θ(nk) queries) and practice (heuristics that do much better), and situating the work relative to SBM and other interactive clustering literature.
*   The main results are stated clearly and upfront, immediately giving the reader the core takeaway message. The problem is formulated precisely. The high-level ideas behind the proofs are also explained intuitively, which is very helpful.
*   The one slight drawback, common to many theory papers at NIPS, is the necessary delegation of detailed proofs to the appendix. This makes a full verification of the technical arguments challenging from the main body alone.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   While interactive clustering and SBM are established areas, this paper is the first to rigorously formalize the connection between them in terms of query complexity. Asking how side information quantitatively reduces the number of queries needed for perfect recovery is a novel and insightful question.
*   The central result—that the Hellinger distance H(f+||f-) characterizes this reduction—is a fundamental conceptual contribution. It provides a principled way to measure the "value" of a given similarity matrix for clustering.
*   The algorithmic techniques for the parameter-free setting and the proof techniques for the adaptive lower bound appear novel.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are of high significance to both the theoretical and practical machine learning communities.
*   Theoretically, it provides a foundational understanding of the interplay between active querying and passive side information in a canonical learning problem. The work opens up several interesting avenues for future research, such as extending the analysis to the sparse SBM regime or considering more complex side information models.
*   Practically, it provides a compelling theoretical justification for why many modern entity resolution and data cleaning systems, which combine machine-generated similarity scores with human-in-the-loop feedback, are so effective. The Hellinger distance can now serve as a concrete metric for practitioners to evaluate and engineer better similarity features for such tasks. The development of parameter-free algorithms makes these ideas more readily applicable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, well-motivated problem with a novel and insightful formulation.
*   Provides a tight, near-complete theoretical characterization of the query complexity.
*   The results are significant, offering a principled way to quantify the value of side information and explaining the success of practical systems.
*   The proposed algorithms are efficient and parameter-free, increasing their practical relevance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and could be strengthened with comparisons to existing heuristics.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a foundational theoretical study of a practically relevant problem. Its strengths in technical quality, originality, and significance far outweigh the minor weakness in the experimental section. It makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of interactive learning and is very likely to inspire follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
