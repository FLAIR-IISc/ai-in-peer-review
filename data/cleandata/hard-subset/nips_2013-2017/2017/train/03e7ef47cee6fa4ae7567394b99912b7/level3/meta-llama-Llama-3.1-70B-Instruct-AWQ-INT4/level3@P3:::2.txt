This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical study of this problem, giving strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds. The problem is well-motivated, and the authors do a good job of explaining the context and relevance of the problem.

The main contribution of the paper is to show the dramatic power of side information in reducing the query complexity of clustering. The authors develop a generic framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering using side information and design efficient algorithms for arbitrary distributions that nearly match the lower bound.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of their approach. The writing is clear, and the notation is well-defined. The authors also provide a good discussion of the related work and the significance of their contributions.

However, there are some issues with the paper. The proofs are dense and hard to verify, and some of the sentences are confusing or incomplete. The formal model is unclear, and the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes are not explicitly stated. Theorem 2 as stated is likely incorrect or incomplete, and it appears to implicitly assume k → ∞ but does not state this.

Additionally, the numerical experiments are weak, and they only compare to trivial baselines and do not benchmark against existing methods. The paper also contains numerous grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting errors that require thorough proofreading.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on originality and significance. The problem is new, and the approach is novel. The results are also significant, and they have the potential to impact the field of machine learning.

However, the paper scores poorly on quality and clarity. The proofs are dense and hard to verify, and some of the sentences are confusing or incomplete. The formal model is unclear, and the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes are not explicitly stated.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with major revisions. The authors need to address the issues with the proofs, clarify the formal model, and improve the writing and notation. They also need to provide stronger numerical experiments and benchmark their approach against existing methods.

Here are some specific suggestions for improvement:

* Clarify the formal model and the minimax formulation.
* Provide a clear and explicit statement of Theorem 2 and its assumptions.
* Improve the writing and notation to make the paper more readable.
* Provide stronger numerical experiments and benchmark the approach against existing methods.
* Address the issues with the proofs and make them more verifiable.
* Provide a more detailed discussion of the related work and the significance of the contributions.

By addressing these issues, the authors can improve the quality and clarity of the paper and make it more suitable for publication.
