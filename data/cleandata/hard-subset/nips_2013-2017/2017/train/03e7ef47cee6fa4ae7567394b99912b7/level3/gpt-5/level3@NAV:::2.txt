Summary and relation to prior work: This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering under a noiseless same-cluster oracle when the algorithm additionally observes a random “side-information” matrix: entries are i.i.d. from f+ within clusters and from f− across clusters (unknown to the algorithm). The authors give upper bounds via parameter-free Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms with query complexity roughly O(k log n / H^2(f+||f−)) (and O(n log n) overhead for Las Vegas), and an information-theoretic lower bound Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}), claiming near-tightness. The model subsumes SBM/labeled-SBM as a special case and seeks to quantify the reduction in queries enabled by side information. This review focuses on this noiseless-oracle paper; I also evaluated the companion paper on noisy queries.

Strengths:
- Ambitious and timely problem with clear practical motivation (entity resolution, interactive clustering).
- If correct, the near-matching upper/lower bounds are valuable, especially the adaptive lower-bound framework and the parameter-free algorithms that estimate Hellinger separation on the fly.
- The Hellinger-based analysis and iterative thresholding idea could be useful beyond this setting.

Weaknesses and required revisions:
Quality:
- Proofs are very dense and hard to verify; several arguments appear incomplete. Most notably, Theorem 2 as stated seems incorrect or incomplete: it implicitly assumes k → ∞ (the 1/6 − O(1/√k) bound) and appears to contradict known k = O(1) regimes where side information alone can yield exact recovery with Q = 0. Conditions such as “k large,” cluster size constraints, and the precise minimax setup must be stated in the theorem statements, not only in the proof sketch.
- The formal model is underspecified: clarify whether results are minimax over partitions, allowable cluster-size imbalance (including extreme imbalance), independence assumptions, and whether “parameter-free” guarantees hold uniformly in f+, f−.
- Numerical experiments are weak: they use only trivial baselines on one dataset and do not compare against existing methods (e.g., SBM/labeled-SBM likelihood or spectral methods; active querying for SBM such as Gadde et al., ISIT 2016).

Clarity:
- The writing is poor: many confusing or incomplete sentences, missing text, inconsistent notation, unclear “Monte Carlo/Las Vegas” usage (add “average/expected” qualifiers), suspicious footnote/formatting issues, and language like “clearly exhibiting.” The abstract is too long and delays the main contributions.

Originality:
- Novelty claims are overstated (“first,” “initiates,” “significantly generalizes”) and should be tempered. Several relevant works are omitted, including labeled/weighted SBMs and active learning for community detection; please cite Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) and related NIPS/FOCS/COLT SBM literature.

Significance:
- Potentially high, contingent on corrected statements and clearer proofs; experiments should benchmark against stronger baselines across multiple datasets.

Recommendation: After author response I raise my rating to accept, conditional on a careful, thorough revision addressing: (i) precise theorem statements with explicit conditions (e.g., on k and cluster sizes), (ii) strengthened and verified proofs, (iii) expanded related work with correct positioning, (iv) improved experiments with competitive baselines, and (v) full proofreading for grammar and formatting.
