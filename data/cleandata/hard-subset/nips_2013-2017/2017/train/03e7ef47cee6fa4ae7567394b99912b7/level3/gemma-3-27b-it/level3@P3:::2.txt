This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically focusing on a model where pairwise queries are answered by an oracle and a similarity matrix provides noisy pairwise relationships. The authors aim to provide theoretical upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve optimal clustering, demonstrating the power of side information in reducing complexity compared to the case without it. While the problem is well-motivated and the potential contributions valuable, the paper suffers from significant issues in clarity, presentation, and technical rigor.

The work builds on a line of research exploring interactive clustering and active learning, particularly in the context of entity resolution. It connects to established areas like stochastic block models (SBMs), though this connection isn't fully explored and lacks sufficient citation of relevant SBM literature, including work on active learning *within* SBMs (e.g., Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). The paper's claim to be the “first work” to address this problem, or to “significantly generalize” existing results, is overstated and should be toned down; related work like Ashtiani et al. (NIPS) considers pair-wise queries for clustering. 

The core technical contribution lies in establishing upper and lower bounds on query complexity. The authors claim an upper bound of O(k² log n H(f+||f−)) and a lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k² H(f+||f−)}). However, the proofs are extremely dense and difficult to verify. Theorem 2, in particular, appears problematic; it implicitly assumes k → ∞ but doesn’t explicitly state this, and may contradict known results for k = O(1) cases where Q = 0 suffices. The formal model itself lacks clarity. The minimax formulation is not clearly defined, and restrictions on cluster sizes – particularly the possibility of highly imbalanced clusters – are not addressed explicitly.

The experimental evaluation is exceptionally weak. The paper compares the proposed algorithms only to trivial baselines and doesn’t benchmark against existing clustering or active learning methods. This severely limits the ability to assess the practical impact of the theoretical results. The experimental setup with the Cora dataset is described, but the results presented are not compelling, and the link between the theoretical findings and the observed performance is unclear.

Beyond the technical issues, the paper is plagued by numerous problems with writing quality. Many sentences are confusing or incomplete, suggesting missing text or unclear arguments in the proofs. There are pervasive grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting errors that require thorough proofreading. Minor issues include an overly long abstract that delays the statement of contributions, unclear usage of "Monte Carlo"/"Las Vegas" terminology (missing the qualifier “average”), suspicious footnote notation, ambiguous phrasing implying non-adaptive algorithms, disagreements between wording and algorithm descriptions, and the use of small brackets in the appendix.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and relevant to modern machine learning applications involving active learning and crowdsourcing.
*   The theoretical results, *if correct*, would represent a significant contribution to our understanding of query complexity in clustering.
*   The lower bound provides a potentially useful framework for proving lower bounds in interactive settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The proofs are extremely dense and difficult to verify, with potential issues in Theorem 2.
*   The formal model is unclear and lacks important details regarding cluster size distributions.
*   The writing quality is very poor, hindering understanding and potentially masking technical errors.
*   The experimental evaluation is inadequate and fails to demonstrate the practical relevance of the results.
*   Relevant prior work is omitted from the related work section.
*   Claims of novelty are overstated.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting theoretical study but is currently not of sufficient quality for acceptance to NIPS. The potential contributions are significant, but they are overshadowed by the lack of clarity, technical rigor, and polish.

**Recommendation:**

I initially rated this paper as a weak reject. However, after author feedback, I raise my rating to **accept with major revisions**. The authors must address the following critical issues:

1.  **Clarity and Writing:** Thoroughly revise the paper for clarity and grammatical correctness. Every sentence should be clear and unambiguous.
2.  **Technical Rigor:** Provide more detailed and accessible proofs. Specifically, carefully examine and clarify Theorem 2, explicitly stating any assumptions (e.g., k → ∞) and addressing potential contradictions with known results. Define the minimax formulation more formally.
3.  **Related Work:**  Incorporate citations to relevant prior work, including Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) and other key papers in the SBM and active learning literature.
4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** Conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, benchmarking against existing clustering and active learning algorithms.
5.  **Model Clarification:** Explicitly address the impact of imbalanced cluster sizes and clearly define any restrictions on the problem formulation.



Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable contribution to the field.
