This review assesses the paper "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information." I have also reviewed a companion paper by the same authors on clustering with noisy queries. This review focuses specifically on the present paper, which studies clustering with a noiseless oracle and the aid of random side information.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper investigates the query complexity of interactive clustering. The setting involves a set of `n` elements belonging to `k` unknown clusters, and an oracle that can answer pairwise queries of the form "do elements `u` and `v` belong to the same cluster?". The central contribution is to analyze how access to "side information"—a noisy similarity matrix `W`—affects the number of queries needed to recover the true clustering. In the proposed model, the similarity value `w_uv` is drawn from a distribution `f+` if `u` and `v` are in the same cluster, and from `f-` otherwise.

The authors establish both lower and upper bounds on the query complexity. They show that without side information, Θ(nk) queries are necessary and sufficient. With side information, they claim a dramatic reduction to O(k² log(n) / H²(f+||f-)), where H² is the squared Hellinger distance between the distributions. They present efficient Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms that are parameter-free (i.e., do not require knowledge of `k`, `f+`, or `f-`) and nearly match their information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(k² / H²(f+||f-)). The work connects this problem to the stochastic block model (SBM), as the SBM is a special case where `f+` and `f-` are Bernoulli distributions.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and significant problem. The gap between the theoretical Θ(nk) query complexity of standard interactive clustering and the much better empirical performance of practical heuristics that use similarity scores is a real and important one to understand. This paper provides a formal model to explain this discrepancy.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** If correct, the theoretical results are valuable. Providing tight, information-theoretic upper and lower bounds that precisely characterize the dependence on the quality of the side information (via Hellinger distance) is a significant contribution to the fields of active learning and clustering.
3.  **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require prior knowledge of the number of clusters or the underlying distributions is a major practical advantage and a non-trivial technical achievement.
4.  **Novel Connection:** The paper draws an explicit and interesting connection between interactive clustering and the extensive literature on the stochastic block model, potentially opening up new research directions.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's greatest weakness is its poor quality of writing. The prose is often confusing, with numerous grammatical errors, incomplete sentences, and awkward phrasing. The proofs in the main body and appendix are dense and extremely difficult to follow, which makes it challenging to verify the technical correctness of the claims. This is not a minor issue; it fundamentally obscures the paper's contributions and correctness.
2.  **Unclear Formal Model and Theorem Statements:** The formal problem statement lacks precision. It is unclear if the bounds are for the worst-case (minimax) over all possible clusterings or an average case. Assumptions about cluster sizes (e.g., whether extremely imbalanced clusters are allowed) are not explicitly stated. Furthermore, the statement of Theorem 2 appears to be incorrect or at least incomplete. As written, it gives a lower bound that does not seem to hold for constant `k` where, for sufficiently separated distributions `f+` and `f-`, perfect recovery is possible with zero queries (a known result from the SBM literature). The theorem seems to implicitly assume `k` is large (e.g., `k → ∞`), but this crucial condition is not stated, making the claim misleading.
3.  **Inadequate Referencing and Overstated Novelty:** The authors miss several relevant pieces of prior work. For instance, the literature on the labeled or weighted stochastic block model is highly relevant but not discussed. A key missing citation is "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" by Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016), which directly addresses oracle queries in an SBM context. Consequently, claims of novelty like this being the "first rigorous theoretical study" are overstated and should be moderated.
4.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are not convincing. The proposed algorithm is only compared against trivial "best" and "worst" case querying strategies, not against existing practical heuristics for interactive entity resolution or active learning methods for community detection. Without benchmarking against established methods, it is impossible to gauge the practical significance of the proposed algorithms.

**Detailed Comments for Improvement**

*   **Writing and Clarity:** The paper requires a thorough and careful revision by a native English speaker. Many sentences are confusing (e.g., in the proof of Lemma 1). The abstract is too long and delays the statement of contributions. The distinction between "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" algorithms should be made clearer, and the bounds should explicitly state if they are for the expected number of queries.
*   **Theorem Precision:** The statement of Theorem 2 must be corrected to include all necessary assumptions. If the lower bound requires `k` to be sufficiently large, this must be part of the theorem statement.
*   **Related Work:** Please incorporate and discuss the missing literature, including the Gadde et al. paper and works on labeled SBMs. Claims of novelty should be carefully rephrased to be accurate.
*   **Experiments:** To demonstrate significance, the experimental section should be expanded to include comparisons with one or two relevant, existing algorithms from the entity resolution or active learning literature.
*   **Minor Issues:**
    *   There are numerous formatting and punctuation errors (e.g., footnote notation, inconsistent capitalization).
    *   Phrases like "clearly exhibiting" should be used with caution, especially when the argument is non-trivial.
    *   The use of tiny brackets in equations in the appendix makes them very hard to read.

**Conclusion**

This paper tackles an important problem and presents potentially significant theoretical results. However, the manuscript in its current form is borderline due to severe issues with clarity, imprecise theorem statements, missing citations, and a weak experimental section. While the core ideas seem promising, the poor presentation makes it difficult to confidently assess the technical correctness and the true extent of the contribution.
