## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with the aid of a similarity matrix (side information) provided to an interactive oracle. The authors present upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to achieve optimal clustering, demonstrating a significant reduction in complexity when side information is available compared to the case without it. They frame the problem in terms of pairwise queries and consider a general, noisy model for the similarity matrix where values are drawn from distributions f+ and f− depending on cluster membership.

The work builds upon the established field of interactive clustering, drawing connections to existing heuristics used in entity resolution and crowdsourcing. It further relates to the stochastic block model (SBM), positioning itself as a generalization that allows for arbitrary cluster sizes and distributions, and a lack of prior knowledge about the parameters. The core contribution lies in characterizing the impact of side information on query complexity and providing information-theoretic bounds.

**Strengths:** The problem addressed is well-motivated and practically relevant, particularly in contexts like entity resolution where obtaining fully labeled data is expensive. The derivation of upper and lower bounds, *if correct*, would represent a significant advancement in our theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. The attempt to frame the problem in a general and flexible manner (arbitrary distributions, unknown k) is commendable.  

**Weaknesses:** Despite the potential impact, the paper suffers from several critical issues. The proofs are exceptionally dense and difficult to follow, making verification challenging. The claim of novelty, using phrases like "first work" and "significantly generalizes," is overstated and requires substantial revision.  Crucially, several relevant prior works are omitted, including papers on labeled/weighted SBMs and, notably, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) on active learning for community detection, which directly relates to querying models. 

The formal model is unclear; the minimax formulation and restrictions on cluster sizes (potentially favoring balanced clusters) are not explicitly stated. Theorem 2, as presented, appears questionable and may require substantial clarification regarding the asymptotic behavior of *k*. The experimental evaluation is particularly weak, only comparing against trivial baselines and lacking any benchmark against existing clustering or active learning methods.  The writing quality is significantly hampered by numerous grammatical errors, incomplete sentences, and inconsistencies.

**Arguments For Acceptance:** The potential theoretical contribution is substantial, and the problem is important. The authors address a fundamental question about the role of side information in clustering.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:** The unclear writing, unverified proofs, overstated claims of novelty, missing related work, and weak experimental results severely undermine the paper's credibility. 

**Recommendation:** While the underlying ideas have merit, I initially leaned towards rejection due to the severity of the issues. However, given the author’s post-feedback willingness to address these concerns comprehensively, I revise my recommendation to **Accept with Major Revisions**. The authors *must* significantly improve the clarity of writing, rigorously verify and clarify the proofs (particularly Theorem 2, explicitly stating any asymptotic assumptions), expand the related work section, strengthen the experimental evaluation, and carefully re-evaluate and moderate all claims of novelty. A thorough proofreading pass is essential.
