

Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the query complexity of clustering with side information (similarity matrix), showing that side information reduces the complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)). This work is the first rigorous study of this problem, generalizing the stochastic block model (SBM) to arbitrary distributions f+ and f- without requiring knowledge of k, f+, or f-. The paper's main strengths include the tight theoretical bounds (nearly matching upper and lower bounds), the parameter-free algorithms, and the connection to community detection literature.

However, the paper has several serious weaknesses that require revision before acceptance. The writing quality is poor with numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent capitalization, and unclear phrasing that makes parts of the paper difficult to follow. For example, Section 1 contains incomplete sentences like "This is analogous to our interactive clustering framework. But intriguingly, as shown by extensive experiments on various real datasets, these heuristics use far less queries than nk [42, 43, 40]–barring the Ω(nk) theoretical lower bound." The phrase "barring the Ω(nk) theoretical lower bound" is confusing and poorly constructed.

The paper significantly overstates its novelty. The authors claim "first work" and "initiates study" but omit several relevant prior works. Most notably, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) studied active learning for community detection in SBM, which directly relates to the querying model considered here. The omission of this work is significant as it shows the problem has been studied in related contexts. The authors should revise their claims to accurately position their work relative to existing literature.

Theorem 2, which states the lower bound, appears to have a critical flaw. The theorem states that "any (possibly randomized) algorithm with the knowledge of f+, f-, and the number of clusters k, that does not perform Ω(min{nk, k²/H²(f+||f-)}) expected number of queries, will be unable to return the correct clustering with probability at least 1/6 - O(1/√k)." However, this bound does not hold for small k (e.g., k=2), where it's known that exact recovery can be achieved with zero queries when f+ and f- are sufficiently different. The authors should explicitly state the condition that k must be sufficiently large for this bound to hold.

The formal model is also unclear. The paper does not specify whether it considers the minimax setting (worst-case clustering) or average-case, and it fails to address how it handles extremely imbalanced cluster sizes, which is common in real data. The authors should clarify these points.

The experimental section is weak. The authors compare only to trivial baselines (best/worst case strategies) rather than benchmarking against existing methods. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art clustering methods that use side information would strengthen the paper significantly.

The paper contains numerous minor issues that need attention: inconsistent use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology, missing "average" qualifier in the query complexity statement, suspicious footnote notation, and ambiguous phrasing implying non-adaptive algorithms. The appendix contains small brackets that need fixing.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's theoretical contribution is valuable. The lower bound technique could be of independent interest for other interactive learning problems. With careful revision addressing the writing quality, precise theorem statements (including conditions like k → ∞), and the omitted references, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

Pro: Rigorous theoretical bounds on query complexity, parameter-free algorithms, significant connection to SBM
Con: Poor writing quality, overstated novelty, potential flaw in Theorem 2, weak experiments, numerous grammatical errors

I recommend acceptance pending major revisions to address the above concerns, particularly the issues with Theorem 2 and the omitted prior work. The paper has the potential to be an important contribution to the field once these issues are resolved.
