Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when an oracle answers noiseless same-cluster pairwise queries and the algorithm also has access to random “side-information” in the form of a similarity matrix: for each pair (u, v), Wuv is drawn from f+ if u and v are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The main results are information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds (up to log n) on the number of oracle queries needed to recover the true clustering, expressed in terms of the squared Hellinger divergence H2(f+‖f−). The authors provide two parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas), and they argue connections to stochastic block models (SBMs).

This review focuses on the noiseless oracle + random side-information setting (the companion paper handles noisy oracles). The model generalizes SMS/SBM by allowing arbitrary unknown f+, f− and unknown k, and by not assuming balanced clusters. Related NeurIPS/FOCS/COLT work on SBM thresholds and parameter-free recovery is discussed at a high level, but several relevant lines of work on labeled/weighted SBMs and active querying for community detection are missing. In particular, “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) studies active queries in SBM; there is also a substantial literature on labeled/weighted SBMs that is directly relevant to side-information models.

Quality
Strengths
- If correct, the bounds are valuable: the lower bound framework for interactive settings and the upper bounds that are parameter-free and expressed via Hellinger divergence are both interesting.
- The iterative-update idea for learning effective thresholds from side-information without known parameters is novel and, if sound, broadly useful.
- The connection to SBM and a discussion of how queries may shift recovery thresholds are appealing and could spur follow-on work.

Weaknesses
- Proofs are dense and hard to verify; several statements appear incomplete or potentially incorrect as written.
- Theorem 2 likely requires additional conditions (e.g., k → ∞ or k sufficiently large). As stated, it appears to imply a positive Ω(1) query lower bound even when k = O(1) and side-information alone suffices for exact recovery (e.g., constant-k SBM above the exact-recovery threshold), contradicting known results with Q = 0. Please explicitly state the scaling regime(s) for k, any balance assumptions, and the probability-of-error target in the theorem.
- The formal model is underspecified. Is the analysis minimax over partitions? Are there constraints on cluster sizes (e.g., lower bounds like Ω(log n/H2) for correctness)? How are extremely imbalanced clusters handled in both the lower and upper bounds?
- The numerical experiments are weak: they compare only to trivial strategies on a single dataset and do not benchmark against existing baselines from the ER/interactive clustering or SBM literature.
- Writing quality issues (organization, grammar, incomplete sentences) impede verification and comprehension.

Clarity
- Many arguments (e.g., the extension of Sanov’s theorem to Hellinger-based concentration, the product-measure Hellinger bounds, and the interactive lower-bound construction) need cleaner exposition and complete conditions.
- Terminology around Monte Carlo vs Las Vegas should precisely distinguish success probability and average vs worst-case query complexity; please include the “average” qualifier where appropriate.
- The paper oscillates between adversarial and random generative assumptions. Please give a clear, self-contained model section with: (i) whether guarantees are high-probability over W, minimax over clusterings; (ii) any assumptions on k and cluster sizes; (iii) the exact success criterion; and (iv) adaptivity of algorithms.

Originality
- The interactive lower-bound framework is promising, but claims like “first work,” “initiates the study,” and “significantly generalizes” are overstated given prior work on labeled/weighted SBMs and active querying for community detection. Please reword claims to be precise about what is new: parameter-free algorithms for arbitrary unknown f+, f− with Hellinger-based rates and matching lower bounds up to log n in this particular oracle+side-information model.

Significance
- If the theorems are corrected/clarified and the writing improved, the results could be influential, both conceptually (information measures governing query complexity) and practically (parameter-free querying schemes). The current empirical evaluation does not yet demonstrate practical impact beyond a toy comparison.

Specific comments and requested changes
- Theorem 2: State the exact regime (e.g., H2(f+‖f−) ≤ 1/18; k growing; cluster sizes; probability-of-error target). Clarify why the lower bound does not contradict known Q = 0 results for constant k SBMs above exact-recovery thresholds. If the bound requires k → ∞, say so in the theorem. Also reconcile the constants and the dependence on H2.
- Model/assumptions: Clarify minimax vs average-case, constraints on minimal cluster size, and whether extremely imbalanced partitions are allowed. The algorithms seem to require clusters of size Ω(log n/H2) to estimate in-cluster distributions reliably.
- Proofs: Provide full details for (a) the Hellinger distance inequality over product measures used in Lemma 1; (b) the adaptation from Sanov (KL) to Hellinger-based concentration; and (c) the “anti-concentration” (Lemma 6) underpinning the small size of Waiting(C). Several steps currently read as sketches.
- Experiments: Compare against established baselines, e.g., interactive clustering heuristics (Awasthi et al. 2014; Ashtiani et al. 2016), SBM-based/parameter-free community detection (including weighted/labeled SBM methods), and active community detection (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Report accuracy vs query budget on multiple datasets and parameter regimes; include ablations showing robustness to imbalance and to misspecified f+, f−.
- Related work: Add and discuss (at minimum) Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) and prior work on labeled/weighted SBMs; also correct several omissions in the current references.
- Tone down novelty claims as noted above.

Writing and presentation issues
- Numerous grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting errors throughout; thorough proofreading is needed.
- Several sentences are confusing or incomplete (e.g., missing qualifiers, truncated phrases in the introduction and proofs). Please revise for clarity.
- Minor issues: abstract is too long and only later states contributions; clarify “Monte Carlo” vs “Las Vegas” and include “average” where applicable; fix footnote notation; avoid phrasing that seems to imply non-adaptive algorithms when adaptivity is used; rephrase “clearly exhibiting”; fix tiny brackets/notation in the appendix.

Pros for acceptance
- Potentially strong and general theoretical contributions (upper/lower bounds) with an appealing parameter-free algorithmic approach.
- Interesting conceptual link to SBM thresholds under querying.

Cons for acceptance
- Proofs difficult to verify; at least one central theorem (Theorem 2) appears incomplete as stated.
- Model and assumptions insufficiently specified.
- Empirical evaluation does not validate practical gains over existing methods.
- Writing quality currently below the bar.

Post-rebuttal note and recommendation
After author feedback, I raise my rating to accept, contingent on a careful revision that: (i) cleans up the writing; (ii) states precise, self-contained theorems with all needed conditions (e.g., large-k regimes explicitly in the theorem statements where required); (iii) clarifies the formal model and assumptions; (iv) tempers novelty claims; (v) adds the missing related work; and (vi) strengthens the experimental section or clearly positions it as illustrative.
