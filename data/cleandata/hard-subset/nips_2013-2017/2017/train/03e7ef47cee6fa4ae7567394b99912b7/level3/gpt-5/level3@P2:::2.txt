Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with access to noiseless pairwise same-cluster queries and a noisy similarity matrix (“side information”). The side information is generated by drawing each pairwise weight from f+ if the endpoints share a cluster and from f− otherwise; the algorithm is not given k, f+, or f−. The main results are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds: any (possibly randomized, adaptive) algorithm requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) expected queries (where H^2 = H^2(f+‖f−) is squared Hellinger divergence), and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1.
- Nearly matching upper bounds: parameter-free algorithms achieve O(min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}) queries via a Las Vegas variant and O(min{nk, k^2 log n / H^2}) (or O(k^2 log n / H^2) when smaller) via a Monte Carlo algorithm that succeeds with probability 1 − o(1/n). The main technique learns empirical inter/intra distributions and uses an iterative-update rule to decide when side information is reliable enough to avoid queries.
The work is framed as a generalization of SBM/community detection (Bernoulli f±), and the lower-bound technique tackles the adaptivity of queries. Connections to active learning and crowdsourced entity resolution are discussed.

Relevant prior work at NIPS and elsewhere includes interactive clustering and ER heuristics [Awasthi et al., Balcan & Blum; Vesdapunt et al.; Wang et al.], query-based clustering [Ashtiani et al.], and community detection in SBM and general SBM [Abbe et al., Abbe & Sandon, Hajek–Wu–Xu, Mossel–Neeman–Sly]. Closely related to the querying aspect is “Active Learning for Community Detection in SBM” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016), which should be cited and contrasted. There is also literature on labeled/weighted/general SBMs (e.g., general qij models) that is highly relevant to the side-information model and should be incorporated.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical question: how side information can dramatically reduce query complexity.
- Clear dependence on an information measure (Hellinger divergence), with lower and upper bounds nearly matching up to log n.
- Parameter-free algorithms that do not assume knowledge of k or f±, with an iterative-update mechanism that is potentially broadly useful.
- Lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive settings is interesting and may be of independent value.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Several claims and proofs are hard to verify from the current presentation. The lower bound (Theorem 2) appears to miss key conditions (e.g., explicit dependence on k) and, as stated, risks contradicting regimes where Q = 0 suffices (e.g., exact recovery from side information alone). The bound uses 1/6 − O(1/√k), which implicitly assumes large k; this needs to be made explicit and the constant/parameter ranges carefully stated.
- The formal model is not crisply specified: Is the analysis minimax over all partitions, including highly imbalanced clusters? What assumptions (if any) on minimum cluster size enter the algorithms’ correctness? For continuous f±, what support/regularity is needed?
- The “Las Vegas” terminology is used informally; a Las Vegas algorithm always returns a correct answer but its expected runtime/query count should be stated precisely. The current statement “recovers with probability 1” yet gives query bounds that hold with probability 1 − o(1/n) is confusing.
- Experiments are weak: one dataset, comparisons only to trivial/best/worst strategies, and no benchmarking against prior methods (e.g., Ashtiani et al.; practical ER algorithms guided by side info). There is no evaluation of robustness (e.g., imbalanced clusters, varying H^2), nor ablations illustrating the iterative-update mechanism.

Clarity
- The writing quality is poor enough to hinder verification: many confusing or incomplete sentences, abrupt transitions, and notational inconsistencies. The abstract is too long and delays the core contributions; several grammar, capitalization, and formatting errors persist throughout; footnote markers and appendix bracket sizes are inconsistent. The algorithmic phases and thresholds (Phase 3A/3B) are difficult to parse; the “Monte Carlo/Las Vegas” terminology should be clarified carefully.
- The paper sometimes reads as if the algorithm is non-adaptive (“we select one arbitrary point from every cluster”)—please ensure the adaptive nature and the selection rule are clear.

Originality
- The general side-information model, parameter-free adaptive algorithm with Hellinger-based thresholds, and the adaptive lower-bound framework are novel and valuable. However, claims such as “first rigorous study” and “significantly generalizes SBM” are overstated and should be tempered, with proper integration of labeled/weighted/general SBM and active-query community detection literature (e.g., Gadde et al., ISIT 2016; Abbe & Sandon; Chen–Kamath–Suh–Tse for locality; degree-corrected/inhomogeneous variants where appropriate).

Significance
- If the results hold as stated and are clarified, the paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution, explaining the gap between nk worst-case and much smaller query counts observed in practice with side information, and providing tools that others can build upon. The weak empirical section currently limits practical impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clear message: side information can reduce query complexity to O(k^2 log n / H^2), with near-tight lower bounds.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms and an iterative-update design that may be of broad interest.
- Lower-bound ideas for adaptive interactive settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Theorem statements and conditions require clarification/correction (especially Theorem 2 and the “Las Vegas” result).
- Writing and organization need substantial revision; proofs are difficult to follow.
- Experimental evaluation is inadequate and lacks comparisons to prior querying methods.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Precisely state the regimes of Theorem 2 (ranges of k, n, H^2) under which the constants yield a nontrivial lower bound; reconcile with regimes where Q = 0 suffices from side information alone.
- Specify the minimax setting: are cluster sizes adversarial? Are there any lower bounds that depend on minimum cluster size? How do the algorithms behave with extreme imbalance?
- For continuous f± or discrete supports growing with n, which parts of the analysis change?
- Define the expected query complexity of the Las Vegas algorithm and the event on which the stated bound holds.
- Provide pseudocode and a clear description of the Membership test and the thresholds, with all constants and their dependencies.
- Compare empirically against existing baselines (e.g., Ashtiani et al., ER heuristics guided by similarities) on multiple datasets; report precision/recall vs queries and runtime; study sensitivity to H^2 and to distributional misspecification.

Minor comments
- Rephrase novelty claims more cautiously; add missing citations (e.g., Gadde et al., ISIT 2016; works on labeled/general SBM; other interactive clustering).
- Fix grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and formatting throughout; tighten the abstract; ensure footnotes and appendices are legible; avoid ambiguous phrases like “clearly exhibiting.”

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. After the rebuttal I am inclined to accept, contingent on a careful revision that (i) states precise conditions in theorems (including explicit dependence on k and H^2), (ii) clarifies the model and algorithm descriptions, (iii) substantially improves writing, and (iv) strengthens the empirical comparison to prior work. The core ideas are promising and potentially impactful if presented rigorously and clearly.
