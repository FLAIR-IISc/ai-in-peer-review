Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries, in the presence of side information in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W. The side information is generated from an unknown pair of distributions (f+, f−) over similarities: similarities between same-cluster pairs are i.i.d. from f+, and different-cluster pairs are i.i.d. from f−. The authors show that side information can dramatically reduce the number of oracle queries needed for exact recovery, from Θ(nk) without side information to O(k log n / H2(f+‖f−)), where H2 denotes the squared Hellinger divergence, with nearly matching information-theoretic lower bounds. They provide two efficient, parameter-free algorithms (a Monte Carlo algorithm with success probability 1 − o(1/n), and a Las Vegas algorithm with success probability 1), both not assuming knowledge of k, f+, or f−. The lower bounds hold even if the algorithm knows k and the distributions.

This model generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM): SBM corresponds to Bernoulli f+, f−; here f+, f− are arbitrary (unknown) distributions, cluster sizes and k can be adversarial, and W entries are independent conditional on the ground truth. The paper connects to a large literature on community detection thresholds (e.g., Abbe et al.; Mossel–Neeman–Sly; Hajek–Wu–Xu) and to interactive clustering with pairwise queries (e.g., Ashtiani–Kushagra–Ben-David; Awasthi–Balcan–Voevodski), as well as crowdsourced ER heuristics (e.g., Wang et al.; Vesdapunt et al.; Verroios–Garcia-Molina). It also relates to decision tree complexity and active learning (e.g., Gadde et al. for active SBM). The work explains why practical systems that leverage similarity scores can use far fewer same-cluster queries than nk, and identifies H2(f+‖f−) as the right information measure to assess the usefulness of side information.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual advance: isolates and quantifies the value of side information via Hellinger divergence, yielding tight (up to log n) query bounds.
- Novel lower-bound framework for interactive, adaptive querying that goes beyond standard Fano-type tools; applies to randomized algorithms and known-parameter settings.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms that do not assume k, f+, or f−, with a principled iterative-update procedure that learns effective thresholds from data.
- Connection and generalization to SBM, along with a discussion of how queries should shift detection thresholds.
- Theoretical guarantees match lower bounds within O(log n), and the algorithms run in time O(n^2), dominated by reading W.
- Practical relevance to entity resolution and crowdsourced clustering; small empirical study on CORA illustrates feasibility and robustness to approximate f+, f−.

Weaknesses:
- Modeling assumptions: W entries are i.i.d. given the clustering; in many real datasets, similarity scores exhibit dependencies and graph transitivity, which may affect query complexity and the validity of concentration arguments.
- Discrete support assumption for f+, f− in the algorithmic development is restrictive; while lower bounds allow continuous distributions, algorithmic guarantees for the continuous case are only sketched via Sanov-type arguments.
- Some notational and presentation inconsistencies (e.g., occasional ambiguity around whether the upper bound is O(k log n / H2) or involves k^2 factors; clarify consistently across abstract, theorem statements, and discussion).
- Empirical evaluation is limited: one dataset, no comparison against strong baselines (e.g., practical ER querying heuristics), and no controlled synthetic experiments varying k, H2, and cluster size distributions to validate the predicted scalings.
- Independence from k is a plus in theory, but real systems often have prior estimates of k; it would be useful to quantify how such knowledge could further reduce queries or improve constants.
- Runtime O(n^2) can be heavy for large n; practical memory/time considerations and data structures are not discussed.

Quality:
The theoretical results are technically sound to the extent presented. The lower bound argument, via carefully identifying lightly queried elements and bounding success using total variation/Hellinger, is well motivated for interactive settings. The parameter-free algorithm and its convergence analysis via an extension of Sanov’s theorem are nontrivial and insightful. However, some proofs are deferred, and the small-scale experiment does not fully validate the theoretical scalings. Clarifying the exact dependence on k in both upper and lower bounds is important.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear problem formulation and motivation. The iterative-update idea is explained at a good level, but the notation and asymptotic statements sometimes mix H and H2 and occasionally suggest conflicting k or k^2 dependences. Tightening the exposition, unifying notation, and adding a concise “at a glance” table of bounds would help. More discussion on the role of imbalanced cluster sizes would also improve readability.

Originality:
High. The core idea—bringing side-information divergence into the query-complexity picture and providing interactive lower bounds that handle adaptivity—is new. The parameter-free iterative scheme that learns effective thresholds from data is also original in this context.

Significance:
High. The results bridge a gap between practice and theory for interactive clustering/ER, give a clean metric (Hellinger divergence) to assess similarity-matrix quality, and generalize SBM insights. The lower-bound framework could be useful beyond clustering (e.g., interactive testing/estimation).

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Please reconcile the factors of k versus k^2 in the bounds; at several points the text seems inconsistent. Precisely state the tightest known dependencies for MC and LV algorithms and the lower bounds.
- The algorithmic analysis assumes discrete support; can the authors state a formal version for continuous distributions with bounded densities (e.g., via discretization) and the resulting dependence on binning?
- How sensitive is the method to strong cluster-size imbalance or very small clusters? Are there worst-case constructions where the parameter-free estimates require many more queries before convergence?
- Can the independence assumption on W be relaxed (e.g., exchangeable random arrays, graphons) while retaining the main scaling with H2?

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong and novel theoretical contributions with near-tight bounds.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with clear practical motivation.
- Unifies and extends SBM and interactive clustering lines of work.
- Identifies a simple, interpretable quantity (H2) governing query savings.

Cons for acceptance:
- Some ambiguity in the exact k dependence and minor presentation issues.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to baselines.
- Assumptions on W may be restrictive in practice.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and standardize all asymptotic statements; include a summary table of bounds (MC/LV vs lower bounds).
- Add synthetic experiments varying k, H2, and cluster balance to empirically verify the predicted scalings; compare to heuristic baselines used in ER.
- Discuss practical implementation details (memory/time for O(n^2)), and whether sampling entries of W suffices.
- Outline extensions to continuous f+, f− and to noisy oracles, even if deferred to the companion work.

Overall assessment: A strong theoretical paper with clear novelty and potential impact. With clarified statements and strengthened experiments, it would make a valuable contribution.

Confidence: High in the assessment of the theory and positioning; medium on empirical significance due to limited experiments.
