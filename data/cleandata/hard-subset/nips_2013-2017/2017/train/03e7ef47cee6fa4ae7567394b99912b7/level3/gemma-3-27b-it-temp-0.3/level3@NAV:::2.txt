## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with access to an oracle providing pairwise similarity information. The core contribution lies in establishing upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed to recover clusters, given a similarity matrix representing noisy pairwise relationships. The authors demonstrate that incorporating side information—the similarity matrix—can dramatically reduce query complexity compared to clustering without it, achieving a reduction from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)).

The work builds upon the interactive clustering framework, motivated by applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data is costly. It connects to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM), framing the side information as a generalization of the SBM’s adjacency matrix. The authors position their results as a theoretical justification for the effectiveness of heuristics used in practice, which often leverage similarity matrices to reduce querying costs. 

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues. The writing is often dense and convoluted, making it difficult to follow the arguments and understand the precise assumptions of the model. The formal model itself lacks clarity; the minimax formulation and any restrictions on cluster sizes (e.g., handling extremely imbalanced clusters) are not explicitly stated. Theorem 2, in particular, appears problematic as stated, potentially requiring the assumption of k → ∞ for validity, which isn’t explicitly mentioned and could contradict known results for small, fixed k.

Furthermore, the paper omits relevant prior work. Notably, it fails to cite papers on labeled or weighted stochastic block models, which are directly related to the problem of clustering with side information. The citation of "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) is also missing, given its relevance to querying models in a similar context. The authors' claims of novelty ("first work," "initiates study," "significantly generalizes") are overstated and should be toned down.

The numerical experiments are weak, comparing only to trivial baselines and lacking a benchmark against existing clustering algorithms. The algorithms are also not clearly explained. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results, *if correct*, represent a valuable contribution to understanding the fundamental limits of interactive clustering.
*   The connection to the SBM provides a theoretical grounding for practical heuristics.
*   The lower bound provides a general framework for proving lower bounds for interactive classification.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor writing quality and lack of clarity severely hinder understanding.
*   Potential inaccuracies in Theorem 2.
*   Omission of relevant prior work.
*   Weak experimental evaluation.
*   Overstated novelty claims.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the potential significance of the results, the paper’s current state necessitates a **major revision** before it can be considered acceptable. The authors must prioritize clarity, address the concerns regarding Theorem 2, incorporate missing related work, strengthen the experimental evaluation, and revise the novelty claims. A thorough proofreading to correct numerous grammatical and formatting errors is also essential. I have raised my rating to **accept with major revisions** based on the potential of the work, but this is contingent on a substantial and careful revision addressing the points outlined above.
