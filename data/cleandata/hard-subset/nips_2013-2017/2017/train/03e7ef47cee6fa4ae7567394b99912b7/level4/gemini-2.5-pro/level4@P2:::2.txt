This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering. The goal is to recover a latent clustering of `n` elements into `k` clusters by making pairwise oracle queries of the form "do elements `u` and `v` belong to the same cluster?". The primary contribution is to analyze how this query complexity changes with the addition of "side information" in the form of a noisy similarity matrix `W`, where the similarity value for a pair is drawn from a distribution `f+` if they are in the same cluster, and `f-` otherwise. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and provide nearly matching, parameter-free upper bounds (algorithms). Their key result shows that side information can dramatically reduce the query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence. This work relates to and generalizes the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Fundamental Problem:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and fundamental problem at the intersection of active learning and clustering. Quantifying the value of noisy side information is an important theoretical question with practical implications for areas like entity resolution.
2.  **Significant Theoretical Result:** The primary result—characterizing the drastic reduction in query complexity as a function of the statistical distance between the side information distributions—is a strong and insightful contribution.
3.  **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms are parameter-free (i.e., they do not require knowledge of `k`, `f+`, or `f-`), which is a significant advantage for practical application.
4.  **Novel Lower Bound Technique:** The development of an information-theoretic lower bound for an interactive, adaptive setting is technically challenging and a valuable contribution in its own right.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's primary weakness is its presentation. The manuscript would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading, as numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing currently impede readability. For example, commas are frequently misplaced (e.g., after "we have," or "note that,"), and sentence structure is often unconventional. While the technical contribution is paramount, the current state of the writing detracts from the paper's quality and makes the dense technical arguments more difficult to follow.

2.  **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper's claims of novelty may be overstated, as some highly relevant work is not cited or discussed.
    *   The idea of generalizing the SBM from Bernoulli distributions to more general ones is not new. This has been explored under names like the "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model."
    *   There is existing work on active learning in the SBM context, such as "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). While their query model is different (node labels vs. pairwise comparisons), for constant `k` and large clusters, the models are functionally similar. A discussion of how this paper's results relate to or differ from such work is necessary.
    *   In light of this, claims like "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study" or "this is the first work that..." should be revised to more accurately reflect the state of the literature.

3.  **Potential Issue with the Main Lower Bound:** There is a potential concern with the statement of Theorem 2. For the SBM with constant `k` (e.g., k=2), it is known that exact recovery is possible with zero queries even in certain regimes where the Hellinger distance is small (e.g., scaling with log(n)/n). This appears to contradict the theorem's Ω(k²/H²) lower bound. The proof seems to rely on an assumption that `k` is large (`k -> infinity`), but this condition is not stated in the theorem. This is a critical point that requires careful clarification. If the result only holds for sufficiently large `k`, this must be stated explicitly in the theorem.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section provides limited value. It does not compare the proposed algorithms against any existing methods or heuristics from the extensive literature on entity resolution or interactive clustering. To demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed methods, a comparison against relevant baselines is needed.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The problem setup could be more explicit. It is not immediately clear if the analysis is for a minimax setting over all possible clusterings. For instance, does the analysis hold for highly imbalanced cases, such as `k-1` singleton clusters and one large cluster of size `n-k+1`?
*   **Terminology:** Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" standard in this context? When the Las Vegas algorithm is first introduced, it should be clarified that it has a guaranteed correct output but a variable (average-case) query complexity.
*   **Clarity of Sentences:** Some sentences are confusing, such as "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound." The latter seems to misinterpret when the Ω(nk) bound applies (i.e., in the absence of side information).
*   In the formal problem statement, "find Q ⊆ V × V" could be misinterpreted as a non-adaptive algorithm. It should be clarified that `Q` can be constructed adaptively.
*   The abstract seems overly long, and the paper takes nearly three pages to state its main contributions, which slows down the reader's ability to grasp the core results.

**Recommendation:**

This paper tackles an important problem and appears to contain significant theoretical contributions. However, it is held back by major issues in presentation, positioning relative to prior work, and a potential flaw in the statement of its main lower bound. While the technical core seems valuable, the number and severity of the required revisions are substantial. My recommendation is borderline, but I lean towards acceptance on the condition that the authors thoroughly address the concerns raised above, particularly the correctness of Theorem 2 and the engagement with related literature. A careful and extensive revision of the manuscript for clarity and grammar is also essential.
