

This paper establishes upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented by random side information associated with each pair. While the authors address a relevant problem, my assessment is tempered by several significant concerns regarding novelty, technical precision, and exposition. I am reviewing this paper alongside another by the same authors on noisy queries without side information, but focus here on this specific submission.

The contributions appear valuable if correct, but the writing requires substantial revision. My inability to thoroughly verify the lengthy upper bound proof due to the paper's density prevents me from fully endorsing the technical claims. More critically, the paper overlooks key prior work, necessitating major revisions to its claims of novelty. Specifically:

1.  **Novelty Claims Require Revision:** The paper repeatedly asserts that it "initiates a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes," or is "the first work" in this area. However, the generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) to arbitrary edge distributions (beyond Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q)) is not new. A search for "labeled stochastic block model" (or "labelled," "weighted stochastic block model") reveals multiple prior works exploring similar generalizations. Furthermore, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models") studies an SBM with an active learning querying component. While their model (querying a single node to get its label) differs, for fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-size communities, it is equivalent to the pairwise querying model described here (via random sampling to identify anchor nodes). The paper's lower bound appears to contradict Gadde's sublinear-sample upper bound; this discrepancy likely arises because the lower bound implicitly assumes k → ∞, a condition not stated in the theorem. The authors must remove or reword all claims of novelty, including "first work," "significantly generalizes," "no systematic theoretical study," and "initiate a rigorous theoretical study."

2.  **Technical Precision Issues:**
    *   **Theorem 2:** This theorem appears incorrect as stated. For k=O(1) (e.g., k=2), clustering is possible with Q=0 in certain SBM regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The proof likely assumes k → ∞ without stating it explicitly; this condition must be added to the theorem statement. The theorem must also be carefully checked for contradictions with cases where Q=0 suffices.
    *   **Problem Formulation:** The minimax nature of the bounds is not explicitly stated. Crucially, the problem formulation must clarify whether cluster size restrictions exist (e.g., allowing k-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size n-k+1).
    *   **Formal Problem Statement:** The phrase "find Q subset of V x V" implies a non-adaptive algorithm, which conflicts with the adaptive querying model described elsewhere.

3.  **Expository Deficiencies:**
    *   **Clarity:** The model and problem statement need significant clarification. Unclear phrasing includes "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" (text appears missing) and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" (this bound applies only without side information).
    *   **Simulations:** The numerical section lacks value as it only compares against trivial baselines and omits comparison to existing methods. The claim that "your methods are the only ones that can be considered/implemented" is unsupported.
    *   **Grammar and Mechanics:** The paper contains pervasive grammatical errors, typos, inconsistent capitalization, incorrect plurals, spacing issues, article misuse (a/an/the), and rampant comma misuse (e.g., commas incorrectly placed after "let," "we have," "this means," "assume that," "since," "while," "note that," and before equations). Sentences frequently begin with "And," "Or," "Plus." The word "else" is misused (often "otherwise" is appropriate). Footnotes begin with lowercase and lack terminal punctuation. This is a severe distraction for readers.

4.  **Minor Points:**
    *   The abstract is overly long; the paper should present contributions sooner.
    *   Terminology "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" should be verified for standard usage. When first introducing Las Vegas (p.4), "average query complexity" must be specified.
    *   Footnote 1: The "1-" after p= appears incorrect.
    *   "clearly exhibiting" (Simulations section conclusion) is inaccurate.
    *   Many appendix brackets are too small.

**Post-Author Feedback:** The authors addressed key technical points (e.g., clarifying the Gadde contradiction and emphasizing the k → ∞ condition for Theorem 2), leading me to update my recommendation to *accept*. However, the writing issues remain critical. I strongly urge a meticulous proofread for the final version. I acknowledge that I was the sole reviewer highlighting the writing concerns, but these issues—especially grammatical errors, punctuation, and unclear phrasing—will significantly hinder readability for a diverse NeurIPS audience. Crucially, the grammatical and stylistic issues were *not* the basis for the borderline recommendation; they are separate, essential improvements required for publication. The authors should revise the use of "else," sentence starters ("And," "Or," "Plus"), the phrase "assume, otherwise...", and all other identified grammatical errors. Precise theorem statements (explicitly including asymptotic conditions like k → ∞) are non-negotiable.
