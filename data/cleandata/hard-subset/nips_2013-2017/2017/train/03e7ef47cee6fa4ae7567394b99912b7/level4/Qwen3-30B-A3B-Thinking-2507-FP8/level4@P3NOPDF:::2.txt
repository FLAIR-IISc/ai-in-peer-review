

This paper addresses query complexity for clustering via noiseless pairwise comparisons with random side information. While the problem is relevant and the contributions appear valuable if correct, the paper suffers from significant issues in presentation, technical precision, and literature context that substantially undermine its impact. The writing is dense and riddled with grammatical errors, typos, and unclear phrasing, making it difficult to assess the core technical claims. Below, I detail critical concerns that must be resolved before acceptance.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Inadequate Literature Context & Overstated Claims:** The paper repeatedly claims novelty ("first work," "significantly generalizes," "no systematic theoretical study") that conflicts with established work. Key omissions include:
    *   Generalizations of the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) using arbitrary distributions for edge labels (e.g., "labeled SBM" or "weighted SBM" literature).
    *   Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016), which studies active community detection in SBMs with a node-querying model. For fixed *k* (e.g., *k*=2, 100) and linear-sized communities, their model is effectively equivalent to pairwise querying (via random sampling to identify seed nodes). The paper's lower bound (Theorem 2) appears to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound; this discrepancy likely arises because the lower bound assumes *k* → ∞ (not stated in the theorem). All claims of novelty must be revised to accurately reflect prior work.

2.  **Theorem 2: Critical Technical Flaw:** Theorem 2 states a lower bound on query complexity for the SBM. However, for fixed *k* (e.g., *k*=2), clustering is often possible *without any queries* when edge probabilities satisfy certain conditions (e.g., Hellinger distance scaling as log(*n*)/*n*). The theorem's proof implicitly assumes *k* → ∞, which is **not stated in the theorem statement**. This omission renders the theorem misleading for practical settings where *k* is constant. The theorem *must* explicitly state the asymptotic regime (*k* → ∞) as a condition.

3.  **Clarity of Model and Minimax Assumptions:** The problem setup lacks clarity. It is unclear whether the minimax bound assumes no restrictions on cluster sizes (e.g., allowing *k*-1 singleton clusters and one large cluster). This significantly impacts the interpretation of the results and lower bounds. The model must be precisely defined, including all assumptions about cluster structure.

4.  **Poor Writing Quality:** The paper contains pervasive grammatical errors, typos, inconsistent capitalization, incorrect article usage ("a/an/the"), and misused commas (e.g., after "let," "assume that," "since"). Sentences frequently start with "And," "Or," or "Plus." The phrase "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" is grammatically incorrect and unclear. The term "else" is misused (often "otherwise" is correct). Footnotes violate standard formatting (capitalization, punctuation). These issues are not merely stylistic; they impede comprehension and reflect poorly on the work's rigor. A thorough proofread is mandatory.

**Additional Concerns:**

*   **Numerical Experiments:** The simulations section compares only against trivial baselines and lacks comparison to existing methods, diminishing its value.
*   **Terminology:** The use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" is non-standard; "Las Vegas" must be paired with "average query complexity" on first use.
*   **Problem Statement:** The phrase "find Q ⊆ V × V" implies a non-adaptive algorithm, which conflicts with the adaptive nature of the proposed method.
*   **Abstract & Structure:** The abstract is overly long. The paper spends excessive space on background before presenting contributions (3 pages before the first key result).
*   **Minor Errors:** Footnote 1 contains a stray "1-", and appendix brackets are often too small.

**Post-Author Feedback Note:** The authors addressed the Gadde contradiction and clarified the asymptotic assumption for Theorem 2. However, their response did not adequately address the pervasive writing issues or the need to revise all novelty claims. While I have updated my recommendation to *accept* based on the technical content (after verification of the corrected theorem), the paper **must undergo a meticulous revision** to fix the writing flaws and accurately position the work against prior literature. The grammatical and stylistic issues are substantial and will hinder reader comprehension for the NIPS audience.

**Recommendation:** Accept *with Mandatory Revision*. The core technical contribution is potentially valuable, but the current presentation is unacceptable for NIPS. The authors must:
1.  Remove all unsubstantiated claims of novelty.
2.  Explicitly state *k* → ∞ as a condition for Theorem 2 (and related results).
3.  Clarify the minimax model assumptions (cluster sizes).
4.  Conduct a comprehensive proofread to eliminate grammatical errors, typos, and stylistic issues.
5.  Revise the abstract and structure to prioritize contributions.
6.  Ensure all terminology and notation are precise and standard.

The writing flaws are not "minor" in the context of NIPS; they significantly reduce the paper's accessibility and perceived rigor. The authors' dismissal of these issues as "not deciding factors" is misplaced—their presence directly impacts the paper's reception. A careful revision is essential for the final version to meet NIPS standards.
