

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when equipped with side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors study a fundamental problem where an oracle can answer whether two elements belong to the same cluster, and the goal is to minimize the number of such queries required to recover the true clustering. Without side information, the query complexity is known to be Θ(nk), where n is the number of elements and k is the number of clusters. The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that with side information—a similarity matrix W where entries are drawn from f+ (same cluster) or f- (different clusters)—the query complexity reduces dramatically to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H denotes the Hellinger divergence. The authors establish that this bound is information-theoretically optimal up to a logarithmic factor, and they develop parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-.

The work is closely related to the stochastic block model (SBM), a well-studied framework in community detection. The authors generalize SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- rather than the Bernoulli distributions typical in SBM. This generalization is significant because it reveals how side information affects the query complexity threshold, which has not been systematically analyzed before. The paper's lower bound technique, which provides a general framework for proving lower bounds in interactive learning settings, is particularly noteworthy and could have broader applications beyond clustering.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity in clustering with side information, addressing a gap in the literature where most prior work has been heuristic. Second, it establishes tight bounds that connect the quality of the similarity matrix (measured by Hellinger divergence) to the number of queries needed. Third, the algorithms are computationally efficient and parameter-free, which is crucial for practical applications where the distributions f+ and f- are unknown. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate that the greedy strategy based on Hellinger divergence performs nearly as well as the optimal strategy, validating the theoretical findings.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a comprehensive theoretical analysis supported by detailed proofs. The connection to SBM is well-explained, and the authors appropriately cite relevant work on community detection and active learning. The experimental validation on a real-world dataset adds practical relevance to the theoretical contributions.

One limitation is that the paper does not address the case where the oracle might provide erroneous answers, though the authors note that this is handled in a companion paper. This omission is understandable given the focus on the idealized setting, but it would strengthen the paper to briefly acknowledge this limitation.

Strengths:
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity with side information
- Tight upper and lower bounds that are nearly optimal within a logarithmic factor
- Parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-
- Clear connection to SBM and broader implications for community detection
- Well-supported experimental validation on a real-world dataset
- Novel lower bound technique applicable to interactive learning settings

Weaknesses:
- Does not consider noisy oracle answers (though acknowledged as a topic for future work)
- The lower bound proof is technically complex and may be challenging for some readers to follow
- The experimental section could be slightly more detailed in explaining how the distributions f+ and f- were estimated

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper addresses an important theoretical gap in interactive clustering
- The results are significant and generalize prior work on SBM
- The algorithms are practical and parameter-free, making them valuable for real applications
- The theoretical contributions are well-supported with rigorous proofs
- The paper provides a general framework for lower bound analysis in interactive learning

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper does not consider the more realistic case of noisy oracle answers
- The experimental results are limited to a single dataset
- The technical complexity of the proofs might limit accessibility to some readers

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The results are novel, technically sound, and well-motivated. The paper provides a valuable framework for analyzing query complexity in clustering that extends beyond the specific problem studied. The theoretical insights have clear implications for practical applications in entity resolution and other areas where clustering with limited supervision is needed. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and makes a significant contribution to the field.
