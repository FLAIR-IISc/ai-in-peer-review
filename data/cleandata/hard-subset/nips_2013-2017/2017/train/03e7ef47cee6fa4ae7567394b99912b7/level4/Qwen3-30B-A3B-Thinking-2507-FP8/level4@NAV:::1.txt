

This paper investigates query complexity in clustering when provided with 'side information' in the form of a similarity matrix W. The matrix entries W_ij are drawn from distributions f⁺ or f⁻, indicating whether nodes i and j belong to the same cluster or different clusters, respectively. The primary contribution establishes that Θ(nk) queries are necessary without side information, while only O(k log n / H²(f⁺ || f⁻)) queries suffice with side information, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors further demonstrate that this bound is tight up to logarithmic factors in n.  
I find this work to be an interesting theoretical contribution for multiple reasons. It closely connects to the active area of Stochastic Block Models (SBM), yet poses novel questions regarding how query capabilities alter the clustering threshold. The result highlights that side information can significantly reduce query complexity, a notion not unexpected given that the similarity matrix may encode full cluster structure. Crucially, the paper quantifies how to evaluate the quality of the similarity matrix, offering a precise measure of its utility for reducing queries.
