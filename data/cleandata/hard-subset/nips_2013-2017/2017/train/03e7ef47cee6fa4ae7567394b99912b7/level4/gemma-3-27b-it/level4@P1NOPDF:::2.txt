NOTE: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors and on the same general topic. The other one is on noisy queries without any side information.

This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, incorporating random side-information for each pair. While I am familiar with some related literature, my knowledge isn’t comprehensive, making a complete assessment of the contributions challenging. The paper’s density also limited my ability to thoroughly verify the correctness of all details – particularly within the lengthy upper bound proof.

My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions appear valuable (assuming correctness), but the writing requires substantial improvement. I recognize that the significance of the contributions outweighs the quality of the writing; however, the numerous issues led to a borderline recommendation. A further round of review to verify suggested changes would have been beneficial, but I understand this isn’t possible for NIPS.

Regarding this paper specifically, several relevant works were not cited:

- The generalization of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) distributions to a more general form within the stochastic block model is not novel. A search on Google (or Google Scholar) using terms like “labeled stochastic block model” (or “labelled stochastic block model”) or the less common “weighted stochastic block model” reveals several papers exploring similar generalizations.
- There is at least one existing work investigating the SBM with an additional querying (active learning) component: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016). While this work employs a different querying model – querying a single node to obtain its label – for fixed *k* (e.g., *k*=2 or *k*=100) and linear-sized communities, this is functionally equivalent to your pairwise querying model. One could quickly find a node from each community via random sampling and then obtain the label of any new node through pairwise queries. Initially, your lower bound appears to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound, but this may stem from your lower bound holding only as *k* approaches infinity (see below).

In light of these related works, I recommend the authors revise or remove statements such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study”, “significantly generalizes them”, “This is the first work that…”, “generalize them in a significant way”, and “There is no systematic theoretical study.”

Here are some further comments:

- The model and problem statement could be presented more clearly. It appears you are considering minimax bounds, but this isn’t explicitly stated. Furthermore, does the minimax model impose any restrictions on cluster sizes? For example, do you allow for *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size *n-k+1*?
- Theorem 2, as stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with *k*=2 (or generally any *k*=O(1)), no queries are required in certain instances where the Hellinger distance scales as log(*n*)/ *n*. The proof seems to assume *k* approaches infinity, which is not stated in the theorem itself and absolutely must be included if this assumption is necessary. I’m uncertain if this is the sole issue—please carefully verify whether Theorem 2 generally contradicts cases where clustering is possible even with *Q*=0.
- The numerical section lacks significant value because it doesn’t compare against existing work, only against trivial baselines. Are your methods the only viable candidates for implementation/consideration?
- Numerous sentences are confusing or unclear, such as “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is text missing after “assume?”) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound only apply in the absence of side information?).
- The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Issues arise with capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and article usage (a/an/the). Commas are consistently misused (e.g., they generally shouldn’t follow “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” or “note that,” and are rarely needed before equations). Sentences shouldn’t begin with “And,” “Or,” or “Plus.” The word “else” is often used inappropriately (“otherwise” would be better). Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This is not an exhaustive list – regardless of the decision, I strongly recommend the authors carefully proofread the paper.

Some less significant comments:

- The abstract is somewhat lengthy, and generally, spending three pages before presenting the contributions isn't ideal.
- Is your terminology ("Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas") standard? Also, when you first mention “Las Vegas” on page 4, it should be accompanied by the term “average” query complexity (the word ‘average’ is missing).
- Footnote 1: Is the “1-” necessary after *p*= ?
- The phrasing “find Q subset of V x V” in the formal problem statement implies a non-adaptive algorithm.
- I disagree with the statement “clearly exhibiting” in the final sentence of the Simulations section.
- Brackets in the appendix are consistently too small.
