This paper investigates the query complexity for clustering tasks when "side information" is available. The side information is provided in the form of a similarity matrix W, where the entry Wij is distributed according to either f+ or f-, depending on whether i and j belong to the same cluster or different clusters. The main contribution of the paper is the demonstration that, in the absence of side information, Theta(nk) queries are required, whereas with side information, only O(k log n/H^2(f+||f-) ) queries are sufficient, where H represents the Hellinger distance between the two distributions. Furthermore, the authors show that this bound is tight up to a logarithmic factor.

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. The problem is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), which has been an active area of research in recent years. However, this work poses intriguing questions about how the availability of queries affects the threshold for successful clustering. The paper highlights that incorporating side information can drastically alter the query complexity, which is likely not unexpected given that the side information can potentially contain complete cluster information. Nevertheless, the authors make a significant contribution by identifying a method to assess the quality of the similarity matrix, which is a crucial aspect of this problem.
