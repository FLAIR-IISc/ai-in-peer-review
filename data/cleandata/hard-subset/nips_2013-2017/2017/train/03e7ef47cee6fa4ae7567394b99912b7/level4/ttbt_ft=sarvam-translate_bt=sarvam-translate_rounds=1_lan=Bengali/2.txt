Please note: I am reviewing two papers written by the same authors on the same general topic. The other one deals with a confusing question without any supporting information.

This paper provides an upper and lower bound on the complexity of clustering questions using un-confused pair comparisons, as well as the random supporting information associated with each pair. Although I am aware of some related literature, my knowledge is not complete, so it is difficult to fully judge the value of the contributions. The paper is so complex that I could not devote enough time to verify its correctness – especially in the lengthy section on proving the upper bound.

My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions are valuable (if correct), but the writing needs to be much improved. I acknowledge that the contributions are more important than the writing, but there are many problems that still make my recommendation borderline. It would be very useful if there was another review where the suggestions were vetted, but I understand that this is not allowed by NIPS.

Regarding this paper, there are a few extremely relevant works that were not cited:

- The idea of replacing Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) with a common distribution in the stochastic block model is not new. Searching Google (or Google Scholar) for "labeled [sometimes spelled labeled] stochastic block models" or the less common "weighted stochastic block models" yields several papers discussing similar generalizations.
- There is at least one work that studies SBM with additional question (active learning) components: "Active learning for community detection in stochastic block models" (Gade et al, ISIT 2016). This is related to a different question model, where a single node is asked and its label is found. But for a specific k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-sized communities, it is essentially the same as your pair question model – just find a node quickly from each community using a random sample, and then you can get the label of any new node by pair questioning each of those. At first glance, this may seem to contradict your lower bound with their (sublinear-sample) upper bound, but this is probably because your lower bound only applies up to k -> infinity (see below).

Anyway, in light of the above works, I would request the authors to remove/rewrite all statements in lines such as "we start with a rigorous theoretical study", "they generalize significantly", "this is the first work that...", "they generalize in an important way", "there is no systematic theoretical study" etc.

Here are some more comments:

- I think the model/problem should be presented more clearly. It seems you are looking at the minimax bounds, but this is not explicitly stated. Moreover, is the minimax model applicable without any restrictions on the cluster size? For example, do you approve a situation where the size of the k-1 cluster is 1 and the other is n-k+1?
- It seems that theorem 2 is not stated correctly. For SBM with k=2 (or possibly any k=O(1)), we know that in some cases, no question is needed where the Heelinger distance scales as log(n)/n. It seems that part of the problem is that the proof assumes k -> infinity, which is not mentioned in the theorem (it certainly should be). I am not sure if this is the only part of the problem – please check very carefully whether theorem 2 more generally contradicts those cases where clustering is possible with Q=0.
- The numerical section does not seem very valuable, mainly because it does not compare with any existing work, and only compares with very trivial baselines. Are your methods really the only methods that can be considered/implemented?
- There are many confusing/ambiguous sentences here, such as "assume, otherwise clustering has been done" ("is there any writing missing after 'assume'?") and "the theorem is already proven from the nk lower bound" (is the lower bound only applicable when there is no supporting information?).
- The paper is full of grammatical errors, typos, and strange word choices. Capital letters, plurals, spacing, and articles (a/an/the) are often incorrect. Commas are consistently used incorrectly (e.g., they should not typically appear after statements like "let" / "we have" / "this means" / "assume that" / "since" / "while" / "note that", and should rarely be used just before equations). The sentence "And/Or/Plus" should not begin. The word "else" is used strangely (often "otherwise" would be better). The footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This list of problems is not complete – whatever the conclusion, I strongly urge the authors to proof-read the paper as carefully as possible.

Some less important comments:

- The abstract seems very long, and in general, I think it is not ideal to spend 3 pages before reaching the contributions.
- Is your use of the words "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" standard? Also, when you first mention Las Vegas on p4, it should be mentioned along with the AVERAGE question complexity (the word 'average' is missing here).
- Footnote 1: Should 1- after p= really be there?
- The formal problem statement is written in such a way that "find the Q subset of V x V" as if the algorithm is non-adaptive.
- I disagree with the statement in the last sentence of the simulation section that "clearly demonstrates".
- Many parentheses throughout the appendix are too short.
