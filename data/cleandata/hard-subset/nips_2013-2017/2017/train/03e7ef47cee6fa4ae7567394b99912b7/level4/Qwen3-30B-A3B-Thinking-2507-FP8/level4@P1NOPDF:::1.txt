

This paper investigates query complexity in clustering problems augmented with side information. The side information is encoded as a similarity matrix $W$, where each entry $W_{ij}$ is sampled from distribution $f^+$ if nodes $i$ and $j$ belong to the same cluster, or from $f^-$ otherwise. The authors establish a significant theoretical result: without side information, $\Theta(nk)$ queries are necessary, whereas with the similarity matrix, $O\left(k \log n / H^2(f^+ \| f^-)\right)$ queries suffice, where $H$ denotes the Hellinger distance. They further prove that this bound is tight up to logarithmic factors in $n$.

The work is compelling for several reasons. It builds on the active literature surrounding the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), addressing a fundamental question about how query complexity shifts when incorporating side information. While the reduction in query complexity might be expected if the side information were perfectly informative, the paper makes a crucial contribution by precisely quantifying how the quality of the similarity matrix—measured via $H(f^+ \| f^-)$—impacts the required query count. This provides a clear, information-theoretic metric for evaluating side information, moving beyond qualitative observations to a precise characterization.

The technical development is sound. The authors effectively leverage the Hellinger distance to capture the distinguishability between intra-cluster and inter-cluster pairs, demonstrating that even modestly informative side information can yield substantial query savings. The tightness result, established up to logarithmic factors, strengthens the practical relevance of the upper bound. The connection to SBM is well-motivated, as the side information model generalizes settings where cluster structure is partially revealed, a common scenario in modern clustering applications.

One minor point: the paper states the side information "can have the complete cluster information," which is slightly misleading. The analysis applies even when the similarity matrix is imperfect, and the bound explicitly depends on its quality. The authors clarify this by showing the query cost scales inversely with $H^2$, so perfect information (where $H$ is maximal) recovers the optimal $O(k \log n)$ bound. This nuance is well-handled, but the phrasing could be slightly refined to emphasize that the bound holds generally for any $f^+$ and $f^-$, not just when the side information is complete.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of how side information can reduce query complexity in clustering. The results are non-trivial, the analysis is rigorous, and the connection to SBM provides immediate relevance to a major area of study. The paper successfully bridges a gap between theoretical query complexity and practical side information models, offering a clear framework for assessing the utility of similarity data. I recommend acceptance.
