This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information. The side information is provided as a similarity matrix **W**, where the entry *W<sub>ij</sub>* is distributed according to *f<sub>+</sub>* if *i* and *j* belong to the same cluster, and according to *f<sub>-</sub>* otherwise. The main result demonstrates that while Θ(*nk*) queries are required for clustering without side information, only O(*k* log *n* / *H<sup>2</sup>(f<sub>+</sub>||f<sub>-</sub>)*) queries suffice when side information is available, where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors also prove that this bound is tight up to a log(*n*) factor. 

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. The work is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a topic that has received significant attention recently, but it poses a novel question regarding how the ability to make queries alters the necessary threshold for successful clustering. The paper clearly shows that incorporating side information can dramatically reduce query complexity. While this outcome isn’t entirely unexpected – given that the side information *could* encode complete cluster membership – the paper importantly identifies a way to quantify the quality of the similarity matrix and its impact on query complexity.
