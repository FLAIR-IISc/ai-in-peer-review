This paper studies query complexity for clustering with 'side information'. The 'side information' is provided as a similarity matrix W, whose Wij entry is distributed as f+ or f-, depending on if i,j belong to the same cluster or different cluster. The main result of the paper is that one requires Θ(nk) queries without side information whereas they show that only O(k^2 log n/H^2(f+||f-) ) queries are sufficient with side information, where H is the Hellinger distance. They also show that up to a log(n) factor, this is tight.

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. This is closely related to the stochastic block model (SBM), which has seen a lot of activity in recent years, but asks some interesting questions as to how allowing queries change the threshold. It shows that allowing side information can drastically change query complexity. This itself is probably not surprising as the side information can have the complete cluster information, but the paper identifies how to assess the quality of the similarity matrix.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of clustering with side information, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors develop strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds when side information is available, and characterize the effect of side information on query complexity precisely. The proposed algorithms are computationally efficient and parameter-free, making them extremely important in practice.

The connection to popular community detection models, such as SBM, is also noteworthy. The authors show that their model is a direct and significant generalization of SBM, and that their results could be of independent interest in studying query complexity of interactive clustering with side information.

The experimental results on a popular bibliographic dataset are also impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms in practice. The comparison of different strategies, including the greedy strategy, shows that the performance of the algorithm is very close to that of the best strategy.

Overall, I think this is a well-written and well-organized paper that makes significant contributions to the field of clustering with side information. The results are interesting and well-motivated, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem.
