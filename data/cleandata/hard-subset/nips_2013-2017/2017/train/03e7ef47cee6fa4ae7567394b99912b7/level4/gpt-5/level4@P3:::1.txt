Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when an algorithm can ask pairwise “same-cluster?” queries to an oracle and also has access to side information in the form of a similarity matrix W. Each similarity entry w(i,j) is drawn i.i.d. from an unknown distribution f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on the number of queries needed to recover the true clustering. Without side information, Θ(nk) queries are both necessary and sufficient. With side information, they show that the number of queries can be reduced drastically, with bounds expressed in terms of the squared Hellinger divergence H^2(f+||f−). They present efficient, parameter-free algorithms (not requiring k, f+, or f−) for which query complexity scales polylogarithmically in n and inversely with H^2(f+||f−). The results relate to and generalize the stochastic block model (SBM), where f+ and f− would be Bernoulli with different edge probabilities.

Main contributions relative to prior work
- Establishes a general lower-bound framework for interactive clustering with side information in an adaptive setting, where classic Fano-type arguments do not directly apply.
- Shows that side information (akin to an SBM-type similarity matrix) can reduce the query complexity by orders of magnitude compared to the Θ(nk) barrier without side information.
- Designs efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas variants) that work for arbitrary unknown discrete f+, f−, with runtime O(n^2) dominated by processing W.
- Connects to exact recovery thresholds in SBM and raises the question of how active queries shift these thresholds.
- Provides a small empirical study (on CORA) illustrating that a greedy query strategy guided by estimated H^2 performs close to an oracle best-case strategy.

Quality
- The lower-bound technique is interesting and appears technically sound at a high level: it reduces assignment of a lightly queried point to a k-hypothesis testing problem, controls adaptivity by isolating “weak” clusters, and uses Hellinger divergence to bound total variation. The proofs leverage Sanov’s theorem and properties of Hellinger distance to control concentration and product distributions.
- The algorithms are plausible and carefully motivated: the iterative-update approach estimates empirical “intra/inter” distributions, adapts a cluster-size threshold ME ≈ C log n / H^2 to decide when side information is reliable, and queries only in gray zones. This yields parameter-free behavior.
- However, there is a critical inconsistency in the stated bounds. In several places the paper claims an upper bound of O(k log n / H^2) (Monte Carlo) while elsewhere (abstract, Las Vegas bound, and the lower bound) the dependence is k^2. As written, the lower bound Ω(k^2 / H^2) contradicts an O(k log n / H^2) upper bound for general k. This must be clarified and corrected.
- Some assumptions are strong: i.i.d. similarities across pairs, noiseless oracle, and discrete (finite-support) f+, f− for the upper bound. The lower bound is stated for discrete or continuous f+, f−; it would help to spell out precisely which results hold under which assumptions.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized conceptually, but the presentation suffers from typos, formatting artifacts, and inconsistent notation (e.g., H vs H^2, and where the dependence is in the numerator or denominator). Several equations are hard to parse, references are sometimes incomplete, and constants are not always clearly defined.
- The Membership function and thresholds in Phase 3 would benefit from cleaner notation and a short pseudocode summary. Bringing the key algorithm and theorem statements (with the exact k and H^2 dependences) into the main body would improve readability.

Originality
- High. To my knowledge, this is the first near-tight characterization (with information-theoretic lower bounds) of query complexity for clustering with side information under arbitrary unknown f+, f−, including parameter-free algorithms. The lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive settings is particularly novel.

Significance
- High. The work clarifies when and how side information can substantially reduce query complexity in interactive clustering, a practical setting relevant to active learning and crowdsourcing. The connection to SBM suggests broader implications for community detection with queries. The parameter-free approach is valuable in practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and timely question with practical relevance.
- General and principled lower-bound methodology for interactive settings.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with theoretical guarantees that nearly match lower bounds (pending clarification of k vs k^2).
- Clear connection to and generalization of SBM; raises interesting open questions about thresholds under queries.
- Illustrative experiment showing strong reductions in queries with minimal loss in accuracy.

Cons for acceptance
- Inconsistency between upper and lower bounds on the k dependence (k vs k^2) is a serious issue that must be resolved.
- Presentation issues (typos, broken equations, incomplete references) hamper readability.
- Empirical evaluation is limited (single dataset, no comparison to prominent baselines such as [Wang et al. 2012; Vesdapunt et al. 2014] or [Awasthi et al. 2014]).
- Assumptions (i.i.d. similarities, noiseless oracle) may limit applicability; discussion of robustness to model misspecification is brief.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please reconcile the discrepancy between O(k log n / H^2) and O(k^2 log n / H^2) in the upper bounds, and between Ω(k / H^2) vs Ω(k^2 / H^2) in the lower bounds. Which statements are correct for the Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms?
- Clarify precisely which results require discrete f+, f− and which extend to continuous distributions. Can the parameter-free algorithm handle continuous-valued similarities without discretization?
- Discuss sensitivity to violations of the i.i.d. assumption on W (e.g., metric constraints, correlations induced by feature sharing).
- Provide a brief pseudocode for the main algorithm in the paper body, unify notation for Hellinger distance/divergence, and clearly state constants and threshold definitions.
- Expand experiments to include additional datasets and comparisons to existing interactive clustering/ER heuristics. Reporting wall-clock time and the effect of cluster imbalance would be useful.
- Comment on whether the techniques extend to a noisy oracle (beyond the companion work) and to settings with k-wise queries.

Overall evaluation
This paper tackles an important and timely problem with strong theoretical contributions and promising practical implications. Provided the authors resolve the k vs k^2 inconsistency and improve clarity, I believe this work would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
