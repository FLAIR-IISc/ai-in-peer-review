This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering in the presence of “side information,” formalized as a similarity matrix **W**. The entries *W<sub>ij</sub>* are distributed according to *f<sub>+</sub>* if *i* and *j* belong to the same cluster, and *f<sub>-</sub>* otherwise. The core contribution is demonstrating that, while Θ(*nk*) queries are necessary for clustering without side information, only O(*k* log *n* / *H<sup>2</sup>*( *f<sub>+</sub>* || *f<sub>-</sub>* )) queries suffice when leveraging the similarity matrix, where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors further establish that this bound is tight up to a log(*n*) factor.

I find this to be a compelling and theoretically significant result. The work is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a topic of considerable recent interest, but introduces a novel perspective by examining how the ability to make queries alters the fundamental limits of cluster recovery. Specifically, the paper effectively illustrates the dramatic impact that side information can have on query complexity. While the potential for side information to reduce query requirements isn’t entirely unexpected – as a perfect similarity matrix would trivially reveal cluster structure – this paper provides a rigorous quantification of that benefit. Critically, it identifies a measure, the Hellinger distance between the intra- and inter-cluster similarity distributions, that characterizes the quality of the similarity matrix and dictates the achievable query complexity. 

The connection to the SBM is well articulated, and the paper’s exploration of how queries modify the traditional SBM recovery threshold is a valuable contribution. The authors clearly frame their work within the existing literature and highlight the differences and advantages of their approach. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the notation is generally clear. 

However, a few aspects could benefit from further clarification. While the theoretical result is strong, the practical implications are not fully explored. Discussing scenarios where such a similarity matrix **W** might naturally arise (e.g., from pre-trained embeddings, collaborative filtering data, or expert knowledge) would strengthen the paper’s impact. Furthermore, elaborating on the assumptions made about the distributions *f<sub>+</sub>* and *f<sub>-</sub>* – beyond simply requiring them to be probability densities – would be beneficial. Are there specific classes of distributions for which the result holds, or for which it might fail?

Additionally, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the computational cost of making each query. The query complexity analysis focuses on the number of queries, but the cost per query could be significant depending on the specific implementation. Acknowledging this tradeoff would offer a more complete picture of the practical considerations.

Despite these minor points, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to our understanding of clustering with side information. The result is sharp, the analysis is rigorous, and the connection to the SBM is well-motivated. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the points raised above regarding practical applicability and distributional assumptions.
