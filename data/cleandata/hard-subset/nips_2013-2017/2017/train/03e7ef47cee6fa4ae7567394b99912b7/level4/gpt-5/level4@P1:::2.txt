I am reviewing two papers by (what appears to be) the same authors on related topics; the other concerns noisy queries without side information.

Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with an oracle that answers noiseless pairwise same-cluster queries, supplemented by side information in the form of a similarity matrix whose entries are drawn from f+ or f− depending on the true cluster relation. The authors give information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, and propose efficient, parameter-free algorithms. They highlight connections to the stochastic block model (SBM), where their side-information model generalizes Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) to arbitrary distributions.

While this generalization is natural and useful, similar ideas have appeared in the literature under the names “labeled/labelled SBM” and “weighted SBM,” where edges carry labels or weights drawn from different distributions. There is also prior work on active learning for SBM: Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) consider a node-label query model. For fixed k and linear-sized communities, their model is essentially equivalent to pairwise queries (one can quickly identify a representative per community and then label new nodes via pairwise queries to those representatives). At first glance, their sublinear sample upper bounds might appear to contradict the present lower bounds, but this seems to stem from regimes and assumptions (e.g., k growing) that should be stated explicitly. In light of these works, claims such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes,” “this is the first work,” and “there is no systematic theoretical study” should be toned down or reworded.

Strengths:
- Tackles an important and timely problem: quantifying how side information reduces query complexity for clustering with noiseless pairwise queries.
- Provides information-theoretic lower bounds and parameter-free, efficient algorithms with upper bounds that nearly match (up to logarithmic factors).
- Highlights an interesting and relevant connection to community detection/SBM and suggests avenues for future work.
- If correct, the results would be valuable to both theory and practice.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Model clarity: The paper appears to adopt a minimax perspective, but this is not stated explicitly. Are there any restrictions on cluster sizes? Do you allow highly unbalanced cases (e.g., k−1 singleton clusters and one cluster of size n−k+1)? Please make the decision-theoretic setup and admissible instances precise.
- Theorem 2 as stated appears problematic. For SBM with k=2 (or generally any k=O(1)), exact recovery is possible with Q=0 in regimes where the squared Hellinger distance between f+ and f− scales like log n/n. Your lower bound, as written, would seem to preclude this. The proof appears to rely on k→∞ (this is used in the constants and probability-of-error terms), but this is not stated in the theorem. The theorem (and any lemmas it depends on) must state the precise parameter regimes (e.g., asymptotics in k, n, and H2(f+‖f−)) under which the bounds hold. Please also check carefully for any contradictions with known Q=0 recovery results.
- Numerical section: The experiments compare only to trivial baselines and not to existing methods (e.g., heuristics from the ER literature or SBM algorithms). If comparable methods exist, please implement or at least discuss them; otherwise, explain clearly why such comparisons are not feasible. As it stands, the empirical section adds limited value.
- Presentation: The paper is very dense, and the upper bound proof is long and difficult to check. The writing needs substantial improvement for clarity. Some sentences are confusing or incomplete (e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done”; “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound,” which seems to refer to the no-side-information case). Precision in theorem statements and assumptions is essential.

Specific presentation/terminology issues (non-exhaustive):
- The abstract is too long; more generally, the paper spends ~3 pages before clearly stating the main contributions.
- “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” are standard terms, but when introducing Las Vegas on p.4, please discuss average query complexity explicitly (the word “average” is currently missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” really appear after p=? Please check.
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V×V” makes the algorithm sound non-adaptive; please clarify adaptivity.
- I disagree with the phrase “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the simulations section; the evidence presented does not clearly establish the claimed behavior.
- Many brackets in the appendix are too small; please fix typography.

Language and style:
The manuscript has numerous grammatical errors and unconventional phrasing. Common issues include capitalization, plurals, spacing, and article usage; overuse/misplacement of commas (e.g., after “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that,” and before equations); starting sentences with “And/Or/Plus”; unusual use of “else” where “otherwise” is appropriate; and footnotes that do not start with a capital letter or end with a period. Regardless of the final decision, I strongly urge a careful, line-by-line proofreading.

Pros for acceptance:
- Important problem with practical motivation (ER, crowdsourcing).
- New lower bounds and nearly matching, parameter-free algorithms.
- General side-information model tied to SBM; potential to influence future work.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing citations and over-strong novelty claims relative to labeled/weighted SBMs and active querying in SBM.
- Lack of clarity and precision in problem setup and theorem statements (especially parameter regimes for Theorem 2).
- Empirical section is weak relative to the breadth of related work.
- Writing quality significantly hinders readability.

Post-author feedback:
After the authors’ clarifications, I updated my recommendation to accept. I appreciate the explanation regarding the lack of contradiction with Gadde et al. However:
- Theorems and lemmas must state precise regimes (e.g., if a result relies on k sufficiently large, this must be explicit in the statement, not only in the proof).
- Please revise language such as Else/And/Or/Plus, the “assume, otherwise…” sentence, and similar constructions. While capitalization and commas were not deciding factors, the cumulative effect of these issues is substantial for NIPS readers, especially those outside the authors’ subfield.
- Please reword broad novelty claims to acknowledge prior work on labeled/weighted SBMs and active learning in SBM.
