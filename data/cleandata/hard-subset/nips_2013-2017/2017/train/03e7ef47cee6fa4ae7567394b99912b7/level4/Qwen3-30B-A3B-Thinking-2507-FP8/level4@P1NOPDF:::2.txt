

This paper establishes upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented by random side information associated with each pair. I am reviewing this paper alongside another by the same authors addressing noisy queries without side information. While I recognize the potential value of the contributions, my familiarity with the relevant literature is limited, making a comprehensive assessment challenging. Furthermore, the paper's density hindered thorough verification of the lengthy upper bound proof.

My overall assessment of both papers is that the technical contributions appear valuable if correct, but the writing requires substantial improvement. Although the core ideas outweigh stylistic issues, the pervasive writing problems render the paper difficult to follow, leading to a borderline recommendation. I acknowledge that additional review rounds are not permitted at NIPS, but a more careful revision would significantly enhance the paper.

**Critical Technical Concerns:**  
The paper fails to cite several highly relevant works. First, the generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli(p/q) to arbitrary distributions is not novel—searches for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" yield multiple prior studies. Second, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) study an active learning SBM with a single-node querying model. For fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-size communities, this is functionally equivalent to pairwise querying: sample a node from each community, then determine any new node's cluster via pairwise queries to these representatives. The authors’ lower bound appears to contradict Gadde’s sublinear-sample upper bound; this discrepancy likely arises because the lower bound assumes k → ∞ (a condition absent from Theorem 2). I request removal of all claims such as "initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes," "first work," or "no systematic theoretical study" to avoid overstatement.

**Major Clarity and Correctness Issues:**  
- **Model Specification:** The minimax framework is implied but not explicitly stated. Crucially, the paper does not clarify whether cluster size constraints (e.g., allowing k-1 singleton clusters) are imposed.  
- **Theorem 2:** As stated, this result is incorrect for fixed k (e.g., k=2). For small k, clustering may require zero queries even when Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The proof implicitly assumes k → ∞, which must be explicitly included in the theorem statement. I urge the authors to verify whether this is the sole issue or if the theorem contradicts known cases of Q=0 clustering.  
- **Numerical Section:** The experiments lack meaningful baselines beyond trivial comparisons, offering little insight into the method’s practical utility relative to existing work.  
- **Ambiguous Statements:** Phrases like "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" (missing context) and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" (which assumes no side information) require clarification.  

**Writing Quality:**  
The paper contains pervasive grammatical errors, typos, and unnatural phrasing. Key issues include:  
- Incorrect article usage (a/an/the), inconsistent capitalization, and misplaced commas (e.g., after "let," "we have," "since").  
- Sentences starting with "And," "Or," or "Plus."  
- Misuse of "else" (often "otherwise" is preferable).  
- Footnotes beginning with lowercase letters or omitting periods.  
- Overly long sentences and unclear terminology (e.g., "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" should be defined; "Las Vegas" must specify "average" query complexity on first mention).  
- Minor errors: Unnecessary "1-" in "p= 1-"; "Q subset of V × V" suggesting non-adaptive algorithms; oversized brackets in the appendix.  
The abstract is overly verbose, and the 3-page introduction delays the contribution. The phrase "clearly exhibiting" in the simulations section is unsubstantiated.

**Post-Author Feedback:**  
The authors addressed key concerns (e.g., clarifying the Gadde contradiction and emphasizing k → ∞ in Theorem 2), prompting my updated recommendation of *acceptance*. However, I strongly urge a meticulous final revision to resolve all writing issues. While I was the sole reviewer highlighting writing problems, these are critical for accessibility—particularly for readers from diverse academic backgrounds. The writing flaws were not the *primary* reason for the borderline initial score (the technical gaps were), but they significantly impact readability. Specific revisions needed:  
- Retain precision in theorem statements (e.g., "for sufficiently large k" must appear in the theorem, not just the proof).  
- Correct grammatical errors like "assume, otherwise..." and eliminate "else" misuses.  
- Proofread meticulously for articles, capitalization, and sentence structure.  

The paper’s technical merit is promising, but its impact will be diminished without these corrections. I recommend acceptance only after a thorough language and clarity overhaul.
