Note: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors on closely related topics; the other concerns noisy queries without side information.

This paper studies clustering via noiseless pairwise comparisons with random pairwise side information, providing upper and lower bounds on query complexity. While I am familiar with parts of the literature, my knowledge is not exhaustive. The paper is also quite dense, and I could not fully verify all details—especially in the long proof of the upper bound. My overall impression (for both papers) is that the contributions appear valuable if correct, but the writing needs significant improvement. Although content matters most, the presentation issues are substantial enough that my recommendation was borderline. A further revision cycle to address reviewer feedback would have been very helpful, though I understand NIPS does not allow this.

Related work that should be cited and discussed:
- Generalizing the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) to arbitrary edge-label distributions is not new. Searching for “labeled (labelled) SBM” or “weighted SBM” reveals several prior works with similar generalizations.
- Active learning for SBMs has also been studied, e.g., “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Their query model differs (querying a single node to obtain its label), but for fixed k and linear-size communities, one can simulate node labels via pairwise queries by first finding a representative from each community. At a glance, your lower bound might seem to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound; this appears to be resolved if your lower bound holds only as k → ∞ (see comments on Theorem 2).

In light of the above, please remove or rephrase claims such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes,” “This is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “There is no systematic theoretical study.”

Further comments:
- The model and problem setup should be stated more clearly. It appears you are proving minimax bounds, but this is never made explicit. Do you allow arbitrary cluster sizes? For example, can there be k−1 singleton clusters and one of size n−k+1?
- Theorem 2, as stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with k = 2 (and likely any fixed k = O(1)), exact clustering can be possible with Q = 0 even when the Hellinger distance scales like log n / n. Your proof seems to assume k → ∞, but this is not stated in the theorem and must be. Please also check carefully that Theorem 2 does not contradict known Q = 0 achievability results for fixed k.
- The numerical section is not very informative. It does not compare to existing methods and uses only trivial baselines. Are there truly no other comparable or implementable methods?
- Several sentences are confusing or incomplete, e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (missing content after “assume”?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (does that bound apply when side information is present?).
- The paper has pervasive language issues: grammar, typos, capitalization, plurals, spacing, and use of articles (a/an/the). Commas are frequently misplaced (e.g., usually not after “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that”), and rarely belong immediately before an equation. Avoid starting sentences with And/Or/Plus. “Else” is often misused (consider “otherwise”). Footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a period. Regardless of the decision, a thorough proofread is strongly recommended.

Minor comments:
- The abstract is too long; more generally, it takes about three pages to reach the main contributions.
- Please confirm that your use of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” is standard. When first mentioning Las Vegas on p. 4, also specify that it refers to average query complexity (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” appear after “p=”?
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V × V” suggests a non-adaptive algorithm; please clarify adaptivity in the definition.
- I disagree with the phrase “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets in the appendix are too small.

- Theorems and lemmas must be stated precisely. If a result holds only for sufficiently large k, this must be stated in the theorem itself, not only in the proof.
