This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering where, in addition to an oracle that can answer pairwise same-cluster queries, the algorithm has access to side information. This side information is provided as a similarity matrix W, where the entry for a pair of items (i, j) is drawn from a distribution f+ if they belong to the same cluster, and from f- otherwise. The authors contrast the known baseline query complexity of Θ(nk) without side information to their new findings. The central result is a dramatic reduction in complexity when side information is available: the paper presents efficient, parameter-free algorithms that require only O(k² logn / H²(f+||f-)) queries, where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f-. This upper bound is shown to be nearly tight via a corresponding information-theoretic lower bound of Ω(k² / H²(f+||f-)). The work establishes a strong connection to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), which can be seen as a special case of this paper's model, and raises interesting questions about how active queries can alter recovery thresholds in such models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental and practically motivated problem. While many heuristics for interactive clustering and entity resolution implicitly use a similarity matrix to guide querying, this work is the first to provide a rigorous theoretical characterization of how this side information quantitatively affects the query complexity. The result, showing a potential exponential improvement, is highly significant. The problem formulation itself is novel and elegantly connects the fields of active learning, clustering, and community detection.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and comprehensive. Providing both an algorithmic upper bound and a nearly-matching information-theoretic lower bound gives a complete picture of the problem's complexity. The identification of the squared Hellinger distance as the key quantity governing this complexity is a major insight. A key strength is that the proposed algorithms are parameter-free—they do not require prior knowledge of the number of clusters (k) or the distributions (f+, f-)—which is crucial for real-world applicability. Furthermore, the technique developed for the lower bound in an adaptive, interactive setting is novel and could be of independent interest.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates its contributions and their relationship to prior work. The connection to SBM is particularly well-explained and insightful.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on the Cora dataset serve as a valuable proof of concept. However, the empirical evaluation could be expanded to further strengthen the paper's claims. For example, experiments on synthetic datasets would allow for a controlled analysis of how the number of queries scales with the key theoretical parameters (k and H²(f+||f-)), which would provide a more direct validation of the theoretical bounds.
*   **Gap in Bounds:** There is a log(n) factor gap between the upper and lower bounds. While this is a minor point and the bounds are already strong, it would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss whether they believe this gap is an artifact of the analysis or if it might be fundamental to the problem.
*   **Proof Intuition:** The proof sketch for the lower bound in Section 2 is quite dense. While space is limited and full proofs are in the appendix, adding a few more sentences to build intuition for the construction (e.g., how "weak" clusters are identified and used in the hypothesis testing framework) could improve the paper's accessibility.

**Summary:**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of interactive clustering. The problem is well-motivated, the results are strong and nearly tight, and the proposed algorithms are practical due to their parameter-free nature. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to potential areas for expansion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity for clustering with side information, a common practical scenario.
*   Delivers strong, nearly-tight information-theoretic upper and lower bounds, establishing H²(f+||f-) as the critical parameter.
*   The proposed algorithms are efficient and, importantly, parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage.
*   The work establishes an insightful and novel connection to the Stochastic Block Model.
*   The lower-bounding technique for an interactive setting is a novel technical contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation, while positive, could be more comprehensive in testing the derived scaling laws.
