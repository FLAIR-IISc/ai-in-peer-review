

This paper establishes upper and lower bounds on query complexity for clustering via noiseless pairwise comparisons with random side information. While the topic aligns with a companion paper (addressing noisy queries without side information), this review focuses on the current submission. The contributions appear valuable if technically sound, but the paper suffers from significant writing issues and critical omissions that undermine its impact. I recommend borderline acceptance pending major revisions.

**Major Concerns:**
1.  **Critical Missing Citations & Overstated Claims:** The paper repeatedly claims novelty (e.g., "initiate a rigorous study," "first work," "significantly generalizes"), which is inaccurate. Key prior work includes:
    *   The "labeled" or "weighted" stochastic block model (SBM) generalizes the Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) assumption to arbitrary distributions (e.g., [Citation 1], [Citation 2]).
    *   Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) studies active learning for SBM community detection via single-node queries. For fixed k and linear-sized communities, this is equivalent to pairwise querying (via random sampling to identify seed nodes). The paper's lower bound appears to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound; this likely stems from the lower bound requiring k → ∞ (not stated in Theorem 2). **The authors must remove all overstated novelty claims and properly cite these works.**

2.  **Theorem 2 Requires Clarification:** The stated theorem (for general k) contradicts established results for fixed k (e.g., k=2), where clustering is possible with zero queries when the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The proof implicitly assumes k → ∞, which must be explicitly stated in the theorem statement. The current formulation is misleading and requires correction.

3.  **Problem Formulation & Minimax Context:** The minimax nature of the bounds is not explicitly stated. Crucially, it is unclear whether cluster size constraints (e.g., allowing k-1 singleton clusters) are considered. The problem statement must clarify the minimax setting and its assumptions.

4.  **Limited Value of Numerical Experiments:** The simulations lack meaningful comparison against existing methods, relying only on trivial baselines. The authors must demonstrate how their approach compares to relevant prior work to justify its utility.

**Significant Writing & Technical Issues:**
*   **Grammar & Clarity:** The paper contains pervasive grammatical errors, typos, inconsistent capitalization, incorrect plurals, misplaced commas, and awkward phrasing (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" – missing text; "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" – incorrect context). Sentences frequently start with "And," "Or," or "Plus." The word "else" is misused. Footnotes lack standard capitalization and punctuation. These issues severely hinder readability.
*   **Terminology & Precision:** The use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" for query complexity needs standard justification. The first mention of "Las Vegas" (p.4) should specify "average" query complexity. The phrase "clearly exhibiting" (Simulations section) is unsupported. The problem statement ("find Q subset of V x V") implies non-adaptive querying, which is inconsistent with the adaptive nature of the algorithm.
*   **Structure:** The abstract is overly long, and the paper spends excessive space before presenting core contributions (3 pages). The appendix contains inconsistent bracket sizing.

**Post-Author Feedback:**
The authors' responses addressed the Gadde et al. contradiction and clarified Theorem 2's k-dependence, which is positive. However, their dismissal of writing issues ("capitalization and commas were deciding factors") is misplaced. While technical correctness was the primary concern, the writing flaws significantly impede comprehension, especially for readers outside the authors' immediate field. I have updated my recommendation to **accept** based on the technical revisions, but **strongly insist on a meticulous final revision addressing all writing and citation issues**.

**Specific Revision Requests:**
1.  **Remove all claims of novelty** regarding the SBM generalization and cite relevant prior work (labeled/weighted SBM, Gadde et al.).
2.  **Explicitly state the k → ∞ assumption** in Theorem 2 (and any similar results).
3.  **Clarify the minimax setting** and cluster size assumptions.
4.  **Revise the numerical section** to compare against relevant baselines.
5.  **Conduct a thorough proofread** focusing on:
    *   Grammar, punctuation (especially commas), and sentence structure.
    *   Correct use of articles ("a/an/the"), plurals, and capitalization.
    *   Avoiding sentence-starting conjunctions.
    *   Precise terminology (e.g., "average" query complexity for Las Vegas).
    *   Fixing ambiguous phrases (e.g., "assume, otherwise...").
6.  **Shorten the abstract** and streamline the early sections to reach contributions faster.

**Final Recommendation:** The technical contribution, once clarified and properly contextualized, is valuable. The writing deficiencies are severe enough to warrant a borderline recommendation *before* the authors' responses. The post-feedback revisions addressed the core technical concerns, leading to acceptance. However, the paper's accessibility and credibility depend entirely on a rigorous final proofread addressing the extensive writing issues listed above. I strongly urge the authors to prioritize this revision. The current manuscript, as submitted, would be difficult for many NIPS readers to follow due to its linguistic shortcomings.
