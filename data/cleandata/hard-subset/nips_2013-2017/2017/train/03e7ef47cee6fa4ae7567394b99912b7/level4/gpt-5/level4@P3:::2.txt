Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when an oracle answers noiseless pairwise “same-cluster?” queries and the algorithm additionally observes side information: a similarity matrix W with entries drawn i.i.d. from f+ for within-cluster pairs and from f− for cross-cluster pairs. The authors give information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, showing that side information can dramatically reduce the number of queries compared to the Θ(nk) worst-case bound without side information.

The main results are:
- Lower bounds: Any (possibly adaptive and randomized) algorithm requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) queries in expectation to succeed with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) to succeed with probability 1, where H^2 denotes squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−.
- Upper bounds: Efficient, parameter-free algorithms (a Monte Carlo algorithm that succeeds with high probability and a Las Vegas algorithm that succeeds with probability 1) that use Õ(k^2/H^2) queries, with an additional O(n log n) term for the Las Vegas variant. The algorithms do not assume knowledge of k, f+, or f− and rely on an iterative scheme that estimates f+, f− from side information and uses Hellinger divergence as a stopping criterion.

The model connects directly to stochastic block models (SBMs). The case of no queries corresponds to community detection with general edge-weight distributions; the paper frames how a limited number of oracle queries shifts the information-theoretic thresholds. While the paper cites works on general SBMs, it should also acknowledge related “labeled/weighted SBM” literature, which studies non-Bernoulli edge labels and weights, and active querying for SBMs, e.g., Gadde et al. (ISIT’16) on node-label queries (the authors cite this but should clarify the relationship).

Quality
- Technical depth and correctness: The lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive settings is interesting and nontrivial; the upper-bound algorithmic ideas are plausible and thoughtfully designed. I could not check all details of the long proofs, but the approach appears sound. However, the statement of Theorem 2 seems to implicitly rely on k being sufficiently large (e.g., the error lower bound involves terms that vanish with large k), which must be made explicit. Moreover, the paper should carefully reconcile its lower bounds with known Q=0 exact-recovery thresholds for constant k in SBMs to avoid any appearance of contradiction.
- Consistency and precision: There is an inconsistency in the stated complexities (sometimes O(k log n/H^2), elsewhere O(k^2 log n/H^2)); please unify these statements. If the lower bound scales as k^2/H^2, the upper bound should be Õ(k^2/H^2) to be “nearly matching.”
- Empirics: The experiments illustrate feasibility but do not compare against existing methods (e.g., heuristic ER workflows or other interactive clustering baselines). Even a modest comparison would strengthen the empirical section.

Clarity
The paper is dense and would benefit from a careful rewrite. Specific issues:
- Clearly state the problem in minimax terms up front, including what (if any) constraints exist on cluster sizes. Do you allow highly imbalanced partitions (e.g., k−1 singleton clusters)?
- Precisely state assumptions in theorems (e.g., ranges of H^2, regimes of k, whether results require “sufficiently large k”).
- Define “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” precisely, and for the latter, explicitly refer to average query complexity.
- Fix language that suggests non-adaptivity (e.g., “find Q ⊆ V × V”); make adaptivity explicit.
- Substantial copy-editing is needed: capitalization, articles, plurals, spacing, comma usage, and awkward connectors (e.g., Else/And/Or/Plus at sentence starts). Avoid “assume, otherwise ...” and similar constructions; replace “else” with “otherwise” where appropriate; ensure footnotes start with capital letters and end with periods. Several sentences are unclear (e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done”; “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound”). Bracket sizing and punctuation around equations should be corrected.
- Tone down priority claims. Given prior work on labeled/weighted SBMs and active querying in SBMs, please avoid phrases such as “we initiate the first rigorous study” or “no systematic theory exists,” and accurately position the paper’s novelty: query complexity with general unknown f+, f− and parameter-free, nearly tight algorithms.

Originality
The specific combination of:
- an interactive query model with side information drawn from general, unknown f+, f−;
- information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive algorithms; and
- efficient, parameter-free algorithms with near-matching complexity,
appears novel and of broad interest. The lower-bound framework for interactive settings is particularly appealing.

Significance
The work has the potential to influence both theory (interactive learning lower bounds, community detection with queries) and practice (crowdsourced entity resolution). The near-tight dependence on Hellinger divergence is insightful. The empirical section is modest; stronger comparisons would increase practical impact.

Pros
- Addresses an important and timely problem; clear connection to ER and community detection.
- Information-theoretic lower bounds for adaptive querying.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with near-matching upper bounds.
- Sensible use of Hellinger divergence and iterative estimation.

Cons
- Clarity and writing issues throughout; several statements are imprecise or inconsistent.
- Theorems need exact regimes and assumptions stated (e.g., role of k, cluster sizes).
- Inconsistency between O(k log n/H^2) vs O(k^2 log n/H^2) in different sections.
- Limited empirical comparisons; baselines are trivial.
- Overstated novelty claims relative to labeled/weighted SBM and active querying literature.

Recommendation
Weak accept (after author response). The core ideas and results are valuable, and the contributions appear significant if the proofs hold. For the camera-ready version, I strongly recommend:
- Precisely stating all assumptions and regimes in theorems and lemmas, especially where bounds rely on sufficiently large k, specific ranges of H^2, or cluster-size conditions.
- Reconciling claims with the Q=0 SBM literature (constant k, balanced clusters), and clarifying the relation to node-label querying (Gadde et al. 2016).
- Unifying complexity statements (k vs k^2 factors) across abstract, theorems, and proofs.
- Thorough rewriting for clarity and style, addressing the specific language and formatting issues above.
- Moderating claims of novelty and adding citations to labeled/weighted SBM literature.
- Strengthening the experimental section with comparisons to existing interactive clustering/ER baselines.
