This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, supplemented by random side-information. While the theoretical contributions appear significant, the paper's density and numerous presentation issues make it challenging to fully verify the correctness of the results and assess their novelty. My primary concern is that the quality of the writing significantly obscures the work's potential value, leading to a borderline recommendation.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's claims of novelty appear overstated due to the omission of several highly relevant works. A more thorough literature review is needed to accurately position the contribution.
    *   The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) to handle general distributions instead of just Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) is not new. This has been explored in papers on the "labeled stochastic block model" (or "labelled SBM") and the "weighted stochastic block model."
    *   The problem of active learning in SBM has been studied before. For instance, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) investigates a model where single-node queries reveal community labels. For a fixed number of communities (k), this is functionally similar to the pairwise query model presented here.
    *   In light of this existing work, strong claims such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "This is the first work that...," and "There is no systematic theoretical study" should be removed or substantially rephrased.

2.  **Technical Clarity and Potential Issues:**
    *   **Ambiguous Problem Formulation:** The paper does not explicitly state the precise theoretical framework. It appears to be a minimax analysis, but this is not clarified. Furthermore, it is unclear if the analysis holds for any cluster size distribution, including highly imbalanced cases (e.g., k-1 clusters of size 1).
    *   **Potentially Incorrect Statement of Theorem 2:** As written, Theorem 2 seems to contradict known results for the SBM with a small, constant number of communities. For example, with k=2, clustering is often possible with zero queries (Q=0) in regimes where the theorem's lower bound would suggest otherwise. The proof appears to rely on an unstated assumption that k tends to infinity. This condition is critical and must be explicitly stated in the theorem itself for it to be correct. The authors should carefully check if Theorem 2 more generally conflicts with known results where clustering is possible with Q=0.

3.  **Presentation and Writing Quality:** The paper suffers from significant presentation issues that hinder readability and comprehension.
    *   **Weak Experimental Section:** The simulations only compare against trivial baselines and do not include any existing methods from the literature. This limits their value in demonstrating the practical advantages of the proposed algorithms.
    *   **Unclear Phrasing and Structure:** Many sentences are confusing or grammatically incomplete (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done," "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound"). Furthermore, the paper takes nearly three pages to introduce its main contributions, which is excessively long.
    *   **Systematic Grammatical Errors:** The manuscript is replete with errors in grammar, punctuation, and style. These include incorrect capitalization, pluralization, article usage (a/an/the), and spacing. Commas are frequently misused (e.g., following phrases like "we have," "since," or "note that"). Sentences improperly begin with "And," "Or," or "Plus." This high volume of errors detracts from the paper's professionalism and makes the technical arguments difficult to follow.

**Minor Comments**

*   Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology standard in this context? When "Las Vegas" is first introduced, it should be specified that it relates to the *average* query complexity.
*   In Footnote 1, the "1-" after "p=" appears to be a typo.
*   The phrase "find Q subset of V x V" in the problem statement suggests a non-adaptive algorithm, which may not be the intention.
*   The claim of "clearly exhibiting" a phenomenon in the last sentence of the Simulations section is an overstatement.
*   Many mathematical expressions in the appendix use brackets that are too small, harming readability.

**Post-Rebuttal Update**

Following the author response, I have raised my recommendation to **Accept**. The authors have clarified the relationship between their lower bound and the work of Gadde et al., resolving the apparent contradiction.

However, I strongly maintain that the paper requires a thorough revision before publication. I wish to clarify that my initial borderline recommendation was driven by major concerns about technical clarity (e.g., the precise conditions for Theorem 2) and the paper's positioning relative to existing literature, not by minor grammatical issues. It is crucial that all theorems are stated with full precision, including all necessary assumptions (such as k being sufficiently large) in the theorem statements themselves, not just buried in the proofs.

I continue to urge the authors to carefully proofread and revise the manuscript to address the numerous writing and clarity issues raised in the initial review, as this will significantly improve the accessibility and impact of their work for the NIPS audience.
