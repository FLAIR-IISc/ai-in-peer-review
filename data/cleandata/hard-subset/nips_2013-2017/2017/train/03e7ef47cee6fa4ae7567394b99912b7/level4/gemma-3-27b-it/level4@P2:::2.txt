This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering with pairwise comparisons and random side-information. The work tackles a fundamental problem in interactive clustering and attempts to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis. While the contributions appear potentially valuable, the density of the paper and its writing style create challenges for thorough evaluation. My overall assessment is borderline acceptance, contingent on significant improvements to clarity and presentation.

Several related works, particularly in the area of generalized stochastic block models and active learning for community detection, were not cited. The generalization of the SBM to allow arbitrary distributions for pairwise relationships is not novel, with prior work (easily found via Google Scholar searches for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model") exploring similar ideas. Furthermore, the paper neglects "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016), which, while employing a different querying model, addresses a related problem with similar underlying principles. Consequently, the authors should revise statements asserting novelty or initiating a theoretical study in this area.

The paper's model and problem formulation could benefit from greater clarity. Specifically, it should explicitly state whether the analysis focuses on minimax bounds and whether it allows for arbitrary cluster size distributions, including highly unbalanced scenarios (e.g., one large cluster and many singletons). Theorem 2, as currently stated, seems potentially incorrect. For the standard SBM with a fixed number of clusters (k=2 or a small constant), clustering can often be achieved without any queries, particularly when the Hellinger distance between the distributions is small. The theorem's statement requires clarification regarding its dependence on *k*; it appears to implicitly assume *k* tends to infinity, which must be explicitly stated.

The experimental section lacks substantial value due to the absence of comparisons with existing methods and reliance on trivial baselines. A stronger evaluation would demonstrate the advantages of the proposed algorithms against relevant alternatives. The paper also contains numerous instances of unclear or awkward phrasing, such as "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound." Moreover, the writing is replete with grammatical errors, typos, and inconsistencies in style (capitalization, plurals, spacing, articles, comma usage, sentence beginnings). Addressing these issues is crucial for improving readability and impact.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper proposes information-theoretic bounds on the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information. These bounds potentially offer valuable insights into the trade-offs between query cost and accuracy.
*   **Novelty (to some extent):** While building on existing work, the paper extends previous models to allow for arbitrary distributions for pairwise relationships, offering a more general framework.
*   **Algorithm Development:** The authors propose algorithms that achieve query complexity close to the theoretical lower bounds.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Writing Clarity:** The paper is dense and poorly written, hindering comprehension and thorough evaluation.
*   **Lack of Related Work:** Important relevant works, particularly in generalized SBMs and active learning, are not cited.
*   **Potential Incorrectness:** Theorem 2’s generality is questionable and requires careful scrutiny and clarification of assumptions about *k*.
*   **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The numerical results lack sufficient comparison with existing methods and meaningful baselines.
*   **Grammatical Errors:** The pervasive grammatical errors and stylistic issues detract from the paper’s credibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contributions, if correct, are significant and advance our understanding of interactive clustering.
*   The proposed algorithms are promising and achieve near-optimal query complexity.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem with practical relevance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The writing quality is significantly below the standard expected for NIPS.
*   The lack of thorough engagement with related work raises concerns about the paper’s originality and context.
*   The potential issues with Theorem 2 require careful resolution.
*   The weak experimental evaluation limits the practical impact of the work.

**Recommendation:**

Borderline Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent on a substantial revision addressing the identified weaknesses. Specifically, the authors must:

1.  Thoroughly proofread and revise the paper for clarity, grammar, and style.
2.  Properly cite relevant related work, including the mentioned SBM extensions and active learning approaches.
3.  Carefully examine and clarify the assumptions and limitations of Theorem 2.
4.  Strengthen the experimental evaluation by comparing the proposed algorithms with existing methods and using more informative baselines.

I acknowledge that the authors have responded to my initial comments and clarified some points. However, the fundamental issues regarding writing quality and the need for a more thorough engagement with the existing literature remain. A careful revision is essential to elevate this paper to the standard expected for NIPS.
