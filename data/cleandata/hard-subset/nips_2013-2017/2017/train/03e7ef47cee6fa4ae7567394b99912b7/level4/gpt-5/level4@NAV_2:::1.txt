This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when “side information” is available as a similarity matrix W, where each entry Wij is drawn from f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The central claim is that, in the absence of side information, Θ(nk) pairwise same-cluster queries are needed, whereas with side information one can succeed with only O(k log n / H^2(f+‖f−)) queries, where H denotes the Hellinger distance; moreover, this bound is tight up to a logarithmic factor.

I view this as an interesting theoretical contribution for several reasons. It directly relates to the stochastic block model (SBM), an active area of recent research, and poses natural questions about how allowing queries shifts recovery thresholds. It demonstrates that side information can dramatically reduce the number of required queries—unsurprising in the extreme (since side information could, in principle, fully reveal the clustering), but the paper’s value lies in formalizing how to measure the usefulness of the similarity matrix via the Hellinger divergence.
