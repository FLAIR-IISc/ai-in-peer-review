This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering in the presence of side information, specifically a similarity matrix *W* where entries *W<sub>ij</sub>* are drawn from distributions *f<sup>+</sup>* (for points within the same cluster) and *f<sup>-</sup>* (for points in different clusters). The core contribution demonstrates a significant reduction in query requirements when leveraging this side information. The authors establish that, while Θ(*nk*) queries are needed for clustering without side information, only O(*k* log *n* / *H<sup>2</sup>*( *f<sup>+</sup>* || *f<sup>-</sup>* )) queries suffice with side information, where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance. They further prove that this bound is tight up to a log(*n*) factor.

I consider this a compelling theoretical advancement. The work is well-motivated by its connection to the extensively studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM) and explores the impact of query access on clustering thresholds – a nuanced and important question. Demonstrating the substantial benefits of side information on query complexity is a key finding. While the potential for side information to encode cluster structure is intuitive, the paper provides a rigorous framework for quantifying the effectiveness of the similarity matrix *W* and its influence on query efficiency.
