This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented with random side-information for each pair. While I have some familiarity with the related literature, my knowledge isn’t comprehensive, making a complete assessment of the contributions challenging. The density of the presentation also hindered a detailed correctness check, particularly of the lengthy upper bound proof.

My overall impression, shared with a related paper by the same authors, is that the contributions appear valuable (assuming correctness), but the writing requires significant improvement. While the strength of the contributions outweighs writing quality, the numerous issues pushed my initial recommendation to borderline. An additional review cycle to address these concerns would have been beneficial, but I understand NIPS constraints prevent this.

Several relevant works were not cited. The generalization of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) to a broader distribution within the stochastic block model (SBM) is not novel; a search for “labeled stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals existing research on similar generalizations. Furthermore, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) investigates the SBM with an active learning component, albeit with a different querying model. However, for fixed *k* and linear-sized communities, their querying approach is fundamentally comparable to pairwise querying. While an initial comparison suggested a contradiction with their sublinear-sample upper bound, the authors’ response clarified this depends on the scaling of *k*, which must be explicitly stated in the theorem statements. 

In light of these related works, I recommend the authors revise or remove statements asserting novelty—specifically, phrases like “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “This is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “There is no systematic theoretical study.”

Several points require further attention. The model and problem statement lack clarity. It appears the authors aim to establish minimax bounds, but this isn't explicitly stated. Is the model unrestricted regarding cluster sizes? Specifically, does it permit scenarios like *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size *n-k+1*? 

Theorem 2, as stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with small *k* (e.g., *k*=2), clustering can be achieved without querying in certain instances where the Hellinger distance scales as log(*n*)/ *n*. The proof appears to implicitly assume *k* → ∞, a condition not present in the theorem statement and absolutely essential for its validity.  The authors need to carefully verify whether Theorem 2 more generally contradicts cases where clustering is possible without any queries (*Q*=0).

The numerical section is not particularly insightful, lacking comparison to existing methods and relying solely on trivial baselines. Are there no other relevant algorithms that could be considered for implementation and comparison?

The paper contains numerous instances of unclear or confusing sentences, such as “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (appears incomplete) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (this lower bound applies only without side information).

Critically, the paper is replete with grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Issues include incorrect capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and article usage (a/an/the). Commas are consistently misused after phrases like “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” and “note that,” and rarely before equations. Sentences should not begin with “And,” “Or,” or “Plus.” The word “else” is often inappropriately used; “otherwise” is preferable. Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This is not an exhaustive list, and thorough proofreading is strongly recommended regardless of the final decision.

Minor comments include: the abstract is lengthy; spending three pages before presenting the contributions isn’t ideal; the usage of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” should be standard, and the mention of “Las Vegas” on page 4 should be accompanied by “average” query complexity; and footnote 1 should be checked for the extraneous “1-”. The statement "find Q subset of V x V" in the formal problem statement suggests a non-adaptive algorithm. I also disagree with the claim of “clearly exhibiting” in the Simulations section’s final sentence. Several brackets in the appendix are too small.
