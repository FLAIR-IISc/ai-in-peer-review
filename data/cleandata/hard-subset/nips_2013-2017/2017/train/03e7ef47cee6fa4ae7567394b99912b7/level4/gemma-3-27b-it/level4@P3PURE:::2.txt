NOTE: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors and on the same general topic. The other one is on noisy queries without any side information.
This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented with random side-information associated with each pair. While I am familiar with some of the related literature, my knowledge is not exhaustive, making a complete assessment of the contributions challenging. Furthermore, the paper’s density hindered a thorough correctness check, particularly of the lengthy upper bound proof.

My overall impression is that the contributions, if correct, are valuable. However, the writing requires substantial improvement. While I recognize that the contributions are paramount, the numerous issues significantly impact the clarity of the work and contribute to a borderline recommendation. A further round of review to verify suggested changes would have been beneficial, though I understand this is not possible within the NIPS review process.

Specifically, several relevant works were not cited:
- The generalization of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) to a general distribution within the stochastic block model (SBM) is not novel. A search for “labeled [or labelled] stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals existing research exploring similar generalizations.
- Furthermore, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016) studies the SBM with an active learning component, albeit with a different querying model (single node queries). However, for fixed *k* (e.g., *k*=2 or *k*=100) and linear-size communities, this is functionally equivalent to the pairwise querying model presented here – one can readily identify a node from each community via random sampling and then query new nodes in relation to those. While an apparent contradiction exists between the authors’ lower bound and Gadde et al.’s sublinear-sample upper bound, this may stem from the lower bound’s applicability as *k* approaches infinity (see below). 

In light of these existing works, I recommend the authors revise or remove statements implying novelty, such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes them," "This is the first work that…," "generalize them in a significant way," and "There is no systematic theoretical study."

Here are further comments:
- The model and problem statement should be clarified. The paper appears to explore minimax bounds, but this is not explicitly stated. Furthermore, does the minimax model impose any restrictions on cluster sizes? Specifically, does it allow for scenarios with *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size *n*-*k*+1?
- Theorem 2, as stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with *k*=2 (or generally small *k*), no queries are required in certain cases even with a Hellinger distance scaling as log(*n*)/ *n*. The proof seems to assume *k* → ∞, a condition not stated within the theorem itself (it absolutely needs to be). I am uncertain if this is the sole issue, but the authors should rigorously verify if Theorem 2 generally contradicts cases where clustering is possible even with *Q*=0.
- The numerical section lacks value due to the absence of comparisons with existing works; it only compares against trivial baselines. Are there no other methods that can be considered or implemented?
- Numerous sentences are confusing or unclear, such as "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" (is text missing after "assume"?) and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" (doesn’t that lower bound only apply in the absence of side information?).
- The paper contains a pervasive number of grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasings. Issues include incorrect capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and article usage (a/an/the). Commas are consistently misused (e.g., typically not required after statements like "let," "we have," "this means," "assume that," "since," "while," "note that," or immediately before an equation). Sentences should not begin with "And," "Or," or "Plus." The word "else" is frequently used inappropriately; "otherwise" is often a more suitable substitute. Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This is not an exhaustive list, and I strongly urge the authors to proofread the paper meticulously, regardless of the final decision.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is overly lengthy, and ideally, the paper should reach its core contributions more quickly.
- The terminology "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" should be standard; ensure clarity regarding their usage. Also, the first mention of "Las Vegas" on p4 should explicitly include "average query complexity" (the word 'average' is missing).
- Footnote 1: The “1-” after *p*= appears extraneous.
- The phrase "find Q subset of V x V" in the formal problem statement suggests a non-adaptive algorithm.
- I disagree with the statement "clearly exhibiting" in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Brackets throughout the appendix are frequently too small.
