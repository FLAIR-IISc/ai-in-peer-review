- Problem: studies query complexity of interactive clustering with pairwise queries and side information given by a similarity matrix drawn from two distributions (f+ for same-cluster, f- otherwise).  
- Main contributions: information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching algorithms; without side info complexity Θ(nk), with side info reduced to ~O(k²/H²(f+||f-)).  
- Algorithms: parameter-free, do not require knowledge of k, f+, or f-, using an iterative-update technique.  
- Novelty/connection: frames the model as a significant generalization of the Stochastic Block Model (SBM).  
- Strength: addresses an important, practically relevant gap between theory and heuristics in clustering with side information.  
- Strength: provides rigorous theoretical analysis with matching lower and upper bounds.  
- Strength: parameter-free algorithm design is practically valuable and potentially broadly useful.  
- Strength: insightful linkage to SBM that could spur further research.  
- Weakness: poor clarity and presentation—dense writing, many grammatical and stylistic errors hindering verification.  
- Weakness: incomplete positioning against related work—misses prior labeled/weighted SBM literature and active-learning-for-SBM (e.g., Gadde et al. ISIT 2016).  
- Weakness: imprecise theoretical statements—Theorem 2 appears to omit critical assumptions (e.g., k→∞) and the minimax/model assumptions (cluster size balance) are not clearly specified.  
- Weakness: experimental evaluation is weak, comparing only to simplistic baselines rather than existing heuristics.  
- For acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem and delivers substantial theoretical advances with useful parameter-free algorithms.  
- Against acceptance: suffers from very poor presentation, insufficient related-work discussion, imprecise main theorems, and weak experiments.  
- Recommendation: Accept with major revisions.  
- Required revisions: thoroughly rewrite for clarity and grammar; properly survey and cite related work (generalized SBMs, active SBM work); state all theorems/assumptions precisely (including conditions on k and cluster sizes); and (recommended) strengthen experimental comparisons.
