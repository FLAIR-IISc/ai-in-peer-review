This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using pairwise comparisons and associated random side-information. While I am somewhat familiar with the relevant literature, my knowledge isn't comprehensive, making a complete assessment of the contributions challenging. The density of the paper also hindered a thorough verification of the technical details, particularly the lengthy upper bound proof.

My overall impression, shared across the two related papers from these authors, is that the contributions appear valuable (assuming correctness), but the presentation requires substantial improvement. While technical merit is paramount, the numerous writing issues lead to a borderline recommendation. An additional review cycle to address these concerns would have been beneficial, though I recognize this is not possible within the NIPS process.

Specifically, several relevant works are missing from the citations: the generalization of the Bernoulli(p), Bernoulli(q) model to a more general distribution within the stochastic block model (SBM) – a search for “labeled stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” will reveal relevant papers – and Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016), which studies the SBM with an active learning component. Although their querying model differs, for fixed *k* and linear-size communities, it becomes comparable to the pairwise querying model presented here. The authors clarify that their lower bound’s dependency on *k* approaching infinity resolves any apparent contradiction with Gadde et al.’s sublinear-sample upper bound. 

In light of these related works, I recommend the authors carefully review and revise any statements implying novelty, such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” or “This is the first work that…”.

Further comments include: the model and problem formulation could benefit from clearer presentation, specifically explicitly stating whether minimax bounds are being considered and whether there are restrictions on cluster sizes (e.g., allowing highly imbalanced clusters). Theorem 2 appears incorrect as stated; for the SBM with small *k*, no queries may be required even with a Hellinger distance scaling as log(n)/n. The proof’s assumption of *k* tending to infinity should be explicitly stated in the theorem itself. The numerical section lacks value due to the absence of comparisons to existing methods, relying only on trivial baselines.  The paper contains several unclear sentences, such as “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound.” 

Finally, the paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing – issues with capitalization, plurals, spacing, articles, and comma usage are pervasive. Sentences should not begin with "And," "Or," or "Plus," and the use of "else" should be reviewed. Footnotes should be properly capitalized and punctuated. A thorough proofread is strongly encouraged, regardless of the final decision.

Minor comments: the abstract is lengthy, and the presentation of contributions is delayed. The terminology "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" should be used in a standard manner, with "average" explicitly included when first mentioning Las Vegas. Footnote 1 contains a potentially extraneous "1-". The phrasing "find Q subset of V x V" in the formal problem statement suggests a non-adaptive algorithm. The statement "clearly exhibiting" in the Simulations section is debatable.  Bracket sizes in the appendix are often too small.

[POST-AUTHOR FEEDBACK COMMENTS]

Following the authors’ responses, I have updated my recommendation to acceptance. However, a careful revision addressing the aforementioned writing concerns is still highly recommended. While I was the only reviewer to extensively comment on the writing style, I believe addressing these issues would improve the paper’s accessibility to a broader audience.

Specifically, the authors should not interpret the comments on capitalization and commas as decisive factors, but rather as suggestions for improvement. The clarification regarding the relationship to Gadde et al.’s work is appreciated. Precise statement of theorem and lemma conditions—specifically, explicitly stating the dependence on *k* for results applicable only to large *k*—is crucial. Finally, continued attention to issues like the use of "Else," "And," "Or," "Plus," and the sentence structure “assume, otherwise…” is recommended, even if the authors disagree with my assessment.
