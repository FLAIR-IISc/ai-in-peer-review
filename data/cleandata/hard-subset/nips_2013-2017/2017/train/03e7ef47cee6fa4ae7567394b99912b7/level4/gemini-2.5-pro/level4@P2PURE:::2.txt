This review pertains to one of two related submissions by the same authors on clustering with pairwise queries. The other submission addresses noisy queries without side information.

The paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity for clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented with random side information for each pair. While I have some familiarity with the related literature, my expertise is not exhaustive, which makes a definitive assessment of the contribution's novelty challenging. The paper's density, particularly in the lengthy upper bound proof, precluded a thorough verification of all technical details within the time available.

My overall impression is that the paper's contributions, if correct, are valuable. However, the quality of the writing is a significant concern and is the primary reason for my borderline recommendation. While technical contributions are paramount, the numerous clarity and grammatical issues detract from the work. A revision cycle to address these points would be highly beneficial, though I recognize this is not part of the NIPS review process.

Regarding the paper's content, it fails to cite several highly relevant works:
- The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) to include arbitrary distributions instead of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) has been explored previously. Searches for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" yield several related papers.
- The problem of SBM with an active learning component has also been studied. For instance, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) investigates a model with a different querying mechanism (querying a node for its label). However, for a fixed number of communities (k) and linear-sized communities, their model is functionally similar to the pairwise querying model presented here. A potential contradiction between their sublinear-sample upper bound and this paper's lower bound might be resolved by the fact that this paper's lower bound appears to hold only as k approaches infinity.

In light of this prior art, claims such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes them," "This is the first work that...," and "There is no systematic theoretical study" should be removed or substantially rephrased to accurately reflect the state of the field.

Further comments:
- The problem formulation needs to be clarified. The paper appears to target minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. Furthermore, it is unclear if the model imposes any restrictions on cluster sizes (e.g., are highly unbalanced clusters with k-1 singletons permitted?).
- Theorem 2 appears incorrect as stated. For the SBM with a small, constant k (e.g., k=2), it is known that clustering is achievable with zero queries (Q=0) in regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n, which seems to contradict the theorem. The proof's reliance on the assumption that k approaches infinity is not stated in the theorem itself, but it is a critical condition and must be included. Please carefully verify that the theorem, even with this condition, does not contradict other known results where clustering is possible with Q=0.
- The numerical experiments are of limited value as they only compare against simple baselines and not against any existing methods from the literature. The authors should justify why their proposed methods are the only ones considered or implemented for comparison.
- Several sentences are confusing or incomplete. For example, "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" seems to be missing text. Another example, "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound," is unclear, as that lower bound appears to apply only to the case without side information.
- The manuscript is replete with grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Common issues include incorrect capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and use of articles (a/an/the). Commas are frequently misused (e.g., appearing after phrases like "we have" or "note that"). Sentences often begin with conjunctions like "And" or "Or," and the word "else" is used where "otherwise" would be more appropriate. Footnotes lack proper capitalization and punctuation. This list is not exhaustive; the paper requires thorough proofreading.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is overly long, and the main contributions are not presented until page 3, which delays reader engagement.
- Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology standard in this context? When "Las Vegas" is first introduced on page 4, it should be explicitly linked to *average* query complexity.
- Footnote 1: The "1-" in "p=1-..." seems erroneous. Please confirm.
- In the formal problem statement, the phrase "find Q subset of V x V" implies a non-adaptive algorithm, which may not be the intended meaning.
- The claim of "clearly exhibiting" in the final sentence of the Simulations section seems overstated.
- Many mathematical expressions in the appendix use brackets that are too small for their contents.
