This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering in the presence of “side information,” formalized as a similarity matrix **W**. The entry *W<sub>ij</sub>* is drawn from a distribution dependent on whether points *i* and *j* belong to the same cluster (distribution *f<sub>+</sub>*) or different clusters (*f<sub>-</sub>*). The authors establish a key result: while clustering without side information requires Θ(*nk*) queries, incorporating the similarity matrix **W** reduces the query complexity to O(*k* log *n* / *H<sup>2</sup>*( *f<sub>+</sub>* || *f<sub>-</sub>* )), where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance between the two distributions. Furthermore, they demonstrate that this query complexity is, up to a log(*n*) factor, asymptotically optimal.

I find this a compelling theoretical contribution for several reasons. The work is closely connected to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a well-studied area of recent research, yet it frames a novel question concerning the impact of allowing queries on clustering thresholds. Specifically, the paper clearly demonstrates how leveraging side information can substantially reduce the number of queries needed for effective clustering. While the benefit of side information – potentially containing complete cluster membership information – isn't inherently surprising, the paper's significant contribution lies in quantifying the *quality* of the similarity matrix **W** needed to achieve this reduction in query complexity, via the Hellinger distance. 

The connection to SBM is particularly well-articulated and highlights the novelty of the query setting. The authors don't simply apply existing SBM results; they adapt the framework to specifically address the impact of queries, showcasing a good understanding of both areas. The tightness result further strengthens the contribution, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the observed reduction in query complexity.
