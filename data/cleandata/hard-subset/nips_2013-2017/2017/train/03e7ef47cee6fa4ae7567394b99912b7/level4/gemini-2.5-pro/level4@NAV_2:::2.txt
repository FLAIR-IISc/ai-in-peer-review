This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering, where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether two items belong to the same cluster. The main contribution is to characterize how access to a noisy similarity matrix (side information) can dramatically reduce the number of queries needed. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds from efficient, parameter-free algorithms. The query complexity is shown to decrease from Θ(nk) without side information to a quantity dependent on the squared Hellinger divergence between the similarity distributions for same-cluster and different-cluster pairs. This work connects to the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM), as the side information model can be seen as a significant generalization of the SBM from Bernoulli distributions to arbitrary ones.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Motivation:** The problem is well-motivated by practical applications like entity resolution, where human-in-the-loop systems are common. Formally analyzing the benefit of machine-generated similarity scores on the cost of human annotation is an important and impactful research direction.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing both upper and lower bounds on the query complexity. Characterizing the complexity reduction in terms of the Hellinger divergence between the underlying distributions is an elegant and insightful result.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require knowledge of the number of clusters (k) or the underlying distributions (f+, f-), which is a significant practical advantage and makes them more applicable to real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Positioning and Related Work:** The paper's claims of novelty seem overstated in light of existing literature.
    *   The generalization of the SBM to arbitrary distributions is not entirely new; related ideas have been explored in works on the "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model."
    *   The combination of SBMs with active querying has also been studied. For instance, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) explores a related setting. While their query model is different (node labels vs. pairwise), for a fixed number of linear-sized communities, the models are closely related. The authors should carefully position their contribution relative to such works and moderate claims like "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study" or "This is the first work that...".

*   **Correctness of Main Lower Bound (Theorem 2):** There appears to be a potential issue with the statement of Theorem 2. For the standard SBM with constant k (e.g., k=2), it is known that exact recovery is possible with zero queries even in regimes where the Hellinger distance is small (e.g., scaling as log(n)/n). The theorem as stated seems to contradict this. It is possible that the proof relies on an unstated assumption, perhaps that k grows with n or is sufficiently large. If so, this condition must be made explicit in the theorem statement for it to be correct.

*   **Clarity of the Model:** The problem setup could be defined more precisely. It is not explicitly stated whether the analysis is for a minimax framework over all possible clusterings. In particular, the bounds should clarify any assumptions made on cluster sizes (e.g., are highly imbalanced clusters with k-1 singletons permitted?).

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The numerical section provides limited insight. The experiments are conducted only against trivial baselines rather than comparing with any existing or competing methods. This makes it difficult to assess the practical performance and advantages of the proposed algorithms.

*   **Clarity of Writing:** The paper's quality is significantly hindered by widespread grammatical errors, typos, and unconventional phrasing. Issues with capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and misuse of commas are frequent. Sentences often begin with "And" or "Plus," and the phrasing can be confusing (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done"). This lack of polish makes the paper difficult to read and obscures the technical contributions. A thorough proofreading and revision by a native English speaker is strongly recommended.

**Minor Comments:**
*   The introduction feels overly long, taking nearly three pages to state the main contributions.
*   The problem statement "find Q ⊆ V × V" suggests a non-adaptive algorithm, which seems to contradict the interactive setting described. This should be clarified.
*   In Footnote 1, the "1-" after "p=" appears to be a typo.

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem and presents potentially valuable theoretical results. However, the recommendation is borderline due to serious concerns regarding the correctness of a main theorem, significant omissions in the discussion of related work, and the overall poor quality of the writing. If the technical concerns can be thoroughly addressed and the paper is carefully revised, it could represent a solid contribution.
