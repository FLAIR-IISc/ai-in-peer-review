

# Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information, where an oracle answers pairwise queries about whether elements belong to the same cluster, and a similarity matrix provides side information about pairwise relationships. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on the number of queries required to recover the optimal clustering. Their key insight is that side information drastically reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k² log n / H²(f⁺‖f⁻)), where H² denotes the squared Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity values within and across clusters.

The paper builds on the stochastic block model (SBM), a well-studied framework for community detection, by generalizing it to arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻ for within-cluster and between-cluster similarity values. While SBM has been extensively studied with binary edge probabilities, this work considers general probability distributions, which represents a meaningful extension. The authors' results also connect to practical applications in entity resolution, where similarity matrices generated from data attributes guide interactive clustering with human annotators.

**Quality**: The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical analysis. The information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorem 2) and algorithms (Theorem 1) are well-motivated and properly proved. However, the lower bound statement requires clarification regarding its asymptotic regime (it holds for sufficiently large k, which should be explicitly stated in the theorem). The authors' claim that "this is the first work" to systematically study interactive clustering with side information is inaccurate given prior work on SBM with general distributions and active learning (e.g., Gadde et al., ISIT 2016, which studies a related active learning model for community detection). The experimental results on the Cora dataset are reasonable but lack comparison against existing methods, limiting their significance.

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The writing is dense and contains numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent punctuation, and awkward phrasing (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound"). The use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" algorithms is standard, but the paper fails to explicitly state that the Las Vegas algorithm has "expected" query complexity. The formal problem statement incorrectly suggests non-adaptive queries. The presentation of the main results could be substantially improved with clearer organization and more precise mathematical statements.

**Originality**: The core contribution—characterizing how side information reduces query complexity—is novel and significant. The authors establish a general framework for proving lower bounds in interactive settings that could be valuable for other classification problems. However, the paper overstates its novelty by claiming to "initiate a rigorous theoretical study" and "significantly generalize" prior work on SBM with general distributions, which has been explored in other contexts. The connection to SBM is valuable, but the paper should more precisely acknowledge prior work on generalizing SBM.

**Significance**: The results have clear practical significance for applications like entity resolution where human input is expensive. The ability to reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) to nearly optimal bounds with side information represents an important step toward efficient interactive clustering. The parameter-free algorithms are particularly valuable for real-world applications where cluster sizes and distributions are unknown. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of comparison against existing heuristics in experiments.

**Strengths**:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with tight bounds
- Parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f⁺, or f⁻
- Connection to practical applications in entity resolution
- Generalization of SBM to arbitrary distributions
- Insightful lower bound framework for interactive learning

**Weaknesses**:
- Overstated novelty claims regarding prior work
- Significant writing and grammatical issues throughout
- Experimental section lacks comparison against existing methods
- Theorem 2 requires clarification about its asymptotic regime
- The paper does not clearly distinguish between the minimax model and other settings

The paper makes an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The technical results are sound, and the parameter-free algorithms are practically valuable. However, the writing needs substantial improvement, and the claims about novelty should be carefully revised to acknowledge prior work. The paper would benefit from a thorough rewrite to clarify its contributions relative to existing literature, fix grammatical issues, and improve the presentation of results.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors must make significant revisions to address the clarity issues and revise the claims about novelty. The technical content is strong, but the paper's impact would be significantly enhanced by a more precise and well-written presentation. The reviewers' concerns about the writing quality should be taken seriously, as they will affect the paper's accessibility and impact on the broader NIPS community.
