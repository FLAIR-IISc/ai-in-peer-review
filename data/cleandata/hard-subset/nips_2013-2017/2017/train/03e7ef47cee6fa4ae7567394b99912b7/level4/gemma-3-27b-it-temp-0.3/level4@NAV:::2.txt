This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented with random side-information for each pair. While I possess some familiarity with the related literature, my knowledge isn’t comprehensive, making a complete assessment of the contributions challenging. The density of the paper also limited my ability to thoroughly verify the correctness of all details, particularly within the extensive upper bound proof.

My overall impression, shared across both papers from these authors, is that the contributions appear valuable (assuming correctness) but require substantial improvement in presentation. While the technical contributions are paramount, the numerous writing issues raise concerns, leading to a borderline recommendation. An additional review cycle to address these points would be beneficial, though I recognize this isn’t feasible within the NIPS review process.

Specifically regarding this paper, several relevant works were not cited: 
- The generalization of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) distributions to a more general distribution within the stochastic block model (SBM) is not novel. A search for “labeled stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals existing research exploring similar generalizations.
- Furthermore, work exists on the SBM incorporating active learning: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016). Though employing a different querying model (single node label queries), for fixed *k* and linear-size communities, this is functionally equivalent to the pairwise querying model presented here – simply sample nodes from each community and use pairwise queries for new nodes. While an initial comparison suggests a potential contradiction with the authors’ lower bound, this may resolve if the lower bound’s applicability is limited to *k* approaching infinity (see below).

In light of these related works, I recommend the authors revise or remove statements asserting novelty, such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “This is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “There is no systematic theoretical study.”

Further comments include:
- The model and problem statement could benefit from greater clarity. The paper appears to focus on minimax bounds, but this isn’t explicitly stated. Moreover, are there restrictions on cluster sizes within the minimax model? Specifically, is a configuration with *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size *n-k+1* permitted?
- Theorem 2, as currently stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with a fixed *k* (or any *k* = O(1)), clustering can be achieved without queries in certain cases where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The proof seems to implicitly assume *k* → ∞, a condition not stated in the theorem itself (and which is crucial). I urge careful verification to ensure Theorem 2 doesn’t generally contradict scenarios where clustering is possible with Q=0.
- The numerical section lacks substantial value as it doesn't compare against existing methods, only trivial baselines. Are there no other viable methods for comparison or implementation?
- Several sentences are confusing or unclear, such as “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is text missing?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound require the absence of side information?).
- The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Issues with capitalization, plurals, spacing, articles, and comma usage are pervasive. Sentences should not begin with “And,” “Or,” or “Plus.” The word “else” is frequently misused; “otherwise” is often more appropriate. Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a period. This list is not exhaustive, and I strongly recommend a thorough proofread regardless of the final decision.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is lengthy, and the paper spends considerable space before presenting its core contributions.
- The use of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” terminology should be verified for standard usage. When “Las Vegas” is first introduced on page 4, it should be explicitly linked to AVERAGE query complexity (the word ‘average’ is missing).
- Footnote 1: The “1-” preceding *p* is questionable.
- The phrasing “find Q subset of V x V” in the formal problem statement suggests a non-adaptive algorithm.
- I disagree with the “clearly exhibiting” claim in the final sentence of the Simulations section.
- Brackets in the appendix are consistently too small.
