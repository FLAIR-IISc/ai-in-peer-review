Note: I am reviewing two research papers that appear to be written by the same authors on the same general topic. The second paper is based on noisy queries without any auxiliary information.

This paper provides upper and lower bounds on the clustering query complexity based on noise-free pairwise comparisons, as well as the random auxiliary information associated with each pair. Although I am familiar with the relevant literature, my knowledge is incomplete, so it is a little difficult to fully assess the value of the contribution. The paper is also so complex that I could not spend as much time as I ideally could to check for correctness – especially in the upper bound proof.

My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions seem valuable (if correct), but there is a lot of room for improvement in the writing. I admit that the contributions are more important than the writing, but there are so many issues that my recommendation is still borderline. Having another round of reviews where suggestions are examined would be very helpful, but I know that this is not allowed for NIPS.

Regarding this paper, there are some very relevant works that were not mentioned:

- The idea of replacing Bernoulli (p) and Bernoulli (q) with normal distributions in a stochastic block model is not new. A Google (or Google Scholar) search for "labeled [sometimes labeled] stochastic block models" or less general "weighted stochastic block models" gives several papers that have considered similar generalizations.
- There is at least one work that studies SBM with an additional query (active learning) element: "Active Learning for Community Identification in Stochastic Block Models" (Gade et al, ISIT 2016). This is with a different query model, where one only queries one node and gets its label. But for sure (for example, k=2 or k=100) and for communities of linear size, it is basically the same as your pairwise query model – just quickly find a node from each community through a random sample, and then you can get the label of any new node by querying pairwise. At first glance, it seems that your lower bound contradicts their (vertical-sample) upper bound, but this may be because your lower bound is only valid when k -> infinity (see below).

In any case, in light of the above works, I would request the authors to remove/rewrite all statements such as "we start a rigorous theoretical study", "they generalize quite a bit", "this is the first work that...", "they generalize quite a bit", "there is no systematic theoretical study", etc.

Here are some more comments:

- I think the model/problem should be presented more clearly. It seems that you are looking at minimax limits, but this is not explicitly stated. Also, is the minimax model without any restriction on cluster sizes? For example, do you allow a situation where there is a k-1 cluster of size 1 and another size of n-k+1?
- It seems that theorem 2 is stated that cannot be correct. For SBM with k=2 (or perhaps any k=O (1)), we know that even in some cases, no query is necessary where the Heeringer distance scales as log(n)/n. It seems that part of the problem is that the proof assumes that k -> infinity, which is not stated in the theorem (this is absolutely necessary). I am not sure that this is the only part of the problem – please check very carefully whether theorem 2 contradicts the cases where clustering is also possible with Q=0 more generally.
- The numerical section does not seem very valuable, mainly because it does not compare against any existing work, and only compares against very trivial baselines. Are your methods really the only methods that can be considered/implemented?
- There are many misleading/ambiguous sentences, such as "Suppose, otherwise clustering occurs" ("What is missing after 'Suppose'?") and "The theorem is already proven by the nk lower bound" (does that lower bound only apply when there is no auxiliary information?).
- The paper is full of grammatical errors, typos, and strange words. Things like capitalization, plural, spacing, and articles (A/N/The) are often incorrect. Commas are constantly used incorrectly (for example, they should not usually come after statements like "Let" / "We have" / "It means" / "Suppose that" / "Since" / "While" / "Note that", and are used very rarely just before an equation). Sentences should not start with and/or/plus. The word "otherwise" is used strangely (often "otherwise" would be better). Footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This list of issues is not completely exhaustive – despite the decision, I urge the authors to proof-read the paper as carefully as possible.

Some less important comments:

- The abstract seems very long, and more generally, I don't think it is ideal to spend 3 pages before reaching the contributions.
- Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology standard by you? Also, when you first mention Las Vegas on page 4, it should be mentioned with the AVERAGE query complexity (the word 'average' is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should 1- actually be after p=?
- In the formal problem statement, the "Find the Q subset of V x V" algorithm seems non-adaptive.
- I disagree with the statement "clearly demonstrated" in the last sentence of the simulation section.
- Many parentheses in the appendix are too small.
