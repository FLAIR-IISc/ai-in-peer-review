NOTE: I am reviewing two papers by (likely) the same authors on closely related topics. The other paper treats noisy queries without side information.

Summary
This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering when the learner can interact with an oracle that answers noiseless same-cluster queries and also has access to pairwise “side information” drawn from unknown distributions f+ (within-cluster) and f− (across-cluster). The main results are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds showing that side information can reduce query complexity from Θ(nk) (without side information) to roughly Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}), where H^2 is the squared Hellinger divergence between f+ and f−.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms: a Monte Carlo method with query complexity roughly O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) and a Las Vegas method with O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}) queries, achieving exact recovery with high probability or probability 1, respectively.
The work highlights connections to the stochastic block model (SBM), where Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) are a special case of f+/f−, and argues that side information (weighted/labeled edges) can dramatically lower the number of required queries.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practically motivated problem (interactive clustering with side information) with clean, information-theoretic analysis.
- Provides nearly matching upper and lower bounds (up to log factors) and parameter-free algorithms that do not assume knowledge of k, f+, or f−.
- The lower-bound framework for interactive settings may be of independent interest, and the link to SBM is conceptually useful.
- The algorithms are computationally efficient (near O(n^2)) and emphasize practical design (iterative updates based on learned distributions).

Weaknesses
- Exposition is dense and difficult to follow; theorem statements and assumptions (asymptotic regimes, cluster-size conditions) need to be made precise.
- Related work is incomplete, particularly on labeled/weighted SBMs and active learning for SBM.
- The empirical section is limited and lacks comparisons with existing methods or stronger baselines.
- Some statements overclaim novelty; language should be toned down.
- Numerous grammatical errors and stylistic issues impede readability.

Relation to prior work and missing citations
- Generalizing SBM from Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) to weighted or labeled edges is well-studied under “labeled (labelled) SBM” and “weighted SBM.” Please cite representative works from this literature and clarify how your results differ (assumptions, regimes, guarantees).
- Active learning in SBM has been studied (e.g., Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Their querying model differs (single-node label queries), but for fixed k and linear-size communities the models are closely related. Please discuss this connection and clearly delineate regimes to avoid perceived contradictions.

Modeling and clarity
- Please state explicitly whether results are minimax and what constraints (if any) are imposed on cluster sizes. Do you allow highly imbalanced partitions, e.g., k−1 singleton clusters and one cluster of size n−k+1?
- Specify the asymptotic regimes in each theorem (e.g., k fixed, k→∞, balanced vs. adversarial sizes). This is essential for interpreting the bounds and for consistency with known SBM thresholds.

Comments on Theorem 2
- As written, Theorem 2 appears to conflict with known zero-query recovery results in SBM for constant k under certain (sparse) regimes where H^2 scales like log n/n. The proof seems to rely on k→∞ effects; if so, this must be stated in the theorem and discussion. Please carefully check that Theorem 2 does not contradict cases with Q=0 and exact recovery for small (constant) k.

Empirical evaluation
- The experiments use one dataset and compare against trivial strategies only. Please add comparisons to existing interactive/crowdsourcing heuristics or relevant SBM-based methods, and evaluate sensitivity to k, imbalance, and H^2. Otherwise, the empirical evidence is too thin to substantiate practical gains.

Writing and presentation
- Many sentences are unclear (e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done”; “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound”). Please revise for clarity and correctness.
- Grammar and style need significant editing: articles, plurals, capitalization, spacing, and punctuation (especially commas) are often incorrect; avoid starting sentences with And/Or/Plus; prefer “otherwise” to “else” in prose; ensure footnotes start with a capital letter and end with a period. Tighten the abstract (it is too long) and reduce front matter before contributions.

Minor comments
- Consider whether “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” are the most informative labels here. When introducing Las Vegas, mention average query complexity explicitly.
- Footnote 1: Please check whether the “1−” after p= is intended.
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V×V” reads as non-adaptive; clarify that queries may be adaptive.
- In the simulations, avoid strong phrasing such as “clearly exhibiting” without stronger evidence.
- Several brackets/parentheses are too small in the appendix; check typesetting.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually important question; rigorous lower bounds and practical, parameter-free algorithms.
- Nearly matching bounds suggest a tight understanding of the role of side information.
- Potentially broad impact on interactive learning and community detection.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete related work and overstatements of novelty.
- Theorems and assumptions not always stated precisely; risk of confusion with known SBM results.
- Limited empirical validation.
- Substantial writing and grammar issues hinder accessibility.
