

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity for clustering when provided with side information in the form of a similarity matrix. The authors consider the problem of clustering n elements into k unknown clusters using an oracle that answers pairwise "same cluster" queries, with the additional side information of a similarity matrix W where W_ij is drawn from f+ if i and j belong to the same cluster and from f- otherwise. The main contribution is establishing that without side information, the query complexity is Θ(nk), while with side information, it reduces to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H denotes the Hellinger divergence. The authors prove that this bound is information-theoretically tight within an O(log n) factor.

The paper connects this work to the stochastic block model (SBM), a well-studied model for community detection. The authors demonstrate that their model generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- rather than restricting to Bernoulli distributions, and by not requiring assumptions about cluster sizes or the number of clusters k. This connection is particularly valuable as it reveals how the addition of queries affects the recovery threshold in SBM, a question that has received significant attention in recent years.

The theoretical contributions are strong. The authors develop a novel framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in the interactive setting, which is challenging due to the adaptive nature of the queries. They also design efficient, parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-. The algorithms are computationally efficient (O(n²) time complexity) and achieve query complexity nearly matching the lower bound.

The paper's practical relevance is well-motivated through a discussion of entity resolution, where human input is used to improve clustering accuracy. The authors cite recent works in crowdsourced clustering that use similarity matrices to guide query selection, but note that these heuristics lack theoretical guarantees. This paper fills that gap by providing rigorous theoretical bounds for the query complexity when side information is available.

The writing is generally clear, though the technical depth of the lower bound proof (Section 2) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with information-theoretic techniques. The experimental results on the Cora dataset are limited to one dataset, though they demonstrate that the algorithm performs well even when using approximate distributions for f+ and f-.

Strengths:
- Theoretical rigor with strong lower and upper bounds
- Novel connection between interactive clustering and SBM
- Practical relevance to entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications
- Parameter-free algorithms that are important for real-world applications
- Clear exposition of the relationship between side information quality and query complexity

Weaknesses:
- The connection to practical applications could be strengthened with more examples
- The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset
- The paper could provide more intuition for why Hellinger distance is the appropriate measure
- The lower bound proof, while technically sound, might benefit from a more intuitive explanation

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a well-motivated problem
- The results provide fundamental insights into how side information affects clustering complexity
- The work connects to an active area of research (SBM) and opens new directions for future work
- The algorithms are efficient and parameter-free, addressing a practical need
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical depth

Arguments against acceptance:
- The experimental section is limited and could better demonstrate the algorithm's practical utility
- The connection to real-world applications could be expanded to include more specific examples
- The paper could provide more intuition about why Hellinger distance is the right measure for this problem

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information. The results are significant because they establish a precise relationship between the quality of side information (as measured by Hellinger divergence) and the reduction in query complexity. The connection to SBM is particularly valuable as it bridges a gap between theoretical community detection and practical interactive clustering. The parameter-free algorithms are especially noteworthy for their practical applicability, as they do not require prior knowledge of k or the underlying distributions f+ and f-.

The paper would be strengthened by more extensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and a clearer explanation of why Hellinger distance is the appropriate measure for this problem. However, these are minor issues that do not detract from the paper's significant theoretical contributions.

I recommend acceptance as the paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field of theoretical machine learning with strong technical results that advance our understanding of interactive clustering. The work provides both theoretical insights and practical algorithms that are likely to be influential in future research on clustering with side information.
