NOTE: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors and on the same general topic. The other paper is on noisy queries without any side information.

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with noiseless pairwise comparisons and random side information associated with each pair, providing upper and lower bounds. While I am familiar with some of the related literature, my knowledge is not exhaustive, and the paper is dense enough that I could not devote as much time as ideal to checking correctness—particularly for the long upper-bound proof.

Overall, my impression of both papers is that the contributions seem valuable (if correct), but the writing needs substantial improvement. I acknowledge that contributions matter more than presentation, yet the number of writing issues leaves my recommendation borderline. A further review round to verify revisions would have been very useful, but I understand this is not permitted at NIPS.

Related work and claims of novelty:
- The idea of replacing Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) in the stochastic block model (SBM) by a general distribution is not new. Searching for “labeled/labelled stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals several papers that consider similar generalizations.
- There is prior work on SBM with an active querying element: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Their querying model is different—one queries a single node and obtains its label—but for fixed k (e.g., k = 2 or k = 100) and linear-size communities, this is effectively equivalent to your pairwise querying model: one can first identify a representative from each community via random sampling, then determine the label of any new node by performing pairwise queries to those representatives. At first glance, your lower bound appears to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound, but this may stem from your lower bound only holding as k → ∞ (see below).
- In light of the above, please remove or reword statements such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “this is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “there is no systematic theoretical study.”

Major comments:
- Clarity of the model: Please present the problem more clearly. It appears you are proving minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. Furthermore, is the minimax setup unrestricted with respect to cluster sizes? For example, do you allow k − 1 clusters of size 1 and the remaining cluster of size n − k + 1?
- Theorem 2 as stated appears incorrect. For the SBM with k = 2 (and likely any k = O(1)), we know that no queries are required in certain regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. Part of the issue seems to be that the proof implicitly assumes k → ∞, which is not stated in the theorem; this must be made explicit. Please also check carefully whether Theorem 2 more generally contradicts cases where accurate clustering is possible even with Q = 0.
- Experimental evaluation: The numerical section is of limited value because it does not compare against existing methods and only uses very trivial baselines. Are your methods really the only reasonable approaches that can be implemented in this setting? If not, please include comparisons.
- Writing quality: The paper contains numerous issues in grammar, typography, and style. Examples include capitalization, plurals, spacing, and article usage (a/an/the). Commas are frequently misplaced (they generally should not follow “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that,” and are rarely needed immediately before an equation). Avoid starting sentences with “And,” “Or,” or “Plus.” The word “else” is used in nonstandard ways; “otherwise” is usually more appropriate. Footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a period. This list is not exhaustive; I strongly urge thorough proofreading regardless of the decision.
- Clarity issues: Some sentences are confusing or incomplete, e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is text missing after “assume”?), and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound apply only when there is no side information?).

Minor comments:
- The abstract seems overly long, and, more broadly, it takes three pages before the contributions are stated.
- Is your use of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” standard? When Las Vegas is first mentioned (p. 4), it should be explicitly tied to average query complexity (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” really appear after “p =”?
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V × V” makes the algorithm sound non-adaptive.
- I disagree with the phrase “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets are too small throughout the appendix.

Specific follow-ups:

- It is essential that theorems and lemmas be stated precisely. For example, if a result only holds for sufficiently large k, this must be stated in the theorem itself, not only in the proof.

These usages are generally ungrammatical or unsuitable for an academic paper (even if one could argue they are not strictly incorrect).
