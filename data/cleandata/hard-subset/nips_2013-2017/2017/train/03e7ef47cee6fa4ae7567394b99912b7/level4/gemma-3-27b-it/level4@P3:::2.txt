This paper gives upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, along with random side-information associated with every pair. The authors present a rigorous theoretical study of this interactive clustering problem, demonstrating the significant impact of side information on reducing query complexity. While the contributions appear valuable, the density of the paper and its writing style present challenges to thorough evaluation. My recommendation is acceptance, but strongly encourages significant revisions to improve clarity and polish.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning – efficient clustering with limited labeled data – and provides both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is insightful, and the parameter-free algorithms are a notable strength. However, several related works were not cited, including research on labeled and weighted SBMs, and “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016). This oversight weakens claims of novelty. The authors should carefully re-evaluate statements emphasizing the pioneering nature of their work, replacing phrases like "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study" and "significantly generalizes them" with more nuanced language.

The model and problem statement could be clearer. The paper focuses on minimax bounds, but this isn't explicitly stated. The range of permissible cluster size distributions should also be clarified – specifically, does the model allow for highly imbalanced clusters (e.g., k-1 clusters of size 1 and one of size n-k+1)? 

Theorem 2, as initially stated, appears problematic. Known results for the SBM with a constant number of communities (k=O(1)) suggest that clustering can often be achieved with zero queries under certain conditions (e.g., sufficient separation in edge probabilities). The authors must explicitly state any assumptions on *k* (e.g., *k* approaches infinity) or carefully revise the theorem to avoid contradiction.

The experimental section lacks meaningful comparison to existing methods, relying primarily on trivial baselines. Demonstrating the practical benefit of the proposed algorithms relative to state-of-the-art approaches would significantly strengthen the paper.

The writing throughout the paper is dense and contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. These issues impede understanding and detract from the overall quality of the presentation. Specific examples include incorrect capitalization, improper comma usage, and unclear sentence structure ("assume, otherwise the clustering is done"). Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop. Careful proofreading and editing are essential.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information.
*   **Information-Theoretic Bounds:** The derived upper and lower bounds are significant contributions to the field.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of algorithms that do not require knowledge of underlying parameters (k, f+, f-) is a practical advantage.
*   **Connection to SBM:** The link to the well-studied stochastic block model provides valuable context and potential for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Writing Quality:** Numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing hinder clarity.
*   **Limited Related Work:** Key related works, particularly those on labeled/weighted SBMs and active learning for community detection, are missing from the citations.
*   **Potential Issues with Theorem 2:** The stated theorem may be incorrect or require clarification regarding the behavior of k.
*   **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section lacks meaningful comparisons to existing methods.
*   **Density and Clarity:** The paper is dense and challenging to follow, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the specific subfield.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contributions are valuable and potentially impactful.
*   The parameter-free algorithms address a practical challenge in clustering.
*   The connection to the SBM provides a strong foundation for further research.
*   The authors have responded to feedback and clarified some issues.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The numerous writing issues significantly detract from the quality of the presentation.
*   The lack of thorough comparison to related work weakens the claims of novelty.
*   The potential issues with Theorem 2 raise concerns about the correctness of the theoretical analysis.



**Overall Recommendation:** Accept with Major Revisions. I strongly urge the authors to address the writing issues and carefully revise the paper based on the feedback provided. While the theoretical contributions are promising, the current presentation significantly limits the paper's accessibility and impact. A careful revision is essential to fully realize the potential of this work.
