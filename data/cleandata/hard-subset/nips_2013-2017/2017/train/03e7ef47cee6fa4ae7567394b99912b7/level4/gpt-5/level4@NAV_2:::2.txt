NOTE: I am reviewing two submissions that appear to be from the same authors and on closely related topics. The companion paper addresses noisy queries without side information.

This paper studies the query complexity of exact clustering using noiseless pairwise same-cluster queries, supplemented by random pairwise side information, and provides information-theoretic lower bounds along with nearly matching upper bounds. I am familiar with parts of the literature but not all of it, and the paper is dense enough that I could not devote as much time as ideal to checking all details—especially the lengthy upper-bound proof.

My overall take on both papers is that, if correct, the contributions are meaningful; however, the exposition needs significant work. I recognize that technical contributions are paramount, but there are enough presentation issues that my initial recommendation was borderline. A second review round to verify revisions would have helped, although I understand NIPS does not allow this.

For this paper specifically, several relevant works are missing:
- Extending SBM from Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) to general edge-weight/label distributions is not new. A search for “labeled/labelled stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals numerous prior works with similar generalizations.
- There is at least one paper on SBM with an additional querying (active learning) component: Gadde et al., “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (ISIT 2016). Their query model reveals a single node’s label, which differs from pairwise queries, but with fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-size communities, the two models are essentially equivalent: one can first identify a seed from each community via random sampling, and then classify new nodes via one pairwise query per seed. At first glance, your lower bound might appear to contradict their sublinear sample upper bound; this likely stems from your lower bound effectively requiring k → ∞ (see below).

In light of these, I suggest removing or softening claims such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “this is the first work that...,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “there is no systematic theoretical study.”

Additional comments:
- Clarify the problem/model statement. You seem to take a minimax perspective, but this is not stated explicitly. Also, are there no constraints on cluster sizes? For example, do you allow k−1 singleton clusters and one cluster of size n−k+1?
- Theorem 2 appears incorrect as written. For SBM with k=2 (and more generally k=O(1)), exact recovery sometimes requires no queries even when the Hellinger distance scales like log(n)/n. Part of the issue seems to be that the proof assumes k → ∞, but this assumption is not stated in the theorem (and must be). Please also verify carefully that Theorem 2 does not contradict known cases where Q=0 suffices.
- The empirical section is not very informative, as it compares only to trivial baselines and not to existing algorithms. Are your methods truly the only contenders that can be implemented?
- Several sentences are confusing or incomplete, e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (text missing after “assume”?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (that lower bound applies without side information, so the linkage here is unclear).
- The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings. Capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and articles (a/an/the) are often incorrect. Commas are frequently misplaced (e.g., typically not used after “let”/“we have”/“this means”/“assume that”/“since”/“while”/“note that,” and rarely just before an equation). Avoid starting sentences with And/Or/Plus. “Else” is used in unusual ways (often “otherwise” is better). Footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a period. This list is not exhaustive; regardless of the decision, a thorough proofread is strongly recommended.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is too long, and more broadly, three pages pass before the contributions are stated.
- Confirm that your use of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” aligns with standard terminology; when first introducing Las Vegas on p.4, mention that the guarantee pertains to average query complexity (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” really appear after p= ?
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊂ V × V” suggests a non-adaptive algorithm; please rephrase to make adaptivity explicit.
- I disagree with “clearly exhibiting” in the final sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets in the appendix are too small.
