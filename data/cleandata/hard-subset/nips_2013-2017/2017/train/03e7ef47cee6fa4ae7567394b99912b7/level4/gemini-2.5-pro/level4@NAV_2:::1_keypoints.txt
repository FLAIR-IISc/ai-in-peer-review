- Central result: With side information W, query complexity drops from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)) with a nearly-matching lower bound Ω(k² / H²(f+||f-)).  
- Key metric: Squared Hellinger divergence H²(f+||f-) between same-cluster and different-cluster similarity distributions governs query complexity.  
- Algorithmic contribution: Efficient, practical, parameter-free algorithms that do not require knowing k or the distributions f+, f-.  
- Information-theoretic contribution: Novel lower-bound technique for adaptive interactive querying, giving a nearly-tight converse.  
- Connection to prior work: Model generalizes and connects elegantly to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), raising questions about active queries and recovery thresholds.  
- Significance/originality: First rigorous theoretical characterization of how similarity-based side information quantitatively reduces query complexity.  
- Technical quality: Strong, comprehensive analysis with both algorithmic upper bounds and near-matching lower bounds.  
- Clarity: Well-structured exposition with a clear explanation of contributions and the SBM connection.  
- Experimental weakness: Empirical validation is limited to Cora; more experiments (especially synthetic) are needed to test scaling with k and H².  
- Gap in bounds: A remaining log n factor gap between upper and lower bounds should be discussed—unclear if analytical artifact or fundamental.  
- Proof intuition: Lower-bound proof sketch is dense; adding more intuition about the construction (e.g., “weak” clusters and hypothesis testing) would aid accessibility.  
- Overall evaluation: Excellent, nearly-tight theoretical work with minor weaknesses mainly around expanded experiments and exposition of the lower-bound intuition.  
- Arguments for acceptance: First rigorous analysis, identifies H² as critical, provides efficient parameter-free algorithms, links to SBM, and introduces a novel interactive lower-bound method.  
- Argument against acceptance: Experimental validation is not comprehensive enough to fully confirm the theoretical scaling laws.
