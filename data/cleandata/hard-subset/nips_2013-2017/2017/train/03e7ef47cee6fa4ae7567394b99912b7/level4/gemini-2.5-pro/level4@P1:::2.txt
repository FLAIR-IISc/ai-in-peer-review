This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm has access to both an oracle for noiseless pairwise comparisons and random side information for every pair of items. The authors provide information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds for this problem, showing a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available compared to when it is not.

My overall impression is that the paper's contributions are potentially valuable, assuming the technical results are correct. The paper is quite dense, and the proofs, particularly for the upper bound, are involved. However, the current manuscript has significant issues with its positioning, the precision of its theoretical claims, and its overall presentation, which detract from its quality and clarity.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:** A primary concern is the positioning of this work relative to the existing literature. Several highly relevant works appear to have been overlooked, which calls into question some of the paper's claims of novelty.
    *   The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli edges to more general distributions is not new. Prior work on the "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" has explored similar settings.
    *   The problem of SBM with an active learning component has also been studied previously. For example, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) explores a model where single-node labels can be queried. For a fixed number of communities (*k*) and linear-sized communities, their model is functionally similar to the pairwise querying model presented here.
    *   In light of this prior work, broad claims of novelty such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "this is the first work that...," and "there is no systematic theoretical study" should be carefully re-evaluated and rephrased to accurately reflect the paper's specific contributions.

2.  **Clarity and Correctness of Theoretical Results:**
    *   **Model Definition:** The problem setting could be defined more precisely. The paper appears to derive minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. It is also unclear what assumptions, if any, are made about the cluster sizes. For instance, do the results hold for highly imbalanced clusters, such as *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size *n* - *k* + 1? Furthermore, the formal problem statement, "find Q ⊆ V × V," makes the algorithm sound non-adaptive, which seems to contradict the interactive setting described.
    *   **Theorem 2:** Theorem 2 appears to be incorrect or at least imprecisely stated. For the standard SBM with a constant number of communities (e.g., *k*=2), it is known that exact recovery is possible with zero queries (*Q*=0) even in regimes where the Hellinger distance is small (e.g., scaling as log(*n*)/*n*). The theorem as written seems to contradict this. A likely issue is that the proof relies on an assumption that *k* → ∞, but this crucial condition is not stated in the theorem itself. It is very important that theorems and lemmas are stated precisely. The authors should state all assumptions explicitly in their results and carefully verify that their bounds do not contradict known results for the *Q*=0 case.

**Other Comments**

*   **Experiments:** The experimental section provides limited insight, as it only compares against simple baselines and not against any existing methods from the literature. To better demonstrate the practical value of the proposed algorithms, a comparison with other relevant approaches would be necessary. The claim that the results are "clearly exhibiting" the algorithm's strengths seems overstated.

*   **Writing and Presentation:** The manuscript requires a thorough round of proofreading and editing. It contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrases that impede readability. Specific issues include incorrect capitalization, plurals, spacing, and use of articles. Comma usage is frequently incorrect (e.g., misplaced commas after phrases like "we have" or "note that"). Sentences often begin with conjunctions like "And" or "Plus," and the word "else" is sometimes used where "otherwise" would be more appropriate. These issues are pervasive and significantly detract from the paper's professionalism. The abstract also seems overly long, and the introduction takes nearly three pages to arrive at the main contributions.

*   **Minor Points:**
    *   Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" standard in this context? When the Las Vegas algorithm is introduced, it should be specified that it has a target *average* query complexity.
    *   There may be a typo in Footnote 1 ("p = a logn n").
    *   Many of the brackets in the appendix are too small for the enclosed expressions.

**Post-Rebuttal Update:**

The authors have provided clarifications that address some of the concerns raised, particularly regarding the potential contradiction with the work of Gadde et al. Based on this feedback, my recommendation has been updated to acceptance.

However, I strongly urge the authors to undertake a very careful revision for the final version, taking all of the above comments into account. The issues of writing and presentation, while secondary to the technical content, are significant and will make the paper less accessible to the broad NIPS audience. In particular, it is essential that theorems are stated with full precision, including all necessary conditions and assumptions (such as dependencies on *k*), directly in the theorem statements and not just in the proofs.
