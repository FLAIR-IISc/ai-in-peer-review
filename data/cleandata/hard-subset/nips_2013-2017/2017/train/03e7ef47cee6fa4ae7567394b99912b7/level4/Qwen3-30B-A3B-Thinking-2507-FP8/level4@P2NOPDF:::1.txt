

This paper investigates query complexity in clustering when augmented with side information in the form of a similarity matrix $W$. Each entry $W_{ij}$ is independently drawn from distribution $f_+$ if nodes $i$ and $j$ belong to the same cluster, or $f_-$ otherwise. The authors establish that without side information, $\Theta(nk)$ queries are necessary and sufficient to recover clusters, whereas with side information, $O\left(k \log n / H^2(f_+ \| f_-)\right)$ queries suffice, where $H$ denotes the Hellinger distance between $f_+$ and $f_-$. Crucially, they prove this bound is tight up to a $\log n$ factor.  

The work offers a compelling theoretical contribution with clear significance. It extends the active Stochastic Block Model (SBM) literature by introducing query-based side information, thereby addressing a natural question: *how does the ability to query pairs alter the clustering threshold?* While the potential for side information to drastically reduce query complexity might be expected (e.g., if $W$ is perfect), the paper’s key insight lies in precisely quantifying this reduction through the Hellinger distance. This provides a rigorous metric for assessing the "quality" of side information—specifically, how distinguishable $f_+$ and $f_-$ are—directly linking the side information’s statistical properties to the query complexity. This bridges a gap between SBM theory and query-based learning, offering a refined perspective on when side information becomes practically beneficial.  

The technical execution is strong. The upper bound is derived via a carefully designed adaptive algorithm, while the lower bound employs information-theoretic arguments that elegantly leverage the Hellinger distance. The paper contextualizes its results within SBM work, demonstrating how the query model modifies phase transitions (e.g., the threshold for exact recovery shifts from $\Theta(n \log n)$ to $\Theta(k \log n)$ when side information is incorporated). The analysis is precise and well-motivated, with clean proofs that avoid unnecessary abstraction. One minor limitation is the reliance on Hellinger distance as the distinguishability metric; while standard, the authors might briefly compare it to alternatives (e.g., KL divergence) to clarify its suitability for their setting. However, this does not undermine the core contribution.  

The paper’s clarity and focus make it highly relevant for NIPS. It resolves a fundamental question about the interplay between side information and query complexity in clustering, providing a tight, generalizable bound that applies beyond specific SBM assumptions. The result also has implications for practical scenarios where side information (e.g., from human experts or noisy sensors) is imperfect—offering a principled way to estimate the number of queries needed based on the side information’s fidelity. Given the surge of interest in query-based learning and structured inference, this work is timely and conceptually robust.  

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical advance that significantly clarifies the role of side information in clustering. The results are both elegant and impactful, advancing the field beyond prior SBM-focused studies. I recommend acceptance.
