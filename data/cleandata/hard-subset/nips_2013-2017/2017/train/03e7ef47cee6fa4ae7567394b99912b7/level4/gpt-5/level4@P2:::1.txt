Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when, in addition to an oracle answering same-cluster queries, the algorithm has access to “side information” in the form of a similarity matrix W. Each entry wij is sampled from f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise; the algorithm does not know f+, f−, or k. The main contributions are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds: Any (possibly adaptive, randomized) algorithm requires Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+||f−)}) queries for exact recovery with constant probability (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H^2}) for success with probability 1), where H is the Hellinger distance. This holds even when k, f+, and f− are known to the algorithm.
- Nearly matching upper bounds: The authors give efficient, parameter-free Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms that recover the true clustering with high probability or probability 1, respectively, using O(min{nk, (poly in k)·log n / H^2(f+||f−)}) queries. Up to a log n factor, the bounds match the lower bounds.
- Methodologically, they introduce a lower-bounding approach for interactive settings where queries are adaptive, and an iterative-update framework that estimates the statistical gap between f+ and f− from the side information and uses it to determine when to rely on W vs. asking queries.

Relation to prior work
The model generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM): SBM corresponds to Bernoulli f+, f− with known parameters and typically balanced clusters; here f+, f− can be arbitrary distributions (discrete or continuous), unknown to the algorithm, cluster sizes can be adversarial, and k can be unknown and large. The work connects recovery thresholds in SBM to the availability of a budget of same-cluster queries, quantifying how side information (and queries) reduces the need for oracle interaction. It contrasts with heuristic, side-information-guided querying strategies empirically studied in entity resolution and crowdsourcing, and with recent analyses of SBM recovery thresholds, as well as prior interactive clustering models that allow different feedback types.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower bound framework carefully handles adaptivity, going beyond standard Fano-type arguments; the use of Hellinger distance is natural for product distributions and for relating TV distance to testing error. The upper bounds are constructive, computationally efficient (time O(n^2)), and parameter-free, relying on empirical estimates of f+, f− and Sanov-type concentration. The proofs (sketched in the main text and detailed in the appendices) appear rigorous.
- Assumptions: The oracle is noiseless; handling noisy oracles is deferred to a companion work. The main analysis for algorithms assumes discrete distributions with fixed support, though the lower bounds also cover continuous distributions. Independence of wij is assumed.
- Empirics: Experiments on the Cora dataset illustrate the practicality of the approach and the small gap between using exact vs. approximate learned distributions, with query counts far below nk.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, but there are typographical and notational inconsistencies that should be corrected. In particular, statements of the query complexity sometimes use k vs. k^2; the final forms should be made consistent across abstract, theorems, and algorithm sections. Some definitions (e.g., the exact success probability targets, support size assumptions) would benefit from early, unified statements. The iterative-update algorithm would be easier to follow with a concise high-level pseudocode.

Originality
The interactive lower bound technique for adaptive querying, the parameter-free algorithm that learns to rely on side information via empirical Hellinger gap, and the precise dependence on H^2(f+||f−) are original and timely. The work bridges active/interactive learning, SBM, and entity resolution.

Significance
The results give a near-complete characterization (up to log factors) of query complexity when side information is available, explaining why practical heuristics can use far fewer than Θ(nk) queries. The techniques should influence future work on interactive learning lower bounds, active community detection, and practical parameter-free clustering with guidance from noisy similarities.

Strengths
- Sharp information-theoretic lower bounds that handle adaptivity.
- Efficient, parameter-free algorithms with near-matching upper bounds.
- Clear conceptual message: side information with divergence H^2 greatly reduces query needs.
- Connection to SBM and active community detection.
- Practical relevance and preliminary empirical validation.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity/consistency: There are instances where the upper bound is written as O(k log n / H^2) and elsewhere as O(k^2 log n / H^2). Please unify and explain the exact dependence on k (and min{nk, ·}).
- Modeling assumptions: The algorithmic analysis focuses on discrete distributions with fixed support; please clarify how the approach and bounds extend to continuous distributions in practice.
- Oracle correctness: While the companion work handles noisy queries, a brief discussion of robustness (e.g., tolerance to a small error rate) would strengthen the main paper.
- Practicality: The algorithm requires O(n^2) preprocessing to read W; comment on scalability and on streaming/sparse settings.
- Experiments: Comparisons to strong interactive baselines (e.g., popular heuristics that exploit side information) under controlled budgets would better showcase benefits. Reporting query counts vs. exact recovery rate would connect more directly to theory.

Suggestions for improvement
- Standardize the query complexity statements (k vs. k^2) across abstract, theorems, and algorithm sections, and clearly state all min{nk, ·} forms.
- Provide a concise pseudocode for the iterative-update and membership tests and a figure outlining the phases.
- State assumptions on f+, f− (support size, independence) up front, and summarize which results cover continuous distributions.
- Add a brief robustness note for small oracle error rates and discuss whether thresholds change smoothly.
- Expand the empirical section with comparisons to established heuristics, ablations on H^2, and sensitivity to misspecification of f+, f−.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theory with tight bounds in an important interactive clustering setting.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms that match the lower bounds up to log factors.
- Clear and interesting connection to SBM and active learning.
- Useful lower-bound techniques for adaptive queries.

Arguments against acceptance
- Minor clarity issues and inconsistent notation around the dependence on k.
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparisons to leading baselines.
- Assumes noiseless oracle in the main text and discrete distributions for algorithmic analysis.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with practical implications. With improved clarity and strengthened experiments, it would be a strong addition to the program.
