Review - NOTE: I am evaluating two submissions that appear to be by the same authors and cover closely related topics. The companion paper concerns noisy queries with no side information.
This manuscript establishes upper and lower bounds on the number of queries needed for clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented with random side information for each pair. While I know some of the related work, my familiarity is incomplete, which makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty. The paper is also very dense, and I was unable to devote as much time as ideal to verifying all details—especially the lengthy upper-bound proof.
My overall take on both papers is that the contributions appear valuable (assuming correctness), but the exposition needs substantial improvement. I recognize that contributions outweigh presentation, yet the number of writing issues still leaves my recommendation marginal. A further review round to verify revisions would have helped, but I realize this is not permitted at NIPS.
For this paper, several closely related works are not cited:
- The replacement of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) with general edge-label distributions in the stochastic block model is not new. A quick Google (or Google Scholar) search for “labeled [sometimes spelled labelled] stochastic block model” or the less common “weighted stochastic block model” turns up numerous papers considering similar generalizations.
- There is at least one study of the SBM with an additional querying (active learning) component: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Their query model differs (query a single node to obtain its label), but for fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linearly sized communities, this essentially matches your pairwise querying model—one can rapidly identify a representative from each community via random sampling and then label any new node via pairwise queries to those representatives. At first glance, your lower bound seems to contradict their sublinear upper bound, but this may be because your lower bound only applies as k → ∞ (see below).
In light of the above, please revise or remove statements such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “This is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” “There is no systematic theoretical study,” and similar claims.
Further comments:
- The problem setup should be stated more explicitly. It appears you are studying minimax bounds, but this is not clearly declared. Also, does the minimax formulation impose no constraints on cluster sizes? For example, do you allow k−1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size n−k+1?
- Theorem 2, as written, appears incorrect. For SBM with k=2 (and likely any k=O(1)), we know that zero queries suffice even in regimes where the Hellinger distance is on the order of log(n)/n. It seems the proof assumes k → ∞, which is not stated in the theorem (it absolutely should be). I am not sure if that is the only issue—please verify carefully that Theorem 2 does not contradict cases where clustering is achievable with Q=0.
- The empirical section is not particularly informative, as it neither compares against prior methods nor goes beyond trivial baselines. Are there truly no existing approaches suitable for comparison or implementation?
- Several sentences are unclear or confusing, e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is part of the sentence missing after “assume”?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that bound apply only without side information?).
- The manuscript is replete with grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Issues with capitalization, plurals, spacing, and articles (a/an/the) are frequent. Commas are often misused (they generally should not follow phrases like “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that,” and are seldom needed immediately before equations). Avoid starting sentences with And/Or/Plus. “Else” is used oddly (often “otherwise” is preferable). Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a period. This is not an exhaustive list—regardless of the decision, I strongly encourage thorough proofreading.
Less significant comments:
- The abstract seems overly long, and more broadly, spending three pages before presenting the main contributions is suboptimal.
- Is your usage of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” standard? Also, when Las Vegas algorithms are first mentioned on p.4, “average” query complexity should be stated explicitly (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” really appear after p= ?
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V × V” suggests non-adaptivity.
- I disagree with the phrase “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many parentheses are too small throughout the appendix.

Specific notes:
- The authors are mistaken if they believe that “issues like capitalization and misplaced commas” determined my recommendation. These were listed at the end as revision suggestions, not as grounds for a borderline recommendation.
- Thank you for clarifying the absence of a contradiction with Gadde et al.
- It is crucial that theorems and lemmas be stated precisely. For instance, if a result requires sufficiently large k, this MUST appear in the theorem statement, not only in the proof.
- Regarding the grammar points discussed in the response, I still recommend revising uses of Else/And/Or/Plus, the sentence “assume, otherwise…,” and similar constructions. In my view, most are grammatically incorrect in context, or at least unsuitable for an academic article. (The authors may disagree.)
