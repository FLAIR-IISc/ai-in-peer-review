This paper provides upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons with random side-information associated with every pair. Given the authors' related work, my familiarity with the literature is limited, making it challenging to fully assess the contributions' value. The paper's density and the extensive upper bound proof also hindered my ability to thoroughly verify the correctness.

My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions appear valuable, assuming their correctness, but the writing requires significant improvement. While the contributions are more important than the writing, numerous issues affect my recommendation, which remains borderline. An additional round of reviews to address the suggested improvements would have been beneficial, although I understand this is not feasible for NIPS.

Regarding this paper, several relevant works were overlooked:
- The concept of replacing Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) with a general distribution in the stochastic block model is not novel. A search for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" reveals multiple papers that have explored similar generalizations.
- The work "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016) examines the SBM with an extra querying element. Although the querying model differs, it is comparable to the pairwise querying model for fixed k and linear-size communities. The lower bound appears to contradict their upper bound, but this discrepancy might be due to the lower bound only holding as k approaches infinity.

In light of these works, I suggest the authors rephrase statements claiming to initiate a rigorous theoretical study or significantly generalize existing works.

Additional comments:
- The model/problem presentation should be clarified, explicitly stating the minimax bounds and any cluster size restrictions.
- Theorem 2 appears incorrect, as it contradicts known results for the SBM with k=2. The assumption that k approaches infinity should be stated in the theorem.
- The numerical section lacks value due to the absence of comparisons with existing works and the use of trivial baselines.
- Several sentences are unclear or confusing, and the paper is plagued by grammar mistakes, typos, and strange wording. A thorough proof-read is essential to address these issues.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is too long, and the contributions are not introduced until page 3.
- The use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology should be verified as standard.
- Footnote 1 contains a potential error, and the formal problem statement may imply a non-adaptive algorithm.
- The statement "clearly exhibiting" in the Simulations section is disputed.
- Many brackets in the appendix are too small.
