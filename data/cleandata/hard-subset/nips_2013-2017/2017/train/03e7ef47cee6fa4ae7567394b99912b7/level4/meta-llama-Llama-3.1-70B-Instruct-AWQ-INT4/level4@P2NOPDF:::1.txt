This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering algorithms when "side information" is available. The side information is provided in the form of a similarity matrix W, where the entry Wij is distributed according to either f+ or f-, depending on whether data points i and j belong to the same cluster or different clusters. The primary contribution of the paper is the derivation of query complexity bounds, demonstrating that Theta(nk) queries are required without side information, whereas only O(k log n/H^2(f+||f-)) queries are sufficient when side information is available. Here, H denotes the Hellinger distance between the two distributions. Furthermore, the authors show that their bound is tight up to a logarithmic factor in n.

I find this result theoretically interesting for several reasons. The study of query complexity in clustering is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), which has garnered significant attention in recent years. This work poses intriguing questions about how the availability of queries affects the threshold for successful clustering. The paper demonstrates that incorporating side information can substantially alter the query complexity, which, while not entirely unexpected, highlights the importance of assessing the quality of the similarity matrix. The authors provide a framework for evaluating the usefulness of the side information, making this result more than just a theoretical curiosity.
