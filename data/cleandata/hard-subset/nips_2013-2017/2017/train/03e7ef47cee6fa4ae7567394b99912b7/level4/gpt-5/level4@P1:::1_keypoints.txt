- Main result: With side information the paper shows query complexity reduces to O(k log n / H^2(f+‖f−)), with matching information-theoretic lower bounds up to log factors.  
- Model: Similarity matrix entries drawn from f+ for same-cluster pairs and f− otherwise; oracle answers pairwise same-cluster queries.  
- Prior work relation: Connects to SBM and community detection; explores how adaptive queries shift recovery thresholds compared to no-query results.  
- Key metric: Uses Hellinger distance H^2(f+‖f−) as a principled, distribution-agnostic measure of side-information quality.  
- Strength: Clear, realistic problem formulation capturing interactive clustering with noisy similarities.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical contribution giving a near-tight characterization of query complexity in terms of k, n, and H^2.  
- Strength: Explains why practical heuristics using similarity matrices can require far fewer queries than Θ(nk).  
- Weakness: Qualitatively unsurprising that good side information helps (value is in quantifying it via Hellinger distance).  
- Weakness/Open: Hints at implications for SBM thresholds with queries but does not resolve the exact shifts — remains open.  
- Quality: Technical claims are well-defined and supported by matching lower and upper bounds (tight up to log factors).  
- Clarity: Motivation and main theorems are clear and well-organized, though some sections are dense.  
- Originality: Novel precise query-complexity characterization in the interactive setting and a clean dependence on Hellinger distance.  
- Significance: Reconciles theory with practice, setting a rigorous benchmark for queries needed given side-information quality.  
- Pros summarized: Near-tight bounds, bridges SBM and interactive clustering, provides principled metric, explains empirical efficiency.  
- Cons summarized: Qualitative benefit expected in extremes; exact SBM recovery threshold changes with queries left unresolved.
