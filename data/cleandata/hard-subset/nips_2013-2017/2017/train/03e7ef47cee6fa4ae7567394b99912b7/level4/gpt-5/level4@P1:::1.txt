Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when “side information” is available in the form of a similarity matrix W. Each entry Wij is drawn from distribution f+ if i and j belong to the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The oracle can answer pairwise “same-cluster?” queries. Without side information, the best-known query complexity is Θ(nk), and this is tight even for randomized algorithms. The paper shows that with side information the number of queries can be reduced to O(k log n / H^2(f+‖f−)), where H is the Hellinger distance between f+ and f−. They also establish information-theoretic lower bounds that match this dependence up to a logarithmic factor, showing the result is essentially tight.

Relation to prior work
The problem is closely related to the stochastic block model (SBM) and more generally to community detection with noisy pairwise information. Recent NIPS work has focused on recovery thresholds for SBM without queries; this paper explores how allowing adaptive queries changes those thresholds. It clarifies the gap between practical heuristics for interactive clustering/entity resolution that often use far fewer than nk queries and the worst-case lower bounds without side information. The use of Hellinger distance to quantify the quality of side information provides a principled way to relate distributions f+ and f− to query complexity.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing problem formulation that captures practical interactive clustering with a noisy similarity matrix.
- Strong theoretical contribution: a near-tight characterization of query complexity that cleanly depends on k, n, and H^2(f+‖f−).
- The result explains why heuristics that leverage a similarity matrix can do far better than the Θ(nk) baseline.
- The connection to SBM is natural and timely, and the work raises interesting questions about how queries shift classical recovery thresholds.

Weaknesses
- While the dramatic improvement with side information is compelling, the qualitative effect is not surprising in the extreme (since side information could be perfect). The value here is in quantifying the dependence via Hellinger distance, which the paper does.
- The discussion hints at implications for SBM thresholds with queries but does not resolve them; this remains open.

Quality
The technical claims are well-defined and supported by information-theoretic lower bounds and matching upper bounds up to log factors. The main result—O(k log n / H^2(f+‖f−)) queries with side information—is stated precisely, and the tightness claim is clear. The modeling choice of using Hellinger distance is sensible and provides a robust, distribution-agnostic measure of separation between f+ and f−.

Clarity
The paper’s motivation and main theorems are clearly stated. The relationship to prior work (especially SBM) is helpfully outlined, and the role of Hellinger distance in quantifying side-information quality is well-motivated. Some sections are dense, but overall the organization supports the flow from problem definition to lower bounds to algorithms.

Originality
The work is original in its precise query-complexity characterization for clustering with side information and in establishing near-matching lower bounds in this interactive setting. The dependence on Hellinger distance as the key quantity is a clean and novel way to measure the utility of side information.

Significance
This is a significant theoretical contribution. It reconciles theory with practice by explaining the empirical success of interactive clustering methods that use a similarity matrix. The results should influence subsequent work on active clustering, entity resolution, and community detection by providing a rigorous benchmark for the number of queries needed as a function of side-information quality.

Pros
- Near-tight bounds linking query complexity to H^2(f+‖f−), k, and n.
- Conceptually bridges SBM/community detection and interactive clustering.
- Provides a principled metric (Hellinger distance) to assess side-information quality.
- Explains the discrepancy between Θ(nk) lower bounds without side information and the efficiency observed in practice.

Cons
- The qualitative benefit of side information is expected in the limit; the novelty lies in the quantitative characterization.
- Leaves open the exact shift in SBM recovery thresholds when queries are allowed.

Overall assessment
An interesting and timely theoretical contribution that precisely quantifies how side information reduces the query complexity of clustering. The results are clean, nearly tight, and well-connected to the literature on SBM. While the basic phenomenon is not surprising in extreme cases, the paper’s identification of Hellinger distance as the governing parameter and the near-matching lower and upper bounds make it a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.
