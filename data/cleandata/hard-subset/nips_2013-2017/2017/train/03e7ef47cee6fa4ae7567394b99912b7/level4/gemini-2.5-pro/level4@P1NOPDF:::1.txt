This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering in the presence of side information. This side information is provided as a similarity matrix W, where the entry W_ij is drawn from a distribution f+ if items i and j belong to the same cluster, and from f- otherwise. The paper's main result establishes a sharp contrast between clustering with and without this information: whereas clustering without side information requires Theta(nk) queries, the authors show that only O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f-)) queries are sufficient when the side information is available. Here, H represents the Hellinger distance between the distributions f+ and f-. The authors also show that this upper bound is tight up to a log(n) factor.

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. The work is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a topic that has seen a great deal of activity in recent years, yet it explores the novel question of how allowing queries changes the information-theoretic recovery threshold. The paper demonstrates that access to side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this finding is not surprising in itself—as the side information could, in an extreme case, contain the complete clustering information—the paper's key contribution is to precisely quantify this relationship. It successfully identifies how to assess the quality of the similarity matrix, showing that the Hellinger distance between the underlying distributions is the crucial parameter governing the problem's difficulty.
