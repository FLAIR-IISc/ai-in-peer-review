Please note: I am reviewing two articles by the same author on the same general topic. The other discusses a misleading question without any supporting information.

This article provides an upper and lower bound on the complexity of clustering questions using non-misleading pair comparisons, as well as random supporting information related to each pair. Although I am aware of some related literature, my knowledge is not complete, so it is difficult to fully judge the quality of the contributions. The article is so complex that I could not devote enough time to verify its accuracy – especially in the lengthy section proving the upper bound.

My overall impression of both articles is that the contributions are valuable (if correct), but the writing needs a lot of improvement. I acknowledge that the contributions are more important than the writing, but there are many problems that still marginalize my recommendation. It would be very useful if there were another review where the suggestions were vetted, but I understand that this is not approved by NIPS.

Regarding this article, there are a few extremely relevant works that were not cited:

- The idea of replacing Bernoulli (p) and Bernoulli (q) with a common distribution in the stochastic block model is not new. A Google (or Google Scholar) search for "labeled [sometimes spelled labeled] stochastic block model" or less commonly "weighted stochastic block model" yields several articles discussing similar generalizations.
- There is at least one work that studies SBM with additional question (active learning) elements: "Active learning for community detection in stochastic block models" (Gade et al, ISIIT 2016). This is related to a different question model, where a single node is asked and its label is found. But for a specific k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-sized communities, it is essentially the same as your pair question model – just find a node quickly from each community using a random sample, and then you can get the label of any new node by pair questioning each of them. At first glance, this may conflict with their (sublinear-sample) upper bound with your lower bound, but it is probably only applicable up to k -> infinity due to your lower bound (see below).

However, in light of the above works, I would request the authors to remove/rewrite all statements in lines such as "we start with a rigorous theoretical study", "they generalize significantly", "this is the first work that...", "they generalize in an important way", "there is no systematic theoretical study" etc.

Here are some more comments:

- I think the model/problem should be presented more clearly. It seems you are looking at minimax bounds, but this is not explicitly stated. Moreover, is the minimax model applicable without any restriction on the cluster size? For example, you approve a situation where the k-1 cluster size is 1 and the other is n-k+1?
- It seems that theorem 2 is not stated correctly. For SBM with k=2 (or possibly any k=O(1)), we know that in some cases, there is no need for a question that scales as the Heiliger distance log(n)/n. It seems that part of the problem is that the proof assumes k -> infinity, which is not mentioned in the theorem (which it certainly should be). I am not sure if this is the only part of the problem – please check very carefully whether theorem 2 conflicts more generally with the cases where clustering is possible with Q=0.
- The numerical section does not seem very valuable, mainly because it does not compare with any existing work, and only compares with very trivial baselines. Are your methods really the only methods that can be considered/implemented?
- There are many misleading/ambiguous sentences here, such as "assume, otherwise clustering has been done" ("is there any writing missing after 'assume'?") and "the theorem is already proven from lower bounds" (lower bounds are only applicable when there is no supporting information?).
- The article is full of grammatical errors, typos, and strange word choices. Capital letters, plurals, spacing, and articles (a/an/the) are often incorrect. Commas are consistently used incorrectly (e.g., they should not be displayed after statements like "let" / "we have" / "this means" / "assume that" / "since" / "while" / "note that" and should be used very rarely just before equations). The sentence "and/or/plus" should not be started. The word "else" is used strangely (often "otherwise" would be better). The footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This list of problems is not complete – whatever the conclusion, I strongly request the authors to proof-read the article as carefully as possible.

Some less important comments:

- The abstract seems very long, and in general, I think it is not ideal to spend 3 pages before reaching the contributions.
- Is your use of the words "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" standard? Also, when you first mention Las Vegas on p4, it should be mentioned with the AVERAGE question complexity (the word 'average' is missing here).
- Footnote 1: p= after 1- really should be there?
- The formal problem statement is written in such a way that "find the Q-subset of V x V" as if the algorithm is non-adaptive.
- I disagree with the statement in the last sentence of the simulation section that "clearly demonstrates".
- Many parentheses throughout the appendix are too short.
