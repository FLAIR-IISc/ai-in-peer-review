

This paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information, where the goal is to cluster n elements into k unknown clusters using pair-wise queries of the form "do elements u and v belong to the same cluster?" The key contribution is showing that a similarity matrix (side information) dramatically reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f⁺‖f⁻)), where H denotes Hellinger divergence. The authors provide matching information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient, parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f⁺, or f⁻.

The paper relates to recent work on interactive clustering [22, 40, 13, 42, 43], which has seen heuristic development but lacks theoretical guarantees. It also generalizes the stochastic block model (SBM) by allowing arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻ rather than just Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q). The connection to SBM is particularly valuable as it opens new theoretical directions for community detection with interactive queries.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-constructed lower bounds that provide a general framework for proving information-theoretic limits in interactive settings. The algorithms are efficient, parameter-free, and nearly match the lower bounds. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate practical viability, showing that the greedy strategy closely approaches optimal performance with only modest query overhead.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's presentation and some technical claims. First, the writing contains numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology, and awkward phrasing that significantly hinder readability. For instance, the abstract contains several grammatical issues, and the paper consistently misuses commas, capitalization, and sentence structure. These issues are not merely stylistic; they make the paper difficult to follow for non-native English readers, which is particularly problematic for an international conference like NIPS.

Second, the paper makes several overstatements that require careful revision. Claims such as "this is the first rigorous theoretical study" and "there is no systematic theoretical study" are inaccurate given prior work on SBM generalizations and active learning for community detection. The authors should cite relevant literature on "labeled stochastic block models" and "weighted stochastic block models" (e.g., [2, 15, 24, 36]) that have considered similar generalizations. The paper also fails to acknowledge the connection to Gadde et al.'s "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (ISIT 2016), which studies a related active learning model for SBM. While the querying models differ (pairwise vs. node labeling), for fixed k and linear-sized clusters, these are effectively equivalent approaches.

Third, Theorem 2 requires clarification. The lower bound appears to assume k → ∞, which is not stated in the theorem statement. For small k (e.g., k=2), the lower bound contradicts known results where clustering is possible with no queries when Hellinger distance scales appropriately (e.g., as log n/n). The authors must explicitly state the asymptotic regime for which their bound holds.

Fourth, the experimental section lacks meaningful comparison to existing methods. The authors compare against trivial baselines rather than relevant approaches from the literature, making it difficult to assess the practical advantages of their method.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with tight upper and lower bounds
- Efficient, parameter-free algorithms that match theoretical limits
- Clear connection to stochastic block models and community detection
- Strong experimental validation on real-world dataset

Weaknesses:
- Overstated claims about novelty that misrepresent prior work
- Significant grammatical and stylistic issues that hinder readability
- Theorem 2 lacks necessary asymptotic context (k → ∞)
- Experimental section lacks meaningful comparison to existing methods
- Inconsistent use of technical terms (e.g., "Monte Carlo" vs. "Las Vegas" algorithms)

The paper makes valuable theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of query complexity in clustering with side information. However, the current presentation contains serious issues that need addressing before the paper can be accepted. The overstatements about novelty require careful revision to accurately position the work within existing literature. The grammatical and stylistic issues need thorough proofreading to ensure the paper meets NIPS' standards for clarity and professionalism. The technical claims about Theorem 2 must be clarified with appropriate asymptotic context.

I recommend acceptance only if the authors address these concerns in the final version. The theoretical contributions are strong and meaningful, but the paper's current presentation significantly undermines its impact. The authors should carefully revise the manuscript to accurately represent prior work, clarify the scope of their theoretical results, and eliminate grammatical and stylistic errors that impede understanding.

The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of interactive learning and clustering, but its current form does not fully realize this potential due to presentation issues. With careful revision addressing the points above, this could be a strong NIPS paper.
