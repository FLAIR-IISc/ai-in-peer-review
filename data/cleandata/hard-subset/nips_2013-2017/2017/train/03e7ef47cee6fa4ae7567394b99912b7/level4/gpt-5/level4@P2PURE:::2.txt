Note: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors on closely related topics. The other paper addresses noisy queries without side information.

This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, together with random side information for each pair. While I am familiar with part of the related literature, my knowledge is not exhaustive, making it somewhat difficult to fully assess the novelty of the contributions. The paper is also very dense; I could not devote as much time as ideal to checking correctness, especially for the long proof of the upper bound.

My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions appear valuable (assuming correctness), but the writing needs substantial improvement. I recognize that contributions matter more than presentation, but the number of issues keeps my recommendation borderline. A further review round to verify revisions would have been very helpful, though I understand this is not permitted for NIPS.

For this paper, several highly relevant works were not cited:
- Replacing Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) with a general distribution in the stochastic block model is not new. A quick Google or Google Scholar search for “labeled [or labelled] stochastic block model” or the less common “weighted stochastic block model” reveals multiple papers exploring similar generalizations.
- There is at least one paper studying the SBM with an additional querying (active learning) component: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Their querying model differs—one queries a single node and learns its label—but for fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and communities of linear size, this is essentially equivalent to your pairwise querying model. One can first identify a representative from each community via random sampling, and then determine any new node’s label by querying it against these representatives. At first glance, your lower bound seems to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound, but this may be because your lower bound only applies in the regime k → ∞ (see below).

In light of these works, I recommend removing or rephrasing language such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “this is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “there is no systematic theoretical study,” etc.

Additional comments:
- The model/problem should be presented more clearly. It appears you are proving minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. Moreover, is the minimax setting unconstrained with respect to cluster sizes? For example, do you allow k−1 clusters of size 1 and a remaining cluster of size n−k+1?
- Theorem 2, as stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with k=2 (and likely any k=O(1)), we know that zero queries suffice in certain regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. It seems the proof assumes k → ∞, but this is not stated in the theorem and absolutely must be. I am not sure this is the only issue—please check carefully whether Theorem 2 more generally conflicts with known cases where clustering is possible even with Q=0.
- The numerical section adds limited value because it does not compare against existing methods and only includes very trivial baselines. Are your methods really the only ones worth considering or straightforward to implement?
- Several sentences are confusing or unclear, such as “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is text missing after “assume”?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound apply only when there is no side information?).
- The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Common issues include capitalization, plurals, spacing, and articles (a/an/the). Commas are frequently misused (e.g., they generally should not follow phrases like “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that,” and are rarely appropriate immediately before an equation). Avoid starting sentences with And/Or/Plus. The word “else” is often misused (in many places “otherwise” would be better). Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a period. This list is not exhaustive—regardless of the decision, I strongly urge a thorough proofread.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is too long; more broadly, spending three pages before clearly stating the contributions is not ideal.
- Is your use of the terms “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” standard? Also, when you first mention Las Vegas on p. 4, it should be presented alongside average query complexity (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” really appear after “p=”?
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V × V” makes the algorithm sound non-adaptive.
- I disagree with the phrase “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets in the appendix are too small.
