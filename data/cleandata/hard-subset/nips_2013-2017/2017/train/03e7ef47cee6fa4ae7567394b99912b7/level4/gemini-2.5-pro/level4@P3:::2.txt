### Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

**Summary**

This paper investigates the query complexity of interactive clustering. The setting involves a set of `n` elements to be partitioned into `k` unknown clusters. The algorithm can query an oracle with pairs of elements to determine if they belong to the same cluster. The key contribution is analyzing how this query complexity is affected by the presence of "side information"—a similarity matrix where pairwise similarity values are drawn from one of two distributions (`f+` or `f-`) depending on whether the pair is in the same cluster or not.

The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching algorithmic upper bounds on the number of queries required. They show that without side information, the complexity is Θ(nk), but with it, the complexity can be dramatically reduced to roughly O(k²/H²(f+||f-)), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence. The proposed algorithms are notably parameter-free, operating without knowledge of `k`, `f+`, or `f-`. The paper also draws connections between this problem and the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM), framing the side information model as a significant generalization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and practically relevant problem. The discrepancy between the Θ(nk) theoretical query complexity and the much lower number of queries used by practical heuristics is a significant gap in understanding. The hypothesis that side information is the key to closing this gap is compelling and well-explored here.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of the paper is its rigorous theoretical analysis. Providing both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds gives a comprehensive picture of the problem's complexity. If correct, these results represent a substantial contribution to the understanding of interactive learning and clustering.

3.  **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The development of parameter-free algorithms is a significant practical advantage. In real-world scenarios, parameters like the number of clusters (`k`) or the underlying data distributions (`f+`, `f-`) are rarely known. The iterative-update technique proposed is clever and could be of broader interest for designing other parameter-free methods.

4.  **Novel Connection:** The paper makes an insightful connection to the SBM, framing its model as a generalization. This link could stimulate further research at the intersection of community detection and active learning.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's overall quality is severely diminished by its writing. It is dense and difficult to parse, which hinders the verification of its claims. There are pervasive grammatical errors, typos, and stylistic issues, including incorrect capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and misuse of punctuation (e.g., unnecessary commas after "we have," "note that," etc.). Sentences often begin with "And" or "Plus," and the phrasing can be unconventional (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done"). A thorough proofreading and editing pass by a native English speaker is essential.

2.  **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper overlooks several highly relevant lines of work, which weakens its claims of originality.
    *   The idea of generalizing the SBM from Bernoulli distributions to more general distributions is not new. A search for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" reveals prior work on similar models.
    *   The paper "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) studies the SBM with an active querying component. While the query model is different (node labels vs. pairwise comparisons), for a constant `k` and balanced clusters, they are functionally similar. The authors should carefully discuss this and other related works and temper claims like "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study" or "this is the first work that...".

3.  **Precision of Theoretical Claims:** There are potential issues with the precision of the main lower bound theorem.
    *   **Theorem 2:** As stated, the theorem appears to contradict known results for the SBM with a constant number of clusters (e.g., `k=2`). In some regimes, perfect recovery is possible with zero queries (Q=0), even when the Hellinger distance is small, whereas the theorem would imply a non-zero lower bound. This discrepancy likely arises because the proof relies on an assumption that `k` is sufficiently large (`k -> infinity`), but this crucial condition is not stated in the theorem itself. For a theoretical paper, theorems must be stated with complete precision, including all necessary assumptions and asymptotic regimes.
    *   **Model Specification:** The paper should be more explicit about the minimax model being analyzed. For example, are there any assumptions on the cluster sizes? The lower bounds may depend on whether highly imbalanced clusters (e.g., `k-1` singletons) are permitted.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is not very compelling. It compares the proposed algorithm only against simple best-case and worst-case baselines, rather than against existing heuristic methods from the literature it aims to analyze. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental problem at the intersection of clustering, active learning, and theory.
*   The core theoretical results, establishing tight upper and lower bounds on query complexity in the presence of side information, are a significant contribution.
*   The design of efficient, parameter-free algorithms is a valuable result for practitioners.

**Arguments against Acceptance**

*   The clarity of the paper is extremely poor, making it difficult to read and verify.
*   The paper fails to properly cite and position itself against key related works, leading to overstated claims of novelty.
*   The main lower bound theorem (Theorem 2) appears to be imprecisely stated, omitting critical assumptions about `k`, which is a serious flaw for a theoretical contribution.
*   The experimental validation is weak and does not provide sufficient evidence of the algorithm's practical advantages over existing methods.

**Recommendation: Accept (with major revisions)**

Despite significant weaknesses in its presentation and positioning, the core theoretical contribution of this paper appears valuable and novel enough for acceptance at NIPS. However, my recommendation is conditional on the authors performing a major revision. The final version must be thoroughly rewritten for clarity and grammatical correctness. Crucially, the authors must:
1.  Properly survey and discuss related work, especially on generalized SBMs and active learning for community detection, and revise claims of novelty accordingly.
2.  State all theorems and lemmas with full precision, explicitly including any assumptions (such as `k` being large) required for them to hold. This is non-negotiable.
3.  (Optional, but recommended) Strengthen the experimental section by comparing against at least one relevant heuristic from the literature.

If these issues are addressed, the paper will be a strong contribution to the conference.
