This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information. This side information is provided in the form of a similarity matrix W, where the entry W_ij is drawn from a distribution f+ if items i and j belong to the same cluster, and from a distribution f- otherwise. The main result of the paper is that while Θ(nk) queries are required for clustering without side information, the authors demonstrate that only O(k log n / H²(f+ || f-)) queries are sufficient when side information is available, where H²(f+ || f-) is the squared Hellinger distance. They also show that this upper bound is tight up to a log(n) factor.

I find this to be an interesting and significant theoretical result for several reasons. The work is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a topic that has attracted considerable recent attention, but it explores the novel question of how allowing active queries alters the information-theoretic recovery threshold. The paper clearly demonstrates that side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this principle may not be surprising in itself—as the side information could, in an extreme case, contain the complete clustering—the key contribution lies in precisely quantifying the quality of this information. The authors identify the Hellinger distance between the in-cluster (f+) and out-of-cluster (f-) distributions as the crucial factor governing this complexity reduction.
