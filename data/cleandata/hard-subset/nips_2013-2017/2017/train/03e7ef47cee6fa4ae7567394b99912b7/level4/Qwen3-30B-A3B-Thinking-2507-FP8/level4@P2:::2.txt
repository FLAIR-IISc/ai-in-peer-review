

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, where an oracle answers pairwise "same cluster" queries and a similarity matrix provides noisy side information. The authors establish information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds for the query complexity when side information is available. Their key contribution is showing that the query complexity reduces from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k² log n / H²(f⁺‖f⁻)) with side information, where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between the distributions of similarity values within and across clusters. The algorithms are efficient, parameter-free (requiring no knowledge of k, f⁺, or f⁻), and work for arbitrary discrete distributions.

The work connects to the stochastic block model (SBM) as a special case where f⁺ and f⁻ are Bernoulli distributions. While the authors claim to initiate a rigorous theoretical study, this overlooks several important prior works. The "weighted" or "labeled" stochastic block model has been studied before (e.g., by Decelle et al. and others), and Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) studied community detection with active learning in SBMs, which is closely related to the pairwise query model. The authors should revise claims of novelty like "first work" or "significant generalization" to accurately reflect prior literature.

**Quality**: The theoretical contributions are strong, with well-constructed lower bounds (Theorem 2) and efficient algorithms. However, the lower bound proof requires careful attention to the condition that k must be sufficiently large, which should be explicitly stated in Theorem 2 (the current statement implies it holds for all k). The connection to SBM is valuable but could be better articulated to clarify how the authors' results generalize prior work. The experimental section is limited, comparing only against trivial baselines without comparing to existing clustering algorithms with side information.

**Clarity**: The paper is technically dense and suffers from significant writing issues that impede understanding. There are numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent capitalization, misplaced commas, and unclear phrasing (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" appears to be missing text). The distinction between Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms is not sufficiently clarified, and the term "Las Vegas" should be accompanied by "average-case" or "expected" query complexity. The formal problem statement incorrectly implies non-adaptive algorithms by stating "find Q ⊆ V × V" without noting the adaptive nature of the queries.

**Originality**: The core technical contribution—showing the dramatic reduction in query complexity with side information—is novel and well-justified. The generalization of SBM to arbitrary distributions f⁺ and f⁻ is valuable, though the novelty claim over prior "weighted SBM" work should be revised. The connection to community detection thresholds and the development of a general framework for lower bounds in interactive settings are significant contributions.

**Significance**: The problem is well-motivated by real applications like entity resolution, where human-labeled data is expensive but improves clustering accuracy. The theoretical results provide a foundation for understanding when side information can drastically reduce the need for human queries. However, the experimental validation is limited, and the paper would benefit from more concrete comparisons to practical approaches in the literature.

**Specific Recommendations**:
1. Revise all claims of novelty to accurately reflect prior work on weighted/stochastic block models and active learning in community detection.
2. Explicitly state in Theorem 2 that the lower bound holds for sufficiently large k (e.g., k ≥ k₀ for some constant k₀).
3. Improve writing quality by addressing grammatical errors, inconsistent capitalization, and unclear phrasing throughout the paper.
4. Clarify the distinction between Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms, ensuring "Las Vegas" is accompanied by "expected" query complexity.
5. Expand the experimental section to compare against relevant existing approaches (e.g., those by Mazumdar & Saha or Wang et al.).
6. Provide a more precise connection to SBM, explaining how their framework generalizes existing results and what new insights it provides about recovery thresholds.

**Overall Assessment**: This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to interactive clustering with side information, establishing tight bounds on query complexity. The core technical results are sound, but the paper would be significantly stronger with improved clarity, more accurate attribution of prior work, and better experimental validation. The writing quality needs substantial improvement to meet NIPS standards, though this is a separate issue from the technical contribution. With careful revision addressing the above points, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. The authors should prioritize clarifying the relationship to prior work and fixing the writing issues before final publication.
