This paper investigates the query complexity for clustering problems when "side information" is available. The side information is provided in the form of a similarity matrix W, where the entry Wij is distributed according to either f+ or f-, depending on whether i and j belong to the same cluster or different clusters. The main contribution of the paper is the demonstration that, without side information, clustering requires Theta(nk) queries, whereas with side information, only O(k log n/H^2(f+||f-) ) queries are sufficient, where H denotes the Hellinger distance between the two distributions. Furthermore, the authors show that this bound is tight up to a logarithmic factor.

I find this result to be theoretically interesting for several reasons. The problem is closely related to the stochastic block model (SBM), which has received significant attention in recent years, but it raises intriguing questions about how the availability of queries affects the threshold for clustering. The paper highlights the substantial impact of side information on query complexity, demonstrating that it can drastically alter the number of required queries. Although it is not surprising that side information can potentially contain complete cluster information, the authors provide a valuable contribution by identifying a method to assess the quality of the similarity matrix.
