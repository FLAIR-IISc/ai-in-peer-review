- Paper studies query complexity of clustering with same-cluster oracle plus side information W whose entries follow f+ (same cluster) or f− (different), unknown to algorithm and with unknown k.  
- Information-theoretic lower bound: any algorithm needs Ω(min{n k, k^2 / H^2(f+||f−)}) queries for exact recovery with constant probability (and Ω(n + min{n k, k^2 / H^2}) for probability 1).  
- Nearly matching upper bounds: efficient, parameter-free Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms recover clustering using O(min{n k, (poly(k)·log n) / H^2(f+||f−)}) queries, matching lower bounds up to log factors.  
- Methodological contributions: a new lower-bounding technique for adaptive interactive queries and an iterative-update framework that empirically estimates the Hellinger gap to decide when to trust W vs. querying.  
- Model generalizes SBM by allowing arbitrary unknown f+, f− (discrete/continuous), adversarial cluster sizes, and unknown large k, connecting SBM recovery thresholds to query budgets.  
- Technical soundness: proofs appear rigorous, lower bounds handle adaptivity, Hellinger distance use is natural, and upper bounds are constructive and computationally efficient (O(n^2)).  
- Key assumptions: noiseless oracle (noisy case deferred to companion work), algorithmic analysis focused on discrete distributions with fixed support, and independence of W entries.  
- Empirics: experiments on Cora show practicality and small gap between using exact vs. learned distributions, with query counts far below n k.  
- Clarity issues: typographical/notation inconsistencies (notably k vs. k^2), unclear statements of success probabilities and support-size assumptions, and iterative-update algorithm lacks concise pseudocode.  
- Originality: novel interactive lower bound for adaptive querying, parameter-free algorithm relying on empirical Hellinger gap, and precise dependence on H^2 are original.  
- Significance: near-complete characterization (up to log factors) of query complexity with side information, explaining practical reductions in query needs and influencing future interactive learning work.  
- Strengths: sharp adaptive lower bounds, efficient parameter-free near-matching algorithms, clear conceptual message about H^2 reducing queries, SBM connections, and practical relevance.  
- Weaknesses/questions: inconsistent k-dependence (O(k log n / H^2) vs O(k^2 log n / H^2)), limited extension discussion to continuous distributions, noiseless oracle assumption, O(n^2) preprocessing scalability, and sparse experimental baselines.  
- Suggested improvements: standardize query-complexity notation, add concise pseudocode and a phase figure, state f+, f− assumptions up front and summarize continuous-case coverage, include brief robustness discussion for noisy oracles, and expand empirical comparisons and sensitivity analyses.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong novel theory with tight bounds, parameter-free efficient algorithms, and meaningful connections to SBM and active learning.  
- Arguments against acceptance: minor clarity/notation issues, limited empirical evaluation and baseline comparisons, and main text assumes noiseless oracle and discrete distributions.  
- Overall: solid theoretical contribution with practical implications; accept with improvements in clarity and stronger experiments.
