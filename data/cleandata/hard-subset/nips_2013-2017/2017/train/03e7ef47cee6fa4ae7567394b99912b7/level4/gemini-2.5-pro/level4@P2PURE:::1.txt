This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when provided with "side information" in the form of a similarity matrix W. The entry W_ij is drawn from a distribution f+ if items i and j are in the same cluster, and from f- otherwise.

The paper's central result establishes that while clustering without side information requires Theta(nk) queries, access to side information reduces the sufficient number of queries to O(k log n / H^2(f+||f-)), where H is the Hellinger distance between the two distributions. The authors also show that this upper bound is tight up to a log(n) factor.

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. The work is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a topic of significant recent research, but it investigates how allowing active queries changes the recovery thresholds compared to passive observation. The paper demonstrates that access to side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this principle is not surprising—the side information could, in an extreme case, contain the complete cluster information—the paper's key contribution is to formalize the quality of the similarity matrix and precisely characterize its impact on query complexity.
