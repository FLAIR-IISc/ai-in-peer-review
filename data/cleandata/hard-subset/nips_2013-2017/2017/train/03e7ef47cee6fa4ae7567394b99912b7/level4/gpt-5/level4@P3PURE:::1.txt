This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information. The side information is given as a similarity matrix W, where each entry Wij is drawn from distribution f+ or f− depending on whether i and j belong to the same cluster or different clusters. The main result shows a sharp contrast: without side information, Θ(nk) queries are required, whereas with side information only O(k log n / H^2(f+||f−)) queries suffice, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. They further show this bound is tight up to a log n factor.

I find this an interesting theoretical contribution for several reasons. It is closely related to the stochastic block model (SBM), which has been extensively studied in recent years, and it asks how allowing queries changes the relevant information-theoretic thresholds. The results demonstrate that side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this is not surprising in the extreme case where the side information could encode the complete cluster structure, the paper’s contribution is to quantify the impact by identifying an interpretable measure—the Hellinger distance between f+ and f−—that captures the quality of the similarity matrix and governs the required number of queries.
