This paper analyzes the query complexity of clustering when augmented with side information. The side information is given as a similarity matrix W, where each entry W_ij is drawn from distribution f+ if i and j are in the same cluster, and from f− otherwise. The main result shows a sharp separation: without side information, Θ(nk) queries are required, whereas with side information O(k log n / H^2(f+, f−)) queries suffice, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors further show that this bound is tight up to a logarithmic factor in n.

I find these results theoretically compelling. The problem is closely related to the stochastic block model, which has seen substantial recent progress, yet this work focuses on how query access and side information shift fundamental limits. It demonstrates that side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this is not entirely surprising—since, in the extreme, side information might encode the full clustering—the paper offers a principled metric, via the Hellinger distance between f+ and f−, for assessing the quality of the similarity matrix.
