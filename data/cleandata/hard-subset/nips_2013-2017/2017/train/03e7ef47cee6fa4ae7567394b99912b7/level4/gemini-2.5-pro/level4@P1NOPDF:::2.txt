NOTE: This review pertains to one of two related submissions from the same authors. This paper provides upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, augmented by random side information for each pair. While I have some familiarity with the literature, my expertise is not comprehensive, making a definitive judgment on the contributions' novelty challenging. The paper is also quite dense, which, combined with time constraints, made it difficult to verify the correctness of all claims, particularly the long proof for the upper bound.

My overall impression is that the paper's contributions are likely valuable, assuming they are correct. However, the presentation is in serious need of improvement. Although the technical content is paramount, the sheer volume of writing issues makes my recommendation borderline. A revision cycle would have been beneficial for verifying improvements, but I understand this is not part of the NIPS review process.

Regarding this paper, there are several key issues:

1.  **Omission of Relevant Prior Work:** The paper omits several highly relevant works from its discussion and bibliography:
    -   The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) edges to general distributions is not a new idea. A search for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" reveals prior work on similar generalizations.
    -   There is existing work on the SBM that incorporates active querying. For instance, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) studies a model where one queries a node to receive its true label. For a fixed number of communities (k) and linear-sized communities, this model is functionally similar to the pairwise querying model presented here; one can simply find a representative node from each community via random sampling and then query new nodes against these representatives. At first glance, the lower bound in this paper seems to contradict the sublinear-sample upper bound in Gadde et al., but I suspect this is because the current paper's lower bound only holds in the k → ∞ regime (see below).

    In light of this prior art, claims of novelty such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes them," and "This is the first work that..." should be removed or substantially rephrased.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Problem Formulation:** The problem formulation lacks clarity in several respects. The paper appears to derive minimax bounds, but this is never explicitly stated. Furthermore, it is unclear what assumptions, if any, are made about the cluster sizes. For example, does the analysis account for highly unbalanced scenarios, such as k-1 clusters of size 1 and one large cluster of size n-k+1?

3.  **Potential Incorrectness of Theorem 2:** Theorem 2 appears to be incorrect as stated. For the SBM with a fixed number of communities (e.g., k=2), it is well-known that clustering is possible with zero queries (Q=0) even when the Hellinger distance between edge distributions scales as log(n)/n. The theorem, as written, seems to contradict this. A potential issue is that the proof appears to assume k → ∞, a critical condition that is not mentioned in the theorem statement. This condition must be stated explicitly. The authors should carefully verify whether the theorem, even with this change, contradicts other known results where clustering is possible without any queries.

4.  **Weak Numerical Section:** The numerical section offers limited value. The experiments compare the proposed methods only against trivial baselines, without benchmarking against any existing work from the literature. It is not clear why other relevant methods were not considered or implemented for comparison.

5.  **Clarity and Writing Style:** The paper suffers from numerous confusing sentences and a high density of grammatical errors. For example, phrases like "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" are incomplete, and the justification "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" is unclear, as that bound seems to apply only to the case without side information.
    Systemic issues include incorrect capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and use of articles (a/an/the). Comma usage is frequently incorrect (e.g., appearing after "let," "since," "note that"). Sentences often begin with conjunctions ("And," "Or"), and word choice is sometimes awkward ("else" is often used where "otherwise" would be more appropriate). Footnotes lack standard formatting. This is not an exhaustive list. Regardless of the final decision, the paper requires a thorough and careful proofreading to be suitable for publication.

**Some less significant comments:**
*   The abstract is overly long, and the paper takes three pages to state its main contributions, which is not ideal.
*   The use of the terms "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" may not be standard in this context. When "Las Vegas" is first introduced on page 4, it should be specified that it relates to *average* query complexity.
*   Footnote 1: The term `1-` after `p=` appears to be a typo.
*   In the formal problem statement, the phrase "find Q subset of V x V" incorrectly suggests the algorithm must be non-adaptive.
*   The claim of "clearly exhibiting" in the last sentence of the Simulations section is an overstatement.
*   Throughout the appendix, many mathematical delimiters (e.g., brackets) are improperly sized.

The author response has clarified several key points, and I have updated my recommendation to "accept". However, I strongly urge the authors to undertake a careful revision of the final manuscript, taking the comments above into account.

To be clear, my initial borderline recommendation was driven by the technical concerns raised above (e.g., missing citations, potential contradictions with prior work, and imprecisely stated theorems), not by presentational issues. The numerous grammatical and stylistic errors were a secondary, but still significant, concern.

I thank the authors for clarifying the relationship between their work and the Gadde et al. paper.

It is crucial that all theorems and lemmas are stated with full precision. For example, if a result only holds for sufficiently large k, this condition *must* be included in the theorem statement, not just mentioned in the proof.

Finally, regarding the specific language issues addressed in the response, I maintain that the paper's use of "Else," starting sentences with "And/Or/Plus," and phrases like "assume, otherwise..." is either grammatically incorrect or stylistically inappropriate for a formal academic paper. I hope the authors will reconsider these points during their revision.
