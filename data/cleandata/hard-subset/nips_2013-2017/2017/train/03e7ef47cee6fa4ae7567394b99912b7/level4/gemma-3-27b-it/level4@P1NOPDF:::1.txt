This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with ‘side information’. This side information takes the form of a similarity matrix W, where the entry Wij is distributed according to f+ if i and j belong to the same cluster, and f- otherwise. The main result of the paper demonstrates that while Θ(nk) queries are required for clustering *without* side information, only O(k log n / H²(f+ || f-)) queries suffice *with* side information, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors also prove that this bound is tight up to a log(n) factor.

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. It is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), an area that has received considerable attention in recent years, but frames an intriguing question regarding how the allowance of queries impacts the necessary threshold. The paper clearly shows that incorporating side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this reduction isn’t entirely surprising – given that the side information *could* potentially reveal complete cluster membership – the paper importantly identifies a way to quantify the quality of the similarity matrix and relate it to this reduction. 

The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis and the presented results are well-motivated within the context of existing clustering literature. The connection to SBM is valuable, highlighting the relevance of this work to a broader research community. The use of the Hellinger distance to characterize the quality of the similarity matrix is also a thoughtful choice.

However, I have a few minor points. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the practical implications could be further discussed. Specifically, how robust is this query complexity reduction to noise or imperfections in the similarity matrix W? A brief discussion of potential real-world scenarios and the applicability of the results in those contexts would strengthen the paper. Additionally, it would be helpful to briefly compare the assumptions made about the distribution of f+ and f- with those typically used in SBM literature.

Overall, this is a solid and well-written theoretical contribution. The results are significant and the analysis is sound. Addressing the minor points above would further enhance the paper’s impact. I recommend acceptance.
