

NOTE: I am reviewing two papers by the same authors addressing related topics (this paper focuses on noiseless pairwise comparisons with side information; the other examines noisy queries without side information).  

This paper establishes upper and lower bounds on the query complexity for clustering via noiseless pairwise comparisons with random side information per pair. While I am familiar with some related work, my incomplete knowledge makes it challenging to fully assess the novelty and significance of the contributions. Additionally, the paper's density impedes thorough verification of technical details, particularly the extensive upper bound proof.  

My overall assessment of both papers is that the contributions appear valuable if correct, but the writing requires substantial improvement. Although content outweighs presentation, the cumulative effect of numerous issues results in a borderline recommendation. A second round of revisions to address reviewer feedback would have been beneficial, though I acknowledge this is prohibited for NIPS.  

**Key omissions in related work:**  
- The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) beyond Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) to arbitrary distributions is not novel. Searches for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" reveal multiple prior studies.  
- Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models") studies SBM with an active learning querying component (single-node queries to infer labels). For fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-size communities, this model reduces to pairwise querying (via random sampling to identify seed nodes, then pairwise queries for new nodes). The paper’s lower bound appears to contradict Gadde’s sublinear-sample upper bound, but this discrepancy likely stems from the lower bound’s implicit k→∞ assumption (not stated in Theorem 2).  

**Required revisions to avoid overstatement:**  
The authors must remove or rephrase all claims such as:  
- "We initiate a rigorous theoretical study"  
- "Significantly generalizes them"  
- "This is the first work that..."  
- "Generalize them in a significant way"  
- "There is no systematic theoretical study"  

**Technical concerns:**  
1. **Model clarity:** The minimax framework is not explicitly stated. Clarify whether cluster size constraints (e.g., allowing k−1 singleton clusters and one large cluster) are imposed.  
2. **Theorem 2 flaw:** The stated bound is incorrect for fixed k=O(1), where clustering may require zero queries (e.g., when Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n). The theorem must explicitly state the k→∞ regime.  
3. **Numerical results:** The simulations lack meaningful comparisons against existing methods, relying only on trivial baselines. Justify why no comparable approaches exist.  
4. **Ambiguous phrasing:**  
   - "Assume, otherwise the clustering is done" (incomplete sentence; clarify the assumption).  
   - "The theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" (the bound assumes no side information; clarify applicability).  
5. **Writing quality:** The manuscript contains pervasive grammatical errors, typos, and stylistic issues:  
   - Incorrect articles (a/an/the), plurals, capitalization, and spacing.  
   - Misplaced commas (e.g., after "let," "we have," "this means," "assume that," "since," "while," "note that").  
   - Sentences starting with "And," "Or," "Plus."  
   - Misuse of "else" (typically "otherwise" is preferable).  
   - Footnotes lacking capitalization and terminal punctuation.  
   - *Note:* These issues are not the primary reason for the borderline recommendation but require correction for publication.  

**Minor comments:**  
- Abstract is overly lengthy; avoid excessive preliminary content.  
- Clarify "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology (e.g., specify "average query complexity" when first introducing Las Vegas).  
- Footnote 1: Remove the extraneous "1-" after p=?.  
- Problem statement: "Find Q ⊆ V × V" implies non-adaptive algorithms; clarify if adaptive querying is permitted.  
- Disagree with "clearly exhibiting" in the Simulations section’s final sentence.  
- Appendix: Many brackets are inadequately sized.
