Summary
This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with noiseless pairwise comparisons supplemented by random side information on each pair. It presents upper and lower bounds and discusses both Monte Carlo and Las Vegas variants. I am reviewing this alongside a companion paper by (apparently) the same authors on noisy queries without side information. While I am broadly familiar with the area, my coverage is not exhaustive, and the paper is very dense; I could not fully verify the long upper-bound proof. My overall impression is that the technical contributions are potentially valuable, but the writing and presentation require substantial improvement. Before the author response my recommendation was borderline; after clarification I am updating to acceptance, contingent on careful revision.

Relation to prior work and novelty claims
- The idea of replacing Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) edges in SBM with a general pairwise distribution is not new. The “labeled/labelled stochastic block model” and the “weighted SBM” have been studied in multiple papers. Please cite and discuss these works and position your results relative to them.
- There is related work on active learning in SBM: Gadde et al., “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (ISIT 2016). The querying model differs (node-label queries), but for fixed k and linear-size communities, node-label queries and pairwise queries can simulate each other up to a small overhead. Please clarify the relationship and differences in assumptions and guarantees.
- In light of the above, please temper statements such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “this is the first work,” and “generalize them in a significant way.” The contributions may still be strong, but the novelty should be stated precisely and conservatively.

Technical clarity and correctness
- Model specification: It appears you are studying minimax query complexity, but this is not stated explicitly. Please make this explicit and describe the adversarial class clearly. Do you impose any constraints on cluster sizes (e.g., balance, minimum size)? Are extreme cases like k−1 singleton clusters plus one large cluster allowed?
- Assumptions on k: Theorem 2, as currently stated, appears problematic. For k = 2 (and likely any fixed k), there are regimes where exact recovery is possible with Q = 0 even when the Hellinger distance scales as log n / n. Your lower bound seems to rely on k → ∞; if so, this must be stated in the theorem and in any corollaries depending on it. Please also check carefully that no theorem contradicts known Q = 0 recovery regimes for fixed k.
- Adaptivity: The formal problem statement “find Q ⊆ V × V” reads as non-adaptive. If the algorithm is adaptive, please state that queries may depend on prior answers and on side information.
- Monte Carlo vs. Las Vegas: Please define these terms precisely and consistently. When you first introduce Las Vegas, explicitly connect it to average query complexity (the word “average” was missing).
- Numerical section: The simulation study compares only with trivial baselines and does not engage with existing algorithms from related literatures (e.g., spectral or likelihood-based methods using side information). Please either strengthen the comparisons or clearly justify the choices and the insights the experiments provide.
- Notation and footnotes: Check Footnote 1 (does the “1−” after p= belong?). Several equations have mismatched or too-small brackets; please standardize.

Writing and presentation
- The paper contains many grammatical errors and unclear sentences. Please perform a thorough language edit. Common issues include:
  - Articles (a/an/the), plurals, capitalization, spacing, and misuse of commas (often incorrectly placed after “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that”).
  - Avoid starting sentences with “And/Or/Plus.” Prefer “otherwise” over “else” in prose.
  - Footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a period.
  - Do not place commas immediately before displayed equations unless grammatically required.
- Unclear or confusing phrases to revise:
  - “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (appears to be missing text after “assume”).
  - “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound apply only with no side information? Clarify scope and assumptions).
- Structure: The abstract is too long, and the paper spends roughly three pages before clearly stating the main contributions. Please tighten the abstract and bring the contributions and main results (with assumptions) forward.

Additional minor comments
- “Clearly exhibiting” in the Simulations section seems overstated; please soften or justify.
- Ensure that all theorems and lemmas are stated self-contained with all necessary conditions (e.g., whether k is fixed or grows, distributional assumptions, side-information model).
- Many brackets and delimiters are visually too small in the appendix; adjust sizes for readability.

Post-author feedback
- Thank you for clarifying the relationship to Gadde et al. and for addressing parts of the lower bound issue. Based on your responses, I am updating my recommendation to acceptance.
- However, it is essential that the final version states assumptions precisely in the theorem statements themselves (e.g., whether results hold only for sufficiently large k, or under particular side-information distributions), not only in the proofs.
- To reiterate, comments about capitalization and commas were not decisive for the initial borderline assessment; they were intended as guidance for revision. That said, a careful language edit will materially improve readability and impact, especially for readers outside the authors’ immediate community.
- Please revisit the specific phrasing concerns (“Else/And/Or/Plus,” “assume, otherwise…,” etc.). Even if some uses might be defensible, they are generally unsuitable for a formal academic manuscript.

Overall evaluation
- Contributions: Potentially significant bounds for clustering with pairwise queries and side information; if correct, they advance understanding of query efficiency in this setting.
- Concerns: Missing citations and overstatement of novelty; insufficiently precise theorem statements; unclear model assumptions; limited empirical evaluation; substantial writing issues.
- Recommendation: Accept, conditional on a careful and thorough revision that (i) precisely states all assumptions and regimes, (ii) positions the work within the labeled/weighted SBM and active-learning literature, (iii) strengthens or better motivates the empirical study, and (iv) substantially improves clarity and grammar.
