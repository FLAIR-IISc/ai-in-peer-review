This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering *n* items into *k* clusters. The learning model is based on noiseless pairwise comparison queries, where an oracle reveals whether two items belong to the same cluster. The key novelty is the inclusion of random side-information for every pair, modeled as a draw from one of two distributions depending on the ground-truth clustering.

**Overall Impression**

The problem studied is theoretically interesting and extends existing work on query-based clustering in a meaningful way. The information-theoretic approach to establishing lower bounds and the development of algorithms to match them are valuable contributions. However, the paper in its current form suffers from significant issues in its presentation, clarity, and positioning within the relevant literature. While the technical contributions appear promising, the quality of the writing makes it difficult to fully verify their correctness and appreciate their novelty. My recommendation is therefore borderline, but I lean towards acceptance on the condition that the authors undertake a major revision to address the points raised below.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Clarity of Problem Formulation:** The problem setup should be defined more precisely at the outset. While the paper seems to be focused on deriving minimax bounds, this is not stated explicitly. Furthermore, it is unclear what assumptions are made about the cluster sizes. Does the analysis hold for any partition of *n* items into *k* clusters, including highly imbalanced cases (e.g., *k*-1 clusters of size 1)? Such assumptions are critical to the interpretation of the results and should be clearly articulated.

2.  **Positioning and Citation of Prior Work:** The paper overstates its novelty and fails to cite several highly relevant lines of research. Claims such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study" or "This is the first work that..." should be removed or substantially rephrased in light of the following:
    *   The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) edges to more general distributions is not new. A significant body of work exists under the names "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" that explores such extensions.
    *   The problem of active learning for community detection has also been studied before. For example, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) explores a model with node-based queries. While the query model is different, the connection is strong and warrants a thorough discussion, especially concerning how the bounds compare and contrast.

3.  **Correctness and Precision of Theoretical Claims (Theorem 2):** The statement of Theorem 2 appears to be incorrect or at least incomplete. For a constant *k* (e.g., *k*=2), it is well-known that for certain parameter regimes of the SBM, perfect clustering is possible with high probability using zero queries (Q=0), relying solely on the side-information. The theorem's lower bound seems to contradict this. The proof appears to rely on an unstated assumption that *k* -> ∞. If a result only holds under specific asymptotic conditions (e.g., for sufficiently large *k*), this *must* be stated explicitly in the theorem itself, not just buried within the proof. All theoretical claims should be carefully checked for precision and completeness.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations section is currently weak. The proposed algorithms are only compared against trivial baselines. To demonstrate the practical value of the contributions, the experiments should include comparisons against other relevant existing methods. If no direct competitors exist, the authors should justify their choice of baselines more rigorously or consider adapting methods from related areas.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Writing and Readability:** The paper is hindered by a very large number of grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Issues with capitalization, pluralization, spacing, articles (a/an/the), and comma usage are pervasive. For instance, commas are frequently misplaced after phrases like "we have," or "since." Sentences should not begin with "And," "Or," or "Plus," and the word "else" is often used where "otherwise" would be more appropriate. A thorough and careful proofreading, ideally with the help of a professional editing service or a native English-speaking colleague, is essential before final publication.

*   **Structure:** The introduction feels overly long. The core contributions should be presented more concisely and earlier in the paper, ideally within the first two pages.

*   **Specific Sentences/Phrases for Revision:**
    *   The phrase "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" is incomplete and confusing. Please rewrite for clarity.
    *   The justification "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" is unclear. Please explain this logic, as the *nk* bound seems to apply to the case without side information.
    *   The claim of "clearly exhibiting" in the last sentence of the Simulations section is not well-supported by the current results.

*   **Terminology and Notation:**
    *   Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" standard for this type of analysis? When "Las Vegas" is first introduced on page 4, it should be specified that this refers to *average* query complexity.
    *   In the formal problem statement, "find Q subset of V x V" suggests a non-adaptive algorithm, which seems to contradict the nature of the proposed methods. Please clarify.
    *   Many mathematical expressions in the appendix use brackets that are too small, making them difficult to parse.

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem and likely contains valuable theoretical results. However, its significant presentational flaws obscure these contributions and prevent a confident assessment. I strongly urge the authors to perform a major revision to improve the clarity, positioning, and precision of their work.
