This work investigates the query complexity of clustering when provided with side information. This side information is modeled as a similarity matrix W, where each entry Wij is drawn from a distribution f+ if items i and j are in the same cluster, and from f- otherwise. The central result is a demonstration that, in contrast to the Theta(nk) queries required without side information, only O(k log n / H^2(f+||f-)) queries are sufficient when it is available, where H is the Hellinger distance. The authors also establish a nearly matching lower bound, showing this result is tight up to a log(n) factor.

I consider this to be a compelling theoretical contribution. The problem is closely related to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), an active area of recent research, but it poses the interesting question of how allowing queries affects the recovery threshold. The results clearly show that side information can drastically reduce query complexity. While this principle may not be surprising in itself, as perfect side information could contain the complete clustering, the paper's key insight is in identifying how to formally quantify the quality of the similarity matrix and its impact.
