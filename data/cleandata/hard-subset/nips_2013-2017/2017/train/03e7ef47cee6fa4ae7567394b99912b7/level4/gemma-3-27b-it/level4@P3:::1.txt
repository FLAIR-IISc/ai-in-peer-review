This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix *W*. The authors demonstrate that while clustering without side information requires Θ(*nk*) queries, incorporating a similarity matrix reduces the query complexity to *O*( *k* log *n* / *H*<sup>2</sup>(*f*<sub>+</sub> || *f*<sub>-</sub>) ), where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance between the distributions *f*<sub>+</sub> and *f*<sub>-</sub>. Crucially, they prove that this reduction is information-theoretically optimal, up to a log(*n*) factor.

This work presents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. The problem setting, bridging traditional clustering with active learning, is highly relevant to modern machine learning applications where labeled data is expensive. The connection drawn to the stochastic block model (SBM) is insightful; by framing the problem in this context, the authors leverage a rich body of existing work while highlighting how the ability to pose queries fundamentally alters the recovery threshold. The demonstration of a dramatic reduction in query complexity with side information, and the quantification of this reduction in terms of the Hellinger distance, is a valuable result. The fact that the result is parameter-free – not requiring knowledge of *k*, *f*<sub>+</sub>, or *f*<sub>-</sub> – enhances its practical appeal.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous information-theoretic lower and upper bounds on query complexity, establishing a clear understanding of the achievable performance.
*   **Novel Contribution:**  The analysis of query complexity *with* side information is a novel and important direction, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Connection to SBM:** The link to the well-studied SBM provides context and allows for comparisons with existing results, showcasing the benefits of the interactive query setting.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require prior knowledge of the cluster number, or the underlying distributions, making them directly applicable to real-world scenarios.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is clearly defined and motivated by practical applications like entity resolution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** While the algorithm is outlined, the description could benefit from a more detailed walkthrough with a small example to illustrate how it operates in practice. The iterative-update aspect and the criteria for stopping each phase could be more clearly explained.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results, while helpful, are limited to a single dataset. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a wider range of datasets, with varying characteristics (e.g., different degrees of noise, different cluster structures), would strengthen the paper.
*   **Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical aspects. A brief discussion of the practical challenges of implementing and deploying these algorithms in real-world systems (e.g., oracle cost, query selection strategies beyond the greedy approach) would be beneficial.
*   **Related Work Coverage:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, briefly mentioning recent advancements in active learning for graph clustering (beyond entity resolution) could broaden the context. For example, [Gadde et al., 2016] and [Verroios et al., 2015] are relevant.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information.
*   The results are rigorous, well-supported by proofs, and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing bounds.
*   The connection to the SBM and the parameter-free nature of the algorithms add to the value of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited experimental evaluation and the lack of detailed discussion on practical considerations might raise concerns about the immediate applicability of the results.
*   The algorithm description could be more detailed and accessible.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of clustering and active learning. The rigorous analysis, novel contribution, and connection to established concepts make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focused on clarifying the algorithm description and expanding the experimental evaluation.
