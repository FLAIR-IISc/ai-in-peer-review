- Paper gives upper and lower bounds on query complexity for clustering with noiseless pairwise queries and random pairwise side-information; contributions appear valuable if correct.  
- Presentation is very dense and long, making correctness checking (especially the long upper bound proof) difficult.  
- Writing quality is poor: many grammar mistakes, typos, unclear sentences, and awkward phrasing throughout.  
- Authors overstate novelty; several claims like "first work" or "no systematic study" should be removed or reworded.  
- Missing related work: labeled/weighted stochastic block model literature generalizing Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) is not cited.  
- Missing related work: Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) on active learning for community detection, which relates to the querying model.  
- Model and problem statement need clearer presentation: minimax setup not explicitly stated and cluster-size restrictions (e.g., tiny clusters) are unclear.  
- Theorem 2 likely incorrect or misstated: seems to implicitly assume k → ∞ and may contradict known SBM cases where Q=0 suffices.  
- Numerical experiments are weak: compare only to trivial baselines and do not benchmark against existing methods.  
- Many confusing phrases and apparent missing text (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done") reduce clarity.  
- Some proofs/claims appear to misuse or ignore side-information bounds (e.g., "nk lower bound" comment).  
- Terminology concerns: use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" should be standard and Las Vegas should be paired with "average" query complexity when introduced.  
- Typographical/formal issues: Footnote 1 and p= expression may have incorrect signs; small/bracket sizing issues in the appendix.  
- Formal problem formulation "find Q subset of V x V" sounds non-adaptive and should clarify adaptivity.  
- Abstract is too long and the paper takes ~3 pages before stating contributions.  
- Specific phrasing issues: misuse of commas after phrases like "let"/"assume that"; sentences starting with "And/Or/Plus"; strange use of "else".  
- Qualitative summary: technically promising but hard to verify; clarity is poor; originality overstated; significance plausible but uncertain.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically grounded bounds, well-motivated problem, efficient parameter-free algorithms.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor writing, inadequate contextualization with prior work, weak experiments, potential errors (esp. Theorem 2), and overstated novelty.
