This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering problems when leveraging ‘side information’ in the form of a similarity matrix, W. Specifically, W’s entries (Wij) are distributed according to either f+ or f-, indicating whether nodes i and j belong to the same or different clusters, respectively. The core contribution demonstrates a significant reduction in query requirements: while clustering without side information necessitates Θ(nk) queries, the authors prove that only O(k log n / H^2(f+||f-)) queries suffice when W is available, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. Furthermore, they establish the near-optimality of this bound, up to a log(n) factor. 

I consider this a compelling theoretical advancement. The work builds upon the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM) framework, a currently active area of research, and thoughtfully explores the impact of query access on clustering thresholds. The findings clearly illustrate the substantial benefits of incorporating side information on query complexity – a result intuitively plausible given the potential for W to encode complete cluster assignments. Importantly, the paper also provides a method for quantifying the utility of the similarity matrix, W, based on its quality as measured by the Hellinger distance.
