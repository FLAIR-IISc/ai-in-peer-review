This paper gives upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, along with random side-information associated with every pair. While I am familiar with some of the related literature, my knowledge of it is far from complete, so it's a little hard to fully judge the value of the contributions. The paper is also so dense that I couldn't spend as much time as ideal checking the correctness – especially in the long upper bound proof. 

My overall impression is that the contributions seem valuable (if correct), but the writing could be significantly improved. I acknowledge that the contributions are more important than the writing, but there are so many issues that my recommendation was initially borderline. Another round of reviews to check suggestions would have been helpful, but I understand this is not possible for NIPS. 

There are a few relevant works that were not cited:
- The idea of replacing Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) by a general distribution in the stochastic block model is not new. A Google (or Google Scholar) search for "labeled stochastic block model" or the less common "weighted stochastic block model" reveals several papers that have looked at similar generalizations.
- There is also at least one work studying the SBM with an extra querying (active learning) element: "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016). It uses a different querying model – querying a single node and getting its label – but for fixed *k* (e.g., *k*=2 or *k*=100) and linear-size communities, this is conceptually similar to your pairwise querying model. One could quickly find a node from each community via random sampling, and then obtain the label of any new node with a pairwise query. Initially, it seemed your lower bound might contradict their (sublinear-sample) upper bound, but this is likely because your lower bound holds as *k* approaches infinity (see below).

In light of these works, I suggest the authors remove or re-word statements along the lines of "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study", "significantly generalizes them", "This is the first work that...", "generalize them in a significant way", "There is no systematic theoretical study", etc.

Here are some further comments:
- The model/problem should be presented more clearly. It looks like you are considering minimax bounds, but this isn't stated explicitly. Moreover, is the minimax model without restriction on cluster sizes? For instance, do you allow the case where there are *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one of size *n*-*k*+1?
- Theorem 2 appears incorrect as stated. For the SBM with *k*=2 (or likely any *k*=O(1)), no queries are required in certain cases where the Hellinger distance scales as log(*n*)/ *n*. The proof seems to assume *k* approaches infinity, which is not stated in the theorem and needs to be explicitly clarified. I'm not sure if this is the only issue – please carefully check whether Theorem 2 generally contradicts cases where clustering is possible with *Q*=0.
- The numerical section isn't particularly valuable because it doesn’t compare against existing works, only against trivial baselines. Are your methods the only ones that can be considered/implemented?
- Several sentences are confusing or unclear, like "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" (is text missing after “assume?”) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound only apply without side information?).
- The paper contains many grammar mistakes, typos, and awkward wordings. Issues with capitalization, plurals, spacing, and articles (a/an/the) are frequent. Commas are often misused (e.g., they generally shouldn’t follow “let”, “we have”, “this means”, “assume that”, “since”, “while”, “note that”, and rarely precede an equation). Sentences shouldn’t start with “And”, “Or”, “Plus”. “Else” is used strangely; “otherwise” is often more appropriate. Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This list isn’t exhaustive, and I strongly urge the authors to proofread the paper carefully.

Some less significant comments:
- The abstract is too long, and it’s generally best to avoid spending three pages before presenting the main contributions.
- Is your use of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” standard terminology? Also, when first mentioning “Las Vegas” on page 4, mention AVERAGE query complexity (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the 1- really be there after *p*= ?
- In the formal problem statement, "find Q subset of V x V" sounds like the algorithm is non-adaptive.
- I disagree with the statement “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets are too small throughout the appendix.
