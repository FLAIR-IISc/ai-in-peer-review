- Paper gives upper and lower bounds on query complexity for noiseless pairwise comparisons with random side-information; contributions look valuable if correct.  
- Dense writing and very long, complex proofs made verification of correctness difficult.  
- Several relevant prior works are missing from citations, notably labeled/weighted SBM literature and Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) on active learning for SBM.  
- Claims of being first or significantly generalizing prior work are inaccurate and should be removed or rephrased.  
- The model and problem statement need clearer exposition: state whether minimax bounds are considered and any cluster-size restrictions.  
- Theorem 2 appears incorrect as stated; it implicitly requires k → ∞ and that condition should be explicit in the theorem.  
- The lower bound may superficially contradict Gadde et al., but the discrepancy likely arises because the lower bound only holds for large k — this must be clarified.  
- Numerical experiments are weak: they only compare to trivial baselines and omit comparisons with existing methods.  
- Numerous unclear sentences and grammatical errors make the paper hard to read and affect assessment.  
- Many stylistic and typographical issues (capitalization, pluralization, spacing, comma misuse, sentences beginning with "And/Or/Plus") should be fixed.  
- The abstract is too long and the main contributions are not introduced early enough.  
- Terminology usage ("Monte Carlo", "Las Vegas") is nonstandard and the first mention of Las Vegas omits "average" query complexity.  
- Footnote 1 likely contains an error ("1-" after "p=") that needs correction.  
- The formal problem statement's wording ("find Q subset of V x V") unintentionally implies non-adaptive algorithms and should be clarified.  
- Several minor presentation issues: tiny brackets in the appendix and disputed phrasing like "clearly exhibiting" in simulations.  
- Post-author feedback raised confidence and updated the recommendation toward acceptance, but a careful revision addressing all noted issues is still required.  
- Precision in theorem/lemma statements is crucial (e.g., explicitly state "for sufficiently large k" where needed).  
- Authors should revise awkward sentence constructions (e.g., "assume, otherwise...") to conform to standard academic English.
