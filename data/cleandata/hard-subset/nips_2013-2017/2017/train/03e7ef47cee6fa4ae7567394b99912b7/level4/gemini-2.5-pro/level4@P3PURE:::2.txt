**Summary**
This paper provides upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, supplemented with random side-information for each pair. While I have some familiarity with the related literature, my expertise is not exhaustive, which makes a full evaluation of the contributions challenging. Furthermore, the paper is quite dense, and I was unable to verify the correctness of all proofs—particularly the lengthy upper bound proof—in the time available.

My overall impression is that the paper's contributions, if correct, are valuable. However, the quality of the writing is a significant concern and detracts from the work, making my recommendation borderline. I acknowledge that the technical contributions are more important than the presentation, but the sheer number of issues is problematic. A review cycle that allowed for checking revisions would be beneficial in this case, though I understand this is not part of the NIPS process.

**Major Comments**

*   **Missing Citations and Novelty Claims:** The paper overlooks several highly relevant works and, as a result, overstates its novelty.
    *   The generalization of the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) to a more general distribution is not new. A search for "labeled stochastic block model" or "weighted stochastic block model" will reveal prior work on similar generalizations.
    *   There is existing work on the SBM that incorporates an active learning or querying component. For instance, "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016) uses a different querying model (querying a node for its label), but for a fixed number of linear-sized communities (e.g., k=2 or k=100), it is functionally similar to the pairwise model presented here. One could simply find a representative node from each community through random sampling and then determine any new node's label via pairwise queries. At first glance, the lower bound in this paper seems to contradict the sublinear-sample upper bound in Gadde et al., though this may be resolved if the lower bound here only holds as k → ∞ (see next point).
    *   In light of this prior work, the authors should remove or rephrase all claims such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes them," "This is the first work that...," and "There is no systematic theoretical study."

*   **Clarity and Correctness:**
    *   The problem formulation could be significantly clarified. The paper appears to study minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. It is also unclear if any restrictions are placed on the cluster sizes (e.g., are highly imbalanced clusters with k-1 singletons permitted?).
    *   More critically, Theorem 2 does not appear to be correct as stated. For the SBM with a constant number of communities (e.g., k=2), it is known that clustering is possible with zero queries (Q=0) in regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The theorem seems to contradict this. I suspect this is because the proof assumes k → ∞, a condition that is not mentioned in the theorem statement but is essential for its validity. The authors should carefully check whether the theorem contradicts other known results where Q=0.

*   **Experiments:** The numerical section is of limited value as it only compares against trivial baselines and does not include any existing methods. It is unclear if the proposed methods are the only ones applicable to this problem.

*   **Writing and Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous issues with clarity, grammar, and style.
    *   Sentences are often confusing or incomplete (e.g., "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound," where the latter seems to ignore the role of side information).
    *   There are pervasive grammatical errors, including incorrect capitalization, plurals, spacing, and use of articles (a/an/the). Comma usage is frequently incorrect (e.g., commas should generally not follow phrases like "we have," "since," "assume that," or "note that"). Sentences should not begin with conjunctions like "And," "Or," or "Plus." The word "else" is often used where "otherwise" would be more appropriate. Footnotes should be formatted as complete sentences. This list is not exhaustive, and I strongly urge the authors to proofread the manuscript with great care.

**Minor Comments**
*   The abstract is too long, and dedicating nearly three pages to setup before presenting the main contributions is not ideal.
*   Is the use of "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" terminology standard in this context? When "Las Vegas" is first introduced on page 4, it should be specified that it relates to the *average* query complexity.
*   Footnote 1: The expression `p = 1 - ...` appears to be incorrect.
*   In the formal problem statement, the phrase "find Q subset of V x V" suggests a non-adaptive algorithm, which may not be the intention.
*   I disagree with the assertion "clearly exhibiting" in the final sentence of the Simulations section.
*   Many brackets in the appendix are incorrectly sized for the expressions they contain.
