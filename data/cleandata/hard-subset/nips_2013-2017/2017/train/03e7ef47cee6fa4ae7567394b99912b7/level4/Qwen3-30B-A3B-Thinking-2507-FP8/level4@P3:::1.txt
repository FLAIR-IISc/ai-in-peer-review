

This paper investigates the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information, a problem that has gained practical relevance in applications like entity resolution where human feedback is used to improve clustering accuracy. The authors consider a setting where an algorithm can query an oracle about whether two elements belong to the same cluster, while simultaneously having access to a similarity matrix W where W_ij is drawn from distribution f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and f- otherwise. The key contribution is establishing the precise query complexity of this problem: without side information, Θ(nk) queries are necessary and sufficient, while with side information, the complexity reduces to O(k² log n / H²(f+‖f-)), where H denotes the Hellinger divergence. The authors prove this bound is tight up to a logarithmic factor.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides the first rigorous theoretical framework for interactive clustering with side information, addressing a gap in the literature where previous work focused on heuristic approaches without theoretical guarantees. The authors develop a novel technique for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings, which could have broader applications beyond clustering. Second, the work establishes a meaningful connection to the stochastic block model (SBM), a well-studied model for community detection. The authors generalize SBM by allowing arbitrary distributions f+ and f- rather than restricting to Bernoulli distributions, and they do not assume knowledge of k or cluster size distributions, making their framework more broadly applicable.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong theoretical foundation. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by connecting it to practical applications like entity resolution, where the theoretical query complexity gap (Ω(nk) vs. O(k² log n / H²(f+‖f-))) explains why heuristic approaches using side information require significantly fewer queries than the theoretical worst-case bound. The technical sections are well-structured, with careful definitions and rigorous proofs of both upper and lower bounds. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of how their results relate to previous work on SBM, noting that their framework extends existing results by allowing arbitrary distributions and adversarial cluster assignments.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The quality of the theoretical contributions is excellent, with tight bounds proven through novel techniques. The originality is high, as the authors identify a fundamental gap in the literature and provide a comprehensive solution. The significance is substantial, both theoretically (advancing our understanding of query complexity in interactive settings) and practically (providing a theoretical foundation for heuristic approaches used in entity resolution and other applications). The algorithms presented are computationally efficient, parameter-free (requiring no knowledge of k, f+, or f-), and have practical implementation details.

While the paper is strong, there are a few minor weaknesses. The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (Cora), which, while sufficient to demonstrate the algorithm's performance in the theoretical framework, would benefit from additional validation on more diverse datasets. Additionally, the paper assumes a noiseless oracle, which is a reasonable simplification for theoretical analysis but could be briefly discussed in relation to practical scenarios where oracle responses may contain errors.

The work relates well to previous NIPS contributions. It builds upon the extensive literature on SBM (e.g., Abbe et al. 2015, 2016, Mossel et al. 2014) by introducing a novel interactive element that changes the fundamental query complexity. The authors properly contextualize their work against related contributions, noting that previous work on clustering with queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al. 2016, Balcan et al. 2010) either focused on different query models or did not provide theoretical guarantees for arbitrary similarity distributions.

Arguments for acceptance:
1. Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of query complexity for interactive clustering with side information
2. Establishes tight bounds (up to logarithmic factors) that explain the empirical success of heuristic approaches
3. Develops a novel technique for proving lower bounds in interactive settings with broad potential applications
4. Connects to the well-studied SBM while generalizing it in meaningful ways
5. Presents efficient, parameter-free algorithms with clear practical relevance

Arguments against acceptance:
1. Limited experimental validation on real-world datasets
2. Theoretical focus may make the paper less accessible to some practitioners
3. Brief discussion of error-prone oracles could be expanded

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering, which has both fundamental importance and practical relevance. The results provide a much-needed theoretical foundation for heuristic approaches that have been used in practice for years. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. While the experimental section could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it represents a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering and provides a rigorous foundation for practical applications. The work demonstrates excellent technical depth, clear exposition, and significant novelty in the context of NIPS research.
