This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when “side information” is available. The side information is given as a similarity matrix W, where each entry Wij is drawn from f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and from f− if they are in different clusters. The main result shows that, without side information, Θ(nk) queries are necessary, whereas with side information only O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f−)) queries suffice, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors also prove that this bound is tight up to a log n factor.

I find this result theoretically interesting for several reasons. It is closely related to the stochastic block model, which has been studied intensively in recent years, but it asks a complementary question about how allowing queries affects the statistical and computational thresholds. The paper demonstrates that side information can dramatically reduce the number of required queries. While this is not entirely surprising—since, in principle, the side information could perfectly reveal the clusters—the contribution here is to quantify how the quality of the similarity matrix impacts query complexity. In particular, using the Hellinger distance between f+ and f− provides a clean and interpretable measure of signal strength that governs the query/sample trade-off.
