This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering in a setting where side information is available. This information is modeled as a similarity matrix W, where each entry W_ij is sampled from one of two distributions—f+ if items i and j are in the same cluster, and f- if they are in different clusters.

The central result is a demonstration of a significant reduction in query complexity enabled by this side information. Compared to the baseline of Θ(nk) queries required in the standard setting (without side information), the authors show that O(k log n / H²(f+ || f-)) queries are sufficient. Here, H²(f+ || f-) represents the squared Hellinger distance between the two distributions, which quantifies the quality of the side information. This upper bound is shown to be nearly tight, matching a corresponding lower bound up to a logarithmic factor in n.

This is a compelling theoretical result for several reasons. The work is well-situated within the active research area of the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) but poses a novel and interesting question: how do active queries alter the information-theoretic thresholds for community recovery? The paper's key contribution is that it moves beyond the simple intuition that side information should be helpful. While it is not surprising that query complexity can be reduced—as the side information could, in the extreme, perfectly encode the true clustering—the authors provide a precise characterization of this relationship. They identify the squared Hellinger distance as the crucial quantity that measures the quality of the similarity matrix, directly linking the statistical distinguishability of the clusters from the side information to the resulting query complexity.
