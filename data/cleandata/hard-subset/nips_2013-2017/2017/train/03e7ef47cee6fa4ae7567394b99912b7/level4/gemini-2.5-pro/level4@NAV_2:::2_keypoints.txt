- Main contribution: characterizes how noisy similarity side information reduces interactive clustering query complexity, giving info-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching efficient, parameter-free upper bounds.  
- Key technical result: query complexity decreases from Θ(nk) without side information to a quantity determined by squared Hellinger divergence between same-cluster and different-cluster similarity distributions.  
- Connection: model generalizes the Stochastic Block Model (SBM) from Bernoulli to arbitrary similarity distributions.  

Strengths:
- Well-motivated problem with practical relevance (e.g., entity resolution, human-in-the-loop annotation).  
- Strong theoretical contribution: rigorous upper and lower bounds and an elegant characterization via Hellinger divergence.  
- Algorithms are parameter-free (do not require k or the underlying distributions), improving practical applicability.  

Weaknesses:
- Positioning/related work is overstated; prior works on labeled/weighted SBMs and active community detection (e.g., Gadde et al. ISIT 2016) are not adequately discussed.  
- Potential correctness issue in Theorem 2: statement may contradict known SBM results (exact recovery without queries for constant k); likely missing assumptions (e.g., k growing with n) that must be stated.  
- Model clarity lacking: unclear whether results are minimax and what assumptions on cluster sizes (e.g., highly imbalanced clusters) are allowed.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak, comparing only to trivial baselines and not to existing methods.  
- Writing quality is poor with many grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing, making the paper hard to read.  

Minor points:
- Introduction is overly long (nearly three pages) before stating main contributions.  
- Problem statement "find Q ⊆ V × V" suggests non-adaptive querying, contradicting the interactive setting and needing clarification.  
- Small typos (e.g., "1-" after "p=" in Footnote 1).  

Overall verdict:
- Borderline recommendation: important and potentially solid contribution but requires fixes to a main theoretical claim, stronger related-work positioning, clearer model assumptions, better experiments, and major rewriting.
