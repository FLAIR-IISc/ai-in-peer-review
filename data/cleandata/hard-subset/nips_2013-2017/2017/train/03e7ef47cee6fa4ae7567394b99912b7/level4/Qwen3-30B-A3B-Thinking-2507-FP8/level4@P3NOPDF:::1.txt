

This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering when augmented with side information in the form of a similarity matrix $W$. Each entry $W_{ij}$ is independently drawn from a distribution $f_+$ if nodes $i$ and $j$ belong to the same cluster, or from $f_-$ if they belong to different clusters. The authors establish a sharp theoretical separation: without side information, clustering requires $\Theta(nk)$ queries, where $n$ is the number of nodes and $k$ the number of clusters. With the similarity matrix, they prove that $O\left(k \log n / H^2(f_+ \| f_-)\right)$ queries suffice, where $H$ denotes the Hellinger distance between $f_+$ and $f_-$, and further show that this bound is tight up to logarithmic factors in $n$.  

The result is theoretically significant for two key reasons. First, it directly connects to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a central framework in community detection that has driven extensive recent work. While SBM studies clustering from observed edges or signals, this paper shifts focus to *query complexity*—how many similarity queries are needed to recover clusters—and demonstrates how side information fundamentally alters the threshold. Specifically, it quantifies how the *quality* of the side information (captured by $H^2(f_+ \| f_-)$) dictates the reduction in query cost. This is a meaningful refinement beyond the well-known observation that side information can sometimes enable exact recovery; the paper provides a precise, information-theoretic measure for its utility.  

Second, the work offers a clean, generalizable framework for analyzing side information in clustering. The Hellinger distance serves as a natural metric for distinguishing the two distributions $f_+$ and $f_-$, reflecting how "noisy" or "informative" the similarity matrix is. The authors' analysis avoids restrictive assumptions (e.g., specific $f_+$/$f_-$ families) and yields a bound that scales gracefully with cluster size ($k$) and problem dimension ($n$). The tightness result—showing the logarithmic factor cannot be removed—adds robustness to the claim.  

The paper’s technical contributions are well-motivated and clearly presented. The proof strategy leverages standard tools from information theory and combinatorial search, but adapts them to the side-information setting with elegant precision. The main theorem (Theorem 1) is the centerpiece, and the supporting lemmas (e.g., Lemma 2 on Hellinger bounds) are appropriately detailed. The discussion of related work situates the contribution effectively against prior results on query-based clustering (e.g., [1,2]) and SBM (e.g., [3,4]), though it could briefly clarify how this work *differs* from recent SBM studies that incorporate side information (e.g., via labeled nodes).  

A minor point for improvement: the paper states the lower bound as "up to a $\log n$ factor," but it would be helpful to explicitly note that the tightness is *with respect to the dependence on $n$*—i.e., the $O(k \log n / H^2)$ upper bound matches the lower bound up to $\log n$, not that the bound itself contains an extra $\log n$. This nuance is standard in complexity analysis but worth emphasizing for clarity.  

The broader impact lies in providing a framework to evaluate *how much* side information is needed for efficient clustering. This has practical relevance for applications where obtaining similarity queries is costly (e.g., human-in-the-loop systems, biological assays), as it allows practitioners to estimate the trade-off between query cost and side-information quality. The result also suggests a design principle: when constructing side information mechanisms (e.g., for crowdsourcing), maximizing $H^2(f_+ \| f_-)$ should be a priority to minimize query demands.  

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of clustering with side information. The problem is well-motivated, the results are novel and rigorous, and the analysis is clear. The connection to SBM literature is particularly valuable, as it bridges a major active area with the emerging query-complexity perspective. The paper avoids overclaiming: it does not assert that side information *always* reduces complexity (which would be false without the Hellinger condition), but precisely characterizes *when* and *by how much* it does. This precision is a hallmark of good theoretical work.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a clear, non-trivial advance in a relevant problem space with sound technical execution. It will be of interest to the NIPS community working on theoretical machine learning, community detection, and active learning. The minor suggestions above are easily addressed in revision but do not detract from the core contribution.
