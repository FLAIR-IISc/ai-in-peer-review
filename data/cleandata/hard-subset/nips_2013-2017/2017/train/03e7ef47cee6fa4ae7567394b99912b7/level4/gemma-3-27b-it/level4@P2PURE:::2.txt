NOTE: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors and on the same general topic. The other one is on noisy queries without any side information.
This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering based on noiseless pairwise comparisons, along with random side-information associated with every pair. While I am familiar with some of the related literature, my knowledge is not exhaustive, making a complete evaluation of the contributions challenging. The paper’s density also limited my ability to thoroughly verify the correctness of the arguments, particularly within the extensive upper bound proof.

My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions, if correct, are valuable. However, the writing requires substantial improvement. While I acknowledge the primacy of contributions, the numerous writing issues lead to a borderline recommendation. An additional round of review to check suggested revisions would have been beneficial, but I understand this is not possible within the NIPS review process.

Regarding this paper specifically, several relevant works were not cited:
- The generalization of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) to a general distribution within the stochastic block model is not novel. A search on Google (or Google Scholar) for “labeled [or labelled] stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals prior work exploring similar generalizations.
- Furthermore, at least one work studies the SBM with an active learning component: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al, ISIT 2016). While employing a different querying model – querying a single node and obtaining its label – for fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-size communities, this is functionally equivalent to the pairwise querying model presented here; one can quickly identify a node from each community via random sampling and then determine the label of any new node using pairwise queries. The lower bound presented in this paper appears to contradict the sublinear-sample upper bound in Gadde et al., but this may be due to the lower bound's reliance on k tending to infinity (see below).

In light of these works, I request the authors to revise or remove statements such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study”, “significantly generalizes them”, “This is the first work that…”, “generalize them in a significant way”, and “There is no systematic theoretical study”, etc.

Here are some further comments:
- The model and problem statement should be clarified. The paper appears to focus on minimax bounds, but this is not explicitly stated. Additionally, are there any restrictions on cluster sizes within the minimax model? For example, is a configuration with k-1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size n-k+1 permitted?
- Theorem 2, as stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with k=2 (or generally any k=O(1)), no queries are required in certain scenarios where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The proof seems to assume k → ∞, a condition not stated in the theorem itself (and one that is necessary). I am uncertain if this is the sole issue, but the authors should carefully verify whether Theorem 2 generally contradicts cases where clustering is possible with Q=0.
- The numerical section lacks significant value, as it does not compare against existing methods but only against trivial baselines. Are there no other viable methods that can be considered or implemented?
- The paper contains several confusing or unclear sentences, such as “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is text missing after "assume"?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn't that lower bound only apply in the absence of side information?).
- The paper exhibits numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Issues with capitalization, plurals, spacing, and article usage (a/an/the) are frequent. Commas are consistently misused (they generally should not follow phrases like "let", "we have", "this means", "assume that", "since", "while", "note that", and rarely precede equations). Sentences should not begin with "And", "Or", or "Plus". The word "else" is often inappropriate, with "otherwise" being more suitable. Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a full stop. This list is incomplete, but regardless of the decision, I strongly recommend a thorough proofread.

Some less significant comments:
- The abstract is lengthy, and generally, it is not ideal to dedicate three pages before presenting the core contributions.
- Is the terminology “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” used conventionally? Additionally, the first mention of “Las Vegas” on p4 should be accompanied by the term “average” query complexity (the word is missing).
- Footnote 1: The “1-” after p= seems extraneous.
- The formal problem statement, “find Q subset of V x V,” implies a non-adaptive algorithm.
- I disagree with the assertion “clearly exhibiting” in the final sentence of the Simulations section.
- Brackets throughout the appendix are frequently too small.
