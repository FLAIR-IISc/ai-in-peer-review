This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, modeled as a similarity matrix *W*. The entries of *W* are distributed according to *f⁺* or *f⁻*, depending on whether the corresponding elements belong to the same or different clusters. The central contribution is demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available. Without side information, the query complexity is Θ(*nk*), while with side information, it is reduced to O(*k² log n* / *H²(f⁺ || f⁻)*), where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors also establish the near-optimality of this bound, up to a logarithmic factor.

This work presents a compelling theoretical result with connections to several important areas. It builds upon the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM), posing a novel question: how does the ability to actively query influence the recovery threshold?  The paper effectively highlights the substantial impact of side information on query complexity, and importantly, provides a metric—the Hellinger distance—to quantify the quality of the similarity matrix and its effect on the number of queries required.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees on query complexity, addressing a fundamental question in interactive clustering. The bounds are tight (up to a log factor) and demonstrate a significant improvement with the availability of side information.
*   **Connection to SBM:** Linking the problem to the Stochastic Block Model places the work within a well-established research area and allows for comparisons and potential extensions. The investigation of how query access modifies recovery thresholds in SBM is a valuable contribution.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is clearly defined, with a well-motivated model for side information and a focus on the information-theoretic limits of query complexity.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms do not require prior knowledge of the number of clusters (*k*) or the distributions *f⁺* and *f⁻*, making them practically appealing.
*   **Lower Bound Technique:** The development of a general framework for proving lower bounds in the interactive setting is noteworthy and could be useful in other contexts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper does not deeply explore the practical implications of the Hellinger distance as a measure of similarity matrix quality.  How easily can this distance be estimated in real-world scenarios?
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are somewhat limited. While they demonstrate the algorithm's performance on a single dataset, a more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with different similarity matrix qualities would strengthen the claims. Comparing against existing heuristics more thoroughly would also be valuable.
*   **Clarity of Proof Sketches:** Some of the proof sketches are high-level, and following the technical details requires careful reading of the appendix. While detailed proofs are appropriate for a theoretical paper, more intuition could be provided within the main body of the text.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** While the paper cites relevant work, the discussion could be more nuanced. For instance, further elaborating on how this work differs from recent results on clustering with noisy queries [34] (mentioned briefly) could be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental question in interactive clustering with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   The connection to the SBM and the development of a novel lower bound technique are significant contributions.
*   The parameter-free algorithms are appealing from a practical perspective.
*   The problem is well-defined and the results are clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited and could be strengthened.
*   Some aspects of the proofs are dense and could benefit from more intuition.
*   The practical implications of the Hellinger distance as a quality metric need further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of query complexity in interactive clustering. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical results are significant and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance**.
