This paper studies the query complexity of clustering when “side information” is available in the form of a noisy similarity matrix W. Each entry Wij is drawn from distribution f+ if i and j are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise. The authors show a sharp contrast between the no-side-information and side-information regimes: without W, Θ(nk) queries are necessary; with W, O(k log n / H^2(f+ || f−)) queries suffice, where H denotes the Hellinger distance between f+ and f−. They also prove a near-matching lower bound (tight up to a log n factor), establishing the essential optimality of their result.

Overall assessment:
- The problem is timely and well-motivated, sitting at the intersection of active clustering, statistical query complexity, and stochastic block model (SBM)-style inference.
- The main results are clean and interpretable. The dependence on the Hellinger distance provides a natural measure of the quality of the side information, and the near-tight bounds are compelling.
- The paper clarifies how even modestly informative side information can reduce query complexity dramatically, quantifying precisely how information in W trades off against the number of queries.

Strengths:
1. Conceptual clarity: The model connecting W to latent clusters via f+ and f− is a natural abstraction that generalizes several familiar noise models (e.g., Gaussian or sub-Gaussian similarities, or bounded distributions), while remaining analytically tractable.
2. Sharp results: The contrast between Θ(nk) without side information and O(k log n / H^2) with side information is striking and aligns with intuition that good side information can replace many queries. The log n factor in the upper bound is standard, and the nearly matching lower bound is a strong point.
3. Metric choice: Using Hellinger distance is a principled choice; it is symmetric, well-behaved, and often yields tight information-theoretic bounds. The result neatly quantifies how stronger separation between f+ and f− in Hellinger distance reduces the number of queries.
4. Relation to SBM: The setup is closely related to the SBM and recent work on detection and exact recovery thresholds. The paper contributes by focusing on query complexity with side information rather than purely passive observation, which is both novel and practically relevant.

Questions and points for clarification:
- Assumptions on W: Are the entries of W conditionally independent given the clustering? Are f+ and f− known to the algorithm or only assumed for analysis? If unknown, can the algorithm estimate them adaptively without changing the asymptotics?
- Oracle model: What exact queries are allowed (e.g., same-cluster queries) and are responses noiseless? If queries can be noisy, does the dependence on H(f+, f−) change, or do we get an additional dependence on the query noise?
- Cluster size conditions: Do the results require balanced clusters or a lower bound on minimum cluster size? If clusters are highly imbalanced, does the Θ(nk) lower bound (without side information) or the O(k log n / H^2) upper bound change?
- Adaptivity: Are the O(k log n / H^2) queries adaptive or non-adaptive? If adaptive, is there a non-adaptive scheme with similar complexity (perhaps incurring an extra log factor)?
- Computational complexity: Are the proposed algorithms polynomial-time with reasonable constants? If some procedures are information-theoretic, it would help to separate the algorithmic from purely existential guarantees.
- Tightness details: The lower bound is tight up to a log n factor. Is this due to a union bound in controlling failure probability, or is there a fundamental barrier to removing it? Clarifying the source of the log n would be helpful.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide concrete examples of f+ and f− (e.g., Gaussians with different means, Beta distributions on [0,1]) and compute H^2 explicitly to illustrate the scaling. A small table or figure could make the dependence more tangible.
- Discuss robustness to model misspecification (e.g., mild dependencies in W or slight deviations from f+ and f−). Are the results stable under contamination models?
- Briefly compare Hellinger-based results to what one would obtain with other f-divergences (e.g., KL or total variation). Are the bounds similar up to constants, or is Hellinger essential?
- If space permits, include a short simulation study demonstrating how query complexity decreases as H^2 increases, and how performance scales with k and n.
- Clarify whether the results extend to settings where only a subset of W is observed (i.e., missing side information), and whether the same query savings can be realized.
- Relate more explicitly to active learning and semi-supervised clustering literature. Positioning the contribution among works on pairwise queries with and without side information would help non-specialist readers.

Clarity and presentation:
The paper is generally clear, but a more explicit statement of the model assumptions and algorithmic procedures up front would aid readability. In particular:
- State the exact oracle, failure probability, and success criterion (exact recovery vs. small error).
- Precisely list any distributional assumptions (support, boundedness, moment conditions, parametric vs. nonparametric).
- Highlight which bounds are information-theoretic and which are achieved by implementable algorithms.

Significance:
The work makes a meaningful contribution by quantifying the savings in query complexity afforded by side information in a natural probabilistic model. The near-tight results and the crisp dependence on Hellinger distance provide a solid theoretical foundation that should inform algorithm design in practical semi-supervised clustering tasks.

Overall, I find the paper interesting, technically solid, and relevant to the NIPS audience. Addressing the above clarifications and adding a few illustrative examples would further strengthen the presentation.
