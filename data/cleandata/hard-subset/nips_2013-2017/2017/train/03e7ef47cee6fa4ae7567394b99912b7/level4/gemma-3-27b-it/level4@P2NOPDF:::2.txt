This paper presents upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons, supplemented with random side-information for each pair. While I have some familiarity with the related literature, my knowledge isn't comprehensive, making a full evaluation of the novelty challenging. The density of the paper also limited my ability to thoroughly verify the correctness of all claims, particularly the lengthy upper bound proof.

My overall impression is that the contributions, if correct, are valuable. However, the writing requires substantial improvement. While technical contribution is paramount, the numerous writing issues lead to a borderline recommendation. An additional review cycle to address these points would have been beneficial, though I recognize this isn’t possible within the NIPS review process.

Several relevant works were not cited. The generalization of Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) distributions in the stochastic block model (SBM) has been explored previously; a search for “labeled stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals related publications. Furthermore, Gadde et al. (ISIT 2016) study the SBM with an active learning component, albeit with a different querying model. Their approach of querying single nodes and leveraging pairwise queries for label propagation, for fixed *k* and linear-size communities, is functionally similar to the presented pairwise querying model. While a direct contradiction isn't apparent (as the authors clarified), the authors should acknowledge this related work. Consequently, statements such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “This is the first work that…”, “generalize them in a significant way,” and “There is no systematic theoretical study” should be removed or rephrased.

Here are further observations:

*   The model and problem statement could be clarified. It appears the authors are aiming for minimax bounds, but this isn't explicitly stated. Furthermore, are there restrictions on cluster sizes in the minimax model? Is a configuration with *k*-1 clusters of size 1 and one of size *n-k+1* permitted?
*   Theorem 2, as currently stated, appears incorrect. For the SBM with small, fixed *k* (e.g., *k*=2), clustering can often be achieved without any queries, even when the Hellinger distance scales as log(*n*)/ *n*. The proof seems to implicitly assume *k* tends to infinity, a condition that should be explicitly stated in the theorem itself. The validity of the theorem should be carefully checked, particularly concerning cases where clustering is possible with *Q*=0.
*   The numerical section lacks impact, as it doesn't compare against existing methods, only trivial baselines. Are there no other applicable algorithms that could be implemented for comparison?
*   Several sentences are confusing or unclear, for example, “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound.”
*   The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasings. Issues include incorrect capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and article usage. Commas are frequently misused, particularly after phrases like "let," "we have," "this means," "assume that," "since," "while," and "note that," and rarely before equations. Sentences should not begin with “And,” “Or,” or “Plus.” The word “else” should generally be replaced with “otherwise.” Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a period. Addressing these issues is crucial, regardless of the final decision.

Some less critical points:

*   The abstract is lengthy. It might be more effective to delay detailed explanations until after presenting the core contributions.
*   The terminology “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” should be used consistently and standardly. The term “average” is missing when “Las Vegas” is first introduced on page 4, where “average query complexity” is intended.
*   Footnote 1: The “1-” after *p*= should be clarified.
*   The phrase “find Q subset of V x V” in the formal problem statement suggests a non-adaptive algorithm.
*   The claim of “clearly exhibiting” in the Simulations section’s last sentence is questionable.
*   Brackets in the appendix are often too small.
