Note: I am reviewing two papers that appear to be by the same authors on closely related topics. The other paper concerns noisy queries without side information.

This paper studies the query complexity of clustering using noiseless pairwise comparisons with random side information for each pair, providing upper and lower bounds. While I’m familiar with some related work, my knowledge is not exhaustive, and the paper is dense; I could not verify all details, especially in the long upper-bound proof. My overall impression of both papers is that the contributions seem valuable (if correct), but the writing needs substantial improvement. I recognize that content matters more than presentation, but the number of issues makes my recommendation borderline. A second review round to check revisions would have been very helpful, though I understand this is not permitted at NIPS.

Related work not cited:
- Replacing Bernoulli(p)/Bernoulli(q) with a general distribution in the stochastic block model (SBM) is not new. Searching for “labeled (or labelled) stochastic block model” or “weighted stochastic block model” reveals multiple papers exploring similar generalizations.
- There is work on SBM with active querying: “Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models” (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Their model queries a single node to obtain its label. For fixed k (e.g., k=2 or k=100) and linear-size communities, this is effectively equivalent to pairwise querying: sample to find one representative per community, then label any node by pairwise querying against these representatives. At first glance, your lower bound appears to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound, but this may be because your lower bound only holds as k → ∞ (see below).

In light of this literature, please remove or soften claims such as “we initiate a rigorous theoretical study,” “significantly generalizes them,” “this is the first work that…,” “generalize them in a significant way,” and “there is no systematic theoretical study.”

Further comments:
- The model should be presented more clearly. It appears you study minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. Also, is the minimax formulation unconstrained with respect to cluster sizes? For example, do you allow k−1 clusters of size 1 and one cluster of size n−k+1?
- Theorem 2 appears incorrect as stated. For the SBM with k=2 (and likely any k=O(1)), no queries are needed in regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as log(n)/n. The proof seems to assume k → ∞, but this is not stated in the theorem and must be. Please also check carefully that Theorem 2 does not more generally contradict known regimes where clustering is possible with Q=0.
- The numerical section is not very informative, as it does not compare against existing methods and only against trivial baselines. Are your methods really the only ones applicable?
- Several sentences are confusing or incomplete, e.g., “assume, otherwise the clustering is done” (is text missing after “assume”?) and “the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound” (doesn’t that lower bound apply only without side information?).
- The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. Common issues include capitalization, plurals, spacing, and articles (a/an/the). Commas are consistently misused (e.g., they generally should not follow “let,” “we have,” “this means,” “assume that,” “since,” “while,” “note that,” and rarely precede an equation). Avoid starting sentences with And/Or/Plus. “Else” is often misused; “otherwise” would be better. Footnotes should start with a capital letter and end with a period. This list is not exhaustive; regardless of the decision, I strongly urge a careful proofread.

Less significant comments:
- The abstract is too long; more generally, it takes about three pages to reach the main contributions.
- Is your use of “Monte Carlo” and “Las Vegas” standard? When first mentioning Las Vegas on p.4, pair it with “average query complexity” (the word “average” is missing).
- Footnote 1: Should the “1−” really appear after p=?
- In the formal problem statement, “find Q ⊆ V × V” makes the algorithm sound non-adaptive.
- I disagree with “clearly exhibiting” in the last sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets in the appendix are too small.
