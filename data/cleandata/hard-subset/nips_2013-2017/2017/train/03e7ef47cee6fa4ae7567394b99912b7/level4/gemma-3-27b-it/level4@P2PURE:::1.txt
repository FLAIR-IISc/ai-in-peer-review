This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering problems when leveraging “side information” in the form of a similarity matrix *W*. Specifically, *W<sub>ij</sub>* is distributed according to *f<sub>+</sub>* if *i* and *j* belong to the same cluster, and *f<sub>-</sub>* otherwise. The central contribution is demonstrating that while Θ(*nk*) queries are necessary for clustering without side information, only O(*k* log *n* / *H<sup>2</sup>*( *f<sub>+</sub>* || *f<sub>-</sub>* )) queries suffice when *W* is available, where *H* denotes the Hellinger distance. The authors also establish that this bound is tight up to a log(*n*) factor. 

I consider this to be a compelling theoretical result for several reasons. It is closely connected to the Stochastic Block Model (SBM), a topic of considerable recent interest, yet introduces a novel perspective by examining how the ability to make queries impacts the clustering threshold. The paper clearly demonstrates that incorporating side information can dramatically reduce query complexity, a result that, while intuitively plausible given that *W* could potentially encode complete cluster membership, is rigorously quantified by identifying a metric – the Hellinger distance – to assess the quality of the similarity matrix.
