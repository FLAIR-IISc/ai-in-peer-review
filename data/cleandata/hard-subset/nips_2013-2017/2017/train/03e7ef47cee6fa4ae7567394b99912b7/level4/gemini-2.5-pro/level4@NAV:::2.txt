NOTE: This review covers two related papers, presumably by the same authors. The companion paper deals with noisy queries in the absence of side information.

This paper establishes upper and lower bounds on the query complexity of clustering, utilizing a model of noiseless pairwise comparisons augmented by random side information. While I possess some familiarity with the related literature, my knowledge is not exhaustive, making it difficult to fully appraise the novelty of the contributions. The paper is also exceptionally dense, which prevented me from dedicating the ideal amount of time to verifying correctness, especially concerning the lengthy upper-bound proof.

My overall assessment for both papers is that the technical contributions appear significant, assuming their correctness, but the quality of the writing requires substantial improvement. I recognize that contributions outweigh presentation; however, the sheer volume of issues makes my recommendation borderline. A second review cycle to check revisions would have been highly beneficial, though I understand this is not part of the conference's process.

Regarding this specific paper, several highly relevant works have been omitted:
- The concept of generalizing the stochastic block model (SBM) from Bernoulli(p) and Bernoulli(q) edges to a more general distribution is not new. A literature search for "labeled stochastic block model" or the "weighted stochastic block model" reveals numerous papers that have explored similar generalizations.
- At least one prior work has studied the SBM with an active learning component: "Active Learning for Community Detection in Stochastic Block Models" (Gadde et al., ISIT 2016). Although its query model differs (querying a single node for its label), for a fixed number of communities `k` and linear-sized communities, the models are functionally comparable. One could simply identify a representative node from each community via random sampling and then determine any new node's label through pairwise queries. At first glance, this paper's lower bound seems to contradict their sublinear-sample upper bound, though this discrepancy might be explained if the presented lower bound only holds as `k` approaches infinity.

In light of the existing literature, I urge the authors to remove or rephrase all claims of originality, such as "we initiate a rigorous theoretical study," "significantly generalizes them," "This is the first work that...," and "There is no systematic theoretical study."

Further comments are as follows:
- The problem formulation needs to be clarified. The paper appears to be deriving minimax bounds, but this is not stated explicitly. Furthermore, it is unclear whether the model imposes restrictions on cluster sizes. For instance, is the case of `k-1` singleton clusters and one cluster of size `n-k+1` permitted?
- Theorem 2, as stated, appears to be incorrect. For the SBM with a constant `k` (e.g., `k=2`), it is known that clustering is achievable with zero queries (`Q=0`) even in certain regimes where the Hellinger distance scales as `log(n)/n`. The theorem seems to contradict this fact. A potential source of the error is the proof's assumption that `k` approaches infinity, a critical condition that must be stated within the theorem itself, not just in the proof. The authors should meticulously check whether Theorem 2 more generally conflicts with known cases where clustering is possible with `Q=0`.
- The numerical experiments section is of limited value. It lacks comparison to any existing works and only benchmarks against trivial baselines. Are the proposed methods truly the only ones applicable or implementable in this setting?
- The text contains many confusing or unclear sentences. For example, "assume, otherwise the clustering is done" seems incomplete, and "the theorem is already proved from the nk lower bound" is ambiguous, as that lower bound seems to apply only to the setting without side information.
- The manuscript is replete with grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. There are pervasive issues with capitalization, pluralization, spacing, and the use of articles (a/an/the). Comma usage is frequently incorrect (e.g., they should generally not follow phrases like "let," "we have," or "since"). Sentences should not begin with "And," "Or," or "Plus." The word "else" is often used where "otherwise" would be more appropriate. Footnotes should begin with a capital letter and end with a period. This list is far from exhaustive. Regardless of the decision, the authors must perform a meticulous proofread of the paper.

Some less significant comments:
- The abstract is too long. In general, dedicating nearly three pages to introductory material before presenting the main contributions seems suboptimal.
- Is the use of the terms "Monte Carlo" and "Las Vegas" standard in this context? When "Las Vegas" is first introduced on page 4, it should be associated with the *average* query complexity.
- In Footnote 1, is the "1-" after `p=` intentional?
- In the formal problem statement, the phrase "find Q subset of V x V" suggests a non-adaptive algorithm, which may be misleading.
- I disagree with the assertion "clearly exhibiting" in the final sentence of the Simulations section.
- Many brackets in the appendix are incorrectly sized for the enclosed expressions.
