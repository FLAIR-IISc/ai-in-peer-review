This paper investigates the query complexity for clustering problems when 'side information' is available. The side information is provided in the form of a similarity matrix W, where the entry Wij is distributed according to either f+ or f-, depending on whether data points i and j belong to the same cluster or different clusters. The main result of the paper is that, in the absence of side information, one requires Θ(nk) queries to achieve clustering, whereas with side information, only O(k log n/H^2(f+||f-)) queries are sufficient, where H denotes the Hellinger distance. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that, up to a logarithmic factor, this bound is tight.

I find this to be an interesting theoretical result for several reasons. The study is closely related to the stochastic block model (SBM), which has garnered significant attention in recent years. However, this work poses intriguing questions regarding how the incorporation of queries affects the threshold for successful clustering. The paper highlights that allowing side information can drastically alter query complexity. Although it is not entirely surprising that side information can significantly impact query complexity, as it can potentially contain complete cluster information, the paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a framework for assessing the quality of the similarity matrix.
