- Paper studies query complexity of clustering with a similarity matrix and an oracle for pairwise relationships.
- Provides information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds on query complexity.
- Shows similarity matrix substantially reduces query complexity compared to no side information.
- Characterizes the reduction in query complexity via Hellinger divergence between within- and between-cluster similarity distributions.
- Well-motivated by applications such as entity resolution and builds on interactive clustering and SBM literature.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous lower and upper bounds.
- Novel explicit characterization of the impact of side information on query complexity.
- Parameter-free algorithms that require no prior distributional knowledge.
- Good connection to existing areas (SBM, active learning, crowdsourcing).
- Experimental results support theoretical claims.

Weaknesses:
- Proofs are dense and may be hard to follow for non-experts; more intuition or intermediate steps needed.
- Limited exploration of practical implications of different similarity distribution characteristics beyond Hellinger divergence.
- Experimental evaluation is restricted to a single dataset and lacks diversity in cluster/noise settings.
- Little treatment of noisy oracle responses and mitigation strategies in crowdsourced settings.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Significant theoretical advance in understanding interactive clustering with side information.
- Novel, potentially impactful results that can guide algorithm design.
- Practical advantage from parameter-free methods and relevance to real-world applications.
- Empirical evidence, albeit limited, aligns with theory.

Arguments for rejection:
- Technical density may hinder accessibility to a broader audience.
- Insufficient experimental validation across varied datasets.
- Failure to address practical oracle noise issues.

Overall assessment:
- Strong paper with significant theoretical contributions; strengths outweigh weaknesses.

Recommendations:
- Essential: Add more intuitive explanations or intermediate proof steps for accessibility.
- Important: Broaden experimental evaluation across diverse datasets and noise regimes.
- Optional: Discuss noisy oracle models and mitigation strategies (e.g., majority voting).
- Optional: Analyze sensitivity to different characteristics of the similarity distributions.
