

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of interactive clustering with side information. The authors rigorously analyze the query complexity of clustering when an oracle can answer pair-wise queries ("do two elements belong to the same cluster?"), and a similarity matrix is available as side information. They establish that with side information (where pairwise similarities follow distributions f+ for same-cluster pairs and f- for different-cluster pairs), the query complexity reduces from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H is the Hellinger divergence. The authors provide matching information-theoretic lower bounds and efficient parameter-free algorithms that work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-.

The paper's main strengths lie in its strong theoretical results and clean connection to the stochastic block model (SBM). The lower bound technique is novel and could be of independent interest for proving lower bounds in interactive learning settings. The algorithms are computationally efficient and parameter-free, which is particularly valuable for practical applications where parameters are often unknown. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings, showing that the greedy strategy closely matches the optimal performance with significantly fewer queries than naive approaches.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough introduction that contextualizes the problem within existing literature on entity resolution and interactive clustering. The authors effectively address the gap between theoretical lower bounds (Ω(nk)) and practical heuristics that use far fewer queries by showing that side information is the key factor enabling this reduction.

However, there are some areas for improvement. The connection to existing heuristic algorithms in the literature could be more explicit. While the paper cites works like [40, 43] that use similar approaches, it would strengthen the contribution to more clearly explain how the theoretical results explain the empirical success of these heuristics. For instance, it would be helpful to quantify how much the theoretical results predict the performance gains observed in practice.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the results. While the Cora dataset experiment is promising, it would be valuable to discuss how sensitive the algorithm is to the quality of the side information, how it would scale to larger datasets, and what the practical limitations might be in real-world applications. The paper mentions that the distributions f+ and f- are unknown, but it would be helpful to discuss how the algorithm behaves when the side information is imperfect or noisy.

The technical depth of the lower bound proof is substantial, and while the paper provides a clear summary of the proof technique, a more intuitive explanation of why Hellinger divergence is the right measure for this problem would make the results more accessible. The paper could also benefit from a brief discussion of how the results might extend to cases where the oracle answers are noisy, as this is an important practical consideration mentioned in the paper but not directly addressed.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their model (e.g., assuming perfect oracle answers) and discuss how their results relate to the SBM literature.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The results are significant for both theoretical and practical reasons, providing a clear theoretical explanation for the success of existing heuristics and offering new algorithms that are parameter-free and efficient. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and opens up many interesting directions for future research.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS 2017. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, the algorithms are practical and efficient, and the connection to the SBM literature is insightful. The minor weaknesses identified above could be addressed in the camera-ready version, but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.
