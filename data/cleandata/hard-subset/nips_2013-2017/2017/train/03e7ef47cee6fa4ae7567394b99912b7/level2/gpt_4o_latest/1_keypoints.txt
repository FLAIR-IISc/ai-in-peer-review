- Rigorous theoretical study of query complexity in interactive clustering using a noisy similarity matrix to reduce pairwise queries.  
- Main contributions: information-theoretic lower bounds, nearly matching upper bounds, and efficient parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas).  
- Uses Hellinger divergence as the principal metric and derives strong bounds nearly tight up to a logarithmic factor.  
- Algorithms are computationally efficient and do not require knowing number of clusters or similarity distributions.  
- Establishes connections to the stochastic block model (SBM) and studies how query complexity relates to SBM recovery thresholds.  
- Experimental validation on Cora shows the greedy strategy attains high precision and recall with few queries, even using approximate distributions.  
- Strength — Novelty/significance: tackles an important, underexplored problem with practical implications for active learning and crowdsourcing.  
- Strength — Theoretical rigor: strong information-theoretic results and well-justified use of Hellinger divergence.  
- Strength — Parameter-free methods: practical algorithms that work without prior parameter knowledge.  
- Strength — Opens future work: SBM connection offers promising research directions.  
- Weakness — Clarity/accessibility: dense presentation and lengthy proofs; needs higher-level exposition and visual aids.  
- Weakness — Experimental scope: limited to a single dataset (Cora) and focused on precision/recall; lacks runtime and large-scale scalability analysis.  
- Weakness — Practical assumptions: assumes a perfect oracle; noisy oracle responses are only briefly mentioned, not deeply explored.  
- Weakness — Theoretical gap: a logarithmic gap remains between lower and upper bounds, left for future work.  
- Recommendation: accept; strengths outweigh weaknesses, with suggested improvements to clarity and expanded experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and impactful contributions, strong theory, practical parameter-free algorithms, and real-data validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense presentation, limited empirical evaluation, and potentially unrealistic oracle assumption.
