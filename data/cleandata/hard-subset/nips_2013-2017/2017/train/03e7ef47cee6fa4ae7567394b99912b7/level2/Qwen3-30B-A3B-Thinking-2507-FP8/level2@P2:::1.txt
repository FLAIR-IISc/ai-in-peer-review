

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the query complexity of clustering with side information, a problem motivated by practical applications in entity resolution and crowdsourcing. The authors study a model where an oracle answers pairwise "same cluster" queries, and the algorithm has access to a similarity matrix generated from side information (where similar elements have distributions f+ and dissimilar elements have distributions f-). The main contribution is showing that side information reduces the query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+‖f-)), with matching lower bounds.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical contribution is novel and well-justified, providing the first rigorous study of query complexity in interactive clustering with side information. The lower bound technique is particularly impressive, as it addresses the challenge of proving information-theoretic bounds in the interactive setting where standard techniques like Fano's inequality fail. The authors develop a general framework for proving such lower bounds that could be applicable to other interactive learning problems. The algorithms are computationally efficient, parameter-free (work without knowledge of k, f+, or f-), and nearly match the lower bounds within a logarithmic factor. The connection to the stochastic block model (SBM) is insightful, showing how their model generalizes SBM and opens up new research directions about how queries affect recovery thresholds in SBM.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the gap between theoretical Ω(nk) query complexity and practical heuristics that use far fewer queries, attributing this to the use of side information. The technical contributions are presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental results on the Cora dataset provide practical validation of the theoretical findings. The authors demonstrate that their greedy strategy achieves near-optimal performance with only slightly more queries than the best strategy.

However, some weaknesses are worth noting. The log n gap between the lower and upper bounds is a bit disappointing, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. While the paper assumes a perfect oracle (no errors), this is a reasonable simplification for the theoretical study, and the authors mention that they address noisy oracles in a companion paper. The paper could have provided more discussion about the practical implications of the theoretical results beyond the Cora dataset. For instance, how would the algorithms perform on datasets with different characteristics (e.g., more clusters, more noise in the similarity matrix)? The experimental section is somewhat limited in scope.

The paper's originality is strong, as it addresses a gap in the literature where heuristic methods have been used without theoretical guarantees. The connection to SBM is particularly valuable, as it shows how the problem generalizes an extremely well-studied model while providing new insights into how queries affect recovery thresholds. The significance of the results is clear: the dramatic reduction in query complexity has direct practical implications for reducing human effort in entity resolution and other clustering applications where labeled data is expensive.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, with rigorous proofs for both upper and lower bounds. The algorithms are efficient (O(n²) time complexity), and the parameter-free nature of the algorithms makes them highly practical. The clarity of the presentation is good, though some technical details in the lower bound proof might be challenging for readers without a strong background in information theory.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of interactive clustering with side information. The results are significant for both theory and practice, and the paper is well-motivated and well-executed. The main limitation is the log n gap between the bounds, but this is acknowledged by the authors and could be addressed in future work. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field and deserves acceptance at NIPS.
