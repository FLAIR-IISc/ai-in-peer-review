## Review of "Query Complexity of Clustering with Side Information"

This paper investigates the query complexity of interactive clustering with access to a similarity matrix (side information). The authors establish strong information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available compared to the case without it. The work is motivated by practical applications like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data is expensive, and heuristics often outperform theoretical limits.

The core contribution lies in showing that the query complexity reduces from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)) with the availability of a similarity matrix, where f+ and f− represent the distributions of similarity values for elements within the same and different clusters, respectively. This result is accompanied by a matching lower bound (within a log n factor), establishing the optimality of their approach. The algorithms proposed are parameter-free, a significant advantage for practical deployment. The connection drawn to the stochastic block model (SBM) is insightful, framing the problem within a well-studied context.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and a clear presentation of the theoretical results. The authors acknowledge the limitations, such as not addressing erroneous oracle answers, and suggest future work in that direction. The experimental validation on the Cora dataset provides empirical support for the theoretical findings, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the Hellinger divergence term. While the theoretical results are elegant, understanding how this term behaves in real-world datasets and how it impacts the query complexity in practice would be valuable.  Additionally, the experimental section is somewhat limited; exploring performance on a wider range of datasets with varying characteristics would strengthen the conclusions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results with tight bounds.
*   Practical relevance to interactive clustering and entity resolution.
*   Parameter-free algorithms.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Connection to established models like SBM.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed discussion on the practical implications of the Hellinger divergence.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering. Despite the minor shortcomings in experimental validation and discussion, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
