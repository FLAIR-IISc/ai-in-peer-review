

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the query complexity of interactive clustering with side information, establishing tight bounds on the number of queries needed to recover clusters when given a similarity matrix. The work addresses a fundamental problem in interactive learning with practical applications in entity resolution and crowdsourcing, where the goal is to minimize human effort (queries) while maximizing clustering accuracy.

The main contribution is establishing that side information (a similarity matrix with noisy pairwise relationships) dramatically reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H² is the squared Hellinger divergence between distributions of similarity values for same-cluster and different-cluster pairs. The paper provides both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, with algorithms that are computationally efficient and parameter-free (working without knowledge of k, f+, or f-). The theoretical results generalize the stochastic block model by allowing arbitrary distributions for similarity values and making no assumptions about cluster sizes or the number of clusters.

The quality of the paper is high. The lower bound proof is technically sophisticated, introducing a novel framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds in interactive settings. The upper bounds are supported by a parameter-free Monte Carlo algorithm that meets the lower bound within an O(log n) factor. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by experiments on the Cora dataset, which demonstrate that the algorithm performs nearly as well as the optimal strategy, even when using approximate similarity distributions. The paper also establishes interesting connections to the stochastic block model and community detection literature.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by contrasting the theoretical Ω(nk) lower bound with practical heuristics that use far fewer queries. The technical sections build logically on this foundation, with sufficient detail for experts to follow the proofs. The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The experimental results are presented clearly and support the theoretical claims.

The originality of the work is significant. To the best of the reviewer's knowledge, this is the first rigorous theoretical study of interactive clustering with side information. The paper generalizes previous work on the stochastic block model by allowing arbitrary distributions for similarity values and making no assumptions about cluster sizes. The connection between side information and query complexity reduction is novel, and the parameter-free nature of the algorithms addresses an important practical limitation of prior work.

The significance of the results is substantial. The query complexity reduction from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)) represents a dramatic improvement in practical terms, with the potential to significantly reduce the human effort required for interactive clustering applications. The parameter-free algorithms are particularly valuable in practice, as they require no prior knowledge of the underlying data distributions. The results provide a theoretical foundation for many heuristic approaches currently used in entity resolution and crowdsourcing applications.

A few minor suggestions for improvement: The experimental section could be strengthened by including results on additional datasets to better demonstrate the generality of the approach. The connection to the companion work on noisy queries [34] could be clarified to explain how it relates to the current paper's results. The paper could provide more intuition about why Hellinger divergence is the appropriate measure for this problem, rather than KL divergence or other metrics. While the lower bound proof is technically sound, a more intuitive explanation of the key ideas would make the results more accessible to a broader audience.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive learning with side information. It provides tight bounds on query complexity, develops efficient parameter-free algorithms, and establishes connections to the stochastic block model. The results are both theoretically elegant and practically relevant, making this an important contribution to the field. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem in interactive learning.
