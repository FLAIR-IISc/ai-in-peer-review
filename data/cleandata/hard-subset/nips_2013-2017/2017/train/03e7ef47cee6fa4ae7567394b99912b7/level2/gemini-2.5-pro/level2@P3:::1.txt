This paper addresses the query complexity of interactive clustering when side information, in the form of a pairwise similarity matrix, is available. The central problem is to recover a latent clustering of `n` items into `k` clusters by making a minimum number of pairwise queries to an oracle that reveals whether two items belong to the same cluster. The work aims to bridge the gap between the known `Θ(nk)` query complexity in the absence of side information and the much better performance of practical heuristics that leverage such information.

The paper models the side information as a similarity matrix where the value for a pair of items is drawn from a distribution `f+` if they are in the same cluster, and from `f-` if they are in different clusters. The key contributions are twofold. First, it establishes a strong information-theoretic lower bound on the query complexity, showing that `Ω(min{nk, k^2 / H^2(f+ || f-)})` queries are necessary in expectation, where `H^2` is the squared Hellinger divergence. This result formalizes the intuition that the number of queries needed depends on the distinguishability of the two distributions. Second, the paper presents efficient Monte Carlo and Las Vegas algorithms that are parameter-free (i.e., they do not require knowledge of `k`, `f+`, or `f-`) and achieve query complexities that nearly match the lower bound, up to a logarithmic factor in `n`. The proposed model is shown to be a generalization of the well-studied Stochastic Block Model (SBM), and the work provides new insights into the interplay between queries and side information in community detection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and well-motivated problem. The formal analysis of how side information reduces clustering query complexity is a novel and important contribution. The work provides a compelling theoretical explanation for an observed phenomenon in practical systems (e.g., entity resolution) and establishes a clear connection between the information content of the side information (quantified by Hellinger divergence) and the query cost.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and appear to be sound.
    *   The information-theoretic lower bound for an interactive, adaptive querying setting is technically challenging and represents a significant achievement. The proof technique, which identifies elements that are infrequently queried and analyzes the multi-hypothesis testing problem for those elements, is elegant and could be of independent interest for proving lower bounds in other interactive learning settings.
    *   The proposed algorithms are a major strength. The fact that they are parameter-free makes them highly relevant for practical applications where model parameters are rarely known a priori. The iterative-update scheme, which dynamically estimates the distributions and adapts its strategy, is a clever approach to this challenge.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by connecting it to real-world applications and prior theoretical work. The relationship to the Stochastic Block Model is insightful and helps position the work within a broader context.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Arguments:** While generally clear, some of the technical sections are quite dense, which is understandable given the space constraints.
    *   The lower bound proof in Section 2 could benefit from additional intuition. For example, a brief explanation of the roles of the sets `J'` and `J''` (identifying clusters that are unlikely to be queried and unlikely to be chosen via side-information, respectively) before their formal definition might improve readability.
    *   The description of the algorithm's analysis in Section 3 relies heavily on the appendix. Including a more detailed intuitive summary of why the key lemmas (e.g., Lemma 3 on the convergence of the distribution estimates and Lemma 6 on the bounded size of the `Waiting(C)` set) hold would strengthen the main body of the paper.

2.  **Assumptions:** The algorithm is presented for discrete distributions `f+` and `f-` over a fixed set of points. While the lower bound is more general, this assumption for the upper bound is a potential limitation. It would be valuable to include a discussion on the challenges and potential approaches for extending the algorithm to continuous distributions or distributions with non-constant support size.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results on the Cora dataset serve as a useful proof of concept. However, the evaluation could be made more compelling. A comparison against some of the practical heuristics for entity resolution or interactive clustering cited in the introduction (e.g., [40, 42, 43]) would provide a much stronger validation of the proposed algorithm's practical utility and its performance relative to the existing state of the art. While this may be a significant undertaking, even a limited comparison would substantially strengthen the paper's impact.

4.  **Gap Between Bounds:** The paper establishes upper and lower bounds that match up to a `log n` factor. It would be helpful to briefly comment on whether this gap is thought to be an artifact of the proof techniques (e.g., from the use of concentration inequalities) or if it might be fundamental to the problem.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper provides a rigorous and nearly complete theoretical treatment of a fundamental problem in interactive machine learning. The results are novel, significant, and technically strong. The development of parameter-free algorithms with near-optimal guarantees is a standout contribution with high potential impact for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The main weaknesses are the limited experimental comparison to prior art and the simplifying assumption of discrete distributions for the algorithm. However, these do not detract from the core theoretical contributions of the work.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of interactive clustering. The strengths, particularly the novel theoretical framework and the design of a parameter-free, near-optimal algorithm, far outweigh the weaknesses. The suggested improvements are primarily aimed at increasing the paper's clarity and practical validation, and could be addressed in a future version.
