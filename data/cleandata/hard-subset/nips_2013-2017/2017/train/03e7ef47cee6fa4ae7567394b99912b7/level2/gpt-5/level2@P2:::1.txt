Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering with an oracle that answers pairwise same-cluster queries, in the presence of “side information” given as a noisy similarity matrix. Side information is modeled by pairwise similarities drawn i.i.d. from f+ if the pair is in the same cluster and from f− otherwise (with f+, f− unknown to the algorithm). The main results are (i) information-theoretic lower bounds showing that, when H2(f+‖f−) is small, any (possibly adaptive, randomized) algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2}) queries in expectation to achieve low error (and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H2}) for success with probability 1), and (ii) efficient, parameter-free algorithms achieving query complexities within polylog(n) of these lower bounds. The algorithms iteratively estimate intra/inter distributions from a small number of queries, use Hellinger divergence to decide when side information is reliable for assignment, and achieve exact recovery w.h.p. The work connects to and generalizes SBM recovery by allowing arbitrary, unknown f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes. A small empirical study on CORA illustrates substantial query savings.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within clustering with queries, crowdsourcing/ER, and SBM recovery. It provides an explicit information-theoretic framework for interactive clustering that complements heuristic ER methods [Wang et al., Vesdapunt et al., Firmani et al.] and extends recent theoretical work on SBM thresholds (e.g., Abbe et al., Mossel et al.) to an interactive setting. Compared to prior theory on clustering with queries (e.g., Ashtiani et al. for metric k-means; Mazumdar & Saha for noisy queries), this work targets a different oracle model and emphasizes side information. The lower bound approach (multi-hypothesis testing with adaptive queries) appears novel and of independent interest.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Sharp, intuitive characterization of how side information reduces query complexity, with dependence on H2(f+‖f−).
- Theory: General, adaptive lower bounds that hold even when k, f+, f− are known to the algorithm; upper bounds are parameter-free and nearly match the lower bounds up to log factors.
- Generality: Allows arbitrary (discrete) f+, f− and adversarial cluster sizes; does not require knowing k or model parameters.
- Algorithmic novelty: Iterative-update approach that estimates Hellinger gaps on the fly and uses them to decide when side information suffices.
- Connections: Clear link to SBM and a plausible roadmap for analyzing “active SBM.”
- Practicality: Empirical results suggest the greedy strategy is close to best and robust to approximate f+, f−.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Notational and statement inconsistencies: There are conflicting expressions for the upper bounds (O(k log n/H2) vs O(k^2 log n/H2)). The abstract and Theorem 2 suggest the latter is the correct scaling to match the lower bound; Theorem 1 (Monte Carlo) and various places use the former. This must be reconciled unambiguously.
- Assumptions on side information: Independence and fixed finite discrete support are strong; the upper bounds currently require discrete support. The authors claim extensions to any finite support (with linear scaling) but not to continuous f+, f−. Practical guidance for discretization and its impact on guarantees would strengthen the paper.
- Experimental evaluation: Limited in scope; lacks comparisons to established heuristics (e.g., from [40, 43, 18]) under the same query budget. No synthetic tests to stress dependence on k, cluster size imbalance, or H2(f+‖f−).
- Proof accessibility: Many key details (e.g., adaptation of Sanov’s theorem to Hellinger) are deferred. Given the centrality of these technical steps, a more self-contained sketch in the main text would help.
- Robustness: The model assumes a perfect oracle. While a companion work addresses noisy queries, some discussion of how the algorithm or bounds adapt (e.g., via repeated queries/majority vote) would be beneficial.
- Constant and polylog factors: The bounds contain nontrivial constants and a logarithmic gap; discussion of whether these can be tightened under common parametric families (Bernoulli/Gaussian) would add value.

Quality
The results are technically solid and carefully motivated; the lower bound technique is a highlight. Some derivations are intricate but plausible. The main quality concerns are the bound inconsistencies and reliance on appendices for key arguments.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but there are several typos, formatting glitches (sub/superscripts, missing constants), and the aforementioned inconsistencies in query complexity expressions. The algorithm could benefit from compact pseudocode and a clearer explanation of Phase 2 thresholds and stopping criteria. Unify notation for H2(·‖·) and be explicit about what is estimated and when.

Originality
High. The interactive lower bound framework and parameter-free algorithms using Hellinger-based iterative updates for arbitrary unknown f+, f− seem novel.

Significance
High. Establishes a principled understanding of when and how side information can dramatically reduce query complexity in interactive clustering, with implications for ER and active community detection. The techniques should be broadly useful.

Questions for authors
1) Please clarify the correct upper bound scalings: is the Monte Carlo algorithm O(k log n/H2) or O(k^2 log n/H2)? Which result matches the lower bound within log factors?
2) How sensitive is the algorithm to deviations from i.i.d. side information (e.g., degree corrections, correlations)? Any partial robustness guarantees?
3) Can your approach handle continuous f+, f− via adaptive discretization without degrading the rates beyond polylog factors?
4) In practice, how is memory/time handled for the O(n^2) side-information matrix?

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all inconsistencies in bounds and notation; provide a consolidated theorem with precise conditions/constants.
- Add a brief proof sketch in the main text of the key concentration step that uses Hellinger instead of KL.
- Expand experiments: compare against strong baselines under identical query budgets; include synthetic tests varying k, imbalance, and H2.
- Discuss discretization for continuous similarities and provide a guideline for binning.
- Include pseudocode and complexity summaries for the algorithms.
- Add a short discussion on handling noisy oracles (pointer to companion paper is fine, but outline the changes needed).

Pros
- Strong, general lower bounds for adaptive interactive clustering with side information.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with near-optimal query complexity.
- Clean dependence on Hellinger divergence; insightful link to SBM and active community detection.
- Practical iterative-update design that learns reliability of side information.

Cons
- Inconsistencies in key query complexity statements.
- Upper bounds assume discrete finite support and i.i.d. similarities; robustness not fully addressed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks baseline comparisons.
- Many technical details deferred; clarity can be improved.
