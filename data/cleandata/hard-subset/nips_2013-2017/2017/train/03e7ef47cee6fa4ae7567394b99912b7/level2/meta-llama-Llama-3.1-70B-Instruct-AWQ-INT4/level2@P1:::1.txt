This paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity of clustering with side information. The authors consider a model where an oracle can answer pair-wise queries about whether two elements belong to the same cluster, and the goal is to recover the optimal clustering by asking the minimum number of queries. The authors show that with side information, the query complexity reduces drastically from Θ(nk) to O(k^2 log n H^2(f+‖f−)), where H^2 denotes the squared Hellinger divergence.

The authors also provide a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering algorithms, which holds even for randomized algorithms, and even with the full knowledge of f+, f−, and k. The lower bound matches the query complexity upper bound within a logarithmic factor.

The paper also proposes two algorithms, a Monte Carlo algorithm and a Las Vegas algorithm, both of which are completely parameter-free and meet the respective lower bounds within an O(log n) factor. The algorithms are computationally efficient and have a query complexity of O(min(nk, k^2 log n H^2(f+‖f−))).

The experimental results on a popular bibliographic dataset show that the algorithm performs well and is robust to perturbations in the distributions.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity of clustering with side information.
* The authors provide a general framework for proving information-theoretic lower bounds for interactive clustering algorithms.
* The paper proposes two algorithms that are completely parameter-free and meet the respective lower bounds within an O(log n) factor.
* The algorithms are computationally efficient and have a query complexity of O(min(nk, k^2 log n H^2(f+‖f−))).

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the oracle always returns the correct answer, which may not be the case in practice.
* The paper does not consider the more complex scenarios of handling erroneous oracle answers.
* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the algorithms work and how they achieve the claimed query complexity.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of clustering with side information and provides a rigorous theoretical study of the query complexity. However, the paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the algorithms and their workings.
