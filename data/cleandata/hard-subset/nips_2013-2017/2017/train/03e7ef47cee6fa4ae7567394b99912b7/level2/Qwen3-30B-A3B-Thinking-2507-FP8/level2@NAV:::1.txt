

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the problem of interactive clustering with side information, addressing a fundamental gap between the theoretical query complexity bounds and practical heuristics. The main claims are: (1) side information (a similarity matrix) reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) to O(k² log n / H²(f+||f-)), where H is the Hellinger divergence; (2) this bound is information-theoretically optimal within an O(log n) factor; and (3) the proposed algorithms are efficient and parameter-free (work without knowing k, f+, or f-).

The paper excels in theoretical rigor, providing strong information-theoretic lower bounds (Theorem 2) that match the upper bounds (Theorem 1) within logarithmic factors. The lower bound framework is particularly novel, as it handles the interactive nature of the problem where standard techniques fail. The connection to stochastic block models is well-established, and the paper correctly positions its work within this rich literature. The experimental results on the Cora dataset demonstrate practical viability, showing that the greedy strategy performs nearly as well as the optimal strategy with minimal additional queries.

The paper demonstrates exceptional quality through its clear theoretical development, with well-structured proofs and careful attention to the challenges of parameter-free algorithms. The algorithms are efficiently implementable (O(n²) time), and the analysis of the iterative-update method provides a valuable recipe for designing parameter-free algorithms. The paper's significance is clear: it explains why heuristic approaches in entity resolution work well in practice, offering a theoretical foundation for their efficiency.

A minor limitation is that the paper doesn't address erroneous oracle responses (noting this is covered in companion work), though this is reasonable given the focus on side information. The logarithmic gap between lower and upper bounds (O(log n) factor) is acknowledged as an open question, which is appropriate for a theoretical contribution.

The paper is exceptionally clear in its presentation, with well-motivated problem formulation and effective use of the Hellinger divergence as the right measure for the analysis. The experimental section provides strong validation without overclaiming.

This work makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of interactive clustering and establishes a new framework for analyzing query complexity with side information. It successfully bridges theory and practice, explaining why heuristic approaches outperform theoretical bounds in real-world applications. The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at NIPS as a high-quality theoretical contribution with clear practical relevance.
