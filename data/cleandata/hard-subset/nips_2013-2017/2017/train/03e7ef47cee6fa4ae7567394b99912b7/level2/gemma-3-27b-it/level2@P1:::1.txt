This paper studies the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix. The problem is framed as an interactive process where an oracle provides pairwise cluster membership information upon query. The core contribution lies in showing that the availability of a similarity matrix drastically reduces the query complexity from Θ(nk) (without side information) to O(k² log n H(f+||f−)), where H is the Hellinger divergence between the distributions governing similarity values for elements within and between the same cluster. The authors also provide matching lower bounds (within a log n factor) and present algorithms that achieve these bounds, crucially without requiring knowledge of the number of clusters or the underlying distributions. The work connects to the stochastic block model and opens avenues for further research in community detection.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in clustering where obtaining accurate similarity functions is difficult. The introduction clearly explains the problem and its relevance to applications like entity resolution. The theoretical contribution is significant, providing a rigorous analysis of the impact of side information on query complexity. The matching upper and lower bounds are a strong result. The connection to the stochastic block model is insightful and provides a broader context for the work. The proposed algorithms are parameter-free, a desirable property for practical applicability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper delivers tight bounds on the query complexity, offering a fundamental understanding of the problem.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem is well-motivated by real-world applications and the parameter-free algorithms are promising for practical use.
*   **Novelty:** The rigorous theoretical analysis of clustering with side information appears novel, particularly the combination of upper and lower bounds and the characterization of the impact of the similarity matrix.
*   **Clear Connection to SBM:**  The linkage to the well-studied Stochastic Block Model provides a valuable perspective and context.
*   **Well-Written:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized.  The motivation and contributions are clearly stated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Evaluation Limited:** While the experiments on the Cora dataset are helpful, they are relatively limited. Exploring performance on a wider range of datasets and comparing against existing heuristic algorithms more thoroughly would strengthen the paper. Specifically, comparing against the heuristics mentioned in the introduction (e.g., [22, 40, 13, 42, 43, 18, 39, 12, 21, 29]) would be crucial. The experiments could be extended to demonstrate the impact of different similarity matrix qualities (varying Hellinger divergence).
*   **Technical Detail in Appendices:** While understandable given conference length constraints, the core algorithmic details are relegated to the appendices. This makes it more difficult for readers to fully grasp the intricacies of the proposed algorithms without significant effort. Providing a higher-level overview within the main body might improve accessibility.
*   **Assumptions on Distributions:** While the algorithms are designed to work without *knowing* f+ and f-, the analysis relies on them being *discrete* with fixed cardinality of support. The implications of relaxing this assumption (e.g., continuous distributions) aren't fully discussed.
*   **Hellinger Divergence Complexity:** The computation of Hellinger divergence itself can be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional distributions. This aspect is not discussed in the context of algorithmic efficiency.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information.
*   The matching upper and lower bounds are a strong indicator of the quality of the results.
*   The parameter-free algorithms have the potential for practical impact.
*   The connection to the SBM enriches the theoretical foundation of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of the algorithms compared to existing methods.
*   The technical details in the appendices may hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   The assumption of discrete distributions with fixed cardinality may limit the applicability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, and the problem is well-motivated. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical novelty and potential impact outweigh this weakness.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with relevant heuristic algorithms on diverse datasets, demonstrating the benefits of the proposed approach.
2.  Provide a higher-level description of the algorithms in the main body of the paper, even if the detailed proofs remain in the appendices.
3.  Discuss the limitations of the assumption of discrete distributions and potential avenues for extending the results to continuous distributions.
