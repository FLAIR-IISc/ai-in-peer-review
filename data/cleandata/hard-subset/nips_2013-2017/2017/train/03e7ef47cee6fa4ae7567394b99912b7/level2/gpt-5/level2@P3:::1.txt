Summary and contributions
The paper studies the query complexity of interactive clustering with pairwise same-cluster queries in the presence of side information (a similarity matrix). The side information is modeled as a noisy matrix W where entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ if two items are in the same cluster and from f− otherwise; both f+ and f−, as well as k, are unknown. The main results are:
- Information-theoretic lower bounds showing that, for small Hellinger separation H2(f+‖f−), any (possibly randomized) algorithm needs Ω(min{nk, k^2/H2(f+‖f−)}) queries in expectation to recover with constant probability, and Ω(n + min{nk, k^2/H2(f+‖f−)}) for success probability 1.
- Parameter-free, polynomial-time algorithms with query complexities (as stated) of O(min{nk, k log n/H2(f+‖f−)}) for a Monte Carlo algorithm and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H2(f+‖f−)}) for a Las Vegas algorithm, achieving exact recovery with high probability or probability 1 respectively.
- A general lower-bound technique for adaptive querying based on Hellinger divergence and a careful reduction to multi-hypothesis testing that accounts for adaptivity.
- An iterative-update, parameter-free design that estimates p+ and p− from side information to calibrate when side information suffices vs. when to query, analyzed via a Sanov-type large deviations argument using Hellinger distance.
- A connection to stochastic block models (SBM) as a special case (Bernoulli f+, f−), and a discussion of how queries might shift recovery thresholds.

Relation to prior work
Without side information, the Θ(nk) bound is consistent with prior work on clustering with same-cluster queries (e.g., [13], [40], [5]). Heuristic query strategies that leverage similarity matrices are common in entity resolution and crowdsourcing [42, 43, 40], but lacked query-complexity guarantees and often assume known parameters; recent analyses suggest suboptimality for simple distributions [18, 33]. The setting generalizes SBM recovery [1, 2, 24, 36] to arbitrary unknown f+, f− and unknown k, and relates to active querying in SBM [19], but focuses on pairwise same-cluster queries rather than label queries. The lower-bound framework for adaptive querying appears novel and of potential interest beyond this problem.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically motivated and timely question: how side information can dramatically reduce query complexity for exact clustering.
- Clear separation of regimes with and without side information, quantified via Hellinger divergence; the dependence on H2(f+‖f−) is intuitive and information-theoretically grounded.
- Parameter-free algorithms that do not require k, f+, or f−, supported by a nontrivial analysis (iterative estimation + Sanov/Hellinger).
- Lower bounds that handle adaptive querying and match upper bounds up to logarithmic factors (subject to the k vs. k^2 clarification below).
- Connection to SBM and discussion of how queries may shift thresholds.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Apparent mismatch on the k vs. k^2 factor: the abstract, lower bound (Theorem 2), and Las Vegas upper bound suggest k^2/H2 scaling, while the Monte Carlo upper bound is stated as k log n/H2. If the correct scaling for the Monte Carlo bound is k^2 log n/H2 (as suggested elsewhere), this should be stated consistently; if k log n/H2 is indeed achievable, the gap to the Ω(k^2/H2) lower bound must be explained (different success criterion, model differences, or a typo).
- The upper-bound analysis assumes discrete similarity values with fixed support size q. The lower bound allows continuous f+, f−. Practical similarity matrices are often continuous or high-cardinality. A clear statement on how to extend the algorithm (e.g., via binning) and how query complexity degrades with q or discretization would improve applicability.
- Independence of similarity entries given the partition is a strong assumption; real side information often exhibits correlations (e.g., shared features). Some robustness discussion (e.g., mixing conditions or mild dependencies) would be valuable.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to one dataset, with no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., [40, 43], or the analyzed heuristics in [18]). The reported strategies (“best,” “worst,” “greedy”) are ablations, not baselines. The experiments also rely on known/approximated f+, f− rather than fully parameter-free estimation as in the theory.
- A few presentation issues hinder readability: inconsistent notation and minor typos; several algorithmic steps could benefit from pseudocode; the role of constants C, thresholds τx/MEx, and the “grown/Waiting” logic deserve a more concise, centralized description.

Quality
The technical core is compelling. The lower bound carefully handles adaptivity and uses Hellinger divergence to relate to total variation, with a clean per-item hypothesis testing view and a “lightly queried” element argument. The upper bound’s iterative estimation and Sanov-based control of H(p+‖p−) deviations are reasonable for discrete alphabets, and the membership rule is well-motivated. The main technical concern is the k vs. k^2 discrepancy; resolving it is essential to assess tightness.

Clarity
Overall well written, but long and dense. Important definitions (membership, “grown” clusters) are somewhat scattered. Consistent notation and a dedicated algorithm block would improve clarity. Fixing the factor discrepancy is critical.

Originality
High. Both the adaptive lower-bound technique and the parameter-free algorithmic framework appear novel in this setting. The use of Hellinger throughout (rather than KL) is a thoughtful and technically useful choice.

Significance
High. The work formalizes and quantifies a widely observed empirical phenomenon (query savings from side information), provides broadly applicable lower-bound ideas for interactive settings, and connects to SBM recovery thresholds. If the upper/lower bounds indeed match up to log factors, the result is likely to influence future theoretical and practical work on interactive clustering and entity resolution.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and well-motivated theoretical contributions; near-tight bounds tied to a meaningful statistical separation measure.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms with clear practical appeal.
- Novel lower-bound framework for adaptive querying; potential impact beyond clustering.
- Meaningful connection to SBM and active community detection.

Cons for acceptance
- Unresolved discrepancy on the k vs. k^2 dependency in query complexity.
- Upper-bound assumptions (discrete support, independence) may limit applicability without further discussion.
- Experimental section is limited and lacks comparisons to established baselines; reliance on approximate true distributions blurs the “parameter-free” message.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Resolve and clearly state the correct dependence on k in Theorem 1 and throughout (abstract, main text, appendices); reconcile with Theorem 2.
- Add a brief section on handling continuous/high-cardinality similarities (e.g., discretization schemes) and how this affects guarantees.
- Provide pseudocode summarizing the Monte Carlo algorithm, including how MEx, thresholds, and the “Waiting” mechanism interact.

Recommended (optional but valuable):
- Add experimental baselines (e.g., [40, 43], and/or variants analyzed in [18]) and report query vs. accuracy and runtime.
- Investigate sensitivity to class imbalance and to small H2(f+‖f−).
- Discuss robustness to mild dependence in W (e.g., via a coupling or concentration argument).
- Clean up typos/notation and centralize key definitions.

Overall, the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical relevance. Addressing the noted discrepancies and expanding the empirical validation would significantly strengthen the submission.
