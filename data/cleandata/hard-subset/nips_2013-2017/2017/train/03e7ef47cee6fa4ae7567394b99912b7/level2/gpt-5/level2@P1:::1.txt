Summary
The paper studies the query complexity of clustering when an algorithm can ask pairwise same-cluster queries to an oracle and also has access to side information in the form of a similarity matrix W. The side information is generated from an unknown pair of distributions: for a pair within a cluster, the observed similarity is drawn i.i.d. from f+, and otherwise from f−. The main results are (i) information-theoretic lower bounds that account for adaptive querying, and (ii) efficient, parameter-free algorithms whose query complexities nearly match the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors. Without side information, Θ(nk) queries are necessary and sufficient; with side information, the authors show an expected lower bound of Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2}) and provide algorithms achieving O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) (Monte Carlo) and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}) (Las Vegas), where H^2 = H^2(f+||f−) is the squared Hellinger divergence. The work generalizes the SBM (f+, f− Bernoulli) and contributes a lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive settings. A parameter-free “iterative-update” algorithm learns empirical intra/inter distributions and uses Hellinger tests to decide when to trust W vs. when to query.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The lower-bound technique carefully handles adaptivity by identifying under-queried elements and converting assignment into a k-hypothesis test; the argument leverages Hellinger distance and product distributions. The algorithmic side provides a principled, parameter-free procedure with concentration analysis via Sanov-type arguments (adapted to Hellinger). The bounds align up to O(log n). Assumptions are clearly stated (i.i.d. side information conditioned on cluster membership).
- Completeness: Core proof ideas for the lower bound are in the main text; full algorithmic proofs appear in the appendices. The experiments validate feasibility but are limited in scope.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with clear problem setup and a helpful discussion of connections to SBM and crowdsourced ER. However, there are several notational and formatting inconsistencies that impede readability:
  - The query complexity sometimes appears as O(k^2 log n/H^2) and elsewhere as O(k log n/H^2); please standardize and distinguish Monte Carlo vs. Las Vegas clearly in the abstract and theorems.
  - The symbol H is used for Hellinger and sometimes its square; please fix to avoid ambiguity.
  - The Membership score is defined as a negative Hellinger divergence; consider defining a positive affinity to reduce sign confusion.
  - Some constants (C, B) appear without a clean summary; collecting them in one place would help.
- Please proof-read for minor typographical issues and unify notation.

Originality
- The paper offers a novel and rigorous treatment of interactive clustering with side information, including (a) lower bounds that explicitly handle adaptive queries and (b) parameter-free near-optimal algorithms for arbitrary unknown f+, f−, and unknown k. The choice of Hellinger as the core divergence that drives both lower and upper bounds is interesting and cohesive. The iterative-update approach to learn when side information is reliable is a useful design pattern.

Significance
- The results clarify why practical systems that leverage a similarity matrix can use far fewer queries than Θ(nk), and quantify the role of distributional separation via H^2. The connection to SBM and the suggested impact of queries on SBM recovery thresholds are compelling directions. The lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive settings may have broader applicability.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation; first rigorous theory for interactive clustering with side information.
- Tight bounds up to log factors; algorithms are efficient and parameter-free.
- Lower-bound technique addresses adaptivity—a nontrivial aspect often glossed over.
- Unifies analysis using Hellinger divergence, matching lower and upper bounds.
- Practical relevance (ER), with an interpretable dependence on H^2.

Weaknesses and questions
- Model assumptions:
  - W assumes i.i.d. entries conditioned on cluster memberships. Many real similarities (e.g., entity attributes) induce dependencies; how sensitive are guarantees to mild correlations or heteroskedasticity across elements?
  - The main Monte Carlo analysis assumes discrete, fixed support. How to extend to continuous similarities (e.g., Gaussian)? Is a data-driven discretization sufficient, and how does its resolution affect query complexity?
  - Lower bound requires H^2 ≤ 1/18. While stated as the regime of interest, can constants be relaxed? Are there matching constants for larger H^2?
- Algorithmic details:
  - Complexity: Computing intra-cluster histograms pC scales with |C|^2; globally this is O(n^2), which matches reading W but could be heavy. Any incremental updates or sparsity-aware variants?
  - Cluster size imbalance: Guarantees are stated without distributional assumptions on cluster sizes, but Lemma 1 uses equal-sized clusters. Please make explicit how the general lower bound extends to arbitrary partitions (beyond the reduction sketched).
  - Robustness: How do results change if the oracle is noisy (even at a small rate)? A short discussion bridging to the companion paper would help.
- Experiments:
  - Only one dataset; no synthetic studies showing the predicted scaling in k and H^2, nor robustness to model misspecification.
  - No comparison to baselines (e.g., heuristics guided by W, active SBM methods) in terms of queries vs. accuracy.
  - The Las Vegas variant is not empirically evaluated.
- Presentation:
  - The conjecture on shifting SBM thresholds with Q queries is intriguing—consider adding a small synthetic experiment to illustrate the trend empirically.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify notation and clearly separate the Monte Carlo and Las Vegas complexities in the abstract and theorem statements.
- Add a brief section on extending to continuous-valued W (e.g., via adaptive binning) and the impact on bounds.
- Include synthetic experiments varying k, H^2, and cluster-size distributions to verify scaling; compare against practical baselines.
- Discuss robustness to mild dependence in W and to oracle noise (even informally), and clarify practical guidance for choosing histogram resolution.
- Provide a short complexity and memory analysis table for the phases, with notes on incremental updates.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, near-tight bounds with a clean dependence on H^2; addresses a timely and practically important setting.
- Novel lower-bound framework for adaptive interactive learning.
- Parameter-free, efficient algorithms likely to influence future work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is limited and does not fully substantiate the scaling claims or robustness.
- Some clarity issues and notational inconsistencies.

Overall recommendation: Accept (leaning). The theoretical contributions are substantial and timely; with improved clarity and stronger experiments, this work will be impactful.
