This paper investigates the query complexity of clustering with side information, specifically a similarity matrix, in an interactive setting where an oracle provides pairwise cluster membership labels. The authors establish both information-theoretic lower bounds and nearly matching upper bounds, demonstrating a significant reduction in query complexity when side information is available compared to the case without it. They also connect their work to the well-studied stochastic block model (SBM) and propose parameter-free algorithms.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem in areas like entity resolution where obtaining labeled data is costly. The core contribution – showing the dramatic impact of side information on query complexity – is significant. The connection to SBM is insightful and places the work within a broader theoretical context. The derivation of both lower and upper bounds is a substantial achievement. The proposed algorithms are appealing due to their parameter-free nature, making them potentially more practical.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing clear bounds on query complexity. The lower bound is particularly noteworthy as it offers a fundamental limit on performance.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications, and the proposed algorithms have the potential to be useful in those settings.
*   **Connection to SBM:**  The link to the SBM allows the authors to leverage existing knowledge and provides a valuable perspective on the problem.
*   **Parameter-Free Algorithms:** Designing algorithms that do not require knowledge of underlying parameters (k, f+, f-) is a significant advantage in practical applications.
*   **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear introduction and well-defined contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Evaluation Limited:** While the paper includes experimental results on the Cora dataset, the scope is limited. It would be beneficial to evaluate the algorithms on a wider range of datasets with varying characteristics (e.g., different cluster structures, noise levels, and similarity distributions). A comparison to existing heuristic algorithms in terms of performance (accuracy *and* number of queries) would also be valuable.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs of the main results, especially the lower bound, appear quite involved. While technically sound, they may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific technical tools used to fully grasp. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps might improve accessibility.
*   **Assumptions in Lower Bound:** The lower bound is presented with the knowledge of k, f+, and f-. While the authors acknowledge this is a stronger assumption than the upper bounds, its impact on the practical applicability of the lower bound is not fully discussed. A discussion of how the lower bound changes if k, f+, and f- are unknown would be helpful.
*   **Discussion of Practical Challenges:** The paper largely focuses on theoretical guarantees. A discussion of potential practical challenges related to estimating the similarity matrix or dealing with noisy oracle responses would strengthen the work.
*   **Minor Clarifications Needed:** In section 3, the notation seems a bit heavy. A brief summary of the key variables would be helpful, as well as more intuition behind the iterative update scheme.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the query complexity of clustering with side information.
*   The results are novel and well-supported by rigorous analysis.
*   The connection to the SBM provides valuable context and potential for future research.
*   The parameter-free algorithms are a valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited experimental evaluation raises concerns about the practical impact of the theoretical results.
*   The complexity of the proofs may hinder broader adoption of the work.
*   The assumptions made in the lower bound may limit its applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical results are compelling and the connection to the SBM is insightful. The parameter-free algorithms are a significant practical advantage. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more intuitive explanations of the key proofs. Specifically, adding a table comparing the proposed algorithms to relevant baselines on multiple datasets would dramatically improve the paper.
