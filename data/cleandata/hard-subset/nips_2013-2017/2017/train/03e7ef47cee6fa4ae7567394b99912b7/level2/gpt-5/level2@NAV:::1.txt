Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies interactive clustering with pairwise “same-cluster?” queries in the presence of side information: a noisy similarity matrix whose entries are drawn i.i.d. from f+ (same cluster) or f− (different cluster). The main claims are: (1) information-theoretic lower bounds showing that side information reduces query complexity from Θ(nk) to Ω(min{nk, k^2/H^2(f+‖f−)}), up to constants; (2) efficient, parameter-free algorithms (Monte Carlo and Las Vegas) achieving O(min{nk, k log n/H^2}) and O(n log n + min{nk, k^2 log n/H^2}), respectively, thus nearly matching the lower bounds up to logarithmic factors; and (3) conceptual links to stochastic block models (SBM), generalizing beyond Bernoulli to arbitrary (unknown) f+, f−. This connects to NIPS work on SBM thresholds and recovery [Abbe et al.; Mossel et al.], active community detection [Gadde et al.], and clustering with same-cluster queries [Ashtiani et al.], as well as crowdsourced entity resolution pipelines.

Quality: The technical development is strong. The interactive lower bound framework is nontrivial and leverages Hellinger divergence to control total variation under adaptive queries. The upper bounds are constructive and parameter-free via an iterative update that estimates p+, p− and uses a Hellinger-based membership test. Proof sketches are plausible; full details are deferred to appendices. One clarity issue: the dependence on k in the upper bounds appears inconsistent (k vs k^2 across abstract/Theorem/algorithms) and should be unified.

Clarity: Generally well written, with clear problem setup and motivation. However, notation is heavy; assumptions (discrete vs continuous f+, f−; support size) and the precise success probabilities for Monte Carlo vs. Las Vegas should be consolidated. Pseudocode would aid reproducibility.

Originality: High. The interactive, information-theoretic lower bounds for clustering with side information and parameter-free algorithms for arbitrary unknown f+, f− appear novel. The use of Hellinger (rather than KL) is insightful for both bounds and algorithms.

Significance and usefulness: Significant theoretically and practically. The results explain why side information dramatically reduces crowd queries in ER-like workflows and provide guidance via H^2(f+‖f−). The requirement of a full O(n^2) similarity matrix may limit scalability, but is standard in ER/graph settings.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Empirics are limited to one dataset and do not compare to strong baselines (e.g., CORLEONE, transitivity-based heuristics) under identical budgets; add broader comparisons, ablations, and runtime/memory reports.
- Discuss robustness to continuous f+, f− and to oracle errors; provide experiments with noisy queries and misspecified side information.
- Clarify and reconcile k vs k^2 factors, success probabilities, and constants; provide end-to-end pseudocode and release code.

Arguments for acceptance: novel theory; near-tight bounds; parameter-free, efficient algorithms; timely connection to active clustering/ER and SBM.

Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation; scalability concerns (full similarity matrix); clarity inconsistencies on k-dependence; some proofs relegated to appendix.
