- Introduces PROXASAGA: an asynchronous SAGA variant for finite-sum convex optimization with nonsmooth regularizers.  
- Leverages sparse updates and block coordinate-wise gradients to extend variance reduction to composite problems.  
- Proves linear convergence under strong convexity and Lipschitz gradient assumptions, with a slightly smaller step size than SAGA.  
- Empirical results show strong speedups (up to 12x) on large-scale, multi-core sparse datasets.  

Strengths:
- Novel extension of asynchronous optimization to nonsmooth objectives combining variance reduction, sparsity, and asynchrony.  
- Rigorous, well-organized convergence proof with a reasonable bounded-delay assumption (up to √n/10).  
- Compelling practical relevance: outperforms ASYSPCD and FISTA, especially on sparse problems.  
- Clear statement of contributions (Sparse Proximal SAGA, PROXASAGA, analysis of convergence/speedup regimes).  
- Reproducible: includes implementation details and open-source code reference.  

Weaknesses:
- Clarity issues: inconsistent notation and undefined terms (e.g., distinction between "virtual iterates" and "inconsistent reads").  
- Limited generalization: speedup guarantees rely on sparsity, reducing applicability to dense problems.  
- Experimental scope narrow: primarily tests ℓ1+ℓ2-regularized logistic regression; lacks other composite objective benchmarks.  
- Minor typographical/formatting errors and occasional undefined variables (e.g., α).  

Recommendation:
- Accept: significant theoretical and experimental contribution with strong practical implications.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Advances state of the art for asynchronous optimization on nonsmooth objectives.  
- Clean theoretical framework with strong empirical validation.  
- Practical utility for large-scale machine learning on multi-core architectures.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Dependence on sparsity limits general applicability.  
- Minor clarity and notation issues need fixing.  

Final verdict:
- Accept.
