This paper proposes an asynchronous type of SAGAs, a stochastic gradient method for finite sum convex optimization (DeFeazio et al., 2014). According to my understanding, this paper addresses two challenges: asynchronousity and proximity. The authors have made three improvements compared to the original SAGAs. Firstly, they propose a sparse update according to block coordination, using extended support for gradients while disintegrating regularization. Secondly, they design an asynchronous type of SAGAs where the amount of delay can be up to sqrt(n)/10. Finally, they can deal with non-smooth regulators through proximal operators. In terms of convergence results, they still achieve a linear convergence rate under strong convexity of the individual लिपशिट्ज gradients of the overall sum function f and fi. Although the step-size is slightly smaller compared to step-size SAGAs, the convergence factor remains comparable. This is probably due to different assumptions.

In fact, this paper combines many advanced ideas from existing works like SAGAs to improve all these techniques, such as reduction in variance, sparse updates, hogwild, asynchronousity, etc. The evidence is quite clear and well-organized.

In my opinion, such improvements are important and significant in practice for obvious reasons. The numerical results also strongly support their theoretical contributions. These experiments are conducted on three large-scale data sets, and empirically show the linear speed of the new algorithm.

Overall, this paper has a significant contribution in terms of both theory and experiment. It is worthy of acceptance by the NIPS.
