The main contribution of this paper is presenting a convergence proof for minimizing the sum phi (x) + g (x) in an asynchronous setting, where phi (x) is smooth and g is non-smooth. The problem is well-motivated; to my knowledge, there isn't actually a known proof for it.

An important aspect of this work is the block-decompositionability of the variable, which allows for some decomposition gain in the prox step (because the gradient of each phi can only affect certain indices of x). This is an important practical assumption, otherwise the prox becomes locked, which can be unavoidable. (It would also be interesting to see if element-wise prox, like compression, can also be decomposed.)

It has two main theoretical results. Theorem 1 gives a convergence rate for proxSAGA, which is progressively better than the previous result. Theorem 2 gives a rate for an asynchronous setting, which is more unprecedented.

Overall, I think this is a good paper, with a very good theoretical contribution that is also very useful in practice. I think it would be even stronger without the notion of rarity, but the authors also say that checking asynchronousity without rarity for non-smooth functions is an open problem, so it is accepted.
