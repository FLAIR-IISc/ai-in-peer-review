- Introduces PROXASAGA: an asynchronous, variance-reduced optimizer extending SAGA to nonsmooth, composite objectives with sparse proximal updates.  
- Contribution 1: a sparse proximal SAGA variant leveraging gradient sparsity and block-separable penalties to cut computation.  
- Contribution 2: an asynchronous, lock-free parallel implementation with theoretical linear speedup under certain assumptions.  
- Empirical claim: shows substantial runtime improvements and up to 12x speedup on a 20-core machine vs. state-of-the-art.  
- Strength — Novelty: fills a gap by supporting nonsmooth composite objectives in an asynchronous variance-reduced framework.  
- Strength — Theoretical rigor: provides detailed convergence guarantees and speedup analysis using the perturbed iterate framework.  
- Strength — Practical usefulness: well-suited for problems like Lasso/group Lasso on large sparse datasets.  
- Strength — Empirical validation: thorough experiments against strong baselines demonstrating faster performance and good scaling.  
- Strength — Clarity: well-organized presentation with implementation details (e.g., memory compression) aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness — Sparsity reliance: performance depends heavily on gradient/penalty sparsity, limiting applicability to dense settings.  
- Weakness — Memory access overhead: empirical speedups are sublinear due to memory bottlenecks not captured in theory.  
- Weakness — Incomplete empirical baselines: missing comparisons to additional methods (e.g., ProxSVRG) for completeness.  
- Weakness — Limited scalability testing: experiments only up to 20 cores; behavior on much larger/core-distributed systems is unclear.  
- Recommendation: accept — substantial contribution with robust theory and empirical support despite limitations.  
- For acceptance: novel asynchronous nonsmooth extension of SAGA, strong guarantees, and significant empirical gains.  
- Against acceptance: limited to sparse problems and observed sublinear speedups due to memory overhead.
