- Presents a novel "triage-then-cluster-then-pursuit" pipeline (YASS) for spike sorting on dense MEAs with NN-based detection, coreset reduction, and DP-GMM clustering.  
- Claims robustness, scalability, modularity, and state-of-the-art accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency on synthetic and real MEA data (including 512-electrode).  
- Achieves faster-than-real-time processing on a single CPU core and outperforms KiloSort, Spyking Circus, and MountainSort.  

Strengths:
- Technically rigorous with theoretical analysis and extensive validation using biophysically realistic simulations and real data.  
- Highly scalable via coreset construction and divide-and-conquer clustering strategies.  
- Novel combination of NN-based detection, coreset-based clustering, and DP-GMM with split-merge methods.  
- Significant impact potential for large-scale neural recording studies and open-source availability increases accessibility.  

Weaknesses:
- Some sections (clustering and coreset construction) are dense and need clearer exposition or examples.  
- NN-based detection relies on prior training data, which may limit applicability for labs lacking dense MEA training sets.  
- Evaluation could be broadened to datasets with template drift or nonstationarity to better assess real-world robustness.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Advances spike sorting state-of-the-art in accuracy and computational efficiency.  
- Modular, open-source design increases community impact and usability.  
- Strong technical rigor and thorough experiments support the claims.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Clarity issues could hinder comprehension by readers unfamiliar with clustering/data reduction.  
- Generalizability concerns due to reliance on training data for NN detection.  

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance, with suggested revisions to improve clarity and address generality concerns.
