- Introduces YASS: a "triage-then-cluster-then-pursuit" spike-sorting pipeline using neural-network detection, DP-GMM clustering, and matching pursuit recovery.  
- Aims for robustness, scalability, and modularity; reports state-of-the-art performance on synthetic and real MEA datasets.  
- Addresses a critical neuroscience bottleneck by enabling scalable, reliable spike sorting for dense MEAs.  
- Modular design allows iterative improvements; neural-net detection cuts false positives and improves alignment.  
- Coreset-based clustering and divide-and-conquer strategy enhance scalability for large datasets.  
- Empirical comparisons show superior accuracy, stability, and computational efficiency versus Kilosort, Spyking Circus, and MountainSort, especially at low SNR.  
- Achieves near real-time performance on a single CPU core, highlighting computational efficiency.  
- Main text lacks clarity with key methodological details relegated to supplementary material.  
- Detection requires hand-labeled data, introducing supervision that is not explicitly acknowledged in comparisons with unsupervised methods.  
- Discarding overlapping signals on multiple electrodes may bias comparisons and disadvantage YASS in some scenarios.  
- Novelty is unclear due to missing detailed performance breakdown separating new contributions from integrated existing techniques.  
- Pro: strong empirical performance, scalable modular approach, and broad comparisons.  
- Con: insufficient transparency, unacknowledged supervised components, and unclear novelty.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions to clarify methods, explicitly state supervised elements, and provide a detailed performance breakdown.
