[Original Review]

The authors develop a multi-step spike sorter, affectionately named YASS, which follows a classical pipeline of spike detection, feature extraction, and clustering. The most appealing aspect of this work is that the developed YASS system performs better in several cutting-edge ways. Despite this commendable performance, this work raises several concerns, as enumerated below.

Major Concerns:

1.  YASS's impressive performance comes at the cost of requiring hand-labeled data (for the detection step), which is not necessary for all the methods YASS is compared to. In this sense, the provided comparisons are not as accurate. Requiring the experimenter to provide such hand-labeled data is an additional burden of effort that is not required by fully untrained methods.
2.  Several components in the YASS pipeline are derivative of previous work. While there are certainly novel components (possibly sufficient to be published in NIPS), the authors do not provide performance details to demonstrate that these novel contributions are necessary to achieve the stated levels of performance. For example, it may be that the overall performance is due to the specific selection of pre-developed components, rather than the inclusion of newly developed components.

Minor Concerns

3.  If my reading was correct, the proposed method omits signals appearing on multiple electrodes, keeping only the waveform signal with the largest amplitude (lines 138-140). This is in contrast to the approach of KILOSORT, for example. This seems disadvantageous, and raises another flag that the comparison may not be as accurate as against KILOSORT. If the experimental data does not contain overlapping signals across channels, YASS's performance will not be affected by this design choice, and KILOSORT's performance will not shine in comparison because one of its key features will not be in the trend. If my understanding is not correct here, this text should be clarified. Nevertheless, the authors should clarify the exact application setting in relation to overlapping signals across channels.
4.  The writing style is somewhat unclear. I would encourage the authors to clarify their presentation by boiling down important mathematical and algorithmic details (whether words or equations) and including them in the main paper. Currently, almost none of these important details are available without diving into the supplement. This separation of details presents a significant burden on the reader and reviewer.
