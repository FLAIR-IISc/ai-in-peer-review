This article discusses the significant contemporary problem of multi-electrode array (MEA) spike detection and sorting. Efficient algorithms are crucial for this problem because the number of such tests and neurons recorded is increasing. Scaling to a range of 104 to 106 electrodes is difficult and requires sophisticated analysis methods.

The authors accurately identify the key characteristics of developing robust algorithms, such as robustness, scalability, modularity, and the use of prior knowledge. This article uses numerous methods and tools that the authors are well-acquainted with and have a good understanding of the problem.

The algorithm presented at the beginning of this article is not particularly helpful or readable and does not provide anything that is not in the text description. The overview section at the beginning is important, but the writing here is not very clear and it quickly discusses various topics.

Determining the true validity of the proposed algorithms is very difficult. In particular, the neural net training phase is interesting, but it is difficult to understand how it will work. Using different training data for each lab can be problematic, and it is difficult to predict how well it will work, although the authors have provided some good simulations.

In summary, this method may be interesting, but the complexity of the method, its apparent immediacy, and its relatively transparent presentation make it less appealing. This method is interesting, but its technical justification is absent in the main article or limited to complementary materials, which are sufficiently extensive.
