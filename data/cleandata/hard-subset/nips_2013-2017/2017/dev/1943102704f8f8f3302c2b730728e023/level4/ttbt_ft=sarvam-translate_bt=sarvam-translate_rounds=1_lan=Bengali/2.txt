[Original Review]

The authors have created a multistage spike sorter, beautifully named YASS. It follows the classical method of spike detection, feature extraction, and clustering. The most appealing aspect of this work is that the improved YASS system performs better than several advanced methods. Despite this commendable performance, this work raises several concerns, which are listed below:

Major Concerns:

1.  The impressive performance of YASS requires manual labeling of the data (for the detection step), which is not necessary for other methods compared to YASS. In this respect, the comparisons provided are not entirely accurate. If the experimenter is asked to provide manually labeled data, it creates an additional burden of effort, which is not entirely necessary for unsupervised methods.

2.  Many components of the YASS pipeline are taken from previous work. Although there are some new components (which are probably worthy of publication in NIPS), the authors did not want to prove by providing a performance analysis that these new components were necessary to achieve the mentioned level of performance. For example, it may be that the overall performance is due to the selection of previously created special components, and not due to the inclusion of new components.

Minor Concerns:

3.  If my understanding is correct, the proposed method cancels the signals displayed on multiple electrodes, keeping only the largest amplitude waveform signal (lines 138-140). For example, this is the opposite of the sort by key method. This seems inconvenient and raises another question that this comparison may not be accurate with sort by key. If the experimental data did not have overlapping signals across channels, YASS's performance would not be affected by this design, and sort by key's performance would not be as good either, because one of its main features would not work here. If my understanding is not correct here, this should be clarified. However, the authors should clarify the specific application of overlapping signals across channels.

4.  The writing style is quite unclear. I would encourage the authors to clarify their presentation, so that important mathematical and algorithmic details (whether words or equations) are summarized and included in the main paper. Currently, almost none of these important details are available without going to the supplement. This division of details is quite burdensome for the reader and reviewer.
