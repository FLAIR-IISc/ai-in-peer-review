This paper discusses the significant contemporary problem of spike detection and sorting in multi-electrode arrays (MEA). Efficient algorithms are crucial for this problem because the number of such tests and the number of neurons recorded are increasing. Scaling to a range of 10^4 to 10^6 electrodes is difficult and requires sophisticated analysis methods.

The authors accurately identify key characteristics in the development of robust algorithms, such as robustness, scalability, modularity, and the use of prior knowledge. The paper uses numerous methods and tools with which the authors are well-versed and have a good understanding of the problem.

The algorithm 1 presented at the beginning of the paper is not particularly helpful or readable and does not provide anything that is not in the text description. Initially, the overview section is important, but the writing here is not very clear and it quickly discusses various topics.

Determining the true validity of the proposed algorithms is very difficult. In particular, the neural net training phase is interesting, but it is difficult to understand how it will work. Using different training data for each lab can create problems, and it is difficult to predict how well it will work, although the authors have provided some good simulations.

In summary, this method may be interesting, but the complexity of the method, its seemingly instantaneous nature, and the relatively transparent presentation make it less appealing. This method is interesting, but its technical justification is absent in the main paper or limited to complementary materials, which are sufficiently extensive.
