[Original Review]

The paper discusses YASS: Yet Another Spike Sorter, a well-designed pipeline for selecting multi-electrode array (MEA) recordings. The method is sound, creating a complete pipeline combining many state-of-the-art approaches. The paper is well-written, follows a clear structure, and provides credible evidence that the work truly enhances state-of-the-art in retinal MEA data selection.

The paper is a valuable contribution in itself, but I believe its potential is even greater if the authors addressed some of the issues detailed below. In summary, my main concerns are:

(1) Lack of publicly available code,
(2) Weak description of the neural network detection method,
(3) Difficulty in applying the pipeline to cortical data.

Details:

(1) Code is not available (or at least no URL is given in the manuscript). Since spike selection is primarily an engineering problem at the moment (but an important one), a general description of the method is half as valuable as the actual code implementing the pipeline. Therefore, I encourage the authors to put in the extra effort to make the code available.
(2) Neural network spike detection. This seems to be the only innovative part (all other components of the pipeline have been described/used before). However, it is still unclear to me how the authors created their training data. Section C.2 describes various ways to create training data, but it is not clear which way (or combination) the authors used.
(a) Using pre-existing selection. Firstly, most labs have pre-existing selection when going to dense MEA, but the data obtained is not accurately selected, because they do not have a pipeline for selecting such array data, and - as the authors mentioned - existing methods cannot be accurately scaled.
Secondly, it is not clear to me how pre-existing selection will help train a more robust neural network for detection. Did the authors inspect each waveform snippet and identify them as clean? If not, on what algorithm did they base their decision on which waveform snippets of the training data are clean? Why do they need to train a neural network without just using this algorithm? How do they deal with incorrect alignment, which creates label noise?
If the authors used pre-existing selection, they need to provide more information about how they did it and why it works. In its current form, their work cannot be reproduced.
(b) Creating synthetic training data by imposing waveform templates on background noise. This could be a reasonable approach. Is it used for data augmentation or not at all, and only described as a possible alternative? What is the evidence that this method is effective? Synthetic data may not represent real recordings.
(c) Generalization to cortical data. I am quite confident that the pipeline works well for retinal data, but I doubt it will do so for cortical data (some arguments are given below). I think this limitation needs to be discussed and acknowledged more clearly (summary, introduction, conclusion).
(a) In cortical recordings, waveform drift is a serious problem that is seen in almost all non-chronic recordings (and high-density MEA that works chronically has not yet been demonstrated). Therefore, it is absolutely essential to model drift for recordings that last more than a few minutes.
(b) It is more difficult to find good training data for NN detection. Good ground truth (or well-verified data as described in Appendix I) is not available, and creating synthetic data as described in C.2 may not be realistic, because spike also creates background noise and neurons often fire in a highly correlated manner (resulting in a problematic method of imposing templates on spike-free noise).

Small Comments:

- Panel below figure 3: The Y axis is strange. Does 1^-x mean 10^-x? Also, it exaggerates the small precision difference between 0.99 and 0.999, while both methods are essentially perfect.
- The authors use spatially whitened data (according to Section 2.1), but I did not find any description of spatial whitening methods in the manuscript or appendix.
