- Proposal: Deep Hyperalignment (DHA) uses deep neural networks as a flexible kernel for functional alignment, with rank-m SVD and SGD to reduce runtime and allow alignment of new subjects without training data; shows superior classification accuracy.
- Strength: Deep-net kernel provides greater flexibility than fixed kernels; rank-m SVD + SGD yields computational efficiency for large datasets.
- Strength: Extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets demonstrates superior classification accuracy, runtime, and feature representations versus CAE, SRM, and SVDHA.
- Strength: Method scales to high-dimensional data and large numbers of subjects, improving on prior HA techniques.
- Strength: Experimental setup and comparisons are clearly documented and use diverse datasets to support generalizability.
- Weakness: Novelty is unclear—DHA appears to combine existing ideas (multi-set CCA, DGCCA, deep networks) without a clearly articulated fundamental advance.
- Weakness: Missing important citations (e.g., Georg Lang and other functional alignment work) that would contextualize and contrast the contributions.
- Weakness: Evaluation via classification tasks conflates alignment quality with representational improvements, making it unclear what drives performance gains.
- Weakness: No mechanism to map aligned data back to anatomical space, limiting neuroscientific interpretability.
- Weakness: Template G is non-unique, raising questions about robustness and reproducibility that are acknowledged but not resolved.
- Weakness: Reporting best results across parameter ranges without nested cross-validation risks overfitting.
- Weakness: Unclear whether DHA's gains depend on prior anatomical spatial normalization; applicability without normalization is not assessed.
- Recommendation: Conditional acceptance suggested if authors address novelty, add missing references, and clarify interpretability and robustness issues.
