Summary:

This paper proposes a hyperalignment method based on using neural networks instead of basis functions within a framework similar to kernel hyperalignment. This method is applied to several real fMRI datasets. Increased classification accuracy with reduced computation time is shown compared to another deep learning architecture.

Comments:

1. The novelty of this paper is not clear. From the text, the proposed method appears to be a combination of previous contributions. Does using neural networks instead of basis functions achieve an objective that is too difficult to optimize? The optimization process seems "inspired" by previous work.
2. The work done by George Lang on functional alignment should be mentioned.
3. The primary purpose of alignment is to establish subject correspondence to model subject variability. The best way to demonstrate better correspondence is to show that the activation patterns of subjects are better aligned. Using a classification task for the evaluation done in this work, it is difficult to tell whether better alignment resulted in better classification or whether better performance resulted from a better functional representation. In fact, it is not clear how to map the aligned data to the original physical location for explanatory purposes.
4. Since G is not unique, will this have a significant impact on the results?
5. The results may reflect overfitting, i.e., bias, because, unlike proper nested cross validation, the best performance is reported on a series of parameter values. Models with more flexibility tend to perform better. Also, the range of parameter values will have a significant impact on the results.
6. Functional hyperalignment may not necessarily require pre-physical alignment. Does the performance benefit remain if no spatial normalization is performed?
