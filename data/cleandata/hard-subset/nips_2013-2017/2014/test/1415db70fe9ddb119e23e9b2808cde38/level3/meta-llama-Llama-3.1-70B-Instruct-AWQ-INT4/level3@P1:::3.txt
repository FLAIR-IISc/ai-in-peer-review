This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN) model, which extends traditional Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) by incorporating a feedbackward step during inference. This allows the model to capture contextual information and make more accurate predictions on tasks such as aspect-specific sentiment classification.

The GB-RNN model is well-motivated by the need to capture contextual information in natural language processing tasks. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of traditional RNNs and how the GB-RNN model addresses these limitations. The model is also compared to other models, including bidirectional RNNs and recurrent neural networks, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the differences between these models.

The paper is generally well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the GB-RNN model and its components. However, the motivation for the model could be stronger, and the authors could provide more theoretical justification for the use of a feedbackward step during inference.

The evaluation of the GB-RNN model is also a major weakness of the paper. The authors present ensemble results, which makes it difficult to compare the performance of the GB-RNN model to other models. Additionally, the composition of the compared models is unclear, and the authors do not specify how the models were tuned or how the ensemble/best models were selected. It is also unclear whether the benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned the same way or used the same augmented data as the GB-RNN.

Furthermore, the authors do not provide enough information about the experimental methodology. For example, it is unclear whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines used hybrid word vectors, and whether the benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned the same way or used the same augmented data as the GB-RNN.

Despite these weaknesses, the results presented in the paper are reasonable, and the GB-RNN model appears to outperform other models on the task of contextual sentiment analysis. However, more work is needed to fully understand the strengths and limitations of the GB-RNN model and to provide a clearer comparison to other models.
