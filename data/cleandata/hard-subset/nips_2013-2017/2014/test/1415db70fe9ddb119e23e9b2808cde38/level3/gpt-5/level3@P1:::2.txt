This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which augment standard feedforward recursive neural networks with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The upward pass produces context-independent node representations; a learned top-down decomposition then propagates global sentence information back to all internal and leaf nodes. Final predictions at each node concatenate forward and backward representations, enabling word/phrase labels to be context-sensitive (e.g., “Android” positive in “Android beats iOS”). The authors also introduce hybrid word embeddings (fixed unsupervised vectors concatenated with a small supervised, trainable segment) and apply dropout on node vectors. On SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and competition systems; an ensemble of GB-RNNs further improves results.

Relation to prior work: The model is close in spirit to bidirectional recursive networks (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), but differs in the top-down computation: the backward vectors depend on both the forward and backward parent representation via a decomposition matrix, rather than only the forward parent. This architectural choice increases parameters modestly and is argued to better “disentangle” child representations. The hybrid embedding idea resonates with prior work on unsupervised word vectors fine-tuned for supervised tasks, but here the unsupervised part is frozen and only a small supervised tail is trained. Compared to earlier tree-structured sentiment models (e.g., Socher et al. on the Sentiment Treebank), GB-RNN directly targets context-dependent, aspect-level labeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual elegance: a simple, single top-down pass turns a purely feedforward tree model into a context-aware predictor without requiring sliding windows or sequence recurrence.
- Clear modeling contribution vs. related bidirectional recursive models: explicit equations, parameterization, and a rationale for using both forward and backward parent signals.
- Strong empirical results: state-of-the-art performance on SemEval-2013 Task 2; clear gains over a standard RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline.
- Practical training insights: hybrid embeddings and dropout materially improve performance; the staged training with distant supervision is reasonable for this domain.
- Clarity: generally well-written with helpful figures; the task and motivation are compelling.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Experimental setup fairness: It is unclear whether the bidirectional recursive baseline was trained with the same enhancements (dropout, hybrid embeddings, and external/distantly supervised data). Table 2 suggests shared word vectors and dropout, but the paper should state explicitly for each baseline which resources and regularization were used.
- Ensemble reporting: Mixing single-model and ensemble results in the same comparison table is potentially misleading; ensembles should be separated or clearly marked and compared only to other ensembles.
- Ambiguity in Section 4.4: “widely used mix of both” is vague—please define precisely what resources (lexicon entries, distant tweets, movie reviews) were used in which stage for which models.
- External resources: While SemEval allowed external data, the paper should document de-duplication, time period, and overlap controls to rule out leakage and to ensure reproducibility.
- Statistical rigor: No significance testing is reported; given the relatively modest margins among strong baselines, confidence intervals or bootstrap tests would strengthen claims.
- Scope of evaluation: All results are on SemEval-2013 (tweets/SMS) with parser-dependent trees. Given known parsing errors on noisy text, an analysis of robustness to parse errors (or using alternative/noisy parsers) would be valuable.

Quality
- Soundness: The model is technically sound, with well-defined forward and backward recursions and a clear training objective (cross-entropy; backprop through structure; AdaGrad; dropout). The comparison to prior bidirectional recursive models is explicit at the equation level.
- Empirical support: Results are strong, with ablations showing the contribution of the downward pass, hybrid embeddings, and additional data. However, the exact parity of training conditions across baselines needs clarification, and significance testing is missing.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Suggested fixes: unify terminology (“feedbackward” vs. “downward”), explicitly list which baselines use dropout/hybrid vectors/external data, separate ensemble rows, and clarify Section 4.4 wording.

Originality
- The idea of top-down propagation in tree models has precedents, but the particular backward computation and the demonstration that a single feedback pass at inference materially improves token-level sentiment are novel and well-motivated. The hybrid embedding formulation is a practical, incremental contribution.

Significance
- The method advances state of the art on a widely used benchmark and addresses an important limitation of feedforward compositional models—context-sensitive labeling. The approach is likely to be adopted for other token/phrase-level tasks (aspect sentiment, slot filling, WSD).

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify training parity for all baselines (dropout rates, hybrid embeddings, distant/lexicon data, two-stage training).
- Report single-model and ensemble results separately; include variance over multiple runs.
- Add significance tests and per-domain (tweets vs. SMS) breakdowns.
- Clarify “widely used mix of both” and enumerate all external resources with counts and preprocessing.
- Provide ablations on the V transform (vs. identity), backward dimensionality nd, and number of feedback passes (one vs. iterative).
- Analyze sensitivity to parsing quality and tree structures; consider a sequence model comparison with long-context architectures.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, principled architectural change with clear, measurable gains.
- Strong empirical performance on a competitive benchmark, with state-of-the-art results.
- Clear positioning relative to closely related bidirectional recursive models.
- Practical training recipe (hybrid embeddings + dropout) that others can reuse.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguities in the experimental setup raise questions about baseline fairness.
- Mixing ensemble and single-model results in main comparisons.
- Limited analysis of robustness and lack of significance testing.
- Heavy reliance on external resources without exhaustive documentation.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The core idea is solid and useful, the results are compelling, and the paper is well written. Addressing the experimental clarity and reporting issues would make it a clear accept.
