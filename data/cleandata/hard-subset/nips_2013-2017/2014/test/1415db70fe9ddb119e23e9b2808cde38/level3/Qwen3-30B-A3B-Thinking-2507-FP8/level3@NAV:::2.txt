

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard recursive neural networks by adding a downward pass to propagate global context information to all tree nodes. The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the limitation of feedforward architectures in capturing context-dependent phrase labels, particularly for aspect-specific sentiment classification. The model is conceptually simple, using a top-down recursive computation that combines with the standard bottom-up pass to allow all nodes to update based on sentence-level context. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on SemEval 2012 Task 2, with a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs.

The paper has several strengths: the core idea is well-motivated and elegantly implemented, the writing is clear and well-organized, and the authors make valuable contributions regarding hybrid word vector representations and the effectiveness of dropout for RNN training. The extension from prior work [12] is novel in its downward computation, using both forward and backward parent nodes rather than just the forward parent.

However, the experimental evaluation raises significant concerns. The baseline comparison with [12] in Table 2 lacks crucial details about whether the baseline used dropout, additional training data, or hybrid vectors, making it impossible to fairly assess the claimed performance improvement. The ensemble result should not be presented alongside individual-model comparisons in the same table, as this creates misleading comparisons. Additionally, Section 4.4 contains ambiguous language about "widely used mix of both" without clarifying what this refers to.

In terms of evaluation criteria: Quality is compromised by the unclear experimental setup; Clarity is good overall but undermined by experimental ambiguities; Originality is moderate as the high-level idea builds on prior work; Significance is limited by the experimental issues, though the problem addressed is important for contextual sentiment analysis.

While the core idea is promising and the writing is strong, the experimental ambiguities prevent a confident assessment of the claimed state-of-the-art performance. I recommend rejection due to these methodological concerns, though the authors could address these issues in a revised submission.
