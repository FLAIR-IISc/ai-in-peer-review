This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), an extension to standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) designed to address contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea is to incorporate a feedbackward (top-down) pass during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations and labels. This is a well-written and motivated contribution that tackles a significant problem in NLP – accurately determining sentiment when it depends on context, as exemplified by aspect-specific sentiment classification. The authors also introduce a hybrid approach to word vector initialization, combining unsupervised and supervised representations, which appears to be a key component of their success.

The paper effectively relates its work to previous research in compositional semantics and sentiment analysis, citing relevant papers on distributional word vectors [27, 11, 15], recursive neural networks [26], and bidirectional recurrent/recursive models [5, 12, 14]. However, the comparison to Irsoy and Cardie’s [12] bidirectional RNN, while acknowledged, feels incomplete. The distinction between their approach and the proposed GB-RNN needs further clarification, specifically detailing how the backward pass is computed and how this difference impacts performance and interpretability. The use of the term “belief propagation” is potentially misleading; a more descriptive term like “forward-backward” might be more accurate.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical soundness, clear presentation, and focus on a challenging problem. The theoretical justification for incorporating a feedbackward pass is convincing, and the empirical results on the SemEval 2013 dataset are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The introduction of hybrid word vectors is a noteworthy contribution, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. The authors are careful to present both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging potential limitations.

However, there are several weaknesses that need to be addressed. The experimental setup raises some concerns regarding fairness and clarity. It’s unclear whether all baseline models (RNN, bidirectional RNN) utilized the same hybrid word vectors. Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding model selection and hyperparameter tuning. Was a single best model selected based on the development set, or were ensembles used? A more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter search and validation procedure is necessary.

Section 3.5, detailing the training procedure, is surprisingly brief. A more comprehensive explanation of backpropagation through structure, the network unfolding process, and how gradients propagate to update the word embeddings is crucial for reproducibility and understanding. The description of the feedbackward computation (Eq. 3) could also benefit from more intuitive explanation.

Section 3.6, comparing the GB-RNN to Irsoy and Cardie’s model, remains confusing. The mathematical differences are highlighted, but a clearer explanation of the *intuitive* difference in how information flows between the two models would be beneficial. The parameter count comparison, while present, doesn't fully explain the performance differences. 

Section 4.1 assumes a level of familiarity with the SemEval 2013 task that may not be shared by all reviewers. A brief explanation of the specific subtask addressed and the constraints imposed by the Stanford Parser would improve accessibility for a broader audience. Moreover, the parser constraints imposed might introduce bias in the evaluation and should be further discussed.

Merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 would improve the flow and readability of the experimental results. This would allow for a more integrated discussion of the impact of different components of the GB-RNN, including the hybrid word vectors and the additional training data.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to contextual sentiment analysis with a theoretically sound justification.
*   State-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 dataset.
*   Introduction of hybrid word vector representations, demonstrating improved performance.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient clarity regarding the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Lack of detail in the training procedure description.
*   Incomplete and potentially misleading comparison to Irsoy and Cardie’s bidirectional RNN.
*   Potential for bias introduced by the parser constraints.
*   The term “belief propagation” might be inaccurate.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea of incorporating a feedbackward pass into RNNs is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the clarity of the experimental setup, the training procedure, and the comparison to related work. Specifically, providing more detail on hyperparameter tuning, the training process, and a more intuitive explanation of the differences between GB-RNN and the bidirectional RNN from Irsoy and Cardie will significantly strengthen the paper.
