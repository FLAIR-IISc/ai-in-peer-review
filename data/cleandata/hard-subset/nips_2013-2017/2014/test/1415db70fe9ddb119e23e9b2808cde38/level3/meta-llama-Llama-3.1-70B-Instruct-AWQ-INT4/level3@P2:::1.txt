The paper "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks" introduces a novel approach to natural language processing by extending standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to influence word embeddings and labels, enabling the model to capture context-dependent compositional vector representations of variable-length phrases. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The paper is well-written and well-motivated, presenting an interesting variant of bidirectional recursive models. The key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors, which combines unsupervised and supervised word vector representations. This approach allows the model to capture both task-specific information and general semantic knowledge.

However, there are some issues with the paper that need to be addressed. The relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is incomplete, and the distinction from that bidirectional recursive model needs clearer clarification. The term "belief propagation" is also misleading, and "forward-backward" might be a more appropriate name. Furthermore, the experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear, as it is not specified whether the baselines (RNN, B-RNN) used hybrid vectors, and how the models were tuned/selected (single best vs ensemble).

The training section is also too short and lacks details on the training procedure, such as backpropagation-through-structure, network unfolding, and how deep updates reach word embeddings. Additionally, Section 3.6 is confusing and should explicitly clarify the differences from Irsoy & Cardie.

The paper's quality is high, with well-supported claims and a clear evaluation methodology. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on a challenging task, and the results are impressive. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the training procedure and a clearer comparison to prior work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to natural language processing, and the introduction of hybrid word vectors is a significant contribution. The paper also provides a clear explanation of how the model differs from prior work, such as Irsoy and Cardie (2008).

The significance of the paper is high, as it presents a new approach to natural language processing that achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task. The model has the potential to be applied to a wide range of NLP tasks, such as word-sense disambiguation.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to natural language processing that achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task.
* The introduction of hybrid word vectors is a significant contribution to the field.
* The paper provides a clear explanation of how the model differs from prior work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is incomplete, and the distinction from that bidirectional recursive model needs clearer clarification.
* The experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear.
* The training section is too short and lacks details on the training procedure.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Clarify the relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) and explicitly state the differences between the models.
* Provide more details on the training procedure, such as backpropagation-through-structure, network unfolding, and how deep updates reach word embeddings.
* Ensure that the experimental comparisons are fair and clear, and specify whether the baselines used hybrid vectors and how the models were tuned/selected.
* Consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 for clarity.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, and with some revisions to address the issues mentioned above, it has the potential to be a strong paper.
