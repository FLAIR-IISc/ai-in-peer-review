This paper introduces global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs), a novel variant of recursive neural networks (RNNs) that incorporates a top-down (backward) inference step to standard bottom-up recursive networks. This allows phrase-level predictions/embeddings to influence word embeddings and labels. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The paper is well-written and well-motivated, presenting an interesting variant of bidirectional recursive models. The key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors, which may be the main difference from prior work. However, the relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is incomplete, and the distinction from that bidirectional recursive model needs clearer clarification. Additionally, the term "belief propagation" is misleading, and "forward-backward" might be a more appropriate name.

The experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear, as it is not specified whether the baselines (RNN, B-RNN) used hybrid vectors, and how the models were tuned/selected (single best vs ensemble). Furthermore, Section 3.5 (Training) is too short and needs details on the training procedure, such as backpropagation-through-structure, network unfolding, and how deep updates reach word embeddings. Section 3.6 is confusing and should explicitly clarify the differences from Irsoy & Cardie.

Section 4.1 requires better explanation for non-NLP readers, clarifying which SemEval 2013 subtask is addressed and parser constraints. Additionally, consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 for clarity.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, the authors could be more careful in evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. The paper is a complete piece of work, but some sections, such as the training procedure, need more details.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. However, some sections, such as Section 3.6, need clarification. The paper provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results, but some sections could be improved for non-NLP readers.

Originality: The problems or approaches are new, and the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. However, the relation to prior work, such as Irsoy and Cardie (2008), needs clearer clarification. The paper adequately references related work, but some comparisons, such as the experimental comparisons, need more details.

Significance: The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides unique conclusions on existing data. However, the impact of the work on practitioners or researchers is not explicitly stated.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel variant of recursive neural networks that incorporates a top-down (backward) inference step.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.
* The paper is well-written and well-motivated, presenting an interesting variant of bidirectional recursive models.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The relation to prior work, such as Irsoy and Cardie (2008), needs clearer clarification.
* The experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear, and some sections, such as the training procedure, need more details.
* Some sections, such as Section 3.6, need clarification, and the paper could be improved for non-NLP readers.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to recursive neural networks, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness on a challenging task. However, some sections need clarification, and the relation to prior work needs clearer explanation.
