

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward pass during inference to incorporate global sentence context for contextual sentiment analysis. The authors argue that standard RNNs cannot resolve context-dependent labels (e.g., "Android" being positive in "Android beats iOS"), and propose GB-RNNs to allow phrase-level representations to update based on root-level context. The model combines feedforward and feedbackward node vectors, uses hybrid unsupervised/supervised word vectors, and applies dropout. It achieves a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs and state-of-the-art results on SemEval 2013.

**Strengths:**  
The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a strong connection to prior NIPS work (e.g., Socher et al. 2011, 2013 on recursive networks; Irsoy & Cardie 2013 on bidirectional RNNs). The hybrid word vector approach—combining fixed unsupervised vectors with task-specific trainable components—provides a sensible solution to the "antonym problem" in sentiment analysis. The 3% F1 gain over standard RNNs is meaningful, and the empirical results on SemEval 2013 are competitive with top competition entries. The use of dropout to improve training is well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quality & Methodology:* The core innovation (feedbackward propagation) is mislabeled as "belief propagation," which is misleading as it bears no relation to Bayesian belief propagation. More critically, the evaluation methodology is flawed:  
- The paper reports *ensemble* results (top 5 GB-RNN models) as the main result, but fails to justify why ensembling was necessary or how the models were trained (e.g., independent runs? same hyperparameters?). This obscures comparisons to non-ensemble baselines (Table 2 vs. Table 3).  
- It is unclear whether the RNN and bidirectional-RNN baselines used the same hybrid word vectors (Table 3 suggests they did, but this is not stated). If they used pure unsupervised vectors, the 3% gain is inflated.  
- Hyperparameter tuning details are insufficient (e.g., how were the "top 5" models selected? Were baselines tuned identically?). The claim that GB-RNN outperforms "all other baselines" is undermined by ambiguity in benchmarking.  
- The off-the-shelf Stanford Parser’s accuracy on tweets (a noisy domain) is unreported, raising questions about parsing quality.  

*Originality:* The model is a minor extension of Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN (NIPS 2013 Deep Learning Workshop), with a different feedbackward computation (Eq. 3 vs. Eq. 4). The distinction is technical but not conceptually novel. The paper lacks clear theoretical justification for why this specific feedback mechanism is superior to bidirectional approaches.  

*Significance:* The task (contextual sentiment analysis) is relevant, but the gains are incremental. The model’s applicability beyond sentiment (e.g., word-sense disambiguation) is claimed but not demonstrated. The SemEval 2013 results are competitive but do not clearly surpass the *best* 2013 systems (e.g., Socher et al.’s sentiment treebank work), as the paper does not compare directly to them.  

*Clarity:* Minor issues include incomplete citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie 2013 reference lacks venue) and the misleading "belief propagation" terminology. The motivation (Fig. 1) is vague—why is "The Android’s screen is better than the iPhone’s" context-dependent? The phrase "Android" is neutral in isolation, but the sentiment is tied to the *comparison*, not the phrase itself.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark, clear writing, useful hybrid vector approach.  
*Con:* Flawed evaluation (ensemble misuse, unclear baseline comparisons), misleading terminology, incremental contribution over prior bidirectional work.  

**Recommendation:**  
Reject. While the paper presents a reasonable extension to recursive networks with a modest performance gain, the experimental methodology is insufficiently rigorous. The ensemble results are unjustified, and the lack of clear baseline comparisons (e.g., did baselines use hybrid vectors?) undermines the claimed superiority. The "global belief" framing is misleading and distracts from a technically minor modification to bidirectional RNNs. For NIPS, we require stronger theoretical grounding or more significant empirical advances. The paper would benefit from a full reevaluation of experimental design, including: (1) reporting non-ensemble results; (2) clarifying baseline hyperparameters and data usage; (3) removing "belief propagation" terminology. As written, the contribution does not meet NIPS’s bar for quality or significance.
