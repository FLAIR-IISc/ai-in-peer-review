This paper proposes the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN) model, which extends traditional Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) by incorporating a feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words, enabling the model to capture contextual information and improve sentiment analysis performance. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the GB-RNN model on the SemEval 2013 challenge, achieving state-of-the-art performance and outperforming other baselines.

The paper is generally well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the GB-RNN model and its components. However, the motivation for the model is somewhat vague, and the authors could have provided more theoretical justification for their approach. Additionally, the evaluation section is a major weakness of the paper. The authors present ensemble results, which makes it difficult to compare the performance of the GB-RNN model to other baselines. Furthermore, the composition of the compared models is unclear, and the authors do not specify how the models were tuned or how the ensemble/best models were selected.

The authors also do not provide enough information about the benchmarks in Table 2, making it unclear whether they were tuned in the same way or used the same augmented data as the GB-RNN model. Additionally, it is unclear whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines used hybrid word vectors. The authors could have also provided more information about the Stanford Parser used for tweets, including its accuracy on this data.

Despite these weaknesses, the results presented in the paper are reasonable, and the GB-RNN model shows promise for improving sentiment analysis performance. The authors also provide some insights into the importance of using hybrid word vectors and the benefits of applying dropout to improve training.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1. The GB-RNN model is a novel extension of traditional RNNs, and its performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge is state-of-the-art.
2. The authors provide a clear explanation of the GB-RNN model and its components.
3. The paper demonstrates the importance of using hybrid word vectors and the benefits of applying dropout to improve training.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1. The motivation for the GB-RNN model is somewhat vague, and the authors could have provided more theoretical justification for their approach.
2. The evaluation section is a major weakness of the paper, with unclear composition of compared models and lack of information about the benchmarks.
3. The authors do not provide enough information about the Stanford Parser used for tweets, including its accuracy on this data.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

1. Provide more theoretical justification for the GB-RNN model and its components.
2. Clarify the composition of the compared models and provide more information about the benchmarks.
3. Provide more information about the Stanford Parser used for tweets, including its accuracy on this data.
4. Consider presenting non-ensemble results to facilitate comparison with other baselines.
