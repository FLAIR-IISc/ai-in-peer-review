

# Review: Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard recursive neural networks by adding a "backward pass" to propagate context information from the root of the parse tree down to the leaves. The authors argue that standard recursive neural networks fail to correctly predict phrase or word labels determined by context (e.g., "Android" being positive in "Android beats iOS"), and that their approach addresses this limitation by allowing phrase-level predictions to feedback to words.

The paper builds on prior work in recursive neural networks [25, 26, 28] and bidirectional models [12, 14], but makes a key distinction in how the backward pass is computed. While prior bidirectional recursive neural networks [12] compute backward vectors using only the forward parent node, the GB-RNN uses both forward and backward parent information. This design choice, as the authors argue, better disentangles children from their backward parent vector and enables more effective context propagation.

The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on SemEval 2013 Task 2 for contextual sentiment analysis, improving F1 performance by 3% over standard RNNs. They also introduce hybrid word vectors combining unsupervised and supervised representations, which they show outperforms either approach alone.

The paper's strengths are numerous: the core idea is elegant and well-motivated, the technical execution is clear, and the experimental results are compelling. The authors provide thorough ablation studies and a thoughtful discussion of word vector representations. The paper is well-written, with clear figures and explanations, making it easy to follow the model's operation.

However, there are several concerns regarding the experimental evaluation that need addressing. First, the comparison in Table 2 with the bidirectional RNN baseline [12] lacks critical details: it's unclear whether the baseline was trained with dropout, additional training data, or hybrid word vectors. Given the paper's emphasis on the importance of these elements (particularly dropout and hybrid vectors), this omission makes the comparison potentially misleading.

Second, the paper presents an ensemble result alongside individual model comparisons in Table 2, which is confusing and inappropriate. Ensemble results should be reported separately from individual model performances, as they represent a different kind of experimental setup.

Third, Section 4.4 contains ambiguity when the authors state "we found that a widely used mix of both [unsupervised and supervised word vectors] works better." It's unclear what "widely used mix" refers to—whether it's a standard practice in the field or a specific technique they're referencing.

The paper's claims about the superiority of GB-RNNs over prior work are well-supported by the results, but the experimental setup details are insufficient to fully validate these claims. The authors should clarify the exact experimental conditions used for all baselines, particularly the [12] baseline, and separate the ensemble results from individual model comparisons.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with appropriate theoretical grounding and thorough experimental validation. The claims are well-supported by the results, though the presentation of the experimental comparisons could be improved. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections and helpful visualizations. The originality is strong, as the core idea of bidirectional propagation with a specific backward computation is novel compared to prior work, though it builds on the broader concept of bidirectional models.

The significance of this work is high for natural language processing tasks requiring contextual understanding, particularly aspect-based sentiment analysis. The approach could be extended to other NLP tasks requiring context-dependent interpretation, such as word-sense disambiguation.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated approach to contextual phrase representation
- Clear, elegant, and effective solution to a real problem in NLP
- State-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark
- Well-written and well-organized paper

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Ambiguous experimental setup details that undermine the comparison claims
- Inappropriate presentation of ensemble results alongside individual models
- Lack of sufficient detail about baseline implementations

Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling idea that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With minor revisions to clarify the experimental setup and presentation, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The technical content is sound, the motivation is clear, and the results are impressive. The concerns raised are primarily about presentation and experimental detail rather than fundamental flaws in the approach or results.
