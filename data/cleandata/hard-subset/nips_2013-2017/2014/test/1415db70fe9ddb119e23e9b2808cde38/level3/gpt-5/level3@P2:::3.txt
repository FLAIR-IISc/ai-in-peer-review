Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) for contextual (aspect-specific) sentiment analysis. Starting from a standard tree-structured RNN that composes bottom-up phrase representations, the model adds a single top-down “feedbackward” pass during inference/training. This yields, for each node, a pair of vectors: a feedforward (bottom-up) vector and a feedbackward (top-down) vector. The two are concatenated and fed to a softmax classifier to produce context-dependent labels at all nodes, including words. The approach is motivated by the need to update local predictions using sentence-level context, e.g., assigning entity-targeted sentiment in “Android beats iOS.” Additional contributions include (i) a “hybrid” word representation that concatenates fixed unsupervised embeddings with small task-tuned vectors, and (ii) the use of dropout in RNN training. Experiments on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment) report improvements over a standard RNN, a bidirectional-RNN variant, and competition systems; ensembles of GB-RNNs achieve the best reported results.

The top-down pass places GB-RNNs near prior bidirectional-recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie, NIPS DL Workshop 2013) and inside–outside style recursive models. The proposed backward equations differ slightly in parameterization from these works (e.g., the use of both forward and backward parent vectors and an additional root transform), but the high-level idea—propagating information down the parse tree to inform token-level predictions—is similar. The “belief” terminology evokes belief propagation in graphical models, but the method remains a deterministic neural top-down pass rather than message passing in a probabilistic model.

Strengths:
- Addresses a relevant limitation of purely bottom-up recursive models by incorporating sentence-level context for node-level predictions.
- Clear, implementable architectural modification; compatible with standard backpropagation through structure.
- Empirical gains on SemEval, including with a single model and further with an ensemble.
- Useful practical observations: hybrid word vectors and dropout improve performance.
- Ablations that separate some contributions (e.g., hybrid vectors vs. random init) are informative.

Weaknesses:
Quality:
- Evaluation methodology is unclear in several places, which makes it difficult to assess the size and fairness of gains:
  - The ensemble setup (how many models, selection criterion, variance across runs) is not justified and obscures comparison to single-model baselines; single-model results should be primary.
  - It is not stated clearly whether baselines (RNN and bidirectional-RNN) use the same hybrid word vectors, the same external resources (NRC lexicon, distant supervision), and the same tuning protocol. Table 2 suggests shared settings, but details are missing.
  - The composition and tuning of “best” vs. “ensemble” models are not specified (e.g., whether dev or “dev-test” was used for selection, how hyperparameters were searched).
  - External resources and distant supervision substantially affect performance; ablations without these additions are needed to isolate the contribution of the GB-RNN architecture.
- The parser is off-the-shelf for noisy tweets; no evidence is provided about parsing quality on this domain or robustness of results to parsing errors.

Clarity:
- Overall writing is good, but motivation for the “belief” framing is vague and lacks a precise theoretical justification; the method is better characterized as a top-down recursive pass.
- The distinction with prior bidirectional-recursive models is described, but the practical implications of the parameterization differences are not clearly motivated.
- Several citations are incomplete.

Originality:
- The top-down pass in tree-recursive models has been explored contemporaneously (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; inside–outside RNNs). The specific backward equations and the root transform provide a modest architectural variation, but the conceptual novelty is limited.
- Hybrid embeddings and dropout are orthogonal and have appeared in related contexts; they are useful but not novel.

Significance:
- Results on SemEval are competitive and improvements over a standard RNN are plausible and potentially useful for contextual sentiment.
- Without fully controlled comparisons and capacity-matched baselines (e.g., equal parameter counts), it is difficult to attribute the gains cleanly to the proposed backward pass.
- The approach could be valuable for other token-level tasks on trees (e.g., WSD), but evidence beyond SemEval Twitter is not provided.

Questions for the authors:
1. Please report single-model results as primary, with ensembles only as secondary. How many models in the ensemble, how were they selected, and what is the standard deviation across runs?
2. Did the RNN and bidirectional-RNN baselines use the exact same hybrid embeddings, external lexicon augmentation, distant supervision, and tuning budget? If not, please add matched results.
3. Can you provide ablations that remove (a) NRC lexicon augmentation and (b) distant-supervised tweets, to isolate the architectural gain?
4. How were hyperparameters chosen (search space, selection criterion, number of trials)? Was “dev-test” used for selection?
5. Please clarify parameter counts and compare to a capacity-matched bidirectional-recursive baseline (e.g., adding parameters to equalize nd·n terms).
6. What is the accuracy of the parser on tweets used here, or at least a sensitivity analysis to parsing errors?
7. Is the second entry in Table 3 the configuration used in Tables 1–2? If not, please align or add the most comparable baseline.

Reasons to accept:
- Addresses a real limitation of bottom-up recursive models for contextual sentiment.
- Competitive empirical results on a standard benchmark; improvements over strong neural baselines are plausible.
- Simple, generally applicable architectural idea; practical tips (hybrid vectors, dropout) likely of interest to practitioners.

Reasons to reject:
- Conceptual novelty over existing bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models is limited and the “belief” framing is potentially misleading.
- Experimental methodology lacks clarity and fully controlled, capacity-matched comparisons; reliance on ensembles and added data makes it hard to isolate the core contribution.
- Missing analyses on parser robustness and incomplete citations.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reframe terminology away from “belief propagation” unless a probabilistic justification is provided; “top-down recursive pass” or “inside–outside RNN” would be clearer.
- Make single-model, data-matched, capacity-matched comparisons the centerpiece; move ensembles to supplementary.
- Provide thorough ablations isolating architecture vs. external resources vs. hybrid embeddings vs. dropout.
- Report variance across seeds and give full hyperparameter/tuning details.
- Add parser robustness analysis or use a Twitter-robust parser; include error analysis.
- Complete citations (e.g., full author lists, venues, years) and expand the comparison to closely related bidirectional-recursive work.

Overall assessment:
The results are promising and the model is practically useful, but the contribution over closely related bidirectional recursive models is incremental and the empirical evaluation needs to be clarified and controlled. With stronger, cleaner experiments and clearer positioning, this could be a solid contribution. As it stands, I lean toward rejection, but I would be positively swayed by a revision addressing the evaluation and clarity concerns.
