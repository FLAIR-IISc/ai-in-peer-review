This paper introduces global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs) for contextual sentiment analysis. The GB-RNN extends traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) by incorporating a feedbackward step during inference, allowing phrases to update their predictions based on global sentence context. This architecture is particularly useful for tasks such as aspect-specific sentiment classification, where the sentiment of a word or phrase depends on the surrounding context.

The authors relate their work to previous research in natural language processing, including the use of neural word vectors, recursive neural networks, and bidirectional-recurrent neural networks. They also discuss the limitations of existing approaches, such as the inability of purely feedforward architectures to capture contextual information.

The GB-RNN model is described in detail, including the use of hybrid word vector representations that combine unsupervised and supervised learning. The authors also introduce a novel training method that uses backpropagation through structure and dropout to improve model performance.

The experimental evaluation of the GB-RNN is conducted on the SemEval 2013 dataset, where it outperforms several baseline models and achieves state-of-the-art performance. However, the evaluation methodology has some limitations, such as the use of ensemble results and the lack of clarity on how models were tuned and selected.

Strengths of the paper include the introduction of a novel architecture that addresses a specific limitation of traditional RNNs, and the achievement of state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow.

However, there are several weaknesses and limitations to the paper. The motivation for the GB-RNN is not clearly justified, and the evaluation methodology is not sufficiently clear. The use of ensemble results is not justified, and the comparison to non-ensemble benchmarks is obscured. Additionally, the composition of compared models is unclear, and it is not specified how models were tuned and how ensemble/best models were selected.

In terms of originality, the GB-RNN is a novel extension of traditional RNNs, and the use of hybrid word vector representations is also a new contribution. However, the idea of using a feedbackward step during inference is not entirely new, and similar approaches have been explored in other contexts.

The significance of the paper is that it addresses a specific limitation of traditional RNNs and achieves state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset. The GB-RNN has the potential to be applied to a wide range of natural language processing tasks that require contextual information.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel architecture that addresses a specific limitation of traditional RNNs.
* The GB-RNN achieves state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark dataset.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The motivation for the GB-RNN is not clearly justified.
* The evaluation methodology is not sufficiently clear.
* The use of ensemble results is not justified, and the comparison to non-ensemble benchmarks is obscured.
* The composition of compared models is unclear, and it is not specified how models were tuned and how ensemble/best models were selected.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a clearer motivation for the GB-RNN and a more detailed theoretical justification.
* Clarify the evaluation methodology and provide more details on how models were tuned and selected.
* Avoid using ensemble results and instead report the performance of individual models.
* Provide more details on the composition of compared models and how they were tuned.

Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field of natural language processing, but it requires some improvements in terms of motivation, evaluation methodology, and clarity of presentation.
