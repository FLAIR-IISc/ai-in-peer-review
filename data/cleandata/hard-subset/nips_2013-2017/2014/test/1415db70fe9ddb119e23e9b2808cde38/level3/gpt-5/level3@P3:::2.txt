Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a bidirectional recursive architecture that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a single, top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The downward pass propagates sentence-level context back to every tree node, enabling context-sensitive predictions (e.g., aspect-level sentiment for specific entities within a sentence). Concretely, the model computes forward node vectors with a standard RNN composition, then computes distinct downward vectors via a learned transformation at the root and a top‑down decomposition, and finally classifies each node using the concatenation of forward and downward vectors. The paper further proposes hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed, unsupervised vectors with small supervised task‑specific vectors, and shows dropout improves training.

The work is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; inside–outside RvNNs), and to bidirectional recurrent networks on sequences. Compared to those, GB-RNN uses a different downward computation that conditions on both forward and backward parents, allows a different dimensionality for the downward vectors, and claims improved performance. The paper is positioned within the compositional semantics line of work (Socher et al., RNTN and related) and sentiment analysis on Twitter (SemEval Task 2).

Empirical results are reported on SemEval Twitter sentiment. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance versus SemEval systems and strong baselines; the single model outperforms a standard RNN and a bidirectional RvNN baseline, and an ensemble of top GB‑RNNs yields the best numbers. Note: the manuscript repeatedly references SemEval 2013 Task 2; if the intended claim is state-of-the-art on SemEval 2012 Task 2, please reconcile this discrepancy.

Strengths
- Simple, elegant extension: a single top‑down pass with a small set of additional parameters, well motivated by contextual disambiguation needs.
- Clear derivation and architectural comparison to prior bidirectional recursive models; the use of separate dimensionality and a V transform for downward vectors is sensible and empirically motivated.
- Strong results: substantial gains over a feedforward RNN and prior bidirectional recursive baseline; competitive with SemEval systems while using relatively modest feature engineering.
- Useful auxiliary ideas: hybrid embeddings and dropout for RNNs are practical, and ablations suggest they matter.
- Writing is generally clear, with intuitive motivation and helpful figures.

Weaknesses and concerns about experimental setup
- Fairness/clarity of baselines: It is unclear whether the bidirectional recursive baseline in Table 2 was trained with the same regularization (dropout), additional training data, and hybrid vectors. If GB‑RNN benefits from hybrid embeddings and extra data but the baseline does not, the comparison is confounded.
- Mixing ensemble and single‑model results: The ensemble’s performance is shown alongside single‑model results; this is not an apples‑to‑apples comparison. Ensemble results should be separated or clearly boxed off, and single‑model comparisons should be emphasized in the main table.
- Additional data and training regime: Section 4.4 mixes multiple external resources (NRC lexicon, 176k distant‑supervision tweets, movie reviews) and a two‑stage training scheme (RNN pretrain, then GB‑RNN finetune). It is unclear which subsets are used for which baselines, and how each resource contributes to the final numbers. The phrase “widely used mix of both” is ambiguous; please specify precisely which datasets, label sources, and proportions are used for each experiment.
- Reproducibility details: Important hyperparameters are reported, but full training details (random seeds, number of runs/variance, early stopping criteria, parse failures/coverage) are limited. Confidence intervals or variance across runs would help.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are only on Twitter/SMS SemEval; additional tree‑structured tasks (e.g., phrase‑level sentiment with different domains, semantic role labeling, or WSD) would strengthen the generality claim.

Quality
- The model is sound and well specified. The downward computation is carefully contrasted with related work, and the training procedure is standard (BPTS + AdaGrad + dropout).
- Claims are mostly supported by experiments, but the ambiguity about baseline training conditions and additional data weakens causal attribution. A controlled comparison where all models share identical embeddings, dropout, and data is needed.
- Ablations on hybrid vectors and dropout are useful; more granular ablations (e.g., no V transform, same vs different downward dimensionality) would be valuable.

Clarity
- Clear exposition overall with good intuition. Minor terminology oddity (“feedbackward”) could be standardized to “downward” or “top‑down.”
- Please clarify: (a) whether the bidirectional baseline used identical embeddings/dropout/data, (b) data usage per model in Tables 1–3, (c) what “widely used mix of both” refers to in Section 4.4, and (d) whether the SOTA claim targets SemEval 2013 or 2012.

Originality
- The high-level idea (bidirectional recursion) is not entirely new, but the specific downward computation, parameterization, and its effective use for contextual sentiment constitute a meaningful and well‑motivated variant.
- The hybrid-embedding setup is a pragmatic combination rather than a conceptual novelty.

Significance
- Context propagation to all nodes is broadly useful; if results generalize beyond Twitter, this could influence a range of tree‑structured NLP tasks.
- The performance improvements over strong baselines and over a related bidirectional recursive model indicate real practical value.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a controlled comparison where all single‑model variants (RNN, bidirectional RvNN, GB‑RNN) use the same embeddings, dropout, and training data. Report averages over multiple runs with variance.
- Separate ensemble results from single‑model tables.
- Clarify the data recipe, including counts per source, label quality, and which models used which subsets.
- Expand evaluation or at least provide a second dataset to demonstrate generality.
- Add ablations on the V transform and downward dimensionality.

Questions for the authors
- Were the bidirectional baseline and the standard RNN trained with dropout, hybrid vectors, and the same external data as GB‑RNN?
- What exactly is meant by “widely used mix of both” in Section 4.4?
- Is the claimed SOTA on SemEval 2013 Task 2 or 2012 Task 2?

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant architectural extension with clear motivation
- Strong empirical gains; competitive with SemEval winners
- Simple to implement and potentially broadly applicable
- Clear writing and reasonable analyses

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity about fairness of baseline training and data usage
- Mixing ensemble and single‑model results in main comparisons
- Limited evaluation scope and missing variance estimates

Overall recommendation
A strong paper with a simple, effective idea and excellent results. Conditional on clarifying and tightening the experimental comparisons (especially baseline parity and data usage) and separating ensemble results, I lean toward acceptance.
