- Introduces GB-RNNs: a recursive neural network with a feedbackward pass allowing phrase-level predictions to inform word-level representations.  
- Motivated by context-dependent sentiment examples (e.g., correctly inferring sentiment toward “Android” vs “iOS”).  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment dataset, outperforming prior winner.  
- Explores benefits of dropout and a hybrid word-vector initialization combining unsupervised and supervised embeddings.  
- Situates work within distributional semantics and compositional models, building on prior RNN and bi-directional models.  
- Notes missing discussion of attention mechanisms as an alternative context-incorporating approach.  
- Strength — Technical soundness: clear mathematical formulation and well-motivated feedbackward pass.  
- Strength — Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and explains model and experiments clearly.  
- Strength — Originality: novel global belief propagation mechanism differentiating backward computation from prior work.  
- Strength — Significance: meaningful improvement on aspect-specific sentiment tasks and applicability to other NLP tasks.  
- Weakness — Experimental rigor: unclear implementation details for baselines (dropout, extra data, hybrid vectors).  
- Weakness — Comparison issues: ensemble result presented alongside single-model comparisons is misleading.  
- Weakness — Ambiguity in methods: vague phrase “widely used mix of both” in Section 4.4 hinders reproducibility.  
- Weakness — Limited error analysis: lacks detailed analysis of remaining model errors and failure modes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound approach with strong empirical results and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about baseline comparisons, clarity in some sections, and insufficient error analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Required revision 1: Clarify baseline RNN setup in Table 2 (use of dropout, extra training data, hybrid vectors).  
- Required revision 2: Present ensemble results separately from single-model comparisons.  
- Required revision 3: Specify exactly what is meant by the “widely used mix of both” datasets in Section 4.4.  
- Required revision 4: Add a brief error analysis to identify model weaknesses and future improvements.
