Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) for contextual (aspect-specific) sentiment analysis. Starting from a standard tree-structured RNN that composes word vectors bottom-up along a parse tree, the method adds a single top-down (“feedbackward”) pass that conditions each node’s prediction on a parent-propagated context vector, and then classifies with the concatenation of bottom-up and top-down vectors. The authors also introduce “hybrid” word vectors (concatenating fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small supervised trainable segment) and apply dropout to improve training. Experiments on SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment show gains over a vanilla RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline (Irsoy & Cardie), and an ensemble of GB-RNNs surpasses prior systems.

Conceptually, the model is a two-pass tree network closely related to unfolded recursive autoencoders and to bidirectional recursive models developed in parallel (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema). The paper’s “belief propagation” terminology is potentially confusing, as the mechanism is deterministic neural inference rather than probabilistic message passing on a factor graph.

Strengths
- Clear, well-executed idea: a top-down contextualization pass over trees is a natural extension of feedforward RNNs and addresses the need for context-sensitive labeling.
- Empirical improvements on a widely used benchmark; results are competitive with SemEval systems while using relatively few hand-crafted features.
- Sensible engineering: hybrid word vectors and dropout are simple, effective, and likely of independent interest to practitioners.
- The approach is generally explained clearly with concrete equations and an intuitive motivation.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Terminology/motivation: Calling the approach “global belief” or “belief propagation” is misleading. There is no graphical-model factorization, calibration, or marginal consistency; the method is better described as a two-pass (top-down/bottom-up) tree-RNN or an encoder–decoder over a parse tree without a reconstruction loss. Please align terminology and provide a clearer theoretical justification for why the particular downward parameterization should improve local predictions beyond prior bidirectional recursive models.
- Evaluation methodology:
  - Ensembles: The headline state-of-the-art relies on averaging five independently trained GB-RNNs. This obscures comparisons with single-model baselines and with non-ensemble systems from SemEval. Unless ensembles are used for all models, single-model results should be primary; ensembles can be reported secondarily.
  - Fairness of training data: It is unclear whether all baselines in Table 2 (RNN, Bidirectional-RNN) received the same augmented resources: NRC lexicon-derived examples, 176k noisy tweets, and movie reviews, and whether they were trained in the same two-stage fashion (pretrain bottom-up RNN on generic data, then fine-tune full GB-RNN on SemEval). Section 4.4 suggests the two-step regimen was used for the final model; please specify precisely for each baseline.
  - Features for baselines: The paper states hybrid word vectors with dropout were used for RNN, Bidirectional-RNN, and GB-RNN, but this is not tied explicitly to the entries in Table 2. Please make explicit which baselines used hybrid vectors, dropout, and the same hyperparameter search.
  - Hyperparameter tuning and model selection: The composition of compared models is under-specified. What were the search ranges, how many trials, and what criteria were used for selecting the “best” single models and the ensemble members? Were dev sets shared across models? How were random seeds handled?
  - Comparability across tables: In Table 3 (word-vector ablations), the second entry (fixed unsupervised vectors concatenated with a small supervised segment) appears to be the fairest single-model configuration to compare with Tables 1–2 for assessing GB-RNN vs. other architectures. Please clarify which configuration underlies Tables 1–2 and report all comparisons with that same configuration.
- Parser choice for tweets: The use of a general-domain constituency parser on noisy Twitter text is surprising. Since the model’s structure and supervision depend critically on the tree, please report parser accuracy (or at least qualitative diagnostics) on this domain, or consider a Twitter-optimized parser. At minimum, discuss robustness to parsing errors.
- Clarity and completeness:
  - Some citations (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie; several others in the references) are incomplete; please provide venues/years consistently.
  - Notational inconsistencies (e.g., “feedbackward”) and occasional typos detract slightly from clarity.
- Analysis depth: Beyond a few qualitative examples, there is limited error analysis or probing of where the top-down pass helps (e.g., long-range polarity scope, negation, entity disambiguation). Ablations removing external data and removing the top-down pass would strengthen causal claims.

Quality
Technically sound as an incremental architectural extension; training and optimization are standard. However, claims about “global belief” are not theoretically grounded, and the evaluation leaves ambiguity around fairness (ensembles, extra data, tuning parity). More rigorous ablations and clearer accounting would improve confidence.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow at the model level. Clarify terminology, baselines’ configurations, data usage, and model-selection procedures. Fix incomplete citations and minor notation issues.

Originality
Moderately novel. The top-down pass is closely related to contemporaneous bidirectional recursive models and to unfolded encoder–decoder views of tree networks. The hybrid word-vector trick is a simple, useful combination rather than a conceptual advance.

Significance
Results are reasonable on a relevant task, but the contribution is incremental. With clearer methodology and stronger single-model baselines, the work could be a solid building block for context-sensitive tree models.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely which models used the external NRC lexicon, noisy tweets, and movie reviews? Was the two-stage training applied to baselines?
- Are the Table 2 baselines trained with the same hybrid word vectors, dropout rate, and hyperparameter search? Please enumerate per row.
- Provide single-model results for all systems in Table 1 and report statistical significance vs. strongest single-model non-ensemble baselines.
- Report the parser’s performance or robustness on tweets and any constraints you imposed (beyond “keep labeled phrase in a subtree”).
- Provide an ablation isolating the effect of the downward pass when controlling for external data and hybrid embeddings.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive two-pass tree architecture with measurable gains.
- Competitive results with minimal feature engineering.
- Practical insights (dropout on tree nets; hybrid embeddings).

Cons
- Terminology (“belief propagation”) and motivation are confusing.
- Evaluation obscures fair comparisons (ensembles, external data).
- Incomplete details on baseline composition and tuning.
- Limited analysis of when/why the top-down pass helps.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sound and empirically promising, but the paper lacks a precise motivation/terminology and, more importantly, a sufficiently transparent and controlled evaluation. With clarified experimental methodology, single-model comparisons under identical data, and tighter positioning relative to bidirectional recursive models, this would be much stronger.
