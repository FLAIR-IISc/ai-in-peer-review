This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive, tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up compositional RNN with a single top-down (“feedbackward”) pass at inference time. The forward pass composes word vectors along a parse tree; the top-down pass computes context-conditioned vectors for every node by propagating information from the root to the leaves. Final node predictions concatenate forward and backward representations. The paper targets contextual (target-dependent) sentiment in tweets (SemEval-2013 Task 2), reports that the feedbackward step improves F1 by roughly 3% over a feedforward RNN, and achieves state-of-the-art performance when ensembling several GB-RNNs. Additional contributions include (i) hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed unsupervised embeddings with a small, supervised task-tuned component, and (ii) the use of dropout in the recursive architecture. The model is positioned relative to bidirectional recurrent nets (Schuster & Paliwal), bidirectional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema’s inside–outside), and prior recursive/sentiment work at NIPS/EMNLP (Socher et al., RAE/RNTN; Collobert et al.; GloVe).

Strengths
- Clear and practical motivation: many decisions (e.g., aspect/target sentiment) depend on global context beyond local windows; a top-down pass is a simple, architecture-aligned way to inject context into all nodes.
- Simple extension to a widely used RNN framework; compatible with standard backprop-through-structure training and efficient at inference (one additional pass).
- Empirical improvements on SemEval-2013 sentiment, including competitive single-model results and stronger ensemble performance; qualitative examples are reasonable.
- Sensible engineering: hybrid embeddings and dropout on node vectors yield measurable gains and are likely to be of practical interest.
- Relation to concurrent bidirectional recursive models is discussed, with an explicit parameterization difference and a claim of better performance.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Terminology and framing: calling the top-down pass “global belief” and referring to “belief propagation” is misleading. The method lacks a probabilistic model or message-passing semantics; it is better described as a deterministic top-down contextualization akin to inside–outside or encoder–decoder style passes on trees. This matters for clarity and for relating to prior graphical-model literature.
- Motivation/theory: the paper argues for a top-down pass but does not provide theoretical justification, analysis of when/why it should help, or a clear conceptual separation from existing bidirectional recursive/inside–outside models. The difference from Irsoy & Cardie (and inside–outside) is mostly parametric, not conceptual.
- Evaluation methodology: main claims rely on an ensemble without justifying ensembling relative to baselines. It is unclear whether all compared systems (Table 2) used the same external resources (NRC lexicon, noisy-labeled tweets) and the same hybrid embeddings/dropout. Without matched training data and tuning budgets, the fairness of comparisons is uncertain.
- Tuning transparency: the paper does not specify how hyperparameters were selected for each baseline, how many trials were run, or how the “best” single model and ensemble members were chosen. Variance across random seeds is not reported.
- Baseline configuration: it is unclear whether the RNN and bidirectional-RNN baselines used the hybrid word vectors and identical regularization/search space. The second row of Table 3 appears to be the most comparable baseline to Tables 1–2, but this connection is not made explicit.
- Parsing tweets with an off-the-shelf newswire parser is a fragile choice; the paper should report parser accuracy or at least show sensitivity to parsing errors. The constraint that labeled spans be subtrees may bias results.
- Minor presentation issues: several citations are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) and a few notational inconsistencies/typos (“feedbackward”) detract from clarity.

Quality
- The method is technically sound as an architectural variant and trained with standard tools (AdaGrad, dropout). However, claims about “belief propagation” are not supported by a formal model, and the empirical claims would be stronger with clearer, matched comparisons, single-model results emphasized over ensembles, and variance estimates.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with clear equations and figures. Motivation and naming are vague/misleading; evaluation protocol and baseline details need to be made explicit. Please complete references.

Originality
- Moderate. Top-down passes in recursive models have appeared (inside–outside; bidirectional recursive nets). The specific backward parameterization and hybrid embeddings are incremental rather than conceptual leaps.

Significance
- Practical gains on a popular benchmark and a simple idea that may transfer to other contextual labeling tasks. Broader impact hinges on demonstrating robustness across datasets (e.g., SemEval-2014 ABSA, SST with target annotations) and on stronger, fair comparisons to closely related models.

Requests and suggestions
- Reframe the method without “belief propagation” terminology; relate precisely to inside–outside and bidirectional recursive nets, including matched-parameter ablations.
- Report single-model results as primary; move ensemble numbers to supplementary, and include mean±std over multiple seeds.
- For every row in Tables 1–3, list whether hybrid embeddings, dropout, external lexicons, and noisy-labeled data were used; ensure baselines have matched resources and tuning budgets.
- Clarify which Table 3 configuration underlies the models in Tables 1–2; state the exact dimensions (n, nd) and parameter counts; provide computational cost.
- Detail dropout placement (input vs hidden vs classifier), and provide an ablation.
- Evaluate parser choice: report parsing accuracy on tweets (or use a Twitter-specific tokenizer/parser), and include an experiment assessing sensitivity to parse noise.
- Expand to an additional dataset targeting aspect/target sentiment (e.g., SemEval-2014 ABSA) to support generality.
- Complete and standardize citations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of compositional models (lack of global context).
- Simple, generally applicable architectural tweak with demonstrated gains.
- Competitive results on a standard benchmark; useful engineering insights (hybrid embeddings, dropout).

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty over prior bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models is limited and somewhat misrepresented by terminology.
- Evaluation lacks transparency and relies on ensembles; fairness of comparisons is unclear.
- Limited evidence of generality beyond one dataset; sensitivity to parsing not addressed.

Overall, the results are promising, but the current manuscript would benefit from clearer motivation and substantially stronger, more transparent experimental methodology.
