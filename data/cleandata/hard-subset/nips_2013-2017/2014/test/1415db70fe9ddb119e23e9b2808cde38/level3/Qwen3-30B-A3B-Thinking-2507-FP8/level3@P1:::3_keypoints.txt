- Introduces GB-RNNs with a top-down "feedbackward" pass to incorporate global sentence context into phrase-level sentiment predictions.  
- Uses a hybrid word vector approach (100 unsupervised + 30 supervised dims) and dropout for training.  
- Reports ~3% F1 improvement and claims state-of-the-art on SemEval 2013, outperforming the 2013 competition winner.  
- Problem motivation (contextual sentiment disambiguation) is clear, relevant, and practically important.  
- Hybrid vectors show clear empirical gains and align with prior literature (e.g., Huang et al. 2012).  
- Paper is well-structured with clear technical description and comparisons to bidirectional RNNs.  
- The term "belief propagation" is misleading—feedbackward is a simple top-down recursive pass, not probabilistic belief propagation.  
- Lacks theoretical justification for why a top-down pass is necessary or superior to bidirectional RNNs or window-based methods.  
- Experimental methodology is opaque: the ensemble in Table 1 is unjustified and obscures single-model performance.  
- Baselines in Tables 1–2 are underspecified (unclear whether they used hybrid vectors or dropout), invalidating comparisons.  
- Training details (hyperparameter tuning, data augmentation, use of unlabeled data) are insufficiently reported.  
- Parser accuracy on tweets is not reported, raising concerns about parse quality affecting results.  
- Related work citations and the theoretical comparison to bidirectional RNNs are incomplete or superficial.  
- Model originality is incremental—similar top-down ideas exist (Irsoy & Cardie 2013) and "global belief" framing adds little novel concept.  
- Significance is limited: 3% F1 gains are modest, generalization to other tasks is speculative, and SOTA advancement is not conclusively demonstrated.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject due to mislabeling, poor motivation, and opaque evaluation.  
- Suggested improvements: remove "belief" terminology, clarify baseline implementations (hybrid vectors/dropout), report parser accuracy on tweets, and present single-model results instead of/alongside ensembles.
