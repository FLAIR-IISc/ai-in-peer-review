## Review of “Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks”

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward pass during inference to improve contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea is to allow phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations, addressing the limitation of feedforward RNNs in handling context-dependent sentiment. The authors demonstrate improvements on a Twitter sentiment dataset and achieve state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in compositional semantics and recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2013, 2015; Irsoy & Cardie, 2014). It attempts to address the context-dependence problem highlighted in previous work (Mitchell & Lapata, 2010) by incorporating a backward pass, a concept also explored in bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014). The use of hybrid word vectors, combining unsupervised and supervised representations, is a reasonable approach given the limitations of each individually. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The motivation for the “belief propagation” terminology is weak and feels misleading, as it doesn’t align with standard belief propagation algorithms. More importantly, the experimental methodology is unclear and raises concerns about the validity of the results. Presenting ensemble results without justification obscures comparisons to non-ensemble benchmarks and makes it difficult to assess the contribution of the GB-RNN architecture itself. The composition of the ensemble – how models were tuned, selected, and combined – is not specified. 

Furthermore, it’s unclear whether the benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned using the same parameters or augmented data as the BP-RNN. The paper also doesn’t clarify whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines utilized hybrid word vectors. While the authors acknowledge Irsoy and Cardie (2014), a more detailed comparison of the backward pass computation (Eq. 4 vs. Eq. 5) is needed to fully understand the differences. Minor issues include the incomplete citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) and the surprising choice of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets without reporting its accuracy on this specific data.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Reasonable results on a challenging task, novel extension of RNNs.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Vague motivation, unclear experimental methodology, unjustified use of ensembles, lack of clarity in baseline comparisons.

**Overall:** While the results are promising, the lack of strong theoretical justification and the methodological issues significantly detract from the paper’s contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing the concerns raised regarding experimental clarity and motivation.
