This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a novel extension to traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) that incorporates a feedbackward step during inference to capture contextual information. The GB-RNN is evaluated on the task of contextual sentiment analysis using the SemEval 2013 dataset.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the GB-RNN architecture and its components. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, including a comparison to other recursive neural network models and sentiment analysis techniques. The experimental evaluation is also well-presented, with a clear description of the datasets, experimental setup, and results.

However, there are some concerns regarding the proposal. The term "belief propagation" is used to describe the feedbackward step in the GB-RNN, which may be misleading. Belief propagation typically refers to a specific algorithm for computing marginal probabilities in graphical models, whereas the GB-RNN's feedbackward step is a more general mechanism for incorporating contextual information.

The motivation for the GB-RNN is not entirely clear, and the authors could have provided a more detailed explanation of the benefits of incorporating a feedbackward step in the RNN architecture. Additionally, the experimental evaluation could have been more comprehensive, with a clearer comparison to other state-of-the-art models and a more detailed analysis of the results.

One of the major weaknesses of the paper is the evaluation methodology. The authors present ensemble results, which may obscure comparisons to non-ensemble benchmarks. It is unclear how the models were tuned and how the ensemble/best models were selected. Furthermore, it is unclear whether the benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned the same way or used the same augmented data as the GB-RNN. The authors should have provided more details on the experimental setup and the evaluation methodology.

The composition of the compared models is also unclear. The authors do not specify how the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines were implemented, including whether they used hybrid word vectors. This lack of detail makes it difficult to compare the results of the different models.

The paper has some minor issues, including the use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets without reporting its accuracy on this data. Additionally, some citations are incomplete, which may make it difficult for readers to access the referenced papers.

Overall, the results presented in the paper are reasonable, but the model lacks strong motivation and the experimental methodology is not sufficiently clear. The authors should have provided more details on the experimental setup and the evaluation methodology to make the results more comparable to other state-of-the-art models.
