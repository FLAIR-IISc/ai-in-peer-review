- Proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) that add a single top-down “feedbackward” pass to tree-structured RNNs, producing feedforward and feedback vectors concatenated for context-dependent node labels.  
- Introduces a hybrid word representation (fixed unsupervised embeddings + small task-tuned vectors) and uses dropout in RNN training.  
- Reports empirical improvements on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), with ensembles achieving state-of-the-art results.  
- Method is closely related to prior bidirectional-recursive and inside–outside RNNs; backward equations differ modestly in parameterization.  
- “Belief” terminology is misleading since the method is a deterministic top-down neural pass, not probabilistic belief propagation.  
- Strength: addresses limitation of bottom-up recursive models by incorporating sentence-level context for token-level predictions.  
- Strength: clear, implementable architectural change compatible with standard backpropagation-through-structure.  
- Strength: empirical gains on SemEval and practical findings (hybrid vectors, dropout) that are useful for practitioners.  
- Strength: ablation studies partially informative (e.g., hybrid vs. random init).  
- Weakness (evaluation): ensemble setup is unclear (number of models, selection criteria, variance), obscuring comparison to single-model baselines.  
- Weakness (evaluation): unclear whether baselines used identical resources (hybrid embeddings, lexicon, distant supervision) and tuning, making fairness of comparisons uncertain.  
- Weakness (evaluation): composition/tuning of “best” vs. “ensemble” models and hyperparameter search/selection details are missing.  
- Weakness (evaluation): external resources and distant supervision likely affect performance; ablations without these are needed to isolate architectural contribution.  
- Weakness (robustness): no evidence on parser accuracy for noisy tweets or sensitivity analysis to parsing errors.  
- Clarity issue: “belief” framing lacks precise theoretical justification; better termed a top-down recursive pass.  
- Clarity issue: practical implications of parameterization differences from prior work are not well motivated; several citations are incomplete.  
- Originality issue: top-down passes have been explored previously; the novelty is modest (parameter tweaks and a root transform).  
- Originality issue: hybrid embeddings and dropout are useful but not novel contributions.  
- Significance: competitive SemEval results are promising, but attribution of gains to the backward pass is unclear without capacity-matched and controlled comparisons.  
- Potential impact: approach could generalize to other token-level tree tasks (e.g., WSD), but no experiments beyond SemEval are provided.  
- Requests for authors: report single-model primary results, ensemble details, and standard deviation across runs.  
- Requests for authors: confirm whether baselines used identical embeddings/resources/tuning and provide matched comparisons.  
- Requests for authors: add ablations removing NRC lexicon and distant-supervised tweets to isolate architectural gains.  
- Requests for authors: detail hyperparameter search (space, trials, selection criterion) and whether dev-test was used.  
- Requests for authors: provide parameter counts and compare to capacity-matched bidirectional-recursive baselines.  
- Requests for authors: report parser accuracy on tweets or perform sensitivity analysis to parsing errors.  
- Suggestions: reframe terminology away from “belief propagation,” center single-model and capacity-matched comparisons, provide full ablations and variance reporting, add parser robustness/error analysis, and complete citations.  
- Overall assessment: promising and practically useful but incremental over related work; empirical evaluation and clarity need strengthening, reviewer leans toward rejection pending revisions.
