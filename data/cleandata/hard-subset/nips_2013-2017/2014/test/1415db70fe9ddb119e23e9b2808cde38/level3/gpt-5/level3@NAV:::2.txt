This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a bidirectional recursive architecture that augments standard bottom-up tree composition with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass. The downward pass propagates larger-context information from the root to all nodes, and final predictions use the concatenation of upward and downward representations. Applied to contextual (aspect-specific) sentiment on SemEval 2013 Task 2, the model achieves state-of-the-art results, with notable gains over a standard RNN and over prior bidirectional-recursive variants. The paper also explores hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings concatenated with a small supervised component) and dropout. The approach is closely related to parallel work on bidirectional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema’s inside–outside RNN), but differs in its specific top-down computation and the use of a separate transform for the root and parent-conditioned decomposition.

Quality: The model is technically sound, the motivation is clear, and the architectural differences from prior bidirectional-recursive methods are well explained. Empirical results are strong. However, the experimental setup leaves important ambiguities: it is unclear whether the prior bidirectional-recursive baseline reported in Table 2 was trained with the same ingredients (dropout, additional training data, and hybrid vectors). The inclusion of an ensemble system alongside single-model baselines in the same table is misleading; these should be clearly separated. Section 4.4 contains an ambiguity (“widely used mix of both”); please specify precisely which resources and how they were combined. Reproducibility would benefit from explicit training details for all systems (data used per model, exact hyperparameters, seeds) and a statement about code/embeddings release.

Clarity: Generally excellent. The writing is crisp and the model is well motivated and illustrated. The noted ambiguities and table presentation issues should be addressed.

Originality: Moderate. The idea of a downward pass in tree models is known, but the proposed downward computation and its integration with hybrid embeddings yield clear improvements.

Significance: High for NLP practitioners using tree-structured models; the method advances the state of the art on a widely used benchmark and is simple to adopt.

Pros:
- Simple, elegant extension with compelling intuition and strong gains.
- Clear writing and useful analysis of hybrid embeddings and dropout.
- Demonstrated SOTA on SemEval 2013 Task 2.

Cons:
- Fairness of baseline comparisons is unclear.
- Ensemble conflated with single-model results.
- Ambiguity in Section 4.4; incomplete training details.

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying the experimental setup and separating ensemble results from single-model comparisons.
