Summary
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured model that augments standard feedforward recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference/training time. The upward pass computes context-independent compositional representations; the downward pass propagates global contextual information from the root to all nodes, enabling context-sensitive labeling (e.g., aspect-specific sentiment at words/phrases far from their disambiguating context). The method uses a new top-down parameterization that conditions a child’s downward vector on both the parent’s forward and downward vectors and allows the downward representation to have a separate dimensionality. The paper also proposes hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings concatenated with task-tuned dimensions) and applies dropout to node vectors, reporting gains. On SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), GB-RNN improves over a standard RNN and over contemporaneous bidirectional/bottom-up–top-down recursive models, and achieves state-of-the-art results, including further gains from an ensemble.

Relation to prior work
The model builds directly on Socher et al.’s compositional RNNs and sentiment treebank work for bottom-up composition, and contrasts with (i) window-based and recurrent models such as Collobert et al. and bidirectional RNNs (Schuster & Paliwal), and (ii) bidirectional/bottom-up–top-down recursive models such as Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN and Le & Zuidema’s inside–outside RNN. Compared to these, GB-RNN uses a different downward computation that incorporates both forward and downward parent context, and reports stronger empirical results on Twitter sentiment. The idea of unfolding in autoencoder-based recursive models is also discussed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is well specified, including the distinct downward transform V and decomposition matrix W↓, and the use of concatenated forward/downward vectors for classification. Training is via backpropagation through structure with AdaGrad and dropout. The ablations (RNN vs. bidirectional-RNN vs. GB-RNN; hybrid vs. non-hybrid embeddings; with/without additional data) and qualitative examples support the claims.
- Experimental concerns: Several aspects of the experimental setup are ambiguous, potentially confounding the attribution of gains:
  1) Fair baseline parity: It is unclear whether the compared bidirectional/inside–outside recursive baseline(s) were trained with the same dropout scheme, hybrid word vectors, and additional resources (lexicon, distant supervision, movie reviews). Please clarify and, ideally, rerun those baselines under identical settings.
  2) Ensemble vs single models: The ensemble result is reported alongside single-model baselines; this should be separated or clearly marked to avoid misleading comparisons.
  3) Section 4.4 ambiguity: The phrase “widely used mix of both” is unclear—does “both” refer to the NRC lexicon and distant-supervision tweets, or to tweets and SMS, or to unsupervised and supervised embeddings? Please specify exactly which datasets/resources are mixed and how.
  4) Parsing: The approach relies on constituency parses for noisy Twitter/SMS. Although you constrain trees to contain labeled spans, please quantify parser error rates, the proportion of examples needing constraints, and the performance sensitivity to parsing errors or to alternative trees.
  5) Cost: Provide complexity and runtime comparisons (training and inference) for GB-RNN vs. standard RNN and other bidirectional recursive models, including the impact of the extra parameters (n·nd) and the downward pass.
- Additional suggestions: Report the effect of nd and the V transform via ablations; analyze gains as a function of distance between target span and its disambiguating context; clarify how dropout is applied on trees (per node, per step; at test time disabled).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for context-sensitive labeling is compelling, and the model equations are explicit. Areas to improve clarity:
- Unify and consistently name related baselines (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema) and ensure the comparative equations are aligned with what is implemented.
- Clean up Section 4.4 wording (“widely used mix of both”) and clearly list all external data and how each is used.
- Separate single-model from ensemble results in tables, and state whether all models use the same embeddings and regularization.
- Minor: ensure all reference numbers map to the intended works in text (avoid ambiguous bracketed numbers when multiple similar works exist).

Originality
The high-level idea of combining bottom-up and top-down passes on trees is not entirely new, but the specific downward parameterization (conditioning on both forward and backward parent, separate dimensionality, and simple one-step top-down propagation integrated with a softmax classifier) is a neat and effective variant. The hybrid embedding and dropout application to recursive models are useful engineering contributions. The originality is incremental but meaningful.

Significance
The gains on SemEval-2013 Task 2 are practically significant, especially for aspect/context-sensitive sentiment, and the approach is broadly applicable to other token-/span-level labeling tasks requiring long-range context on trees (e.g., word sense disambiguation, entity-level sentiment). If the fairness issues are addressed, this work should be influential as a simple, general upgrade to tree models.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, well-motivated extension of recursive models with a principled top-down pass.
- Strong empirical improvements over standard RNNs and reported SOTA on SemEval-2013.
- Solid ablations showing benefits of hybrid embeddings and dropout.
- Method is simple to implement and potentially useful beyond sentiment analysis.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Ambiguities in experimental setup may inflate the margin over competing recursive baselines.
- Ensemble and single-model results are mixed, complicating interpretation.
- Heavy reliance on parsing for noisy text without a thorough robustness analysis.
- Novelty relative to other bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models is moderate.

Questions for the authors
1) Were Irsoy & Cardie and Le & Zuidema baselines trained with identical embeddings, dropout, and external data? If not, can you provide parity experiments?
2) What is the impact of removing V (setting p↓root = proot and sharing dimensions), and of varying nd?
3) How sensitive is GB-RNN to parser errors? Can you report performance with automatically parsed trees without span constraints, or with alternative parses?
4) Please clarify the datasets/resources used in Section 4.4 and quantify the contribution of each (lexicon vs distant tweets vs movie reviews).
5) How exactly is dropout applied on tree nodes? Is it per node vector independently, and is it also applied to word embeddings?

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying baseline parity and separating ensemble results. The paper presents a clean and effective idea with solid results and likely impact; addressing the experimental ambiguities would strengthen the case.
