

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel extension to standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) that incorporates a feedbackward pass to propagate global contextual information to all nodes in the parse tree. The authors address a critical limitation of existing RNNs for sentiment analysis: their inability to correctly predict context-dependent labels (e.g., "Android" being positive in "Android beats iOS" but neutral in isolation). The GB-RNN adds a top-down recursive pass that combines with the standard bottom-up feedforward pass, allowing all phrase and word representations to incorporate global sentence context. The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 Task 2 sentiment analysis challenge, improving F1 by 3% over standard RNNs and outperforming all competition baselines.

The paper builds upon two key strands of prior work: recursive neural networks for compositional semantics (Socher et al., 2011, 2013) and bidirectional architectures (Schuster & Paliwal, 1997; Irsoy & Cardie, 2014). While the latter work shares the high-level idea of bidirectional information flow, the authors provide a theoretically sound distinction: their feedbackward computation explicitly uses both forward and backward parent vectors at each node (Equation 5), whereas Irsoy & Cardie (2014) use only the forward parent vector. This subtle difference enables more effective context propagation, as demonstrated by the superior empirical results. The paper also introduces a hybrid word vector representation combining pre-trained unsupervised vectors with task-specific supervised vectors, showing that this approach outperforms both pure unsupervised and supervised methods.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The core idea is elegant and well-motivated by clear examples of context-dependent sentiment (Fig. 1), making it easy to understand why standard RNNs fail and how GB-RNNs address this limitation. The technical contribution is significant yet accessible, with the feedbackward pass adding minimal complexity while substantially improving performance. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple strong baselines including the SemEval 2013 competition winner, and demonstrating the effectiveness of the hybrid word vectors and dropout regularization. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture and experiments, making it easy for readers to understand and potentially reproduce the work.

However, several concerns regarding the experimental setup warrant attention. The comparison with the Irsoy & Cardie (2014) baseline in Table 2 is problematic: it's unclear whether their model was trained with dropout, the hybrid word vectors, or additional training data, making the 3% performance gap potentially attributable to these factors rather than the model architecture alone. The paper states the GB-RNN outperforms the "winner of the Sentiment competition of SemEval 2013," but Table 2 shows the competition winner achieved 62.1% F1, while the GB-RNN ensemble achieved 65.1%, though the ensemble result is presented in the same table as individual model results (Table 2), which is inappropriate as ensemble results cannot be directly compared to single-model results. The authors should have presented ensemble results separately.

Additionally, Section 4.4's claim that "the widely used mix of both [unsupervised and supervised word vectors] is superior" lacks specificity - it's unclear what "widely used mix" refers to, and the paper doesn't provide a clear comparison against standard approaches like GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) or word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013). The authors should clarify this comparison.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong empirical results, though the experimental comparisons need clarification.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the model and experiments.
- Originality: While the high-level idea of bidirectional processing is not entirely novel, the specific implementation with the feedbackward pass is novel and well-motivated, differentiating it from prior work.
- Significance: The approach addresses a meaningful limitation in compositional NLP models with clear practical impact on sentiment analysis, and the framework could generalize to other NLP tasks like word-sense disambiguation.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to compositional NLP models by demonstrating how to effectively incorporate global context into recursive architectures. The core idea is simple yet powerful, and the results are compelling. While the experimental comparisons require refinement, the fundamental contribution is strong. The authors have provided a clear, well-executed study that advances the state of the art in contextual sentiment analysis.

Pro: Elegant solution to a well-identified problem, clear writing, strong empirical results, significant improvement over standard RNNs.
Con: Experimental comparisons lack necessary detail for fair comparison, ensemble results improperly presented with single-model results.

Overall, this is a strong NIPS submission that makes a clear technical contribution to the field of compositional neural networks. With minor revisions to clarify the experimental comparisons, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
