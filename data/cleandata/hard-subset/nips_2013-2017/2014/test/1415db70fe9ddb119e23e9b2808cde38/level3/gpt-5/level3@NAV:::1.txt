This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive model that augments the standard bottom-up composition with a single top-down pass at inference so that phrase-level predictions can influence word-level representations and labels. The model further proposes hybrid word vectors by concatenating fixed unsupervised embeddings with small supervised task-specific components, and uses dropout. The approach is applied to contextual/targeted sentiment on SemEval 2013 Twitter data, reporting gains over a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and prior SemEval systems. Relative to prior work on recursive and bidirectional models (e.g., Socher et al.; Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema’s inside–outside RNN), the novelty is the top-down pass paired with hybrid vectors and an emphasis on inference-time backward information flow.

Quality: Empirically promising with ablations and state-of-the-art results; however, experimental fairness and training details are underspecified. The paper claims strong gains, but comparisons to SemEval systems mix single vs ensemble models; the role of external data is not fully disentangled. Section 3.5 lacks sufficient specification of backpropagation-through-structure, unfolding, and gradient flow into word embeddings (especially the hybrid setup) to ensure reproducibility.

Clarity: Generally well written and motivated. Terminology (“belief propagation,” “feedbackward”) is misleading; “forward–backward” or “top-down” would be clearer. Section 3.6’s relation to Irsoy & Cardie needs a sharper, self-contained formal distinction. Section 4.1 should specify SemEval Task 2 subtask (A vs B), and clearly describe parser constraints and failure handling. Consider merging Sections 4.3–4.4.

Originality: The top-down step in a recursive classifier is closely related to bidirectional recursive models; the hybrid word vectors appear to be the clearest new contribution. The paper would benefit from an explicit discussion of differences to Irsoy & Cardie’s backward equations, parameter counts, and why the proposed coupling should help.

Significance: If clarified, the method is likely to influence work on context-sensitive labeling beyond sentiment (e.g., WSD, coreference cues).

Specific concerns and requests:
- Report single-model test results vs baselines (all using the same hybrid vectors, dropout, and tuning budget) and separately report ensemble gains.
- Detail training: BPTS unfolding for both passes, how deep gradients reach, whether unsupervised dimensions are frozen throughout, optimization schedule, seeds, early stopping, and compute.
- Provide complexity/runtime and parameter-count comparisons to bidirectional RNNs; add an ablation isolating the effect of hybrid vectors vs the top-down pass.
- Clarify SemEval subtask, external resources used, and provide results without external data.
- Rename the method to avoid “belief propagation.”

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear motivation; simple, generally applicable idea.
- Strong empirical gains and sensible hybrid embeddings.
- Potentially impactful beyond sentiment.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete comparison to prior bidirectional recursive models.
- Reproducibility gaps in training/inference details.
- Some evaluation confounds (ensemble vs single; external data).
