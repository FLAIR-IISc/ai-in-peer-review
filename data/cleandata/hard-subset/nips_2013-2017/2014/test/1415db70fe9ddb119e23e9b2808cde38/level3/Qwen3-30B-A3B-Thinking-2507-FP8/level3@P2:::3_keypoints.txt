- Introduces GB-RNNs: adds a feedbackward pass to recursive nets to incorporate global sentence context during inference.  
- Uses hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + task-specific trainable) and dropout.  
- Reports ~3% F1 gain over standard RNNs and competitive SemEval 2013 results.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and well-connected to prior work.  
- Hybrid vector approach sensibly addresses antonym/context issues in sentiment.  
- Dropout usage and training details are well-motivated.  
- Misleading terminology: calls the method "belief propagation" though it is unrelated to Bayesian belief propagation.  
- Evaluation flaws: main reported results are ensembles (top 5 GB-RNNs) with no justification or training details for ensembling.  
- Unclear baseline comparisons: not stated whether baselines used the same hybrid vectors, possibly inflating reported gains.  
- Hyperparameter tuning/model selection procedures (e.g., how top-5 were chosen) are insufficiently documented.  
- Data preprocessing concern: no report of Stanford Parser accuracy on noisy tweet domain.  
- Originality is limited: a minor technical variation of Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN with no clear theoretical advantage.  
- Significance is incremental: claims of broader applicability (e.g., WSD) are unsupported and paper doesn't clearly beat best 2013 systems.  
- Minor clarity issues: incomplete citations and a vague motivating example.  
- Recommendation: Reject; advise reporting non-ensemble results, fully documenting baselines/hyperparameter tuning, and removing the "belief propagation" framing.
