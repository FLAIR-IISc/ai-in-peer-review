- Introduces GB-RNNs: a recursive model adding a single top-down inference pass so phrase-level predictions inform word-level representations and labels, plus hybrid word vectors and dropout.
- Applied to targeted sentiment on SemEval 2013 Twitter data, reporting gains over standard RNNs, bidirectional recursive baselines, and prior SemEval systems.
- Empirically promising with ablations and state-of-the-art results, but experimental fairness and training details are underspecified.
- Comparisons to SemEval systems conflate single models and ensembles and do not fully disentangle the role of external data.
- Section 3.5 lacks specification of backpropagation-through-structure, unfolding, and how gradients flow into hybrid word embeddings, hurting reproducibility.
- Terminology like “belief propagation” and “feedbackward” is misleading; clearer terms would be “top-down” or “forward–backward.”
- Relation to Irsoy & Cardie and other bidirectional recursive models needs a sharper, self-contained formal distinction and parameter-count comparison.
- Section 4.1 should specify which SemEval Task 2 subtask was used and detail parser constraints and failure handling; consider merging Sections 4.3–4.4 for clarity.
- Recommend reporting single-model test results with identical hybrids/dropout/tuning, then separately report ensemble gains.
- Request full training details: BPTS unfolding for both passes, gradient depth, whether unsupervised dimensions are frozen, optimization schedule, seeds, early stopping, and compute.
- Ask for complexity/runtime and parameter-count comparisons to bidirectional RNNs and an ablation isolating hybrid vectors vs the top-down pass.
- Clarify use of external resources and provide results without external data; rename the method to avoid “belief propagation.”
- Pros: clear motivation, simple generally applicable idea, strong empirical gains, and useful hybrid embeddings.
- Cons: incomplete comparison to prior bidirectional recursive models, reproducibility gaps in training/inference details, and evaluation confounds (ensemble vs single; external data).
