- Introduces GB‑RNN: a bidirectional recursive network with a single top‑down “feedbackward” pass that propagates sentence‑level context to every tree node for context‑sensitive predictions.  
- Model computes forward node vectors, a distinct learned downward decomposition (root transform + top‑down pass), and classifies nodes using concatenated forward + downward vectors.  
- Proposes hybrid word embeddings (fixed unsupervised + small supervised vectors) and finds dropout improves training.  
- Downward computation differs from prior bidirectional recursive models by conditioning on both forward and backward parents, allowing different downward dimensionality and a V transform.  
- Evaluated on SemEval Twitter sentiment; single GB‑RNN outperforms standard RNN and a bidirectional RvNN baseline; an ensemble yields best overall numbers.  
- Manuscript inconsistently references SemEval 2013 vs 2012 — needs reconciliation of the SOTA claim.  
- Strength: simple, elegant architectural extension with few extra parameters, well motivated for contextual disambiguation.  
- Strength: clear derivation and comparison to prior work; separate downward dimensionality and V transform are sensible and empirically motivated.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains over baselines and competitive performance with SemEval systems using modest feature engineering.  
- Strength: hybrid embeddings and dropout are practical, and ablation studies show they matter.  
- Strength: writing is generally clear with intuitive motivation and helpful figures.  
- Weakness: unclear whether bidirectional baseline had identical regularization, hybrid embeddings, and external training data, risking confounded comparisons.  
- Weakness: ensemble and single‑model results are mixed together, making comparisons non‑apples‑to‑apples.  
- Weakness: Section 4.4 mixes multiple external resources and a two‑stage training scheme without specifying which subsets were used for which models or their contributions.  
- Weakness: limited reproducibility details (random seeds, number of runs/variance, early stopping, parse failures/coverage); confidence intervals absent.  
- Weakness: evaluation scope limited to Twitter/SMS SemEval; generality across other tree‑structured tasks is untested.  
- Quality: model is sound and well specified; training procedure is standard and contrasts to related work are careful.  
- Quality concern: ambiguity about baseline training/data weakens causal attribution; a controlled comparison is needed.  
- Ablation: existing ablations on hybrid vectors and dropout are useful, but more granular ablations (e.g., no V transform, downward dimensionality choices) are needed.  
- Clarity: exposition is clear, but the term “feedbackward” is odd — recommend “downward” or “top‑down”; several data/experiment clarifications requested.  
- Originality: bidirectional recursion is not entirely new, but the specific downward computation and parameterization are a meaningful, well‑motivated variant.  
- Originality: hybrid‑embedding setup is pragmatic but not a conceptual novelty.  
- Significance: propagating context to all nodes could be broadly useful and influential if results generalize beyond Twitter.  
- Suggestions: provide controlled comparisons with identical embeddings/dropout/data and report variance across runs.  
- Suggestions: separate ensemble results from single‑model tables and fully specify the data recipe (counts, label sources, which models used which data).  
- Suggestions: expand evaluation to at least one additional dataset and include ablations on the V transform and downward dimensionality.  
- Questions: were baselines trained with the same dropout, hybrid vectors, and external data as GB‑RNN?  
- Questions: what does “widely used mix of both” refer to in Section 4.4?  
- Questions: is the claimed SOTA on SemEval 2013 Task 2 or 2012 Task 2?  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant extension, strong empirical gains, simple to implement, potentially broadly applicable, clear writing.  
- Cons for acceptance: ambiguity about baseline parity and data usage, mixed ensemble/single‑model reporting, limited evaluation scope and missing variance estimates.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper likely to be accepted conditional on clarifying/tightening experimental comparisons, baseline parity, data usage, and separating ensemble results.
