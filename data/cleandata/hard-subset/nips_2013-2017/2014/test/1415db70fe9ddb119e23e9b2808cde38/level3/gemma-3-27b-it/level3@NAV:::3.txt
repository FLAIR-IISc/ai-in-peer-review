## Review of “Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks”

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward step during inference to improve contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea is to allow phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations, addressing the limitations of purely feedforward approaches in tasks requiring contextual understanding, like aspect-specific sentiment classification. The authors demonstrate improvements on the SemEval 2013 dataset and claim state-of-the-art performance.

The motivation for this work, while pointing to a valid problem in compositional semantics, feels somewhat vague. While the “Android beats iOS” example is illustrative, the theoretical justification for the “belief propagation” mechanism—essentially a single feedback pass—is underdeveloped. Referencing related work like Irsoy & Cardie (2012) on bidirectional RNNs is good, but the distinctions aren’t fully fleshed out, and the term "belief propagation" is a misnomer considering its established meaning in probabilistic inference. The connection to unfolding autoencoders (Socher et al., 2012) is mentioned but doesn't appear to fully differentiate the proposed method. 

The primary weakness lies in the experimental methodology. The presentation of ensemble results without a clear justification is problematic. While ensembles are common, reporting only the ensemble score obscures the performance of the core GB-RNN model and hinders comparison to non-ensemble baselines. The composition of the compared models is also unclear. The paper doesn’t detail how baselines were tuned or if they used the same augmented data as the BP-RNN, making fair comparison difficult. Specifically, it’s unclear whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines utilized hybrid word vectors, as described in Section 3.4. The likely most comparable baseline (second entry in Table 3) deserves more prominent discussion for assessing the impact of the BP-RNN's modifications.

Minor issues include the surprising choice of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets—reporting its accuracy on this data is crucial—and several incomplete citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie, 2012). 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Reasonable results, demonstrating a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs and achieving state-of-the-art performance on SemEval 2013. The idea of incorporating contextual feedback is intuitively appealing.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Weak motivation, misleading terminology ("belief propagation"), and a flawed experimental setup that hinders clear evaluation and comparison. Lack of clarity regarding experimental details.

**Overall:** While the results are promising, the lack of strong theoretical grounding and the issues with the experimental methodology prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. Significant revisions addressing these points are required.
