This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) for contextual/targeted sentiment in tweets. The model augments a standard feedforward RNN over parse trees with a single top-down pass at inference, concatenating forward and “feedbackward” vectors before classification. The authors further use hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + supervised) and dropout. The approach is related to prior recursive architectures used at NIPS and elsewhere: RNTN and unfolding recursive autoencoders (Socher et al.), bidirectional-recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside RNN), and bidirectional RNNs. Despite the “belief” terminology, the method is a deterministic, parameterized top-down pass rather than probabilistic message passing.

Quality: The model is a reasonable architectural variant, and results on SemEval’13 Twitter sentiment are competitive. However, the evaluation has major methodological gaps. Ensemble numbers are emphasized without justification and obscure comparability to non-ensemble baselines. It is unclear how models were tuned, how the “best” and ensemble members were selected, whether baselines (RNN/BiRNN) used the same hybrid word vectors and dropout, and whether all systems in Table 2 were trained with the same augmented data (NRC lexicon, distant supervision). The second entry of Table 3 appears to be the fairest single-model baseline for assessing BP-RNN; this comparison should be foregrounded with significance tests.

Clarity: Writing is generally clear, but the motivation is vague and lacks theoretical justification for the specific top-down parameterization. The term “belief propagation” is misleading in the context of graphical models. Details missing: parser accuracy on tweets, exact hyperparameter search space, and training protocol across comparisons.

Originality: The idea of a top-down pass in recursive nets is not new; the contribution is an alternative parameterization and application to aspect-level sentiment with hybrid embeddings. Positioning vs. Irsoy & Cardie and Le & Zuidema should be sharpened with controlled ablations.

Significance: Improvements are modest and hard to attribute given confounds (ensembles, extra data). Potentially useful if clarified, but current evidence is insufficient.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, simple architectural variant with some empirical gains.
- Practical insight on hybrid word vectors and dropout.

Cons for acceptance:
- Misleading terminology; weak theoretical motivation.
- Inadequate experimental transparency and fairness.
- Overreliance on ensembles; unclear baseline parity.
- Missing/incomplete citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie).

Minor: Report parser accuracy on tweets; fix incomplete references.

Overall: Results are reasonable, but motivation and methodology need substantial clarification before the work can be reliably assessed.
