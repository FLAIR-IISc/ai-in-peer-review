This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard recursive neural networks with a backward pass to incorporate contextual information into phrase and word representations. The motivation stems from the limitations of feedforward RNNs in handling context-dependent sentiment, illustrated with examples like aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The authors claim a 3% F1 performance improvement on a contextual sentiment task and state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The work is well-written and motivated, presenting a logical progression from the limitations of existing models to the proposed GB-RNN architecture. The idea of incorporating a backward pass to refine representations based on global context is a natural and interesting extension to bidirectional recursive models. The introduction of hybrid word vectors—combining unsupervised and supervised representations—appears to be a key contribution, potentially differentiating this work from prior approaches.

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. The connection to Irsoy and Cardie (2012) is inadequately addressed. While the authors acknowledge their work, the distinction between the backward computation in GB-RNNs and their bidirectional RNN is not sufficiently clear, particularly regarding equations 4 & 5 in the paper. A more detailed comparison, including a tabular summary of the algorithmic differences, would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the use of the term "belief propagation" feels misleading; “forward-backward” or “bidirectional recursive” more accurately reflect the core mechanism.

The experimental setup raises questions regarding fairness and clarity. It is unclear whether the baseline models (RNN, B-RNN) utilized the same hybrid word vectors as the GB-RNN, a critical factor impacting comparison validity.  Furthermore, details regarding model tuning and selection are sparse. Were results based on a single best model or an ensemble? If an ensemble was used, the averaging method should be specified.

Section 3.5 (Training) is surprisingly brief. It mentions backpropagation through structure but lacks details crucial for reproducibility.  Specifically, how is the network unfolded for backpropagation? How deep do the gradients reach when updating word embeddings?  The impact of dropout, while acknowledged, requires more elaboration on hyperparameter selection and its observed effects.

Section 3.6’s comparison with Irsoy and Cardie is convoluted and difficult to follow.  The equations are presented without sufficient explanation of the differences. A clearer, more concise explanation of the key distinctions is essential.

Section 4.1, describing the SemEval 2013 task, assumes a level of familiarity with NLP tasks that may not be present in all NIPS attendees. Clarifying which subtask of SemEval 2013 is being addressed and providing a more intuitive explanation of the parser constraints would broaden the paper's accessibility. The integration of Sections 4.3 and 4.4 into a unified discussion of experimental results would also improve flow.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension to recursive neural networks addressing a clear limitation.
*   Well-written and clearly motivated.
*   Demonstrates improved performance on a challenging sentiment analysis task and achieves state-of-the-art results on SemEval 2013.
*   Hybrid word vector approach presents a potentially valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently clear distinction from Irsoy and Cardie (2012).
*   Experimental setup lacks clarity regarding baseline comparisons and model selection.
*   Training details are too sparse for reproducibility.
*   "Belief propagation" is a misleading term.
*   Section 3.6 is confusing.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to contextual sentiment analysis with a novel extension to recursive neural networks. However, the lack of clarity in several areas—particularly the comparison to prior work and the experimental details—detracts from its overall impact. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and increase confidence in its contributions.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above. A focused clarification of the differences with Irsoy and Cardie, a more detailed description of the experimental setup, and a revision of the terminology are crucial for bolstering the paper’s credibility and impact.
