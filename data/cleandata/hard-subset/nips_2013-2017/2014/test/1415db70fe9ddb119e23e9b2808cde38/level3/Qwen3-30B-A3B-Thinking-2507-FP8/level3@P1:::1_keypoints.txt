- Proposes Global Belief RNNs (GB-RNNs) that add a top-down inference step to standard recursive neural networks for context-dependent phrase and word classification.  
- Targets limitation of recursive models in handling labels that depend on broader sentence context, illustrated via aspect-specific sentiment analysis.  
- GB-RNN computes both forward (bottom-up) and backward (top-down) representations so phrase-level predictions can influence word embeddings and labels.  
- Evaluated on SemEval 2013 aspect-specific sentiment task, achieving state-of-the-art results and ~3% F1 gain over standard RNNs (5%+ in ablation comparisons).  
- Builds on prior recursive and bidirectional RNN work (Socher et al.; Irsoy & Cardie), but the distinction from Irsoy & Cardie is not sufficiently clarified.  
- Main claimed novelty (feedbackward mechanism) may be overstated; hybrid word vector representation (unsupervised + supervised) appears to be the key driver of improvements.  
- Refers to the method as “belief propagation,” which is misleading—it's more accurately a forward-backward inference procedure, not BP in graphical models.  
- Strengths: clear problem statement, well-motivated approach, strong empirical performance, and thorough experimental setup with ablation studies.  
- Shows hybrid word vectors outperform purely supervised or unsupervised vectors, and dropout helps train high-dimensional representations.  
- Experimental setup is thorough, but some comparisons may be unfair if baselines did not use the same hybrid vectors—this needs explicit confirmation.  
- Section 3.6’s comparison to Irsoy & Cardie is confusing and fails to clearly explain differences in backward computation and parent usage.  
- Section 3.5 (training procedure) is too brief, lacking details on backpropagation through structure, how word embeddings are updated, and feedbackward implementation.  
- Section 4.1 lacks sufficient explanation of the SemEval task details, which subtask is addressed, parser constraints, and dev/test dataset differences (tweets vs SMS).  
- Paper is generally well-written but would benefit from improved section organization; Sections 4.3 and 4.4 should be consolidated.  
- Quality: technically sound and empirically supported; clarity is good overall but needs expansion in key methodological sections.  
- Originality: moderate—feedbackward idea is novel in this exact form but bidirectional/contextual processing has prior art.  
- Significance: strong practical value for sentiment analysis; broader NLP impact is less certain, though hybrid vectors have wider applicability.  
- Recommendations for revision: clarify distinction from Irsoy & Cardie with precise math, expand training/backprop details, explicitly state which vectors baselines used, improve SemEval/task description, and merge related analysis sections.  
- Overall verdict: useful extension with strong empirical results; core contribution likely hybrid vectors rather than feedbackward mechanism; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
