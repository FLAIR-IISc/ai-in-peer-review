- Proposal: GB-RNNs add a single top-down “feedbackward” pass to tree-structured RNNs to produce context-conditioned node vectors and improve target-dependent sentiment classification.
- Empirical claim: Feedbackward step yields ~3% F1 improvement over feedforward RNN and ensembles achieve state-of-the-art on SemEval-2013 Task 2.
- Contribution: Hybrid word vectors combining fixed unsupervised embeddings with small supervised task-tuned components.
- Contribution: Use of dropout in recursive architectures for regularization.
- Strength: Clear, practical motivation that many decisions need global context and a top-down pass injects that context simply.
- Strength: Simple extension to standard RNNs, compatible with backprop-through-structure and efficient at inference (one extra pass).
- Strength: Measurable empirical improvements and reasonable qualitative examples.
- Strength: Sensible engineering choices (hybrid embeddings, dropout) with practical gains.
- Strength: Paper positions itself relative to bidirectional recursive models and claims parametric differences and better performance.
- Weakness: Terminology is misleading—calling the top-down pass “global belief” and “belief propagation” is inappropriate without a probabilistic model.
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical justification or analysis of when and why a top-down pass helps versus existing inside–outside/bidirectional recursive models.
- Weakness: Main claims rely on ensembles without justifying ensembling relative to baselines; fairness of comparisons is unclear.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter tuning and selection procedures are not transparent; variance across random seeds is unreported.
- Weakness: It's unclear whether baselines used the same hybrid embeddings and regularization; comparable baseline configuration is not explicitly matched.
- Weakness: Using an off-the-shelf newswire parser for tweets is fragile; parser accuracy and sensitivity to parsing errors are not reported.
- Weakness: Minor presentation issues—incomplete citations, notational inconsistencies, and typos (e.g., “feedbackward”).
- Quality: Technically sound as an architectural variant, but claims about “belief propagation” are unsupported and empirical claims need clearer, matched comparisons and variance estimates.
- Clarity: Generally well written with clear equations and figures, but motivation/naming and evaluation/baseline details need clarification.
- Originality: Moderate; top-down passes and inside–outside-style methods exist—contribution is mainly parametric/engineering incremental.
- Significance: Potentially useful practical tweak with benchmark gains, but broader impact depends on robustness across datasets and fair comparisons.
- Request: Reframe method without “belief propagation” terminology and relate precisely to inside–outside and bidirectional recursive nets with matched-parameter ablations.
- Request: Report single-model results as primary, move ensemble results to supplementary, and include mean±std over multiple seeds.
- Request: For all tables, indicate use of hybrid embeddings, dropout, external lexicons, and noisy-labeled data to ensure matched resources for baselines.
- Request: Clarify which Table 3 configuration underlies Tables 1–2, provide exact dimensions and parameter counts, and report computational cost.
- Request: Detail dropout placement and provide an ablation study.
- Request: Evaluate parser choice by reporting parsing accuracy on tweets or using a Twitter-specific parser and test sensitivity to parse noise.
- Request: Expand evaluation to another aspect/target sentiment dataset (e.g., SemEval-2014 ABSA) to demonstrate generality.
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses lack of global context in compositional models, is a simple generally applicable architectural tweak with demonstrated gains, and offers useful engineering insights.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited conceptual novelty over prior inside–outside/bidirectional recursive models, misrepresented terminology, opaque/ensemble-heavy evaluation, and limited evidence of generality.
