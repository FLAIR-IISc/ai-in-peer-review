Summary
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive neural model that augments the standard bottom-up composition with a single top-down (“feedbackward”) inference step. The top-down pass produces additional node representations that are combined with the bottom-up vectors to make local (word/phrase) predictions. The motivation is contextual, aspect-specific sentiment: phrase- and sentence-level evidence should influence the labeling of words and targets, e.g., labeling “Android” as positive in “Android beats iOS.” The paper also proposes “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with smaller supervised, task-tuned dimensions. Experiments on SemEval-2013 Twitter sentiment show that a single GB-RNN outperforms an RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline; an ensemble attains state-of-the-art results for the competition setting. The model is related to bidirectional recursive networks (notably Irsoy & Cardie) but differs in how the top-down vectors are computed and combined.

Relation to prior work
- Recursive models over trees (Socher et al.; Pollack) provide the bottom-up backbone.
- Bidirectional recurrent/recursive models are the closest relatives; Irsoy & Cardie conduct a backward pass but use different parameterization and conditioning. Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside RvNNs and unfolding autoencoders are also related.
- The “hybrid” word vector idea combines unsupervised embeddings (e.g., word2vec/GloVe) with supervised tuning, echoing trends in fine-tuning but here implemented by concatenation and freezing the unsupervised part.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for adding a top-down pass in tree models for context-sensitive labeling.
- The hybrid word vector idea is simple, effective, and well-supported empirically.
- Strong empirical results on a widely used benchmark; inclusion of ablations (RNN, Bi-RNN, GB-RNN) is valuable.
- Writing is generally clear, and the model is easy to implement on top of standard RvNNs.
- Thoughtful use of regularization (dropout) and practical training choices; two-stage training is sensible.

Weaknesses and clarifications requested
Quality
- Section 3.5 (Training) is too terse. Please detail:
  - Exact backpropagation-through-structure for the two-pass network: what loss terms are applied (nodes/targets), where gradients originate, and how they flow through the backward and forward passes into word embeddings.
  - Unfolding and update depth: do errors from target leaves propagate to root and then back to words via the top-down path? Are both forward and backward parameters updated jointly from the same losses?
  - How dropout is applied: on forward node vectors, backward node vectors, word embeddings, or all? Is dropout shared across the two passes or independent?
  - Optimization specifics: learning-rate schedule under AdaGrad, early stopping, gradient clipping, epoch counts.
- Section 3.6 is confusing. The contrast to Irsoy & Cardie should be made explicit in prose with a small diagram and shape annotations:
  - What inputs condition the backward children (forward parent only vs. forward+backward parent)?
  - Parameter count and implications for capacity/regularization.
  - Intuitive effect: how GB-RNN’s use of both forward and backward parents disambiguates left/right children compared to Irsoy & Cardie.
- Terminology: “Global Belief”/“belief propagation” is misleading. The method is not performing belief propagation in a graphical-model sense. “Forward–Backward Recursive Neural Network” or “Top–Down Recursive Neural Network” would better reflect the mechanism.
- Experimental fairness:
  - Baselines: In 4.3 you state RNN and Bi-RNN use the same hybrid vectors and dropout. Good—make this explicit earlier and in the main experimental tables.
  - Model selection vs. ensembling: Report single-model results for all systems when comparing architectures, and clearly separate ensemble results. The competition comparison (4.2) uses an ensemble of five GB-RNNs—please clarify whether competing systems are single models or ensembles and include your single-model score for apples-to-apples comparison.
  - Hyperparameter tuning: specify the search spaces and selection criterion for each model.
- Dataset/task clarity (Section 4.1):
  - Explicitly state that you evaluate on SemEval-2013 Task 2, Subtask B (term-level/targeted sentiment), if that is the case.
  - Explain the parser constraint: how often could the labeled term not be a subtree? Were examples discarded, split, or heuristically adjusted? Given noisy Twitter parses, quantify any coverage loss.
- Scope: Results are limited to a single domain/dataset; it would strengthen claims to include a second task (e.g., aspect sentiment in reviews or entity-targeted sentiment in news) to demonstrate generality.

Clarity and organization
- The paper is generally well written, but:
  - Expand Section 3.5 as above.
  - Rewrite Section 3.6 with cleaner notation and an intuitive explanation before equations.
  - Section 4.1 should be more accessible to non-NLP readers (briefly define the subtask, labels, and how “phrases” are chosen).
  - Consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 to present a single, coherent ablation table covering hybrid vectors, dropout, additional lexicon data, and two-stage training.

Originality
- The two-pass tree computation is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive models; the core architectural novelty is incremental. The most distinctive contribution appears to be the hybrid word vectors plus a clean, single backward inference step tailored to target-level sentiment. Please sharpen the distinction from Irsoy & Cardie and related inside–outside models, and emphasize where your design choices matter empirically.

Significance
- Targeted/contextual sentiment is important, and the method is practical and likely to be adopted. The hybrid vectors observation is useful beyond this task. However, significance would be higher with broader evaluations and stronger, clearly controlled baselines.

Questions for authors
- Do gradients from leaf classifiers update the unsupervised part of the hybrid embeddings or only the supervised tail? Are backward vectors tied to or independent of the forward embeddings?
- How sensitive is performance to nd (backward dimension) and to using V vs. identity at the root?
- How robust is the method to parse errors, especially in Twitter?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated extension of recursive models to context-sensitive labeling.
- Strong empirical results and useful ablations; simple, effective hybrid word vectors.
- Easy to reproduce and likely to influence practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to existing bidirectional recursive models; terminology may overstate conceptual leap.
- Training and comparison details need to be more transparent; potential fairness concerns (ensembles vs. single models).
- Limited evaluation scope; reliance on parsing assumptions in noisy domain.

Overall
A solid and well-presented paper with practical impact. With clearer positioning versus prior bidirectional recursive work, more detailed training description, and tighter experimental reporting, it would make a strong NeurIPS contribution. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the above clarifications.
