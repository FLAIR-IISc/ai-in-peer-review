- Proposes Global Belief RNN (GB-RNN) that adds a feedbackward inference pass to let phrase-level predictions influence word representations for contextual sentiment.
- Builds on prior RNN and distributional-semantics work, but the “belief propagation” framing is tenuous and potentially misleading.
- Clear presentation of the GB-RNN architecture and method.
- Competitive empirical results on the SemEval 2013 benchmark.
- Includes useful ablation studies (e.g., hybrid word vectors) that give insight into model behavior.
- The term “belief propagation” is misleading—mechanism does not align with established belief propagation algorithms.
- The theoretical motivation for adding a single feedback pass is weakly justified; lacks rigorous explanation why it helps and limits of feedforward approaches.
- Main evaluation reports ensemble (top-5 averaged) results, obscuring single-model performance and hindering fair comparison.
- No justification for ensembling (e.g., high variance) and no single-model results provided.
- Baseline tuning/details are missing (hyperparameter ranges, optimization, model selection), making comparisons unclear.
- Unclear whether baselines used the same data augmentation (NRC lexicon, movie reviews) or hybrid word vectors, affecting fairness.
- The bidirectional RNN with hybrid vectors is likely the most comparable baseline and should be explicitly highlighted in comparisons.
- Using an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is questionable; parser accuracy on the Twitter dataset should be reported.
- Several citations are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie), reducing clarity of related-work grounding.
- Strengths summarized: clear method, promising results, and informative ablations.
- Weaknesses summarized: weak theory, misleading terminology, unjustified ensembles, and unclear baseline comparability.
- Reviewer recommendation: reject until ensemble reporting, baseline fairness, and stronger theoretical grounding are addressed.
