This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a variant of bidirectional recursive models for context-dependent natural language tasks. The core contribution is a two-pass architecture: a standard bottom-up recursive pass computes initial phrase representations, followed by a top-down "feedbackward" pass that propagates global context from the sentence root back to the leaves. This allows phrase-level information to refine word-level embeddings and labels, addressing tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis where local word sentiment is ambiguous without broader context. This work is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive models, notably Irsoy and Cardie (2012), and also proposes a novel hybrid word vector representation that combines fixed unsupervised embeddings with fine-tuned supervised ones.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, and the proposed architecture is an intuitive extension of recursive models. A significant strength is the empirical result, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task. The ablation study on hybrid word vectors also provides strong evidence for their effectiveness, which is a valuable contribution in itself.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its claims of originality and lack of technical clarity. The primary concern is the insufficient differentiation from the bidirectional recursive model of Irsoy and Cardie. Section 3.6 attempts this comparison but is confusing and fails to convincingly establish a fundamental architectural novelty. This makes it difficult to assess the originality of the core GB-RNN model. Furthermore, the chosen name "Belief Propagation" is misleading, as the mechanism is more accurately described as a forward-backward or two-pass inference scheme.

For reproducibility, the paper lacks crucial details. Section 3.5 on training is too brief and should elaborate on the backpropagation-through-structure implementation, especially how gradients from the second pass update word embeddings. The experimental setup could also be clarified; while the paper states baselines used hybrid vectors, the tuning process and the distinction between reporting single-best versus ensemble results should be made more explicit to ensure the comparisons are perceived as fair.

In summary, while the paper presents a model with strong empirical performance, its central claims of novelty are not sufficiently supported due to an unclear comparison with prior art. The lack of key implementation details also hinders reproducibility. Significant revision is needed to address these points.
