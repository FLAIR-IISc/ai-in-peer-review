This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel extension of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) that incorporates a top-down (backward) inference step to capture contextual information in natural language processing tasks. The GB-RNN architecture allows phrase-level predictions and representations to influence word embeddings and labels, enabling the model to correctly predict sentiment labels that are determined by context.

The paper is well-written and well-motivated, presenting an interesting variant of bidirectional recursive models. The key contribution of the paper is the introduction of hybrid word vectors, which combines unsupervised and supervised word vector representations to improve performance. The experimental results show that the GB-RNN outperforms several baselines, including the standard RNN and bidirectional RNN, on the task of contextual sentiment analysis.

However, there are some areas that need improvement. The relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is incomplete, and the distinction from that bidirectional recursive model needs clearer clarification. The term "belief propagation" is misleading, and "forward-backward" might be a more appropriate name. The experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear, as it is not specified whether the baselines used hybrid vectors and how the models were tuned or selected. Section 3.5 (Training) is too short and needs more details on the training procedure, such as backpropagation-through-structure, network unfolding, and how deep updates reach word embeddings.

Additionally, Section 3.6 is confusing and should explicitly clarify the differences from Irsoy & Cardie. Section 4.1 requires better explanation for non-NLP readers, clarifying which SemEval 2013 subtask is addressed and parser constraints. It is also suggested to merge Sections 4.3 and 4.4 for clarity.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to natural language processing tasks, but some areas need improvement to make the paper more complete and clear.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to natural language processing tasks.
* The GB-RNN architecture is well-motivated and well-explained.
* The experimental results show that the GB-RNN outperforms several baselines.

* The relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is incomplete and needs clearer clarification.
* The term "belief propagation" is misleading.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the areas mentioned above.
