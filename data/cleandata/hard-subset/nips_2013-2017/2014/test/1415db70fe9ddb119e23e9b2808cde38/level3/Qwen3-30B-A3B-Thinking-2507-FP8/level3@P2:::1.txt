

The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a top-down feedbackward pass to enable context-dependent phrase labeling. This addresses a key limitation in existing recursive models (e.g., Socher et al., NIPS 2011; NIPS 2013) that cannot disambiguate sentiment based on global context (e.g., classifying "Android" as positive in "Android beats iOS" without isolated phrase labels). GB-RNNs compute forward (bottom-up) and backward (top-down) node vectors, concatenating them for final predictions. The work also proposes hybrid word vectors (unsupervised + task-specific supervised components) and demonstrates 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs on SemEval 2013 sentiment task, achieving SOTA performance.

**Strengths**  
The paper is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in contextual sentiment analysis, where prior recursive models fail to leverage global sentence context. The problem is clearly articulated with strong examples (e.g., Fig. 1), and the proposed GB-RNN structure is logically derived from the need for context propagation. The hybrid word vector approach is a significant technical contribution—empirically outperforming both purely unsupervised and supervised vectors (Table 3)—and the ablation studies (Table 2) convincingly show the value of the feedbackward step over standard RNNs. The clarity of the model description (Section 3) and thorough experimental validation (Section 4) align well with NIPS standards for empirical rigor.

**Weaknesses**  
*Originality & Relation to Prior Work*: The core contribution (feedbackward propagation) overlaps substantially with Irsoy and Cardie’s bidirectional RNN (NIPS 2013 Deep Learning Workshop), yet Section 3.6 fails to clarify key distinctions. The paper claims their model uses "both forward and backward parent nodes" (Eq. 5) while Irsoy & Cardie’s uses "forward parent only" (Eq. 4), but this is not explicitly analyzed. Crucially, the paper mislabels its approach as "belief propagation" (Section 3.3), a term reserved for probabilistic graphical models (e.g., Pearl, 1988), which is misleading and detracts from the technical contribution. A clearer "forward-backward" framing would better align with the method. The relation to Irsoy & Cardie needs rigorous comparison (e.g., parameter counts, empirical differences in Table 2), not just a footnote.

*Quality & Clarity*: Section 3.5 (Training) is insufficiently detailed. The paper states dropout improved performance but omits critical implementation specifics (e.g., *when* dropout is applied—only to word vectors? hidden layers?—and how backpropagation through structure handles the feedbackward pass). Section 4.1’s parser constraints (e.g., "constrained to keep labeled phrases in subtrees") require elaboration for non-NLP readers. The experimental setup is ambiguous: it is unclear whether baselines (RNN, B-RNN) used the same hybrid vectors as GB-RNN (Table 2), risking an unfair comparison. The ensemble approach (Section 4.2) should clarify if baselines were also ensembled.

*Significance*: While the model advances contextual sentiment analysis, its applicability is limited to tree-structured tasks (e.g., sentiment, not general NLP). The claim that "this idea is more general than RNNs" (Section 5) lacks evidence beyond sentiment. The hybrid vectors are a strong standalone contribution, but the paper does not sufficiently contextualize how they integrate with the feedbackward mechanism.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Strong empirical results, clear problem motivation, novel hybrid word vectors, and a well-structured model that solves a real NLP limitation.  
*Con*: Misleading terminology, insufficient distinction from prior work (Irsoy & Cardie), ambiguous experimental details, and underdeveloped significance beyond sentiment.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution to contextual NLP, but its originality is undermined by inadequate differentiation from Irsoy & Cardie (2013). The term "belief propagation" should be corrected to "forward-backward" to avoid confusion. The authors must: (1) revise Section 3.6 to explicitly contrast model architectures (including parameter counts and computational differences); (2) clarify experimental details (hybrid vectors in baselines, dropout application, parser constraints); and (3) temper the significance claims to focus on sentiment tasks. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong fit for NIPS. As-is, the novelty gap prevents a top-tier acceptance, but the technical soundness and empirical results warrant a borderline acceptance. **Weak Accept**.
