This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel variant of recursive neural networks (RNNs) that incorporates a top-down inference step to capture contextual information. The GB-RNN is designed to tackle the limitations of standard RNNs in handling tasks such as aspect-specific sentiment classification, where the sentiment of a word or phrase depends on the context.

The authors provide a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem and the proposed solution. The GB-RNN architecture is described in detail, including the use of hybrid word vectors that combine unsupervised and supervised representations. The paper also presents an experimental evaluation of the GB-RNN on the SemEval 2013 dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in contextual sentiment analysis.

The key contribution of the paper appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors, which may be the main difference from prior work. However, the relation to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is incomplete, and the distinction from that bidirectional recursive model needs clearer clarification. The term "belief propagation" is also misleading, and "forward-backward" might be a more appropriate name.

The experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear, as it is not specified whether the baselines (RNN, B-RNN) used hybrid vectors, and how the models were tuned/selected (single best vs ensemble). Additionally, Section 3.5 (Training) is too short and needs details on the training procedure, such as backpropagation-through-structure, network unfolding, and how deep updates reach word embeddings.

The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the differences between the GB-RNN and Irsoy and Cardie's bidirectional recursive model in Section 3.6. Furthermore, Section 4.1 requires better explanation for non-NLP readers, clarifying which SemEval 2013 subtask is addressed and parser constraints.

Considering the quality of the paper, it is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is well-written and well-organized, and it adequately informs the reader. However, there are some areas that need improvement, such as providing more details on the training procedure and clarifying the differences with prior work.

Originality:
The paper presents a novel variant of recursive neural networks that incorporates a top-down inference step to capture contextual information. The use of hybrid word vectors is also a new contribution. However, the idea of bidirectional recursive models is not new, and the paper needs to provide a clearer explanation of how it differs from prior work.

Significance:
The paper addresses an important problem in natural language processing, namely, contextual sentiment analysis. The proposed GB-RNN architecture has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in this task and can be applied to other NLP tasks that require contextual understanding.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
* The GB-RNN architecture is well-described, and the experimental evaluation is thorough.
* The use of hybrid word vectors is a new contribution that can be applied to other NLP tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The relation to prior work, particularly Irsoy and Cardie (2008), is incomplete and needs clearer clarification.
* The experimental comparisons may be unfair or unclear, and more details are needed on the training procedure.
* The paper requires better explanation for non-NLP readers, particularly in Section 4.1.

Recommendation:
The paper is a good contribution to the field of natural language processing, and with some revisions, it can be even stronger. I recommend accepting the paper, but the authors should address the issues mentioned above, particularly providing more details on the training procedure and clarifying the differences with prior work.
