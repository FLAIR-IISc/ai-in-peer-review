Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard tree-structured recursive neural networks (RNNs) by adding a single top-down pass at inference: a “feedbackward” (backward) computation that starts from the root and propagates information to internal nodes and leaves. Final node/word predictions use the concatenation of bottom-up (forward) and top-down (backward) representations. The model is evaluated on contextual (aspect/target-specific) sentiment analysis on the SemEval-2013 Twitter dataset, reporting about 3% F1 improvement over a standard RNN and state-of-the-art performance on the competition benchmark. The paper also reports two practical findings: (i) dropout improves training of recursive models with high-dimensional embeddings; (ii) a hybrid word representation that concatenates a fixed unsupervised vector with a small trainable supervised vector outperforms either alone. The training uses backpropagation through structure (BPTS) and mini-batched AdaGrad; additional external resources (NRC sentiment lexicon, noisy tweets, and sentiment treebank) are incorporated in a two-stage training regimen.

The work is grounded in prior compositional and recursive models (e.g., Socher et al.’s RNNs/RNTNs; inside-outside recursive models), bidirectional recurrent/recursive models (Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie), and unsupervised word vectors (e.g., GloVe/word2vec). Conceptually, the proposed top-down pass is closely related to bidirectional-recursive approaches. The paper argues its top-down equations use both forward and backward parent information and a learned transform V, differing from earlier bidirectional recursive formulations.

Quality
- Soundness: The model is well-motivated and the equations are plausible. However, several implementation details are under-specified. Section 3.5 is too brief to guarantee reproducibility: more specifics on BPTS through both passes, gradient flow into hybrid embeddings (fixed vs trainable parts), dropout placement (which layers/units), initialization, and optimization settings are needed.
- Empirical validation: The core claims are supported by SemEval results and ablations indicating the value of the backward pass, dropout, and hybrid embeddings. To fully assess fairness, please make explicit that all strong baselines (RNN, bidirectional-RNN) used the same hybrid embeddings, dropout, parser, and tuning budget. In the text you state this for §4.3; highlight it prominently and report per-model hyperparameter ranges and selection criteria. Report single-model vs ensemble performance separately on both dev and test, and provide variance over multiple random seeds. Since additional external resources are used, include comparisons with and without them to contextualize gains over SemEval systems.
- Comparisons: The discussion in §3.6 contrasting GB-RNN with the bidirectional recursive model of Irsoy & Cardie is confusing and incomplete. A controlled empirical comparison under equalized parameter budgets would strengthen the claim (e.g., your equations vs theirs, with and without V; same nd and n; report runtime and memory).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well motivated, but:
  - The term “global belief” (and “feedbackward”) risks confusion with probabilistic belief propagation. Consider adopting “forward-backward recursive neural network” or clearly stating that no graphical-model-style beliefs or BP are involved.
  - §3.5 (Training) needs substantially more detail: precise BPTS description for the two-pass tree, where loss is applied (which nodes), how unlabeled nodes are handled, whether forward and backward parameters are updated jointly or staged, schedule for two-stage pretraining, AdaGrad settings, early stopping, and depth/length handling.
  - §3.6 should present a clearer, self-contained mathematical contrast to Irsoy & Cardie with consistent notation, parameter counts, and a short complexity discussion (asymptotic and empirical).
  - §4.1 should clearly state which SemEval 2013 Task 2 subtask is used (A: message-level, B: term-level), how “phrases marked for classification” are provided, and the exact tweet/SMS composition of splits. For non-NLP readers, briefly explain the parser constraint to ensure each labeled span is a subtree and discuss the potential impact of parsing errors on noisy Twitter text.
  - Consider merging §§4.3–4.4 for a cleaner narrative of ablations and added resources.

Originality
- The forward-backward recursion on trees is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; inside-outside RNNs). The specific parameterization (using both forward and backward parents and a V transform) is a reasonable variant but seems incremental. The hybrid embedding idea (concatenating frozen unsupervised vectors with a small trainable tail) is practical but not fundamentally novel relative to common fine-tuning or multi-channel embeddings.
- The main novelty is thus an engineeringly clean instantiation and empirical validation on contextual sentiment.

Significance
- Contextual (target-specific) sentiment is important, and showing consistent gains over a strong RNN baseline is useful. The approach could generalize to other contextual labeling tasks (e.g., word sense, coreference cues). That said, reliance on accurate constituency parses for tweets may limit robustness; an analysis of performance vs parsing quality would help. The ensembling and external resources complicate SOTA claims; single-model, no-external-data results should be highlighted.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Clarify target conditioning: How is the target entity specified at test time, and how does the model disambiguate multiple entities in the same sentence? Is the classifier applied to pre-marked target spans only?
- Report per-class F1 (pos/neg/neutral) and SMS vs tweet breakdown; include statistical significance tests over multiple runs.
- Provide an ablation equalizing parameter counts between your top-down parameterization and Irsoy & Cardie’s; test variants: without V, with/without using backward parent in Eq. 3, varying nd.
- Detail training: number of epochs, batch size, learning-rate schedule, weight decay, dropout locations/probabilities for embeddings and hidden states, activation choice (sigmoid vs ReLU) and why sigmoid worked better.
- Reproducibility: specify the unsupervised vector source (e.g., GloVe corpus, dimensionality), code release, and exact data preprocessing (tokenization, handling of negations/mentions/hashtags, OOV strategy).
- Analyze runtime/memory overhead of the top-down pass.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear motivation for context-dependent labeling; simple, effective extension to standard RNNs.
- Solid empirical gains on a widely used benchmark; thorough ablations showing the value of the backward pass and hybrid embeddings.
- Approach is broadly applicable beyond sentiment.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty over prior bidirectional recursive models is incremental and not crisply argued.
- Important training and comparison details are underspecified; fairness and statistical robustness need to be demonstrated more clearly.
- Terminology (“belief”) may mislead; dataset/task description needs to be clearer for a general audience.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated paper with practical impact and encouraging results, but clarity and positioning relative to prior bidirectional recursive work should be improved, and experimental reporting strengthened. I lean toward acceptance if the authors address the comparison and clarity issues outlined above.
