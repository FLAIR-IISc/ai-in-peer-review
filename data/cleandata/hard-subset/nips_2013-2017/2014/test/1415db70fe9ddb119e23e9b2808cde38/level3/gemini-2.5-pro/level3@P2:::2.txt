### Summary

This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), an extension of standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) designed to handle context-dependent classification tasks in natural language processing. The key idea is to augment the standard bottom-up (feedforward) pass, which composes word meanings into phrase and sentence representations, with a top-down (feedbackward) pass. This second pass propagates information from the global sentence context (captured at the root of the parse tree) back down to every node, allowing the model to refine the representations and predictions for individual words and phrases. This is particularly motivated by tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis, where the sentiment of a word (e.g., "Android") can depend on the surrounding words (e.g., in "Android beats iOS"). The paper also explores the benefits of using dropout in RNNs and proposes a hybrid word vector representation that combines pre-trained unsupervised vectors with task-specific supervised vectors.

The proposed model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming previous competition winners and several strong baselines, including the standard feedforward RNN. The work is closely related to contemporary work on bidirectional recursive neural networks [12], and the authors provide a clear theoretical and empirical comparison, showing their proposed downward computation is different and performs better on their task.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Elegant Model:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of purely feedforward compositional models: their inability to capture global context for local predictions. The proposed GB-RNN, with its feedbackward pass, is an intuitive, simple, and elegant solution to this problem.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The model is described clearly with equations and a helpful diagram. The authors also do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to prior art, particularly the very similar bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie [12], providing a detailed comparison of the different formulations.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrates significant performance gains. The 3% F1 improvement over a standard RNN baseline clearly validates the benefit of the feedbackward pass. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a competitive shared task (SemEval 2013) is a strong testament to the model's effectiveness.
4.  **Useful Secondary Contributions:** The paper provides evidence that dropout can improve RNN training and that a hybrid approach to word vectors (concatenating fixed unsupervised vectors with trainable supervised ones) is beneficial. These are valuable practical findings for the community.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the potential ambiguity and lack of clarity in the experimental setup, which raises concerns about the fairness of the comparisons.

1.  **Unclear Baseline Configuration:** While the paper states that the RNN, bidirectional RNN, and GB-RNN baselines in Table 2 were trained with the same hybrid word vectors and dropout, it is not clear if they also benefited from the additional training data and two-stage training procedure described in Section 4.4. This procedure (pre-training on a large corpus of noisy/general sentiment data before fine-tuning on the task-specific data) seems to have been applied to the final GB-RNN model and provided a 1% accuracy boost. If this procedure was not applied to the baselines, particularly the bidirectional RNN [12], then the comparison is not entirely fair, and the performance gains may be attributable to the training scheme rather than just the model architecture. The authors should clarify whether all models reported in Table 2 were trained under the exact same conditions, including the use of external data.
2.  **Presentation of Ensemble Results:** In Table 1, the best-performing GB-RNN result is from an ensemble of 5 models. While using ensembles is a common practice, presenting an ensemble result alongside single-model results from other systems can be misleading. It would be better to report both the best single GB-RNN model and the ensemble in that table for a more transparent comparison.

### Detailed Comments

- The theoretical comparison with the bidirectional RNN [12] in Section 3.6 is excellent. It clearly dissects the differences in the equations for the backward pass, justifying the authors' design choices and highlighting the parameter differences. This is a model for how to compare closely related concurrent work.
- The ablation study in Table 3 convincingly shows the superiority of the proposed hybrid word vectors over purely supervised or purely unsupervised alternatives.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro:**
*   The paper introduces a novel, well-motivated, and effective extension to recursive neural networks.
*   The model achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard, challenging task.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.
*   It addresses a fundamental problem of context-dependency in language understanding that is of broad interest to the NIPS community.

**Con:**
*   The experimental comparison to key baselines may not be entirely fair due to ambiguity in the training procedure, particularly concerning the use of additional data described in Section 4.4. This weakens the central claim of the paper's superiority over related models like [12].

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a strong core contribution. The idea is elegant, the execution seems solid, and the results are impressive. The main drawback is the lack of clarity regarding the experimental conditions for the baselines, which makes it difficult to definitively attribute the performance gains to the proposed architectural innovations alone. However, given the strength of the idea and the overall quality of the paper, I lean towards acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to clarify the experimental details in their rebuttal.
