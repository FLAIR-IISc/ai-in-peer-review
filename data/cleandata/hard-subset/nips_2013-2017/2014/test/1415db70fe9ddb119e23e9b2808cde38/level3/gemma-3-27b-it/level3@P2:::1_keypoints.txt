- Introduces GB-RNN: extends recursive neural networks with a top-down feedbackward pass to let phrase-level predictions influence word-level representations and labels.  
- Proposes hybrid word vector initialization combining unsupervised and supervised representations, which is key to performance.  
- Well-written, well-motivated contribution addressing contextual/aspect-specific sentiment analysis.  
- Strong relation to prior work in distributional vectors, recursive models, and bidirectional architectures.  
- Comparison to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN is acknowledged but incomplete; backward-pass computation and impact need clarification.  
- The term “belief propagation” is potentially misleading; “forward-backward” may be more accurate.  
- Theoretical justification for a feedbackward pass is convincing and technically sound.  
- Empirical results are impressive, achieving state-of-the-art on SemEval 2013.  
- Hybrid word vectors are a noteworthy and effective contribution.  
- Experimental fairness unclear: uncertain whether baselines used the same hybrid vectors.  
- Insufficient detail on model selection, hyperparameter search, and whether ensembles were used.  
- Section 3.5 (training) is too brief; backpropagation through structure, unfolding, and embedding updates need fuller explanation.  
- Feedbackward computation (Eq. 3) lacks an intuitive explanation.  
- Section 3.6’s mathematical comparison to Irsoy & Cardie is confusing; needs clearer intuitive description of information flow differences.  
- Parameter-count comparison does not fully explain performance differences.  
- Section 4.1 assumes SemEval familiarity; should explain the specific subtask and Stanford Parser constraints.  
- Parser constraints may introduce evaluation bias and should be discussed.  
- Suggest merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 to improve flow and integrate analysis of GB-RNN components.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong theory, SOTA results, effective hybrid vectors, clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear experimental/hyperparameter reporting, insufficient training detail, incomplete comparison to related work, potential parser bias, and possibly inaccurate terminology.  
- Overall: strong paper recommended for acceptance conditional on addressing clarity, reproducibility, and comparison concerns (hyperparameters, training details, and clearer distinction from bidirectional RNN).
