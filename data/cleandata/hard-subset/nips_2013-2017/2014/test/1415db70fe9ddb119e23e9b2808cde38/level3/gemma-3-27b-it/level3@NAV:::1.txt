## Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), an extension of standard recursive neural networks aiming to incorporate top-down (backward) inference to improve contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea—allowing phrase-level predictions to influence word embeddings and labels—is a logical step in refining bidirectional recursive models and addresses the limitations of purely feedforward architectures in tasks requiring contextual understanding. The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, and the problem it tackles is relevant to current NLP research.

A key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors, combining unsupervised and supervised representations, which the authors demonstrate improves performance. This is a pragmatic and potentially valuable addition to the toolkit for compositional semantics. The experimental setup focuses on SemEval 2013’s contextual sentiment analysis task, providing a concrete evaluation ground.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The connection to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) is inadequately addressed; the authors need to clearly delineate the differences in the backward propagation mechanism, beyond simply stating a difference in the computation of the backward vectors. The term “belief propagation” feels misleading, as it doesn't fully represent the top-down inference step and "forward-backward" may be more apt. 

Furthermore, the experimental comparisons raise concerns. It’s unclear whether the baseline models (RNN, B-RNN) utilized the same hybrid vector approach, making it difficult to isolate the impact of the GB-RNN’s backward pass. The authors mention ensembles but provide limited detail on model selection and tuning procedures. Section 3.5, detailing training, is strikingly brief. It lacks crucial information on backpropagation-through-structure, network unfolding, and, importantly, how gradient updates propagate to the initial word embeddings. Section 3.6 feels convoluted and requires a clearer explanation of how the proposed method differs from Irsoy and Cardie’s approach. Finally, Section 4.1 could benefit from more accessible language for non-NLP specialists, clarifying the specifics of the SemEval 2013 subtask and parser constraints. Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could be merged for a more streamlined presentation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of recursive neural networks with a logically sound motivation.
*   Potential improvement in contextual sentiment analysis.
*   Hybrid word vector approach is a worthwhile contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incomplete comparison to related work (Irsoy & Cardie).
*   Unclear experimental setup and potential unfair comparisons.
*   Lack of detail in training procedure.
*   Sections 3.6 & 4.1 need significant clarification.



**Overall:** The paper presents an interesting idea with potential. However, the lack of clarity in several areas and the issues with experimental details prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the concerns raised in the review.
