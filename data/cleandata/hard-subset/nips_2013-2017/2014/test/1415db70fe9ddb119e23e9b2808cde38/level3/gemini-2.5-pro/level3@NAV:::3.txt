This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed for contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea is to augment a standard bottom-up, feedforward Recursive Neural Network (RNN) with a top-down "feedbackward" pass. This second pass propagates information from the sentence-level representation (the root of the parse tree) back down to the word and phrase level, allowing local predictions to be influenced by global context. This approach builds upon prior work in recursive deep models for semantics but aims to address their limitation in capturing context-dependent meaning, distinguishing it from related bidirectional recursive models.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear writing and strong motivation for tackling an important problem in sentiment analysis. The empirical results presented on the SemEval 2013 dataset appear to be state-of-the-art.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. First, the motivation for the model is vague and lacks clear theoretical justification. The chosen terminology of "belief propagation" is misleading, as the mechanism is a deterministic two-pass encoding and decoding of phrase vectors, not a probabilistic inference method as the name suggests.

The most critical weakness is the evaluation methodology. The main results in Table 1 are for an ensemble of five models, which is an unjustified comparison against non-ensemble benchmarks and obscures the true performance of a single GB-RNN. The paper also lacks crucial details regarding the experimental setup. It is unclear how baseline models were tuned, or whether they used the same augmented data and hybrid word vectors as the proposed model. For instance, did the standard RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines also use the novel hybrid vectors? This lack of clarity makes it impossible to isolate and assess the architectural contribution of the feedbackward pass. The second entry in Table 3 is likely the most comparable baseline, and the performance gains here, while positive, are less pronounced.

Minor issues include the use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets without reporting its accuracy on this noisy domain, and several incomplete citations.

Overall, while the results are reasonable, the model's contribution is undermined by a weak theoretical motivation and a substantially flawed experimental methodology. The lack of clarity and unfair comparisons make it difficult to validate the paper's claims.
