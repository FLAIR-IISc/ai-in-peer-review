

This paper introduces "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks" (GB-RNNs) for contextual sentiment analysis, claiming to extend standard RNNs with a "feedbackward" step to incorporate global sentence context. The core idea modifies recursive networks by adding a top-down pass to update phrase representations using root-level context, aiming to solve aspect-specific sentiment tasks (e.g., identifying "Android" as positive in "Android beats iOS"). The authors report a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs on SemEval 2013 and claim state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:** The technical description of the GB-RNN architecture is clear, and the hybrid word vector approach (combining unsupervised and supervised embeddings) is reasonably motivated. The paper builds logically on prior work (e.g., Socher et al.’s recursive RNNs, Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNNs) and demonstrates empirical gains on a standard benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Misleading Terminology:** The term "belief propagation" is inaccurate (it describes a message-passing algorithm in graphical models, not the proposed top-down recursion). This misalignment with established terminology undermines technical precision.  
2. **Evaluation Flaws:** The ensemble results (Table 1) obscure comparisons, as the paper does not clarify how models were tuned or why an ensemble was used. Crucially, the most comparable baseline (Table 3’s second entry) is not directly contrasted with the ensemble results in Tables 1–2, violating reproducibility standards.  
3. **Ambiguous Methodology:** It remains unclear whether baselines (RNN, BiRNN) used the same hybrid word vectors, augmented data, or tuning procedures as GB-RNN. The lack of transparency in model selection (e.g., "top 5 models") prevents fair assessment.  
4. **Weak Motivation:** The argument for context-dependence is valid, but the paper fails to justify *why* the specific feedbackward mechanism is necessary over existing bidirectional approaches (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie), which the authors acknowledge but do not convincingly outperform.  
5. **Minor Issues:** The off-the-shelf parser’s accuracy on tweets is unreported, and several citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) are incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:** While the results are reasonable and the architecture is novel in its specific implementation, the paper’s major weaknesses in evaluation methodology and unclear comparisons significantly weaken its contribution. The work does not advance the state-of-the-art demonstrably beyond existing bidirectional models, and the experimental design lacks rigor required for NIPS. The motivation remains vague, and the terminology error further erodes credibility. The paper would benefit from a revised evaluation section with direct comparisons to the most relevant baselines and full methodological transparency. **Recommendation: Reject.**
