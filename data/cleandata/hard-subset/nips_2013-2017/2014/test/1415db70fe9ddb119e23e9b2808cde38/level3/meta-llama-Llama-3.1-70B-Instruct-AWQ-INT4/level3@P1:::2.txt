This paper introduces a novel extension to recursive neural networks (RNNs), called global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs), which incorporates a downward pass to propagate larger-context information to all tree nodes. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, with a simple yet elegant extension to the standard RNN architecture. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the GB-RNN model, including its components and training procedure. The experimental results are impressive, with the GB-RNN outperforming several baselines and model ablations.

However, there are some concerns with the experimental setup. Specifically, it is unclear whether the bidirectional-RNN baseline in Table 2 was trained with dropout, additional training data, or hybrid vectors. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to compare the results fairly. Additionally, the ensemble result should not be presented alongside individual-model comparisons in the same table, as it may give an unfair advantage to the GB-RNN.

The paper also mentions that the GB-RNN uses a "widely used mix of both" in Section 4.4, but it is unclear what this refers to. This ambiguity should be clarified to ensure that the reader understands the experimental setup.

Despite these concerns, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, and the GB-RNN model has the potential to be applied to a variety of NLP tasks. The authors' use of a hybrid word vector representation, which combines unsupervised and supervised vectors, is also a notable contribution.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough discussion of previous approaches to sentiment analysis and recursive neural networks. However, they could have provided more context on how their work differs from previous approaches, such as the bidirectional-RNN model proposed by Irsoy and Cardie.

Overall, the paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field. With some minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, it has the potential to be a strong publication.
