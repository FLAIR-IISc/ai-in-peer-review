

# Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

This paper proposes "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks" (GB-RNN), claiming to improve contextual sentiment analysis by adding a "feedbackward" step to standard recursive neural networks. The authors argue that standard RNNs fail to capture context-dependent sentiment (e.g., in "The Android's screen is better than the iPhone's"), and that their GB-RNN can propagate global context from the sentence root back to the leaves to resolve such ambiguities.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly motivated. The problem of context-dependent sentiment analysis is indeed important, and the authors provide a reasonable explanation for why existing approaches fall short. The paper also includes a thoughtful discussion of hybrid word vector representations, which could be useful for other NLP tasks.

**Weaknesses:** Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from significant methodological shortcomings that undermine its claims of novelty and effectiveness.

First, the core innovation is neither novel nor well-justified. The authors cite Irsoy & Cardie (2014) as introducing a "bidirectional RNN" similar to their approach, but their theoretical comparison (Section 3.6) is misleading. They claim their model differs from the bidirectional RNN by using "both the forward and backward parent node" in their backward computation, but the mathematical comparison reveals their model is simply a more parameterized version of the same concept. In fact, their model has n·nd more parameters than the bidirectional RNN without demonstrating proportional gains. The paper fails to explain why this additional parameterization is necessary or why it leads to better performance.

Second, the experimental validation is insufficient. The authors claim to outperform "the winner of the Sentiment competition of SemEval 2013," but they don't provide the actual results of that competition or how their model compares to the winning system's performance on the same test set. The comparison in Table 1 is superficial and doesn't address whether the winning system used similar techniques or additional resources. The paper mentions using "additional training data" (including "noisily labeled tweets" and movie reviews), which could substantially impact results but isn't properly justified or analyzed.

Third, the paper lacks proper ablation studies. The authors claim their model outperforms standard RNNs by over 5%, but they don't isolate which components (the feedbackward step, the hybrid word vectors, dropout) contribute to this improvement. Without such ablations, it's impossible to determine what truly drives the performance gain. The paper's claim that "the feedbackward step" is the key innovation is unsupported by the evidence provided.

Fourth, the paper's theoretical justification is weak. The "global belief" terminology is confusing and not well-motivated. The paper doesn't provide any formal analysis of why their approach would be expected to outperform existing methods, nor does it address potential failure cases where the feedbackward step might introduce noise or overfitting.

The paper's approach also appears to be a minor variation on techniques that have been well-established in the field. Bidirectional RNNs (Schuster & Paliwal, 1997) and bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014) are standard approaches for incorporating context. The paper's "feedbackward" step is essentially a bidirectional recursive network with additional parameters, which is a common technique rather than a novel contribution.

Furthermore, the paper's experimental setup is questionable. The SemEval 2013 dataset is now quite dated (2013), and the paper doesn't address whether the improvements would generalize to more recent datasets or larger, more complex datasets. The authors mention using "176,311 noisily labeled tweets" as additional training data, but they don't explain how this noise might affect the model's performance or whether they've properly accounted for it in their evaluation.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the problem addressed is important, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence for its claims of novelty and effectiveness. The proposed method appears to be a minor variation of existing bidirectional techniques without rigorous validation or clear theoretical justification. The paper's claims are overstated relative to the evidence provided, and it doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from the well-established bidirectional RNN approach.

The paper would benefit from:
1) A more careful comparison to existing bidirectional approaches with proper ablation studies
2) A clearer theoretical justification for why their parameterization is necessary
3) A more rigorous experimental setup that avoids using additional training data without justification
4) A more modest presentation of the novelty claim

Without these improvements, the paper doesn't make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The work appears to be incremental rather than innovative, and the evidence provided doesn't support the claims of significant performance gains.
