Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass. The goal is to contextualize node (word/phrase) representations using information from the entire sentence, enabling context-dependent classification (e.g., target/phrase-level sentiment in tweets). The forward pass computes standard inside representations; the backward pass starts at the root and recursively produces top-down vectors for each node based on the parent’s forward and backward vectors. Final classification at each node uses the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The model is paired with “hybrid” word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with a small supervised, task-trained vector; dropout is applied to node vectors. Experiments on SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment show improvements over a plain RNN, a bidirectional-recursive baseline (Irsoy & Cardie), and competition systems; additional external data (NRC lexicon-derived phrases, emoticon tweets, movie reviews) and model ensembling further improve results.

This work is closely related to earlier recursive models for compositional semantics (Socher et al.) and to inside-outside/bidirectional recursive architectures (e.g., Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside RNN, Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN). The core idea—computing top-down “outside” representations to contextualize local decisions—has precedent; the paper differentiates the specific parameterization and concatenation scheme and evaluates on a contextual sentiment task.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for contextual (target/phrase-specific) sentiment and limitations of purely bottom-up composition.
- Simple architectural extension to a well-known model; efficient to implement given a parser and tree structure.
- Empirical gains over a strong plain RNN baseline and a bidirectional-recursive variant on a popular benchmark; sensible ablations (plain vs GB-RNN; hybrid vs unsupervised vs supervised embeddings; effect of extra data).
- Practical hybrid-embedding design that balances generalization and task specificity; use of dropout for structural nets.
- Discussion of resource usage and a two-stage training strategy when large weakly supervised data are available.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Technical correctness: Several shape/notation inconsistencies and typos make it hard to fully verify the model. Examples: W↓ dimensions appear inconsistent with equations mapping [parent_fwd; parent_bwd] (size n+nd) to [left_bwd; right_bwd] (size 2·nd); the classifier weight Wc is said to be RC×(n+1) despite concatenating n+nd features; “V ∈ Vnd×n” is malformed. Please fix all shapes and provide a precise specification (including whether W↓ is shared across all nodes and whether a single matrix outputs both children).
- The claim that window/bi-recurrent methods cannot incorporate distant context is overstated; BiRNNs do condition on the entire sequence (though not its hierarchical structure).
- Empirical evaluation: The strongest numbers come from an ensemble and from substantial extra resources (lexicon-derived labeled phrases, 176k emoticon tweets, movie reviews). While this is allowed, the paper should cleanly separate results into: (a) in-domain supervised only; (b) +weak supervision; (c) +ensemble, and ensure all baselines are allowed the same resources to isolate architectural gains. Statistical significance testing is missing.
- Baseline coverage: The most relevant prior for top-down context on trees is inside-outside recursive models (e.g., Le & Zuidema). An empirical comparison (or at least a careful analytical one) would materially strengthen the case for novelty and effectiveness of the proposed parameterization.
- Reproducibility: Important details are underspecified (pretrained embedding source/training, exact sizes n and nd per best model, optimization schedule, early stopping, runtime/parameters, parser configuration for noisy tweets).

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable with helpful intuition, but suffers from numerous typographical errors (“feedbackward/ feedwordard/ shold”), malformed equations, and formatting glitches (random “Correct …” lines in Section 3.6). These negatively affect confidence and reproducibility.
- Please present a compact algorithmic description: tensor shapes, parameter counts, forward/backward recursions with explicit block matrices, and a diagram clarifying information flow.
- Clearly define the SemEval subtask (term-level vs message-level), label granularity, and how phrase-level supervision is injected into the tree (constraining subtrees).

Originality
- The idea of combining bottom-up and top-down tree passes is not new; the contribution lies in a specific formulation that conditions the children’s backward vectors on both the forward and backward parent vectors and then concatenates forward/backward vectors for classification. This is a modest, incremental novelty over inside-outside/bidirectional recursive models.
- The hybrid embedding trick (fixed unsupervised + small supervised) is pragmatic but also not conceptually new; its effectiveness here is a useful empirical observation.

Significance
- Within the scope of tree-structured sentiment for noisy social text, the reported improvements and the contextual-disambiguation capability are meaningful and likely of interest. The model is simple enough to be adopted and extended.
- Generality beyond sentiment is plausible (e.g., word sense disambiguation, tagging on trees), but the paper does not validate this; one additional task would strengthen impact.
- Given the incremental novelty, the significance hinges on solid, clean experiments and clarity; current presentation issues and lack of comparisons to the closest inside-outside baselines dampen impact.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide unambiguous equations with correct dimensions and a reference implementation or pseudocode.
- Which SemEval subtask(s) and exact scoring protocol were used? Are the phrase labels truly context-dependent gold annotations?
- How does performance change without external data and without ensembling? Are gains over the bidirectional-recursive baseline still significant?
- How sensitive are results to nd (backward vector size) and to concatenation vs other fusions (e.g., gating)?

Pros
- Well-motivated problem; intuitive extension to capture global context on trees.
- Empirical gains on a standard benchmark; useful ablations.
- Practical hybrid embeddings and dropout usage.

Cons
- Clarity/notation issues; some incorrect or inconsistent equations.
- Limited novelty relative to inside-outside/bidirectional recursive models; missing strongest comparisons.
- Heavily reliant on extra data and ensembling for top results; fairness of comparisons not fully established.
- Reproducibility details missing.

Overall assessment
Promising idea with encouraging results, but the paper needs substantial clarification and stronger positioning against closely related inside-outside recursive work, plus cleaner, resource-controlled experiments. If these concerns are addressed, the contribution would meet NeurIPS standards; as written, it partially meets the criteria for quality and clarity, with moderate originality and significance.
