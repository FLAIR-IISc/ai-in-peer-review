- Introduces GB-RNN: adds a feedbackward pass so phrases update predictions using global sentence context for aspect-specific sentiment.  
- Shows strong empirical performance on a Twitter/SemEval2013 dataset, outperforming standard RNNs, baselines, and the SemEval 2013 winner.  
- Explores hybrid word vectors combining unsupervised and supervised representations to improve results.  
- Addresses contextual disambiguation in NLP with a clear motivating example (Android vs iPhone sentiment).  
- Proposes a novel, conceptually sound extension to RNNs by incorporating a backward pass for global context.  
- Provides strong empirical improvements supported by ablation studies and comparisons to baselines.  
- Presents clear mathematical formulation and good comparison to prior bidirectional RNN work.  
- Hybrid word vector approach is reasonable and empirically beneficial.  
- Adds model complexity and parameter count (extra matrices V, W↓), raising computational and overfitting concerns.  
- Limited discussion of generalizability to other tasks and domains beyond sentiment analysis.  
- Depends on a Stanford Parser, creating potential robustness and error-propagation issues not fully explored.  
- Improvement over bidirectional RNNs is present but not large; needs more extensive tuned comparisons.  
- Presentation issues: Table 3 is hard to interpret and obscures which configurations yielded which results.  
- Overall technically sound, well-written, original, and significant for sentiment/aspect classification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel method, strong empirical results, clear exposition, addresses core NLP challenge.  
- Arguments against acceptance: increased complexity, limited generalizability discussion, parser reliance, incremental gains over Bi-RNN.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions to clarify Table 3 and expand discussion of generalizability and parser dependency.
