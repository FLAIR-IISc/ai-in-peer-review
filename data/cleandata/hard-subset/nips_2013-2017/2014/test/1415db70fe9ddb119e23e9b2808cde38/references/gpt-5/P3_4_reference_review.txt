Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments standard bottom-up recursive neural networks with a top-down “feedbackward” pass. The bottom-up pass computes inside/forward representations at each node, and the top-down pass propagates a global contextual signal from the root to all constituents via a shared decomposition matrix. Final classification at each node concatenates the forward and backward vectors. The motivation is contextual or target-specific sentiment (SemEval 2013 Task 2 Subtask A), where the polarity of a marked term depends on sentential context. The paper also introduces “hybrid” word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with a small supervised trainable component, and applies dropout to node vectors.

This idea is closely related to classical inside-outside computations in probabilistic parsing and to neural instantiations such as inside-outside RNNs (Le & Zuidema) and bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie). The paper positions GB-RNN against bidirectional recursive models, arguing for a different parameterization of the top-down message and demonstrating empirical gains. It also compares against a purely bottom-up RNN and several tweet sentiment baselines, reporting improvements on SemEval 2013 (tweets and SMS).

Strengths
- Addresses a concretely motivated problem (contextual/target-level sentiment), where pure composition is insufficient.
- Clear architectural idea: top-down context propagation over trees, analogous to “outside” representations, with a simple parameter-sharing scheme.
- Empirical evaluation on a standard benchmark with carefully matched baselines (same embeddings/dropout across RNN, bidirectional RNN, GB-RNN). Gains over both the feedforward RNN and prior competition systems are reported.
- Sensible engineering: hybrid embeddings lessen overfitting and improve robustness; dropout on node vectors; two-stage training using noisy auxiliary data and a lexicon.
- The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie highlights differences in the backward pass and notes parameter-count implications.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Notational and dimensional inconsistencies hinder clarity and reproducibility. For instance, Eq. 3’s W↓ is described as R(n+nd)×(n+nd), but the mapping [pparent, p↓parent] → [left↓, right↓] requires 2nd outputs; later block forms (Eq. 5) imply W↓ ∈ R(2nd)×(n+nd). Similarly, Wc is stated as RC×(n+1) although the input concatenation has size n+nd(+1). Please reconcile these shapes.
- The claim “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector” contradicts Eq. 3/5, which explicitly uses the forward parent p in the backward computation. If the intended distinction is “we do not use the forward child vector,” please clarify.
- Fairness of comparisons to bidirectional recursive models: GB-RNN has n·nd more parameters. Improvements might be due to capacity rather than architecture. A controlled comparison at matched parameter budgets (vary nd accordingly) and/or regularization sweeps is needed.
- Heavy dependence on constituency parsing for tweets, a notoriously noisy domain. Constraining the parser so that every target span is a constituent can distort trees. The paper should quantify: (i) fraction of cases requiring constraints, (ii) parsing error rates, and (iii) sensitivity of GB-RNN to parse quality (e.g., using alternative parsers, dependency trees, or unparsed sequence baselines like BiLSTM).
- Scope of baselines: while several are included, strong sequence models that capture arbitrary context (e.g., bidirectional RNN/LSTM on tokens with target markers) should be competitive. The paper claims window methods cannot use distant context; in practice, BiRNNs do. A head-to-head with a BiRNN/BiLSTM sequence model with comparable capacity would strengthen the case.
- Limited analysis of when the top-down pass helps. The paper would benefit from stratified results by span length, distance between target and key context, and ablations isolating the contributions of (a) the backward pass, (b) hybrid embeddings, (c) dropout, (d) external lexicon, and (e) noisy pretraining. Some ablations are mentioned but not reported in detail (no full tables, no statistical significance).
- Reproducibility: many implementation details are under-specified (backprop through structure specifics, dropout placement on forward/backward vectors, inference-time scaling, optimizer hyperparameters beyond Adagrad, training epochs, early stopping). No code availability is mentioned.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and aligns with well-understood inside-outside message passing on trees, instantiated with neural parametrizations. The learning setup (two-stage training, hybrid embeddings) is reasonable. However, clarity issues (typos, dimensional mismatches) and the lack of controlled capacity-matched comparisons and significance testing reduce confidence in the strength of the empirical claims. The parsing reliance in a noisy domain is a potential fragility that is not analyzed.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but mathematical notation needs careful correction, and several claims need precise wording. Figures are referenced as central to understanding but details are absent here; ensure they specify data flow and dimensions. Provide algorithmic pseudocode for the top-down pass and loss backpropagation. Expand experimental details sufficient for reproduction.

Originality
The contribution is incremental but meaningful: a specific top-down parameterization and usage at inference time for contextual classification, plus a hybrid embedding trick. The connection to contemporaneous bidirectional recursive and inside-outside neural models is acknowledged; clearer differentiation and deeper analysis would help. As presented, GB-RNN is a particular instantiation rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.

Significance
Target-level sentiment is important, and showing gains on SemEval is valuable. If the improvements over strong sequence baselines and bidirectional recursive models hold under capacity-matched comparisons and robust analysis, the method could be adopted for other node-level labeling tasks on trees (e.g., semantic role labeling on constituents, word sense disambiguation). As it stands, significance is promising but not yet fully established.

Requested clarifications and additional experiments
- Fix dimensional inconsistencies; precisely define W↓, V, Wc shapes. Clarify the “does not use forward node” statement.
- Capacity-matched comparison to bidirectional recursive networks; report parameter counts for all models.
- Add strong sequence baselines (BiRNN/BiLSTM) with target markers; compare on the same embeddings and data.
- Parse sensitivity study and analysis of the “force-subtree” constraint impact.
- Detailed ablations (backward pass off, no hybrid, no dropout, no external lexicon, no noisy pretraining), with mean/std over multiple runs and significance tests.
- Error analysis highlighting cases where long-distance context is essential.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and relevant motivation; principled architectural extension of RNNs with top-down context.
- Solid empirical gains reported on a standard benchmark; simple and potentially general mechanism.
- Practical embedding/training choices that improve robustness.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological clarity issues and contradictions in notation.
- Potential confound with parameter count; missing capacity-controlled comparisons and strong sequence baselines.
- Heavy reliance on noisy parsing without sensitivity analysis.
- Limited depth of analysis and reproducibility details.

Overall: I lean toward weak reject in the current form due to clarity and experimental gaps, but I see a path to a strong paper with the requested fixes and additional evaluations.
