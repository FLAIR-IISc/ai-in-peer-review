Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural architecture that augments standard bottom-up recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference and training time. The top-down pass propagates information from the sentence-level root back to all nodes, enabling context-dependent reinterpretation of word/phrase representations and predictions. The proposed model is motivated by aspect/target-sensitive sentiment where the local phrase sentiment is undefined without the broader sentence context. The authors also propose “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small trainable supervised component, and they use dropout on node vectors.

The paper situates GB-RNN relative to: (i) feedforward compositional models [Socher et al., 2012–2013; Mitchell & Lapata; Yessenalina & Cardie], (ii) bidirectional recurrent and recursive models [Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie], (iii) inside–outside recursive models [Le & Zuidema], and (iv) word vector learning [LSA; Collobert et al.; Huang et al.; GloVe]. The empirical focus is SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment, including the term-in-context subtask). The authors report that GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and the top SemEval systems, with additional gains from the hybrid embeddings and weakly labeled auxiliary data.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for contextual/target-dependent sentiment: the model allows information flow from the root back to leaves, addressing cases where local phrases are neutral or ambiguous.
- Architectural contribution: a specific top-down recursion that conditions child backward vectors on both forward and backward parents; a thoughtful comparison to a concurrent bidirectional recursive variant.
- Practical embedding idea: combining fixed unsupervised embeddings with a small supervised component plus dropout is simple and effective.
- Empirical relevance: evaluation on SemEval-2013 Twitter sentiment (where context matters); use of noisy labels and lexicons in a two-stage training regimen reflects realistic constraints.
- Ablations are claimed for: feedforward vs GB-RNN, bidirectional recursive variant, hybrid vs purely supervised/unsupervised embeddings, and contributions of auxiliary data.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (organized by review criteria)

Quality
- The mathematical specification has dimensional inconsistencies/typos that obscure exact computations. For example, V ∈ Vnd×n appears to be a typo; W↓ is stated as R(n+nd)×(n+nd) though the output should be 2·nd to produce [child↓left; child↓right]; equation blocks need careful shape checking. These issues hinder reproducibility.
- Empirical evidence is only summarized; key tables/metrics are referenced but not included in the text provided. The competition used avg(F1pos, F1neg), but the paper mixes F1 and accuracy improvements. Please report exact numbers for all systems on the official metrics (with variance/CI) and ensure consistent metrics throughout.
- Comparisons to the bidirectional recursive model confound architecture and capacity: GB-RNN has n·nd more parameters. Provide parameter-matched baselines and/or regularization-controlled comparisons, plus statistical significance tests.
- The two-stage training uses additional weakly labeled tweets and a sentiment lexicon. While justified for the competition, this makes it harder to isolate the architecture’s contribution. Include a comparison where all models receive the same auxiliary data under identical training protocols.

Clarity
- Several typos (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” figure numbering such as “Figure 3.4”), and unclear notation (e.g., mixing p, x, a for node vectors) reduce readability. A careful editing pass is needed.
- Missing or unclear implementation details: parser constraints to force labeled spans to be subtrees could bias the structure; describe exactly how this constraint is enforced and whether it alters original parses. Provide hyperparameters per model (final chosen dims n, nd; activation; dropout placement) and training schedule details for both stages.
- Figures and tables are heavily referenced but not visible here. Ensure they are self-explanatory and include error bars or std across runs.

Originality
- The core idea (top-down information flow in tree models) overlaps with inside–outside/bi-directional recursive formulations [Le & Zuidema; Irsoy & Cardie]. The paper’s variant—using both forward and backward parents to compute child ↓ vectors and concatenating forward/backward representations at classification—is a reasonable design choice but feels incremental. Emphasize what new behaviors this enables (e.g., qualitative analyses where Irsoy & Cardie fails but GB-RNN succeeds) beyond parameter count.
- The hybrid embedding approach is practical but not conceptually novel; still, documenting its effectiveness is useful.

Significance
- Contextual sentiment is important, and improvements over strong SemEval systems would be impactful. However, the current evidence needs firmer grounding (complete metrics, parameter controls, significance tests).
- Generality beyond Twitter sentiment is not demonstrated. A secondary dataset (e.g., aspect-level product reviews) would strengthen claims.
- Reliance on high-quality constituency parses for noisy tweets may limit robustness. Discuss parsing errors’ impact and show sensitivity analyses.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a parameter-matched comparison to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive model and to a stronger sequence baseline (e.g., context windows with CRF or chain RNN using the marked target and full sentence).
- Report official SemEval metrics for all systems, including per-class F1 and macro/micro averages; include results on Tweets vs SMS subsets.
- Clarify the top-down equations with correct dimensions; include a diagram with tensor shapes. Release code or pseudocode for the forward/backward passes.
- Analyze errors: when does GB-RNN help most (negation, comparatives, contrast, long-distance modifiers)?
- Ablate the parser constraint that forces labeled spans to be subtrees; quantify its effect.
- Explore training the backward parameters in the pretraining stage (even without context labels) via auxiliary objectives, or multi-task training.
- Consider controlling for additional data by training all variants with identical weak supervision and lexicons.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of feedforward compositional models (context dependence).
- Thoughtful architectural design and practical embedding recipe.
- Potentially strong empirical gains on a widely used benchmark.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity/notation issues and missing, inconsistent metrics impede evaluation and reproducibility.
- Novelty is incremental relative to contemporaneous bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models.
- Confounded comparisons (parameter count, extra data) and lack of statistical rigor.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The idea is promising and likely useful, but the paper needs clearer, correct technical presentation, parameter- and data-controlled experiments with complete official metrics, and stronger comparisons to closely related bidirectional recursive baselines. With these improvements, this work could be a solid contribution.
