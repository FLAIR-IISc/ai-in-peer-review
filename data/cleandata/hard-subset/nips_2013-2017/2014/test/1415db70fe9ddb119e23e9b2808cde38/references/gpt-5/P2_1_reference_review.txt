Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference/training time. The backward pass computes a contextual vector for each node by recursively propagating information from the root to leaves; final predictions at any node (word/phrase) are made from the concatenation of its bottom-up and top-down vectors. The target task is contextual/target-dependent sentiment classification (SemEval 2013 Task 2, subtask A). The authors also propose “hybrid” word vectors: concatenating fixed unsupervised embeddings with a small supervised trainable segment. Empirically, GB-RNN outperforms a feedforward RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and baselines, and matches/exceeds top SemEval systems; an ensemble of five GB-RNNs achieves the best results. The work is related to bidirectional recurrent/recursive nets, inside-outside recursive models (Le & Zuidema), and prior recursive sentiment work (Socher et al.), and to unsupervised embeddings (e.g., GloVe) with task-specific fine-tuning.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The model is a clear extension of standard tree RNNs with a top-down pass. The equations are mostly adequate, though there are some notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “V ∈ Vnd×n”, “feedbackward”, inconsistent subscripts/spaces). The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie is helpful; however, the fairness of the comparison is not fully established because GB-RNN has more parameters (n·nd more) and there is no parameter-matched ablation. The hybrid embedding ablation is informative and consistent with intuition.
- Empirical support: Results on SemEval 2013 are promising: GB-RNN > RNN by ~5% and an ensemble surpasses the challenge winner. The paper reports ablations (RNN vs. bi-recursive vs. GB-RNN; hybrid vs. pure unsup/sup embeddings; effect of extra data). However, statistical significance, variance across runs, and detailed hyperparameters for nd and W↓ sizes are not fully reported. The inclusion of external lexicons and large distant supervision is allowed by the shared task, but conflates the contribution of the architecture with data scale; a matched setting without extra data would strengthen claims.
- Completeness/caveats: Training details (e.g., exact nd, training time, handling of non-binary trees, parser quality on tweets, sensitivity to parse errors) are light. The method forces the marked target span to be a subtree; this is pragmatic for subtask A (span is given) but may distort tree structure and is not analyzed for impact. The model does not iterate the belief propagation (one top-down pass only); this is reasonable but worth discussing.

Clarity:
The paper is readable and well organized conceptually (motivation → forward RNN → backward pass → training/embeddings → experiments). That said, several typographical/formatting issues (garbled lines in Sec. 3.6, equation typos, missing figure references, inconsistent notation) detract from clarity. More detail is needed on:
- How the parser constraints are implemented and how often the marked term is not a constituent in the unconstrained parse;
- Dimensions and parameter counts (nd values, W↓ shapes) in experiments;
- Whether losses are applied to leaf nodes only or all labeled nodes, and how unlabeled nodes affect gradient flow.

Originality:
The high-level idea—combining bottom-up and top-down passes on trees—is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive models and inside-outside architectures. The paper’s specific backward computation uses both forward and backward parent states and concatenates forward/backward at classification time; this is a plausible variant, but evolutionary rather than revolutionary. The hybrid embedding idea (fixed unsupervised + small supervised segment) is sensible and useful but not entirely novel conceptually. The main novelty is the combination and careful application to contextual sentiment with a clear task fit.

Significance:
Target-dependent/contextual sentiment is a meaningful problem; tree-structured bidirectional composition is a broadly relevant modeling idea for token/phrase labeling in context. Showing gains over a strong feedforward RNN baseline and a bidirectional recursive variant is valuable. However, the broader impact is tempered by:
- Reliance on high-quality parses for noisy Twitter text (no analysis of parse errors);
- Comparisons limited to tree models and simple classifiers; stronger sequence baselines (e.g., windowed models, CRFs; at the time, bidirectional RNNs are included but parameter-matched comparisons are missing) would help;
- Demonstration is on a single benchmark with heavy use of external data and an ensemble for the top result.

Pros:
- Clear motivation for context-dependent labeling on trees; architecture matches task.
- Empirical gains over feedforward RNN and competitive shared-task systems.
- Useful hybrid embeddings with dropout; solid ablation indicating their benefit.
- Theoretical comparison to related bidirectional-recursive formulations.

Cons:
- Incremental novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside-outside tree models; missing parameter-matched comparison.
- Clarity issues: typos/notation, missing details (nd, training regime specifics), and garbled text.
- Limited error/sensitivity analysis: parsing quality on tweets, effect of subtree forcing, robustness to span misalignment.
- Heavy reliance on external resources and ensembling blurs the architectural contribution.
- No statistical significance or variance reported.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide parameter-matched comparisons to Irsoy & Cardie by reducing nd or tying weights, and report variance across runs.
- Report parsing accuracy on this dataset, the proportion of targets not constituents pre-constraint, and an ablation without subtree forcing.
- Detail nd, W↓ dimensions, training time, number of epochs, and whether the backward pass helps when few nodes are labeled.
- Consider a single-pass vs. multi-pass top-down ablation, and an analysis of how top-down changes local predictions.
- Include comparisons without extra lexicons/distant supervision to isolate the architecture’s contribution.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a reasonable and effective model and presents nontrivial empirical gains with ablations. However, the methodological novelty over prior bidirectional/inside-outside tree models is moderate, and clarity/fairness issues remain. With stronger, parameter-matched baselines, clearer exposition, and robustness analyses, this would be a clear accept. As is, it still offers a useful, well-motivated contribution likely to interest NeurIPS NLP readers.
