Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments standard bottom-up recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference and training time. The aim is to update each node’s prediction using global sentence context, enabling contextual (target/aspect-specific) sentiment classification, particularly on noisy Twitter data. Concretely, the model first computes forward node vectors via a standard compositional function (single matrix, pointwise nonlinearity), then computes backward vectors in a root-to-leaves recursion that conditions each child’s backward vector on both the parent’s forward vector and the parent’s backward vector. Final classification at each node concatenates its forward and backward vectors. The authors also introduce “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a smaller, task-trained component and use dropout on node vectors.

The work is related to prior tree models for compositional semantics (e.g., Socher et al.), sentiment tree models, and bidirectional tree/recursive architectures (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema’s inside–outside RNN). The authors compare analytically to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN and empirically on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment). They report that GB-RNN outperforms a purely feedforward tree RNN, a bidirectional tree baseline, and the SemEval winning system, including an ensemble version.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for context-sensitive phrase/sentiment labeling where local phrases are neutral in isolation but polar in context.
- Simple, general mechanism to inject global context into all nodes via a top-down pass; integrates naturally with existing tree RNNs.
- Hybrid embeddings (fixed unsupervised + task-tuned small component) and dropout on node vectors are practical contributions that improve robustness.
- Empirical study on a relevant benchmark (SemEval 2013 Twitter), with comparisons to strong systems and model ablations; shows gains over a feedforward tree RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline.
- Sensible training strategy including noisy pretraining and then fine-tuning on context-sensitive data; use of AdaGrad and mini-batching.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty over previous bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models is incremental. The top-down pass and use of root/parent context to inform children have precedents; the difference in equations and parameterization is modest.
- Experimental details are incomplete: key scores (F1/accuracy) are referenced but not fully reported here; reliance on ensembles and additional external resources complicates fairness. It is unclear how single-model, no-external-data performance compares to prior systems under the same constraints.
- Several notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “feedbackward/ feedwordard,” dimensions, Eq. 3) obscure the exact architecture and make reproducibility harder.
- The approach depends on accurate syntactic parses; tweet parsing is brittle. The paper does not quantify the impact of parse quality or handle malformed trees.
- The backward update does not condition on the child’s forward vector (unlike Irsoy & Cardie). The paper claims this disentangles representations, but there is no ablation to support the design choice; capacity trade-offs are not explored.
- Limited task scope (Twitter sentiment only); claims of broader applicability (e.g., WSD) are not validated.

Quality:
The model is technically sound as a straightforward extension of tree RNNs. The equations are plausible, though the dimensionalities and block structure of W↓ should be clarified, and the distinction between n and nd and where V is used needs to be unambiguous. The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie is useful but would benefit from a stronger empirical ablation isolating (i) using both parent forward and backward vs only backward; (ii) using/not using child forward vector; (iii) the effect of V and nd. Experimental claims appear positive, but the review copy lacks exact metrics, variance, and resource-normalized baselines (single model, no lexicon/noisy data). Evaluation should also report per-class performance and quantify gains from each added resource (noisy tweets, lexicon) separately from the architectural change. Overall, more rigorous reporting is needed to fully substantiate the main claims.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally understandable but has numerous typos and some confusing notation. Figures are referenced but not shown here; ensuring they clearly depict the forward/backward computations would help. The dataset description (train/dev/dev-test sizes) is odd and should be corrected. Please define all dimensions once, keep consistent notation (forward/backward, root vs p2), and provide pseudocode or a precise computational graph for the backward pass. Reproducibility would benefit from exact hyperparameters, training epochs, initialization details, and code release.

Originality:
The idea of propagating information top-down in tree neural models is not new; inside–outside recursive models and bidirectional tree networks exist. The proposed variant differs primarily in how backward vectors are computed (conditioning on both parent forward and backward, and not on the child forward vector) and in the use of hybrid embeddings with dropout. This is a novel combination of familiar techniques rather than a fundamentally new framework. The paper should more thoroughly situate itself with inside–outside RNNs and other structured models (e.g., CRFs on trees).

Significance:
Context-aware sentiment on social media is important, and improving over strong baselines on SemEval would be impactful. If the reported gains hold in a controlled setting, this architecture could be adopted for other node-labeling tasks on trees. However, given the incremental nature of the architectural change and the unclear separation of gains due to additional data/ensembles vs the model itself, the current significance is moderate. Demonstrating broader applicability (e.g., aspect-based sentiment with known targets, semantic role labeling, or another non-sentiment task) would increase impact.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide full quantitative results (per-class F1, macro/micro), with and without external resources and with a single model vs ensemble.
- Add ablations: effect of nd, the V transform, using the child’s forward vector in the backward pass, and left/right-specific parameters.
- Analyze parse errors on tweets and the model’s robustness (e.g., comparing to a sequence model without parses).
- Report computational cost and parameter counts; discuss regularization and overfitting given added parameters.
- Compare to a bidirectional chain RNN/CRF with attention to aspect targets as a non-parse baseline with global context.
- Release code or detailed training recipe for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation of feedforward tree models: lack of contextual disambiguation.
- Simple, general architecture with promising empirical gains.
- Practical embedding/training tricks likely useful to practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside–outside tree models; theoretical contribution is limited.
- Incomplete and somewhat unclear experimental reporting; gains confounded by external resources and ensembling.
- Heavy reliance on noisy parses on tweets without robustness analysis.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline. With clearer experiments isolating the architectural contribution and improved clarity, this could be a solid contribution; as is, originality and evaluation rigor are not quite at the level expected for NeurIPS.
