Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down (“feedbackward”) pass, so that node predictions can incorporate global sentence context. The backward pass computes a “↓” vector for each node by propagating from the root to leaves, and the final classifier at each node uses the concatenation of the bottom-up (forward) and top-down vectors. The authors also introduce hybrid word vectors—concatenating fixed, unsupervised embeddings with small supervised task-specific components—and apply dropout. Experiments on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment, phrase-level) claim GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive model, and the competition winners; further gains are reported from using external resources (NRC lexicon, noisy tweets) and ensembling the top five models.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on tree-structured RNNs for compositional semantics (Socher et al.) and is closely related to bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), which likewise propagate information both bottom-up and top-down. The hybrid embedding idea is related to fine-tuning pre-trained word vectors while partially freezing parameters. The authors attempt to distinguish GB-RNN from other bidirectional recursive formulations by using both forward and backward parent vectors when computing child “↓” vectors, though the presentation is inconsistent.

Strengths
- Tackles contextual (phrase-in-sentence) sentiment, where local polarity depends on global context.
- Simple, plausible architectural extension to tree RNNs; the top-down pass is intuitive.
- Hybrid embedding with partial fine-tuning is practical and potentially useful beyond this task.
- Uses BPTS and regularization (dropout); attempts comparison with a bidirectional recursive baseline.
- Claims to outperform strong SemEval systems, with ablations (pretraining, lexicon data, ensemble).

Weaknesses (technical and presentation)
- Mathematical inconsistencies: multiple dimensional errors and contradictions.
  - Eq. (3): [b↓ c↓] = f(W↓ [p1 p↓1]). If each child ↓ is nd, the left-hand side is 2nd-dimensional, while [p1 p↓1] is (n+nd)-dimensional. Thus W↓ must be in R^{2nd × (n+nd)}. The paper states W↓ ∈ R^{(n+nd) × (n+nd)}, which is wrong.
  - Eq. (2): V ∈ Vnd×n appears to be a typo; presumably V ∈ R^{nd×n}.
  - Classifier: ya = softmax(Wc [a a↓]) with Wc ∈ R^{C×(n+1)} conflicts with concatenation dimension (n+nd), and bias handling is unclear.
  - Parameter count for W↓ is miscomputed; given the correct shape, the count is 2nd(n+nd), not the given 2n^2d + 2n·nd.
  - The text first says backward node uses both forward and backward parent vectors; later it claims “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” directly contradicting Eq. (3).
- Experimental reporting is incomplete:
  - Tables and scores are referenced but not shown. There are no actual F1 or accuracy numbers, confidence intervals, or statistical tests.
  - The paper alternates between SemEval’s official average F1 (pos/neg) and “accuracy,” without clarifying which metric is reported where.
  - The bidirectional recursive baseline is not described with enough detail (hyperparameters, exact variant) to assess fairness.
  - The best leaderboard result is an ensemble, but single-model performance relative to competition winners is not quantified.
- Data and evaluation issues:
  - Parsing tweets with a syntactic parser is error-prone; no analysis of parsing quality or robustness is provided.
  - The authors “constrained the parser to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree.” This is a strong and non-standard intervention that could leak annotation structure into the model. Details are missing (algorithm, effect on tree shape).
  - External resources (NRC lexicon, large noisy tweet corpus, movie reviews) are used but the comparison to SemEval systems may not be apples-to-apples unless those systems used similar resources; this isn’t discussed.
  - Domain shift (tweets vs SMS in the final test) not addressed.
- Method details missing/unclear:
  - Binarization strategy for parse trees is not specified.
  - Dropout: where exactly applied (embeddings, hidden nodes, both)? At what stages of the two-pass recursion?
  - Optimization details (learning rates, schedules, early stopping criteria, random seeds) not given; reproducibility is low.
  - Computational complexity and runtime overhead from the second pass are not discussed.
- Writing/formatting/citation problems:
  - Numerous typos: “feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “root note,” “de-composition,” “shold,” etc.
  - Spurious lines of raw tweet text appear inside Section 3.6, breaking the flow.
  - Many references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues); some claims are not supported with citations or side-by-side comparisons.

Quality
Due to the equation errors, contradictory statements about model inputs, missing metric numbers, and unclear evaluation protocol, the technical soundness and verifiability are insufficient at present. The idea is reasonable, but the paper in its current form does not allow an expert reader to reproduce results or even implement the model unambiguously.

Clarity
Clarity is a major problem: inconsistent notation, dimensional mistakes, missing tables, and formatting issues significantly impair comprehension. The description of differences vs. Irsoy & Cardie and Inside-Outside RNNs is muddled.

Originality
Incremental. The notion of a top-down pass in tree models is established (Inside-Outside RNNs; bidirectional recursive nets). The proposed variant (using both forward and backward parent vectors) could be a small design difference but is not convincingly or cleanly articulated.

Significance
Potentially useful for contextual sentiment, but without concrete, verifiable improvements and careful comparisons, the impact is uncertain. The hybrid embedding idea is pragmatic but not conceptually novel.

Questions for the authors
- Provide the exact shapes for all tensors in Eqs. (2–5) and correct Wc dimensions. Clarify whether backward nodes take forward parent vectors as inputs.
- Include all missing tables with explicit numbers (average F1 on official splits), per-class F1, and standard errors. Report single-model results alongside the ensemble.
- Describe the parser constraint procedure for ensuring labeled phrases are subtrees and its empirical impact.
- Detail the bidirectional recursive baseline(s) and ensure fairness (same embeddings, external data, tuning).
- Report significance tests and runtime/memory overhead. Provide seeds and code details to enable reproduction.

Pros
- Addresses an important contextual classification setting.
- Simple and plausible architectural extension.
- Practical hybrid embeddings; some ablations discussed.

Cons
- Severe clarity and correctness issues (equations, dimensions, contradictions).
- Missing quantitative results and fair, comprehensive comparisons.
- Questionable parser interventions and lack of robustness analysis.
- Formatting/citation problems.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the manuscript contains numerous technical inconsistencies and presentation issues that preclude proper assessment and reproduction. A thoroughly revised version with corrected math, complete experimental results, careful baselines, and cleaner writing could be competitive.
