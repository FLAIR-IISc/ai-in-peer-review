## Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and timely extension to recursive neural networks, addressing a critical limitation of existing models – the inability to fully capture context-dependent meaning. The GB-RNN, by incorporating a backward pass, allows for a more nuanced understanding of sentiment, particularly in cases where the sentiment target is unclear without broader context. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating superior performance on a challenging sentiment analysis task compared to strong baselines, including the SemEval 2013 winner. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough theoretical discussion of the benefits of the backward pass and a more detailed ablation study to pinpoint the exact contribution of each component.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of compositional semantics in natural language, specifically focusing on context-dependent sentiment analysis. It observes that standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) treat phrases in isolation, failing to account for how sentence context alters meaning.  The core contribution is the Global Belief RNN (GB-RNN), which extends standard RNNs by adding a feedbackward pass during inference. This allows phrases to update their predictions based on the global context of the sentence. 

The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader landscape of NLP, referencing key developments in word vectors (Mikolov et al., 2013; Pennington et al., 2014), compositional semantics (Socher et al., 2013), and RNNs (Elman, 1990). They correctly identify bidirectional RNNs and window-based methods as related approaches but emphasize the advantage of GB-RNN in capturing long-range dependencies.  Importantly, they acknowledge and compare their work to a concurrent bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014), highlighting the key differences in their backward pass computation.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of the backward pass in a recursive neural network framework is a genuinely novel contribution. It addresses a clear limitation of existing models and provides a principled way to incorporate global context.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The GB-RNN outperforms state-of-the-art methods, including the winner of the SemEval 2013 competition, on a challenging sentiment analysis task. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Motivation and Explanation:** The authors provide a compelling motivation for their work, using a concrete example (Fig. 1) to illustrate the need for context-dependent sentiment analysis. The explanation of the GB-RNN architecture is clear and well-illustrated (Fig. 2).
* **Thorough Baseline Comparison:** The paper provides a comprehensive comparison to several baselines, including standard RNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and window-based methods.
* **Hybrid Word Vector Representation:** The combination of pre-trained and task-specific word vectors is a sensible approach, leveraging the benefits of both unsupervised and supervised learning.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:**  While the intuition behind the backward pass is clear, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical justification.  Why does this particular backward propagation scheme lead to improved performance?  What are the conditions under which it is most effective? A more detailed analysis would strengthen the paper.
* **Ablation Study Could Be More Detailed:** The ablation study (Table 2) is useful, but could be expanded.  For instance, what is the impact of the dimension of the feedbackward vectors (nd)?  How sensitive is performance to the choice of the decomposition matrix V? 
* **Impact of Additional Training Data:** While the results demonstrating the benefit of the additional training data (Section 4.4) are positive, they are presented somewhat abruptly. A more careful analysis of the impact of each dataset would be beneficial. Did the additional data primarily help the RNN or the GB-RNN?
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The GB-RNN is computationally more expensive than a standard RNN due to the backward pass. The paper doesn't discuss this computational overhead or provide any insights into how it might be mitigated.
* **Parser Dependency:** The reliance on the Stanford Parser introduces a potential point of failure.  The performance of the GB-RNN is directly tied to the accuracy of the parse trees. The authors acknowledge this but don't explore alternative parsing strategies.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to context-dependent sentiment analysis.
*  The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*  The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification for the backward pass is limited.
*  The ablation study could be more comprehensive.
*  The computational cost of the GB-RNN is not discussed.



**Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of compositional semantics and has the potential to impact a variety of NLP applications. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and make it even more compelling.

**Rating:** 8/10
