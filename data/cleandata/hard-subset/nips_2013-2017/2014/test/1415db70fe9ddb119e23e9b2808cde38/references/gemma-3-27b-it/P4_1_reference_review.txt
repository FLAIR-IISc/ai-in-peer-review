The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) which extend standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) by incorporating a feedbackward pass during inference. This allows phrases to update their predictions based on the global sentence context, addressing a limitation of previous feedforward approaches, particularly in aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The authors demonstrate improvements over standard RNNs and other baselines on the SemEval 2013 Task 2 dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the shortcomings of existing compositional models in capturing contextual sentiment. The introduction effectively illustrates the problem with a compelling example. The authors relate their work to relevant areas like word vectors, RNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and sentiment analysis, providing a solid foundation for understanding the contribution. The detailed explanation of the GB-RNN architecture (Section 3) is clear, and the mathematical formulation is presented logically. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to competition systems and various baselines. The inclusion of ablation studies and analyses of additional training data strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of a feedbackward pass in recursive neural networks to incorporate global context is a novel approach. While bidirectional RNNs exist, the specific mechanism of propagating information back down the parse tree as presented here is a distinct contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear and concise, and the mathematical notation is easy to follow. The diagrams (e.g., Fig. 2) effectively illustrate the model's architecture.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The model is theoretically sound and the experimental results support the claims. The authors carefully consider and address potential issues like parameter count and regularization. The use of established techniques like dropout and AdaGrad demonstrates a strong understanding of practical deep learning considerations.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed – contextual sentiment analysis – is an important one, particularly in the context of social media analysis. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods suggest the potential for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While the overall approach is novel, the individual components (RNNs, bidirectional processing) are well-established. The paper's novelty lies in the specific combination and application of these components. The advantage over the related bidirectional recursive models [12, 14] is not overwhelmingly convincing, despite the attempts to delineate differences in Equation 5. The performance gain may not justify the added complexity.
*   **Dataset Limitation:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset (SemEval 2013 Task 2). While this dataset is a standard benchmark, it would be beneficial to see performance on other datasets to assess the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions extensive hyperparameter tuning, but the specific choices and their impact are not fully discussed. More detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable.
*   **Parser Dependency:** The model relies on a parser to create the tree structure. The performance is thus inherently tied to the accuracy of the parser. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of parser errors would be useful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains a complex model.
*   The problem addressed is important and the proposed solution has the potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty is incremental, building upon well-established techniques.
*   The experiments are limited to a single dataset.
*   The model's reliance on a parser introduces a potential source of error.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of natural language processing. The GB-RNN represents a valuable extension to existing recursive neural network models, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its limitations. I recommend acceptance.
