Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments standard bottom-up (feedforward) recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass. The top-down pass propagates information from the sentence root to all nodes, enabling context-dependent predictions at each phrase/word, which is crucial for tasks like contextual or target-specific sentiment (e.g., “Android beats iOS” where polarity toward each entity depends on comparative context). The model concatenates bottom-up and top-down vectors at each node and classifies with a softmax. The authors also introduce hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small trainable, supervised component, plus dropout.

This work relates to:
- Recursive/Tree neural networks for compositional semantics and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al. EMNLP’13; MV-RNN, RNTN). Those are bottom-up and target context-independent.
- Bidirectional recurrent models on chains (Schuster & Paliwal) and prior bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema inside-outside). The paper positions GB-RNN as different in the way the backward vectors are computed (using both forward and backward parent) and claims better empirical results.
- Word embeddings learned from local/global context (e.g., Collobert et al., GloVe), with task-tuning. The hybrid vector idea blends fixed unsupervised and small supervised parts.

The paper evaluates on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), particularly the phrase-in-context subtask, and reports gains over baselines, a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and competition winners. External resources (NRC lexicon; large weakly labeled tweets) are used where allowed.

Strengths:
- Clear and important motivation: many NLP predictions are context-dependent; a top-down pass over trees is a principled way to inject global context into local decisions.
- Simple, general mechanism that can be applied to any tree RNN without changing the encoder’s forward step; conceptually related to inside-outside/belief propagation.
- Hybrid word vectors with dropout is a practical contribution; the rationale is well-argued and likely useful beyond this task.
- Empirical focus on a realistic benchmark (SemEval Twitter sentiment, phrase-in-context); claims to outperform competition winners and strong baselines.
- Discussion of model variants vis-à-vis bidirectional recursive models, with parameterization comparisons.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality:
- Mathematical specification has dimensional inconsistencies that hinder reproducibility:
  - Eq. (3) maps [parent_f (n), parent_b (nd)] to [left_b (nd), right_b (nd)]. The weight matrix should be of size (2·nd) × (n+nd), not R(n+nd)×(n+nd) as stated.
  - The classifier Wc is said to be RC×(n+1) while the input is [x; x↓] of size n+nd (plus bias). This appears incorrect.
  - The text later states “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” which contradicts Eq. (3) that uses [p, p↓] to compute child ↓-vectors.
  Please clarify equations, dimensions, and the exact computation used.
- Experimental reporting lacks crucial details. References to Tables 1–3 and Figures are not accompanied by precise numbers in the text provided (e.g., exact F1 scores per class/domain, stdev across runs, statistical significance). Claims like “over 5% better” or “+2% with NRC lexicon, +1% with two-stage training” need exact metrics and confidence intervals.
- Fairness of comparisons: the best system is an ensemble; are baselines ensembled as well? How much of the gain is due to external resources vs the core GB-RNN? A controlled ablation table (RNN vs Bi-RecNN vs GB-RNN; with/without NRC; with/without weakly labeled tweets; single vs ensemble) would make the contribution more convincing.
- Parsing Twitter with a PCFG parser is error-prone; performance may be sensitive to parse quality. Please analyze errors or robustness to parsing noise (e.g., compare to dependency parses, or to using gold vs automatic trees where available).
- Computational considerations: GB-RNN adds n·nd parameters and a full top-down pass. Training/inference time and memory overhead versus baselines are not reported.

Clarity:
- Generally well-motivated and readable, but the above inconsistencies (notation, dimensions, and the contradiction about using forward vectors) reduce clarity.
- The term “feedbackward” is non-standard; consider “top-down” or “backward (downward) pass” to align with prior bidirectional/inside-outside terminology.
- Dataset description has confusing counts (train/dev/dev-test all “7862”); please correct and specify per-domain (Twitter/SMS) splits and evaluation metrics used (macro-avg F1 of pos/neg per SemEval conventions).
- Provide a schematic for the backward pass with explicit tensor shapes.

Originality:
- The idea of combining bottom-up and top-down passes on trees is known (e.g., inside-outside RNNs; bidirectional recursive networks), but the specific parameterization that conditions children on both forward and backward parent vectors is a plausible, modestly novel variant. The hybrid word vector trick is also a practical, incremental contribution. The paper should more explicitly differentiate from Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside networks and Irsoy & Cardie’s formulation, ideally with side-by-side equations and empirical comparisons on the same setup.

Significance:
- Context-sensitive phrase-level sentiment is a meaningful task; showing improvements over strong competition systems would be valuable. However, the impact is hard to assess without full quantitative details and stronger baselines (e.g., chain BiLSTM with attention for target sentiment; though timelines may vary) and without demonstrating generality on additional tasks (e.g., word sense disambiguation, coreference signals, other token-level labeling).
- The hybrid embeddings and dropout findings are likely broadly useful.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix and clarify all equations, dimensions, and the exact data flow in the backward pass; reconcile the stated contradiction.
- Provide full experimental tables with metrics (macro F1, per-class F1, accuracy), per-domain breakdown, significance tests, and runtime/memory.
- Add ablations: effect of nd, V, using forward parent in backward step or not; hybrid embeddings vs fixed vs fully trainable; dropout.
- Expand comparisons: bidirectional chain RNN/LSTM with large context; inside-outside RNN; window-based strong baselines; parse-free models (CNN/transformers, if within scope).
- Analyze robustness to parse errors; possibly include dependency-tree variant.
- Release code or detailed pseudocode to enhance reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation of tree RNNs with a simple, broadly applicable mechanism.
- Potentially strong empirical gains on a realistic benchmark.
- Practical embedding/training insights (hybrid vectors + dropout).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Mathematical and notational inconsistencies; unclear exact model.
- Insufficient quantitative detail and ablation to isolate the source of gains.
- Limited breadth of evaluation; fairness of comparisons unclear.
- Overlap with existing bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models not fully resolved.

Overall: Promising idea with likely practical value, but the current presentation needs clearer, consistent formulation and more rigorous empirical evidence to meet NeurIPS standards.
