Summary
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference and training time. The goal is to let local (word/phrase) predictions incorporate sentence-level context, motivated by target-dependent sentiment classification (SemEval-2013 Task 2). Technically, the forward pass computes node vectors with a standard composition function; a backward pass from the root computes downward vectors that condition on both the parent’s forward representation and its downward context. Final classification at each node uses the concatenation of forward and downward vectors. The paper also proposes “hybrid” embeddings: a concatenation of fixed unsupervised word vectors and a small trainable supervised segment. Experiments on SemEval twitter sentiment (term-in-context) claim improvements over a feedforward RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), standard bag-of-words baselines, and the 2013 competition winner; additional gains are reported from ensembling, distant supervision, and lexicon-derived training instances.

Relation to prior work
The paper is situated among compositional models for phrases/sentences (e.g., Socher et al., 2010–2013), bidirectional recurrent/recursive architectures (Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie), and inside–outside style recursive models (Le & Zuidema). Its key distinction from bidirectional RecNNs is that the downward signal is computed from the parent’s forward and downward vectors, rather than directly from each child’s forward vector plus a shared downward parent signal; the authors argue this disentangles children from the parent’s global signal. The “hybrid” embeddings idea is related to using pre-trained embeddings with/without fine-tuning; here, the freezing is explicit in part of the vector (akin to multi-channel embeddings).

Strengths
- Clear motivation: Many targets have context-dependent sentiment; a top-down pass is a principled way to inject global information along tree paths.
- Model simplicity and generality: The approach is a straightforward extension of standard RecNNs and can, in principle, be plugged into any tree-based classifier; inference remains linear in tree size.
- Empirical coverage: Comparisons to RNN baselines, bidirectional RecNN, and competition systems are claimed; ablations on hybrid embeddings and extra training data are discussed. The two-stage training (pretrain bottom-up with noisy labels, then fine-tune full model on target-dependent data) is sensible.
- Practical contributions: Hybrid embeddings and dropout over node vectors are well-motivated for regularization with high-dimensional inputs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and notation issues: There are numerous typos (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “root note”), dimensional inconsistencies (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n), and confusing statements. The text says “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” yet Eq. (3) explicitly uses the parent forward vector p1. Please clarify precisely which forward vectors are used at each step.
- Incomplete experimental reporting: The manuscript references tables and figures with numeric results, but no numbers are visible here. For reproducibility and to judge significance, please provide exact scores (F1, accuracy), standard deviations over runs, and single-model vs. ensemble performance on dev and test. Also specify which SemEval subtask(s) (A: term-level; B: message-level) and the exact splits.
- Fairness of comparisons: Final test results are an ensemble and leverage substantial external resources (NRC lexicon examples, 176k distant-supervised tweets, movie reviews). Baselines should be given the same external data if possible, or at least report both “in-domain only” and “with external data” settings for all models. Otherwise, gains may conflate architecture and data.
- Parsing on tweets: The Stanford Parser is brittle on noisy Twitter text. The paper states the parser was “constrained to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree.” How is this enforced? If parses are altered to accommodate targets, this may give the model privileged structural information and bias evaluation. Please detail the constraint algorithm and its impact on trees.
- Comparison to bidirectional RecNN: The theoretical comparison is qualitative and the parameter counting seems off (shapes and counts for W↓ blocks are unclear). Is the improvement due to conditioning on the parent’s forward vector vs. child forward vectors, or simply more parameters? An ablation that equalizes capacity and isolates the source of gains is needed.
- Scope of “global belief”: The model performs one top-down pass on a single tree; it is not truly a global inference procedure (no iterative message passing or global training objective beyond cross-entropy). The terminology may overstate the connection to belief propagation/CRFs. A discussion of this limitation would temper claims.
- Limited baselines: Given the task, it would be useful to compare to strong non-tree target-dependent baselines (e.g., bidirectional chain RNNs with target markers, feature-rich linear models) and to tree-structured alternatives like inside–outside RNNs, to ensure gains are due to the top-down mechanism rather than just using any context.
- Reproducibility: Important hyperparameters (learning rates for AdaGrad, regularization coefficients, exact dropout placement), stopping criteria, and implementation details are insufficiently specified for replication.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide exact metrics, dev/test numbers, and statistical significance. How much does the top-down pass help over bottom-up on the same training budget and without external data?
- How robust are results to parser errors? Do you have performance as a function of parse quality, or a comparison to sequence models that avoid parsing?
- Can you show an ablation where V is identity and/or nd = n to test whether the transform and dimensionality reduction are necessary?
- How many downward passes were tried? Does iterating improve performance?
- For the bidirectional RecNN baseline, were capacities matched (same number of parameters), and were word embeddings/hyperparameters identical?

Minor comments
- Fix notation and dimensionality typos; ensure all equations compile and symbols are defined.
- Provide details on how you constrained trees to contain labeled spans and whether this is guaranteed without re-parsing.
- Consider renaming “global belief” to “top-down contextualized RecNN” to avoid conflation with global graphical-model inference.

Evaluation
- Quality: Promising idea and sensible engineering; however, clarity issues and missing experimental details prevent a definitive assessment.
- Clarity: Needs significant proofreading and clearer math/notation; more details for reproducibility.
- Originality: Incremental but meaningful extension over existing recursive/bidirectional models; the hybrid embedding variant is a practical, known idea given a new instantiation.
- Significance: If substantiated with complete, fair, and reproducible results, the approach would be of interest to the NLP/representation learning community and applicable beyond sentiment.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated architectural extension that addresses a real limitation of purely bottom-up trees.
- Empirically competitive with prior systems on a standard benchmark; ablations suggest the components matter.
- Simple, general technique likely to be reused.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently clear and rigorous presentation; missing numbers and key methodological details.
- Potential confounds (parser constraints, extra data, model capacity) not adequately controlled.
- Limited analysis of failure cases, robustness, and the exact source of gains.

Overall: Borderline; I would lean toward rejection unless the authors substantially clarify the methodology, provide full results and fair comparisons, and tighten the theoretical/empirical comparison to prior bidirectional recursive models.
