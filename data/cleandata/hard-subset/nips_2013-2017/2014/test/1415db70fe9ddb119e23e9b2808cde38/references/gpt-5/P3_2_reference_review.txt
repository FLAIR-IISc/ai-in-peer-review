Summary
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a bidirectional tree-structured neural model for context-dependent phrase classification. A standard bottom-up recursive neural network computes forward node representations; a top-down “feedbackward” pass then propagates a global context vector from the root to every node. Final predictions at each node are made from the concatenation of forward and backward vectors, enabling contextual disambiguation (e.g., entity-targeted sentiment within a tweet). The paper also introduces hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with small, trainable task-specific vectors, and uses dropout. On SemEval-2013 Task 2 Twitter sentiment, GB-RNN reportedly outperforms strong feature-engineered systems and stronger neural baselines, including a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie).

Relation to prior work
- Recursive neural models for compositional semantics (Socher et al., 2010–2013), and inside-outside style passes over trees (e.g., Le & Zuidema) are closely related. The proposed model is essentially a bidirectional tree RNN, where the top-down pass conditions child backward vectors on both the forward and backward parent.
- The bidirectional chain RNN literature (Schuster & Paliwal) and bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema) are relevant. The paper positions GB-RNN as using both forward and backward parent states in the top-down transformation and as having a slightly larger parameterization.
- The hybrid embedding idea builds on findings that unsupervised vectors lack sentiment polarity and that fine-tuning helps; concatenating fixed unsupervised with small trainable segments is a pragmatic variant.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for context-dependent phrase labeling in social media, where sentiment is often target-specific and not inferable from local phrases.
- Conceptually simple and practically useful extension of recursive models: a top-down pass that brings global sentence context to each node without relying on short windows.
- Empirical gains on a widely used benchmark (SemEval-2013 Task 2), including improvements over a bidirectional recursive baseline and over competition winners, suggest the approach can be impactful.
- The hybrid embeddings and dropout are sensible engineering choices that likely improve robustness on noisy tweets.
- The paper discusses training details (two-stage training with distant supervision, AdaGrad, dropout), which is helpful.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Mathematical clarity and internal consistency:
  - There are dimension/notation issues: V is written as “V ∈ Vnd×n” (typo) and W↓ is given as R(n+nd)×(n+nd), which does not match the mapping from [parent_forward (n), parent_backward (nd)] to [left_backward (nd), right_backward (nd)]; it should be in R(2nd)×(n+nd), with an explicit block structure. Please fix shapes and provide a precise computational graph.
  - The text states “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” but Eq. 3 explicitly uses the forward parent p in computing backward children. This contradiction must be resolved.
- Novelty vs prior bidirectional recursive work: The architectural difference from Irsoy & Cardie is incremental (using both forward and backward parent states and separate left/right transforms). The paper should sharpen the conceptual distinction and justify why the added parameters, not just capacity, yield better context propagation (e.g., through analysis or controlled capacity-matched comparisons).
- Evaluation reporting is incomplete in the current draft:
  - Tables are referenced but not present in the text provided; exact numbers, standard deviations, statistical tests (e.g., McNemar on dev/test), and ablation results are needed to substantiate claims (e.g., “5%” over RNN, “2%” from lexicon data, “1%” from two-stage training).
  - It is unclear whether the comparison to the bidirectional recursive baseline is capacity-matched and uses identical resources (hybrid embeddings, dropout, extra data).
- Use of external data and fairness:
  - The system uses NRC lexicon entries and 176k distantly labeled tweets, and ensembles 5 models. While SemEval allowed external resources, the paper should disentangle where the gains come from (model vs data vs ensembling). Provide single-model results without external data and without ensembles.
- Parsing and alignment to labeled spans:
  - Tweets are hard to parse; the paper “constrains the parser to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree.” Please detail how this constraint is enforced and ensure no label leakage. Does this require oracle span constraints at test time? What is the impact if the labeled span is non-constituent?
- Scope of generality:
  - Claims of broader applicability (e.g., WSD) are plausible but untested. A second task (e.g., target-dependent sentiment on other datasets, or a structured labeling task) would strengthen significance.
- Efficiency:
  - The top-down pass doubles computation. Provide complexity and runtime costs and discuss scalability to longer trees.

Quality
The core idea is technically sound, but the current presentation has inconsistencies in equations and lacks complete experimental evidence (numbers, ablations, significance). With clarified math and fuller reporting, the contribution would be more convincing.

Clarity
Generally readable, but the model section needs rigorous notation, corrected shapes, and a clean comparison to alternative formulations. Please include pseudo-code or a figure with precise tensor dimensions. Avoid neologisms like “feedbackward.”

Originality
Moderate. The bidirectional pass over trees is known; the specific conditioning of backward children on both forward and backward parent states and the hybrid embeddings are incremental yet practical contributions. Positioning relative to inside-outside RNNs and Irsoy & Cardie should be expanded.

Significance
If the reported gains hold under controlled comparisons, this is a useful advance for contextual phrase classification in noisy text, likely to be adopted. However, the significance hinges on disentangling model contributions from data and ensembling.

Questions for the authors
- Please correct Equation shapes, and clarify whether backward computation uses forward parent states (as in Eq. 3) or not (as stated later).
- Are comparisons to Irsoy & Cardie capacity-matched? What happens if you match parameter counts?
- Provide single-model results without external lexicons/distant supervision, and with/without hybrid embeddings and dropout.
- How exactly are parsing constraints applied to ensure labeled spans align to constituents at test time?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of standard recursive models with a simple, effective top-down pass.
- Promising empirical results on a standard benchmark.
- Practical insights (hybrid embeddings, dropout) for noisy text.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to prior bidirectional recursive models.
- Incomplete and sometimes inconsistent technical specification.
- Insufficiently controlled experimental analysis and missing details about resources and ensembling.

Overall assessment
Borderline accept, conditional on fixing the mathematical inconsistencies and adding thorough, controlled experiments (including ablations and significance tests). The idea is useful and timely, but the paper must better isolate and justify the model’s contribution. Confidence: medium.
