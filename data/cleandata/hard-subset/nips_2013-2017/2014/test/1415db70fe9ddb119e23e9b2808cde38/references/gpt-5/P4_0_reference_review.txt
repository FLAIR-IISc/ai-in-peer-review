Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model for context-dependent phrase classification. The approach augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The forward pass computes inside vectors using a standard composition f(W[child1; child2]). The backward pass starts at the root with p↓root = f(V proot) and propagates downward: at each internal node, child downward vectors are computed from both the forward parent vector and the parent’s downward vector via a de-composition matrix W↓. Final predictions at each node use the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The paper also introduces hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed, unsupervised vectors with a small supervised part updated during training, coupled with node-level dropout.

The task focus is target-dependent (contextual) sentiment on SemEval 2013 Task 2 tweets. The model reportedly outperforms a standard (purely feedforward) tree-RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and competition systems; the best numbers come from an ensemble of 5 GB-RNNs. The paper provides ablations showing gains from the hybrid embeddings, additional lexicon-based and distant supervision data, and two-stage training (first train the forward RNN on larger context-independent data, then fine-tune the full GB-RNN on the target-dependent data).

The approach is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive models and inside–outside style recursive networks (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), which also compute top-down/outside contextual representations over trees. The paper’s stated distinction is that its downward computation uses both the forward and backward parent vectors, whereas some prior work uses only the forward parent. The idea of unfolding for inference (not just training) is emphasized.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for context-dependent sentiment: local phrases can be neutral, and context may be nonlocal in tree distance.
- Simple, general architectural extension: a top-down pass that conditions node representations on global context.
- Empirical improvements on a standard benchmark; the model beats a feedforward tree-RNN by >5% absolute and a bidirectional recursive baseline on the authors’ setup.
- Practical hybrid embeddings that balance task-specific signal and generalization, with a clear training recipe (dropout, AdaGrad, two-stage training).
- Ablations indicating contributions from external lexicon data and distant supervision.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models, which also compute top-down/outside vectors. The specific use of both forward and backward parent vectors is a modest architectural variation.
- Some technical details are unclear or inconsistent (see Quality/Clarity), which affects reproducibility.
- Fairness of comparisons is hard to assess: the best results use ensembles and additional external resources; it is unclear whether the competition baselines used comparable resources or ensembling. A single-model, no-external-data comparison would help.
- Heavy reliance on constituency parse trees for noisy tweets; no analysis of parsing errors, constrained parsing (forcing targets into subtrees), or their effect on performance.
- Missing comparisons to strong sequence baselines that also incorporate long-range context (e.g., bidirectional RNNs/LSTMs over tokens with attention to marked targets). The statement that window/recurrent methods cannot incorporate long-distance context is too strong without empirical support.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The model formulation is plausible and standard training (backpropagation through structure) is appropriate. The use of the downward vectors solely for features (concatenated with forward vectors) is coherent and trainable end-to-end.
- Some notational and dimensional issues need correction: for example, W↓ is described as R(n+nd)×(n+nd), but the mapping in Eq. (3) appears to require output dimension 2nd given input of size (n+nd). Clarifying nd, W↓ block structure, and output shapes is necessary. The text also contains typos (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard”) and a wrong “V ∈ Vnd×n”.
- Experimental details are incomplete in the excerpt: exact metrics, standard deviations over runs, and concrete numbers versus baselines and SemEval winners are not shown; hyperparameter ranges and model sizes are partially given but could be expanded. The impact of the ensemble vs. single model should be quantified.

Clarity:
- Overall structure is reasonable, but clarity is reduced by typos, broken figure/table references, and some ambiguous notation. The dataset split description (identical sizes for train/dev/dev-test) and whether they refer to tweets or marked targets need clarification.
- The theoretical comparison to Irsoy & Cardie is useful; it would benefit from a clearer, self-contained derivation and a brief complexity vs. performance discussion (parameter counts and overfitting risk).
- Suggest adding a concrete worked example showing how a target’s sentiment flips due to context in the downward pass.

Originality:
- The top-down “global belief” idea closely parallels inside–outside and bidirectional recursive models. The specific way of conditioning the downward computation on both forward and backward parent vectors is a minor architectural difference. The hybrid embedding idea is practical but not conceptually new. The novelty primarily lies in the specific combination and the application to target-dependent sentiment.

Significance:
- On SemEval target-dependent sentiment, the reported improvements suggest practical utility. However, broader impact is limited by dependence on high-quality parses for noisy text and by the incremental nature of the architectural change. Demonstrating gains beyond Twitter and against strong sequence models would strengthen significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses a well-motivated limitation of feedforward tree-RNNs in context-dependent classification.
- Conceptually simple, generally applicable top-down pass; likely useful beyond sentiment (e.g., word sense disambiguation).
- Empirical gains over tree-RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline; ablations show contributions from model components.
- Practical training recipe (hybrid embeddings, dropout, two-stage training) that others can adopt.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited novelty relative to existing inside–outside and bidirectional recursive models; architectural change is modest.
- Clarity issues (typos, dimensional inconsistencies, missing tables/figures) hinder reproducibility.
- Comparisons conflate ensembling and external resources; fairness and effect sizes are unclear without single-model, no-external baselines.
- No analysis of parsing robustness on tweets or the constraint that targets form subtrees.
- Missing comparisons to strong sequence baselines that model long-range context.

Suggestions to improve the manuscript:
- Fix notation/dimension errors; provide explicit shapes for all matrices/vectors and a compact block-matrix equation for W↓ with output size 2nd.
- Report exact metrics (F1 by class and averaged), confidence intervals over multiple runs, and single-model results with/without external data and ensembling.
- Add comparisons to sequence baselines (e.g., bidirectional RNN/LSTM with attention to the marked target and/or CRF).
- Analyze sensitivity to parsing errors and the constraint that marked spans form subtrees.
- Provide qualitative examples demonstrating long-distance contextual effects and error analysis.
- Clarify dataset splits and the number of labeled targets versus tweets; ensure resource parity when comparing to competition systems; consider releasing code.
