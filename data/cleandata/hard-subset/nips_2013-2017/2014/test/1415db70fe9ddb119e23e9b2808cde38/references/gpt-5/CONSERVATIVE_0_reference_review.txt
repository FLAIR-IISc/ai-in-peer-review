Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference/training time. The backward pass computes a context vector for each node by recursively conditioning on both the parent’s forward representation and the parent’s backward representation. Final node-level predictions use the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The authors also introduce “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small supervised, trainable component, and apply dropout. The model is evaluated on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), including contextual phrase-level sentiment classification; the paper claims improvements over a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive variant (Irsoy & Cardie), and the competition winner, with further gains from additional weakly labeled data and ensembling.

Relation to prior work
The work sits among recursive neural models for compositional semantics (Socher et al., 2010–2013) and bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema). The proposed top-down pass resembles inside–outside RNNs that propagate information from root to leaves. The hybrid embedding idea (pretrained vectors plus task-specific fine-tuning) is related to widely used initialization + fine-tuning; here, the “delta” component is separated and concatenated rather than fully fine-tuning, which is incremental.

Strengths
- Addresses a real limitation of purely bottom-up recursive models: local predictions that ignore sentence-level context.
- Model is simple to implement on top of a standard RNN and appears computationally tractable.
- Attempts a fair architectural comparison (RNN vs bidirectional recursive vs GB-RNN) with shared embeddings/dropout.
- Empirically oriented: ablations (hybrid vs purely supervised/unsupervised embeddings; two-stage training) and use of additional noisy data.
- The task (contextual and entity/target-specific sentiment in tweets) is practically relevant.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models is limited. The main difference (using both forward and backward parent in the top-down recurrence, adding parameters) is modest and lacks a principled justification beyond a brief intuition.
- Experimental evidence is not convincingly isolated. Improvements are confounded by ensembling and substantial use of external resources (NRC lexicon, large noisy corpora). It is unclear whether all baselines are trained with identical external data and the same two-stage procedure.
- Reporting is incomplete in the provided text: the paper references tables/figures for quantitative results but does not include the actual numbers here. Without exact scores, statistical significance tests, and per-class breakdowns, it is difficult to assess gains.
- Methodological details are underspecified or inconsistent: the notation for V has typos (V ∈ Vnd×n), dimensions of nd vs n are not carefully motivated, and the training loss over partially labeled trees is not rigorously described (e.g., how gradients propagate when internal nodes lack supervision).
- Reliance on constituency parses of tweets raises robustness concerns; there is no analysis of parse quality or sensitivity to parse errors, and the constraint to force each labeled phrase to be a subtree could introduce biases.
- No computational analysis (parameters, speed, memory) compared to prior bidirectional/inside–outside models.
- Clarity and polish issues: numerous typos (“feedbackward”), dangling text fragments, incomplete references.

Quality
Technically, the recurrence is straightforward and sound, but the paper does not provide theoretical analysis (e.g., why the added term improves disambiguation) or diagnostics demonstrating that top-down signals meaningfully change leaf predictions. The evaluation design leaves open questions about fairness (use of external data/ensembles for some models but not others), missing metrics, and lack of significance testing. Important ablations are absent: GB-RNN without V, varying nd, tying vs untying W↓, training with/without internal node losses, and sensitivity to tree quality.

Clarity
The core idea is understandable, but the manuscript has many typos, inconsistent notation, and missing details. Reproducibility is hindered by the lack of exact hyperparameters for all components, missing quantitative tables, and an imprecise description of the constrained parsing scheme.

Originality
Moderate to low. The top-down pass over trees is known (inside–outside RNN/bi-recursive). The specific formulation (conditioning on both forward and backward parent with extra parameters) is a small variation. The hybrid embedding approach is an incremental twist on common practice.

Significance
Potentially useful if the gains hold under controlled comparisons, but currently limited by the narrow domain (one dataset), dependence on external resources and ensembles, and modest methodological novelty. Broader impact would be clearer with results on additional target-dependent sentiment datasets and with stronger baselines (e.g., sequence models with attention or simple context features) under identical training resources.

Questions for the authors
- Please report exact test metrics (avg F1, per-class F1, accuracy), confidence intervals, and significance tests, for single-model settings without external data and without ensembling, across RNN, bi-recursive, and GB-RNN.
- Were the bi-recursive baseline and standard RNN trained with the same external lexicon and noisy tweets, and with the same two-stage procedure?
- How sensitive is GB-RNN to parse errors and to the “subtree constraint”? What happens if you use dependency trees or off-the-shelf Twitter parsers?
- Provide ablations on nd, removal of V, tying W↓ blocks, and analysis of the incremental benefit of the backward pass over simply enlarging the forward model.
- Can you show qualitative cases where the backward signal flips a leaf prediction and is necessary for correctness?

Pros
- Addresses an important weakness of bottom-up recursive models.
- Simple, potentially general mechanism; practical task with real utility.
- Some ablations and comparisons included; use of hybrid embeddings.

Cons
- Limited novelty over prior bi-recursive/inside–outside models.
- Confounded evaluation (ensembles, external data), missing quantitative details.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues; several typos and notation errors.
- No analysis of computational cost or parse sensitivity.

Recommendation: Reject
The idea is sensible but too close to existing bi-recursive models, and the empirical support is not sufficiently controlled or convincingly presented. With clearer, controlled experiments, thorough ablations, and stronger writing, this could be a solid incremental contribution.

Confidence: 0.8
