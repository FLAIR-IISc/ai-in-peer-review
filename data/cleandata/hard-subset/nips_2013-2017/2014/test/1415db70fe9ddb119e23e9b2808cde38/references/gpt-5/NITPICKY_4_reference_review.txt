Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a bidirectional recursive model that augments a standard bottom-up TreeRNN with a top-down “feedbackward” pass to inject global, sentence-level context into all node representations. The aim is contextual classification (e.g., aspect-targeted sentiment in Twitter), where phrases may be neutral in isolation but polar in context. The model concatenates forward and backward node vectors for classification, introduces a learnable root transform V to initialize top-down states, and uses a “hybrid” input embedding (fixed unsupervised dimensions + small trainable supervised dimensions). Training uses backpropagation through structure with AdaGrad and dropout. The paper compares to standard TreeRNN and a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and claims to outperform SemEval 2013 Task 2 systems, with further boosts from external lexicons, large-scale weak supervision, and ensembling.

Relation to prior work
- Recursive neural networks and compositional semantics: Socher et al. (EMNLP’13, NIPS workshops), Pollack, etc.
- Bidirectional recursive models: Irsoy & Cardie; inside-outside/BI-RNN-tree variants (Le & Zuidema).
- Word vectors: LSA, GloVe, window-based embeddings, and task-specific fine-tuning.
The proposed model is close to existing bidirectional tree architectures; the main distinction is the specific top-down recurrence that conditions children ↓-states on both the forward parent and the parent ↓-state and the hybrid embedding scheme.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant motivation for context-sensitive, target-dependent sentiment (especially important for social media).
- Conceptually simple extension of recursive models to incorporate top-down context, avoiding fixed-size windows.
- Hybrid embedding (fixed unsupervised + trainable supervised) is practical and often strong in low-label settings.
- Attempts a comparison to bidirectional recursive baselines and to SemEval systems; uses dropout with tree models.
- Semi-supervised training pipeline leveraging weakly labeled data is sensible.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness/notation:
1) Dimensional inconsistencies abound:
   - V is said to be “V ∈ Vnd×n” (typo) and later used as p↓root = f(V proot). Should be V ∈ R^{nd×n}.
   - Eq. 3: [a↓ p↓1] = f(W↓ [p2 p↓2]) implies output dimension 2·nd from an input of (n + nd). Yet W↓ is stated as W↓ ∈ R^{(n+nd)×(n+nd)}, which is incompatible. Later block form suggests W↓ should be in R^{(2nd)×(n+nd)}; please fix and be consistent.
   - Classifier: ya = softmax(Wc [a a↓]) but Wc ∈ R^{C×(n+1)}; should be C×(n+nd+1).
2) Logical contradictions:
   - The text states “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” but Eq. 3 explicitly uses [p_forward, p↓_parent] as inputs to compute children ↓-states.
3) Comparison to Irsoy & Cardie:
   - Parameter count derivation is unclear; if your W↓ actually maps (n+nd)→(2nd), your parameter tally changes. Please provide a precise, dimensionally consistent derivation.
4) Training/evaluation:
   - You state “we train with mini-batched AdaGrad,” but do not specify initial learning rate, schedule, or batching scheme for variable-sized trees (node-level or tree-level batching?).
   - Dropout: applied to node vectors “with p=0.5 at each training step”—is the mask per-node, per-time, shared across forward/backward passes? Applied to embeddings, to hidden states, and/or to classifier?
   - You restrict the parser “to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree.” How is this enforced? Does this constraint modify the parser’s objective or post-process parses? Risk of target leakage or unnatural tree structures should be addressed.
5) Data/splits/metrics:
   - SemEval Task 2 counts/splits appear off; verify exact train/dev/test sizes and the SMS/tweet composition. The paper states three equal sets of 7862 with 10681 test; this does not match common descriptions. Please clarify.
   - You report relative improvements (“over 5%,” “+2%,” “+1%”) without absolute numbers, variance, or statistical tests. Tables referenced (Table 1–3, Fig. 2, Fig. 4) are missing, making it impossible to assess significance or reproducibility.
   - Comparing an ensemble (top-5 averaged) to single-model baselines is not apples-to-apples; provide single-model results, results without external resources, and significance tests.
6) Reproducibility:
   - Critical ablations are insufficient: need results for (a) forward-only RNN vs GB-RNN with identical embeddings; (b) GB-RNN with vs without hybrid embeddings; (c) GB-RNN with vs without extra lexicons/weak data; (d) single vs ensemble; (e) bidirectional baseline implemented with matched capacity and dimensionality.
   - No code, no hyperparameter details (regularization strengths, exact dims beyond one best config, epochs, early stopping criteria).
7) Clarity/formatting:
   - Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “feedbackward/feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “shold,” “root note,” “de-composition,” and inconsistent figure/table references (e.g., “Figure 3.4”).
   - Spurious text inserted in Section 3.6 (“Correct ... tonight ... Good luck on Monday ...”) appears to be raw tweets erroneously embedded within equations—this must be fixed.
   - References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues, stray commas), making it hard to verify prior work.
   - The footnote URL in the middle of text is disruptive; use proper footnote/endnote formatting.

Originality
- The top-down pass in tree structures and bidirectional recursive models are known; the paper does cite related work, but the novelty over inside-outside and bidirectional TreeRNNs is incremental (choice of conditioning children ↓-states on both forward and backward parent vectors). The hybrid embedding idea is also common in practice.

Significance
- Context-sensitive sentiment is important, and a clean, well-executed bidirectional tree model could be useful. However, due to missing/incorrect details, unclear fairness of comparisons, and lack of reliable numbers, it is difficult to assess the claimed SOTA improvements.

Pros
- Timely problem; sensible model idea; practical embedding strategy; use of weak supervision; attempts at comparison with prior bi-directional recursive models.

Cons
- Severe clarity and correctness issues (equations/dimensions/contradictions).
- Missing figures/tables and insufficient experimental detail prevent verification.
- Comparisons confounded by ensembles and external resources; lack of statistical rigor.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside-outside tree models.

Recommendation
Reject.

Confidence
High. The accumulation of technical and presentation issues prevents proper assessment despite a potentially useful idea. I encourage the authors to: (1) correct all mathematical inconsistencies; (2) provide complete experimental results with strong, fair baselines and significance tests; (3) clean up formatting/references; (4) add ablations and clarity on parsing constraints and training details; and (5) release code or sufficient pseudo-code for reproduction.
