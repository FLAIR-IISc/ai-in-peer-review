Summary
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a compositional model for contextual (phrase-specific) classification on parse trees. The key idea is to augment a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The top-down state at each node is computed from both the forward parent representation and the backward parent state; local predictions are then made from the concatenation of forward and backward node vectors. The model is applied to aspect-specific/contextual sentiment classification on SemEval 2013 Twitter data, with comparisons to a feedforward (bottom-up) RNN, windowed and recurrent baselines, and a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie). The paper also proposes hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with a small task-tuned component, and uses dropout and multi-stage training with auxiliary data. The authors report that GB-RNN outperforms the baselines, the bidirectional recursive model, and the SemEval winner; an ensemble of GB-RNNs yields the best results.

Relation to prior work
- Compositional/recursive models in NLP (Socher et al.; Pollack) provide the bottom-up framework; bidirectional RNNs (Schuster & Paliwal) and bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema) are closest. The paper’s backward computation differs by conditioning each child’s backward state on both the forward and backward parent, and by explicitly using the backward pass at inference to update local beliefs.
- Hybrid embeddings and task-specific fine-tuning build on Collobert et al. and unsupervised word vectors (e.g., GloVe/Mikolov).
- The “global belief” framing is related in spirit to message passing/belief propagation but instantiated here as a learned top-down pass on trees.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and ambition: a simple, general mechanism to let global sentence context inform local predictions on trees. Using the top-down pass at inference time is a clean, impactful idea likely to generalize beyond sentiment (e.g., WSD, coreference cues, structured tagging on trees).
- Clear architectural contrast with prior bidirectional-recursive models; the design choice to condition on both forward and backward parent is plausible and easy to implement.
- Practical contributions: hybrid word vectors with fixed unsupervised + small supervised tails; dropout at node vectors; two-stage training with noisy supervision. These will be useful to practitioners.
- Empirical claim of non-trivial gains over a strong feedforward RNN baseline (>5% absolute) and over prior competition systems, with both single model and ensemble reports.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/notation issues hinder reproducibility:
  - Several typos and inconsistencies (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard”), and dimensionalities (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n; W↓ sized as (n+nd)×(n+nd) but used to map parent to two children) are hard to follow.
  - Eq. 3 suggests the backward child uses the forward parent (good), but later text says “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” which contradicts Eq. 3. Please clarify.
  - Figures/tables are referenced but not shown here; many critical details (absolute F1/accuracy numbers, variance, dev/test breakdown, SMS vs tweet) are missing.
- Experimental scope and fairness:
  - Evaluation is limited to SemEval Twitter sentiment (plus SMS). No tests on other tree-structured tasks to demonstrate generality.
  - Comparisons rely on additional resources (noisy tweets, lexicon) and an ensemble; while allowed, the fair single-model, same-resource comparison versus bidirectional-recursive baselines is crucial. Parameter-matched comparisons (same nd and total param count) are not reported.
  - No statistical significance tests or reporting of variability across random seeds.
- Methodological details:
  - Ignoring loss for unlabeled internal nodes may limit the benefit of the backward pass; an alternative is weak supervision or hierarchical losses.
  - Parser constraints to force labeled spans to be subtrees may bias evaluation; how does the method handle arbitrary spans at test time?
  - Computational cost and runtime implications of the extra pass and larger parameterization are not discussed.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the paper needs to resolve notational inconsistencies, specify dimensions, and provide comprehensive experimental details (metrics, seeds, hyperparameters, parameter counts, compute). The ablation story is promising but incomplete without numbers in this draft.

Clarity
Readable overall with good motivation, but marred by multiple typos, ambiguous notation, and missing quantitative details/tables. Improving the equations, providing a schematic of forward/backward flows with dimensions, and adding a training/inference algorithm box would help.

Originality
Moderately high. While closely related to bidirectional recursive models, using a learned top-down pass at inference that conditions on both forward and backward parents, and framing it as “global belief” updating on trees, is a valuable and timely idea. The hybrid embeddings are incremental but useful.

Significance
Likely to influence follow-on work in tree-structured NLP. If gains over strong baselines hold under fair, parameter-matched conditions, the approach advances contextual classification on trees. The idea should transfer to other tasks.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide exact dev/test metrics (avg F1 for pos/neg and accuracy), with standard deviations over multiple runs, for: logistic, NB, windowed, RNN, bidirectional-recursive (Irsoy & Cardie), GB-RNN single, GB-RNN ensemble; and with/without external lexicon/noisy data.
- Report parameter counts and runtime for all models; include a parameter-matched comparison to the bidirectional-recursive baseline.
- Clarify Eq. 3 vs the statement about not using forward vectors to compute backward states; fix dimensions for V and W↓ and explain how [parent_fwd, parent_bwd] is mapped to two child backward vectors.
- How sensitive are results to nd and to using only the backward parent (ablation)?
- What happens without enforcing subtree constraints from the parser? Can you classify arbitrary spans?
- Any error analysis on aspect-targeted cases where context flips the sentiment?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and broadly applicable idea (top-down inference-time pass on trees).
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines and competition systems.
- Practical training tricks (hybrid embeddings, dropout) that improve robustness.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity issues and inconsistent equations.
- Incomplete experimental reporting; limited datasets; fairness/parameter matching not demonstrated.
- Heavy reliance on extra data and ensembles in headline results.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core idea is simple, elegant, and potentially impactful, and the empirical gains (as claimed) are encouraging. With clearer presentation and more rigorous, parameter-matched comparisons, this could be a strong contribution. I am inclined to reward the novelty and direction even if some validation details are incomplete. Confidence: medium.
