This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to capture context-dependent meaning in phrases and sentences for tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The core idea is to augment a standard, feedforward (bottom-up) recursive neural network with a "feedbackward" (top-down) pass. The forward pass computes compositional vector representations for phrases in the parse tree, while the backward pass propagates global sentence context from the root of the tree back down to the leaves. For any given phrase, the final prediction is based on the concatenation of its forward and backward vector representations. The paper also proposes a hybrid word vector representation, combining fixed, pre-trained unsupervised vectors with smaller, task-specific vectors that are learned during training. The authors evaluate the GB-RNN on a SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment analysis task, demonstrating superior performance over standard recursive networks, various baselines, and the original competition winner. The work is closely related to bidirectional recursive neural networks developed in parallel [12], but proposes a different formulation for the backward pass.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the need for context-aware models using a clear example ("The Android’s screen is better than the iPhone’s"), a scenario where standard feedforward recursive models fail.
2.  **Intuitive Model:** The proposed two-pass architecture is an intuitive and direct approach to incorporating global context into local representations.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model demonstrates a substantial improvement (+5% F1) over the standard recursive network baseline and outperforms highly tuned systems from the SemEval 2013 competition. This provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed architecture on this task.

**Weaknesses:**

While the core idea is appealing and the results are promising, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and technical description.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Missing Figures:** The paper repeatedly refers to Figures 1, 2, 3.4, and 4 to illustrate the core motivation, model architecture, vector representations, and qualitative results. None of these figures are present in the manuscript, which severely hinders the reader's ability to understand the work. This is a major oversight.
    *   **Confusing Terminology:** The use of the acronym "RNN" for *recursive* neural networks is highly unconventional and prone to confusion with the far more common *recurrent* neural networks. While clarified in the text, this choice of nomenclature is unnecessarily ambiguous.
    *   **Formatting and Referencing:** The reference list is in a state of disarray. Many entries are incomplete, lacking venue, year, or publisher information (e.g., [5], [7], [9], [12]). Some references are malformed (e.g., [1], [4]). A URL is provided in a footnote instead of a proper citation [17]. This lack of scholarly diligence is concerning.
    *   **Minor Typos:** Several typographical errors are present (e.g., "feedwordard", "shold", "disentangled" instead of "disentangle").

2.  **Technical Soundness and Inconsistencies:**
    *   **Incorrect Matrix Dimensions:** There appear to be critical errors in the description of the model's mathematical details.
        *   In Section 3.3, the de-composition matrix `W↓` is claimed to have dimensions `R(n+nd)×(n+nd)`. However, based on Equation (3), the input is of size `n+nd` while the output is of size `nd+nd = 2nd`. Therefore, `W↓` should have dimensions `R^(2nd × (n+nd))`.
        *   Similarly, the classifier weights `Wc` are stated to be `RC×(n+1)`. The input to the softmax is a concatenation of the forward vector `a` (dim `n`) and backward vector `a↓` (dim `nd`), for a total dimension of `n+nd`. A bias can be added, but the stated dimension for the weights is incorrect.
    *   **Flawed Parameter Comparison:** The comparison of the number of parameters with the bidirectional-RNN of Irsoy and Cardie [12] in Section 3.6 seems incorrect. The paper's own derivation and explanation of the parameter counts for both its model and the competing model are confusing (e.g., using non-standard notation like `2n2d`) and appear to miscalculate the parameters for the model in [12]. This undermines the analysis of the models' relative complexity and the justification for the proposed architectural choices.
    *   **Unclear Training Procedure:** The two-stage training process, where a standard recursive model is trained on large, noisily labeled datasets before the full GB-RNN is trained on the smaller target dataset, is a critical methodological detail for reproducibility. However, it is mentioned only briefly in Section 4.4 and lacks specifics. For instance, it is unclear which parameters are frozen or fine-tuned during the second stage.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis:**
    *   The primary novel contribution over [12] is the specific formulation of the backward pass. However, the paper lacks a detailed ablation study to justify its design choices. For example, what is the impact of removing the forward parent's representation (`p1`) from the input to the backward computation (in Eq. 5)? A more thorough analysis is needed to understand which components of the model contribute to its improved performance.
    *   The claim that the model's applicability is "more general" and can be helpful for tasks like word-sense disambiguation is asserted without any supporting experiments or analysis.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper presents a promising direction for compositional models and achieves strong results, the manuscript in its current form falls well below the standards of quality and clarity expected at NIPS. The missing figures, inconsistent technical descriptions, flawed mathematical analysis, and poor reference formatting constitute a substantial number of flaws that obscure the contribution. The authors have a potentially valuable contribution, but the paper requires a major revision to address these serious issues of technical rigor and presentation before it can be considered for publication.
