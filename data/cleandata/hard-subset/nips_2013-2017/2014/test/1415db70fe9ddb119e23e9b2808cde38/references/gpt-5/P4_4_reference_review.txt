Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments standard feedforward recursive neural networks with a top-down “feedbackward” pass. The forward pass computes standard compositional vectors bottom-up over a binary parse tree. The backward pass starts at the root, transforms the root vector via a matrix V, and recursively computes downward vectors for each node using both the forward and backward parent representations. Final predictions at each node/word are made by a classifier over the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The model targets context-dependent labeling (e.g., aspect-specific sentiment) where local phrase polarity depends on sentence-level context.

The paper positions GB-RNN against: (i) prior recursive models for compositional semantics and sentiment (e.g., Socher et al. 2012–2013), which are purely bottom-up; (ii) bidirectional recurrent architectures; and (iii) bidirectional recursive models developed contemporaneously (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), where information flows both up and down trees. The proposed difference relative to bidirectional recursive models is that the downward child computations incorporate both the forward and backward parent states, with an additional transformation at the root, and slightly more parameters. The paper also proposes “hybrid” word embeddings: concatenating fixed, unsupervised pretrained dimensions with a small supervised, trainable segment. Experiments are conducted on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), using constituency parses and subtree constraints to align labeled terms.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Addresses a real limitation of purely bottom-up compositional models for contextual/aspect sentiment, where the correct label depends on information outside the local subtree.
- Architectural simplicity: The top-down pass is straightforward to add to an existing RNN over trees and is trained with backpropagation through structure.
- Intended generality: The idea of passing global context downward is potentially applicable beyond sentiment (e.g., disambiguation).
- Practical modeling choices: Hybrid embeddings and dropout on node vectors are plausible, and the two-stage training (pretrain bottom-up then full model) is sensible given the available data.
- Comparisons outlined: The paper attempts to contrast its formulation with existing bidirectional recursive models and claims empirical improvements over feedforward RNNs, bidirectional RNN variants, and SemEval systems.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Missing quantitative detail: The paper repeatedly reports improvements (“over 5%,” “+2%,” “+1%”) but does not provide actual F1/accuracy numbers, standard deviations, or statistical significance. Tables are referenced but not included in the text provided. This makes it impossible to assess the strength or robustness of the claims.
- Clarity and notation issues: There are several typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., “feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “V ∈ Vnd×n,” “de-composition”), and apparent dimensional inconsistencies. For instance, downward node vectors are stated to be nd-dimensional, but equations suggest outputs like [b↓ c↓] while W↓ is described as square of size (n+nd)×(n+nd). This appears inconsistent with producing 2·nd outputs from an (n+nd)-dimensional input; shapes should be stated precisely for reproducibility.
- Incremental novelty: The core idea—computing both inside (upward) and outside (downward) representations on trees—is established (e.g., inside-outside RNN, bidirectional recursive models). The proposed differences (using both forward and backward parent states; an additional V at the root; slightly more parameters) read as moderate variations. The paper would benefit from a controlled comparison under matched parameter budgets and clearer theoretical justification for the specific downward parameterization.
- Experimental fairness and scope: Best results rely on an ensemble of five models and external resources (NRC lexicon, large noisy datasets). It is unclear how single-model performance without external data compares to strong SemEval systems and to bidirectional recursive baselines trained under identical conditions. No runtime/complexity analysis of the extra pass is provided.
- Dataset and preprocessing clarity: The paper states train/dev/dev-test splits with phrase-level labels for SemEval; more detail is needed on whether this is Task A (term-level) vs Task B (message-level), label distribution, and how the “constraint to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree” is implemented. Tweet parsing is error-prone; there is no analysis of parser errors’ impact.
- Limited analysis: No detailed error analysis, ablations with exact numbers (e.g., effect of hybrid vs fully fine-tuned embeddings, dropout, two-stage training), or significance testing are provided.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Specify exact dimensionalities and correct equations for the downward pass. If [child_left↓; child_right↓] ∈ R2nd and the input [parent_forward; parent_backward] ∈ R(n+nd), then W↓ should be in R(2nd)×(n+nd). Similarly clarify the block matrices and their shapes.
- Clearly define the loss: which nodes are supervised, how unlabeled nodes are treated, and whether class weights or hierarchical supervision are used. Report the number of labeled nodes per tree on average.
- Provide full experimental details: per-class F1 and macro/micro averages on dev and test; single-model vs ensemble; with and without external resources; comparisons to bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema) under matched parameter counts and identical embeddings.
- Report computational cost: training/inference time per sentence and memory footprint relative to feedforward RNNs.
- Clarify the parsing constraint: how subtrees are enforced to contain the labeled term, and whether this introduces annotation leakage or artifacts.
- Consider a non-parser baseline (sequence models with attention or bidirectional RNNs) with large contexts to quantify the added value of tree structure on noisy Twitter text.
- Justify the term “global belief”: there is no explicit probabilistic belief propagation; consider rephrasing to avoid confusion with CRF-style inference.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation (context dependence) in tree-based compositional sentiment.
- Simple, potentially general architectural idea with plausible benefits.
- If the claimed performance gains over strong baselines hold under fair settings, the approach is practically useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete and unclear empirical evidence; key numbers and statistical rigor are missing.
- Notational and dimensional inconsistencies undermine reproducibility and technical clarity.
- Novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models appears modest without stronger theoretical or empirical support.
- Heavy reliance on ensembling and external resources obscures the core model’s contribution.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The direction is relevant and potentially useful, but the current presentation lacks the necessary clarity and quantitative evidence to assess technical soundness and significance. I encourage the authors to fix the dimensional inconsistencies, present complete and fair experimental results (with single-model, matched-parameter comparisons and statistical tests), and expand analysis to strengthen the case.
