Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference and training time. The goal is to let each word/phrase representation be informed by global sentence context, thereby enabling contextual (e.g., target/phrase-specific) sentiment classification. Concretely, the model computes forward node vectors using a standard composition matrix W; then, starting at the root, it computes top-down vectors via a learned root transform V and a decomposition matrix W↓ that conditions each child’s ↓-vector on both the forward parent and the backward parent. Final classification at each node uses the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The paper also introduces a simple hybrid word representation that concatenates fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small, task-tuned vector; dropout is applied to node vectors.

The work is positioned against: (a) feedforward RNNs for compositional sentiment (Socher et al., 2013 EMNLP), (b) bidirectional (inside–outside) recursive models developed contemporaneously (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), and (c) window-based/bidirectional recurrent models that are limited in capturing long-distance tree-structured dependencies. The empirical evaluation centers on SemEval-2013 Task 2 Twitter sentiment (term-in-context appears to be the primary subtask), where GB-RNN is claimed to outperform both a standard RNN and several competition systems, including the winner, with further gains from ensemble averaging and additional external data.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The forward and backward recursions are clearly specified, with a principled top-down propagation that uses both forward and backward parent information. The difference from prior bidirectional RvNNs is made explicit via equations, and the parameterization is justified (more expressive W↓; different dimensionality for ↓-vectors via V).
- Empirical support: The paper reports outperforming baselines (including a ~5% absolute gain over forward-only RNN), benefits from the hybrid embeddings, dropout, and extra training data (+2% from lexicon, +1% from joint training). However, critical details are missing or under-specified in the current draft:
  - Exact scores (F1/accuracy) per dataset split and per system are not shown; the referenced tables are not present here. Statistical significance is not discussed.
  - The comparison to bidirectional RvNN is asserted but lacks numerical outcomes in the text provided (beyond stating GB-RNN outperforms it).
  - The best competition result uses an ensemble of top 5 GB-RNNs; single-model vs ensemble comparisons should be disentangled for fair attribution to the architecture.
  - The model relies on constituency parses of noisy tweets; there is no quantitative analysis of parsing robustness or its effect on performance.
  - Training details are partially given (optimizer, minibatch size, activation, dropout), but key hyperparameters (the ↓-vector dimension nd; learning-rate/adagrad settings; initialization ranges for all matrices) and runtime/complexity are not reported. Reproducibility would benefit from more specifics.
- Completeness: The analysis includes ablations (hybrid vs pure unsupervised vs pure supervised embeddings) and data augmentation effects. An error analysis or qualitative study focusing on long-range context and aspect disambiguation would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, with a helpful conceptual framing (global belief). However, the draft contains several typos and notation issues (e.g., “feedbackward/ feedwordard,” dimensions for V given as “Vnd×n,” “root note”), and missing figures/tables hinder understanding. Clarify whether the evaluation is on SemEval Task 2 Subtask A (term-level) vs B (message-level), and specify the official evaluation metric (they mention average F1 over pos/neg, but not the numeric results).
- The description “we constrained the parser to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree” needs more detail to ensure this constraint does not bias the evaluation.

Originality:
- The idea of top-down context propagation in tree models is known (inside–outside style recursive nets), and bidirectional recursive approaches appeared in parallel. The specific GB-RNN backward parameterization that conditions on both forward and backward parent states, and the design choice to not use forward child vectors in the backward recursion (relying on the classifier to combine forward/backward) are modestly novel and well-motivated.
- The hybrid embedding approach (frozen unsupervised + small trainable task-specific tail) is simple but practical; similar ideas exist, but the ablation is useful evidence of its value here.

Significance:
- If the claimed improvements over strong SemEval systems and over a bidirectional RvNN hold with clear margins for single models, the contribution is meaningful for contextual/targeted sentiment and potentially for other tree-structured token labeling tasks. The model’s ability to leverage sentence-level context for local decisions is of broad interest.
- Scope is somewhat narrow (Twitter sentiment), and reliance on parsers may limit applicability in noisy domains. Demonstrating transfers to other datasets (e.g., aspect-based sentiment benchmarks or phrase-level datasets beyond SemEval-2013) would increase impact.

Pros:
- Clear motivation for incorporating global sentence context into local predictions.
- Principled top-down recursion with an explicit comparison to prior bidirectional RvNNs.
- Empirical gains over feedforward RNN and competition baselines; sensible ablations (hybrid embeddings, dropout, extra data).
- Method likely generalizable to other tree-structured tasks.

Cons:
- Missing concrete quantitative results and significance tests in the current draft; reliance on ensembling clouds attribution.
- Notational/typographical issues and missing figure/table references reduce clarity.
- Limited analysis of parser dependence and robustness on noisy text.
- Incomplete hyperparameter and training details limit reproducibility.
- Novelty is incremental relative to concurrent bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide full tables with single-model and ensemble results, per subtask and per domain (tweets vs SMS), with statistical significance.
- Report ablations controlling for parameter count when comparing to Irsoy & Cardie and other bidirectional RvNNs; vary nd and analyze trade-offs.
- Clarify the SemEval subtask, metric, and the “subtree constraint” procedure; add error analysis for long-range and aspect disambiguation cases.
- Include training details (learning rates, schedules, nd, initialization, epochs), runtime/memory, and parsing accuracy or robustness checks.

Overall assessment:
Borderline. The idea is sensible and likely useful, and the qualitative/ablation evidence suggests a real gain. However, to meet NeurIPS standards on quality and clarity, the paper needs to present complete quantitative results, tighten the comparisons to closely related bidirectional recursive work, and improve clarity/reproducibility. With these addressed, I would lean toward acceptance; as-is, I lean weak reject.
