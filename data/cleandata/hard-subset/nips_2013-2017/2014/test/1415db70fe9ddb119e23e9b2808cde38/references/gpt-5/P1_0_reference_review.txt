Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments standard bottom-up Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference/training time. The backward pass propagates information from the root to all nodes so that each word/phrase prediction can condition on global sentence context. This is motivated by contextual/aspect-specific sentiment, where local phrases may be neutral or ambiguous absent context. The model is evaluated primarily on SemEval-2013 Task 2 Twitter sentiment (subtask A, term-level contextual polarity within tweets), claiming to outperform both a standard RNN and the competition winner; the best system is an ensemble of 5 GB-RNNs. The paper also proposes “hybrid” word vectors: concatenating fixed unsupervised embeddings with a smaller, trainable supervised segment, and uses dropout on node vectors.

The work relates to: (i) compositional distributional semantics and recursive models (Socher et al., EMNLP’13; EMNLP’11; NIPS DL workshop), (ii) bidirectional recurrent/recursive models (Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie, contemporaneous), (iii) sentiment work on Twitter and bag-of-words baselines, and (iv) pre-trained embedding use (word2vec/GloVe) and fine-tuning. The main difference from prior recursive work is the inclusion of a top-down pass that conditions local predictions on global sentence-level evidence, contrasted with prior feedforward-only tree composition. Compared to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive models, the authors argue their backward computation uses both forward and backward parent vectors with a single decomposition matrix, providing richer context.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model definition is mostly clear: forward pass uses a standard composition p = f(W [left; right]); backward pass initializes at the root with p↓ = f(V p) and recurses downward via a decomposition matrix W↓ to produce child ↓ vectors, which are concatenated with forward vectors for classification. Hybrid word embeddings and dropout are reasonable and known to help. However, there are several notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “V ∈ Vnd×n”) and some contradictions in the comparison section: Eq. (3) shows the backward child depends on both the forward parent p and backward parent p↓, but later text says “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” which conflicts with Eq. (3). Parameter count derivations for W↓ variants could be clarified with precise dimensions.
- Empirical support: The paper claims substantial improvements over a feedforward RNN (~5% absolute) and over the SemEval winner, with 2% from adding NRC lexicon instances and 1% from two-stage training (pretraining on noisy and movie review data, then fine-tuning on SemEval). These attributions are useful, but the paper lacks the tables/plots with exact numbers in the text we see, and ablations are incomplete: no explicit ablation for V vs copying root, for using/omitting forward parent in the backward recursion, for nd dimension, or for dropout effects on different components. Fairness is partly muddied by the use of ensembles for the best result and external resources; it is good that single-model comparisons are provided, but the exact numbers and statistical significance are not shown here.
- Methodological concerns: The approach depends on a constituency parse for tweets; parsing noisy tweets is challenging. The authors “constrained the parser to keep each labeled phrase inside its own subtree.” This is a strong constraint that may bias evaluations relative to natural parsing; more detail is needed on how this constraint is implemented and whether it is permissible at test time (in SemEval subtask A the target term is given, so some constraints are legitimate, but the impact on model behavior should be analyzed). Runtime/complexity and training stability are not discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with motivating examples and clear high-level descriptions. However, clarity is hampered by:
- Multiple typos and minor notation errors.
- Missing or inconsistent details in the model comparison to Irsoy & Cardie; please unify notation and clearly show which signals each backward variant uses.
- Experimental section would benefit from explicit tables with mean/std, development vs test, and whether ensembles or external resources are used in each row; also specify which SemEval subtasks (A vs B) and metrics are reported.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of propagating information top-down in tree networks is timely and (per citations) concurrent with bidirectional recursive models. The specific backward computation and the hybrid fixed+trainable word vectors are plausible contributions. The “global belief” framing gestures toward structured inference/belief propagation, though the method remains a deterministic top-down pass rather than an explicit probabilistic inference scheme.

Significance
Moderate-to-strong for NLP practitioners interested in context-sensitive composition and aspect-level sentiment. The performance gains on a prominent benchmark and a simple, general architectural modification are likely to be adopted and extended. Broader impact beyond Twitter sentiment is plausible (e.g., WSD, entity-centric tasks), but additional datasets would strengthen claims.

Pros
- Clear motivation for contextual/target-dependent sentiment and why a top-down pass can help.
- Simple architectural change that is easy to implement on top of TreeRNNs.
- Sensible use of hybrid embeddings and dropout.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines and competition systems; useful analysis of external resource contributions.

Cons
- Notational inconsistencies and a key contradiction about whether forward parents are used in the backward pass.
- Limited ablations: lack of evidence isolating the benefit of the backward pass from ensembling/external data; no analysis of V vs identity, nd sensitivity, or computational overhead.
- Reliance on constrained parsing for tweets is under-specified and may affect generality.
- Comparison to other strong contemporaneous baselines (e.g., windowed/bidirectional RNNs over tokens, TreeLSTM) is limited; the discussion of Irsoy & Cardie needs tightening.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify the exact backward equations and resolve the contradiction regarding use of forward parent vectors. Provide an ablation where the backward child depends only on p↓ vs on (p, p↓).
- Report full quantitative results (dev/test, single vs ensemble, with/without external resources), and significance tests.
- Provide ablations for: copying root vs V, different nd, dropout application sites, and removing hybrid embeddings.
- Describe the parser constraint precisely, and analyze sensitivity to parse errors (e.g., performance vs parsing quality).
- Report training/inference time and parameter counts for all models.
- Consider evaluating on another contextual sentiment dataset (e.g., later SemEval aspect-based tasks) to demonstrate generality.
- Discuss relations to belief propagation/CRFs more concretely if invoking “global belief”.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning accept). The idea is simple and useful, and the empirical gains on a well-known benchmark are promising. Strengthening clarity, ablations, and fairness of comparisons would make this a solid accept. My confidence is moderate.
