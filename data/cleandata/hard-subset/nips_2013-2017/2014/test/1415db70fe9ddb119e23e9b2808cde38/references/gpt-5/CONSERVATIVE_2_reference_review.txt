Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured architecture that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass to inject global sentence-level context into local (word/phrase) predictions. The model concatenates bottom-up and top-down vectors for classification at each node. A hybrid word embedding is used: fixed, unsupervised vectors (e.g., GloVe-like) concatenated with small, task-tuned supervised dimensions, with dropout regularization. The target application is contextual/targeted sentiment on SemEval-2013 Task 2 Twitter data, and the paper reports improvements over a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and competition systems, including an ensemble of GB-RNNs. The paper relates to inside-outside/bidirectional recursion (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema) and argues its top-down computation uses both forward and backward parent states.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: target-/context-dependent sentiment cannot be resolved from local phrases alone.
- Conceptually simple extension of RNNs: a top-down pass that is easy to implement once a tree is available; the hybrid embedding idea is pragmatic and likely broadly useful.
- Uses established training machinery (BPTS, AdaGrad, dropout), and compares to a bidirectional recursive variant.
- Empirical results suggest gains over a feedforward RNN and competition systems on SemEval-2013, and some ablations (hybrid vs. purely supervised/unsupervised embeddings).

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Clarity and internal consistency: The paper alternates between “feedback at inference time only” and training both passes jointly; equations and dimensions contain typos (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n; shapes of W↓ not consistently specified; “feedbackward” vs. “backward”). Figures/tables with critical numbers are not included; several claims are unverifiable.
- Methodological rigor: The GB-RNN has more parameters than the compared bidirectional model; no capacity-matched controls or parameter normalization are reported. No statistical significance testing. No runtime/compute analysis.
- Baselines: The paper omits strong, established baselines for target-dependent sentiment, e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression with n-grams + target-aware features + sentiment lexicons, and flat sequence models (CRF or BiRNN) with wide context windows. The logistic regression and Naive Bayes baselines ignore surrounding context (unfair to the task).
- Fairness of comparisons: The best results use (i) an ensemble of 5 models and (ii) substantial external resources (NRC lexicon entries as labeled training examples; 176k weakly labeled tweets; movie reviews). It is unclear whether competition systems used comparable resources; the internal baselines are trained with the same hybrid embeddings but it is not clear whether they also received the same external supervision. A clean comparison without extra data and without ensembling is needed.
- Task/data clarity: The description of SemEval-2013 splits (train/dev/dev-test each 7,862) and what is evaluated (tweet-level vs. target/term-level) is unclear. The parser is forced to keep each labeled phrase as a subtree—this constraint could bias results and does not reflect realistic parsing; no analysis of parser errors on noisy tweets.
- Missing or underspecified ablations: No systematic study of nd (top-down dimension), removal of V, removal of the backward pass, or depth/sentence length effects. No error analysis.
- Terminology: “Global belief” suggests a probabilistic/structured inference interpretation, but the model is a deterministic top-down pass; no principled belief propagation objective is given.

Quality
The idea is plausible and technically implementable, but the empirical methodology is not sufficiently rigorous. Critical comparisons are confounded by extra supervision and increased capacity; statistical testing is absent; key implementation details and exact metrics are missing. Several notational errors and ambiguous statements make reproduction difficult.

Clarity
The writing is generally readable, but the paper suffers from typos, inconsistent notation/dimensions, and absent tables/figures. The training/inference pipeline for the backward pass needs a precise algorithmic description. The description of datasets, splits, and evaluation (tweet vs. SMS; message vs. term) needs to be much clearer.

Originality
Top-down information in tree models is not new; inside-outside/bidirectional recursive models and related structures exist (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema). The primary novelty is a particular backward computation that uses both forward and backward parent states, plus the hybrid embedding. This is a relatively incremental architectural variation.

Significance
Context-aware sentiment is important, and if validated carefully, the approach could be useful. However, given the confounds and missing details, the current evidence is insufficient to conclude that GB-RNN advances the state of the art beyond capacity/ensemble gains and additional supervision.

Questions for the authors
- Is the top-down pass used during training, inference, or both? Please provide exact forward/backward algorithms, dimensions, and parameter shapes.
- Provide full results tables: micro/macro metrics, per-class F1, with/without external data, single model vs. ensemble, with significance tests.
- Control for capacity: match parameter counts vs. bidirectional recursive baselines and report results.
- Compare against strong target-dependent sentiment baselines with lexicons and target features; include context-window baselines with large k.
- Provide analysis of parser robustness on tweets and impact of enforcing labeled phrases as subtrees.
- Release code/settings or provide enough detail to reproduce.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: context-sensitive local labeling.
- Simple, broadly applicable architectural idea.
- Promising empirical gains (if validated under fair settings).
- Hybrid embedding and dropout observations may be of practical interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficiently rigorous experimental validation; confounded comparisons.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues; missing numbers/tables; notation errors.
- Incremental novelty over existing bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models.
- Heavy reliance on external data and ensembling without clean baselines.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High
