

# Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), an extension of standard recursive neural networks that incorporates a backward pass to allow context-dependent sentiment classification. The authors claim this approach enables models to correctly identify context-specific sentiment (e.g., recognizing that "Android" is positively described in "The Android's screen is better than the iPhone's" while "iPhone" is not). The paper compares GB-RNN to standard RNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and other baselines on the SemEval 2013 dataset.

## Quality

The paper suffers from several technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility:

1. **Model Description Inconsistencies**: In Section 3.3, the backward computation equations (3) are inconsistent with the described top-down propagation. The equations suggest that backward vectors depend on both forward and backward parent vectors, but the description states the backward pass "starts at the root node and propagates all the way down to the single word vectors" without clear dependency specification. Equation (3) shows [a↓ p↓1] = f(W↓[p2 p↓2]), but this implies p↓1 depends on p↓2 (the root's backward vector), which contradicts the described top-down propagation order.

2. **Mathematical Errors**: In Section 3.6, the authors state "Our model also has n · nd more parameters to compute the feedbackward vectors than the bidirectional-RNN." However, their own parameter count calculation shows the GB-RNN has n·nd more parameters, not n·nd. This is a critical mathematical error that affects the paper's technical credibility.

3. **Missing Figure Reference**: The paper references "Figure 3.4" in Section 3.4, but no such figure appears in the manuscript. This is a clear formatting error that indicates sloppy preparation.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The paper claims the GB-RNN "outperforms" baselines by 5% (Table 2), but provides no statistical significance tests to support this claim. The marginal improvements (5% at best) without significance testing make the results questionable.

5. **Arbitrary Hyperparameter Choices**: The paper states "vector sizes of 130 dimensions (100 pre-trained, fixed word vectors and 30 trained on sentiment data)" without justification for these specific numbers. This lack of explanation for critical design choices undermines the paper's technical rigor.

## Clarity

The paper has several clarity issues:

1. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "feedbackward" (with a 'w') throughout, while the standard term is "backward." This inconsistency creates confusion.

2. **Ambiguous Notation**: In Section 3.6, the bidirectional RNN's Equation (4) uses "Vb" and "Vc" without defining what these symbols represent. Are they vectors or matrices? The paper doesn't clarify.

3. **Confusing Explanations**: The claim that "our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector" contradicts Equation (3), which explicitly uses the forward parent vector (p2) to compute the backward vector (p↓1).

4. **Typos**: Section 3.3 states "This can also be interpreted as unfolding the tree and moving up its branches" when it should say "moving down its branches" for a top-down computation.

## Originality

The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from prior work:

1. **Missing Key Citations**: The paper fails to cite the seminal work "Recursive Deep Models for Semantic Compositionality over a Sentiment Treebank" by Socher et al. (2011), which is foundational to recursive neural networks in sentiment analysis. This omission is significant.

2. **Inadequate Differentiation**: While the paper acknowledges bidirectional recursive networks [12, 14], it doesn't clearly explain how the GB-RNN differs in a meaningful way from these models beyond the mathematical differences in their backward pass equations.

3. **Marginal Novelty**: The proposed approach is a minor modification to existing recursive network architectures. The paper doesn't convincingly argue that this small change represents a significant advancement over prior work.

## Significance

The paper's significance is limited by:

1. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The reported improvements over the standard RNN (5% in Table 2) are modest, and the paper doesn't demonstrate why such a small improvement is practically significant for real-world sentiment analysis applications.

2. **Narrow Application Scope**: The experiments are limited to a single sentiment analysis task on a single dataset (SemEval 2013 Task 2), with no analysis of how broadly applicable the approach is to other NLP tasks.

3. **Lack of Broader Impact Discussion**: The paper claims the approach "can be helpful for a variety of NLP tasks," but provides no evidence or analysis beyond sentiment analysis.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses a legitimate problem in context-dependent sentiment analysis, the technical inconsistencies, mathematical errors, and lack of rigorous justification for key design choices significantly undermine its contribution. The marginal performance gains without statistical significance testing, combined with the technical flaws, make it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from prior work, and the lack of clear novelty, coupled with the technical flaws, makes it unsuitable for publication at NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical inconsistencies and lack of sufficient justification for the proposed model's design choices. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address the mathematical errors, clarify the model description, provide statistical significance testing, and properly contextualize the work against prior research.
