Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) for contextual (target-dependent) sentiment analysis. Building on standard tree-structured recursive neural networks (RNNs), the model augments the usual bottom-up composition with a top-down “feedbackward” pass: a vector at the root is transformed and recursively propagated to children, yielding at each node the concatenation of a bottom-up (forward) and top-down (backward) representation used for local classification. The authors also propose hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with small, task-tuned supervised components. On SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment data, GB-RNN is reported to outperform a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and competition systems, with additional gains from lexicon-derived training data, emoticon-labeled tweets, and ensembling.

Relation to prior work
The paper is grounded in compositional distributional semantics and tree-structured neural models (Socher et al.), unsupervised word vectors (e.g., GloVe), and sentiment analysis on Twitter. The top-down pass is closely related to bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside RNN), as well as classical ideas of passing context from parent to children. Compared to Irsoy & Cardie, the authors emphasize using both forward and backward parent vectors to compute children’s backward states, and allowing different dimensionality via a V transform. The “global belief” terminology evokes belief propagation, though the model remains a deterministic top-down neural pass rather than probabilistic message passing.

Strengths
- Addresses a salient limitation of purely bottom-up compositional models by injecting top-down, global context, which is crucial for target-dependent sentiment.
- Simple extension to existing tree-RNNs: reuses the parse structure and adds a top-down pass; conceptually clear and computationally plausible.
- Useful hybrid embedding idea (fixed unsupervised + small supervised tail) to balance generalization and task-specificity; practical and likely broadly applicable.
- Empirical narrative suggests consistent improvements over a feedforward tree RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and SemEval systems; ablations suggest additional benefits from extra data (lexicons, distant supervision) and two-stage training.
- Training with dropout on node vectors and use of AdaGrad are sensible; attention to regularization and hyperparameter tuning.

Weaknesses
- Experimental reporting is incomplete. Tables are referenced but not provided; absolute scores (F1/accuracy), standard deviations, and significance tests are missing. Claims like “over 5% better” and “outperforms the winners” cannot be verified or contextualized without numbers.
- Dataset description is unclear/inaccurate. The repeated “7862” counts for train/dev/dev-test are suspect; SemEval 2013 had multiple subtasks (message-level, term-level), and the paper should clearly state which subtask(s) are used, label distributions, and evaluation metrics. The presence of SMS in the final test also needs clarification for the chosen subtask.
- Comparisons to strong alternatives are limited. Beyond Irsoy & Cardie and a feedforward tree RNN, there is no comparison to sequence BiLSTMs (with/without attention), which are highly competitive on noisy tweets, or to more recent tree models (e.g., tree-LSTM). The claim that recurrent/window methods “cannot incorporate information many words away” is overstated; while difficult, BiLSTMs can and often do capture long-range dependencies.
- Clarity and notation issues: dimensions and block structure of W↓ are confusing; Eq. (3) uses a single matrix to output two children but shapes are not consistent. The term “feedbackward” is unconventional. Several typos (“shold”), and incomplete references. The “belief” framing suggests probabilistic inference, but the model is a deterministic top-down pass; the terminology may mislead.
- Heavy reliance on external resources and ensembling without isolating core contributions. Gains from NRC lexicons, distant supervision, two-stage training, and averaging five models may confound the benefit attributable specifically to the top-down pass.
- Parsing robustness on tweets is not addressed. For noisy, informal text, tree quality can be poor; a sequence model baseline might be more appropriate. No analysis of parse errors or their impact.

Quality
The methodological contribution is technically reasonable, but the presentation has gaps (ambiguous equations, missing metrics) that prevent thorough verification. The empirical section lacks sufficient detail to assess the magnitude and robustness of improvements. Ablations are mentioned but not quantified.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but several sections need tightening. Please fix equation dimensions and explicitly define the block structure of W↓, the role of V, and the exact feature concatenations used in the classifier. Clarify training supervision (which nodes are labeled; how unlabeled nodes are handled), dropout application, and hyperparameter ranges. Clean up typos and complete references.

Originality
Moderate. A top-down contextual pass in tree-structured models has been explored (Irsoy & Cardie; inside-outside RNNs). Using both forward and backward parent vectors and allowing different backward dimensionality is a small but reasonable variant. The hybrid embedding idea is practical but not entirely novel.

Significance
Potentially useful for target-dependent sentiment and other token/phrase labeling tasks where global context matters. However, without clear, quantified gains over strong baselines and on multiple datasets, the broader impact is difficult to judge.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide full numerical results (per-class F1, macro/micro averaging, accuracy), with dev/test splits, including the exact SemEval subtask(s). Include standard deviations and significance tests.
- Disentangle contributions: report single-model results with/without top-down pass, with/without hybrid embeddings, with/without extra data, and with/without ensembling.
- Specify the exact form of W↓ (block matrices), all tensor shapes, and how two child vectors are produced.
- Compare to sequence BiLSTM baselines (with attention or conditioning on the target span), which do not rely on parses, and discuss parsing quality on tweets.
- Provide efficiency analysis (parameters, time per sentence) and discuss the trade-off of added parameters (n·nd).
- Clarify whether backward vectors are used for all nodes or only those with labels; how are targets specified and supervised?

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important limitation of tree-RNNs (lack of top-down context) with a simple architectural idea.
- Promising qualitative motivation and reported gains for target-dependent sentiment.
- Practical embedding trick likely to interest practitioners.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Insufficient experimental detail and missing quantitative results prevent rigorous assessment.
- Limited and potentially weak baseline comparisons; no modern sequence baselines.
- Clarity issues in model specification; terminology potentially misleading.
- Heavy reliance on external resources and ensembling obscures core contribution.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is sensible and could be impactful, but the paper needs clearer, complete empirical evidence and a tighter, more precise presentation to meet NeurIPS standards. With a thorough revision addressing the issues above, this work could be much stronger.
