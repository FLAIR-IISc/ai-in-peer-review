Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural architecture that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference/training time. The top-down pass produces a context-dependent representation for each node by conditioning on global sentence information propagated from the root. The final classification for any span uses the concatenation of its bottom-up and top-down vectors. The motivating application is contextual (target-dependent) sentiment classification, evaluated on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (term-level sentiment in context). The paper also introduces a “hybrid” word embedding: a frozen unsupervised part concatenated with a small supervised trainable part.

The work sits within a line of tree-structured neural models for compositional semantics (e.g., Socher et al., EMNLP 2013; Pollack 1990), competing with sequence models (Collobert et al., 2011) and window methods. It is closely related to bidirectional/inside-outside recursive architectures (Le and Zuidema, inside-outside RNN; Irsoy and Cardie, bidirectional recursive nets), which also propagate information from root to leaves. Compared to those, the main claimed novelty is a different parameterization of the top-down computation that uses both the parent’s forward and backward representations to compute each child’s backward representation, and the hybrid embedding trick. The authors report improvements over feedforward RNNs, bidirectional recursive baselines, and SemEval-2013 winners.

Strengths
- Addresses an important limitation of feedforward compositional models: context-dependent interpretation of spans (relevant to SemEval 2013 Task 2A).
- Simple, plausible architectural idea: add a top-down pass along the parse tree to inject global sentence information into local span predictions; reminiscent of inside-outside inference.
- Empirical focus on tweets, with realistic noise; use of external data and distant supervision is practical.
- Hybrid embeddings (frozen unsupervised + small supervised channel) are a pragmatic contribution; the use of dropout on node vectors is well motivated.
- Inclusion of comparisons to several baselines and to bidirectional recursive nets is appropriate in scope.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)
Quality:
- The technical exposition has inconsistencies and probable errors:
  - Eq. (3) uses both the forward parent p and the backward parent p↓ to compute child backward vectors, yet later the text claims “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector.” These statements conflict; please clarify which variant is implemented.
  - Dimensionalities are inconsistent. Example: Wc ∈ R^{C×(n+1)} for classifying [a; a↓] should instead match C × (n+nd [+1 if bias]). There is also a typo “V ∈ Vnd×n”.
  - The paper contains stray text fragments (e.g., “Correct ... tweets” lines embedded in Section 3.6), which harms readability and suggests possible copy-paste issues.
- Empirical reporting lacks sufficient detail to assess significance and reproducibility:
  - No concrete numbers (F1/accuracy) are shown for comparisons vs RNN, bidirectional recursive baselines, or SemEval-2013 top systems; only statements like “over 5%” are given. Exact scores, standard deviations over multiple runs, and statistical tests are needed.
  - It is not clear whether the comparison to SemEval winners is in exactly the same evaluation setting and with comparable external resources.
  - The bidirectional baseline’s parameterization (Eq. 4) and training details should be fully specified; otherwise differences may be due to implementation choices.
- The model’s dependence on parsing tweets is nontrivial; there is no analysis of parse quality, robustness, or the impact of the constraint that each labeled span must be a subtree (how enforced? does it bias structures at test time?).
- Scalability/efficiency are not discussed; the top-down pass doubles per-tree computation and adds parameters.

Clarity:
- Overall organization is reasonable, but the paper needs careful proofreading, consistent notation, and corrected dimensionalities. Some sections (3.6) are hard to follow due to formatting and inline blocks of unrelated text.
- The training regimen (pretrain forward-only RNN on large data, then train GB-RNN on SemEval) needs clarification: are forward parameters frozen or fine-tuned in stage 2? Is the top-down pass trained jointly with the forward pass?

Originality:
- The core idea is very close to existing inside-outside/bidirectional recursive neural models (Le & Zuidema; Irsoy & Cardie). The specific use of both forward and backward parent vectors to compute child backward states is a modest architectural tweak; the paper should more clearly articulate why this is theoretically advantageous and ablate it against simpler variants.
- The hybrid embedding idea has been explored in spirit in multi-channel embeddings (e.g., contemporaneous work such as static vs fine-tuned channels); situating this contribution in that context would help.

Significance:
- If the reported gains over prior state-of-the-art are substantial and robust, the approach could be useful for targeted sentiment and other contextual labeling tasks. However, the current presentation lacks the quantitative evidence and careful baselining needed to establish clear SOTA advancement.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real shortcoming of tree RNNs with a simple, general mechanism.
- Potentially strong empirical gains on a competitive benchmark.
- Practical embedding/training tricks that others can adopt.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty over existing bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models appears incremental; theoretical contribution is limited.
- Clarity and correctness issues in equations and notation.
- Insufficient quantitative detail and reproducibility; unclear fairness of comparisons.
- No analysis of parsing robustness or error cases; limited exploration beyond a single dataset/task.

Suggestions for improvement
- Resolve technical inconsistencies: precisely define the backward computation, ensure dimensions match, and correct typos/formatting.
- Provide full experimental results: exact F1/accuracy for dev/test, confidence intervals, and significance tests; report with and without external resources; include per-class F1 and micro/macro averages as in SemEval.
- Strengthen baselines and ablations:
  - Compare to Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside RNN and Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive net with matched training.
  - Ablate the use of both parent vectors in the backward step vs using only forward or only backward parent.
  - Ablate hybrid embeddings, dropout, and the two-stage training to isolate contributions.
  - Include a strong sequence baseline (biLSTM/CRF or biRNN with attention over spans) using the same embeddings.
- Analyze the role of syntax: measure sensitivity to parse errors; compare to sequence-structured variants; report results on auto-parsed vs gold (if available).
- Extend evaluation to other contextual sentiment tasks (e.g., SemEval 2014 Task 4 aspect-based) to demonstrate generality.
- Release code and pretrained models to aid reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is promising and likely useful, but the paper in its current form has clarity issues, thin novelty relative to closely related models, and insufficiently detailed empirical evidence. With clearer exposition, stronger and more transparent experiments, and a tighter positioning relative to prior bidirectional/inside-outside recursive work, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
