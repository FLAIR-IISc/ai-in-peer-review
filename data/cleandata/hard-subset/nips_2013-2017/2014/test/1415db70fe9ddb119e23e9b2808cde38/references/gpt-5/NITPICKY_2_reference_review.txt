Summary
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down (“feedbackward”) pass. Each node has a forward (inside) vector and a backward (outside) vector; final predictions for words/phrases use the concatenation of both. The backward vectors are computed recursively from parent forward and backward vectors, starting from a root transform. The model is combined with hybrid word embeddings (a frozen unsupervised part concatenated with a small trainable task-specific part) and dropout. The target application is contextual/targeted sentiment in Twitter; the authors claim GB-RNN outperforms a purely feedforward RNN, a bidirectional-recursive baseline, and the SemEval-2013 Task 2 winner, with additional gains from external lexica and noisily labeled tweets plus an ensemble.

Relation to prior work: The model is very close in spirit to inside–outside recursive neural networks and bidirectional recursive models (Le & Zuidema; Irsoy & Cardie), as well as Socher-style recursive composition models. The hybrid embeddings idea is related to fine-tuning vs freezing pre-trained vectors (Collobert et al.; GloVe), though the exact concatenation scheme is a simple variant.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using sentence-global context to disambiguate local phrase labels.
- Using outside information in a tree is a well-motivated design (inside–outside style), and combining inside/outside for classification is reasonable.
- Hybrid embeddings (fixed unsupervised + small trainable tail) with dropout is a pragmatic idea that often works in practice.
- Training recipe (BPTS + AdaGrad + dropout) is standard and sensible.
- Claims of improvement over a purely bottom-up recursive model are plausible.

Weaknesses (many minor but cumulatively substantial; several major)
Methodological/technical
- Contradictory statements: Section 3.6 asserts “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” but Eq. 3 explicitly uses the forward parent p1 (and p2) in computing child backward vectors. Please reconcile.
- Dimensionality and notation are error-prone: W↓ is described as R(n+nd)×(n+nd), but in Eq. 3 it must map (n+nd) to 2·nd, then split into two nd blocks; as written it maps to (n+nd). Shapes should be made explicit and consistent.
- The role of V at the root is under-justified; if V is used to shrink dimensionality, please state exact dims and how this affects W↓ block structure.
- Training: “unlabeled nodes do not add an additional error” — then how are backward parameters learned for internal nodes? Clarify gradient flow and supervision strategy when many nodes lack labels.
- No complexity/runtime analysis; no discussion of parse errors and their effect on the outside pass; no analysis of stability vs tree depth.

Novelty
- The proposed top-down pass is very close to existing bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models (Le & Zuidema; Irsoy & Cardie). The claimed difference (using both forward and backward parent vs a specific parameterization in [12]) is minor and not convincingly argued to be essential. A principled comparison (same parameter budget, same dims, same data) and clearer theoretical framing are needed.

Experimental design/reporting
- Key quantitative evidence is missing. Tables 1–3 are referenced but not present; there are no actual numbers for accuracy/F1, standard deviations, or significance. Claims of “over 5%,” “+2%,” “+1%” are unsubstantiated.
- The paper intermixes targeted/contextual sentiment motivation (e.g., “Android vs iPhone”) with evaluation on SemEval-2013 Task 2, which is message-level sentiment (not targeted). The text also states that “each labeled example is represented by a single node,” implying phrase-level labels that do not exist in that task. This mismatch is serious; please specify exactly which subtask/dataset is used and how labels align to tree nodes.
- External resources: The system uses NRC lexicon entries as additional labeled examples, 176k noisily labeled tweets, and SST movie reviews, plus an ensemble of five models. This confounds claims of model superiority vs both baselines and SemEval systems. A controlled comparison without external data and without ensembling is necessary.
- Baselines are underspecified: the “bidirectional-RNN” configuration, dims, and training schedule are not detailed; no fairness controls for parameter count are reported.
- No ablation numbers for the hybrid embeddings, dropout placement, or the V transform.

Clarity/formatting
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “feedbackward/ feedwordard,” “root note,” “shold,” “de-composition,” and inconsistent use of “RNN” to mean “recursive” vs “recurrent.”
- Broken references and figures: “Figure 3.4” inside Section 3.4; missing tables; incomplete references lacking years/venues; “Glove” misspelled; several citations malformed.
- Random extraneous tweet lines appear inside Section 3.6 (“Correct FUSION’s 5th General Meeting…”), presumably a copy-paste error.
- Dataset statistics and splits do not match known SemEval-2013 sizes; please verify.

Reproducibility
- Insufficient detail to reproduce: missing exact dataset variant, preprocessing (handling of @mentions, URLs, emojis), vocabulary size, initialization, stopping criteria, and exact numeric results. Code/data availability not discussed.

Pros for acceptance
- Good, intuitive idea of using global (outside) context for local predictions on trees.
- Hybrid embedding trick is practical and might interest practitioners.
- If numbers truly surpass strong baselines, the result would be useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty over prior inside–outside and bidirectional recursive models is thin and not rigorously demonstrated.
- Major clarity and formatting issues; missing tables; contradictory statements.
- Experimental section is incomplete and potentially mismatched to the stated problem (targeted vs message-level sentiment).
- Heavy reliance on external data and ensembling without controlled comparisons.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide the exact metrics and numbers (mean ± std) for all models in Tables 1–3; include ablations and controls without external data and without ensembling.
2. Clarify the dataset/task: which SemEval subtask? How are labels aligned to tree nodes?
3. Resolve the contradiction about whether backward computations use forward parent vectors.
4. Specify all dimensions and shapes for W, V, W↓ (block structure), and classifier layers.
5. Describe training details: loss over which nodes, batch construction, stopping, learning-rate schedules, parameter counts per model.
6. Provide a fair comparison to Irsoy & Cardie and Le & Zuidema with equal parameter budgets.

Evaluation
- Quality: Weak due to missing quantitative evidence and technical inconsistencies.
- Clarity: Below the bar; numerous typos, broken references, missing tables/figures.
- Originality: Limited over prior bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models.
- Significance: Potentially moderate if properly demonstrated, but current evidence is insufficient.

Recommendation: Reject. The core idea is reasonable, but the manuscript has too many clarity/formatting issues, contradictory statements, and incomplete experimental reporting, and the novelty over prior work is not convincingly established. I encourage the authors to clean up the presentation, run controlled experiments with complete results, and more carefully situate the contribution relative to existing inside–outside/bidirectional recursive models.
