### Summary

This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a novel architecture for modeling compositional semantics that incorporates global sentence context. The key idea is to augment the standard bottom-up, feedforward pass of a Recursive Neural Network (RNN) with a top-down, "feedbackward" pass. After computing initial phrase representations in a bottom-up fashion, the model propagates information from the root of the parse tree (representing the whole sentence) back down to the leaves. This allows the representation and classification of each word and phrase to be conditioned on the full context of the utterance. The final prediction for any given node is made using a concatenation of its feedforward and feedbackward vectors. The authors also propose a hybrid word vector representation, combining pre-trained, fixed vectors with smaller, task-specific trainable vectors. The model is evaluated on a challenging contextual sentiment analysis task from SemEval 2013, where it achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming the competition winner and several strong baselines, including a standard RNN and a similar bidirectional-recursive model.

### Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and novel core idea. The standard paradigm for recursive models in NLP at the time was purely feedforward and bottom-up. This work astutely identifies a fundamental limitation of this approach: the inability to model how global context refines the meaning of local constituents. The introduction of a top-down, feedbackward pass is a principled and elegant solution to this problem. It represents a significant conceptual leap, moving beyond simple context windows towards a structured, syntax-aware mechanism for contextualization. This is precisely the kind of work that can open up new research directions.

2.  **Addresses a Fundamental NLP Problem:** The problem of context-dependence is central to language understanding. The motivating example—"The Android’s screen is better than the iPhone’s"—perfectly illustrates why context-independent compositional models are insufficient for many real-world tasks like aspect-based sentiment analysis. By tackling this head-on, the paper addresses a difficult and important problem, with potential applications far beyond sentiment analysis (e.g., word-sense disambiguation, co-reference resolution).

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The novel idea is not merely speculative; it is backed by strong empirical results on a competitive public benchmark. The GB-RNN significantly outperforms a standard feedforward RNN (a >5% improvement), demonstrating the clear practical benefit of the feedbackward pass. Furthermore, it surpasses the winning system of the SemEval 2013 competition, which relied on extensive feature engineering. This shows that the learned representations can capture complex contextual phenomena more effectively than hand-crafted features.

4.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper is clearly written and well-motivated. The core mechanism of the GB-RNN is explained well, aided by diagrams and a step-by-step walkthrough. The authors also do a commendable job of relating their model to concurrent work on bidirectional recursive models, clearly delineating the architectural differences and providing a direct empirical comparison.

### Weaknesses (Potential Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Limited Justification for Specific Architectural Choices:** While the overall idea of a feedbackward pass is well-motivated, the justification for the specific mathematical formulation of the top-down update (Eq. 5) is somewhat brief. The paper contrasts its formulation with that of Irsoy and Cardie [12] and intuits that its choice "helps to push the model to disentangled the children from their backward parent vector." This intuition, while plausible, could be further elaborated or analyzed. While the empirical results support the chosen design, a more in-depth discussion of the architectural trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Dependence on External Parser:** The model, like other recursive neural networks, relies on a pre-computed syntactic parse tree. The quality of the final representations is therefore dependent on the quality of the parser, which can be brittle, especially on noisy text like tweets. The paper does not discuss the model's sensitivity to parsing errors.

3.  **Analysis Could Be Deeper:** The analysis is primarily quantitative. While the examples in Figure 4 are illustrative, a more qualitative analysis would be welcome. For instance, an inspection of the feedforward (`p`) versus the final combined (`[p; p_down]`) representations for the same phrase could provide valuable insight into what kind of contextual information the backward pass is capturing. An analysis of failure cases would also be instructive.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of deep learning for NLP. The introduction of the global belief propagation mechanism via a feedbackward pass on a tree structure is a bold and powerful idea that directly addresses a core weakness of existing compositional models. While the methodological justification for some specific design choices could be deeper and the analysis more thorough, these are minor weaknesses in the face of the conceptual advance and the excellent empirical results. The paper is well-written, the experiments are convincing, and the proposed direction is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. It prioritizes a novel concept over perfecting an existing one, and successfully demonstrates the value of that concept. This is exactly the kind of innovative research that NIPS should champion.
