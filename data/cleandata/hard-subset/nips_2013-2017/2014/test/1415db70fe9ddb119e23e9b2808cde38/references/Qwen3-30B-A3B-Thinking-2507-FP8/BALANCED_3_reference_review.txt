

# Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

## Summary
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), an extension of standard recursive neural networks that incorporates a feedbackward step at inference time to allow context-dependent phrase representation. The authors argue that standard RNNs cannot capture context-dependent sentiment (e.g., identifying that "Android" is the subject of positive sentiment in "The Android's screen is better than the iPhone's"), while GB-RNN updates phrase representations based on global context. The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it claims to outperform standard RNNs and other baselines.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLP: context-dependent sentiment analysis, which is important for real-world applications like social media analysis.
2. The model is clearly described with appropriate mathematical formulations and includes a thorough comparison with related work, particularly the bidirectional RNN approach by Irsoy and Cardie (2014).
3. The experimental section provides a well-structured evaluation with ablation studies showing the contribution of different components.
4. The hybrid word vector approach (combining unsupervised and supervised vectors) is a reasonable and practical solution to the word representation problem.
5. The paper makes a reasonable effort to address the limitations of previous approaches and provides clear motivation for the proposed model.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient novelty**: The core idea of the feedbackward step is extremely similar to the bidirectional recursive neural networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2014), which was presented at the NIPS Deep Learning Workshop. The theoretical comparison in Section 3.6 is inadequate to establish a clear distinction. The paper claims their model "makes uses of both the forward and backward parent node" while Irsoy and Cardie's model uses "forward parent and independent on its position," but this distinction is not clearly explained or justified. This significantly undermines the paper's novelty claim.

2. **Lack of implementation detail**: The paper mentions using "vector sizes of 130 dimensions (100 pre-trained, fixed word vectors and 30 trained on sentiment data)" but provides no justification for these specific numbers. The hyperparameter search appears superficial, with no evidence of systematic exploration of the parameter space.

3. **Evaluation concerns**: The paper uses additional training data (NRC-Canada lexicon and the authors' own dataset) which could give an unfair advantage over other systems in the SemEval competition. The paper doesn't clearly explain how this additional data was incorporated in a way that's comparable to other competition participants, potentially inflating the reported performance gains.

4. **Limited scope**: While the authors mention potential applications beyond sentiment analysis (e.g., word-sense disambiguation), they provide no evidence for this broader applicability. The paper remains narrowly focused on sentiment analysis without demonstrating the model's general utility.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty relative to prior work, particularly the bidirectional RNN approach presented at the NIPS Deep Learning Workshop (2014). While the paper is well-structured and addresses a meaningful problem, the contribution does not represent a significant advance beyond existing work.

## Constructive Suggestions
1. The authors should provide a much more thorough theoretical and empirical comparison with Irsoy and Cardie's work, clearly demonstrating how their model differs and why it should perform better. If the difference is minimal, they should acknowledge this and discuss the implications.

2. The paper should include a detailed explanation of the implementation choices, including a systematic hyperparameter search that justifies the selected dimensions and other parameters.

3. The evaluation should be re-done without the additional training data (or at least clearly report how this data was used compared to other systems) to ensure a fair comparison.

4. To strengthen the significance claim, the authors should demonstrate the model's applicability to at least one additional NLP task beyond sentiment analysis, providing evidence that the context-dependent representation is useful for broader NLP applications.

5. The paper should more clearly articulate the specific advantages of the feedbackward step over existing contextual approaches (e.g., window-based methods, bidirectional RNNs) in a way that justifies the novelty claim.

This is a solid technical contribution to sentiment analysis, but the lack of clear distinction from prior work and insufficient evidence of broader applicability prevent it from meeting NIPS's high standards for novelty and significance.
