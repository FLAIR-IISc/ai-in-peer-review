Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass to inject sentence-level context into local (word/phrase) predictions. The backward vectors are computed from the root downward, then concatenated with forward vectors at each node for classification. The paper also proposes hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings concatenated with a small supervised, trainable segment) and applies dropout at node vectors. The main application is contextual sentiment classification on SemEval 2013 Twitter data. The authors claim that GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive model, and strong SemEval systems (with an ensemble of 5 GB-RNNs).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on recursive neural networks for compositional semantics (Socher et al.; Pollack), sentiment (Socher et al. 2013), and bidirectional recurrent/recursive architectures (Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema). The top-down pass is reminiscent of inside–outside style computations. The hybrid embedding idea relates to using pretrained word vectors with task-specific fine-tuning.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: local sentiment can be context-dependent (aspect-targeted sentiment).
- Architectural idea is simple and practically useful: a top-down pass to supply global context to local classifiers.
- Hybrid word vectors are sensible and likely helpful in low-resource supervision regimes.
- Empirical scope includes baselines and ablations (window-based, feedforward RNN, bidirectional recursive model), and use of additional noisy supervision.
- Practical training details (AdaGrad, dropout, minibatching) and use of external data/lexicons are described at a high level.

Major weaknesses and technical issues
Unfortunately, the paper suffers from numerous inconsistencies and omissions that undermine clarity, correctness, and reproducibility.

Model/notation inconsistencies and likely errors:
- Dimensions for W↓ and outputs in Eq. 3 are inconsistent. You define W↓ ∈ R(n+nd)×(n+nd), but [a↓ p↓1] comprises 2nd elements; the RHS therefore cannot match LHS unless W↓ outputs 2nd dimensions. Later, you block-decompose W↓ into four blocks of shapes nd×n and nd×nd, implying W↓ ∈ R(2nd)×(n+nd), contradicting the earlier definition.
- V ∈ Vnd×n in Eq. 2 appears to be a typographical error; presumably V ∈ Rnd×n.
- The classifier weight matrix Wc is given as Wc ∈ RC×(n+1), but the feature is [a a↓] of dimension n+nd (plus bias). This is a dimensionality mismatch; likely Wc ∈ RC×(n+nd+1).
- Conceptual contradiction: The text states “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” yet Eq. 3 uses [p1 p↓1] to compute [b↓ c↓], and Eq. 2 uses p2 to compute p↓2. This needs reconciliation.
- Terminology alternates among “feedbackward/feedbackward,” “backward,” “down-arrow,” etc., obscuring the algorithmic steps.

Comparison to bidirectional recursive models:
- The derivation comparing Eqs. 4 and 5 is hard to follow and relies on dimensions that are inconsistent elsewhere. The claim that your model “does not use the forward node vector” for the backward computation is contradicted by your own equations. The stated parameter count advantage (n·nd extra) also depends on the correct dimensionalities, which are unclear.

Experimental reporting gaps:
- The paper repeatedly claims to outperform baselines and competition winners, but numerical results, variances, and statistical significance are absent in the provided text. The competition used average F1 over pos/neg; elsewhere you report “accuracy” for dev model selection—metrics are inconsistent.
- The strongest numbers appear to be from an ensemble of 5 GB-RNNs, but comparisons to competition systems should be against a single model as well, for fairness.
- No details on the bidirectional recursive baseline’s implementation choices (nd, regularization) are provided; unclear if hyperparameters were matched across models.
- No runtime, memory, or parameter count (with actual n, nd) is provided; complexity and scalability unclear.

Data and evaluation concerns:
- You “constrained the parser to keep each phrase labeled by the dataset inside its own subtree,” which risks leaking supervision into the input structure and may not reflect realistic deployment (where target spans are not given). Please clarify how this constraint is enforced at test time without using labels.
- Additional data sources (NRC lexicon entries, 176k “noisily labeled” tweets, and movie reviews) are used. There is no discussion of potential overlap with SemEval test data or domain shift handling. How are noisy labels integrated (pretraining only vs joint)? How is class prior mismatch handled?
- The task’s official metric is F1; using accuracy for model selection can bias toward the majority class and is not aligned with the evaluation.

Ablations and analysis:
- The “global” nature of the context is asserted but not demonstrated with controlled analyses (e.g., varying distance between target and sentiment cue, compositional scope, negation). A window-based baseline with sufficiently large windows or hierarchical CNN/LSTM baselines could be stronger.
- No sensitivity analysis for nd (backward vector size), effect of V vs identity, or removing backward pass while retaining hybrid embeddings/dropout.
- The “belief” terminology suggests probabilistic semantics or iterative refinement; the model performs a single deterministic top-down pass with no formal belief propagation or consistency constraints.

Presentation and formatting issues:
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues: “feedbackward,” “root note,” “shold,” “feedwordard,” “de-composition,” “independent on its position,” etc.
- Figure references and numbering are broken (Fig. 1/2/3.4) and figures are not present. “Figure 3.4” suggests section.figure numbering but is used as a figure id.
- Stray lines appear mid-section (e.g., “Correct FUSION’s 5th General Meeting...”), likely copy-paste artifacts from the dataset that interrupt Eq. 4—this must be cleaned up.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues), inconsistent (e.g., “Glove”), and sometimes malformed (“Kozareva P. Nakov”).

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all dimensionalities and provide a clear, self-consistent specification (pseudocode) of the forward/backward passes, including shapes and parameter counts. Clarify the role of p (forward) in computing p↓.
- Report full quantitative results: per-metric numbers (macro F1 consistent with SemEval scoring), standard deviations over runs, significance tests, and a fair single-model comparison to competition systems.
- Provide stronger baselines and analyses: large-window baselines, bidirectional chain RNNs, tree models with top-down signals; controlled tests for long-distance context.
- Justify and evaluate the parser constraint; report performance without it. Discuss robustness to parsing errors on tweets.
- Clean manuscript: remove stray lines, fix typos, consistent figure numbering, complete references.
- Clarify training regimen with external data and ensure no leakage; detail hyperparameter ranges and final settings; consider releasing code/models.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (contextual sentiment).
- Simple, potentially impactful architectural idea.
- Sensible hybrid embeddings and practical training heuristics.

Cons
- Significant technical inconsistencies and unclear model specification.
- Incomplete and sometimes inconsistent experimental reporting and metrics.
- Fairness concerns (ensemble vs single), parser constraints, and potential data leakage.
- Presentation quality issues and malformed references.

Recommendation: Reject
While the core idea is interesting and plausibly useful, the accumulation of technical inconsistencies, missing details, formatting problems, and insufficiently rigorous evaluation prevents acceptance at this time.
