This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to incorporate global sentence context into the representations of words and phrases for classification tasks. The central idea is to augment the standard bottom-up, feedforward computation of a Recursive Neural Network (RNN) with a top-down, "feedbackward" pass. This second pass propagates information from the root of the sentence's parse tree back down to the leaves, allowing each node's representation to be influenced by the entire sentence. The final representation for any given phrase is a concatenation of its feedforward and feedbackward vectors. The authors motivate this approach with the task of aspect-specific sentiment analysis, where the sentiment of a phrase (e.g., "The Android's screen") depends on its surrounding context (e.g., "...is better than the iPhone's"). The proposed model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it is shown to outperform the standard RNN, other baselines, and the winner of the original competition.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly identifies a significant limitation of standard feedforward compositional models. The inability to model context-dependent meaning is a well-known challenge, and the aspect-based sentiment example is a compelling illustration of this problem.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Standard Benchmark:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 Task 2 dataset, outperforming not only a standard RNN baseline by a significant margin (>5%) but also the winning systems of the competition. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach for this specific task.
3.  **Thoughtful Ablations:** The authors provide useful ablation studies. The comparison against the standard RNN effectively isolates the benefit of the backward pass. The experiments in Table 3 also validate their proposed hybrid word vector representation scheme, showing it outperforms purely supervised or unsupervised alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines for Context Modeling:** The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of comparison to more established and powerful methods for context modeling. While the comparison to a feedforward RNN is necessary, it is not sufficient. Bidirectional recurrent neural networks, particularly LSTMs (BiLSTMs), are the standard and highly effective tool for capturing context from both directions in a sequence. The paper dismisses recurrent networks with the brief claim that "the important context can be many words away," but this is precisely the problem LSTMs were designed to solve via their gating mechanisms. A rigorous evaluation requires a comparison against a strong BiLSTM baseline that operates on the word sequence. Without this, it is impossible to determine whether the proposed tree-based bidirectional architecture offers any real advantage over the simpler, more established sequential approach. The superior performance over a standard RNN mainly shows that incorporating context is beneficial, a point that is already well-established in the literature.
2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of a bidirectional, top-down and bottom-up pass on a tree structure is not entirely novel. The authors themselves cite concurrent work by Irsoy and Cardie [12] which proposes a very similar model. The architectural differences highlighted in Section 3.6 appear to be minor variations in the formulation of the backward pass. While the paper demonstrates superior empirical performance over this related model, the contribution feels more like an incremental improvement on an existing idea rather than a fundamentally new approach. The name "Global Belief" also seems to overstate the contribution, as the mechanism is a deterministic two-pass computation rather than a formal belief propagation algorithm.
3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on a single task: aspect-based sentiment on Twitter. While it performs well, the paper concludes by suggesting the idea is "more general" and could be helpful for tasks like word-sense disambiguation. Such claims require substantiation. To be convincing as a general-purpose NIPS contribution, the method should ideally be tested on a wider range of NLP tasks where context is crucial (e.g., semantic role labeling, named entity recognition) to demonstrate that its effectiveness is not confined to a single dataset or task formulation.
4.  **Confounding Factors in Experimental Results:** The comparison with the SemEval competition winner is complicated by the use of additional training data (NRC lexicon, noisily labeled tweets, movie reviews). While permissible by competition rules, it makes it difficult to attribute the performance gain solely to the proposed model architecture. A clearer comparison would involve training both the baseline and the proposed model on the exact same data splits. Furthermore, the paper introduces a "hybrid word vector" scheme and dropout simultaneously with the GB-RNN architecture. While the ablations are helpful, the main results are a combination of multiple novel components, slightly obscuring the precise contribution of the backward pass itself.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**For Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem in compositional semantics.
*   The proposed model is intuitive and presents a clear extension to the recursive neural network framework.
*   It achieves state-of-the-art performance on a recognized public benchmark.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   The evaluation is critically missing a comparison to standard, strong baselines for context modeling, most notably Bidirectional LSTMs. This is a significant methodological flaw.
*   The novelty of the approach is incremental when viewed in the context of concurrent work on bidirectional recursive models.
*   The experiments are limited to a single task, which undermines the claims of general applicability.
*   The comparison to the competition winner is confounded by the use of extensive external data, making it difficult to isolate the architectural contribution.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents an interesting idea and achieves strong results on one task, the methodological weaknesses, particularly the absence of a crucial BiLSTM baseline, are too significant for a NIPS paper. The claims of advancing the state of the art in context modeling are not rigorously supported without comparison to the most relevant and powerful established methods. Therefore, I recommend rejection. The work would be much stronger if the authors could demonstrate the superiority of their tree-based contextualization over standard sequential methods like BiLSTMs on a broader set of tasks.
