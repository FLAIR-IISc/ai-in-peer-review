

# Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a model that extends standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) by adding a "feedbackward" pass to incorporate global context into phrase representations. The authors claim this approach better handles contextual sentiment analysis, where the sentiment of a phrase depends on the global sentence context (e.g., "The Android's screen is better than the iPhone's" where "Android" gets positive sentiment but "iPhone" gets negative). The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it claims to outperform both standard RNNs and the competition winner.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine limitation in existing compositional models (lack of context dependence) and proposes a novel mechanism to address it. The idea of incorporating global context through a feedbackward pass is conceptually interesting, and the empirical results on the SemEval dataset show promising improvements over baselines. The hybrid word vector approach (combining unsupervised and supervised vectors) is a reasonable extension to the standard RNN framework.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Flaws
1. **Inconsistent Terminology and Definitions**: The paper uses both "feedbackward" and "backward" interchangeably, creating confusion. The term "feedbackward" is not standard and should be defined or replaced with "backward" for clarity.

2. **Contradictory Model Description**: In Section 3.3, the paper states "At the root node, the equation for x↓root could be replaced by simply copying x↓root = xroot. But there are two advantages of introducing a transform matrix V." However, Equation 2 explicitly uses V: p↓2 = f(V p2), creating a contradiction. The paper should either use V or not, but not vacillate between the two.

3. **Unclear Parameterization**: The paper claims to use "100 pre-trained, fixed word vectors and 30 trained on sentiment data" (total 130 dimensions), but doesn't explain how this 130-dimensional vector is used in the model. Is it concatenated with other vectors or processed further? This lack of detail undermines reproducibility.

4. **Misrepresentation of Related Work**: In Section 3.6, the paper claims to differ from Irsoy and Cardie's bidirectional RNN [12] by using "both the forward and backward parent node." However, the paper's Equation 5 shows their method is mathematically similar to the bidirectional approach but with more parameters (n·nd additional parameters), without justifying this trade-off.

5. **Inconsistent Context Handling Claim**: The paper claims "Unlike recurrent neural networks or window-based methods [5] the important context can be many words away from the phrase that is to be labeled." This is misleading - window-based methods can handle arbitrary context distance by simply increasing the window size.

### Presentation and Clarity Issues
1. **Missing Figures**: The paper references multiple figures (Figs. 1-4) but none are included in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the described examples.

2. **Typos and Grammatical Errors**: "as well the winner" (should be "as well as the"), "feedbackward" vs. "backward" inconsistency, and awkward phrasing throughout.

3. **Unjustified Claims**: The paper claims "The GB-RNN outperforms both the standard RNN and all other baselines, as well the winner of the Sentiment competition of SemEval 2013" but doesn't provide a direct comparison with the winning method's performance.

4. **Example Text in Paper**: The text "Correct FUSION's 5th General Meeting is tonight at 7 in ICS 213! Come out and carve pumpkins mid-quarter with us!" appears in the paper (Section 3.6) as an example, but this is clearly an error that should not be in a research paper.

5. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides no theoretical argument for why the specific feedbackward mechanism they propose is superior to alternative approaches for incorporating context.

### Experimental Concerns
1. **Insufficient Detail on Data Augmentation**: The paper mentions using "176,311 noisily labeled tweets" but doesn't explain the labeling procedure beyond "heuristics such as smiley faces," which is insufficient for reproducibility.

2. **Vague Hyperparameter Selection**: The paper states "optimal performance was achieved by using vector sizes of 130 dimensions..." but doesn't provide the exact hyperparameters used (learning rate, number of epochs, etc.) for the best model.

3. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: The paper claims the model "correctly classifies that in the sentence of Fig. 1, Android is described with positive sentiment and iOS was not" but provides no examples of the model's predictions to support this claim.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses a valid problem in context-dependent sentiment analysis and presents a novel mechanism for incorporating global context, the technical inconsistencies, presentation errors, and lack of sufficient detail significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The minor flaws identified (inconsistent terminology, contradictory model descriptions, grammatical errors, missing figures, and unjustified claims) collectively exceed the threshold for acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before reconsideration.

The paper is not sufficiently polished or technically precise to warrant acceptance. I recommend rejection.
