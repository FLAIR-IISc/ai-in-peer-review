Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) to address context-dependent labeling of phrases in sentences, with a focus on aspect-term sentiment in Twitter. Starting from a standard tree-structured RNN that computes bottom-up (feedforward) node representations, the method introduces a top-down “feedbackward” pass that propagates information from the root to the leaves. Each node’s final representation for classification concatenates the bottom-up and top-down vectors, enabling local predictions to be conditioned on global sentence context. The authors also introduce hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small supervised, task-tuned subvector, with dropout to regularize high-dimensional inputs. Experiments on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment) show improvements over a standard RNN, a bidirectional RNN variant, and several baselines; an ensemble of GB-RNNs reportedly outperforms the competition winner.

This work sits squarely in the line of compositional distributional semantics and recursive architectures (Socher et al., Pollack), and is closely related to bidirectional recurrent/recursive models (Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie) and inside-outside recursive models (Le & Zuidema). The proposed top-down pass resembles message passing in probabilistic models, but the paper keeps a neural formulation without explicit probabilistic semantics. The hybrid embedding idea builds on unsupervised word vectors (e.g., GloVe/word2vec) and task-specific fine-tuning practices from Collobert et al.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear motivation: context-dependent labeling (e.g., term-level sentiment in SemEval 2013) is not handled by purely bottom-up composition; a top-down signal is appropriate and simple to add to an RNN parser.
- The model is straightforward to implement: a learned transform at the root (V) and a shared decomposition matrix W↓ drive the top-down pass; concatenation with bottom-up vectors feeds a softmax at each node.
- The hybrid embedding + dropout recipe is practical and likely broadly useful; the two-step training (pretrain forward RNN on larger data, then train full GB-RNN) is sensible for low-resource contextual labels.
- Empirically, the model beats a standard RNN and a bidirectional-RNN baseline on SemEval-2013, with further gains from external lexica and noisy pretraining data; ensemble boosts are also reported.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Missing key experimental details and results. The paper claims >5% improvement over RNN and surpassing the competition winner, but does not report exact F1/accuracy numbers, standard deviations, or significance tests. The competition metric (average F1 for pos/neg) is mentioned, yet cross-validation and selection are done on accuracy—this mismatch should be justified and final test metrics reported consistently. It is unclear whether results are for Subtask B (term-level) vs A (message-level) and whether SMS data are included in training.
- Baseline coverage and fairness. The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive model is discussed theoretically, but precise implementation details and numerical comparisons are lacking. Closely related inside-outside RNNs (Le & Zuidema) should be compared empirically. Given the time frame, comparisons to RNTN/RNTN-like models (Socher et al. 2013) would be informative. The reported advantage over SemEval systems may be confounded by the use of large external resources and ensembling; single-model results with and without external data should be emphasized.
- Clarity/notation problems impede reproducibility. Dimensions and equations are inconsistent: e.g., V is written as “V ∈ Vnd×n”; W↓ is variously described as square and block-structured, but the shapes in Eq. 3 require W↓ ∈ R(2nd×(n+nd)). The “feedbackward/feedwordard” terminology and typos (“root note”) recur. The manuscript appears to include stray lines from examples (“Correct …”) in the model section. These issues make it hard to implement the model precisely.
- Analysis is limited. There is little ablation on the role of V, the size nd, the effect of the top-down pass depth, or sensitivity to parse quality. No runtime/memory complexity or training stability report (e.g., speed vs. standard RNN). The “global belief” name suggests a connection to belief propagation, but no formalization or analysis is provided.

Clarity
The high-level idea is understandable, and the figures (as described) help, but the paper needs substantial editing for notation consistency, removal of typographical errors, and a clearer description of the top-down computation graph and classifier interface. Provide a precise algorithmic description and tensor shapes.

Originality
Moderately original. The idea of top-down signals in recursive models is not new (bidirectional/inside-outside recursive nets), but the specific formulation that conditions child backward vectors on both forward and backward parent vectors is a reasonable variant. The hybrid embeddings proposal is a useful engineering tweak rather than a conceptual novelty.

Significance
Promising but currently limited by reporting and clarity. If the method consistently improves term-level sentiment with minimal feature engineering and strong single-model baselines, it would be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on structured neural models. The ideas could transfer to other token-in-context labeling tasks (WSD, coreference cues, semantic role disambiguation).

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated top-down augmentation to tree-RNNs for contextual labeling
- Strong empirical performance claims on a standard benchmark
- Practical hybrid embeddings and training recipe
- Minimal feature engineering

Cons
- Insufficiently detailed and inconsistent experimental reporting; unclear metrics and baselines
- Notation and typos hamper reproducibility
- Limited empirical comparison to closest prior models (inside-outside/bidirectional recursive)
- Confounds from external data and ensembling not fully disentangled

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Report exact scores (avg F1 pos/neg and accuracy) with confidence intervals for single models, with and without external data, and for ensembles. Specify which SemEval subtask(s) and the treatment of SMS data.
- Provide a clean, consistent specification of all equations with correct dimensions; include pseudocode of the forward and backward passes and the classifier usage per node. Clarify whether the softmax is applied at all nodes or only labeled ones during training; explain how unlabeled nodes are handled in loss and whether this biases learning.
- Compare empirically to inside-outside recursive nets and reproduce the Irsoy & Cardie variant; control for parameter count. Include RNTN or other strong tree baselines if feasible.
- Add ablations for V vs. identity, nd size, effect of dropout, and training schedule. Analyze performance vs. distance between target term and sentiment cue, and sensitivity to parse errors.
- Discuss computational complexity and training time relative to the baseline RNN.
- Clean up the manuscript (typos, stray lines, consistent notation). Consider renaming “feedbackward” to “top-down” or “backward” for clarity, and justify the “global belief” terminology or remove it.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sound and potentially useful, but the current manuscript lacks crucial experimental details, contains significant clarity/notation issues, and does not adequately compare to the most closely related models. With improved reporting, stronger and fairer baselines, and a cleaned-up presentation, this work could be competitive.
