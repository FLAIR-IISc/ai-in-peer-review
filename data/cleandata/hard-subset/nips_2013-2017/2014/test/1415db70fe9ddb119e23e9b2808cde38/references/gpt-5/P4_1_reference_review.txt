Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “global belief” pass. The forward pass computes compositionally derived vectors for phrases along a parse tree; a subsequent top-down pass produces “feedbackward” vectors at each node by conditioning on the parent’s forward and backward representations. Final node-level predictions (e.g., sentiment for a target span) use the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The motivation is to capture context-dependent semantics, notably for aspect- or target-specific sentiment, where a phrase’s polarity can depend on distant context within a sentence or tweet.

The model relates to and is compared with: (i) standard RNNs for compositional semantics [Socher et al., EMNLP 2013; related earlier recursive work], which are purely bottom-up and context-independent at the node level; (ii) bidirectional recurrent networks on sequences [Schuster & Paliwal] and bidirectional recursive models [Irsoy & Cardie], which also propagate information top-down but with a different parameterization of the backward pass; and (iii) inside–outside recursive representations for parsing [Le & Zuidema]. The paper also proposes a hybrid word embedding: a concatenation of fixed unsupervised vectors (e.g., GloVe-like) and a small supervised, trainable subvector, with dropout on node vectors.

Empirically, on SemEval-2013 Task 2A (tweet-level contextual phrase sentiment), GB-RNN outperforms a standard feedforward RNN and the bidirectional recursive baseline, and achieves or exceeds the competition winners, using an ensemble of GB-RNNs. Additional experiments analyze the contribution of hybrid embeddings, dropout, and auxiliary training on noisy tweets and lexicon-derived short phrases.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for context-dependent node labeling on trees, with concrete examples where bottom-up models fail.
- Simple, general mechanism for top-down conditioning that preserves compositional structure and allows long-range context, unlike fixed windows.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines (including bidirectional recursive models) on a standard benchmark; both single-model and ensemble results are reported.
- Hybrid embedding approach is practical, reduces overfitting, and is supported by ablation indicating benefits over fully fixed or fully trainable embeddings.
- Training strategy (pretrain bottom-up on large context-independent data, then fine-tune GB-RNN on context-specific labels) is reasonable and aligns with the task.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/notation: There are several typographical and dimensional inconsistencies (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n should be R^{nd×n}; W↓ shape should match outputting two nd-dimensional children but is stated as (n+nd)×(n+nd)). The term “feedbackward” appears to be a typo. Equations (2)–(5) would benefit from explicit tensor shapes and block definitions to avoid ambiguity.
- Novelty relative to concurrent bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models is incremental. The distinction—using both parent forward and backward vectors for computing child backward vectors and a specific parameterization—should be more sharply analyzed. An ablation controlling for parameter count (e.g., widening the bidirectional baseline to match GB-RNN’s additional n·nd parameters) would help isolate architectural benefits from capacity.
- Evaluation breadth: Results are limited to one benchmark (SemEval-2013 Task 2A). Additional datasets (e.g., aspect-level sentiment in reviews) or out-of-domain tests would better establish generality.
- Fairness and factors: Best reported results use an ensemble and external resources (lexicon-derived examples, noisy tweets, movie reviews). While allowed by the shared task, it is important to report single-model results without external data alongside to quantify the contribution of GB-RNN itself. Some of this is present; a clearer breakdown table (no ensemble, no external data; with each resource added) would be valuable.
- Parsing constraints: Forcing labeled spans to be subtrees is practical for training/evaluation but may bias the structure. Please clarify whether the same constraint is applied at test time (it appears so, since spans are given), and discuss implications for general use where labels may not align to parse constituents.
- Missing comparisons: A windowed BiLSTM or bidirectional recurrent baseline over tokens with attention to the target (if available within the time frame) would probe whether tree structure is essential here. At minimum, a capacity-matched bidirectional sequential model would be informative.

Questions for the authors
- Provide exact matrix/tensor shapes for Eq. (3) and (5), and confirm whether backward child vectors depend on both forward and backward parents through a single affine map or separate block matrices.
- Report metrics consistently with the shared task (average F1 for positive/negative) for all baselines, and include dev/test performance on tweets vs. SMS separately, as noted by the task organizers.
- Detail hyperparameters and training regime (epochs, early stopping criteria, optimizer settings) sufficiently for reproduction; include training/inference complexity relative to baseline RNNs.
- Include a parameter-matched comparison to a bidirectional recursive network and/or a larger forward RNN to control for capacity.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically plausible and experimentally supported with improvements over baselines. However, some mathematical details are underspecified or inconsistent in the text, and further controlled ablations are needed to attribute gains specifically to the top-down mechanism rather than capacity/ensembling/external data.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable and well-motivated but hampered by notation errors, a few formatting glitches (stray text near Eq. 4), and incomplete specification of shapes. Suggestions above should improve clarity.
- Originality: Moderately novel. Top-down propagation on trees has close contemporaries; the particular parameterization and hybrid embeddings are incremental contributions rather than fundamentally new paradigms.
- Significance: Practical for contextual sentiment and potentially other token/phrase labeling tasks. Gains on a standard benchmark are encouraging, but broader validation is needed for strong impact claims.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real shortcoming of bottom-up compositional models with a simple, general mechanism.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines on a recognized benchmark.
- Provides useful engineering insights (hybrid embeddings, dropout on node vectors, two-stage training).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty over concurrent bidirectional/inside–outside recursive models; insufficient theoretical or controlled empirical analysis to isolate architectural advantages.
- Clarity issues in equations and shapes; some formatting errors.
- Evaluation focused on a single benchmark, with ensembles and external data complicating attribution of gains.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the model specification, adding parameter-matched ablations, and tightening the empirical analysis to disentangle the effects of architecture, capacity, and external resources.
