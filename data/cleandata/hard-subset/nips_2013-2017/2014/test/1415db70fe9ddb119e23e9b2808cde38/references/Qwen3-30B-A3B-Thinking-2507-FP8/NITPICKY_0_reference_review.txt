

# Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) by adding a top-down backward pass to incorporate global context into phrase representations. The authors claim this allows the model to correctly classify sentiment toward specific entities in context (e.g., identifying "Android" as positive in "The Android's screen is better than the iPhone's"). The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where the authors claim it outperforms prior work.

## Strengths
The paper identifies an important limitation in current compositional models: the inability to capture context-dependent sentiment. The idea of incorporating global context through a backward pass is conceptually interesting, and the empirical results on SemEval 2013 show some improvement over baselines. The hybrid word vector approach (combining unsupervised and supervised vectors) is a reasonable and practical technique.

## Weaknesses

### Terminology and Conceptual Confusions
The paper uses "RNN" to refer to Recursive Neural Networks throughout, creating immediate confusion with standard Recurrent Neural Networks. This is a critical terminology error in the NLP field. Additionally, the term "global belief" is never clearly defined or justified, making it difficult to understand what the authors mean by "belief" in this context. The model is not truly bidirectional in the standard sense (which would process the sequence in reverse), but rather implements a top-down tree traversal.

### Methodological Errors
The comparison with Irsoy and Cardie (2014) in Section 3.6 contains a mathematical inconsistency. The authors claim their model differs from the bidirectional RNN because it "makes use of both the forward and backward parent node," but their equation (5) incorrectly rewrites equation (4) from the cited work. The dimensions don't match: W↓lb and W↓rb are said to be nd×nd, but the equation would require them to be nd×n for the addition to make sense. This undermines the theoretical comparison.

### Experimental and Reproducibility Issues
The paper claims to "outperform the winner of the Sentiment competition of SemEval 2013" but fails to provide specific numerical results or a clear comparison table. Table 1 mentions results but lacks actual accuracy numbers. The paper states that "adding additional training examples increased accuracy by 2%" without providing the baseline accuracy, making it impossible to assess the significance of this improvement.

The description of the ensemble method ("an ensemble of the top 5 GB-RNN models trained independently") is insufficient for reproducibility. How were these models trained independently? What was the ensemble combination method? The paper also mentions "dropout with p = 0.5" but doesn't specify which layers were subjected to dropout.

### Inconsistent and Unclear Explanations
The hybrid word vector representation (Section 3.4) is described as "concatenation of both unsupervised vectors that are kept at their initialization during training and adding a small additional vector into which we propagate the task specific error signal." However, the paper never specifies the exact dimensions of these components or how they're combined. The statement "Figure 3.4 shows the resulting word vectors" is problematic as no such figure is included in the provided text.

### Overstated Claims
The paper claims "the important context can be many words away from the phrase that is to be labeled" as a unique advantage over window-based methods, but this is not true - bidirectional RNNs can also capture long-range context, and window-based methods can be extended to larger windows. The paper fails to provide a clear theoretical justification for why their approach should work better than existing methods.

### Formatting and Writing Issues
The paper contains multiple typos: "de-composition" (Section 3.3), "shold" (Section 3.4), and inconsistent use of terms like "feedbackward" vs "backward." The references have inconsistent formatting (some with "In ACL," others with full conference names), and the paper uses "GB-RNN" without proper definition in the first occurrence.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper identifies a meaningful problem in context-dependent sentiment analysis, the methodological flaws, conceptual confusions, and lack of sufficient experimental detail significantly undermine the contribution. The terminology errors and mathematical inconsistencies are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission. The claims about the model's advantages are overstated relative to the evidence provided, and the paper fails to adequately differentiate itself from existing bidirectional approaches.

The paper presents a reasonable idea but lacks the technical precision, clear theoretical justification, and sufficient experimental detail required for a NIPS publication. The minor flaws identified here collectively constitute a substantial number of issues that would prevent a fair evaluation of the work's true contribution.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper's technical shortcomings, conceptual ambiguities, and insufficient experimental details prevent a clear assessment of its contribution to the field. While the problem being addressed is relevant, the execution and presentation do not meet NIPS standards.
