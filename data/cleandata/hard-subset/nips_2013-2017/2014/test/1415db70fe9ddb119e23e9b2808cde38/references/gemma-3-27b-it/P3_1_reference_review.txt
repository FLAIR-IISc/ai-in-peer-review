## Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and intuitively motivated extension to standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) for compositional semantics, addressing the crucial problem of context-dependence in natural language understanding. The GB-RNN model, by incorporating a backward pass to refine representations based on global context, demonstrably outperforms strong baselines, including the winner of the SemEval 2013 competition. While the technical contribution isn’t groundbreaking – building on established RNN principles – its effective application to a challenging problem and the compelling empirical results make it a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper correctly identifies a limitation of purely feedforward compositional models: their inability to account for how context influences the meaning of phrases.  The authors propose GB-RNNs, which augment standard RNNs with a “backward” pass that propagates information from the root of the parse tree down to the leaves, allowing phrase representations to be updated based on the overall sentence context. This is particularly well-illustrated with the example of sentiment analysis, where the sentiment towards “Android” vs. “iOS” depends on the comparative sentence structure. 

The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant areas like word vectors, standard RNNs, bidirectional RNNs/recursive networks, and sentiment analysis.  The authors accurately position their work relative to Irsoy & Cardie's bidirectional RNN, highlighting a key difference in how the backward pass is implemented. The connection to unfolding autoencoders is also appropriately noted, albeit briefly.  The inclusion of relevant citations (e.g., Mikolov et al., Socher et al.) demonstrates a good understanding of the landscape.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The key strength of this paper is its empirical performance.  Outperforming the SemEval 2013 winner on a challenging task is a significant achievement. The ablation studies (Table 2) provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the GB-RNN (e.g., hybrid word vectors, the backward pass).
* **Intuitive Motivation and Clear Explanation:**  The motivation behind the GB-RNN is very clear – addressing the limitations of context-free compositional models. The model itself is explained well, with a helpful visual illustration (Fig. 2). The equations are presented in a digestible format.
* **Practical Focus:**  The authors address practical concerns like training (backpropagation through structure, AdaGrad optimization, dropout) and hyperparameter tuning.  The inclusion of additional training data and the impact of different initialization strategies (hybrid word vectors) are relevant and contribute to the robustness of the findings.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper engages with relevant, contemporary work, specifically the bidirectional RNN from Irsoy and Cardie, and clearly articulates the differences in approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Technical Innovation:** While the GB-RNN improves upon existing RNNs, the core innovation isn’t a radical departure from established techniques.  The backward pass, while effective, is conceptually a relatively straightforward extension.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the theoretical implications of this backward pass – does it have connections to belief propagation or other related concepts in probabilistic inference?
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a more in-depth qualitative error analysis. Providing specific examples of sentences where the GB-RNN succeeds where other methods fail (beyond the example in the introduction) would strengthen the argument for its superiority.
* **Dependency on Parser Quality:** The performance of the GB-RNN is directly tied to the accuracy of the Stanford Parser.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the model to parsing errors. How robust is the model to incorrect parse trees?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention cross-validation for hyperparameter selection, but the details are somewhat lacking. It would be valuable to provide a more comprehensive overview of the hyperparameter search process and the sensitivity of the results to different settings.




**4. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant empirical improvement over strong baselines and the SemEval 2013 winner.
* Clear and well-motivated approach to a crucial problem in natural language understanding (context-dependence).
* Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental technical innovation – the core idea is relatively straightforward.
* Limited error analysis and discussion of model limitations.
* Reliance on parser accuracy without a thorough robustness analysis.



**5.  Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Expand on Theoretical Connections:** Discuss potential links between the backward pass and concepts from probabilistic inference (e.g., belief propagation).
* **Provide Detailed Error Analysis:** Include specific examples of sentences where the GB-RNN outperforms other methods.
* **Investigate Parser Robustness:** Conduct experiments to assess the sensitivity of the model to parsing errors.
* **Detailed Hyperparameter Report:** Provide a more comprehensive overview of the hyperparameter search process and the impact of different settings.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Briefly discuss the computational overhead introduced by the backward pass.




**Overall:** This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The strong empirical results and clear explanation make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
