This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) to address contextual sentiment classification, arguing that standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) fail to capture the context-dependent nature of sentiment. The core idea is a backward pass through the parse tree, updating node representations with global context. The authors demonstrate improvements over standard RNNs and the winner of the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis competition on a Twitter dataset.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of context-dependent sentiment is clearly articulated with a compelling example (Fig. 1). The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of existing methods. The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of compositional semantics and recursive neural networks, referencing relevant work such as [27, 11, 26, 5]. The related work section is comprehensive, covering neural word vectors, RNNs, bidirectional architectures, and existing sentiment analysis techniques. The technical contribution, GB-RNN, is presented clearly, with equations explaining both the forward and backward passes. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the comparisons with baselines and competition systems are thorough.  The inclusion of ablation studies (Table 2) and analysis of training data additions (Section 4.4) strengthens the evaluation. The writing is generally clear and well-organized.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses.  Firstly, the novelty of the backward pass is somewhat diminished by the acknowledgement and comparison with Irsoy & Cardie [12, 14], who proposed a similar bidirectional recursive model *concurrently*. While the authors point out a difference in the computation of the backward vectors (Eqs. 4 & 5), the discussion lacks depth regarding the theoretical implications of this difference and why the proposed method is superior.  A more detailed comparison, perhaps with a small, targeted experiment specifically isolating this difference, would be beneficial. The claim that the presented model can incorporate information from words "further away" than bidirectional RNNs or window-based methods isn't fully substantiated – a bidirectional RNN, in principle, *can* capture long-range dependencies, although it does so through a different mechanism.  Secondly, while the results on SemEval 2013 are encouraging, the performance gains, while statistically significant, are not overwhelmingly large. The reliance on the Stanford Parser introduces a potential point of failure and dependency on external tools; the impact of parsing errors isn't discussed.  Finally, the hybrid word vector representation, while showing improvement, feels somewhat tacked on and not fully integrated into the core contribution of the GB-RNN.  The explanation in Section 3.4 could be more concise and focused on the rationale behind this hybrid approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in sentiment analysis: context-dependent sentiment.
*   Presents a novel extension to standard RNNs with a theoretically sound motivation.
*   Demonstrates improved performance on a benchmark dataset (SemEval 2013).
*   Thorough experimental evaluation with baselines and ablation studies.
*   Clear and well-organized writing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the backward pass is weakened by concurrent work [12, 14].
*   The claimed advantage over other methods in capturing long-range dependencies is not fully convincing.
*   Performance gains, while significant, are not dramatic.
*   Reliance on the Stanford Parser.
*   The hybrid word vector representation feels somewhat disconnected from the core contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a worthwhile contribution to the field of compositional semantics and sentiment analysis. However, the lack of a more in-depth comparison with related work, particularly Irsoy & Cardie [12, 14], and the modest performance gains prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit, specifically addressing the points raised above. A more thorough theoretical and empirical comparison with [12, 14] is crucial, as is a clearer articulation of the advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches. Further investigation into the impact of parsing errors would also be valuable.
