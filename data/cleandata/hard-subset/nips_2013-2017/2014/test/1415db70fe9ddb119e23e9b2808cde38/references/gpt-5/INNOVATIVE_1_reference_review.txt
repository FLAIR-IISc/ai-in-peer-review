Summary
The paper tackles context-dependent phrase/sentiment classification and proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs). Starting from a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) over a binarized parse tree, the authors introduce a top-down “feedbackward” pass that computes backward vectors for every node, intended to inject global sentence context into local predictions. Final node labels use the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. They also propose “hybrid” word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with a small task-specific trainable component, and they apply dropout to both word and hidden units. Experiments on SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment (phrase-level) report improvements over a feedforward RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and the winning SemEval systems, with additional gains from ensembling and external resources.

Strengths
- Addresses an important limitation of purely bottom-up compositional models: local meaning depends on global context. The top-down pass is a clean, general mechanism to provide sentence-level beliefs to all constituents.
- Empirical results claim notable gains over strong baselines and competition systems using fewer hand-engineered features.
- The hybrid embedding idea is practical and seems to work well with dropout.
- The approach is potentially applicable beyond sentiment (e.g., word sense disambiguation, token-level labeling).

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing bidirectional recursive models and inside–outside style RNNs is limited. The proposed downward computation largely mirrors prior work, with differences that appear to be added parameterization (using both forward and backward parent vectors) rather than a qualitatively new mechanism. The “global belief” framing is attractive but not fundamentally distinct from known top-down/inside–outside passes on trees.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: there are typos and dimensional inconsistencies (e.g., shapes for V and W↓; Eq. 3 suggests W↓ maps to two child backward vectors but the stated dimensions do not match; “feedbackward,” “feedwordard”), and some extraneous lines appear mid-section. Key hyperparameters (e.g., nd) and exact tree/binarization decisions are underspecified. Figures/tables are referenced but not fully included.
- Attribution and fairness of comparisons: the paper notes GB-RNN has more parameters than the bidirectional baseline; it is unclear whether comparisons control for capacity. The source of gains (architecture vs. hybrid embeddings, dropout, extra data, ensembling) is not isolated with targeted ablations.
- Dataset-task mismatch: The motivating examples are target/aspect-dependent sentiment, but the main evaluation is SemEval 2013 phrase-level. Demonstrations where long-range context truly disambiguates local polarity are limited.
- No analysis of computational cost or training stability from the extra top-down pass.

Quality
Technically, the model is straightforward and likely sound, but the paper’s notation and dimensional inconsistencies hinder verification. The training setup (BPTS, dropout, AdaGrad) is standard. The empirical section suggests improvements, but the lack of detailed, controlled ablations leaves uncertainty about what drives the gains. The use of additional lexicons, large noisy pretraining corpora, and ensembling could account for much of the improvement over external baselines.

Clarity
The exposition needs significant polishing. Equations should be corrected with precise dimensions; provide a clear schematic of the downward pass that maps a parent’s forward/backward vectors to children’s backward vectors. Remove stray text and fix typos. Specify nd, activation choices, binarization, and exact loss application across nodes. Present full results tables (numbers, std devs), and detail what resources were used in each comparison.

Originality
The idea of augmenting bottom-up compositional models with a top-down contextual pass is not new; closely related work on bidirectional recursive networks and inside–outside RNNs is cited but the difference appears incremental (use of both forward and backward parent vectors, extra parameters). The hybrid embedding trick is useful but has antecedents. As framed, the conceptual advance over prior bi-recursive models is modest.

Significance
Context infusion for local predictions is important, and if the architecture reliably helps with long-range contextual sentiment, it could be impactful and portable to other tasks. However, the current evidence does not isolate the contribution of the new top-down formulation, and the evaluation focuses on a dataset that may not stress the claimed advantage. With stronger, cleaner empirical validation and clearer differentiation from concurrent work, the contribution could be more compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all notational/dimensional issues; include precise shapes for V, W↓ (likely 2nd × (n+nd)), and a concise algorithmic description of the forward and backward passes.
- Provide controlled ablations: (a) forward-only vs. GB-RNN with matched parameter counts; (b) with/without hybrid embeddings; (c) with/without external lexicon/noisy data; (d) single model vs. ensemble; (e) vary nd.
- Evaluate on truly target-dependent datasets (e.g., SemEval 2014 aspect-based tasks) or construct tests where context is many words away.
- Compare to inside–outside RNNs (Zuidema/Le) with matched capacity and training.
- Report training/inference cost and convergence behavior; release code if possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of compositional models with a simple, general top-down mechanism.
- Promising empirical gains and practical tricks (hybrid embeddings + dropout).
- Potential to generalize to other structured prediction tasks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty over established bidirectional/inside–outside recursive architectures.
- Clarity and correctness issues in the presentation hinder reproducibility.
- Insufficient ablations to attribute gains to the proposed architectural changes.
- Evaluation does not convincingly test the motivating “global context for local decisions” scenarios.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper aims at a valuable direction and may contain a useful variant, but the novelty over prior bi-recursive models is modest, and the clarity/experimental attribution issues are significant. With a crisper formulation, stronger controls, and evaluation on truly contextual tasks, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
Moderate.
