**Summary**
This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to handle context-dependent phrase classification, with a particular focus on aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The core problem is that standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) compose meaning bottom-up, making it impossible for the representation of a phrase (e.g., "The Android's screen") to be influenced by later context (e.g., "is better than the iPhone's"). The proposed GB-RNN addresses this by adding a second, "feedbackward" pass. After a standard bottom-up pass computes compositional vectors for all nodes in a parse tree, a top-down pass propagates information from the root down to the leaves. At each node, the final representation is a concatenation of its forward and backward vectors, which is then used for classification. The paper also proposes a hybrid word vector representation, combining fixed pre-trained vectors with task-specific trainable vectors. The authors evaluate the GB-RNN on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, demonstrating significant improvements over a standard RNN, other baselines, and the winning system of the competition.

**Review**

This is a well-written and well-motivated paper that addresses a clear and important limitation of standard recursive models for natural language. The proposed solution is elegant, and the experimental results are strong, making a convincing case for the model's effectiveness on its target task.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound. The model architecture is clearly defined, and the equations for both the forward and backward passes are laid out explicitly. The training procedure, using backpropagation through structure with AdaGrad and dropout, is standard and appropriate. The experimental setup is rigorous, with comparisons to strong baselines, including the standard RNN, window-based methods, and the winners of the SemEval 2013 competition. The authors also perform a valuable ablation study on their proposed hybrid word vectors, showing their superiority over purely supervised or unsupervised vectors. The claims of the paper are well-supported by these empirical results; the >5% F1 improvement over the standard RNN is a substantial gain and clearly demonstrates the value of the feedbackward pass.

**Clarity**
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively uses the "Android vs. iPhone" example to motivate the need for contextual understanding. The model is built up logically, starting from the standard RNN before introducing the novel feedbackward component. Figure 2 is a helpful illustration of the two-pass mechanism. The comparison to the bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie is particularly well-done, with a direct, side-by-side comparison of the relevant equations that clarifies the subtle but important differences between the two models. The experimental section is also clear and provides sufficient detail to understand the setup.

**Originality**
The primary point of discussion regarding originality is the model's relationship to the concurrent work of Irsoy and Cardie (2013). The authors correctly cite and discuss this work, acknowledging that the general idea of a bidirectional recursive pass is not unique to them. The novelty of the GB-RNN lies in the specific formulation of the backward pass. Whereas Irsoy and Cardie's model computes the backward vector for children based only on the parent's backward vector, the GB-RNN uses a combination of the parent's *forward* and *backward* vectors (Eq. 5). The authors argue this helps disentangle representations, and their empirical results show their formulation outperforms Irsoy and Cardie's. While the core concept of a top-down pass is shared, this specific, effective modification represents a valid and valuable contribution. It is an incremental, rather than a radical, innovation, but one that is well-justified empirically. The proposed hybrid word vectors are a practical and useful technique, but less of a core conceptual contribution.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is high. It directly tackles a fundamental challenge in compositional semantics: the integration of local composition with global context. The aspect-based sentiment task is an excellent testbed for this problem, and the strong results advance the state of the art on a competitive shared task. The model provides a general architectural principle—a feedforward pass followed by a feedbackward refinement pass on a tree structure—that could be applicable to other NLP tasks where global context is crucial for resolving local ambiguity, such as word sense disambiguation, relation extraction, or coreference resolution. While the paper only presents results on one task, the potential for broader impact is clear. The demonstrated effectiveness of this approach likely influenced subsequent work on contextualized representations in tree-structured models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Problem:** Addresses a clear, well-motivated, and important limitation in standard recursive neural networks.
*   **Solution:** The two-pass architecture is an elegant and intuitive way to incorporate global sentence context into local phrase representations.
*   **Results:** The empirical results are strong and convincing. The model significantly outperforms a strong RNN baseline and achieves state-of-the-art on a challenging public benchmark.
*   **Clarity & Analysis:** The paper is very clearly written, and the direct comparison to the most relevant concurrent work (Irsoy and Cardie, 2013) is a major strength.

**Arguments for Rejection (or areas for improvement):**
*   **Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of a bidirectional pass on a recursive structure was proposed concurrently. The main novelty is the specific (and superior) formulation of the update rule, which could be seen as an incremental improvement.
*   **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The model's effectiveness is demonstrated on a single task. Showing its utility on another context-dependent task (e.g., word sense disambiguation) would have made the paper's claims of generality much stronger.
*   **Weak Justification:** The theoretical justification for why their specific backward-pass formulation is superior to Irsoy and Cardie's is brief and somewhat intuitive ("helps to push the model to disentangle..."). While empirically validated, a more in-depth analysis or ablation of the components in Eq. 5 would strengthen this key claim.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that I would recommend for acceptance. The strengths—a clear problem statement, an elegant solution, and strong empirical results on a challenging task—far outweigh the weaknesses. The work makes a solid contribution to the field of deep learning for NLP and provides a valuable architectural idea for incorporating global context into compositional models.
