Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural architecture that augments standard bottom-up recursive neural networks with a top-down “feedbackward” pass. The forward pass computes node representations compositionally along a parse tree; the backward pass starts at the root and propagates context back to all nodes. Final predictions at each node are made from the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The method targets context-dependent labeling, exemplified by aspect/target-specific sentiment in tweets, where the local sentiment of a phrase may depend on distant context. The paper also proposes hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with a small trainable task-specific subvector, and uses dropout on node vectors.

This work is situated among recursive neural models for compositional semantics [Socher et al.; Pollack], bidirectional and inside-outside tree models [Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema], and sequential bidirectional RNNs [Schuster & Paliwal]. Compared to bidirectional recursive models, the GB-RNN backward update uses both the forward and backward parent state to compute a child’s backward state and allows different dimensionalities for forward/backward states via an additional transform V. Experiments focus on SemEval 2013 Task 2 Twitter sentiment, with additional external resources; the GB-RNN reportedly outperforms a feedforward RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and strong competition systems.

Strengths
- Motivation: The need for context-sensitive phrase interpretation is clear, and the top-down pass is a natural, simple way to inject global information back to constituents across long distances in the tree.
- Architectural clarity and generality: The model is a minimal change to standard RNNs on trees and could, in principle, be applied beyond sentiment (e.g., WSD, coreference cues).
- Empirical promise: Reported gains over a purely feedforward RNN (~5% absolute) and over a bidirectional recursive baseline, with ablations indicating contributions from hybrid embeddings, dropout, and extra data.
- Practical embedding trick: Concatenating a frozen unsupervised embedding with a small trainable “delta” is a useful, broadly applicable idea to balance generalization and task-specificity.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Underspecified and inconsistent equations. There are dimensionality inconsistencies: e.g., W↓ is claimed to be in R(n+nd)×(n+nd), but the mapping in Eq. 3 needs a 2nd×(n+nd) matrix to produce [child↓; sibling↓]. V is stated as “V ∈ Vnd×n,” which appears to be a typo. These issues must be fixed to ensure the model is well-defined and reproducible.
- Experimental reporting is incomplete. The manuscript refers to Tables 1–3 but they are not included in the provided text; numerical results, variance across runs, and statistical significance are not visible. The core claims (beating SemEval winners; +5% over RNN; improvements due to lexicon and noisy data) cannot be assessed without full metrics and comparisons.
- Fairness of comparisons. The strongest system is an ensemble of the top 5 GB-RNN models, while it is unclear whether competing baselines are ensembled. The bidirectional recursive baseline is said to have fewer parameters; a parameter-matched comparison would be more convincing.
- Data/task definition ambiguity. SemEval 2013 Task 2 is primarily tweet-level sentiment. The paper states there are “7862 labeled phrases,” that they “constrained the parser to keep each phrase labeled by the dataset inside its own subtree,” and presents node-level predictions. It is unclear how phrase-level supervision is obtained from sentence-level labels, and whether the evaluation target is message-level or phrase-level. The inserted “Correct …” examples appear out of place and not tied to a clear evaluation protocol.
- Heavy reliance on extra resources and ensembling. Gains are partly attributed to NRC lexicons, 176k noisy tweets, SST, and model ensembling. It is important to also report single-model results without external data to isolate the method’s contribution.

Clarity
- Several typos and editorial issues (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “shold”), formatting glitches (stray “Correct …” lines inside the model section), and missing figures/tables detract from readability.
- The “global belief” terminology suggests a probabilistic interpretation, but the method is purely deterministic message passing; a brief justification or alternative terminology would help.

Originality
- The idea of a top-down pass on trees is not new per se (inside-outside, bidirectional recursive models). The paper’s specific backward update—using both forward and backward parent states with separate dimensionalities—is a reasonable variant, but the novelty seems incremental. The hybrid embedding idea is also known in spirit (freeze pre-trained + learn deltas), though useful.

Significance
- If validated with rigorous, transparent experiments, the model could be a practical addition for context-sensitive labeling on trees. However, in its current form, the contribution’s impact is difficult to assess due to missing/unclear details and limited breadth of tasks. The focus on tweets with a noisy parser and without broader benchmarks (e.g., aspect-based sentiment datasets) limits generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of standard tree RNNs with a simple, broadly applicable architectural change.
- Promising reported gains over strong baselines and competition systems.
- Practical embedding technique and training details (dropout on node vectors) that could transfer to other tasks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Mathematical and notational inconsistencies; missing tables/figures; unclear task/evaluation setup.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside-outside tree models, without a compelling theoretical or empirical separation.
- Comparisons confounded by extra data and ensembling; lack of parameter-matched baselines and significance testing.
- Limited scope of evaluation and unclear reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all dimensionality/specification issues; provide a complete, unambiguous description of forward/backward computations with shapes.
- Clarify the supervision and evaluation protocol (phrase-level vs message-level), provide full numeric tables (accuracy/F1 per class, macro/micro), and include statistical significance.
- Report single-model results without external data and without ensembling; then add external resources stepwise to quantify their effects.
- Provide parameter- and compute-matched comparisons to bidirectional/inside-outside recursive baselines; include additional strong baselines relevant to target-dependent sentiment.
- Add error analysis and runtime/memory discussion; examine parser robustness on tweets.
- If “belief” is intended metaphorically, state so; otherwise, connect the method to probabilistic message passing.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the core idea is sensible and likely useful, the paper in its current form has clarity and rigor issues that prevent a confident assessment. With corrected specifications, transparent and comprehensive experiments, and cleaner positioning relative to prior bidirectional tree models, this could become a solid contribution. My confidence in this assessment is moderate.
