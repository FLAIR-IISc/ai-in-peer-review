Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural architecture that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass to inject global sentence context into local (word/phrase) predictions. The model concatenates forward and backward node vectors for classification, and proposes hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed unsupervised embeddings with a small supervised task-specific subvector. The target application is contextual sentiment analysis on SemEval-2013 Task 2 Twitter data; the authors claim improvements over standard RNNs, bidirectional-recursive baselines, and competition winners, with additional gains from ensembling, dropout on node vectors, and extra weakly labeled data.

This work broadly relates to inside-outside style recursive models (e.g., Le & Zuidema), bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie), and unfolding/autoencoder-based recursive architectures; it also builds on RNTN/RNN sentiment models (Socher et al.) and hybrid/retrofit word vectors (e.g., GloVe/word2vec plus task-specific tuning).

Strengths
- Clear motivation for context-dependent labeling on trees; the Android/iPhone example is apt.
- The top-down pass is intuitive and well-aligned with inside-outside ideas; explicit block-wise parameterization (W↓lf, W↓rf, W↓lb, W↓rb) could offer flexibility.
- Hybrid word vector idea (fixed unsupervised + small supervised) and use of dropout on node vectors are practical and likely robustifying.
- Sensible training protocol (AdaGrad, mini-batching, two-stage pretraining on context-independent data then fine-tuning on contextual task).
- Claims to outperform strong SemEval systems and bidirectional recursive baselines, and to benefit from ensembling and weakly labeled tweets.

Weaknesses (technical and presentation), many of which are substantial
- Missing results: Critical quantitative tables/figures are not present (Tables 1–3, F1 scores, standard deviations, ablations), preventing verification of claims. Several figures are referenced but absent (Fig. 1, 2, “Figure 3.4”), impairing clarity and reproducibility.
- Dimensionality/notation inconsistencies:
  - Eq. (3) implies W↓ maps [parent_forward; parent_backward] ∈ R^{n+nd} to [child_left↓; child_right↓] ∈ R^{2nd}; thus W↓ should be in R^{2nd × (n+nd)}. The text states W↓ ∈ R^{(n+nd)×(n+nd)}, which is incompatible.
  - Softmax classifier weight Wc is given as C×(n+1) despite concatenating forward and backward node vectors; should be C×(n+nd+1) if bias is folded.
  - V is typed as “V ∈ Vnd×n”; presumably R^{nd×n}.
- Conceptual inconsistency: The paper alternately claims the backward node computation uses both forward and backward parent vectors (Eq. 3/5) and later states “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector.” These are contradictory and must be resolved.
- Theoretical comparison to bidirectional-recursive models is muddled: The derivation for Eq. (4)/(5) is difficult to follow, and the claimed parameter counts and independence assumptions are not clearly justified. No empirical numbers are shown to substantiate superiority.
- Data and evaluation details are insufficient:
  - The SemEval-2013 Task 2 splits and counts seem unusual (e.g., multiple “7862” splits; “phrases” vs. terms/tweets); clarify exact subtask, number of labeled spans, and scoring (macro F1 of pos/neg?).
  - Fairness of comparison: authors use an ensemble, external sentiment lexicon (NRC), 176k weakly labeled tweets, and an additional movie review corpus; it is unclear which systems in Table 1 used similar resources. Results should be reported for single models without external data, and with/without each resource.
  - Preprocessing: Forcing each labeled phrase to be a subtree with constrained parsing could bias evaluation; please quantify how often constraints alter parses and their impact.
- Reproducibility gaps: No hyperparameter ranges, learning rates, early stopping, random seeds, or runtime/computational costs. nd (backward vector dimension) is never specified; only n=130 is given. The choice of activation (sigmoid vs ReLU) is said to be cross-validated, but not reported per model. No code link.
- Writing/formatting issues:
  - Numerous typos and terminology inconsistencies: “feedbackward/feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “root note,” extraneous commas, etc.
  - A block of unrelated “Correct …” tweet snippets appears in the middle of Section 3.6/eqs, disrupting the exposition.
  - References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing venues/years; author order/name errors; punctuation; “Glove” capitalization).
- Methodological clarity:
  - Training objective: “unlabeled nodes do not add an additional error”—is supervision applied only at dataset-labeled spans? Are all internal nodes otherwise unsupervised? How are class priors handled for neutral?
  - Inference/training mismatch: Early text says the backward step is used “at inference time,” but Section 3.5 trains the full GB-RNN; clarify whether top-down parameters are trained and how gradients flow through forward/backward paths.
  - Complexity: The model increases parameters by n·nd; no discussion of overfitting risk, parameter tying, or regularization beyond dropout/weight decay.

Quality
Given the missing quantitative evidence, internal inconsistencies in equations, and presentation problems, the paper is not yet technically solid. The core idea is promising but underspecified, and claims are not verifiable from the submission.

Clarity
The manuscript needs substantial editing. Missing figures/tables, contradictory statements, notation errors, and reference issues make it hard to follow and to reproduce.

Originality
The general idea of top-down context in recursive models is known (inside-outside RNNs, bidirectional recursive nets). The particular parameterization may be a modest variant, and the hybrid word-vector trick is incremental. Novelty is limited without a clearer theoretical or empirical distinction.

Significance
Contextual sentiment is important, and a solid, well-documented improvement over strong baselines would be valuable. However, given the reporting gaps, the impact is uncertain.

Questions for authors
- Provide full quantitative results (macro F1 with confidence intervals), per dataset (tweets/SMS), for: GB-RNN single vs ensemble; with/without external data; vs RNN and bidirectional-recursive baselines.
- Resolve dimensionality and contradiction issues around W↓, V, Wc, and whether forward vectors are used in backward computations.
- Specify nd, learning rates, number of epochs, early stopping, and seeds. Report training/inference time.
- Clarify SemEval task setup, parsing constraints, and their effects.
- Detail the hybrid embedding sources (which unsupervised vectors, training corpora), and ablate fixed vs fine-tuned.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear motivation; intuitive model extension.
- Practical hybrid embedding and dropout choices.
- Potential empirical gains on a relevant task.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing core results and figures; cannot verify claims.
- Inconsistent equations and contradictory statements.
- Weaknesses in clarity, references, and reproducibility.
- Limited novelty relative to prior bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models.

Recommendation: Reject
Confidence: High. The idea is promising, but the current submission has too many technical and presentation flaws to assess or reproduce the claimed contributions. I encourage the authors to fix the inconsistencies, include complete results and ablations, and resubmit.
