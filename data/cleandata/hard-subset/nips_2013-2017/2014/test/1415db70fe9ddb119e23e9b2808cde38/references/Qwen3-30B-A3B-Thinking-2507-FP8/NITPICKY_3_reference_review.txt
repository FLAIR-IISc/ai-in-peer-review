

# Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

This paper introduces "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks" (GB-RNN), which extends standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) by adding a top-down "feedbackward" pass to incorporate global context for contextual sentiment classification. The authors claim this approach can better handle context-dependent sentiment classification than standard feedforward RNNs or bidirectional approaches.

## Technical Concerns

The paper contains several critical technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The most significant issue is the persistent confusion between "recursive neural networks" (RNNs) and "recurrent neural networks" (RNNs). In the NLP community, "RNN" specifically refers to recurrent neural networks, which process sequences, while "recursive neural networks" process tree structures. The paper incorrectly uses "RNN" to refer to recursive neural networks throughout (e.g., "standard RNN" in Section 3.2, "feedforward RNN" in Section 4.3), creating fundamental confusion about the architecture. This is particularly problematic as the paper claims to outperform "the standard RNN" when it's actually comparing to a recursive neural network.

The paper introduces "feedbackward vectors" with inconsistent notation (↓, "feedbackward", "backward"), and the description of the backward pass (Equation 3) is unclear. The comparison with Irsoy and Cardie's bidirectional RNN (Section 3.6) contains a significant error: the authors describe their model as having "n · nd more parameters" than the bidirectional RNN, but the actual parameter count calculation is incorrect. The paper's parameter count for their model (2n²d + 2n·nd) appears to be wrong based on the matrix dimensions provided.

The paper claims to use "hybrid unsupervised-supervised word vectors" (Section 3.4) but fails to provide evidence that this approach is better than using purely supervised vectors. The paper states "we show that this performs better" but doesn't provide comparative results for the different vector initialization methods in the experiments.

## Presentation and Documentation Issues

Several formatting and presentation issues diminish the paper's quality. The paper refers to "Fig. 3.4" (Section 3.4) but this is a section number, not a figure. The paper mentions "Fig. 2" and "Fig. 1" in the text but no figures are provided. The reference to "http://www.cs.york.ac.uk/semeval-2013/task2/" as a citation is inappropriate and should be replaced with a proper academic citation to the SemEval 2013 Task 2 paper.

The paper claims to outperform "the winner of the Sentiment competition of SemEval 2013" but doesn't name the winning system or provide a citation to it, making it impossible to verify this claim. The paper states "the GB-RNN outperforms both the standard RNN and all other baselines, as well the winner" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these results.

The paper mentions "the development-test (7862)" (Section 4.1) but this is grammatically incorrect and unclear - it should be "development set" or "test set." The paper also states "the final test set is composed of 10681 examples" but doesn't clarify whether these are the same as the SemEval 2013 test set.

## Minor Technical Omissions

The paper claims to use "dropout with p = 0.5" (Section 3.5) but doesn't provide evidence that this is optimal for their model. The paper states "the high-dimensional hybrid word vectors... have obtained a higher accuracy" but doesn't provide the accuracy numbers for the different vector approaches. The paper claims to use "100 pre-trained, fixed word vectors and 30 trained on sentiment data" (Section 4.3) but doesn't explain why these specific dimensions were chosen.

## Strengths and Significance

The paper does present an interesting idea about incorporating global context into recursive neural networks for sentiment analysis, which is a valid problem in NLP. The approach is novel in its use of a top-down pass through the parse tree rather than window-based context. The paper also provides a detailed theoretical comparison with a related bidirectional model.

## Overall Assessment

While the core idea of incorporating global context into recursive neural networks has merit, the paper is undermined by multiple technical errors, inconsistent terminology, and insufficient experimental details. The confusion between recursive and recurrent neural networks is particularly egregious and would cause significant confusion among readers. The paper lacks proper statistical validation of its claims, and the experimental results are not sufficiently detailed to reproduce or verify.

The paper makes a strong claim to outperform SemEval 2013 winners but provides no statistical evidence or comparison to the winning system. The technical errors and presentation issues outweigh the paper's potential contribution. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify the terminology, correct the technical errors, and provide more detailed experimental results.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains multiple critical technical errors that undermine its core claims, and the presentation issues make it difficult to assess the validity of the results. While the idea has potential, the current formulation is not sufficiently rigorous or well-presented for NIPS.
