Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “feedbackward” pass to inject sentence-level context into local (word/phrase) predictions. The forward pass computes node representations via a standard composition function; the backward pass starts from the root and recursively computes top-down vectors for each node using both the forward and backward parent states. Final predictions at each node use the concatenation of the forward and backward vectors. The authors also propose hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed, unsupervised vectors with a smaller, trainable supervised component. They train with backpropagation through structure, AdaGrad, and dropout. Experiments target contextual sentiment classification on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter), where the sentiment of a marked term must be judged in context. The model reportedly outperforms a feedforward tree RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline (Irsoy & Cardie), and the competition winners; an ensemble of GB-RNNs gives the best test performance.

The proposal relates closely to prior recursive models that propagate information in both directions on trees. It is very similar in spirit to Inside-Outside/bi-directional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie 2013; Le & Zuidema’s Inside-Outside RNN), which also compute bottom-up and top-down representations. The key claimed difference is that GB-RNN uses both the forward and backward parent states to compute child backward states, whereas some prior bidirectional-recursive formulations use only the backward parent (possibly plus linear transforms of child forward states). The hybrid embeddings and “freeze + small supervised head” idea has been independently explored in different forms in the literature, but is sensible and well-motivated here.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: context-dependent (target/term-level) sentiment on noisy social media, for which purely compositional bottom-up models or bag-of-words are insufficient.
- Clear architectural intuition: the top-down pass lets global sentence-level beliefs influence local predictions, potentially leveraging information arbitrarily far from the target via the parse tree.
- Empirical results suggest tangible improvements over a standard tree RNN and over a bidirectional-recursive variant, and the system compares favorably to SemEval-2013 systems.
- Simple, practical training and regularization choices (dropout on node states, AdaGrad), and a hybrid embedding approach that improves generalization on sparse, noisy data.
- Ablations indicate added value from the hybrid embeddings and staged training with additional (distantly supervised) data.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and correctness issues in the model description:
  - Dimensional inconsistencies: e.g., W↓ is described as R(n+nd)×(n+nd) but is used to produce concatenated child backward vectors of size 2·nd. Later block decomposition implies W↓ should be R(2·nd)×(n+nd). V has a malformed type (“V ∈ Vnd×n”). These need to be corrected.
  - Conceptual contradiction: the text claims “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” yet Eq. 3/5 explicitly use the forward parent p in computing child backward states. Please reconcile and clarify what information flows where.
  - The manuscript contains obvious editing artifacts (e.g., unrelated “Correct …” tweet lines inserted into the middle of the model section), incomplete references, and typos. This undermines reproducibility and confidence.
- Novelty relative to prior bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models is not convincingly established. Using both forward and backward parent states is a reasonable variation, but the paper does not isolate whether gains come from this design vs. increased capacity (GB-RNN has n·nd more parameters) or other training choices (dropout, hybrid embeddings). Additional controlled ablations are needed.
- Experimental details are insufficient for reproducibility:
  - Exact scores (F1, accuracy) for all systems on dev/test, with and without ensembles, and with/without external resources, are not fully reported in the provided text. Statistical significance is not discussed.
  - The fairness of comparisons is unclear: the best system is an ensemble; were baselines ensembled? How are external resources (NRC lexicon, distant supervision, SST) used across models?
  - Training hyperparameters (learning rate schedules, stopping criteria), model sizes (n, nd), and compute costs (training time, memory) are not thoroughly specified.
- Dataset and preprocessing: Twitter parsing with a standard PCFG parser is error-prone; no analysis is provided on parser quality or robustness to noisy syntax. Since the proposed benefit depends on parse quality, error analysis would be valuable.
- Claims about advantages over window/BiRNN methods are overstated. While fixed windows are limited, bidirectional sequence models can in principle integrate long-range context; the structural inductive bias of trees is the real advantage. A stronger baseline would be a robust bidirectional recurrent model tuned for tweets (or, today, a BiLSTM), though this may not have been standard at the time.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all dimensional inconsistencies and remove editorial artifacts. Provide a clean, unambiguous definition of the backward recursion with shapes, and consider pseudocode for the two-phase inference and training.
- Strengthen novelty claims with targeted ablations:
  - Compare variants that do/don’t use the forward parent in the backward recursion, holding parameter count constant.
  - Match parameter counts between GB-RNN and the bidirectional-recursive baseline to test whether added capacity drives the gains.
  - Report single-model results separately from ensembles; report with/without external resources; include significance tests.
- Provide detailed experimental protocols and resources: exact splits, preprocessing, parser settings, hyperparameters, and code availability.
- Add runtime/memory analysis and complexity discussion for training and inference.
- Include qualitative error analysis showing cases where the backward pass flips a local prediction vs. where it fails; examine sensitivity to parse errors.
- Consider exploring gating/attention in the top-down pass to modulate how much global context is injected, which may improve robustness on noisy trees.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practically relevant task with a principled architectural idea.
- Empirical gains on a standard benchmark.
- Conceptually connects inside-outside style inference to neural composition in a clean way.
- Hybrid embeddings and training recipe are useful to practitioners.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation quality issues and mathematical inconsistencies.
- Novelty relative to closely related bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models is not clearly demonstrated.
- Incomplete experimental details; unclear fairness and significance; reliance on ensembles and external resources without thorough controls.
- Lack of analysis of parse robustness and computational costs.

Overall evaluation
This is a promising direction with encouraging results, but the current manuscript has clarity issues and does not adequately isolate the source of improvements relative to prior bidirectional-recursive models. With corrected equations, stronger ablations, and more thorough experimental reporting, it could be a solid contribution. As it stands, I lean toward rejection, but I encourage resubmission after addressing the concerns above.
