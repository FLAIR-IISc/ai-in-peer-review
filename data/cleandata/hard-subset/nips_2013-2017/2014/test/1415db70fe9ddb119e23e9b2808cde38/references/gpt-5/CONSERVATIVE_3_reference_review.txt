Summary
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass. The forward pass computes phrase representations from leaves to root; the backward pass propagates information from the root to leaves via a learned transform V and a decomposition matrix W↓. Final node-level predictions concatenate forward and backward vectors. The target application is context-dependent sentiment classification of phrases in tweets (SemEval 2013 Task 2). The paper also proposes “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed unsupervised embeddings with a small trainable component, and uses dropout and AdaGrad. The authors claim that GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and the SemEval 2013 winning system, with further gains from external lexicons, weak supervision, and ensembling.

Relation to prior work
The motivation—context-dependent composition and top-down contextualization—is important and aligns with longstanding ideas in NLP (inside-outside style propagation on trees; tree CRFs; bi-directional RNNs). The model is close to bidirectional recursive architectures (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema’s inside-outside RNN), differing mainly in how backward vectors depend on forward/backward parents and in parameterization. The “hybrid” embedding idea is a common practice (fixed pre-trained + task-tuned components).

Strengths
- Addresses a real limitation of purely bottom-up compositional models: lack of global context when labeling internal nodes.
- Clear high-level idea: a learned top-down pass that conditions local predictions on sentence-level context.
- Sensible engineering choices (hybrid embeddings, dropout, AdaGrad) and an attempt at ablations/pretraining.
- Target task (SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment, phrase-level) is appropriate and known to benefit from context.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Insufficient and opaque empirical evidence. The manuscript refers to Tables 1–3 and qualitative examples, but no actual numbers are provided in the text here. Claims such as “over 5% better than the RNN” and “outperforms the SemEval winner” need concrete metrics (F1/accuracy), confidence intervals, and statistical testing. Without them, it is difficult to assess significance.
- Fairness and attribution of gains are unclear. The best system is an ensemble using substantial external resources (NRC lexicon, 176k weakly labeled tweets, SST). There is no controlled comparison isolating the contribution of GB-RNN vs. resources/ensembling. A fair test should compare single models under identical resources.
- Comparison to bidirectional recursive baselines is underdeveloped. Theoretical differences are discussed, but quantitative results versus Irsoy & Cardie (and other contemporaneous bidirectional/inside-outside models) are not convincingly documented here.
- Questionable preprocessing: “We constrained the parser to keep each phrase labeled by the dataset inside its own subtree.” This may simplify the problem and is not generally feasible in deployment. It risks inflating performance and should be analyzed (what fraction of spans are non-constituents without this constraint; performance without enforcing constituency).
- Clarity/technical correctness issues:
  - Inconsistent and sometimes incorrect dimensions (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n, W↓ ∈ R(n+nd)×(n+nd) for a mapping that appears to require 2nd × (n+nd)).
  - Typos and notational confusion (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard”), and a block of unrelated “Correct …” lines embedded in the model section.
  - Ambiguity about whether forward parameters are shared across nodes, how W↓ is applied to produce two children jointly, and computational complexity/runtime.
- Limited evaluation scope: only one dataset family (Twitter) and a single task variant. No tests on robustness to parser noise (a serious issue on tweets), generalization to other domains, or other tasks where top-down signals should help (e.g., WSD, negation scope).
- The “hybrid word vectors” contribution is incremental and widely used; novelty claims should be tempered, and ablations need precise numbers.

Quality
The proposed architecture is reasonable, but the paper—at least in the present form—does not provide enough rigorous, controlled experimentation to substantiate the main claims. The theoretical exposition has dimensional inconsistencies that need correction. Important methodological choices (parser constraint, ensembling, external data) are not sufficiently controlled.

Clarity
The manuscript needs significant editing. Equations and dimensions should be corrected and made consistent; the top-down computation should be specified precisely (shapes, parameter sharing, activation choices). Tables and exact numbers must be included. The stray lines and typos should be removed. Reproducibility details (data splits, preprocessing, hyperparameter ranges, training epochs, seeds) are missing.

Originality
Moderate. The top-down pass on a tree is closely related to prior bidirectional/inside-outside recursive nets and tree-structured belief propagation ideas. The main novelty is a particular parameterization using both forward and backward parent vectors; this is incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful if demonstrated convincingly, but current evidence is not strong. Without solid, controlled gains over established baselines under matched resources, it is hard to judge impact. Relying on heavy external data and ensembling muddles attribution.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide full quantitative results: metrics per split, with and without external resources, single model vs ensemble, and statistical significance across multiple seeds.
- Match resources across models when comparing GB-RNN, standard RNN, and bidirectional recursive nets; include strong non-tree baselines (linear SVM/LogReg on n-grams + lexicons; bi-LSTM with attention) with the same external data.
- Report performance without forcing labeled spans to be constituents; quantify how often spans are non-constituents; analyze parser errors’ impact.
- Clarify and correct all dimensions and equations; explicitly give W↓ as a 2nd × (n+nd) block matrix and justify design choices (use of V, choice of nd).
- Provide ablations: effect of V vs identity, nd size, dropout, hybrid embeddings vs fixed vs fully trainable, two-stage training vs joint.
- Report computational cost (training/inference time) and parameter counts; discuss overfitting risks.
- Evaluate on additional datasets and tasks where top-down context should matter.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of bottom-up compositional models.
- Conceptually simple extension that could be broadly useful.
- Promising initial results claimed on a standard benchmark.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of rigorous, controlled, and transparent empirical validation.
- Ambiguous fairness of comparisons due to external data and ensembling.
- Clarity and correctness issues in the technical presentation.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The idea is plausible, but the paper needs substantially stronger and clearer empirical evidence, corrected and complete technical exposition, and fair comparisons before it meets the bar for NeurIPS.
