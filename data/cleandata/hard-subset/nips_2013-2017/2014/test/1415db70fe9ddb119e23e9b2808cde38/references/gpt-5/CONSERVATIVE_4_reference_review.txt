Summary
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a two-pass tree-structured model for context-dependent phrase/sentiment classification. A standard bottom-up recursive neural network computes forward (feedforward) node vectors; then a top-down pass computes “feedbackward” vectors using both the forward and backward parent representations. Final predictions at each node use the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The paper also proposes “hybrid” word embeddings: concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with additional trainable dimensions. Experiments on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment, term-level) claim that GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional-recursive baseline, and prior competition systems, especially when ensembling and adding external resources (NRC lexicon and distantly supervised tweets).

Relation to prior work
The model is close in spirit to bidirectional-recursive networks (Irsoy & Cardie, 2013) and inside-outside recursive models (e.g., Le & Zuidema, 2014), as well as classical “belief propagation”-style message passing in tree-structured models (e.g., CRFs), though the present method lacks a probabilistic formulation. The hybrid embedding idea is related to freezing parts of embeddings while fine-tuning others, a practice explored in earlier deep NLP work (e.g., Collobert et al.). The paper cites relevant work but the novelty over bidirectional-recursive approaches feels incremental.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for context-dependent sentiment at the term level, where local phrases can be neutral without context.
- Simple architectural change (top-down pass) that is easy to implement on top of a standard recursive net and plausibly useful.
- Uses the same hybrid embeddings and dropout across baselines, which is a reasonable attempt at fairness in ablations.
- Some empirical gains are reported over a standard RNN and a bidirectional-recursive baseline; the hybrid embeddings appear beneficial.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The proposed backward computation differs only modestly from prior bidirectional recursive models; there is no principled inference view or theoretical analysis of when/why the proposed “global belief” update is preferable. The “belief” terminology is not backed by a probabilistic model.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair evaluation: Only one task/dataset. The strongest results rely on ensembling and substantial external data (NRC lexicon; 176k distantly labeled tweets) while claiming to outperform SemEval winners, many of whom reported both constrained and unconstrained settings. A constrained comparison (no external data, no ensembles) is needed.
- Missing numbers and statistical rigor: Tables/figures with exact metrics (macro-F1 per SemEval) are not shown in the submission we received; claims are stated as “over 5%,” “2%,” etc. There are no variance estimates or significance tests.
- Ambiguous parsing/label leakage: The paper states that the parser was “constrained to keep each phrase labeled by the dataset inside its own subtree.” It is unclear how this constraint is enforced at test time and whether it uses the target term span to influence the parse, potentially biasing comparisons. The impact of parsing errors on Twitter text is not assessed.
- Clarity and correctness issues: Notation has inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “feedbackward,” dimensions of V; Wc including a +1 bias term while using concatenation; shapes not always consistent). Core equations are terse, and the exact architectural difference from Irsoy & Cardie is not crisply isolated with a clean, comparable formulation. Some references seem mismatched or incomplete.
- Limited analysis: No error analysis, no runtime/parameter footprint, no sensitivity to embedding freezing ratio, no study of how much gain comes from the backward pass vs. more capacity, and limited discussion of failure cases. It is unclear whether the benefits persist without an ensemble.

Quality
As it stands, the paper lacks sufficient experimental evidence and rigor to substantiate its claims. The architecture is plausible, but the ablations and comparisons do not isolate the contribution cleanly, and key details and numbers are missing. Potential confounds (parse constraints, external data, ensembling) undermine the main claims.

Clarity
The writing is generally understandable but needs careful editing. Fix notation/dimension errors, unify terminology (backward vs. feedbackward), include all referenced tables/figures, and clearly specify metrics, splits, and preprocessing. A description sufficient for reproduction is currently lacking.

Originality
Incremental over existing bidirectional-recursive models. The backward update using both forward and backward parent is a minor variant. The hybrid embedding approach is not new.

Significance
Given the limited and potentially confounded empirical evidence and modest novelty, the current impact is uncertain. The idea could be useful, but stronger, cleaner experiments are needed.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide full, numeric results (macro-F1 for positive/negative as per SemEval) with and without external resources and without ensembling. Are gains significant?
2. Clarify the parsing constraint precisely. Is the parser guided by the target span at test time? If so, how do you ensure fairness to non-tree baselines?
3. Compare to Irsoy & Cardie with identical capacity and resources; report parameter counts and runtime. Does the extra n·nd parameters fully explain the gain?
4. Report sensitivity to nd, dropout, and the ratio of frozen vs. trainable embedding dims. What happens if you fine-tune all embedding dims?
5. Provide error analysis on context disambiguation and a robustness check to noisy parses on tweets.

Pros
- Addresses a real limitation of feedforward compositional models (lack of contextual disambiguation).
- Simple, potentially broadly applicable architectural idea.
- Some positive empirical indications.

Cons
- Modest novelty over prior bidirectional-recursive work.
- Results insufficiently documented and potentially confounded (external data, ensembles, parsing constraints).
- Clarity and reproducibility issues.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. With clearer, controlled experiments (no external data/ensembles; fair, unconstrained parsing), full metrics, significance tests, and a tighter comparison to existing bidirectional-recursive models, this could be a solid incremental contribution.
