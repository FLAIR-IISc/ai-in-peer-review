This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) designed to improve contextual understanding in sentiment analysis, particularly addressing cases where sentiment depends on the relationships between entities in a sentence. The core idea is to propagate information backward through the parse tree, allowing phrases to update their sentiment prediction based on the global sentence context – a feature standard RNNs lack. The authors demonstrate superior performance on the SemEval 2013 Task 2 dataset compared to the competition winner and various baselines, showcasing the effectiveness of this contextualization approach.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear limitation of existing recursive neural networks that struggle with context-dependent sentiment. The problem statement is clearly articulated with a compelling example (the Android/iPhone sentence), and the proposed GB-RNN is a logical extension of standard RNNs. The related work section appropriately cites relevant research on word vectors, RNNs, bidirectional models, and sentiment analysis. The technical explanation of the GB-RNN is reasonably clear, and the experimental setup is well-defined, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies. The hybrid word vector representation is a sensible approach, combining the benefits of pre-trained and task-specific vectors.  The authors also acknowledge and address potential limitations, such as the increased number of parameters and the reliance on a parser. 

However, there are several weaknesses. While the authors briefly mention Irsoy & Cardie (2012) which proposes a bidirectional RNN, the distinction isn’t fully elaborated. Equation 4 in their paper and the discussion that follows suggests a significant difference in how the backward pass is implemented, and the authors’ claim that their approach disentangles children from their backward parent vector feels somewhat hand-wavy without further justification or analysis. The practical impact of this design choice isn’t convincingly demonstrated.  The ablation studies, while present, are not exhaustive. For example, the impact of the *dimension* of the feedbackward vectors (nd) isn’t explored. What happens as nd increases? Does performance saturate? The impact of different parsing methods isn't discussed, which could be important given that performance relies heavily on parse tree accuracy. Furthermore, the datasets used for additional training (NRC lexicon, movie reviews) appear quite different in nature from the Twitter data used for evaluation. While the authors report a modest improvement, a more thorough analysis of the domain gap would be valuable.  Finally, the presentation of results could be improved. Table 3, for instance, simply states that concatenation "performs better" without quantifying the difference. A more precise comparison with statistical significance would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a clear and important limitation in existing recursive neural network models.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on a challenging benchmark dataset, outperforming the competition winner.
*   The proposed GB-RNN is a conceptually sound and well-motivated extension of existing techniques.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The technical distinction between the GB-RNN and related bidirectional RNNs isn't fully justified or analyzed.
*   Ablation studies are limited and don't explore the sensitivity of the model to key parameters (e.g., dimension of feedbackward vectors).
*   The use of additional training data from different domains is not thoroughly discussed.
*   The presentation of results could be more precise and include statistical significance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of compositional semantics and sentiment analysis. While the paper has some weaknesses, the demonstrated performance improvement and the novelty of the approach are significant. I believe that with some revisions addressing the points raised above, this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Provide a more detailed and rigorous comparison to Irsoy & Cardie (2012), clearly articulating the differences and advantages of their approach. 2) Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies, exploring the impact of key parameters. 3) Discuss the potential limitations of using additional training data from different domains. 4) Present results with statistical significance.
