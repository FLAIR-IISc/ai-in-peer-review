- Proposes GB-RNN: a tree-structured model that adds a top-down “feedbackward” pass to standard bottom-up recursive neural nets to provide global context to local predictions.  
- Concatenates bottom-up and top-down vectors at each node and classifies with a softmax.  
- Introduces hybrid word vectors that combine fixed unsupervised embeddings with a small trainable supervised component plus dropout.  
- Positions work relative to recursive tree RNNs (Socher et al.), bidirectional/bottom-up-top-down recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, Le & Zuidema), and word-embedding literature (Collobert, GloVe).  
- Evaluates on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter phrase-in-context sentiment) and reports gains over baselines, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and competition winners, using allowed external resources (NRC lexicon, weakly labeled tweets).  
- Strength: clear motivation—many NLP predictions are context-dependent and a top-down pass is a principled way to inject global context.  
- Strength: simple, general mechanism applicable to any tree RNN without changing the forward encoder.  
- Strength: hybrid embedding+dropout is practical, well-argued, and likely useful beyond this task.  
- Strength: empirical focus on a realistic benchmark with claimed improvements over strong systems.  
- Strength: discussion of parameterization variants and comparison to bidirectional recursive models.  
- Weakness: mathematical specification contains dimensional inconsistencies (e.g., Eq. 3 weight-matrix sizing, classifier Wc sizing) that hinder reproducibility.  
- Weakness: textual contradiction about whether the forward node vector is used in computing backward vectors conflicts with equations.  
- Weakness: experimental reporting omits crucial details—exact F1 scores, per-class/domain breakdowns, standard deviations, and statistical significance.  
- Weakness: fairness of comparisons unclear—best system is an ensemble and contribution of external resources vs core model is not isolated.  
- Weakness: no ablation table to separate effects of GB-RNN, NRC lexicon, weakly labeled data, and ensembling.  
- Weakness: potential sensitivity to noisy parses (Twitter PCFG parsing) not analyzed; robustness to parse errors unclear.  
- Weakness: computational cost (parameters, training/inference time, memory) of top-down pass not reported.  
- Clarity: notation inconsistencies and the nonstandard term “feedbackward” reduce clarity; recommend standard “top-down” or “downward” terminology.  
- Clarity: dataset split counts are confused (train/dev/dev-test all listed as “7862”); need correct per-domain splits and evaluation metric definitions.  
- Originality: idea of bottom-up + top-down on trees is known; the specific child-parameterization is a modest novel variant but needs clearer differentiation from prior inside-outside models.  
- Significance: task is meaningful and gains could be valuable, but impact is hard to assess without fuller quantitative detail, stronger baselines, and additional tasks demonstrating generality.  
- Suggestions: fix and clarify all equations, tensor shapes, and the backward-pass data flow; provide a schematic with shapes.  
- Suggestions: provide full experimental tables (macro F1, per-class F1, per-domain), significance tests, runtimes, and memory use.  
- Suggestions: include ablations for nd, V, use of forward parent in backward step, embedding variants, and dropout.  
- Suggestions: expand comparisons to chain BiLSTMs, inside-outside RNNs, window-based baselines, and parse-free models; test robustness to parse noise and dependency parses.  
- Suggestions: release code or detailed pseudocode to enhance reproducibility.  
- For acceptance: addresses an important limitation of tree RNNs with a simple mechanism and shows practical embedding/training insights.  
- Against acceptance: current paper has notational/mathematical inconsistencies, insufficient empirical detail and ablations, limited evaluation breadth, and unresolved overlap with prior bidirectional/inside-outside models.  
- Overall: promising and potentially useful idea, but needs clearer, consistent formulation and stronger, more rigorous empirical evidence to be acceptable.
