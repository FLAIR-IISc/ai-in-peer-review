- Proposes GB-RNN: a tree-structured model adding a top-down “feedbackward” pass to standard bottom-up recursive nets to infuse node representations with global sentence context.  
- Computes forward node vectors with composition matrix W and top-down vectors via root transform V and decomposition matrix W↓, classifying nodes on concatenated forward/backward vectors.  
- Introduces a hybrid word representation concatenating frozen unsupervised embeddings with a small task-tuned vector and applies dropout to node vectors.  
- Positioned against prior recursive and bidirectional tree models and window/bidirectional sequence models, claiming better handling of long-distance tree-structured dependencies.  
- Technical formulation is clear and principled, with explicit equations and a justified backward parameterization differing from prior bidirectional RvNNs.  
- Empirical results claim substantial gains (≈5% over forward-only RNN) and benefits from hybrid embeddings, dropout, lexicon data, and joint training, but numeric tables are missing.  
- Critical experimental details are under-specified: per-system/split scores, significance tests, and explicit comparisons to bidirectional RvNNs are absent.  
- Ensemble usage (top-5 models) confounds attribution of gains to the architecture versus ensembling; single-model vs ensemble comparisons are needed.  
- No analysis of dependency on constituency parses for noisy tweets or parsing robustness and its impact on performance.  
- Training hyperparameters and runtime details (nd, learning rates, initializations, complexity) are incompletely reported, hindering reproducibility.  
- Paper is generally readable with good motivation but contains typos, notation errors, missing figures/tables, and unclear SemEval subtask/metric specification.  
- Novelty is modest: top-down context propagation is known, but the specific backward parameterization and design choices are a modest, well-motivated contribution.  
- Significance is moderate: meaningful gains for targeted sentiment could generalize to other tree-structured tasks, but scope is narrow and parser reliance limits applicability.  
- Pros: clear motivation, principled recursion, empirical gains, useful ablations, and potential generalizability.  
- Cons: missing quantitative/significance details, notation/clarity issues, insufficient parser robustness analysis, incomplete training details, and incremental novelty.  
- Suggested improvements: provide full single-model and ensemble tables with significance, control ablations for parameter count, clarify SemEval setup and subtree constraint, add error analysis, and report full training/runtime details.  
- Overall assessment: sensible and promising work but currently borderline; needs complete quantitative results, tighter comparisons to related bidirectional work, and improved clarity/reproducibility—currently a weak reject leaning toward acceptance if revised.
