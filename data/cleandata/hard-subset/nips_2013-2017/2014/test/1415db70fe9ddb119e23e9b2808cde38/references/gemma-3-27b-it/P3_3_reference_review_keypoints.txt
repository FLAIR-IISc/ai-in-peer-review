- Overall: Presents a timely extension (GB-RNN) that adds a backward pass to recursive neural nets to better capture context-dependent meaning, with strong empirical results.  
- Core contribution: GB-RNN introduces a feedback/backward pass allowing phrase predictions to be updated using global sentence context.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on word vectors, compositional semantics, and RNN literature and distinguishes itself from bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie 2014).  
- Novelty: Backward pass in a recursive framework is a genuinely new and principled approach to incorporate global context.  
- Empirical strength: Outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, including the SemEval 2013 winner, on a challenging sentiment task.  
- Motivation and exposition: Clear motivation illustrated with examples and a well-illustrated architecture description.  
- Baseline comparison: Comprehensive comparisons to standard RNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and window-based methods.  
- Representation choice: Sensible hybrid of pre-trained and task-specific word vectors.  
- Theoretical weakness: Lacks rigorous theoretical justification for why the backward scheme improves performance and when it is most effective.  
- Ablation weakness: Ablation study is limited—missing sensitivity analyses (e.g., feedback vector dimension nd, decomposition matrix V).  
- Data analysis weakness: Impact of additional training data is presented abruptly without careful per-dataset analysis or clarity on which models benefited most.  
- Computational concern: Paper does not discuss the additional computational cost of the backward pass or mitigation strategies.  
- Engineering dependency: Performance depends on Stanford Parser accuracy; alternatives or robustness to parsing errors are not explored.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—novel, well-motivated, and empirically strong but needs deeper theory, ablation, and cost discussion.  
- Rating: 8/10.
