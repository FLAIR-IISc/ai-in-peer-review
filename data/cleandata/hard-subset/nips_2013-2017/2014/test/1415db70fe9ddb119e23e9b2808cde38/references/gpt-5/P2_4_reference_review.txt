Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The key idea is to compute context-dependent representations for every node (word/phrase) by combining a forward vector (bottom-up composition) with a backward vector (top-down propagation from the root), and then classify each node using the concatenation of both. The authors argue this is important for contextual sentiment classification where the sentiment of a sub-phrase can depend on distant context. They also introduce a “hybrid” embedding: concatenate fixed, unsupervised word vectors with a small, task-specific trainable vector, and apply dropout on node vectors. Experiments on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter) show improvements over a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), classic baselines, and the competition winner. Ablations suggest the backward pass gives >5% absolute gain; external lexicon and weakly labeled data add 2% and 1%, respectively. An ensemble of GB-RNNs yields the best test result.

Relation to prior work
The model sits between standard tree RNNs (Socher et al.) and bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; inside-outside variants). The paper clarifies how its backward computation differs: the child backward vectors are computed as a function of both the parent’s forward and backward vectors via a single block-structured matrix, rather than (as claimed) using only forward-parent or child-forward transformations in prior work. The hybrid embedding idea relates to earlier unsupervised vs supervised word vectors and fine-tuning (Collobert et al., GloVe) but proposes keeping the unsupervised portion fixed while learning a small supervised tail.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level idea is reasonable and known to be effective: adding a top-down pass to incorporate global sentence context into local predictions. However, there are several issues that should be fixed:
  - Mathematical clarity: dimensions and matrices in the backward equations are inconsistent (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n; W↓ stated as (n+nd)×(n+nd) while outputs [b↓ c↓] must be 2nd). The later block decomposition suggests W↓ should be of shape (2nd)×(n+nd). These typos make it hard to reproduce.
  - Fair comparison: The paper states all models use the same hybrid embeddings and dropout, and reports a ~5% absolute gain over a pure forward RNN. This is compelling, but key quantitative tables/figures are not included here, and no statistical significance is provided. The method also uses external lexicons and large weakly labeled data (allowed by SemEval), so both with- and without-external-resource results should be made explicit for all baselines, including the bidirectional recursive model.
  - Training/inference protocol: The paper uses a two-stage training (pretrain forward RNN on large context-independent data, then train GB-RNN on the smaller context-specific data). This is sensible, but a control experiment with joint training from scratch would help isolate where gains come from. Efficiency and convergence behavior are not discussed.
  - Data handling: For tweets, reliance on a constituency parser (and constraining parses so labeled phrases form a subtree) may introduce biases. Some analysis of parser errors and their effect would strengthen the claims.

- Empirical support: Assuming the stated results, the model appears to advance state-of-the-art on SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment, and ablations attribute gains to the backward pass. More detailed error analysis (e.g., long-distance dependencies, negation, entity-specific sentiment) would bolster the central claim about context sensitivity.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-motivated and situates itself in prior work, but clarity suffers from notational inconsistencies, several typos, and missing details:
- Fix matrix dimensions in Section 3.3 and define nd clearly.
- Clean up figure/table references and footnotes embedded mid-text.
- Provide a precise algorithmic description of the top-down pass and the classifier (including bias handling, softmax dimension, and whether the forward vector is used in computing the backward vector).
- Clearly separate the hybrid word vector design from the forward/backward node concatenation at classification time.

Originality
The idea of a top-down pass over trees is not entirely novel (bidirectional recursive/inside-outside models exist), and the differences appear to be in parameterization and in using both forward and backward parent states to compute child backward states. The hybrid embedding with a fixed unsupervised block plus a small supervised block is a modest, practical tweak. Overall, the novelty is incremental but nontrivial, provided the empirical benefits over strong, matched baselines hold.

Significance
Context-dependent labeling at the phrase/term level in noisy social media is important. If the improvements over both feedforward tree RNNs and concurrent bidirectional recursive baselines are robust, this is a useful contribution that others could build on for aspect-based sentiment and other token/phrase-level tasks. The reliance on parses may limit applicability to noisier domains, and stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., chain bidirectional RNNs) are discussed conceptually but could be compared more thoroughly.

Pros
- Clear motivation for incorporating global context into local decisions.
- Simple, general architectural change that can be applied to tree-structured models.
- Ablations suggest meaningful gains from the backward pass beyond external resources.
- Practical embedding tweak (hybrid vectors + dropout) with demonstrated benefits.

Cons
- Clarity issues in the model specification (dimensions, equations) impede reproducibility.
- Novelty relative to bidirectional recursive/inside-outside models is limited and not fully disentangled.
- Heavy reliance on external resources; need clearer apples-to-apples comparisons and significance tests.
- Limited analysis of parser dependency and error modes; lack of runtime/complexity discussion.

Questions for authors
1) Please correct the dimensionalities in Eq. (2–5) and specify W↓’s exact shape. How is nd chosen?
2) Do you ever use the forward vector in computing the backward vector (beyond via the parent)? Any benefit in adding a skip from the node’s own forward state?
3) Report single-model results without external lexicons/weakly labeled data for all compared models, and include statistical significance.
4) How sensitive are results to parser errors and the subtree constraint? Any results with automatic parses vs gold, or with dependency parses?
5) Provide training time and parameter counts; how does GB-RNN scale vs the bidirectional recursive baseline?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely useful, and the empirical story is encouraging. However, the paper needs to (a) fix technical clarity issues, (b) more convincingly differentiate from prior bidirectional recursive work, and (c) present clearer, statistically grounded comparisons without confounds from external resources. With these addressed, it would be a stronger candidate.
