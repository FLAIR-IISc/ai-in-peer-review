## Review of Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), an extension of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) designed to incorporate contextual information during sentiment classification. The core idea is a backward pass through the parse tree, updating node representations based on the global sentence context. The authors demonstrate improved performance on the SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment analysis task, outperforming both standard RNNs and the competition winner.

While the motivation for addressing context-dependence in sentiment analysis is well-articulated and the proposed approach is conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality. My assessment is largely negative, leaning towards rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Contextual sentiment analysis is an important and challenging problem in NLP.
*   **Novelty (Incremental):** The backward pass mechanism, while related to bidirectional RNNs, represents a distinct approach to incorporating global context within a recursive framework.
*   **Empirical Results:** The reported results demonstrate a performance improvement over strong baselines, including the SemEval 2013 winner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is riddled with stylistic inconsistencies. For example, equations are sometimes referred to by number (Eq. 1) and sometimes described verbally ("equation 3"). Mathematical notation lacks consistency; sometimes vectors are bold, sometimes not. Frequent use of phrases like “However,” at the start of paragraphs disrupts the flow.
*   **Technical Details & Reproducibility:** Despite claiming thorough experimentation, crucial details are often missing or vaguely described. The specific parser settings (beyond just stating Stanford Parser was used) are not mentioned. The details of the hyperparameter search (ranges, grid size, etc.) are insufficient. The precise composition of the ensemble (number of models, training splits) is missing. The statement "labeled phrases were taken as a separate example" lacks clarity - how were phrase boundaries determined in the case of overlapping phrases?
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The related work section, while extensive, is presented as a list of papers rather than a cohesive narrative. The comparison to Irsoy and Cardie (2012) feels superficial, primarily focusing on minor equation differences (Eqs. 4 & 5) and failing to fully address the conceptual differences in their approaches to the backward pass. The claim that their method doesn't use the forward node vector is inaccurate given Eq. 4.
*   **Model Justification:** The justification for the transform matrix *V* in the backward pass (Section 3.3) feels weak. The cited benefits (differentiating features, reducing parameters) aren't convincingly demonstrated, and the empirical support is lacking. The authors state it "performs better empirically," but provide no specific results showcasing this improvement.
*   **Hybrid Word Vector Details:** The description of the hybrid word vector approach is unclear. It is not stated how many dimensions come from the unsupervised vectors versus the supervised ones, and how the unsupervised vectors are fixed during training.
*   **Minor Errors:** Several minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the text detract from readability. For example, "de-composition" in section 3.3.

**Specific Concerns/Questions:**

*   Why use a sigmoid activation function as mentioned in Section 4.3, when ReLU is mentioned as an option in the same sentence? Which one was ultimately used?
*   The authors mention applying dropout, but don't specify *where* dropout was applied (e.g., input, hidden layers, word vectors). This is a crucial detail for reproducibility.
*   The impact of the additional training data (NRC lexicon, movie reviews) is reported as a modest 1-2% improvement. Is this improvement statistically significant?
*   The table captions are often incomplete sentences and lack proper formatting.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important, and the approach shows promise.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a quantifiable improvement over strong baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper's numerous stylistic and technical shortcomings undermine its credibility.
*   The lack of detail regarding experimental setup hinders reproducibility.
*   The related work discussion is superficial and lacks critical analysis.
*   The justifications for certain design choices are weak.
*   The overall presentation is unpolished and unprofessional.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the GB-RNN and the encouraging empirical results, the pervasive issues with clarity, technical detail, and presentation significantly detract from the paper's quality. While the core idea has merit, the paper feels incomplete and insufficiently rigorous for acceptance to NIPS. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above, with a strong emphasis on reproducibility and clarity, would be necessary for reconsideration.

**Recommendation: Reject.**
