Summary
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a top-down “feedbackward” (outside) pass. The backward pass starts from the root and propagates context to all nodes; each node is then classified using the concatenation of its bottom-up (inside) and top-down (outside) representations. The paper also introduces “hybrid” word vectors formed by concatenating fixed unsupervised embeddings with small trainable task-specific embeddings. Experiments on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment) claim that GB-RNN outperforms a feedforward RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and the competition winner, with further gains from ensembling and external resources (lexicons, distant supervision).

Relation to prior work
The proposed idea is very close to existing inside-outside/bidirectional recursive models. In particular, Le and Zuidema’s Inside-Outside RNN and Irsoy and Cardie’s bidirectional recursive networks already propagate information top-down in trees. The present work differs mainly in the exact parameterization of the downward pass (using both the forward parent and a backward parent vector), but this distinction is incremental. The hybrid word vector idea is also a known and widely used practice.

Strengths
- Addresses an important limitation of pure bottom-up tree models: node labels often depend on sentential context, especially in targeted sentiment.
- The general message-passing view (inside + outside) is sensible and has a clear intuition.
- Uses reasonably standard training practices (AdaGrad, dropout) and includes some ablations (RNN vs bidirectional vs GB-RNN; hybrid embeddings).
- On SemEval-2013 Twitter sentiment, the approach appears to deliver gains over a pure RNN and over competition systems (though details are sparse).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Novelty and attribution: The model is essentially an inside-outside recursive network. The differences from Le & Zuidema (Inside-Outside RNN) and Irsoy & Cardie are minor and not convincingly established as consequential. The paper acknowledges these works but does not provide a rigorous head-to-head comparison with identical resources and hyperparameters.
- Method clarity and correctness: Several equations and dimensions are unclear or inconsistent (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n; W↓ dimensions; whether the backward vector uses the forward vector is stated inconsistently). The “feedbackward” terminology and typos further obscure the mechanics. It is difficult to reimplement from the current description.
- Experimental rigor: The paper relies on external resources (NRC lexicon, 176k distant-supervised tweets, movie reviews) and an ensemble of the top 5 models to achieve the best results, yet the baseline comparisons do not always use the same resources or ensembling. There are no statistical significance tests, confidence intervals, or variance across runs. Key numbers (absolute F1/accuracy per split, per class) and the details of Tables 1–3 are not included in the text provided.
- Data and preprocessing risks: For tweets, forcing the parser to keep each labeled span as a single subtree is nonstandard and could bias evaluation. The Stanford Parser is not optimized for noisy Twitter text; the paper does not quantify parsing errors or their impact.
- Efficiency and scalability: No reporting of parameter counts vs. performance trade-offs, training time, memory footprint, or inference speed. The additional W↓ and V add parameters; the effect on overfitting and complexity is not analyzed.

Clarity
- The paper has multiple typos and notational issues; the description of the backward recursion is hard to follow and contradictory in places. Important implementation details (activation choices per layer, regularization strengths, stopping criteria, how outside vectors are initialized at the root) are underspecified. Figures/tables referred to in the text are not fully described.

Originality
- The core contribution (top-down context propagation in tree RNNs) is not new; the differences to Inside-Outside RNN and bidirectional recursive nets are incremental and inadequately justified. The “hybrid” embeddings idea is also known.

Significance
- The empirical improvements on SemEval would be interesting if demonstrated convincingly; however, the reliance on ensembles and extra data, the lack of controlled ablations, and limited datasets make it hard to assess broader impact. Claims of generality (e.g., word-sense disambiguation) are speculative and not evaluated.

Missing/weak baselines
- Strong sequence baselines (biLSTM/biRNN over tokens with attention or CRF), even if not tree-structured.
- Inside-Outside RNN (Le & Zuidema) and Tree-LSTM variants in a controlled setting.
- Target-dependent sentiment models (for entity-specific sentiment) if that is the claimed motivation.
- Simple, strong n-gram+lexicon SVM/LogReg baselines tuned on the same resources.

Questions for the authors
1. Please provide exact numbers (F1/accuracy with standard deviations) for all models on the official splits, including single-model GB-RNN without ensembling and without any external resources, to isolate the architectural contribution.
2. How exactly does your W↓ differ from Inside-Outside RNN, and can you show a fair comparison under matched conditions?
3. What is the effect of the subtree constraint on parsing? How often is it violated by an unconstrained parse, and what is the performance without this constraint?
4. How sensitive are results to parser errors on tweets? Have you tried a Twitter-specific parser?
5. Please clarify all shapes and equations for the backward pass and classifier, and provide enough detail to reproduce the model.
6. What is the training/inference cost relative to the baselines?

Pros
- Addresses context-dependent labeling in a principled, structured manner.
- Conceptually sound use of top-down information in trees.
- Some empirical gains on a standard benchmark, with ablations hinting at useful components.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established inside-outside/bidirectional tree models.
- Ambiguities and errors in the technical description hinder reproducibility.
- Experimental comparisons are not sufficiently controlled; heavy reliance on ensembling and extra data.
- Sparse reporting and lack of statistical rigor; potential preprocessing biases.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper’s main idea is too close to prior inside-outside/bidirectional recursive work, and the current empirical validation is not rigorous enough for NeurIPS. A revised version that (i) clearly and correctly specifies the model, (ii) provides fair, controlled comparisons to Inside-Outside RNN and strong non-tree baselines, (iii) reports complete results with statistical significance on multiple datasets, and (iv) avoids reliance on ensembles/external resources for the core claim, could be much more compelling.

Confidence
High.
