## Review of "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a sensible and well-motivated extension to recursive neural networks, addressing a key limitation of standard RNNs: the inability to effectively incorporate global context for nuanced understanding, particularly in sentiment analysis. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the existing literature and establish a strong empirical result, outperforming the SemEval 2013 competition winner. While the improvements are incremental rather than revolutionary, the idea is sound, well-executed, and the paper is clearly written. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of contextualized sentiment analysis, highlighting the failure of standard RNNs to discern sentiment directed *towards* specific entities within a sentence (e.g., “The Android’s screen is better than the iPhone’s”). The authors introduce Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which augment standard RNNs with a backward pass, propagating information from the root of the parse tree down to the leaves. This allows phrases to update their representation based on the global sentence context.  

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature, thoroughly reviewing work on word embeddings (Bengio et al., Mikolov et al.), recursive neural networks (Socher et al.), and bidirectional recurrent/recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie).  A crucial distinction is made between their approach and previous bidirectional recursive networks, emphasizing that their method better disentangles parent and child node information.  The authors also correctly identify related work in unfolding autoencoders, differentiating their approach by emphasizing the use of global context at each step. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Statement:** The authors effectively illustrate the limitations of existing approaches with a compelling example (Fig. 1) and a well-defined problem.
* **Sound Methodology:**  The GB-RNN architecture is a logical extension to standard RNNs, incorporating global context in a principled manner. The equations are clear and well-explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper demonstrates a clear performance improvement over strong baselines, including the winner of the SemEval 2013 competition. The use of a development set for hyperparameter tuning adds credibility.
* **Thorough Related Work:**  The authors demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the relevant literature, acknowledging and differentiating their work from existing approaches.
* **Hybrid Word Vector Approach:** Combining unsupervised and supervised word vector representations is a smart design choice, balancing generalization and task-specific knowledge.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' ideas.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** While the performance gain is statistically significant, the improvement over the baseline RNN (and other baselines) isn't *massive*. It raises the question of how much practical impact this will have.
* **Computational Cost:**  The backward pass adds computational complexity to the model, which isn’t explicitly discussed. A brief analysis of the increase in training/inference time would be helpful.
* **Dependency on Parser:** The performance relies heavily on the accuracy of the Stanford Parser. This is acknowledged, but a discussion of parser errors and their potential impact on results is lacking.  What happens when the parser makes mistakes?
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While a comparison to the standard RNN is provided, a more detailed ablation study varying components of the GB-RNN (e.g., the dimensionality of the feedbackward vectors, the choice of activation function in the backward pass) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** It's not entirely clear how sensitive the model is to hyperparameter choices. A brief discussion of the impact of different parameter settings would be beneficial.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a genuine limitation of standard RNNs for contextual sentiment analysis.
* Presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension to existing models.
* Demonstrates a clear empirical improvement over strong baselines on a standard benchmark dataset.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.
* Contributes a valuable addition to the toolkit for sentiment analysis.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The improvement, while statistically significant, isn’t dramatic.
* Computational cost and dependency on parser accuracy are potential concerns.
* A more thorough ablation study and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.


**5. Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Discuss computational cost:** Include a brief analysis of the additional computational overhead introduced by the backward pass.
* **Address Parser Dependency:** Discuss the potential impact of parser errors on performance. Consider experimenting with different parsers or exploring methods to make the model more robust to parsing inaccuracies.
* **Expand Ablation Study:** Include experiments varying the dimensionality of the feedbackward vectors and exploring different activation functions in the backward pass.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Discuss how the model’s performance changes with different hyperparameter settings.
* **Elaborate on practical implications:** Briefly discuss potential applications of the GB-RNN beyond sentiment analysis.




**Overall Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of recursive neural networks and contextual sentiment analysis. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
