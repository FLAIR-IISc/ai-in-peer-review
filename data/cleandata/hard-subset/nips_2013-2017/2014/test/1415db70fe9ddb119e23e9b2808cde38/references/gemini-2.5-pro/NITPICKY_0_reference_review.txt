### Summary

This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to capture global sentence context for phrase-level classification tasks, with a specific focus on aspect-based sentiment analysis. The core idea is to augment standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) with a subsequent "feedbackward" pass. After computing compositional vector representations for phrases in a bottom-up fashion, the model initiates a top-down pass from the root of the parse tree back to the leaves. This second pass allows each node's representation to be updated with information from the entire sentence context. The final classification for any given phrase is then performed on a concatenation of its feedforward and feedbackward vectors. The authors also propose a hybrid word vector representation that combines fixed, pre-trained unsupervised vectors with smaller, task-specific trainable vectors. The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it outperforms the standard RNN, various baselines, and the winning system of the competition.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Elegant Solution:** The paper effectively identifies a key limitation of standard feedforward compositional models: their inability to handle context-dependent phenomena where the meaning or label of a constituent phrase is determined by the global sentence structure. The proposed two-pass (bottom-up then top-down) mechanism is an intuitive and principled way to address this limitation by propagating global context back down to local phrases.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is convincing. The GB-RNN not only substantially outperforms its most important ablative baseline, the standard RNN (a >5% improvement), but also surpasses the winner of a competitive shared task. This demonstrates a clear and significant advance in performance on a challenging, real-world dataset.

3.  **Direct Comparison to Related Work:** The authors provide a direct and detailed mathematical comparison to the concurrent work of Irsoy and Cardie [12], which is commendable. This helps to clarify the precise architectural differences and contributions of the GB-RNN.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core contribution and strong results, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, technical details, and clarity, which collectively undermine confidence in the work.

1.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Numerous Inconsistencies:**
    *   **Dimensionality Mismatches:** Several equations appear to have incorrect matrix dimensions. In Section 3.3, the de-composition matrix `W↓` is defined as being in `R(n+nd)×(n+nd)`. However, its input is `[p; p↓]` (size `n+nd`), and its output is `[child1↓; child2↓]` (size `2nd`). Therefore, `W↓` should be in `R(2nd)×(n+nd)`. This is a critical error.
    *   Similarly, in the description of the final classifier, `ya = softmax(Wc [a a↓])`, the weight matrix `Wc` is defined as being in `RC×(n+1)`. The input vector `[a a↓]` has dimension `n+nd`, so `Wc` should be in `RC×(n+nd)` (or `RC×(n+nd+1)` if the bias is not folded in). The specified dimension `n+1` is incorrect.
    *   In Section 3.2, the description of word vectors `a ∈ Rn` being columns of `L ∈ Rn×|V|` is standard, but the initialization `ai ∼ U(−0.001, 0.001)` is imprecise. This notation suggests initializing each element of the vector, not the vector itself.
    *   The description of the Irsoy and Cardie model in Eq. 4 also seems dimensionally suspect as written, potentially misrepresenting the related work.

2.  **Poor Formatting and Presentation Quality:**
    *   **References:** The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted and incomplete. Many entries are missing conference names, years, and page numbers, and are riddled with stray commas (e.g., "International AAAI Conference on Weblogs and Social Media,, , ,"). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Missing Figures:** The text explicitly refers to "Figure 3.4" to illustrate the hybrid word vectors, but this figure is not present in the paper. This makes it impossible to fully grasp the proposed representation.
    *   **Footnote Placement:** The footnote in the introduction is awkwardly placed and disrupts the flow of the text.

3.  **Vague Justifications and Overstated Claims:**
    *   The justification for the GB-RNN's specific backward update rule (Eq. 5) compared to Irsoy and Cardie's (Eq. 4) is vague. The claim that it "helps to push the model to disentangled the children from their backward parent vector" is offered without any theoretical or empirical support.
    *   The claim in Section 3.1 that window-based methods "cannot incorporate information from words further away" is an oversimplification. While recursive models are structurally more adept, large windows can and do capture long-range linear dependencies.
    *   The hybrid word vector method, while effective, is an incremental improvement over existing practices (e.g., fine-tuning pre-trained embeddings) and perhaps should not be presented as a major novel contribution.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The terminology "development-test" set in Section 4.1 is non-standard and confusing.
    *   The description of training (Section 3.5), particularly the use of additional noisy datasets and the two-stage training process, could be explained in greater detail to improve reproducibility.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the core idea of the GB-RNN is novel and the empirical results are strong, the paper in its current state does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The sheer volume of minor flaws—ranging from critical errors in mathematical formulations and dimensionality to unprofessional formatting of the bibliography and missing figures—is substantial. These issues collectively create an impression of carelessness and detract from the reader's confidence in the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work. An academic paper is not merely a description of an idea; it is a complete, precise, and carefully crafted artifact. The strengths of the work are significant, and I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript to correct these numerous errors and resubmit to a future venue. However, in its present form, the paper falls short of the quality expected for publication.
