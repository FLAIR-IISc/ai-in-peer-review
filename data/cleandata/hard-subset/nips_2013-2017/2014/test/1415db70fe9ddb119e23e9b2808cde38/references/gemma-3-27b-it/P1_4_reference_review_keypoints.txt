- Summary: Introduces GB-RNNs with a feedbackward pass that propagates root context down parse trees to improve context-dependent semantic interpretation, showing strong results on SemEval 2013 Twitter sentiment data.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on compositional semantics and word embeddings, distinguishing its backward pass from bidirectional/recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) and autoencoder unfolding approaches.  
- Novelty: Proposes a conceptually sound feedbackward mechanism to address context-dependence in RNNs.  
- Empirical results: Outperforms standard RNNs, baselines, and the SemEval 2013 winner on a challenging benchmark.  
- Thorough experimentation: Includes baselines, ablations, various word vector initializations, and use of additional training data.  
- Clear motivation and exposition: Well-motivated problem, illustrative examples, clear equations, and helpful figures.  
- Hybrid word vectors: Combines unsupervised and supervised embeddings effectively, useful when labeled data is limited.  
- Limited theoretical analysis: Lacks deeper formal explanation of why the feedbackward pass improves performance.  
- Parser dependence: Performance relies heavily on Stanford Parser accuracy; robustness to parsing errors is not evaluated.  
- Complexity and scalability: Feedbackward pass adds significant model complexity without detailed computational cost analysis.  
- Comparison with Irsoy & Cardie: Discussion focuses on equation differences but misses a nuanced practical comparison.  
- Lack of error analysis: No detailed analysis of remaining failure cases or example types GB-RNN struggles with.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, technically sound approach with compelling empirical gains, clear writing, and broad potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Weak theoretical justification, reliance on parser accuracy, and increased model complexity may hinder adoption.  
- Overall assessment: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address theory, parser robustness, and complexity discussion.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10
