Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNN), a tree-structured neural architecture that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) with a top-down “feedback” pass at inference/training time. The forward pass composes word vectors along a parse tree via a shared composition matrix. The backward pass starts from the root, applies a transform, and recursively propagates “context” vectors down to children using both the forward parent vector and the backward parent vector; each node’s final label is predicted from the concatenation of its forward and backward vectors. The motivation is contextual disambiguation, e.g., target/term-level sentiment in tweets, where the sentiment of a term depends on sentence-level context. The paper also introduces hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a smaller, trainable supervised component, and uses dropout and AdaGrad.

Empirically, the model is evaluated on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (tweet/SMS sentiment), apparently the term-level subtask, and is reported to outperform (i) a standard feedforward RNN, (ii) a bidirectional recursive model (Irsoy & Cardie), and (iii) the competition winner. Additional performance gains come from adding external resources (NRC lexicon-derived labeled n-grams), large weakly labeled tweets, and movie reviews, and by ensembling five GB-RNN models.

Relative to prior compositional models (e.g., Socher et al. 2011–2013, Mitchell & Lapata 2010) and bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), the main architectural distinction is the specific top-down update that conditions on both forward and backward parent vectors and the use of the concatenated forward/backward node representation for classification. The hybrid word vector idea is related to prior practice of pretraining plus fine-tuning but fixes the pretrained part and only trains an added supervised slice.

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation: contextual/targeted sentiment requires long-range, tree-mediated context beyond k-window methods or unidirectional RNNs.
- Simple, general architectural idea: a top-down pass on parse trees that can be plugged into standard recursive composition; conceptually applicable beyond sentiment.
- Empirical evidence of gains over a strong baseline (feedforward RNN) and over SemEval systems, with analysis of the impact of external resources and staged training.
- Hybrid word vectors with dropout are a pragmatic contribution that appears to improve robustness.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Notation and dimensionality issues: several inconsistencies (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n, W↓ stated as (n+nd)×(n+nd) but equations imply 2nd×(n+nd); classifier Wc dimensions inconsistent with concatenated (n+nd)-dim input). These should be corrected to ensure the method is reproducible and unambiguous.
- Comparison to bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models is underdeveloped. The paper claims superiority and notes a parameter-count difference but does not control for capacity (n·nd more parameters). A parameter-matched ablation is needed to attribute gains to the design rather than capacity.
- Statistical rigor: exact scores, variance across runs, and statistical significance are not reported here. Given ensembling and multiple external resources, statistical comparisons would strengthen claims.
- Fairness of comparisons: the best reported results use ensembling and substantial external data (lexicon-derived labels, large weakly labeled tweets, movie reviews). It is unclear whether competing systems used similar external resources; single-model results without external data and without ensembling should be emphasized.
- Parsing assumptions: tweets are noisy. Constraining the parser to enforce that each labeled term is a subtree may bias evaluation and is an engineering detail that deserves discussion (e.g., how often constraints fail; effect on structure quality).

Clarity
- The manuscript contains numerous typos and terminology inconsistencies (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” “root note”), which hinder readability.
- The task setting (term-level vs message-level sentiment) should be stated clearly upfront; the current text mixes both in examples and datasets.
- Training procedure is only partially specified: exact pretrained embeddings used, vocabulary handling/OOV policy, tree binarization details, and hyperparameter ranges should be consolidated. Provide equations with consistent dimensions, and a concise algorithmic description of the top-down pass.

Originality
- The high-level idea of propagating information top-down in tree models has precedent (inside-outside RNNs; bidirectional recursive nets; unfolding RAEs). The paper’s specific recurrence—using both forward and backward parent vectors in child updates—and the hybrid fixed+trainable embeddings are incremental but reasonable. The paper should more directly position its recurrence relative to Le & Zuidema’s inside/outside formulation and provide stronger ablations to justify novelty.

Significance
- On SemEval-2013 term-level sentiment, improvements over a standard RNN (≈5% absolute per the text) are practically meaningful for targeted sentiment. If confirmed with parameter-matched comparisons and without heavy reliance on external resources/ensembles, the approach could be adopted in other tree-structured labeling tasks (e.g., aspect extraction, disambiguation).
- Generality claims (e.g., to word-sense disambiguation) are speculative without experiments beyond sentiment.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix all notation/dimension errors; provide a clean specification of W↓ block structure and Wc dimensions.
- Report complete quantitative results: per-class F1, macro/micro averages, confidence intervals, and single-model vs ensemble performance. Include parameter-matched comparisons to Irsoy & Cardie and to a variant of GB-RNN with the same parameter count.
- Clarify the subtask and data preprocessing; quantify parsing quality and the effect of subtree constraints.
- Provide ablations: effect of using only pretrained embeddings, only supervised embeddings, and their concatenation; effect of the V transform; depth-wise analysis of where top-down context helps; and removal of external resources.
- Discuss computational cost and scalability; report training/inference time relative to the baseline RNN.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of feedforward recursive models with a simple, effective architectural addition.
- Demonstrates notable empirical gains on a standard benchmark for contextual sentiment.
- Hybrid embeddings and dropout usage are practical contributions likely to generalize.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty over existing bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models is incremental and not conclusively isolated from parameter-count effects.
- Clarity and correctness issues in equations/notation.
- Heavy reliance on external resources and ensembles; limited task coverage.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept if the authors correct the technical/notation issues and strengthen the empirical comparisons (parameter-matched, single-model, significance). Otherwise, the contribution risks being incremental without sufficiently rigorous validation.
