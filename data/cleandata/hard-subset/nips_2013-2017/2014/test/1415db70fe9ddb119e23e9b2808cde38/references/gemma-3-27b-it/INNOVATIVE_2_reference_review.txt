This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) to address contextual sentiment classification, a problem highlighted by the authors as a limitation of existing recursive and recurrent models. The core idea – a backward pass through the parse tree to update node representations with global context – is genuinely novel and addresses a clear shortcoming in current approaches. While the experimental validation is solid, and the results demonstrably outperform baselines and even the SemEval 2013 winner, the boldness of the idea and the potential for broader impact are the most compelling aspects of this work.

The motivation is well articulated. The example in Figure 1 effectively illustrates the context-dependence issue, and the explanation of why standard RNNs fail to capture it is convincing. The authors rightly point out the limitations of both recurrent and window-based methods in handling long-range dependencies. The GB-RNN is presented clearly, with the equations well-defined and the explanation of the forward and backward passes easy to follow. The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN is also helpful, clarifying the key difference in how context is incorporated. The hybrid word vector approach is a sensible addition, and the justification for combining unsupervised and supervised representations is sound.

A significant strength is the empirical evaluation. The authors not only demonstrate superior performance on the SemEval dataset but also provide a thorough ablation study, validating the contributions of different components of the GB-RNN. Including results comparing to the winner of the SemEval competition is impactful, and a strong indication of the model’s effectiveness. The inclusion of several baselines provides further confidence in the results.

However, the paper isn't without minor weaknesses. While the explanation of the backward pass is clear, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this backward pass helps resolve contextual ambiguity might be beneficial. The effect of the dimension *nd* used for the feedback vectors is not extensively explored. Is it critical to be different than *n*? A more thorough analysis of the parameter sensitivity would be valuable. The discussion on the impact of additional training data (NRC lexicon, movie reviews) feels a bit tacked on and could be better integrated with the core results.  The impact of the additional data seems small relative to the core contribution of the GB-RNN itself. Additionally, while the authors mention potential applications beyond sentiment analysis (word-sense disambiguation), a brief discussion on how the model would need to be adapted for other tasks would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The backward pass mechanism is a genuinely novel approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental limitation of existing models and achieves state-of-the-art results.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the model is clearly explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Thorough evaluation with strong results on a standard dataset.
*   **Potential Impact:** The concept of propagating beliefs through recursive networks is potentially applicable to a wider range of NLP tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor parameter sensitivity analysis missing.
*   Discussion of additional training data is somewhat superficial.
*   Limited exploration of applications beyond sentiment analysis.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a highly innovative idea. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. The novelty and potential impact of the GB-RNN are compelling, and I believe this work represents a significant step forward in compositional semantics and contextual understanding. I recommend acceptance despite the minor areas for improvement. The willingness to explore a bold idea and demonstrate its effectiveness through rigorous experimentation outweighs any methodological imperfections.
