This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) aiming to improve contextual sentiment analysis by incorporating a backward pass in the recursive computation. The motivation stems from the observation that sentiment often depends on broader context, a limitation of standard RNNs. The authors propose a novel feedback mechanism and a hybrid word vector initialization strategy. While the problem addressed is relevant and the idea of bidirectional context propagation within a recursive framework is intuitive, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, coupled with a questionable level of novelty, ultimately leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction adequately sets the stage, but feels somewhat repetitive in reiterating the limitations of existing approaches. The example provided in Figure 1 is helpful, but the claim that *all* existing models would fail on it is an overstatement – bidirectional RNNs, for instance, would likely perform better. The justification for the backward pass, highlighting the limitation of k-word windows, is valid, but lacks a strong theoretical underpinning.

The related work section is comprehensive but occasionally lacks critical analysis. Simply stating that bidirectional recursive models were “developed in parallel” is insufficient; a detailed comparison of the proposed method with Irsoy & Cardie’s work (mentioned in section 3.6) should be presented *in the related work section* rather than deferred. The distinction between unfolding for training vs. inference is noted, but the benefits of the proposed approach over simple unfolding are not convincingly articulated.

Section 3, detailing the GB-RNN, is where the majority of the issues reside. The notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent. The explanation of Eq. (3) is particularly unclear – the role of the subcripts 'lf', 'lb', 'rf', and 'rb' are not immediately obvious and require careful tracing back to the previous equation. The justification for introducing the transformation matrix V in Eq. (2) feels weak, hinging on differentiating features and reducing parameters; a more compelling argument would be needed. The description of the hybrid word vector representation is clear, but the motivation for concatenating rather than summing the vectors is missing. The explanation of training relies heavily on vague references to “regularized cross-entropy error” and “mini-batched AdaGrad”; details regarding the regularization terms and AdaGrad parameters are absent. 

The comparison to other models (section 3.6) is superficially presented. The rewrite of Irsoy & Cardie's equation (4) is helpful for highlighting differences, but the authors' claim that their method "helps to push the model to disentangled the children from their backward parent vector" lacks empirical support and sounds speculative. 

The experimental setup (section 4) has several shortcomings. The reliance on the Stanford Parser introduces a potential source of error and bias, which is not acknowledged. The justification for parsing phrases into individual subtrees is weak. The claim of outperforming the SemEval 2013 winners is significant, but the details of the ensemble method are scant. The explanation of additional training data is perfunctory; a more rigorous analysis of the impact of each dataset would be valuable. Table 3 demonstrates the benefit of the hybrid word vector approach, but lacks statistical significance testing.

Numerous minor issues further detract from the paper's quality. Several sentences are awkwardly worded. The figure captions are insufficiently descriptive. There is an inconsistency in the referencing style (e.g., [27, 11, 15] vs. [26]). The inclusion of an acknowledgements section feels premature given the paper’s shortcomings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in sentiment analysis.
*   Presents a novel approach to incorporating contextual information in recursive neural networks.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on a benchmark dataset.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, explanation, and experimental setup.
*   Questionable level of novelty – closely related work exists.
*   Lack of theoretical justification for key design choices.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental analysis.
*   Weakly motivated design decisions.
*   Poor clarity in certain sections.



Overall, while the idea presented in this paper has potential, its execution is marred by a multitude of shortcomings. The lack of clarity, coupled with the superficial treatment of related work and experimental analysis, prevents a positive assessment. Despite achieving competitive results, the paper does not demonstrate a sufficient level of technical rigor or originality to warrant acceptance into NIPS. I recommend rejection.
