## Review of Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), an extension to standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) designed to address the limitations of feedforward architectures in capturing context-dependent meaning, particularly in tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The core idea is to incorporate a feedbackward pass during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on Twitter sentiment data, achieving state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge and showing improved performance over standard RNNs. They also present findings on the benefits of dropout and a hybrid unsupervised/supervised word vector approach.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the shortcomings of existing feedforward RNNs in handling contextual sentiment. The GB-RNN concept is logically presented and the mathematical formulation is reasonably clear. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a consistent 3% F1 performance improvement over standard RNNs and surpassing the performance of the SemEval 2013 winner. The ablation studies, examining the impact of different word vector representations and dropout, are valuable and provide further insight into the model's behavior. The comparison to Irsoy and Cardie’s bidirectional RNN is also helpful in highlighting the differences in approach.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the explanation of the GB-RNN is clear, the justification for the specific feedbackward computation (Eq. 3) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed explanation of *why* this particular formulation is expected to be effective would be beneficial. Furthermore, the discussion of related work, while present, could be more comprehensive. For example, recent attention mechanisms in tree-structured RNNs (e.g., Tai et al., 2015) which also address context dependence, are not mentioned. The paper also lacks a thorough error analysis. Understanding *where* the model still fails could provide valuable directions for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating context into RNNs.
*   Strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance.
*   Clear presentation and well-defined experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited justification for the specific feedbackward computation.
*   Missing related work (e.g., attention mechanisms in tree-structured RNNs).
*   Lack of error analysis.

**Overall:**

Despite some minor shortcomings, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The GB-RNN is a promising approach to addressing the limitations of standard RNNs in capturing context-dependent meaning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed justification for the feedbackward computation and an expanded related work section. The authors should also consider including a brief error analysis to identify areas for future improvement.
