

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard recursive neural networks by adding a "feedbackward" step during inference to allow context-dependent phrase and word labeling. The authors argue that standard RNNs cannot correctly predict labels that depend on sentence context (e.g., determining that "Android" is positive in "Android beats iOS"), and propose GB-RNNs as a solution. The model computes both feedforward and feedbackward vectors at each node of a parse tree, concatenating them for final classification. The authors also introduce a hybrid word vector representation combining unsupervised and supervised vectors, and demonstrate that dropout improves training. On the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, GB-RNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance, improving F1 by 3% over standard RNNs.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of compositional models for NLP. It builds on the recursive neural network work of Socher et al. (2013), which has been influential at NIPS, and extends it with a novel feedback mechanism. The authors adequately contextualize their work against related approaches, including bidirectional RNNs (Irsoy and Cardie, 2013), and provide a detailed theoretical comparison of their model.

Strengths:
1. The core idea of adding a feedbackward step is well-motivated by clear examples of context-dependent sentiment classification. The paper effectively demonstrates that standard RNNs cannot solve this problem.
2. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with ablation studies showing the contribution of the feedback mechanism, hybrid word vectors, and dropout.
3. The results on SemEval 2013 are compelling, with GB-RNN outperforming both standard RNNs and the competition winner.
4. The paper makes a useful contribution to understanding how context can be incorporated into compositional models.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why the feedbackward step works. While the authors compare mathematically to bidirectional RNNs, they don't provide a strong theoretical argument for why their specific formulation is superior. A more detailed analysis of error cases would also help.
2. The comparison to other context modeling approaches is somewhat limited. The paper doesn't discuss how GB-RNNs compare to window-based methods or other context-aware models that might handle long-distance dependencies differently.
3. The paper doesn't sufficiently address computational costs. How much more expensive is the feedbackward step? This is particularly relevant for practical applications.
4. The hybrid word vector approach is well-motivated but could be better justified. The paper states that "this performs better than both the purely supervised and unsupervised semantic word vectors" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence beyond the results in Table 3.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with helpful figures illustrating the model architecture. The mathematical notation is precise and consistent. The related work section adequately covers relevant literature, though it could better distinguish the current work from other approaches to context-dependent modeling in NLP.

Significance: The paper addresses an important limitation of current compositional models in NLP, showing that context can be effectively incorporated to improve performance on sentiment analysis. The approach has potential applications beyond sentiment analysis, as the authors note (e.g., word-sense disambiguation), making it a potentially valuable contribution to the field.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that makes a clear, well-executed advance on existing recursive neural network models. The core idea is novel and well-supported by experiments. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the feedback mechanism and its limitations.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of compositional models for NLP with clear theoretical and empirical results. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work is well-suited for NIPS.
