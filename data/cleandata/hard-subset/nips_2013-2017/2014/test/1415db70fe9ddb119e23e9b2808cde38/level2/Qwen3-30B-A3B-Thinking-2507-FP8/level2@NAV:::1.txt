

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard feedforward recursive neural networks by adding a "feedbackward" step during inference to allow context-dependent phrase and word labeling. The authors position this as a solution to the limitation of current RNNs that cannot correctly predict labels determined by broader sentence context, such as aspect-specific sentiment analysis where "Android" should be labeled positive in "Android beats iOS" but neutral in isolation. The main claims are that GB-RNNs improve F1 performance by 3% over standard RNNs on contextual sentiment analysis, that hybrid unsupervised-supervised word vectors outperform either approach alone, and that the feedbackward mechanism enables accurate context-dependent predictions.

The paper demonstrates reasonable support for its claims through experiments on the SemEval 2013 dataset, showing that GB-RNNs outperform both standard RNNs and the competition winner. The comparison to bidirectional RNNs is thorough, with a detailed theoretical and empirical analysis of the differences in architecture. The use of hybrid word vectors and dropout is well-justified and supported by ablation studies. The paper also provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including hyperparameter settings and training procedures.

The work demonstrates good clarity in explaining the model architecture, with clear equations and figures illustrating the forward and feedbackward passes. The motivation for context-dependent sentiment analysis is well-articulated and addresses a genuine limitation in existing approaches.

The novelty is somewhat incremental, as the paper acknowledges similarities to Irsoy and Cardie's bidirectional RNN work. While the feedbackward mechanism is conceptually interesting, the paper could better clarify how this differs from other context integration approaches (e.g., attention mechanisms) that have been widely adopted in recent NLP work.

The significance is moderate. Contextual sentiment analysis is a practical problem with real-world applications, but the 3% F1 improvement might not be sufficient to justify widespread adoption without further demonstration of broader applicability. The paper would benefit from more discussion of limitations, such as the dependency on parser quality and potential computational overhead.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a modest but meaningful contribution to contextual sentiment analysis. With stronger justification for the novelty and more explicit discussion of limitations, it would be a stronger submission. The quality is good, clarity is high, novelty is incremental but valid, and significance is moderate. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty and limitations discussion.
