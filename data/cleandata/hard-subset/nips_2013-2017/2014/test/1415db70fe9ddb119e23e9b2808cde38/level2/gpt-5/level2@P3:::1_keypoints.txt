- Paper introduces GB-RNNs: adds a single top-down “feedbackward” pass to recursive tree models, concatenating feedback with forward node representations for node-level classification.  
- Targets contextual (target/phrase-specific) sentiment classification where labels depend on long-range context.  
- Compares to standard RNNs, bidirectional recurrent models, and concurrent bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models, arguing differences in backward equations and flexibility.  
- Proposes hybrid word representations (fixed unsupervised embeddings + small task-tuned embeddings) and dropout on node vectors.  
- Reports empirical gains on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter), improved further with external lexicons and distant supervision.  

Strengths:
- Addresses recursive models’ lack of context-dependent label updates with a principled top-down pass leveraging global sentence information.  
- Clear formulation of backward recursion and explicit comparison to related bidirectional recursive work.  
- Practical contributions (hybrid embeddings, node dropout) supported by ablations.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvements on a standard benchmark, focusing on underrepresented target-level sentiment.  
- Uses only parser and word vectors as core resources; gains are improved by optional external resources with ablation discussion.  

Weaknesses / Concerns:
- Reproducibility: key quantitative results are reported narratively without full tables, exact numbers, variances, or significance tests.  
- Fairness of comparisons: best results mix ensembles and external data; single-model/no-external results should be clearly separated.  
- Baseline scope: lacks stronger non-tree neural baselines (e.g., window-based models, BiRNNs); claim that recurrent/window methods can’t capture long-range context is overstated.  
- Dependence on parsing for noisy tweets: reliance on binarized constituency parses and subtree constraint may introduce artifacts or bias; robustness to parse errors not quantified.  
- Clarity/typos: minor presentation issues (typos, stray lines) and Eq. (3) needs clearer shapes/intuitions.  
- Training vs inference confusion: ambiguous claim that backward pass is “during inference” while backward parameters are trained — needs clarification.  

Quality:
- Conceptually sound and well-motivated; architectural differences to concurrent work are articulated.  
- Empirical claims plausible but cannot be fully assessed without exact results and significance testing.  
- Additional diagnostics (distance-to-target, tree distance, error categories) would better support causal claims about the top-down pass.  

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized with good motivation and equations, but needs proofreading and clearer explanation of Eq. (3), tensor shapes, and computational graph.  
- Training details are reasonable; a succinct algorithmic summary of forward/backward passes is recommended.  

Originality:
- Top-down recursion idea is concurrent with other work; specific backward formulation conditioning on both forward and backward parents and hybrid embedding/dropout combination are incremental but nontrivial.  
- Application to target-level sentiment within recursive architecture is timely.  

Significance:
- Addresses an important task; single top-down pass idea is broadly applicable to other span-labeling problems beyond sentiment.  

Questions / Requests:
- Provide exact test-set scores (macro and per-class F1) with confidence intervals for single RNN, single Bi-RecNN, single GB-RNN, and ensemble GB-RNN, indicating external data usage.  
- Report results stepwise: without external resources and ensembling, then with added resources to isolate contributions.  
- Clarify whether the backward pass is used during both training and inference and how its parameters are trained.  
- Explain how subtree constraint is enforced and whether it uses label information; analyze robustness to parse errors.  
- Provide runtime, parameter counts, and overhead of top-down pass relative to standard RNN.  

Essential suggestions:
- Include complete quantitative tables with standard deviations and significance testing; separate single vs ensemble and with/without external data.  
- Add non-tree BiRNN and window-based neural baselines with comparable capacity.  
- Clarify Eq. (3), dimensions, and provide a concise algorithm for forward/backward computations.  

Nice-to-have suggestions:
- Error analysis by token/tree distance to target, dependency types, and parse quality.  
- Robustness study to noisy parses (e.g., using a simpler chunker) and ablation on the subtree constraint.  
- Analysis of hybrid embeddings: gradient flow into supervised part and effect of freezing vs fine-tuning unsupervised embeddings.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap in recursive models with a simple, general mechanism for global context.  
- Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark with ablations indicating contribution sources.  
- Method likely useful beyond sentiment tasks.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental reporting lacks detail for verification; comparisons conflate ensembles/resources with single-model results.  
- Incremental originality relative to concurrent bidirectional recursive work and insufficient baseline coverage.  
- Heavy reliance on parsing in a noisy domain without robustness assessment.  

Overall assessment:
- Promising and practically useful contribution that would benefit from strengthened experimental reporting, clearer baselines, and robustness analyses.
