This paper addresses a key limitation of standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs): their inability to model how global sentence context affects the meaning and classification of constituent words and phrases. The authors propose the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), which extends the standard bottom-up RNN with a top-down "feedbackward" pass during inference. This allows information from the full sentence representation to propagate back down the parse tree, refining the representations and predictions at each node. The work is well-situated in the context of compositional models and appropriately discusses related work on bidirectional recursive models, particularly that of Irsoy and Cardie [12].

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Pros:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of contextual sentiment analysis is important, and the proposed feedbackward mechanism is an intuitive and novel approach to incorporate global information. The model's architecture, while related to other bidirectional models, is distinct in its formulation and demonstrates a clear improvement.
2.  **Quality:** The claims are convincingly supported by strong empirical results on the competitive SemEval 2013 dataset, where the model achieves state-of-the-art performance. The ablation studies are thorough and effectively demonstrate the value of the feedbackward pass (a >5% improvement over the standard RNN), the proposed hybrid word vectors, and the use of dropout.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the model is explained clearly with equations and figures. The comparison to the parallel work of Irsoy and Cardie is particularly helpful for understanding the model's specific contributions.

While the work is strong, there are a few minor weaknesses.
**Cons:**
1.  **Controlled Comparison:** The paper notes that its model has more parameters than the bidirectional-RNN from Irsoy and Cardie [12]. While the GB-RNN outperforms it, it is unclear if this is due to the architectural superiority or simply the larger parameter count. A more controlled comparison matching the number of parameters would strengthen the claim that the specific formulation is better.
2.  **Lack of Limitations Section:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the limitations of the proposed approach. For example, the model's performance is likely sensitive to the quality of the initial syntactic parse. A brief discussion of potential failure modes or areas for future work would make the contribution more complete.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, effective, and well-evaluated model for an important NLP task. The contributions are significant and advance the state of the art. I strongly recommend acceptance.
