- Proposes GB-RNN: a tree-structured model adding a single top-down “feedbackward” pass to bottom-up RNNs so local predictions incorporate sentence-level context.  
- Uses forward RNN to compute node vectors, then top-down vectors via learned V and W↓, classifying nodes from concatenated forward+backward vectors; also introduces hybrid embeddings and node dropout.  
- Positioned relative to RNN/RNTN compositional models, bidirectional recurrent/recursive models, and unfolding/autoencoder approaches.  
- Evaluated on contextual sentiment (SemEval 2013 Task 2 Twitter), reporting ~+3 F1 over standard RNN and state-of-the-art results with ensembling and external resources.  

Strengths:
- Clear, practical motivation: a top-down pass addresses a real shortcoming of purely bottom-up compositional models for contextual sentiment.  
- Simple, modular extension to standard RNNs that is easy to implement on top of parser-based models.  
- Empirical gains supported by ablations showing benefits from feedback, dropout, and hybrid embeddings.  
- Sensible use of external data and two-stage training (pretrain forward RNN, then full GB-RNN).  
- Useful discussion contrasting GB-RNN with bidirectional recursive networks and the design choice to condition backward children on both forward and backward parent vectors.

Weaknesses / Concerns:
- Inconsistency: paper claims backward step used only at inference but trains with backprop through combined architecture; needs clarification whether backward pass is used in training, inference, or both.  
- Contradiction: Section 3.6 states backward node vectors don’t use forward vectors, but Eq.(3) conditions backward on forward parent vectors — undermines clarity.  
- Parameterization confusion: inconsistent/unclear parameter counts for W↓ and V; needs precise shapes and complexity analysis plus wall-clock costs versus baselines.  
- Evaluation details lacking: tables missing here but paper should report official SemEval metrics, mean/variance over runs, and fairness of comparisons to competition systems.  
- Parser reliance: no analysis of robustness to noisy Twitter parses or the effect of forcing labeled spans to be constituents, which may bias results.  
- Baseline matching: bidirectional sequence/tree baselines may not be capacity-matched or trained with same embeddings/dropout, leaving unclear whether gains stem from GB-RNN specifics.  
- Ablation gaps: need more granular tests (e.g., remove V, use only backward vectors, no external data, depth-wise/long-distance analyses).  
- Terminology risk: “Global belief” suggests iterative probabilistic inference, but model is a single learned top-down transform — clarify to avoid misleading readers.

Quality:
- Core idea is sound and verifiable, but inconsistencies about forward/backward conditioning and unclear parameter counts reduce technical clarity.

Clarity:
- Manuscript generally readable but contains typos (“feedbackward”, “feedwordard”, “root note”) and notation slips; needs cleaner equations and explicit algorithmic description for forward/backward passes.

Originality:
- Combining a top-down pass with bottom-up RNNs is a natural but nontrivial, incremental extension; conditioning backward children on both forward and backward parent vectors and hybrid embeddings are useful contributions.

Significance:
- Solves an important practical problem (context-dependent sentiment); if gains hold under fair settings, this is a meaningful and adoptable advance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix contradiction in Section 3.6 and provide a clear diagram/pseudocode showing how backward vectors are computed at train/test time.  
- Provide exact parameter shapes/counts, complexity analysis, and runtime/memory metrics.  
- Expand empirics: report single-model vs ensemble, standard deviations, and apples-to-apples comparisons with matched baselines and no-external-resources conditions.  
- Analyze parsing robustness and effect of constituent constraint for labeled spans; include depth-wise and long-distance contextual evaluations and qualitative error analysis.  
- Clean up typos and notation throughout.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation with a simple, effective architectural modification.  
- Demonstrates empirical gains on a competitive benchmark with practical training tricks.  
- Potentially broadly applicable beyond sentiment (e.g., WSD, NER with context).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Key internal inconsistencies and clarity issues in the model description.  
- Possibly unfair or incomplete comparisons and heavy reliance on ensembling/external resources for top results.  
- Insufficient analysis of parser robustness and generality beyond SemEval Twitter data.

Overall recommendation:
- Weak accept conditional on clarifying the model, resolving inconsistencies, and strengthening controlled empirical comparisons.

- Medium.
