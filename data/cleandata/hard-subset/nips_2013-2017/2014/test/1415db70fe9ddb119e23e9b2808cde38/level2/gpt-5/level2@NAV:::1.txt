Summary and relation to prior work: The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured model that augments standard recursive neural networks with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. This pass conditions node-level predictions on global sentence context, addressing limitations of purely bottom-up composition for context-dependent labeling (e.g., aspect-targeted sentiment). The authors relate GB-RNN to bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, NIPS DL Workshop) and to unfolding/autoencoder-style approaches, and position it against windowed and recurrent baselines. On SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), GB-RNN improves F1 over a standard RNN by ~3–5%, achieves state-of-the-art results (with an ensemble), and shows gains from hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + small supervised part) and dropout.

Quality: The model is technically sound and clearly specified (forward and backward equations, role of V and W↓, training via AdaGrad/BTTS, dropout). Empirical support includes comparisons to competition systems, ablations (RNN vs. bi-recursive vs. GB-RNN), and analysis of external resources. However, statistical significance tests are missing, and it is unclear how much of the SOTA gain comes from ensembling and additional supervision (lexicons, distant supervision) versus the feedbackward step itself. Runtime/complexity and parser error sensitivity are not quantified.

Clarity: Writing is mostly clear, with intuitive motivation and diagrams. Some notation and typos (“feedwordard,” “shold”) should be corrected. Please provide exact hyperparameters and training details sufficient for reproduction (final dims for all matrices, learning rates, early stopping criteria, random seeds), and clarify the constraint that labeled spans align with subtrees.

Originality: The idea of a single top-down pass at inference to condition local predictions on global sentence context is a neat and distinct variant from prior bidirectional recursive formulations. The hybrid word vector concatenation plus dropout is incremental but practical.

Significance/usefulness: The method provides measurable gains on contextual/targeted sentiment and is likely useful to practitioners working with tree-structured models. Broader applicability (e.g., WSD or other token-level labeling) is plausible but untested.

Limitations and constructive suggestions:
- Report per-component ablations isolating: feedbackward step, hybrid embeddings, dropout, external resources, and ensembling. Add significance testing.
- Compare to strong non-tree baselines that capture long-range context (e.g., wide-window CNNs/bi-RNNs) and to structured models (e.g., tree-CRF).
- Analyze robustness to parsing errors and provide efficiency metrics.
- Provide error analysis on aspect-targeted cases and long-distance dependencies.
- Consider multiple feedback iterations and/or joint training objectives.

Pros:
- Clear motivation; simple, effective architectural change
- Solid gains over strong baselines; SOTA on SemEval
- Thoughtful comparison to bidirectional recursive models

Cons:
- Limited significance/ablation depth; ensemble and extra data confound attribution
- Single dataset; no robustness/efficiency analysis
- Reproducibility details incomplete and minor clarity issues

Overall, a promising, well-motivated contribution; with stronger analysis and clearer attribution, it would be a solid accept.
