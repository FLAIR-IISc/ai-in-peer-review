Summary
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured neural model that augments standard bottom-up recursive neural networks with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The goal is to allow phrase- and sentence-level context to revise local predictions, addressing cases where labels of words/phrases depend on broader context (e.g., aspect-level sentiment within a sentence). The forward pass computes standard recursive composition, and the backward pass propagates a context vector from the root to all nodes via learned transformations; final node-level predictions use the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The authors also propose hybrid word vectors (concatenating fixed unsupervised embeddings with small supervised, task-tuned dimensions) and apply dropout to node representations. On SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), GB-RNN outperforms strong baselines, the competition winner, a standard feedforward RNN, and a bidirectional recursive baseline. Ablations indicate that the top-down pass yields 3–5% F1 improvement, hybrid embeddings help, and distant supervision and lexicon-based augmentation add further gains. The paper relates GB-RNN to bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie) and to “unfolding” ideas in autoencoders.

Strengths
- Clear, important motivation: many token/phrase labels are context-dependent; a pure bottom-up tree model is insufficient.
- Simple, general mechanism: a single learned top-down pass over the parse tree that can be plugged onto existing recursive models; conceptually akin to belief propagation.
- Solid empirical results: improved F1 over a standard RNN and over the SemEval 2013 winner; careful ablations for the feedback pass, hybrid word vectors, and dropout.
- Fair comparison to a closely related bidirectional recursive model, with both theoretical discussion and empirical evaluation.
- Practical training recipe (AdaGrad, dropout on node representations) and a sensible data pipeline (distant supervision + fine-tuning on task data).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Inference vs training ambiguity: The abstract stresses “one feedbackward step during inference,” but Section 3.5 trains the full GB-RNN with backprop through structure. Please clarify precisely: is the top-down pass used during training and test, or only at test for re-scoring? If trained jointly, how are gradients flowing through the top-down branch handled; if not, why not?
- Resource confounds: While the paper provides some ablations, please report a table that isolates the effect of the GB-RNN architecture under identical resources (no external lexicon, no distant supervision), comparing: logistic/NB, context-window models, standard RNN, bidirectional recursive baseline, and GB-RNN. Then add rows incrementally with lexicon and distant supervision to make the source of gains unambiguous.
- Statistical significance: Provide significance tests for the reported improvements (e.g., over the bidirectional recursive baseline), especially for the 3–5% F1 gaps.
- Computational complexity and speed: Analyze the additional parameters (n·nd) and runtime of the top-down pass. What are training and inference time multipliers vs standard RNN? How sensitive are results to nd and to the root transform V (versus copying the root)?
- Robustness to parsing noise: The approach depends on accurate binary parses of noisy tweets. Please provide an analysis of performance vs parse quality (e.g., using a chunker, or different parsers), or show that the gains persist on a subset with higher-quality parses.
- Scope of evaluation: Consider adding results on a second dataset with explicit aspect/entity-level supervision (e.g., sentence-level with target mentions) to better demonstrate context-dependent disambiguation beyond SemEval tweets. A comparison on SST (context-independent sentiment) could also help show that GB-RNN does not harm when context is less critical.
- Clarity issues: A few notational/terminological choices impede readability. For example, the paper uses “RNN” to denote both recursive neural networks and recurrent models in the related work; consider disambiguating. There are minor typos and a stray block of unrelated sentences in Section 3.6; please clean these and ensure all equations specify dimensions consistently (especially Eq. 3).
- Baselines: Since the claim centers on long-range context, a strong chain-based BiRNN/BiLSTM with sufficiently wide context would be a useful additional baseline, even if it cannot use arbitrary-length tree dependencies.

Quality
The model is sound and well-motivated. The training procedure is standard (BPTS + AdaGrad + dropout). Comparisons include relevant ablations and a closely related bidirectional recursive architecture. However, the paper would benefit from significance testing, an explicit no-external-resources setting, and a more complete computational analysis.

Clarity
Overall the paper is readable, with clear figures and equations. Some terminology (feedbackward/forward, RNN ambiguity) and minor text artifacts should be addressed. Please clarify the training/inference use of the top-down pass and provide a concise algorithmic description of the full forward computation used at train/test time.

Originality
The idea of a top-down pass on trees is related to bidirectional recursive models and inside–outside style computations, but the specific formulation (concatenated forward and feedbackward vectors, root transform V, and W↓ structure) is a meaningful variant. The hybrid word vectors with dropout are pragmatic and useful, though less novel.

Significance
The gains on a widely used benchmark and the simplicity of the mechanism suggest this approach will be used and extended. The idea of a single global-context pass is broadly applicable to token-level labeling on trees (e.g., WSD, NER with syntactic signals). Strengthening the evaluation and clarifying the role of external resources would further bolster impact.

Questions for the authors
- Is the top-down pass learned jointly with the bottom-up pass, or applied only at test time? If jointly trained, how were the losses placed (all labeled nodes?), and do you use any auxiliary loss to encourage agreement between forward and backward features?
- How sensitive are results to nd and to using V vs copying the root? Any degradation when nd is small?
- Are forward and backward parameters shared across left/right child positions? Did you try position-specific parameterization?
- Can you provide an error analysis showing which phenomena benefit most (e.g., negation scope, long-distance modifiers, comparative constructs)?

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper tackles an important limitation of recursive models with a simple, general, and empirically effective mechanism. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the experimental controls would make it a strong contribution.
