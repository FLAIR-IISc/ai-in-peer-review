- Introduces GB-RNNs: a tree-structured model adding one top-down “feedbackward” inference pass to condition node predictions on global context.  
- Positions GB-RNN relative to bidirectional recursive models and unfolding/autoencoder approaches; compares to windowed and recurrent baselines.  
- Reports ~3–5% F1 improvement over standard RNN on SemEval-2013 Task 2 and state-of-the-art results with an ensemble.  
- Uses hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + small supervised part) and dropout, which contribute to performance.  
- Model specification, forward/backward equations, and training (AdaGrad/BTTS, dropout) are clearly presented.  
- Empirical evaluation includes competition comparisons and ablation between RNN, bi-recursive, and GB-RNN variants.  
- Missing statistical significance tests; unclear how much SOTA gain is due to ensembling and extra supervision versus the feedbackward step.  
- Runtime/complexity and sensitivity to parser errors are not measured or reported.  
- Writing is mostly clear with good motivation and diagrams, but contains typos and some notation issues.  
- Reproducibility details are incomplete: final matrix dimensions, learning rates, early stopping, and random seeds are not provided.  
- Constraint that labeled spans align with subtrees needs clarification.  
- The single top-down inference pass is a novel and distinct variant from prior bidirectional recursive methods.  
- Hybrid embedding concatenation plus dropout is practical but incremental.  
- Method is useful for contextual/targeted sentiment and likely applicable to other token-level tasks, though untested.  
- Suggested improvements: per-component ablations (feedbackward, embeddings, dropout, resources, ensembling) and significance testing.  
- Suggested comparisons: strong non-tree baselines (wide-window CNNs/bi-RNNs) and structured models (tree-CRF).  
- Suggested analyses: robustness to parsing errors, efficiency metrics, error analysis on aspect-targeted and long-distance cases.  
- Consider exploring multiple feedback iterations and joint training objectives.  
- Pros: clear motivation, simple effective change, solid gains, and thoughtful comparison to bidirectional recursive models.  
- Cons: limited ablation/significance depth, confounding by ensemble and extra data, single dataset, and incomplete reproducibility details.  
- Overall: promising and well-motivated; with stronger analysis and clearer attribution, merits acceptance.
