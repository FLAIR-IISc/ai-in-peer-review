This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to address a key limitation of standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs): their inability to model context-dependent phenomena. Standard RNNs compose representations bottom-up from a parse tree, meaning a word or phrase's representation is fixed before the full sentence context is observed. This is problematic for tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis, where the sentiment of a word (e.g., "Android") can depend on the rest of the sentence (e.g., "Android beats iOS"). The proposed GB-RNN extends the standard RNN by adding a "feedbackward" pass. After a standard bottom-up pass computes representations for all nodes in the parse tree, a top-down pass propagates information from the root back down to the leaves, creating new "backward" vectors. The final prediction for any node is then based on a combination of its forward and backward vectors. The paper also proposes using hybrid word vectors (concatenating fixed unsupervised vectors with trainable supervised ones) and demonstrates the utility of dropout for training RNNs. The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it outperforms the standard RNN, other baselines, and achieves state-of-the-art results.

**Quality:**
The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments on a competitive public benchmark. The authors are careful to compare their model not only against simpler baselines like logistic regression but also against a strong standard RNN baseline, ensuring that the reported gains are attributable to the novel components of their model. The inclusion of an ablation study (Table 3) that isolates the contribution of the proposed hybrid word vector representation is commendable and strengthens the paper's conclusions. The empirical results, showing a 3% F1 improvement over the standard RNN and outperforming the winner of the SemEval 2013 challenge, are convincing. The training details are described well, including the use of AdaGrad and cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for the model is clearly articulated in the introduction with a compelling example. Figure 2 provides a helpful visual illustration of the two-pass (feedforward and feedbackward) architecture. The model is described with sufficient mathematical detail. One minor suggestion for improvement would be to further clarify the recursive definition of the backward pass. Equation 3, which defines the computation for the children nodes `a↓` and `p↓1` based on the parent `p2` and `p↓2`, is slightly condensed. A more general, recursive formulation describing how any parent's vectors are used to compute its children's backward vectors might make this core component of the model even easier to grasp. The discussion in Section 3.6, which explicitly compares the GB-RNN to the contemporary bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie, is particularly helpful for situating the work and highlighting its unique contributions.

**Originality:**
The core idea of adding a top-down, feedbackward pass to a recursive neural network to incorporate global context is novel and well-motivated. While bidirectional models exist for sequential data (recurrent networks), this work provides a principled way to achieve a similar effect for tree-structured data. The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from the most closely related work (Irsoy and Cardie, 2013), clearly explaining the differences in the formulation of the backward pass. The proposed hybrid word vector representation, while a simpler contribution, is also a novel and practical idea that effectively combines the strengths of general-purpose unsupervised vectors and task-specific supervised ones.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in natural language processing. Many NLP tasks, including word-sense disambiguation, co-reference resolution, and aspect-specific sentiment analysis, require models to make local predictions that are informed by global context. The GB-RNN offers an elegant and effective solution that is more powerful than simple context-window approaches, as it can propagate information across the entire sentence guided by its syntactic structure. The demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on a challenging task suggests that this approach is highly effective. The ideas presented here are likely to be built upon by other researchers working on compositional models and context-aware NLP tasks.

**Additional Questions and Comments:**
- The model's performance relies on an input parse tree. Could the authors comment on the model's sensitivity to parsing errors, which are known to be more frequent in the type of noisy social media text used in the experiments?
- The feedbackward pass presumably adds computational cost over a standard RNN. A brief comment on the increase in training and/or inference time would be useful for practitioners considering this model.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant model, backs it up with solid experimental results, and is presented with clarity. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the main contributions. I recommend acceptance.
