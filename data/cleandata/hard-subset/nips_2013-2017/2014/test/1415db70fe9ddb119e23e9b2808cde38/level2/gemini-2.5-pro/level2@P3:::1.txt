**Summary**
This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to address the limitations of purely feedforward recursive architectures in capturing context-dependent meaning. Standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) compose representations from the bottom-up based on a parse tree, which makes it difficult to correctly label words or phrases whose meaning is determined by the broader sentence context. The paper's core contribution is a "feedbackward" pass that follows the initial feedforward computation. This top-down pass propagates information from the sentence's root representation back down to the leaves, allowing each node's representation to be updated with global information before a final classification is made. The work also proposes a hybrid word vector representation, combining fixed unsupervised vectors with task-specific trainable vectors, and shows the effectiveness of dropout for training RNNs. The model is evaluated on the task of aspect-specific sentiment analysis using the SemEval 2013 Twitter dataset, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming the standard RNN and the winning systems of the original competition.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by strong empirical results on a challenging and standard benchmark. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons against multiple baselines, an important ablation (the standard RNN), and the top systems from the SemEval 2013 competition. The inclusion of results for different word vector initializations and the use of dropout adds to the robustness of the empirical evaluation.

The analysis provided in Section 3.6, which compares the proposed model to the concurrent bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie, is particularly strong. The explicit comparison of the update equations (Eq. 4 vs. Eq. 5) clarifies the precise architectural differences and provides a basis for understanding the distinct modeling choices. The empirical result showing the GB-RNN outperforms this alternative further validates the design.

A potential area for improvement would be a deeper analysis of the design choice for the backward pass. The intuition that using both the forward and backward parent nodes helps to "disentangle" representations is compelling, but a more formal justification or qualitative analysis (e.g., visualizing how representations change) could further strengthen this claim.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for moving beyond feedforward models is convincingly established in the introduction with a clear example. The model is described with sufficient mathematical detail, and Figure 2 provides a helpful visual schematic of the forward and backward passes.

For improved reproducibility, it would be beneficial to include the final hyperparameter values (e.g., word vector dimensions, regularization parameters, learning rate) that were selected via cross-validation for the best-performing models. While the paper lists the parameters that were tuned, the final choices are not specified. Additionally, the description of the two-step training process in Section 4.4 is an important practical detail; this could be given more prominence, perhaps by framing it more formally as a pre-training strategy.

**Originality**
The work presents a novel extension to recursive neural networks. While the idea of bidirectional processing is well-established for sequential models (e.g., Bi-LSTMs), its application to tree structures was an active area of research at the time. This paper’s formulation of a two-pass, bottom-up then top-down, inference scheme is a distinct and original contribution. The explicit and detailed comparison to the related work of Irsoy and Cardie effectively highlights the novelty of the proposed feedbackward computation. The hybrid word vector representation is also a simple but effective and novel combination of existing ideas.

**Significance**
The results presented are significant. The paper tackles an important and difficult problem in NLP—aspect-specific or context-dependent analysis—which standard compositional models struggle with. By demonstrating a substantial improvement (a 3% F1 gain over the standard RNN) and achieving state-of-the-art results on a competitive shared task, the work clearly advances the field. The core idea of propagating global context down a compositional structure is powerful and generalizable. It is likely that these ideas will be used by other researchers and could be built upon for other context-sensitive tasks, such as word sense disambiguation or relation extraction.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Impact:** The proposed GB-RNN model is a novel and intuitive extension of RNNs that effectively solves a clear limitation of prior models. The idea is likely to have a lasting impact.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a standard, competitive benchmark, validating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Thoroughness:** The work includes careful comparisons to relevant baselines and a detailed analysis of the most closely related concurrent work.
*   **Clarity:** The model and motivation are presented clearly and are easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The work is evaluated on a single task. While the performance is strong, demonstrating the model's utility on an additional context-dependent task would broaden the claims of generality. This is, however, a minor point and more a suggestion for future work than a critical flaw.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a novel contribution, strong results, and likely impact. It clearly meets the standards for publication at NIPS.
