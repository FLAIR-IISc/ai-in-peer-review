This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) to address the limitations of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) in contextual sentiment analysis. The key idea is to incorporate a feedbackward pass during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations, enabling the model to capture context-dependent sentiment. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GB-RNNs on Twitter sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge and showcasing its ability to accurately predict sentiment towards specific entities.

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in NLP, specifically recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2013, 2014) and compositional semantics (Mitchell & Lapata, 2010). It correctly positions itself within the existing literature, acknowledging related approaches like recurrent neural networks (Elman, 1991) and bidirectional models (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014). The authors also address related work on word vector representations (Mikolov et al., 2013; Pennington et al., 2014) and their combination with supervised learning.  The inclusion of a hybrid unsupervised-supervised word vector approach is a sensible extension of existing techniques.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of the feedbackward pass in RNNs is a novel and intuitive extension, addressing a key limitation of standard RNNs in capturing contextual information. This seems genuinely new and well-motivated.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness in a real-world application. The results consistently outperform baselines and the previous competition winner.
*   **Clear Motivation & Explanation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of contextual sentiment analysis and provides a compelling motivation for the GB-RNN approach. The explanations of the model components and training process are generally clear and well-structured.
*   **Thorough Experimentation:**  The authors perform a range of experiments, including comparisons with various baselines, ablation studies, and investigations into the impact of different word vector representations and training techniques (dropout).
*   **Practical Insights:** The findings regarding the benefits of dropout and the hybrid word vector approach offer valuable practical insights for researchers and practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Comparison to Irsoy & Cardie (2014):** While the authors acknowledge Irsoy & Cardie's work, the theoretical comparison (Eqs. 4 & 5) feels somewhat superficial. The claim that their approach "disentangles the children from their backward parent vector" lacks sufficient justification and could be more thoroughly explored. It’s not immediately clear why this disentanglement is beneficial.
*   **Complexity of the Model:** The added complexity of the feedbackward pass and the associated parameters (e.g., the V matrix) could make the model more difficult to train and less interpretable. The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational cost of the GB-RNN compared to standard RNNs.
*   **Parser Dependency:** The reliance on a parser introduces a potential source of error and limits the model's applicability to languages or domains where accurate parsing is challenging.  The impact of parsing errors on performance isn’t explicitly discussed.
*   **Limited Ablation on GB-RNN Components:**  While there are ablations, further analysis on the individual contribution of the feedback pass compared to the hybrid word vectors would be beneficial. For example, what is the performance gain from the feedback pass *without* the hybrid word vectors?
*   **Presentation of Results:** Some tables are a bit dense and hard to quickly grasp. Clearer formatting and concise descriptions could improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed model.
*   The contribution addresses a relevant and challenging problem in NLP.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical comparison to related work (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014) is not fully convincing.
*   The model's complexity and parser dependency could be limiting factors.
*   Some aspects of the presentation could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of sentiment analysis. The proposed GB-RNN model is a novel and effective approach to capturing contextual information, and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, particularly addressing the theoretical comparison with Irsoy & Cardie (2014) and improving the clarity of the presentation. The authors should also briefly discuss the potential impact of parser errors and the computational cost of the model.
