This paper introduces global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs) as an extension of purely feedforward neural networks to include one feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase level predictions and representations to give feedback to words, enabling the model to correctly predict phrase or word labels that are determined by context. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis and show that it outperforms the standard RNN on this task, obtaining state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components. The introduction effectively motivates the need for a model that can capture contextual information, and the related work section provides a comprehensive overview of existing approaches to sentiment analysis and recursive neural networks.

The technical contribution of the paper is significant, as it proposes a novel extension to recursive neural networks that allows for the incorporation of contextual information. The authors provide a detailed description of the model, including the equations for computing the forward and feedbackward node vectors, and the hybrid word vector representations.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the authors provide a comparison of their model to several baselines and model ablations. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the GB-RNN in capturing contextual information and improving performance on the task of contextual sentiment analysis.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to clearly explain complex technical concepts and provide a detailed description of the proposed model. The authors also provide a comprehensive evaluation of their model, including a comparison to several baselines and model ablations.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed model. While the authors mention that the model has more parameters than the bidirectional RNN, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational resources required to train and deploy the model.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components. The technical contribution of the paper is significant, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. The paper is a strong contribution to the field of natural language processing and sentiment analysis.
