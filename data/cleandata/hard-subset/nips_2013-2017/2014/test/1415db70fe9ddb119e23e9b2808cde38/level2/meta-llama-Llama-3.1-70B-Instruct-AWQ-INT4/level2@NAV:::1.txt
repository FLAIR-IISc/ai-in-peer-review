**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel approach to natural language processing (NLP) tasks, specifically contextual sentiment analysis. GB-RNNs extend traditional recursive neural networks (RNNs) by incorporating a feedbackward step during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GB-RNNs on the SemEval 2013 challenge, achieving state-of-the-art performance. They also explore the use of hybrid word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised word vectors, and show that dropout can improve RNN training.

**Evaluation of Claims**

The paper's claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, experiments, and comparisons with other models. The authors provide a clear explanation of the GB-RNN architecture and its advantages over traditional RNNs. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of GB-RNNs on the SemEval 2013 challenge, with a significant improvement in F1 performance over the standard RNN. The authors also provide a thorough comparison with other models, including bidirectional RNNs and logistic regression, showing that GB-RNNs outperform these models.

**Usefulness**

The ideas presented in the paper are practically useful for NLP tasks, particularly contextual sentiment analysis. The GB-RNN architecture can be applied to other NLP tasks, such as word-sense disambiguation, and the hybrid word vector representations can be used in various NLP applications.

**Field Knowledge**

The paper reflects common knowledge in the field of NLP and demonstrates a good understanding of relevant literature. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, including recursive neural networks, bidirectional RNNs, and sentiment analysis.

**Novelty**

The paper presents a significant improvement over existing approaches to NLP tasks, particularly contextual sentiment analysis. The GB-RNN architecture is a novel contribution, and the use of hybrid word vector representations is a new approach to word representation learning.

**Completeness**

The paper provides sufficient details for reproducibility, including a clear explanation of the GB-RNN architecture, experimental setup, and results.

**Limitations**

The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, including the need for additional training data and the potential for overfitting.
