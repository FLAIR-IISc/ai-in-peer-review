Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured compositional model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference, allowing local (word/phrase) predictions to incorporate sentence-level context. The model uses a forward RNN to compute node vectors, then computes top-down vectors from root to leaves via a learned transformation V and decomposition matrix W↓, and classifies each node with a softmax over the concatenation of forward and backward vectors. The authors also introduce hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed, unsupervised vectors with a small supervised, trainable sub-vector, and they apply dropout to node vectors.

The method is positioned relative to RNN/RNTN-style compositional models (Socher et al.), bidirectional recurrent models (Schuster & Paliwal), bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), and unfolding/autoencoder work. The empirical focus is contextual sentiment classification on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter), where entity-/aspect-specific sentiment depends on broader context (e.g., “Android beats iOS”: positive for “Android”). The paper reports improvements over standard RNN baselines, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and competition systems, including an ensemble of GB-RNNs and leveraging external resources (NRC lexicon, distant supervision).

Strengths:
- Clear and practical motivation: a single top-down pass injects sentence-level information into local decisions, addressing a real shortcoming of purely bottom-up compositional models for contextual sentiment.
- Simple, modular extension to standard RNNs: the backward vectors and classifier over concatenated features are easy to implement on top of an existing RNN parser-based model.
- Empirical gains: reported +3 F1 over the standard RNN and state-of-the-art performance on SemEval 2013, with careful ablations indicating benefits from the feedback step, dropout, and hybrid embeddings.
- Sensible use of external data and distant supervision; two-stage training (pretrain forward RNN on context-independent data, then train full GB-RNN on contextual data) is a reasonable strategy.
- Discussion contrasting GB-RNN with bidirectional recursive networks is valuable; the idea of using both forward and backward parent vectors in the top-down recursion is a plausible design choice.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Conceptual/notation inconsistencies: The paper states early that the model adds a backward step “during inference,” yet training uses backprop through the combined architecture; please clarify whether the backward pass is used in training, inference, or both (it appears to be both). More importantly, the text in Section 3.6 claims “our model does not use the forward node vector for computing the backward node vector,” but Eq. (3) explicitly conditions backward children on both forward and backward parent vectors (e.g., f(W↓[p2, p↓2])). This contradiction undermines clarity; reconcile the description with the equations.
- Parameterization and complexity: The stated parameter counts for W↓ differ across the text and include confusing expressions (e.g., “2n2d + 2n·nd”). Please provide a precise shape/parameter count for V and W↓ and a complexity analysis. Also report wall-clock training/inference costs versus baselines.
- Evaluation details are thin in the write-up: The tables are referenced but not shown here; ensure results include the official SemEval metric (avg F1 for pos/neg) on both Twitter and SMS; report mean and variance over multiple runs. The fairness of comparison to competition systems should be discussed more explicitly (semisupervised resources, ensembling). A non-ensemble single-model comparison to the SemEval winner under similar resource constraints would be informative.
- Parser reliance on noisy Twitter text: While the model uses parses, the paper does not analyze robustness to parsing errors or the impact of the constraint to force labeled spans to form constituents. This constraint may bias the tree structures towards the labels and may not generalize outside the evaluation setting.
- Scope of comparisons: The baseline “bidirectional RNN” (sequence) and “bidirectional recursive” (tree) should be carefully matched in capacity (dimensions, parameter counts) and trained with the same hybrid embeddings and dropout to isolate the effect of the GB-RNN’s specific top-down formulation. It is unclear whether the Irsoy & Cardie variant was reimplemented faithfully under identical setups.
- Ablations: Although hybrid embeddings and dropout are ablated, more granular ablations are warranted: (i) removing V (copy-forward root), (ii) using only backward vectors at classification, (iii) training without external lexicon/distant data, (iv) depth-wise analysis showing benefits on long-distance context cases where window/bidirectional recurrent baselines should struggle.
- Terminology: “Global belief” evokes probabilistic message passing/CRFs, but the model performs a single learned top-down transformation, not iterative belief propagation. Consider clarifying the relation to graphical-model “beliefs” to avoid confusion.

Quality:
The core idea is technically sound and easy to verify; the top-down pass is a reasonable architectural bias for contextual disambiguation on trees. However, the inconsistencies around the use of forward vectors in the backward pass and unclear parameter counts reduce technical clarity. The empirical story is promising but needs fuller, controlled comparisons and variance reporting.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally readable but has several typos (“feedbackward”, “feedwordard”, “root note”) and notation slips that should be fixed. The model description would benefit from cleaner equations, consistent dimensions, and an explicit algorithmic description of the forward/backward passes at train/test time.

Originality:
Combining a top-down pass with bottom-up compositional RNNs for token/phrase labeling is a natural but nontrivial extension. Related bidirectional recursive models exist; the paper’s specific choice of conditioning backward children on both forward and backward parent vectors and the hybrid embedding with dropout are incremental but useful.

Significance:
Context-dependent sentiment and aspect-specific labeling are important. If the reported improvements hold under fair, controlled settings, this will be a meaningful advance and likely to be adopted by practitioners working with tree-structured models.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Resolve the contradiction in Section 3.6 and unify the description of how backward vectors are computed; include a clear diagram and pseudo-code.
- Provide precise shapes/parameter counts and complexity; add runtime/memory metrics.
- Expand empirical section: show single-model and ensemble results; report standard deviations; ensure apples-to-apples comparisons to bidirectional baselines with matched parameters.
- Analyze robustness to parsing errors and the effect of the constituent constraint for labeled spans.
- Add targeted evaluations highlighting long-distance contextual effects; include qualitative error analysis.
- Clarify the use of external data and its impact; include a “no external resources” condition.
- Clean up typos and notation throughout.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation of compositional models with a simple, effective architectural addition.
- Demonstrated gains on a competitive benchmark; practical training tricks (dropout, hybrid embeddings) are useful to the community.
- Clear potential for broader applicability beyond sentiment (e.g., WSD, NER with context).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Clarity issues and a key internal inconsistency in the model description.
- Limited, possibly unfair comparisons; reliance on ensembling and external resources for headline results.
- Insufficient analysis of parser robustness and generality beyond SemEval Twitter data.

Overall: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the model and strengthening empirical comparisons.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
