Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive model that augments the standard bottom-up feedforward composition on parse trees with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference/training time. The top-down pass produces feedback vectors for each node using both the forward parent and a backward parent vector, which are then concatenated with forward representations for node-level classification. The model targets contextual (target/phrase-specific) sentiment classification, where a span’s label depends on context far beyond fixed windows. The work compares GB-RNNs to standard RNNs [19, 25, 26], bidirectional recurrent models [21], and bidirectional recursive / inside-outside models developed in parallel [12, 14], arguing differences in the top-down equations (using both forward and backward parents) and flexibility via a separate transformation V and a potentially different backward dimension. The paper also proposes a hybrid word representation (fixed unsupervised embeddings concatenated with small, task-tuned embeddings) and applies dropout to node vectors. Experiments on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter, with additional SMS in the final test) report gains over a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and competition systems, with further improvements from external lexicons and distant supervision.

Strengths:
- Addresses a salient limitation of standard recursive models—lack of context-dependent label updates—using a principled top-down pass that leverages global sentence information.
- Clear, concrete formulation of the backward recursion and its relationship to forward representations, with an explicit comparison to bidirectional recursive alternatives [12, 14].
- Practical contributions that matter in 2014: hybrid word vectors and dropout at node representations, each supported by ablations.
- Demonstrated empirical improvements on a widely used benchmark (SemEval-2013 Task 2). The focus on target-level sentiment is important and underrepresented in tree-structured neural work of that period.
- Uses only a parser and word vectors as core resources; gains are further improved by allowed external resources, with ablation discussion.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Reproducibility and reporting: key quantitative results are described narratively (e.g., “3% F1 over RNN,” “5% better,” “state-of-the-art”) without the corresponding tables/values in the text provided. Precise numbers, variance across runs, and statistical significance are needed.
- Fairness of comparisons: best results use an ensemble of GB-RNNs and additional external data (NRC lexicon, distant supervision, movie reviews). Single-model results without external resources should be clearly separated from ensemble and resource-augmented results when comparing to SemEval systems.
- Scope of baselines: while a bidirectional recursive baseline is included, a stronger set of non-tree neural baselines would help, e.g., window-based neural models [5], and bidirectional recurrent chains (BiRNN) that can capture long-range context without parse trees. The claim that window/recurrent methods cannot incorporate long-distance context is too strong; in practice, gated RNNs (though not cited here) and even non-gated BiRNNs can carry information over long spans, albeit imperfectly.
- Dependence on parsing for noisy tweets: the model’s reliance on binarized constituency parses and the constraint that each labeled phrase is a subtree could introduce artifacts or bias. Robustness to parse errors and the impact of the subtree constraint should be quantified.
- Clarity/typos: several minor issues (e.g., “feedbackward,” “shold,” “root note”) and what appear to be stray lines in Section 3.6 detract from presentation. The description of Eq. (3) would benefit from clearer shapes and intuition.
- The claim that the backward pass is “during inference” but training uses backprop through structure with the backward parameters may confuse readers; the training/inference distinction should be clarified.

Quality:
Conceptually sound and well-motivated. The architectural difference relative to [12, 14] is clearly articulated, and the hybrid embeddings/dropout story is plausible. The empirical section, as summarized, suggests meaningful gains, but the absence of exact results and significance testing in the provided text makes it hard to fully assess. Additional diagnostic analyses (distance to target, tree distance, error categories) would strengthen the case that the top-down pass is the causal factor.

Clarity:
Overall well-organized, with intuitive motivation and equations. Needs careful proofreading and a clearer walk-through of Eq. (3), tensor shapes, and the computational graph. Training details are reasonably described (AdaGrad, dropout, hyperparameters), but a succinct algorithmic summary of the forward/backward passes would help.

Originality:
The idea of top-down recursion in tree networks is concurrent with bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models [12, 14]. The particular backward formulation that conditions on both forward and backward parents, the separate V transform, and the hybrid embedding/dropout combination are incremental but nontrivial. The application to target-level sentiment within a recursive architecture is timely.

Significance:
Target/context-specific sentiment is important, and the proposed approach appears to advance the state of the art at the time. The idea of a single top-down pass to incorporate global belief is broadly applicable to other token/phrase labeling tasks (e.g., coreference spans, WSD, structured sentiment), giving the work potential impact beyond sentiment.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Provide exact test-set scores (macro F1 and per-class F1), with confidence intervals, for: single RNN, single Bi-RecNN baseline [12], single GB-RNN, and ensemble GB-RNN. Indicate which use external data.
- Report results without external resources and without ensembling when comparing to SemEval systems; then add resources stepwise to isolate contributions.
- Clarify whether the backward pass is used during both training and inference; if only at inference initially, describe how W↓ and V are trained.
- Detail how the subtree constraint is enforced and whether it uses label information; analyze robustness to parse errors.
- Provide runtime and parameter counts, and the overhead of the top-down pass relative to a standard RNN.

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized):
Essential:
- Include complete quantitative tables with standard deviations and significance testing; separate single vs ensemble and with/without external data.
- Add a non-tree BiRNN baseline and a window-based neural baseline with comparable capacity.
- Clarify Eq. (3), dimensions, and provide a concise algorithm for the forward/backward computations.

Nice-to-have:
- Error analysis by (a) distance between target and sentiment trigger (token and tree distance), (b) dependency types, and (c) parse quality.
- Robustness study to noisy parses (e.g., using a simpler chunker) and ablation on the subtree constraint.
- Analysis of the hybrid embeddings: how much gradient flows into the supervised part; impact of freezing vs fine-tuning the unsupervised part.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap in recursive models with a simple, general mechanism for global context.
- Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark, with ablations indicating where gains come from.
- Method is likely to be useful beyond sentiment.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental reporting, as presented, lacks sufficient detail for full verification; fairness of comparisons (ensemble/resources) needs clearer separation.
- Incremental originality relative to concurrent bidirectional recursive work; stronger baseline coverage needed.
- Heavy reliance on parsing in a noisy domain without a thorough robustness assessment.

Overall, this is a promising and practically useful contribution; with strengthened experimental reporting and baseline coverage, it would make a solid addition to the program.
