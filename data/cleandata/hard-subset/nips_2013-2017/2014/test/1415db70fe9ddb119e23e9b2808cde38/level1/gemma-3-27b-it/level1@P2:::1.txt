The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) as an extension to standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) to address the problem of contextual sentiment analysis, where the sentiment of a phrase depends on the surrounding text. The core idea is to add a feedbackward pass during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations. The authors demonstrate improvements on the SemEval 2013 task and claim state-of-the-art performance, alongside showing the benefits of dropout and a hybrid word vector approach.

This work builds on a substantial body of research in compositional semantics and deep learning for NLP. The paper correctly situates itself within the context of RNNs [26, 19, 25], bidirectional RNNs [21], and related approaches like window-based methods [5]. The motivation for addressing context-dependence is well articulated, highlighting the limitations of purely feedforward architectures for tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The related work section covers relevant contributions, including those on word vectors [27, 11, 15] and bidirectional recursive models [12, 14].

**Quality:** The technical approach appears sound, with a clear explanation of the GB-RNN architecture and the forward/backward computation steps. The use of backpropagation through structure [9] is a standard technique. The experimental setup, including the use of a development set for hyperparameter tuning and baselines, is appropriate.  However, the ablation studies, while present, could be more comprehensive, perhaps examining the impact of the feedbackward dimension (nd) more thoroughly. The claim of state-of-the-art performance is supported by the reported results, but a more detailed comparison to other recently published methods (beyond just SemEval 2013 winners) would strengthen this claim. The authors are reasonably careful in evaluating the strengths and weaknesses, acknowledging the increased parameter count of the GB-RNN. The hybrid word vector approach and dropout application seem well-justified.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures (especially Fig. 2) are helpful in illustrating the GB-RNN architecture. Equations are clearly presented. However, the explanation of the comparison to Irsoy & Cardie [12] could be more concise and less mathematically dense. Some of the notation (e.g., W ↓lf, W ↓lb) is a bit cumbersome and could potentially be simplified. Minor grammatical errors are present, but do not significantly hinder understanding. The paper provides sufficient information to understand the approach but might fall short of enabling a complete reproduction of the results without further clarification on the parsing specifics and hyperparameter search.

**Originality:** The idea of adding a feedbackward pass to RNNs is novel, representing a departure from purely feedforward approaches. While bidirectional RNNs [12, 14] also introduce backward propagation, the authors convincingly argue that their method differs in how it integrates information, avoiding the limitations of fixed-window approaches and enabling long-range contextual influence. The hybrid word vector approach, while not entirely new, is a sensible combination of unsupervised and supervised learning. The paper presents a meaningful combination of existing techniques with a novel extension.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the GB-RNN for contextual sentiment analysis. The improvement over standard RNNs and other baselines is substantial. The model’s ability to accurately predict the sentiment of specific entities is a valuable contribution. The concept of propagating beliefs through a recursive network has the potential to be applied to other NLP tasks beyond sentiment analysis, such as word sense disambiguation and relation extraction.  The work addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and provides a unique approach to incorporating global context into compositional models.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over strong baselines, including the SemEval 2013 winner.
*   Clear motivation and explanation of the method.
*   Addresses a challenging problem with practical relevance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Ablation studies could be more comprehensive.
*   Comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods is lacking.
*   Some sections (e.g., comparison to Irsoy & Cardie) are overly detailed and could be streamlined.
*   Reproducibility might be hindered by insufficient details on some implementation aspects.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of NLP. The proposed GB-RNN model is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements in contextual sentiment analysis. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised in the review. Specifically, I would suggest adding a brief discussion of recent related work and expanding the ablation studies.
