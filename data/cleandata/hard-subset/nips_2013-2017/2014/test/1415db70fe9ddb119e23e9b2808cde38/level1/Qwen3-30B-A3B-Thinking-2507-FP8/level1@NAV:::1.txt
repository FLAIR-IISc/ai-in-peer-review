

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), addressing a critical limitation in standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) for contextual sentiment analysis: their inability to incorporate global sentence context when predicting labels for phrases or words. The authors propose a novel feedbackward step during inference, allowing phrase-level representations to update based on the entire sentence structure. This extends prior work on compositional models (e.g., Socher et al., EMNLP 2013; Socher et al., NIPS 2011) and bidirectional recursive networks (Irsoy & Cardie, NIPS 2014), while avoiding the fixed-context constraints of window-based methods. The approach combines hybrid unsupervised-supervised word vectors and dropout, demonstrating a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs on SemEval 2013, outperforming competition winners and baselines.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The methodology is technically sound, with thorough ablation studies (comparing to bidirectional RNNs, baselines, and variants) and rigorous experimental validation on a standard benchmark. The hybrid word vector approach and dropout application are well-justified.  
*Originality:* The feedbackward mechanism is a clear, novel contribution to context-aware compositional modeling, distinct from prior bidirectional approaches (Section 3.6). The problem of context-dependent sentiment disambiguation is well-motivated and significant.  
*Significance:* The model achieves state-of-the-art results on a relevant NLP task, with clear implications for social media analysis. The architecture’s potential for broader NLP applications (e.g., word-sense disambiguation) enhances its impact.  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity:* The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie (2014) in Section 3.6 is overly technical; a concise summary of key differences would improve accessibility.  
*Quality:* While computational cost is not explicitly analyzed, the added parameters (n·nd vs. bidirectional RNN) may be non-trivial for large-scale deployment. The paper lacks error analysis (e.g., cases where the model fails), which would strengthen the evaluation.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel architecture solving a well-defined problem with strong empirical results; significant advancement over prior contextual sentiment models.  
*Con:* Limited discussion of computational trade-offs and failure modes.  

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution with clear significance for NLP. The minor clarity and evaluation gaps do not undermine the core scientific merit. **Recommendation: Accept.**
