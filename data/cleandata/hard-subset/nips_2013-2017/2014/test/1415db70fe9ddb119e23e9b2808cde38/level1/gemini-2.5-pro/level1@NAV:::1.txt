This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to address the limitations of standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) in capturing context-dependent meaning. The central idea is to augment the standard bottom-up, feedforward pass of an RNN with a top-down, "feedbackward" pass. This allows information from the entire sentence (the "global belief") to propagate back down the parse tree, refining the representations and predictions of individual words and phrases. This approach is contrasted with previous work on standard RNNs (e.g., Socher et al.) and concurrent bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, 2013). The authors also demonstrate the utility of dropout for RNNs and a hybrid word vector representation combining unsupervised and supervised features.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance & Quality:** The paper tackles a well-known and important problem in compositional semantics: the inability of purely feedforward models to handle contextual phenomena like aspect-specific sentiment. The proposed solution is elegant and intuitive. The claims are strongly supported by experimental results, demonstrating a significant 3% F1 improvement over a standard RNN and achieving state-of-the-art performance on the competitive SemEval 2013 shared task. The ablation studies on word vectors and comparisons to multiple baselines show a thorough and honest evaluation.
*   **Originality:** While building on the established RNN framework, the specific formulation of the feedbackward pass is novel. The authors do an excellent job of differentiating their model from the concurrent work of Irsoy & Cardie, both theoretically (by comparing the update equations) and empirically, which strengthens the paper’s contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is established with a clear, compelling example, and the model is introduced in a logical, step-by-step manner. The provided equations and diagrams make the architecture easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The intuition for why the proposed feedbackward formulation (Eq. 5) is superior to that of Irsoy & Cardie (Eq. 4) could be explored more deeply. While the paper notes the empirical benefit, a more qualitative analysis or error analysis illustrating the advantages of using both the forward and backward parent nodes would provide greater insight.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and effective architecture for a significant NLP problem.
2.  Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging public benchmark.
3.  The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and thoroughly evaluated.
4.  The core idea of a feedbackward pass is generalizable and could inspire follow-up work in other NLP tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The novelty is arguably an incremental, though very effective, extension of existing RNN models.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution, impressive results, and high potential impact. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
