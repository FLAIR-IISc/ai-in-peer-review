- Introduces Global Belief RNNs (GB-RNNs) that add a top-down "feedbackward" pass to standard recursive NNs to incorporate global sentence context into word/phrase sentiment predictions.
- Claims GB-RNNs differ theoretically from bidirectional RNNs by how backward information is propagated and disentangled.
- Proposes a hybrid word vector combining unsupervised pre-trained and supervised task-specific vectors; finds dropout improves training.
- Well-motivated problem: context-dependent sentiment (e.g., "Android beats iOS") clearly demonstrated and justified.
- Architecture and math are clearly presented, with an insightful theoretical comparison to bidirectional models.
- Empirical results show a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs on SemEval 2013, outperforming the competition winner and baselines.
- Hybrid word vectors outperform pure unsupervised and supervised vectors in experiments.
- Authors discuss potential limitations and impact of dropout and extra training data.
- Insufficient empirical comparison to bidirectional RNNs despite claiming the feedbackward step is a key innovation.
- The 3% F1 gain, while statistically significant, is modest and lacks analysis of when/context where it matters most.
- Computational complexity is not adequately addressed; feedbackward increases parameters but training/inference costs are not discussed.
- Claims about accurately predicting entity-level sentiment are not convincingly demonstrated beyond the SemEval dataset examples.
- Comparison to related work could be stronger by including more recent context-aware models (e.g., dynamic pooling approaches).
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and mathematically precise.
- The global belief/feedbackward idea is novel for RNNs but bears conceptual similarity to bidirectional models.
- Significance is moderate: addresses an important limitation and advances state-of-the-art on a benchmark, but practical impact is unclear.
- Pro-accept: novel architecture, solid empirical gains, and useful hybrid vectors; theoretically justified.
- Con-accept: modest improvement, insufficient empirical bidirectional comparison, and missing computational trade-off discussion.
- Overall: solid contribution recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen empirical comparisons and practical implications.
