This paper introduces a novel model called Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) that extends the traditional Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) by incorporating a feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words, enabling the model to capture contextual information that is essential for tasks like aspect-specific sentiment classification.

The authors provide a clear and concise background on the limitations of traditional RNNs and the need for models that can capture contextual information. They also provide a thorough review of related work, including neural word vectors, recursive neural networks, and bidirectional-recurrent and bidirectional-recursive neural networks.

The GB-RNN model is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on its ability to capture contextual information through the feedbackward step. The authors also introduce a hybrid approach to word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised word vectors, which is shown to perform better than either approach alone.

The experimental results are impressive, with the GB-RNN model outperforming several baselines and model ablations on the SemEval 2013 task 2 competition. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance, including the impact of additional training data and the effectiveness of the hybrid word vector approach.

Strengths:

* The GB-RNN model is a novel and well-motivated extension of traditional RNNs that addresses the limitations of these models in capturing contextual information.
* The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain the GB-RNN model and its components.
* The experimental results are impressive, with the GB-RNN model outperforming several baselines and model ablations.
* The authors provide a detailed analysis of the model's performance, including the impact of additional training data and the effectiveness of the hybrid word vector approach.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other bidirectional-recurrent and bidirectional-recursive neural networks, such as the model proposed by Irsoy and Cardie.
* The authors could provide more insight into the interpretability of the GB-RNN model, including the role of the feedbackward step in capturing contextual information.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the GB-RNN model, including its potential limitations in capturing long-range dependencies and its sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning.
