- Introduces GB-RNN, which augments feedforward recursive neural nets with a top-down "feedbackward" pass to let global sentence context influence node predictions.  
- Motivated by aspect-specific sentiment analysis where word/phrase meaning depends on broader sentence context (e.g., "Android" in "Android beats iOS").  
- Bottom-up pass computes node representations; top-down pass propagates a global belief from the root back to leaves; final node prediction concatenates feedforward and feedbackward vectors.  
- Extends prior recursive models (Socher et al.) and adapts bidirectional processing ideas to tree structures.  
- Provides a clear architectural distinction from the concurrent Bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie.  
- Introduces two smaller contributions: hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + task-specific trainable) and applying dropout to RNNs.  
- Strength: significant and novel solution to a fundamental weakness of feedforward compositional models.  
- Strength: strong empirical results — state-of-the-art on SemEval 2013 Task 2 and a ~3% F1 gain over standard RNN in ablation tests.  
- Strength: clear, well-written paper with precise equations, helpful diagrams, and useful comparison to related work.  
- Weakness: computational cost — the extra top-down pass roughly doubles inference time and adds parameters (V, W↓), with no cost analysis.  
- Weakness: depends on syntactic parser quality; sensitivity to noisy parses (e.g., tweets) is not discussed.  
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope — experiments only on one task (Twitter sentiment), no demonstrations of generality to other NLP tasks.  
- Pro-accept: addresses core limitation, presents an intuitive and technically sound architecture, with strong empirical validation and clarity.  
- Con-accept: lacks discussion of efficiency and parser-dependence and is evaluated on a single task only.  
- Overall: strengths outweigh weaknesses; the paper makes a significant, well-supported contribution to tree-structured compositional models.
