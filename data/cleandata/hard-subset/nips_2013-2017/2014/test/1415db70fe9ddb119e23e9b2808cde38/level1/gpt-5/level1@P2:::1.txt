Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a two-pass tree-structured neural architecture for contextual (aspect-specific) sentiment. A standard bottom-up recursive neural network (RNN) computes phrase representations; then a top-down “feedbackward” pass (with distinct parameters) propagates information from the root to leaves, and the model classifies each node using the concatenation of bottom-up and top-down vectors. The motivation is that purely feedforward recursive models cannot disambiguate sentiments of words/entities that depend on sentence-level context (e.g., “Android” in “Android beats iOS”). The paper also proposes hybrid word embeddings (concatenating fixed, unsupervised vectors with a smaller trainable supervised component) and uses dropout to improve generalization.

The approach is compared conceptually and empirically to bidirectional recursive networks (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie), arguing GB-RNN’s top-down step uses both the forward and backward parent vectors when computing child messages. Experiments are on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), with supplementary external resources (NRC lexicon unigrams/bigrams, weakly labeled tweets, movie reviews). The model reportedly improves F1 by ~3–5% over a standard RNN and outperforms competition systems; an ensemble of the top 5 GB-RNNs yields the best leaderboard result.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for contextual disambiguation of sentiment beyond feedforward composition. The top-down pass is a simple, general mechanism to inject global sentence information into local decisions.
- Solid empirical gains on a widely used benchmark (SemEval 2013) with ablations: forward-only RNN, bidirectional recursive baseline, hybrid vs. purely supervised/unsupervised embeddings, and effect of dropout.
- The hybrid embedding idea is practical and yields consistent improvements; the argument for combining fixed unsupervised semantics with a small supervised head is compelling.
- Training details (AdaGrad, minibatching, dropout) and hyperparameter selection are described, and the two-stage training (pretrain forward RNN on large context-independent data, then fine-tune GB-RNN on contextual sentiment) is reasonable.
- The model is easy to extend to other token/phrase-level contextual tasks on trees.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty relative to contemporaneous bidirectional recursive models is incremental. The main difference is the specific parameterization of the top-down message (using both forward and backward parent) and allowing a different dimensionality; this may not warrant a new paradigm name (“global belief”) and risks overstating conceptual novelty.
- Comparisons are potentially confounded by capacity and resources:
  - GB-RNN has more parameters than the bidirectional baseline; no effort is made to equalize model capacity or report parameter counts/regularization sensitivity.
  - The strongest results rely on ensembling and substantial external data; while allowed by SemEval, this diminishes the clarity of the architectural contribution’s effect relative to data/ensembling.
- Methodological concerns about parsing and span handling: tweets are noisy, yet the approach depends on constituency parses. Constraining the parser so each labeled phrase aligns to a single subtree uses annotation to guide structure; it is unclear how this would work at test time for arbitrary spans or in deployment when spans/aspects are not syntactic constituents.
- The “global belief” framing suggests an inference procedure akin to belief propagation or structured prediction with explicit global consistency; in practice it is a deterministic top-down neural pass. There is no global loss tying node predictions together beyond shared representations; this should be clarified.
- Limited breadth of evaluation: beyond SemEval Twitter sentiment, no other contextual labeling tasks (e.g., aspect-based datasets in reviews) are shown. Claims of broader utility (e.g., WSD) are not empirically supported.
- Statistical significance testing is not reported; error analyses are only briefly sketched.
- Clarity issues and typos: “feedbackward” spelling; shape notations (e.g., V ∈ Vnd×n), inconsistent references (“Figure 3.4”), and some notational overload can impede reproducibility.

Quality:
Technically sound overall: the architecture is well-defined, training is standard (backprop through structure with a top-down component), and ablations support the main claim that the top-down pass helps contextual sentiment. However, fairness of comparisons (capacity control; use of external resources; ensembling) and the parsing/span constraint raise questions about how much of the gain comes from the core idea versus auxiliary choices. More rigorous controls (parameter-matched baselines, significance testing) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The high-level presentation and motivation are clear, with helpful figures. Yet several notation/typo issues and incomplete details (exact subtree constraint procedure; how root predictions influence top-down messages; computational cost) should be addressed to aid reproducibility.

Originality:
Moderate. Adding a top-down pass on trees is close to prior bidirectional recursive models; the specific formulation and the hybrid embedding trick are incremental but useful. The application to contextual sentiment on Twitter with a tree-based method is timely for the 2014 context.

Significance:
Reasonable. The reported SOTA on SemEval and consistent ablations suggest the method is practically beneficial. If the approach generalizes, it could influence contextual labeling on trees. The reliance on external data/ensembles tempers the perceived contribution of the core architectural change.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation of recursive nets with a simple, effective mechanism.
- Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark, with informative ablations.
- Practical contributions (hybrid embeddings, dropout usage) likely to be adopted.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental novelty vs. existing bidirectional recursive models; “global belief” framing may be overstated.
- Fairness concerns: higher capacity, ensembling, and external data blur attribution of gains.
- Dependence on parsing and subtree constraints; unclear generality beyond this setup.
- Clarity issues and lack of statistical significance tests.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper tackles a meaningful problem and presents a pragmatic, empirically effective extension to recursive models. With improved clarity, tighter comparisons, and broader evaluation, it would be a solid contribution.
