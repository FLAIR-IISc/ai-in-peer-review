## Review of “Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks”

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) to address the limitations of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) in capturing context-dependent sentiment, particularly in tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The core idea is to augment the feedforward RNN architecture with a single feedbackward pass during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GB-RNNs on Twitter sentiment data, achieving state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge and showing improved performance over standard RNNs and other baselines. They also explore the benefits of dropout and a hybrid unsupervised/supervised word vector approach.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in compositional semantics and recursive neural networks (e.g., Socher et al., 2013, 2010; Mitchell & Lapata, 2010). It acknowledges related work in bidirectional RNNs (Irsoy & Cardie, 2012) and highlights the differences in the backward propagation mechanism. The motivation for addressing context-dependence is clearly articulated, and the example provided (Fig. 1) effectively illustrates the problem. 

**Strengths:** The paper presents a novel and intuitively appealing approach to incorporating global context into recursive neural networks. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating a consistent improvement over baselines and achieving state-of-the-art performance. The ablation studies (dropout, hybrid word vectors) provide valuable insights into the model's components. The writing is generally clear and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:** While the performance gains are significant, the improvement of 3% over the standard RNN feels somewhat modest given the added complexity of the feedbackward pass. The explanation of the theoretical differences between the proposed method and Irsoy & Cardie (2012) could be more detailed and easier to follow. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational cost associated with the feedbackward pass. While the authors mention the increased number of parameters, a more concrete analysis of the runtime implications would be beneficial. The reliance on the Stanford Parser introduces a potential point of failure and could limit the generalizability of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel approach to incorporating global context into RNNs.
* Strong experimental results, including state-of-the-art performance.
* Clear motivation and well-defined problem.
* Thorough ablation studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Modest performance improvement relative to added complexity.
* Limited discussion of computational cost.
* Dependence on external parser.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed GB-RNNs offer a promising approach to contextual sentiment analysis, and the experimental results are compelling. Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh its limitations. I recommend acceptance.
