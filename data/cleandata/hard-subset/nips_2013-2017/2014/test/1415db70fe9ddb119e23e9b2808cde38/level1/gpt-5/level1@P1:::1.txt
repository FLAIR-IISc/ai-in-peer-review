Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured neural architecture that augments standard bottom-up recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. Each phrase/word node obtains two representations: a forward (bottom-up) vector and a backward (top-down) vector computed from the root via a new decomposition matrix that conditions on both the forward parent and the backward parent. Final node-level predictions are made by concatenating forward and backward vectors and applying a softmax. The motivation is contextual disambiguation, exemplified by aspect-targeted sentiment (SemEval-2013 Task 2 Subtask B), where the sentiment polarity of a term depends on sentence-level context. Additional contributions include: (i) hybrid word embeddings that concatenate fixed unsupervised vectors with smaller supervised, trainable dimensions; (ii) use of dropout in RNN training; and (iii) empirical comparisons to baselines and bidirectional recursive models.

This work is positioned among compositional models in NLP (e.g., Socher et al. 2011–2013), bidirectional recurrent nets (Schuster & Paliwal, 1997), and bidirectional/bottom-up–top-down recursive nets developed in parallel (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014; Le & Zuidema’s Inside-Outside RNN, 2014). The “global belief” idea is essentially a single top-down pass on trees, akin to inside-outside style context propagation, tailored for node-level classification.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The high-level idea is sensible and well-motivated for context-dependent labeling. The architecture is clearly described conceptually, and the empirical results indicate consistent gains over a feedforward RNN and competitive SemEval systems. The hybrid embeddings and dropout are plausible enhancements and are tested with ablations.
- However, there are several technical inconsistencies that need correction:
  - Dimensions in Section 3.3: W↓ is stated as R(n+nd)×(n+nd), but the mapping in Eq. 3 must output two nd-dimensional child vectors from an (n+nd)-dim input; thus W↓ should be 2nd×(n+nd), or equivalently block matrices with consistent shapes (as later exposition implies). Please fix all related shapes.
  - The classifier weight Wc is given as RC×(n+1), but inputs are [x; x↓] with dimension n+nd (+bias). This appears to be a typo; clarify the exact dimensions used in experiments.
  - There is a small typo in V ∈ Vnd×n and several minor textual errors (“feedbackward”, “root note”, “shold”).
- Empirical evaluation: The paper compares single-model GB-RNNs and an ensemble to strong SemEval-2013 Task 2 systems, and to standard RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline. The authors also analyze the effects of extra supervised/weakly supervised data and lexicon-derived training instances. This is thorough and valuable; however:
  - Numbers are occasionally inconsistent in the text (3% F1 vs >5% improvement over RNN). Please report consistent metrics (macro-F1 for pos/neg as per SemEval) and present both dev and test results for all models, single vs ensemble.
  - Fairness and resource usage: Because the top-performing SemEval systems rely on extensive features, it is welcome that GB-RNN uses limited hand-crafting. Still, GB-RNN uses additional resources (NRC lexicon entries as labeled instances, large noisy Twitter data, movie reviews) and ensembling. Please isolate the contribution of the top-down pass by reporting: (a) single-model RNN vs GB-RNN without extra data, (b) with extra data, and (c) with ensembling. Some of this is present but could be made crisper.
  - Parsing on Twitter is noisy. Forcing labeled terms to be subtrees is a practical hack; discuss how often this modifies constituency and whether this affects generalization. Consider comparing with a tweet-optimized tokenizer/parser.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear motivation and figures. Fixing the dimensionality errors and typos would substantially improve clarity. More details on training hyperparameters and computational cost (time per epoch, parameters, inference overhead vs RNN) would help reproducibility. A small error analysis focusing on context distances and common failure cases would strengthen the case for the top-down pass.

Originality:
The idea of a top-down pass on trees to provide contextualized node representations overlaps with bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models developed concurrently (Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema). The novelty here is the specific formulation that conditions child backward vectors on both the forward and backward parent and uses concatenation of forward/backward for classification, plus the hybrid embedding and dropout recipe. The contribution is incremental but nontrivial, and it is validated empirically.

Significance:
Context-dependent sentiment at the term level is important and underexplored relative to message-level sentiment. Showing consistent gains over a strong RNN baseline and competitive SemEval systems, with relatively modest feature engineering, is valuable. Broader impact beyond sentiment (e.g., WSD, entity-level stance) is plausible but not demonstrated; a second dataset or task would strengthen significance claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear motivation for context-aware node labeling on trees; simple, general mechanism.
- Empirical improvements over RNNs and bidirectional recursive baseline on SemEval-2013 Task 2.
- Useful practical insights: hybrid embeddings + dropout yield gains.
- Detailed ablations on additional training data and training protocol.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overlap with concurrent bidirectional/inside-outside recursive models; limited novelty.
- Technical inconsistencies in equations/dimensions; needs fixes for reproducibility.
- Heavy reliance on ensembling and additional data; core gain attributable solely to the top-down pass could be made more explicit.
- Evaluation limited to one benchmark/task; parsing issues on Twitter not deeply analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct all dimensionalities and clarify the exact parameterization (shapes of V, W↓ blocks, Wc).
- Report full single-model results, with and without extra data, and without ensembling; quantify the isolated effect of the top-down pass.
- Add runtime/parameter count comparisons and discuss the computational overhead.
- Include an error analysis contrasting cases where context is far vs near, and ablate the V transform (copy-vs-transform at root).
- Consider evaluating on an additional context-dependent labeling task or a non-Twitter dataset to demonstrate generality.
- Discuss the impact of forced subtree constraints and tweet parsing quality.

Overall assessment:
A solid, well-motivated incremental contribution that demonstrates that a simple top-down pass in tree-structured nets helps contextual sentiment classification. With corrections to technical details and clearer isolation of contributions, this would make a good NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance if the authors address the noted issues.
