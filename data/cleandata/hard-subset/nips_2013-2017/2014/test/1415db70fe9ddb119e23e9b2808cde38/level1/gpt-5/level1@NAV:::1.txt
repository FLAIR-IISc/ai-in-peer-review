This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured model that augments standard recursive neural networks with a single top-down “feedbackward” inference step to inject sentence-level context into word/phrase predictions. The model computes bottom-up composition with a standard RNN, then propagates a top-down vector via a distinct transformation (V, W↓) and classifies nodes using the concatenation of forward and backward representations. The motivation is contextual/targeted sentiment (e.g., sentiment toward “Android” vs. “iOS”), for which purely feedforward compositional models [Socher et al., EMNLP’13] underperform. The paper situates GB-RNN relative to bidirectional recurrent networks [Schuster & Paliwal, IEEE TSP] and bidirectional recursive models [Irsoy & Cardie, NIPS DL Workshop], arguing a different backward computation that conditions on both forward and backward parent representations. Additional contributions include hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings + small supervised trainable tail) and dropout on node vectors. On SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter), GB-RNN improves F1 by ~3–5% over a standard RNN, outperforms bi-recursive baselines, and achieves state-of-the-art results with an ensemble, aided by external lexicons and weakly labeled data. This line builds on prior NIPS work on recursive autoencoders and compositional semantics.

Quality: The model is technically sound, clearly specified, and trained with BPTS + AdaGrad. Ablations (hybrid embeddings, dropout) are informative; comparisons include RNN, bidirectional recursive, and strong non-neural baselines. However, significance tests are missing; the impact of external resources vs. model architecture should be disentangled more rigorously (report single-model, no-external-data results side-by-side). Computational cost and inference latency are not analyzed.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized; equations define n vs. nd, though notation could be streamlined. A few typos (“feedbackward,” “shold”). More detail on tree constraints for noisy Twitter parses would help reproducibility.

Originality: The top-down belief pass over trees is a modest but meaningful extension over contemporaneous bidirectional recursive models; the hybrid embeddings are pragmatic rather than novel.

Significance: Results are strong on a widely used benchmark; the idea is broadly applicable to contextual labeling beyond sentiment. Ensemble reliance and external resources reduce purity of the architectural contribution but not its practical value.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, simple architectural idea with consistent gains over strong baselines
- Solid ablations (dropout, hybrid vectors); SOTA on SemEval’13
- Relevant to NIPS topics in structured deep learning and NLP

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to bi-recursive work; limited theoretical analysis
- No significance testing; unclear compute/latency overhead
- Heavy use of external resources; need clearer isolation of architecture-only gains
