Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a tree-structured neural model that augments a standard bottom-up recursive neural network with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference and training time. The forward pass computes inside representations for each constituent; the backward pass conditions each node on an outside context vector propagated from the root via its ancestors. The final node representation is the concatenation of forward and backward vectors, used for node-level classification (e.g., phrase sentiment). The model targets contextual (target-dependent) sentiment classification on SemEval 2013 Twitter data, showing improvements over a standard RNN and competitive systems. Additional contributions include hybrid word vectors (concatenating fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a smaller supervised, task-tunable segment) and the use of dropout in RNN training.

The approach is conceptually close to inside-outside style neural models in parsing, as in inside-outside RNNs and compositional vector grammars, and to bidirectional recursive networks that propagate information down the tree. Compared to Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive models, the proposed backward computation uses both the forward and backward parent representations to compute child outside vectors and allows a dimension mismatch between forward and backward channels via a trainable V transform. Compared to window-based or recurrent sequence models (e.g., Collobert et al.), GB-RNNs condition on unbounded context through the parse structure rather than a fixed window.

Quality
- Methodology: The forward-backward architecture is well motivated for contextual labeling: each node’s prediction depends on the entire sentence via the outside signal. The design is a straightforward, differentiable extension of an RNN over trees with backpropagation through structure. The hybrid embeddings and dropout are sensible regularization choices.
- Theory: The paper remains largely empirical. While the authors give a clear algebraic definition of the backward recursion, there is no formal analysis of expressivity, convergence, or when a single backward sweep suffices relative to iterative message passing. The “belief” terminology suggests probabilistic semantics, but the model is not probabilistic nor calibrated.
- Experiments: On SemEval 2013, the GB-RNN outperforms a feedforward RNN by roughly 3–5 F1 points and beats competition systems, with ablations attributing gains to the backward pass, hybrid embeddings, dropout, and external data. The inclusion of both single-model and ensemble results is appreciated, as is the breakdown of gains from NRC lexicon and weakly labeled tweets. However:
  - The dataset description conflates tasks; SemEval 2013 includes message-level and term-level sentiment. Please clarify which subtask(s) are evaluated and report the official metrics per subtask.
  - The parser was constrained so each labeled phrase forms a subtree. This may bias structure, leak span information into the composition, and is unrealistic for noisy tweets where many targets are not constituents. An analysis of how often spans are non-constituents and a baseline without this constraint would strengthen claims.
  - Parsing quality on Twitter is a major confound; results should include robustness to parser errors or a comparison to sequence models (biLSTM) that do not require trees.
  - The comparison to bidirectional recursive models is welcome, but results should include that baseline under identical settings; the text claims superiority but quantitative tables are not shown here.
  - External resources: The system leverages NRC lexicon and large weakly labeled data; while allowed by SemEval, it blurs how much of the gain stems from architecture vs. data. The ablation (+2% from lexicon, +1% from additional data) is useful; please also report GB-RNN vs. RNN without any external data.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well structured. The architecture is explained with equations and an illustrative example. Some notational issues remain: dimensions are occasionally ambiguous (e.g., V ∈ R^{nd×n}, W↓ block structure) and typos occur. The training section is concise but could better specify which nodes are supervised (only labeled spans) and how missing labels are handled (ignored vs. semi-supervised losses). Reporting full hyperparameters and runtime/complexity would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The main idea—combining inside and outside passes on a tree—is not entirely new; inside-outside neural models and bidirectional recursive networks emerged contemporaneously. The specific backward parameterization that conditions on both forward and backward parent vectors, the dimensional decoupling via V, and the empirical focus on contextual sentiment on noisy social text are useful incremental contributions. The hybrid embeddings idea mirrors multi-channel embeddings seen in other 2014 work but is appropriately adapted here.

Significance
Target-dependent sentiment remains important, and enabling contextual disambiguation in a tree framework is valuable. Demonstrating competitive performance on SemEval with relatively modest feature engineering is a plus, and the approach could apply to other contextual labeling tasks (word sense disambiguation, coreference-related sentiment, semantic role labeling). That said, reliance on parses for tweets and sensitivity to span-as-constituent assumptions may limit practical adoption; comparisons to strong sequence baselines would clarify impact.

Pros
- Clear and well-motivated architecture that injects global context into local predictions via a top-down pass.
- Strong empirical gains over a standard RNN; competitive with SemEval systems.
- Sensible and effective hybrid embeddings and dropout strategy with ablation evidence.
- Thorough comparison to related bidirectional-recursive formulations at the model level.

Cons
- Novelty is incremental relative to contemporaneous inside-outside/bidirectional recursive models; limited theoretical insight.
- Parsing constraints and domain mismatch may inflate results; limited analysis of non-constituent targets and parser errors.
- Dependence on external lexicons and weakly labeled data complicates attribution of gains.
- Missing or unclear baselines: strong sequence models without parses, CRF-style structured baselines, and full quantitative comparison to bidirectional recursive RNNs under identical settings.
- Limited discussion of complexity, inference cost, and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Suggestions and questions
- Clarify which SemEval subtask(s) were evaluated; report official metrics per domain (Tweets vs. SMS) and include confidence intervals.
- Report performance when target spans are not forced to be constituents; quantify how often this occurs and its effect.
- Add a sequence baseline (biRNN/biLSTM with attention to target) and a tree-CRF or structured SVM baseline to contextualize gains.
- Provide quantitative head-to-head results against Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive model using identical embeddings and data.
- Analyze error types, especially cases where global context flips a local prediction, to substantiate the mechanism.
- Discuss runtime and parameter counts, and whether multiple backward passes add value over a single pass.

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-executed empirical paper with a clear idea and promising results, though with incremental novelty and some experimental gaps. With clarifications and stronger baselines, it would be a good fit for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
