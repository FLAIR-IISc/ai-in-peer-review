**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a novel extension of Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) called Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs) for natural language processing tasks. The main idea is to incorporate contextual information into the recursive node vector representations by adding a feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words, enabling the model to capture context-dependent sentiment. The authors also propose a hybrid approach to word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised learning. The model is evaluated on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on RNNs for natural language processing, including the use of recursive neural networks for sentiment analysis. The authors also draw inspiration from bidirectional recurrent neural networks and window-based methods, but argue that their approach is more effective in capturing long-range contextual dependencies. The hybrid word vector representation approach is also related to previous work on combining unsupervised and supervised learning for word embeddings.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their model and experiments. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their model, and provide a thorough comparison to other approaches.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide sufficient information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent throughout. The figures and tables are well-designed and help to illustrate the main points.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel extension of RNNs, and the idea of incorporating contextual information through a feedbackward step is original and interesting. The hybrid word vector representation approach is also a new contribution. While the paper builds upon previous work, the authors provide a significant advance in the state of the art.

**Significance**

The results are important, and the paper demonstrates a significant improvement over previous approaches on the SemEval 2013 challenge. The model has the potential to be widely applicable to other natural language processing tasks, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach can be used for other tasks such as word-sense disambiguation. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and provides a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
