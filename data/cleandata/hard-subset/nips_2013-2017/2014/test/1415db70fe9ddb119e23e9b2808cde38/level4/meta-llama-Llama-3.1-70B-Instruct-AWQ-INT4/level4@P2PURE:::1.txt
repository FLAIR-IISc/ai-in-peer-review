This paper introduces "belief propagation recursive neural networks," which extend feedforward recursive networks by incorporating a backward (top-down) step during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels.

The paper is well-written, and the model is well-motivated and clearly described. It builds upon the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), with a notable contribution being the introduction of hybrid word vectors, which may be the most significant difference from previous work.

However, the experimental comparisons are not entirely fair. It is unclear whether the RNN and B-RNN models also utilized hybrid vectors, and the paper does not provide details on how the "best" models were tuned and selected. Additionally, the fact that the other methods do not use an ensemble approach should be acknowledged. These points require clarification.

The term "belief propagation" may be misleading, and the term "forward-backward" could be more suitable.

Section 3.5 (Training) is brief and could benefit from additional details. It appears that the training process employs the back-propagation through structure algorithm (Goller, 1996), which involves unfolding the recursive model, resulting in a very deep network to update the word embedding part. The authors should provide more information on this crucial step.

Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing, and the differences between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie should be clarified.

Section 4.1 requires improvement to facilitate understanding by readers outside the NLP community. The task description for Semeval 2013, specifically the contextual polarity task, should be presented more clearly, including the parser constraints.

Consider merging sections 4.3 and 4.4.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents an interesting variant of the Bidirectional recursive model.
