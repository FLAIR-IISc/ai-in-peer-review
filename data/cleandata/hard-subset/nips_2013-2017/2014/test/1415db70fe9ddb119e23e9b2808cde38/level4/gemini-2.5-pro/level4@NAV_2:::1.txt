This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a model designed for context-dependent classification tasks in natural language processing. The central idea is to augment standard feedforward (bottom-up) Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward (top-down) pass during inference. This allows representations and predictions at the phrase level to propagate context back down to the word level, enabling the model to make more informed, context-aware predictions. This approach builds upon prior work in recursive and bidirectional models, notably the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks of Irsoy and Cardie (2012). The authors demonstrate their model on an aspect-specific sentiment analysis task, reporting state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 benchmark. Additionally, the paper introduces the use of hybrid word vectors (a combination of unsupervised and supervised representations) and dropout for training RNNs.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of context-dependent classification, which is a known limitation of standard feedforward compositional models. The proposed solution is intuitive and well-motivated.
*   **Originality:** While related to bidirectional recursive models, the proposed GB-RNN architecture and its specific formulation for the feedbackward pass are novel. The introduction and successful application of hybrid word vectors is also a valuable contribution.
*   **Quality:** The model achieves strong empirical results, outperforming previous state-of-the-art systems on a competitive public benchmark.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper's novelty relative to the Bidirectional RNN by Irsoy and Cardie needs to be more clearly articulated in Section 3.6. The current comparison is somewhat confusing, and a more precise explanation of the architectural differences and their theoretical motivations would strengthen the paper. It is also unclear whether the main performance gain comes from the GB-RNN architecture itself or from the novel hybrid word vector representations.
*   **Experimental Fairness:** The experimental comparisons could be made more rigorous. For the results to be fully convincing, it must be clarified whether the baseline RNN and Bidirectional RNN models were also trained using the proposed hybrid vectors and dropout. Furthermore, details on the hyperparameter tuning and model selection process are needed (e.g., how the "single best model" was chosen for comparison). The use of an ensemble for the final competition result should also be more clearly contextualized when comparing against single-model baselines.
*   **Clarity and Detail:**
    *   The term "Global Belief" may be misleading for readers familiar with probabilistic graphical models and belief propagation. A term like "Forward-Backward Recursive Network" might be more descriptive and avoid potential confusion.
    *   The Training section (3.5) is too brief. The authors should explicitly state that they use backpropagation through structure (citing Goller & Küchler, 1996) and elaborate on this process. Given that the feedbackward pass effectively unfolds the tree and creates a very deep network, a more detailed discussion of the training dynamics and challenges would be beneficial.
    *   The task description in Section 4.1 would benefit from more detail to be accessible to readers outside the NLP community. Specifying that the model addresses the contextual polarity subtask of SemEval 2013 Task 2 would provide helpful context for understanding the experimental setup and parser constraints.
    *   The reference to Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete in the submitted draft.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles a significant limitation of existing recursive models by incorporating global sentence context into local predictions.
2.  The proposed GB-RNN model is elegant, and the empirical results are state-of-the-art on a challenging public dataset.
3.  The introduction of hybrid word vectors is a useful and effective contribution that could benefit other models in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The experimental comparisons with key baselines may not be entirely fair, which weakens the claims about the proposed architecture's superiority.
2.  Key sections describing the training procedure and the comparison to closely related work lack the necessary detail and clarity for a top-tier publication.
3.  The paper does not adequately disentangle the performance gains from the novel architecture versus the improved word representations and training techniques (dropout).

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and promising extension to recursive neural models with strong results. However, its claims would be significantly strengthened by addressing the issues of experimental clarity and providing a more detailed and precise technical description.
