Summary
The paper proposes belief propagation recursive neural networks, which extend standard bottom-up recursive models with a top-down (feedback) inference step. This allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to inform word-level embeddings and labels. The approach is motivated, clearly described overall, and yields promising results. A key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors that integrate bottom-up and top-down information.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for augmenting bottom-up composition with a feedback step so that higher-level structure can refine lower-level representations.
- Generally well written and easy to follow at a high level.
- The hybrid word vectors are an interesting idea and may be the main differentiator from prior bidirectional recursive models.
- Empirical results are strong.

Concerns and requests for clarification
1) Terminology. The term “belief propagation” is potentially misleading, as the method does not implement probabilistic BP on a graphical model. “Forward–backward” or “bidirectional recursive” would be more accurate and less confusing.

2) Relation to prior work. The connection to and differences from Irsoy and Cardie need to be clarified and properly cited (the current reference is incomplete; please correct the year and venue). Section 3.6 should make explicit:
- Whether the top-down pass in your model uses the same or separate parameters from the bottom-up pass.
- How your feedback mechanism differs from earlier bidirectional recursive networks (e.g., whether phrase-level labels/embeddings directly modify leaf embeddings, how information is injected, and whether the feedback is iterative or single-pass).
- What, precisely, the hybrid vectors add beyond bidirectionality, and whether similar hybrids were considered in prior work.

3) Training details (Section 3.5). This section is too brief. Please specify:
- Whether you use backpropagation through structure (e.g., Goller and Küchler, 1996) and how the network is unfolded across the tree to propagate gradients to word embeddings.
- How the top-down step is handled during training: is there one bottom-up pass followed by one top-down pass per update, or multiple iterations? Are both passes differentiated jointly?
- Optimization details (objective(s), optimizer, learning rate schedules), regularization (dropout, L2), initialization, and whether embeddings are fine-tuned.
- Any measures to address vanishing/exploding gradients given the potentially very deep unfolded trees (gradient clipping, normalization).
- Computational complexity and runtime overhead of the added top-down step.

4) Experimental fairness and reporting. The current comparisons raise questions:
- Do the RNN and B-RNN baselines also use hybrid word vectors? If not, the comparison may conflate architecture with representation. Please ensure feature parity or report ablations that isolate the effect of hybrid vectors and the top-down pass.
- How are hyperparameters tuned for all models? Describe the search space, selection criterion (e.g., dev set performance), and whether early stopping was used.
- You report ensemble results for your method, while other methods appear to be single models. Please report single-model results alongside ensemble results, and clarify how the ensemble is constructed and selected.
- Explain how the “best” model(s) are chosen and whether test sets are used only once.

5) Dataset/task description (Section 4.1). For readers outside NLP, please provide a clearer overview of the SemEval 2013 Task 2 setup:
- Specify whether you address the contextual polarity or the message-level subtask (it appears to be contextual polarity).
- Describe the data splits, label space, and evaluation metrics.
- Explain parser constraints and how parsing errors are handled during training and inference.

6) Inference procedure. It would help to spell out the exact sequence and number of passes at inference time (one bottom-up pass followed by one top-down pass?), whether any form of convergence/iteration is used, and how phrase-level predictions are combined with word-level predictions.

7) Sections 4.3 and 4.4. These sections could likely be merged for a more concise and coherent presentation.

Suggestions for additional analysis
- Ablate hybrid vectors and the top-down pass to quantify their individual contributions.
- Compare single-pass bidirectional recursion vs. your feedback mechanism if they differ.
- Report sensitivity to parser quality and tree depth.
- Provide qualitative examples showing how phrase-level information changes word-level predictions/embeddings.

Minor presentation issues
- Fix the incomplete citation to Irsoy and Cardie and verify all related work references.
- Clarify notation early and keep it consistent across bottom-up and top-down equations.
- Proofread for minor typos (e.g., “wok” → “work”).

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and clearly presented extension of recursive neural networks that leverages top-down feedback and hybrid word vectors. The idea is interesting and the empirical gains are promising. However, the paper currently overstates the connection to belief propagation, and the experimental methodology and training details need strengthening to ensure fairness and reproducibility. With clearer positioning relative to prior bidirectional recursive models, fuller training/inference details, and fair single-model baselines plus targeted ablations (especially around hybrid vectors), I would lean toward acceptance.
