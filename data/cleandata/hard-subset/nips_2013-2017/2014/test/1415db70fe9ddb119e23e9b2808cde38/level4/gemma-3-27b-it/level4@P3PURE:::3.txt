This paper proposes utilizing both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification. The authors term this approach “belief propagation,” however, this is a misnomer as it does not represent an instance of the standard inference algorithm and consequently may mislead the reader. While generally well-written, the motivation for the proposed model remains vague and lacks a clear theoretical underpinning.

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its evaluation methodology. Presenting an ensemble result is not justified within this experimental context, as none of the benchmarks employ ensembles, and this obscures the performance attributable to the BP-RNN architecture. Furthermore, the exact composition of the compared models is unclear. Critical details requiring clarification include: how were the individual models tuned, and what criteria were used to select the ensemble/best performing models? How were the benchmark models in Table 2 tuned? Specifically, did they utilize the same data augmentation techniques as the BP-RNN? Were the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models also leveraging hybrid word vectors? 

Without this information, it is reasonable to assume that the second entry in Table 3 – representing a single model – is the most directly comparable to the benchmarks presented in Tables 1 & 2 when assessing the effectiveness of the BP-RNN architecture.

Minor points:
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is surprising, given the unique characteristics of this data. Reporting the parser's accuracy on this dataset would be valuable.
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete, as are several others throughout the paper.

In summary, this paper introduces a recursive neural network model for contextual phrasal sentiment classification. The reported results are reasonable, but the model’s motivation is not strongly articulated and the experimental methodology lacks complete clarity.
