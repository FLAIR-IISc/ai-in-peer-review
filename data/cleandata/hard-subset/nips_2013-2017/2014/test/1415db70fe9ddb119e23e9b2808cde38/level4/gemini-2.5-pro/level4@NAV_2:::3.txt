This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed to address the limitations of standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) in capturing context for tasks like aspect-specific sentiment analysis. The central idea is to augment the conventional bottom-up, feedforward pass of an RNN with a top-down, "feedbackward" pass. This second pass propagates information from the global sentence representation (the root of the parse tree) back down to the leaf nodes, allowing word and phrase representations to be refined by their surrounding context. The final prediction for each node is made using a concatenation of its feedforward and feedbackward vectors. The paper also explores the benefits of dropout for training RNNs and proposes a hybrid word vector representation that combines pre-trained unsupervised vectors with task-specific supervised vectors. The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it is compared against previous competition winners and other recursive models, including the similar concurrent work of Irsoy and Cardie (2013).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance & Motivation:** The paper addresses a well-known and important limitation of standard compositional models: their inability to handle context-dependent phenomena. The task of aspect-specific sentiment analysis is an excellent and challenging test case for this problem.
*   **Originality & Approach:** The proposed two-pass architecture is an intuitive and sensible solution. Propagating global context back down the parse tree to refine local predictions is a novel extension to the standard RNN framework. The model demonstrates a clear advance over purely feedforward approaches.
*   **Quality of Results:** The empirical results are strong. The GB-RNN achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 dataset, outperforming both the competition winner and several strong baselines, including a standard RNN and a bidirectional RNN. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Auxiliary Contributions:** The findings that dropout improves RNN training and that hybrid word vectors outperform purely supervised or unsupervised variants are valuable contributions in their own right.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the clarity of the experimental methodology, which makes it difficult to isolate the source of the performance gains and assess the core architectural contribution.
    *   The headline result in Table 1 is from an ensemble of models. This obfuscates the performance of a single GB-RNN model and makes the comparison to other non-ensemble systems less direct. The paper would be stronger if it focused on single-model performance for its primary comparisons.
    *   Crucial details about the training of the baseline models in Table 2 are omitted. To ensure a fair and rigorous comparison, it is essential to know: Were the standard RNN and bidirectional-RNN baselines also trained with the same augmented data (NRC lexicon, noisily labeled tweets)? Were they given the same hybrid word vectors and trained with dropout? Were their hyperparameters tuned with the same care as the GB-RNN? Without this information, it is impossible to determine how much of the reported 5% F1 improvement is due to the feedbackward architecture versus the data augmentation or other training enhancements.
*   **Clarity of Motivation and Terminology:**
    *   While the intuition is clear, the theoretical motivation for the specific update rules (Eq. 3 and 5) could be stronger. A more detailed justification for the design choices in the "de-composition" matrix `W↓` would be welcome.
    *   The name "Global Belief" is potentially confusing, as the method is not an instance of the belief propagation algorithm from graphical models. A more descriptive name focused on contextual feedback or refinement might be more appropriate.

**Minor Points:**

*   The use of a standard off-the-shelf parser for tweets is questionable, given the non-standard grammar and syntax common in such texts. A comment on the parser's accuracy on this domain would be helpful.
*   Several citations in the bibliography are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie, 2013).

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel and effective recursive neural network architecture for context-dependent sentiment classification. The model is well-motivated, and the empirical results appear to be state-of-the-art. However, the evaluation lacks the necessary clarity and detail to fully support the claims about the architectural contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors provided a more rigorous and transparent comparison to the baseline models.
