

This paper proposes leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase-level vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification. The authors term this approach "belief propagation," though this is not an instance of the belief propagation inference algorithm, and the terminology is misleading. While the paper is well-written, the motivation for the proposed model lacks clarity and a strong theoretical foundation.  

The primary weakness lies in the evaluation methodology. The inclusion of ensemble results is unjustified, as none of the benchmarks are ensembles, and this obscures the comparison. The exact composition of the models under comparison is also unclear. Critical questions remain unanswered: How were the models tuned, and how were the ensemble or best models selected? How were the benchmarks in Table 2 tuned? Did they use the same augmented data as the BP-RNN? Were the RNN and bidirectional RNN models also trained with hybrid word vectors?  

Without this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears most comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 for evaluating the BP-RNN architecture.  

Minor points:  
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweet parsing is surprising; its accuracy on this data would be informative.  
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie (as well as several others) is incomplete.  

The paper presents a recursive neural network model for phrasal sentiment classification in context. The results appear reasonable, but the model lacks a compelling motivation, and the experimental methodology requires greater clarity.
