This paper introduces Belief Propagation Recursive Neural Networks (BPRNNs), extending traditional feedforward recursive networks with a novel backward pass during inference. This allows for phrase-level predictions and embeddings to inform and refine word embeddings and labels, creating a potentially more contextually aware model.

The paper is generally well-written and the model is clearly motivated. It builds upon the foundation laid by Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks (BRNNs) introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), though the citation is currently incomplete. A key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors. It is unclear, however, if this is the most significant differentiator from prior work. 

My primary concern lies with the fairness of the experimental comparisons. Were the RNN and BRNN baselines also trained with hybrid word vectors? Further clarification is needed regarding the hyperparameter tuning and model selection process – specifically, how the “best” models were identified. The absence of ensembling in the baseline methods is noted, and the impact of this difference should be discussed. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the validity of the reported results.

The terminology of “belief propagation” feels somewhat misleading in this context. A more descriptive term, such as “forward-backward”, might more accurately reflect the model’s architecture and operation. 

Section 3.5 (Training) is comparatively brief and lacks detail. It is likely that backpropagation through structure (BPTT) is employed – citing the seminal work by Goller (1996) would be beneficial. Implementing BPTT necessitates unfolding the recursive model, effectively creating a very deep network to reach the word embedding layer for gradient updates. A more in-depth explanation of this training process, and its potential challenges (e.g., vanishing gradients), would be valuable.

Section 3.6 currently lacks clarity. The distinction between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie needs to be more explicitly articulated. A direct comparison of the architectural differences and the specific benefits of BPRNNs would be helpful.

Section 4.1 requires improvement to ensure accessibility for readers outside the Natural Language Processing (NLP) community. The SemEval 2013 task 2 consists of two distinct subtasks: contextual and message polarity. The authors should clearly specify which subtask was addressed (I believe it is contextual polarity) and provide sufficient background information to allow readers to understand the task’s nuances, including the rationale behind the parser constraints.

Consider merging sections 4.3 and 4.4 for a more concise presentation.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable variant of the bidirectional recursive model. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding experimental fairness, training details, and clarity of comparison to existing work, would substantially strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
