In NLP, recursive neural networks (RNNs) have been used to produce representations of text snippets by recursively combining pairs of representations of words or shorter snippets. This process starts with the representation of words and proceeds up a pre-specified parse tree until the representation of the entire snippet is obtained at the root node. The unidirectional nature of this process, however, prevents information propagation down the tree from larger to smaller contexts. This paper introduces a bidirectional extension of RNNs in which the upward pass through the tree is followed by a downward pass, augmenting the representations of all nodes with information from larger contexts. The resulting system achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 Task 2.

The extension of the established RNN-based approach proposed in the paper is simple, elegant, and effective. While the high-level idea shares similarities with that of Irsoy and Cardie [12], the computation performed on the downward pass differs, and the resulting performance appears superior. 

The method is well-motivated and clearly presented, and the paper is generally well-written. The experimental results are strong; however, I have some questions regarding the experimental setup. Specifically, was the result for the bidirectional RNN model from Irsoy and Cardie [12] in Table 2 obtained using dropout and the additional training data described in Section 4.4? Were the hybrid word vectors also used in that comparison? Additionally, while the ensemble result is impressive, its inclusion in a table comparing individual model performance is somewhat misleading.

The phrase "widely used mix of both" in Section 4.4 lacks clarity. Overall, this is a well-executed paper based on a simple and elegant idea.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel bidirectional extension to RNNs addresses a clear limitation of existing models.
*   Strong experimental results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark task.
*   Clear and well-motivated presentation of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor ambiguity in the description of experimental details (dropout, data, and vector usage in baseline comparison).
*   Ensemble result should be presented separately from individual model comparisons.
*   Lack of clarity regarding "widely used mix of both" in Section 4.4.
