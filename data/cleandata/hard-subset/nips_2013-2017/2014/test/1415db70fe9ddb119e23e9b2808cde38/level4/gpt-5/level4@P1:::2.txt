Summary
The paper addresses a key limitation of standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) for NLP: their purely bottom-up, feedforward composition prevents information from larger contexts from influencing representations lower in the parse tree. The authors propose a bidirectional extension in which the upward pass is followed by a downward pass that propagates “global belief” from the root back to the leaves. This augments each node’s representation with context from higher nodes and enables context-dependent labeling, such as aspect-specific sentiment (e.g., classifying “Android” as positive in “Android beats iOS”). The approach yields strong results on SemEval Task 2 (reported as 2012 in the paper), outperforming standard RNNs and achieving state-of-the-art performance. The paper also reports gains from dropout and from hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with smaller supervised components.

Relation to prior work
The proposed model is conceptually related to bidirectional recurrent/recursive architectures. Although the high-level idea resembles that of [18], the downward computation here differs: the backward vectors are computed via a top-down recursion that combines both forward and backward parent information, and the empirical performance appears superior. The work builds on established compositional RNNs while extending them with a simple feedbackward step during inference.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: purely feedforward composition misses context-dependent labeling; the proposed top-down pass directly addresses this.
- Simple, elegant extension: the model minimally augments standard RNNs with a backward pass and concatenated forward/backward representations for classification.
- Strong empirical results: state-of-the-art on the target benchmark, with additional insights on dropout and hybrid word vectors.
- Presentation: the model and experiments are described clearly and read well.

Concerns and questions about the experimental setup
- Fairness of the baseline comparison: Was the alternative bidirectional RNN from [18] in Table 2 trained with the same enhancements (dropout, additional training data)? Were the hybrid word vectors also used for that model? Clarifying this is important for a fair comparison.
- Ensemble reporting: The ensemble result is impressive, but it does not belong in a table intended to compare individual models.
- Section 4.4 wording: What does the “widely used mix of both” refer to? Please clarify.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The paper appears technically sound and complete. The claims are supported by experimental results. However, the fairness and exact conditions of the bidirectional baseline comparison need clarification.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. The model description is easy to follow. Minor clarifications as noted above would further improve readability.
- Originality: While the overarching idea of a top-down pass is not entirely new, the specific downward computation and the demonstrated effectiveness are novel in this context. The relation to [18] is acknowledged; emphasizing the precise differences helps delineate the contribution.
- Significance: The results are strong and likely to be of interest to researchers working on compositional semantics and sentiment analysis, especially for aspect-level or context-dependent labeling. The simplicity of the approach increases its potential impact and adoptability.

Pros
- Addresses a real limitation of standard RNNs with a minimal, effective change.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark.
- Clear exposition and solid empirical work.
- Practical insights on dropout and hybrid word vectors.

Cons
- Ambiguity about whether competing bidirectional baselines received identical training enhancements.
- Inclusion of ensemble results alongside single-model comparisons.
- Minor clarity issues (e.g., “widely used mix of both”).

Overall assessment
A well-executed paper based on a simple and elegant idea. The methodological contribution is clear, the empirical results are compelling, and the writing is strong. My main reservation concerns the exact experimental conditions for the bidirectional baseline from [18]; if those comparisons are made fully fair (same dropout, hybrid vectors, additional data), the results will be even more convincing. Clarifying the ensemble reporting and the ambiguous phrasing in Section 4.4 would further strengthen the paper.
