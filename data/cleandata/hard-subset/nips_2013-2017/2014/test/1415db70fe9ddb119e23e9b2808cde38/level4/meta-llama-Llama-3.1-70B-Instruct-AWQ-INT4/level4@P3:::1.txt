This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel extension of feedforward recursive neural networks that incorporates a feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words, enabling more accurate contextual sentiment analysis. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GB-RNNs on the SemEval 2013 challenge and show that they outperform standard RNNs and other baselines.

The paper is well-written, and the model is well-motivated and described. The introduction of hybrid word vectors, which combine unsupervised and supervised word vector representations, is a significant contribution. However, the experimental comparisons could be improved. Specifically, it is unclear whether the RNN and Bidirectional RNN (B-RNN) models used in the comparisons also employed hybrid vectors. Additionally, the authors should clarify how the "best" models were tuned and selected, and how the single best model was chosen. It is also worth noting that the other methods used in the comparisons did not employ an ensemble approach.

The term "Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks" might be misleading, and the authors may consider using a more descriptive term, such as "forward-backward" recursive neural networks.

Section 3.5 (Training) is brief and could benefit from more details. Specifically, the authors should clarify whether they used the back-propagation through structure algorithm (Goller, 1996) and provide more information on how the recursive model is unfolded during training. This would help readers understand how the word embedding part is updated during training.

Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing, and the authors should clarify the differences between their model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie (2008). A more detailed comparison of the two models would be helpful.

Section 4.1 could be improved to make it more accessible to readers outside the NLP community. The authors should provide more context about the SemEval 2013 challenge, specifically the contextual polarity task, and clarify the parser constraints used in the evaluation.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could potentially be merged, as they both present experimental results.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting variant of the Bidirectional recursive model and demonstrates its effectiveness on a challenging task. With some clarifications and improvements to the experimental comparisons, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel extension of feedforward recursive neural networks that incorporates a feedbackward step during inference.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GB-RNNs on a challenging task, outperforming standard RNNs and other baselines.
* The introduction of hybrid word vectors is a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental comparisons could be improved, specifically with regards to the use of hybrid vectors in the RNN and B-RNN models.
* The paper could benefit from more details on the training process, specifically the use of the back-propagation through structure algorithm.
* Some sections, such as Section 3.6, could be clarified to improve the overall readability of the paper.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
