This paper proposes a recursive neural network architecture for sentiment classification that utilizes both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive auto-encoder. While the paper is generally well-written and the empirical results appear promising, the work suffers from a lack of clear motivation and, more significantly, from an insufficiently detailed and potentially misleading experimental evaluation.

**Strengths:**
The paper is clearly written and presents an interesting architectural idea for leveraging more of the information learned by a recursive auto-encoder in a downstream task. The concept of using both encoding and decoding representations for classification is intriguing.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Conceptual Motivation and Terminology:**
    The central weakness from a conceptual standpoint is the paper's motivation and chosen terminology. The authors refer to their method as "belief propagation," but the proposed mechanism is not an instance of the well-known graphical model inference algorithm. This naming choice is confusing and misleading. The paper would be improved by selecting a more descriptive name that accurately reflects the model's operation (e.g., "Bidirectional Recursive Auto-encoder for Classification"). Furthermore, the motivation for using the decoding vector in addition to the encoding vector is not well-justified. While the authors provide some intuition, a more robust theoretical or empirical justification is needed to explain why this architectural choice should lead to better performance.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    The most significant flaw in this paper is the experimental methodology, which lacks the clarity and rigor required for a fair assessment of the proposed model.

    *   **Ensemble Results:** The primary results are presented for an ensemble of models. This is highly problematic as none of the benchmarks used for comparison are ensembles. This obfuscates the true performance of the proposed architecture and makes a direct, fair comparison impossible. The core contribution is the BP-RNN architecture, and its performance should be evaluated and compared as a single model.

    *   **Lack of Experimental Details:** The paper omits crucial details about the experimental setup, making the results difficult to interpret and reproduce. The authors should clarify the following key questions:
        *   **Model Tuning:** How were the hyperparameters for the proposed models (BP-RNN, RNN, Bi-RNN) tuned? How were the final models for the ensemble and the "best" single model selected? Was a dedicated validation set used for this process?
        *   **Benchmark Tuning:** How were the benchmark models in Table 2 tuned? Were they re-implemented and tuned using the same data splits and procedures as the proposed model, or were the results taken from previous publications? If the latter, it is essential to confirm that the experimental conditions were identical.
        *   **Data and Feature Consistency:** Did the baseline models (RNN and Bidirectional-RNN) also use the same augmented data as the BP-RNN? Similarly, were the "hybrid word vectors" used for all compared models to ensure that performance gains are attributable to the architecture rather than the input features?

    Without this information, it is difficult to properly evaluate the paper's claims. As it stands, I would surmise that the second entry in Table 3 ("BP-RNN, hybrid vectors") is the most scientifically sound point of comparison against the benchmarks presented in Tables 1 & 2. This should be the focus of the evaluation.

**Minor Points:**

*   I am surprised that an off-the-shelf parser was used for Twitter data, which is notoriously noisy and grammatically unconventional. It would be valuable to know the parser's accuracy on this specific dataset or to include a discussion of how parsing errors might have impacted the results.
*   The bibliography needs to be revised. Several citations are incomplete, including the one for Irsoy and Cardie.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents an interesting idea for contextual phrasal sentiment classification. However, the model lacks a strong motivation, and the claims are not fully supported by the current experimental evaluation due to a lack of methodological clarity and the confounding use of an ensemble for the main result. For this work to be considered for publication, the authors must address these critical issues by providing a clear justification for their model, abandoning the misleading "belief propagation" terminology, and, most importantly, conducting and presenting a transparent and methodologically sound experimental comparison of their single model against appropriately configured baselines.
