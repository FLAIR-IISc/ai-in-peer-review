Summary
The paper proposes a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment analysis that uses both encoding and decoding phrase representations for classification. The authors refer to this as “belief propagation,” but the method is not an instance of the belief propagation algorithm; this terminology is misleading and should be revised. The paper is generally well written, but the motivation for the proposed architecture is vague and lacks clear theoretical justification.

Strengths
- The task—context-sensitive phrasal sentiment classification—is important, and the empirical results appear competitive.
- The model design (leveraging both encoder and decoder representations) is an interesting idea that could plausibly capture complementary information.

Main Concerns
1) Motivation and justification
- The paper does not adequately explain why combining encoder and decoder phrase vectors should improve sentiment prediction relative to using only one of them. A stronger conceptual or theoretical rationale (e.g., what distinct information each vector is expected to carry and why they are complementary for sentiment) would improve the contribution.
- The use of the term “belief propagation” is inappropriate here and confuses the reader. Please either justify the terminology with a precise connection or adopt a term that accurately reflects the approach.

2) Evaluation design and reporting
- Reporting ensemble results is inappropriate in this comparison, as none of the benchmarks use ensembles. This choice obscures the contribution of the architecture itself. Single-model results should be the primary comparison against baselines.
- The exact composition of the compared models is unclear. Key details are missing:
  • How were hyperparameters tuned for each model?  
  • How were “best” models and ensembles selected (e.g., validation split, model selection criteria, number of runs, variance across runs)?  
  • How were the baseline methods in Table 2 tuned?  
  • Did the baselines use the same data augmentations as BP-RNN?  
  • Did the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines also use hybrid word vectors, or any other enhancements used by the proposed model?

- Without this information, it is difficult to assess fairness and isolate the effect of the proposed architecture. As it stands, the second entry in Table 3 appears to be the most comparable reference point to Tables 1 and 2 for evaluating BP-RNN.

3) Missing ablations and controls
- To substantiate the claimed benefit of using both encoder and decoder phrase vectors, please include ablations that:
  • Use only the encoder representation  
  • Use only the decoder representation  
  • Remove data augmentation and hybrid word vectors  
  • Compare single models versus ensembles (reported separately)
- Report variance across multiple random seeds and conduct significance tests against the strongest single-model baselines under matched settings.

4) Clarity on data, features, and preprocessing
- Clearly specify training/validation/test splits, data augmentation procedures, and whether all systems share identical training data and preprocessing.
- If hybrid word vectors or external resources are used, state which models use them and whether baselines are allowed the same resources.

Minor Comments
- Parsing tweets with an off-the-shelf constituency parser is surprising given domain mismatch. Please report parsing accuracy or a proxy quality measure on this dataset (or cite prior evidence), and discuss the impact of parsing errors on the model.
- The Irsoy and Cardie citation is incomplete, as are several others. Please provide full bibliographic details.
- Consider renaming the approach to avoid confusion with probabilistic belief propagation (e.g., “bidirectional auto-encoding representations” or similar).

Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a meaningful problem and presents a potentially useful idea—combining encoder and decoder phrase vectors for sentiment classification. However, the current presentation suffers from (i) weak motivation and lack of theoretical grounding, (ii) unclear and potentially unfair evaluation practices (especially the emphasis on ensemble results against non-ensemble baselines), and (iii) missing experimental details needed to ensure a sound comparison.

I encourage the authors to:
- Provide a clear conceptual motivation for using both representations, ideally supported by ablations and analysis.  
- Focus on single-model comparisons under rigorously matched conditions (data, features, hyperparameters).  
- Fully document tuning procedures, selection criteria, and resources for all methods.  
- Add variance and significance reporting, and include controls isolating each component’s contribution.

With these clarifications and a cleaner empirical comparison, the contribution would be much easier to evaluate and could be of interest to the community. In its current form, the results are difficult to interpret and the benefits of the proposed architecture remain uncertain.
