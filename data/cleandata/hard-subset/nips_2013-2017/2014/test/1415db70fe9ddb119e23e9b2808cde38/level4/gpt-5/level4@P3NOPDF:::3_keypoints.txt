- Paper proposes a recursive neural auto-encoder for sentiment using both encoder and decoder phrase representations, but calling it “belief propagation” is misleading.  
- Strength: tackles context-sensitive phrasal sentiment classification with competitive empirical results.  
- Strength: leveraging both encoder and decoder representations is an interesting idea that may capture complementary information.  
- Main concern: insufficient motivation and theoretical justification for why combining encoder and decoder vectors improves sentiment prediction.  
- Main concern: the term “belief propagation” is inappropriate unless a precise connection is provided; recommendation to rename or justify.  
- Evaluation concern: reporting ensemble results obscures the architecture’s contribution since baselines are single models; single-model comparisons should be primary.  
- Evaluation concern: key experimental details are missing (hyperparameter tuning, model/ensemble selection criteria, number of runs, variance).  
- Evaluation concern: unclear whether baselines were tuned or used the same augmentations, hybrid word vectors, or enhancements as the proposed model.  
- Fairness concern: without the above details, it is hard to isolate the effect of the architecture; Table 3’s second entry seems the most comparable baseline.  
- Missing ablations: need experiments using only encoder, only decoder, removing data augmentation and hybrid vectors, and single-model vs ensemble comparisons.  
- Statistical rigor: report variance across random seeds and run significance tests against the strongest single-model baselines.  
- Data/feature clarity: specify training/validation/test splits, augmentation procedures, and ensure all systems share identical data and preprocessing.  
- Resource disclosure: state which models use hybrid word vectors or external resources and whether baselines had equal access.  
- Minor: constituency parsing tweets with an off-the-shelf parser may be problematic—report parsing accuracy or cite evidence and discuss error impact.  
- Minor: several citations (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie) are incomplete and should be completed.  
- Minor: consider renaming the approach to avoid confusion with probabilistic belief propagation (e.g., “bidirectional auto-encoding representations”).  
- Overall: meaningful problem and promising idea, but weak motivation, unclear/unfair evaluation, and missing experimental details make the results hard to interpret.  
- Recommended fixes: furnish clear conceptual motivation and ablations, focus on rigorously matched single-model comparisons, fully document tuning/selection, and report variance and significance.
