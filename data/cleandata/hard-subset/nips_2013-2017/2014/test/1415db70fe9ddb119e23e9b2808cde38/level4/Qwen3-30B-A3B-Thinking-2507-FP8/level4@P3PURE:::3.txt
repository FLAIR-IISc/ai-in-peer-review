

This paper proposes utilizing both the encoding and decoding phrase-level vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification, terming this approach "belief propagation." However, this terminology is misleading, as the method does not constitute an instance of belief propagation inference, and its use confuses the reader. While the paper is generally well-written, the motivation for the proposed model is vague and lacks clear theoretical justification.

The primary weakness lies in the evaluation methodology. The inclusion of ensemble results is unjustified in this context, as none of the benchmarks are presented as ensembles, and this obscures the reported findings. Furthermore, the exact composition of the compared models is unclear. Key questions requiring clarification are: (1) how the models were tuned and the ensemble/best models selected; (2) how the benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned; (3) whether the benchmarks used the same augmented data as the BP-RNN; and (4) whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN also employed hybrid word vectors. Without this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears to be the most directly comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 for assessing the BP-RNN architecture.

Minor points:
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweet processing warrants clarification regarding its accuracy on this domain.
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie (as well as several others) is incomplete.

The paper presents a recursive neural network model for phrasal sentiment classification in context. The results appear plausible, but the model lacks a strong theoretical motivation, and the experimental methodology is insufficiently detailed.
