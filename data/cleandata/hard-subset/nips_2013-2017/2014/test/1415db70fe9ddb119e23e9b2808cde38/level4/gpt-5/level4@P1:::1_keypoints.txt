- Paper introduces GB-RNNs: standard feedforward recursive networks plus a single top-down (backward) inference step to allow phrase-level context to influence word-level predictions.  
- Proposes hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised concat small supervised component) and finds dropout improves RNN training.  
- Empirical gains: ~3% F1 improvement over standard RNN on SemEval 2013 Task 2 Twitter data and state-of-the-art results, including an ensemble of top five GB-RNNs.  
- Clear motivation: feedforward recursive models fail to capture aspect-specific, context-dependent sentiment.  
- Simple, conceptually straightforward extension with empirical benefit.  
- Practical contributions (hybrid vectors, dropout) appear to improve performance.  
- Strong qualitative examples demonstrating contextual disambiguation.  
- Concern about fairness of comparisons: unclear whether RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines used the same hybrid vectors, dropout, external resources, and tuning protocol.  
- Need transparency on model selection: how “best” models were tuned and how the “single best” model was chosen, and separation of ensemble vs single-model comparisons.  
- Terminology issue: calling the method “belief propagation” is misleading; prefer “forward–backward” or “top-down/backward pass at inference.”  
- Insufficient training details: Section 3.5 should specify use of backpropagation through structure (BPTS), unfolding depth, and how gradients update word embeddings for reproducibility.  
- Section 3.6 (Comparison to Other Models) is confusing; must explicitly differentiate from Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional recursive model with equations, parameter counts, and role of forward/backward representations.  
- Incomplete reference to Irsoy & Cardie—citation must be corrected and complete.  
- Section 4.1 needs clearer description of SemEval 2013 Task 2 subtasks (contextual vs message polarity) and justification of parser constraints.  
- Organizational suggestion: merge Sections 4.3 and 4.4 to concisely present baselines and the impact of extra training data.  
- Originality concern: backward inference step is related to prior bidirectional recursive work; paper should clarify what is genuinely novel beyond naming and whether hybrid vectors drive gains.  
- Significance: addresses an important limitation and appears broadly applicable, but would be more compelling with clearer comparisons and training details.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses key limitation of feedforward recursive models, strong benchmark results, and practical improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: methodological transparency issues, potentially unfair comparisons, insufficient training/unfolding details, and unclear distinction from prior bidirectional work.  
- Minor: fix Irsoy & Cardie citation, consider renaming to avoid probabilistic BP confusion, and clarify whether baselines used identical hybrid vectors and dropout.
