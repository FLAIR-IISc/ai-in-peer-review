Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive neural architecture that augments the standard bottom-up (feedforward) composition with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The bottom-up pass computes phrase representations on a parse tree; the top-down pass propagates a context vector from the root back to internal nodes and leaves, and the final prediction at each node uses both bottom-up and top-down vectors. The goal is to enable context-sensitive labeling of phrases and tokens, exemplified by aspect-specific sentiment (e.g., determining that “Android” is positive in “Android beats iOS”). The model is contrasted with bidirectional recurrent neural networks (which operate on chains and use local windows) and with bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie), which also include a top-down component but differ in how the backward vectors are computed and combined. The paper further proposes hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed unsupervised embeddings with a smaller supervised, task-learned subvector, and shows that dropout improves RNN training. Experiments focus on SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), reporting improvements over a standard RNN and competitive systems, including an ensemble of GB-RNN models.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive architectural idea that lets global sentence context inform local predictions without relying on short context windows.
- Clear mathematical specification of forward and backward computations, and a careful comparison to bidirectional recursive networks.
- Practical contributions: hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + small supervised component) and application of dropout in RNN training.
- Empirical gains on SemEval-2013 with relatively modest feature engineering; useful qualitative examples for entity/phrase-specific sentiment.
- Ablations indicate benefits from the top-down pass, hybrid embeddings, and additional data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Terminology/motivation: “Global belief” and references to “belief propagation” are potentially misleading; the method is not performing graphical-model BP. A clearer motivation as a learned top-down contextualization (or bidirectional recursion variant) would avoid confusion. The paper would benefit from a crisper theoretical rationale for why this particular top-down parameterization should improve local classification (e.g., discussion of inductive bias vs. alternative top-down transforms).
- Evaluation clarity and fairness:
  - Reporting an ensemble as the headline result obscures the contribution of the architecture relative to baselines, none of which are ensembles. Please foreground single-model results for the main comparison and report the ensemble only as an additional result.
  - Hyperparameter tuning and model selection are not described in enough detail to assess fairness. How were the GB-RNN, RNN, and bidirectional-RNN tuned (search space, selection criterion, dev sets)? Were all models trained with the same hybrid word vectors, dropout settings, and additional resources? Table 2 does not make this explicit.
  - External data: The paper uses sentiment lexicons, noisy-labeled tweets, and movie reviews. Which baselines and comparison systems in Tables 1–2 used these same resources, and how? Please provide controlled ablations showing performance with and without each external resource for all architectures under comparison.
  - Metrics and statistics: The competition score is average F1 for positive and negative. In-text improvements are sometimes reported as accuracy or F1; please standardize and include significance testing for key comparisons.
- Dataset and parsing: The Stanford Parser is used on tweets, but its accuracy on this domain is not discussed. Since parsing errors can propagate through the architecture, a brief analysis of parse quality (or robustness sensitivity to parsing) would strengthen the empirical case. If constraints were used to ensure labeled spans align to subtrees, please clarify how often this failed and how those cases were handled.
- Comparison to bidirectional recursive networks: The paper notes parameter count differences and formulates both backward passes. To ensure a fair comparison, confirm that capacity (dimensions n, nd), regularization, training schedule, and external data are matched. It would also help to report results for variants that swap only the backward equation while keeping all else identical.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Some citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) and others in the references are incomplete. There are occasional typos (e.g., “feedbackward”). Training details such as batch size, learning rates (AdaGrad settings), early stopping criteria, and code/data availability would aid reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall and clearly derived; the top-down pass is well specified, and training is standard backpropagation through structure. The empirical section shows meaningful gains, but stronger evidence of fair, like-for-like comparisons (especially regarding external resources and ensembles) is needed. Statistical significance and clearer reporting of metrics would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The model description is clear, and the comparison to related architectures is helpful. However, the “belief” terminology is confusing, some references are incomplete, and the experimental setup needs more detail on tuning, resources, and parsing. Minor editorial issues remain.

Originality
The work is an incremental but useful extension of recursive neural networks. The top-down contextualization is closely related to concurrent bidirectional recursive models; the novelty lies in the specific backward computation, concatenation scheme, and the hybrid word-vector design. The combination is practical, but the conceptual advance over prior bidirectional recursion is modest.

Significance
Context-sensitive phrase labeling is important for aspect-based sentiment and other NLP tasks. Demonstrating improvements on SemEval-2013 is valuable, particularly with relatively low reliance on hand-engineered features. Broader significance would be enhanced by cleaner single-model wins over strong baselines and by evaluation on additional datasets or tasks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Use clearer terminology (e.g., “top-down recursive pass”) and provide a tighter motivation for the chosen backward formulation.
- Make the single-model GB-RNN the primary result; report ensemble gains separately.
- Provide full tuning details and ensure all compared models share the same embeddings, dropout, and external data; include controlled ablations and significance tests.
- Analyze parser robustness on tweets and the impact of parsing errors.
- Complete and standardize citations; fix minor typos.
- Consider reporting results without any external resources to isolate the architectural contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated architectural tweak that yields consistent gains.
- Competitive results on a standard benchmark with limited feature engineering.
- Practical insights: hybrid embeddings and dropout for RNNs.

Cons for acceptance
- Headline ensemble result obscures architectural contribution; fairness of comparisons is unclear.
- Terminology and motivation could be sharper; overlap with prior bidirectional recursive work.
- Limited analysis of parsing quality and its effect on performance.

Recommendation
Borderline. The approach is promising and empirically effective, but I recommend acceptance only if the authors clarify the evaluation protocol (single-model results, fair tuning, resource usage), tighten the motivation/terminology, and complete the references. Otherwise, this leans to weak reject on methodological clarity grounds.
