- Proposes GB-RNN: a bidirectional recursive network with a top-down “feedbackward” pass that concatenates forward and backward node vectors for context-dependent phrase labeling.  
- Evaluated on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter/SMS) and reports state-of-the-art results, with an ensemble surpassing the competition winner.  
- Introduces hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings + small supervised trainable slice) and shows dropout improves training.  
- Situates work among prior bidirectional recursive/recurrent models; key novelty is the specific backward computation using both forward/backward parent vectors, a decomposition matrix W↓, and a root transform V.  
- Strength: simple, elegant architectural extension with clear motivation for contextual labeling (e.g., aspect-specific sentiment).  
- Strength: strong empirical gains over a standard RNN and prior bidirectional recursive baseline; practical training recipe.  
- Strength: generally clear exposition and equations, and appropriate noisy-text task choice.  
- Weakness: unclear whether baselines used matched resources/training (hybrid embeddings, dropout, lexicons, weakly labeled data)—needs clarification on Table 2 parity.  
- Weakness: ensemble reporting obscures single-model comparisons; main table should focus on single-model results or clearly annotate ensembles.  
- Weakness: insufficient transparency about additional data (NRC lexicon, weakly labeled tweets, movie reviews)—numbers, label distributions, and integration method (pretrain vs joint) and ablations are missing.  
- Weakness: parsing constraints for tweets (forcing labeled phrases to be subtrees) may introduce artifacts; need statistics on failures and sensitivity to parsing errors.  
- Weakness: lacks ablations on backward-pass design choices (effects of V, including/excluding forward vectors, varying backward dimensionality nd).  
- Weakness: adds parameters and compute (n·nd) relative to compared bidirectional model; needs discussion of runtime, memory, and latency.  
- Clarity issues: ambiguous phrasing in Section 4.4 (“widely used mix of both”) and inconsistent SemEval task/year references.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear training via backprop through structure, but comparative rigor needs controlled baselines and more ablations.  
- Originality: backward pass idea related to prior work; novelty rests on specific backward computation and flexible dimensionality via V, plus practical hybrid embedding setup.  
- Significance: meaningful for context-dependent labeling tasks if superiority over prior bidirectional models holds under controlled conditions.  
- Recommendation/consensus: promising paper, lean toward acceptance pending clearer baseline parity and additional ablations.  
- Questions for authors: were baselines trained with identical embeddings/dropout/data; provide ablations for V/nd/forward inclusion; report parsing failure rates and handling; clarify “mix of both”; report runtime/memory overhead.
