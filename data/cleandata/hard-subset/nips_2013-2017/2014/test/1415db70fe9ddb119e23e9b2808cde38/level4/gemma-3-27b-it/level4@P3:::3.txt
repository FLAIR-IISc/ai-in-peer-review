This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard recursive neural networks with a feedbackward pass during inference to improve contextual sentiment classification. The core idea is to allow phrase-level predictions to influence word representations, addressing the limitations of purely feedforward architectures in tasks where sentiment depends on context – for example, determining the sentiment towards "Android" in the sentence “Android beats iOS.” The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge and showing improvements over standard RNNs. They also investigate the benefits of dropout and a hybrid unsupervised/supervised word vector approach.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in compositional semantics and recursive neural networks. The authors correctly position their contribution within this landscape, referencing key papers on word vectors [27, 11, 15], compositional models [25, 28, 24, 10], and recursive neural networks [26].  They also acknowledge related work in bidirectional RNNs [21] and bidirectional recursive models [12, 14], providing a crucial comparison to existing approaches. The connection to unfolding recursive autoencoders [20] is also noted.  The paper effectively grounds itself in the relevant literature, allowing readers to understand its position within the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Contextual sentiment analysis is a challenging task, and the inability of standard RNNs to capture long-range dependencies limits their performance. GB-RNNs offer a principled approach to incorporating contextual information.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The authors demonstrate significant improvements over baseline models and achieve state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark (SemEval 2013). The 3% F1 improvement over the standard RNN is noteworthy.
*   **Thorough investigation of components:** The ablation studies on dropout and hybrid word vectors provide valuable insights into the factors influencing performance. The hybrid approach, combining unsupervised and supervised word representations, is a particularly interesting finding.
*   **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical progression from motivation to model description to experiments.
*   **Novelty:** While building on existing work, the integration of a feedbackward pass into a recursive neural network for sentiment analysis is a novel contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation could be strengthened:** While the authors illustrate the problem with an example, the theoretical justification for the "global belief" propagation is somewhat lacking. A more detailed explanation of *why* this feedback mechanism is expected to improve performance would be beneficial. The term "global belief" itself is slightly misleading and doesn’t clearly connect to a specific inference algorithm.
*   **Experimental details require clarification:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and model selection. Specifically, the methodology for creating and evaluating the ensemble model needs to be more transparent.  It’s unclear how the models within the ensemble were selected and whether they were independently tuned.
*   **Comparison to benchmarks:** The comparison to benchmarks in Tables 1 & 2 is hampered by a lack of detail on their tuning procedures. Were the same hybrid word vectors and training data augmentation techniques applied to all models? This information is crucial for a fair comparison.  The specific configuration corresponding to the single model performance in Table 3 would be helpful to contextualize the result within the presented benchmarks.
*   **Parser accuracy:** Using an off-the-shelf parser on Twitter data is a potential concern. The accuracy of the parser on this specific dataset should be reported, as parsing errors could significantly impact the performance of the recursive neural network.
*   **Citation completeness:** Several citations are incomplete, hindering the ability of readers to locate and review the referenced work (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in natural language processing with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical motivation for the global belief propagation mechanism is somewhat weak.
*   The lack of detail in the experimental setup makes it difficult to fully assess the validity of the results.
*   The impact of parsing errors is not addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of contextual sentiment analysis. While the theoretical motivation could be strengthened and the experimental details clarified, the strong empirical results and novel approach outweigh these weaknesses. I believe this paper is of sufficient quality for acceptance to NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding experimental clarity and providing a more robust justification for the proposed method.  Specifically, I recommend the authors provide a detailed explanation of the ensemble creation process, clarify the hyperparameter tuning strategy for all models, and report the parser accuracy on the Twitter dataset.
