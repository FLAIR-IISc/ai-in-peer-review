This paper proposes leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification. The authors term this approach “belief propagation,” however, this is a misnomer as it does not represent an instance of the standard inference algorithm and instead may confuse readers. Overall, the paper is well-written, but the motivation for the proposed model is somewhat vague and lacks a strong theoretical foundation.

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its evaluation. Presenting an ensemble result feels inappropriate given that the benchmarks are single models. This complicates the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the precise composition of the compared models is unclear. Crucially, the authors should clarify: how were the models tuned, and how were the ensemble/best performing models selected? What tuning procedures were applied to the benchmarks in Table 2? Specifically, did they utilize the same augmented data as the BP-RNN model? Were the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN also trained using hybrid word vectors?

Without this information, it’s reasonable to assume that the second entry in Table 3 offers the most direct comparison to the benchmarks presented in Tables 1 and 2 when evaluating the BP-RNN architecture itself.

Several minor points warrant attention:

* I was surprised to see the use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets. Providing information regarding its accuracy on this particular dataset would be beneficial.
* The citation for Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete, as are a number of other references throughout the paper. 

In summary, this paper introduces a recursive neural network model for contextual phrasal sentiment classification. The reported results are promising, but the model's motivation could be stronger, and the experimental methodology requires further clarification.
