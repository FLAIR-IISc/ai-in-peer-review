This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment classification by utilizing both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors of a recursive neural network auto-encoder. However, the authors' reference to this method as "belief propagation" is misleading, as it does not constitute an instance of this inference algorithm. This nomenclature may confuse readers and detract from the clarity of the paper.

In general, the paper is well-written, but the motivation behind the proposed model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification. The authors could strengthen their argument by providing a more comprehensive explanation for their approach.

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its evaluation. Presenting an ensemble result in this context is unnecessary, as none of the benchmarks are ensembles, and this obfuscates the outcome. Furthermore, the exact composition of the models being compared is unclear. To improve the evaluation, the authors should address the following questions: How were the models tuned, and how were the ensemble/best models selected? What was the tuning process for the benchmarks in Table 2? Did these benchmarks utilize the same augmented data as the BP-RNN? Did the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN also employ hybrid word vectors?

In the absence of this information, it appears that the second entry in Table 3 is the most comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when assessing the BP-RNN architecture.

There are a few minor points worth noting. The use of an off-the-shelf parser to parse tweets is surprising, and it would be interesting to know its accuracy on this data. Additionally, some citations, including the Irsoy and Cardie reference, are incomplete.

Overall, this paper presents a recursive neural network model for classifying phrasal sentiment in context. While the results appear reasonable, the model lacks a strong motivation, and the experimental methodology is not entirely clear.
