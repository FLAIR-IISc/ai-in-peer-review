The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive neural network that augments the usual bottom-up (feedforward) composition with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. In the forward pass, phrase vectors are computed along a parse tree as in standard RNNs; in the backward pass, a root-level transform and a top-down decomposition matrix propagate contextual information back to each node. Final predictions for words/phrases are made by concatenating forward and backward vectors and feeding them to a softmax classifier. The authors also propose hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings concatenated with a small, supervised, task-tuned component) and apply dropout. Experiments focus on contextual/targeted sentiment classification on SemEval 2013 Twitter data, comparing GB-RNN to a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive model, and other baselines; an ensemble of GB-RNNs achieves state-of-the-art results.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on recursive neural networks for compositional semantics (e.g., Socher et al.) and is closely related to bidirectional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), but differs in the specific top-down parameterization: backward node vectors are functions of both forward and backward parent representations, with a root transform allowing a different dimensionality for the backward stream. Compared to window-based or recurrent methods, the tree-based top-down pass can incorporate long-distance, structurally mediated context.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architectural extension that allows local predictions to be informed by sentence-level context.
- Empirical gains on SemEval 2013; the single-model improvements over a standard RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline are promising.
- Useful engineering findings: hybrid (unsupervised + supervised) embeddings with dropout outperform either alone.
- The method is broadly applicable to other context-dependent labeling tasks.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Terminology: “Global belief” and the discussion of “propagating beliefs” evoke belief propagation in graphical models, yet the method is not an instance of that algorithm. This naming risks confusion; consider “top-down contextual pass” or “bidirectional recursive RNN” to align with established terminology.
- Evaluation transparency: The ensemble result is emphasized although competing systems and internal baselines are single models. Please (i) report and highlight single-model GB-RNN performance as the primary comparison, and (ii) relegate ensembles to a secondary analysis.
- Model selection and tuning: The paper should detail how hyperparameters were tuned and how the “best” and “top-5” models in the ensemble were selected (data splits, metrics, stopping criteria). Were development sets shared across models? Were any test-set peeks used?
- Fair baselines: Clarify whether all baselines (RNN, bidirectional RNN, logistic regression, NB, context-window models) used (a) the same hybrid embeddings, (b) the same external resources (NRC lexicon, distant supervision corpora), (c) dropout, and (d) identical preprocessing. If not, please provide matched baselines or ablations that isolate the effect of the backward pass.
- Ablations needed: Report the impact of (i) removing the top-down pass, (ii) using V = I at the root, (iii) varying backward dimensionality nd, (iv) concatenation vs. alternative fusions (e.g., gating), and (v) using only supervised or only unsupervised embeddings. This will sharpen the causal story behind the gains.
- Statistical rigor: Provide confidence intervals or significance tests when comparing single models; for ensembles, report variability across seeds.
- Parsing tweets: The Stanford parser is not optimized for Twitter. Please quantify parsing quality on this domain or analyze robustness to parsing errors (e.g., using dependency vs. constituency parses, or a tweet-adapted parser).

Quality
- The model is technically sound and well-implemented. The architectural choice to condition backward messages on both forward and backward parents is reasonable, though a clearer theoretical motivation (beyond intuition and parameter counting) would strengthen the work. The experimental section is solid but would benefit from the additional controls and reporting noted above.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the terminology around “belief” and “feedbackward” is potentially confusing; standardizing terms would improve clarity. The comparison to bidirectional recursive models is helpful but could be expanded with matched ablations. Some citations are incomplete.

Originality
- Moderate. The idea of a top-down pass in recursive models is known; the contribution lies in a particular parameterization of the backward computation, the use of a root transform to decouple dimensions, and the hybrid embedding/dropout recipe.

Significance
- If the gains hold under matched conditions and for single models, the approach meaningfully advances contextual sentiment classification and could transfer to other token/phrase-level labeling tasks where context matters.

Specific questions and action items
- Provide single-model GB-RNN results as the primary comparison; keep ensemble results as supplementary.
- Specify hyperparameter search spaces, tuning protocol, and selection criteria for all models in Tables 1–3.
- State explicitly whether baselines used the same hybrid embeddings and external data; if not, add matched comparisons.
- Add ablations isolating the effect of the backward pass, V, nd, and fusion choice.
- Report significance testing and variance over random seeds.
- Include an assessment of parsing accuracy on tweets or a robustness analysis.
- Complete and correct citations (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) and standardize terminology.

Minor points
- Using an off-the-shelf news-trained parser on tweets is surprising; please justify or replace with a Twitter-adapted parser, and describe tokenization (e.g., handling of hashtags, emoticons).
- Clean up incomplete references and fix small typographical issues in equations and figure references.

Overall assessment
This work presents a practical and potentially impactful extension of recursive neural networks for contextual sentiment. The empirical results are encouraging, but the paper’s motivation and, especially, the experimental methodology need tightening. With clearer terminology, matched baselines, and fuller ablations, the contribution would be significantly stronger.
