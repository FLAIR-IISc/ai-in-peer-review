This paper addresses a key limitation of standard feedforward Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs), which struggle to predict phrase or word labels that are determined by the broader sentence context. The authors use aspect-specific sentiment analysis as a motivating example, where the sentiment of a word like "Android" in "Android beats iOS" depends on information outside its local phrase.

To solve this, the paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs). The core idea is to augment the standard bottom-up, feedforward pass of an RNN with a second, top-down "feedbackward" pass. This feedbackward pass propagates information from the sentence's root representation back down to the leaf nodes. For any given phrase or word, the final representation is a concatenation of its vector from the feedforward pass and the new vector from the feedbackward pass. This allows global context to inform local predictions. Additionally, the paper explores the use of dropout for training RNNs and proposes a hybrid word vector representation that combines pre-trained unsupervised vectors with task-specific supervised vectors.

The model is evaluated on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, where it reportedly achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming the competition winner and several baselines, including a standard RNN and a bidirectional RNN.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and well-motivated problem: incorporating global context into compositional models. The proposed two-pass (bottom-up, then top-down) architecture is an intuitive and novel way to allow sentence-level information to refine local representations. This is a clear improvement over standard feedforward models and offers a different approach than window-based methods or standard bidirectional recurrent networks.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on a competitive shared task (SemEval 2013). The significant F1 score improvement of the GB-RNN over the standard feedforward RNN (+3%) demonstrates the practical value of the proposed architecture. This provides strong evidence for the paper's central claim.

3.  **Useful Secondary Contributions:** The paper shows that a hybrid word vector representation (concatenating fixed unsupervised vectors and trainable supervised vectors) and the application of dropout both contribute to improved performance. These are valuable practical insights for others working with recursive models.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Clarity of Experimental Comparison:** The main weakness of the paper lies in the clarity and fairness of the experimental evaluation. To strengthen the paper's claims, it would be beneficial to clarify the following:
    *   **Ensemble Results:** Table 1 reports an ensemble result for the GB-RNN against what appear to be single-model systems from the competition. This makes a direct comparison of the architectural novelty difficult. It would be much clearer if the performance of the single best GB-RNN model was also reported in Table 1, as this would provide a more direct "apples-to-apples" comparison.
    *   **Baseline Tuning and Data:** While the paper states that the baselines in Table 2 (RNN, Bidirectional-RNN) used the same hybrid vectors and dropout, it is unclear if they benefited from the same hyperparameter tuning process and, crucially, the same additional training data (NRC lexicon, noisily labeled tweets, movie reviews) described in Section 4.4. For the comparison to be fair, all models should be trained and tuned under identical conditions. Please clarify if this was the case. Without this information, it is difficult to isolate the performance gain from the GB-RNN architecture itself versus gains from data augmentation or more thorough tuning.

2.  **Model Motivation and Naming:**
    *   The theoretical motivation for the specific formulation of the feedbackward update rule (Eq. 3) could be stronger, especially in comparison to the bidirectional RNN (Eq. 4). The paper intuits that using both forward and backward parent nodes helps with disentanglement, but this could be elaborated upon.
    *   The name "Global Belief Recursive Neural Network" is potentially confusing. The term "belief propagation" typically refers to a specific message-passing inference algorithm on graphical models. As this model does not seem to be a direct instance of that algorithm, the name may create misleading expectations. A name more descriptive of the top-down refinement process might improve clarity.

**Minor Points:**

*   The use of the off-the-shelf Stanford Parser on Twitter data is noted. Given that tweet syntax is often non-standard, a brief discussion of the parser's potential impact on performance (e.g., parsing errors) would be a welcome addition.
*   Several citations in the reference list are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie, 2013). These should be corrected for the final version.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel and effective architecture for incorporating contextual information into recursive neural networks. The model is well-motivated, and the strong empirical results on a challenging sentiment analysis task are a significant strength. However, the paper would be substantially improved by clarifying the experimental methodology to ensure the comparisons are fair and transparent. Addressing the questions about the ensemble reporting and baseline training conditions is critical for validating the central claims about the proposed architecture.
