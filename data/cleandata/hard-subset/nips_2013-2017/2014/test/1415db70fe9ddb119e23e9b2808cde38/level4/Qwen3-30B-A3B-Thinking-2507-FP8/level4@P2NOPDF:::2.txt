

This paper introduces a bidirectional extension to recursive neural networks (RNNs) for text representation learning. Standard RNN-based approaches process text by recursively combining word or snippet representations up a pre-specified parse tree, yielding a final representation at the root. However, this unidirectional process cannot propagate contextual information downward from larger to smaller units. The proposed method addresses this by adding a downward pass following the upward tree traversal, enriching all node representations with information from broader contexts. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on SemEval 2012 Task 2, demonstrating clear empirical effectiveness.

The core idea—extending RNNs to operate bidirectionally—is simple, elegant, and well-motivated. While conceptually related to prior work [18], the paper’s downward pass employs distinct computations, leading to superior performance. The method is clearly explained, and the paper is generally well-written, with a strong theoretical foundation. The experimental results are compelling, though I request clarification on two aspects of the evaluation setup. First, for the alternative bidirectional RNN model reported in Table 2 (from [18]), were dropout and the additional training data used? Were hybrid vectors also applied in that baseline? Second, while the ensemble result is impressive, it should not be included in Table 2, which compares individual models. The ensemble represents a distinct experimental configuration and would better suit a separate discussion or table.

Additionally, Section 4.4 refers to "a widely used mix of both" without sufficient context. Clarifying this phrase would strengthen the paper’s presentation. Overall, this is a well-executed paper that successfully leverages a straightforward idea to advance the state of the art. The technical contribution is significant, and the implementation is sound. The minor concerns about experimental details do not undermine the paper’s core contribution but warrant clarification for reproducibility and fair comparison. The work is a valuable addition to the literature on structured neural models for NLP.
