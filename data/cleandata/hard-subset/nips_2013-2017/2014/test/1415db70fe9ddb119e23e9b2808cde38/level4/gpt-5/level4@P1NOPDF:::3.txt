This paper proposes leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase representations from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification. The authors refer to this as “belief propagation,” but it is not an instance of the belief propagation inference algorithm, and the terminology is therefore misleading. Overall the paper is clearly written, but the motivation for the proposed model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification.

The primary weakness is the evaluation. Presenting ensemble results in this setting is unwarranted; none of the baselines are ensembles, and doing so obscures the contribution of the proposed approach. Moreover, the composition of the compared models is insufficiently specified. Several key questions need clarification:
- How were the individual models tuned, and how were the ensemble and “best” models selected?
- How were the benchmarks in Table 2 tuned?
- Did the baselines in Table 2 use the same data augmentation as BP-RNN?
- Did the RNN and Bidirectional RNN baselines also use hybrid word vectors?

Without this information, my best estimate is that the second entry in Table 3 is the fairest point of comparison to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when assessing the BP-RNN architecture itself.

Minor points:
- I am surprised an off-the-shelf parser was used to parse tweets; please report its accuracy on this domain.
- The Irsoy and Cardie citation is incomplete, as are several others.

In summary, the paper presents a recursive neural network model for classifying phrasal sentiment in context. The results seem reasonable, but the model lacks a strong motivation, and the experimental methodology is not sufficiently clear to support firm conclusions.
