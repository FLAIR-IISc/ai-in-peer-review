This paper introduces a method for sentiment classification that leverages both the encoding and decoding vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The authors term this approach “belief propagation,” a label that may mislead readers as it doesn’t align with the standard inference algorithm. While generally well-written, the paper’s motivation feels underdeveloped and lacks robust theoretical grounding.

A significant concern lies with the evaluation methodology. The inclusion of ensemble results is questionable given that the benchmark comparisons are single models, potentially obscuring the true performance of the proposed architecture. Furthermore, details regarding model tuning and selection – specifically, how models were optimized and the ensemble/best models chosen – are missing. Clarification is needed on the tuning procedures applied to the benchmarks presented in Table 2, including whether they utilized the same data augmentation as the BP-RNN and if hybrid word vectors were employed in the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models. 

Without this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears to be the most directly comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 & 2 when evaluating the BP-RNN architecture itself.

Minor issues include: the use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets raises questions about its accuracy on this specific dataset, and the citation for Irsoy and Cardie (and several others) is incomplete.

In summary, this work presents a recursive neural network model for contextual phrasal sentiment classification with promising results. However, the model’s rationale could be strengthened, and greater transparency in the experimental setup is needed.
