This paper introduces “belief propagation recursive neural networks,” which extend standard feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks with a backward (top-down) inference step. This enables phrase-level predictions and embeddings to propagate feedback to word-level embeddings and labels.

Overall, the paper is well written, and the model is clearly motivated and described. It appears to extend the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (note: the reference to that paper is incomplete). One notable contribution is the introduction of hybrid word vectors; this may be the main difference from prior work. If so, this should be emphasized and discussed more explicitly.

However, the experimental comparisons do not seem entirely fair or fully specified. In particular:
- Do the RNN and B-RNN baselines also use hybrid word vectors?
- How are the “best” models tuned and selected?
- How is the “single best” model chosen?
- It is important to note that the other methods do not use an ensemble; please clarify the role of ensembling in your results and ensure a like-for-like comparison.

The term “belief propagation” is potentially misleading in this context; “forward–backward” might be a more appropriate and less overloaded description of the inference procedure.

Section 3.5 (Training) is too brief and should provide more detail. I assume training uses backpropagation through structure (the Goller, 1996 paper could be cited), which implies unrolling the recursive model over the parse tree. In that case, the network becomes very deep when propagating gradients back to the word-embedding layer. If my understanding is correct, please elaborate on this crucial step: how the unrolling is performed, how gradients reach the word embeddings, and any techniques used to manage depth and stability.

Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing; the differences between the proposed model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie should be clarified more concretely (e.g., differences in the message-passing schedule, parameterization, or training objective).

Section 4.1 should be improved for readers outside the NLP community. SemEval 2013 Task 2 includes two subtasks: contextual polarity and message polarity. I infer that you address the contextual polarity subtask. A clearer presentation of the task setup would also make the parser constraints easier to understand.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 might be merged to streamline the presentation and avoid redundancy.

In summary, this is a well-presented paper that proposes an interesting variant of the bidirectional recursive model. Clarifying the terminology, training details, differences from prior work, and the fairness of the experimental setup—especially regarding hybrid vectors and ensembling—would significantly strengthen the contribution.
