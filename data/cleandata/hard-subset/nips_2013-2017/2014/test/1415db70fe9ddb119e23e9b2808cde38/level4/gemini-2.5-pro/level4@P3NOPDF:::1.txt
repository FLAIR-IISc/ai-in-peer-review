This paper introduces "Belief Propagation Recursive Neural Networks" (BPRNNs), an extension of standard feedforward (bottom-up) recursive neural networks. The key innovation is the addition of a backward (top-down) pass during inference, which allows higher-level phrase representations and predictions to refine the embeddings and labels at the word level.

The paper is generally well-written, and the proposed model is well-motivated and clearly described. The concept of using a top-down pass to allow feedback from phrase-level semantics to word-level representations is an interesting and intuitive extension to the recursive neural network framework.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed to strengthen its contributions.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Novelty and Comparison to Prior Work:** The primary point requiring clarification is the model's novelty with respect to the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks (B-RNN) introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2014, not 2008). The current discussion in Section 3.6 is confusing and does not sufficiently differentiate the two models. The authors mention the introduction of hybrid word vectors as one contribution; is this the main difference from previous work? A more precise and detailed comparison is needed to clearly delineate the specific contributions of this paper over the existing B-RNN framework.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and raises concerns about fairness.
    *   **Hybrid Vectors:** Did the baseline models (RNN and B-RNN) also use the proposed hybrid word vectors? If not, the performance gains may be confounded by this architectural choice rather than the top-down inference step itself. This must be clarified.
    *   **Model Selection:** The process for hyperparameter tuning and model selection is unclear. How were the "best" models selected for both the proposed method and the baselines? Furthermore, how was the single best model chosen for reporting final results?
    *   **Use of Ensembles:** The paper appears to report results for an ensemble of BPRNN models while comparing against single models for the baselines. This is an unfair comparison. The results should either be reported for a single BPRNN model or compared against ensembled versions of the baselines.

3.  **Lack of Detail in Training Procedure:** Section 3.5, which describes training, is overly brief and omits crucial details. It is likely that training is performed using an algorithm like back-propagation through structure (a citation to Goller & Küchler, 1996 would be appropriate), which involves unfolding the recursive structure. Given the model's top-down and bottom-up passes, this unfolding would result in a very deep network to backpropagate gradients to the word embeddings. This is a non-trivial and vital part of the model, and the authors should provide a more thorough explanation of the gradient computation and update steps.

**Minor Concerns and Suggestions:**

1.  **Model Terminology:** The term "belief propagation" may be misleading, as the mechanism described does not seem to directly correspond to the standard graphical model algorithm of the same name. A term like "forward-backward" or "bidirectional inference" might be more suitable and less confusing.

2.  **Clarity of Task Description:** Section 4.1, which describes the experimental task, would benefit from more detail to be accessible to readers outside the NLP community. For instance, Task 2 of Semeval 2013 contains two subtasks: contextual polarity and message polarity. The authors should explicitly state which subtask they are addressing (presumably contextual polarity) and briefly explain the task setup to help readers understand the motivation for parser constraints.

3.  **Structure and References:**
    *   For improved readability and flow, consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4.
    *   The reference to Irsoy and Cardie appears to be incomplete. Please check and correct it.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper presents an interesting variant of the bidirectional recursive model. However, its contributions are not clearly distinguished from prior work, and the experimental evaluation is not rigorous enough in its current form. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the issues of fairness in the experimental comparison, providing more detail on the training procedure, and clarifying the model's precise novelty.
