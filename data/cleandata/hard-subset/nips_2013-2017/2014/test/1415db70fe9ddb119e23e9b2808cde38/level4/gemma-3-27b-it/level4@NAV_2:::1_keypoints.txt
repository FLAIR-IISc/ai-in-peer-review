- Introduces GB-RNNs with a backward inference step so phrase-level predictions/embeddings inform word embeddings and labels, addressing context-dependence in tasks like aspect-specific sentiment classification.

- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and builds on prior recursive neural network work.

- Extends the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Network idea of Irsoy & Cardie, though that reference is incomplete.

- Key contribution: hybrid word vector representations that combine unsupervised and supervised learning.

- Unclear whether RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines also used hybrid word vectors, complicating comparisons.

- Unclear model selection/tuning: how the single best model was chosen and whether the same ensemble strategy was applied to all models; GB-RNN’s use of an ensemble may create imbalance.

- The term "global belief" may be misleading; "forward-backward" could be more descriptive.

- Section 3.5 (Training) is too concise; should mention backpropagation through structure and challenges of unfolding recursive models for gradient updates.

- More practical implementation and training details would strengthen the paper.

- Section 3.6 should more precisely delineate differences from Irsoy & Cardie, ideally with direct equation-level comparisons.

- Section 4.1 needs clarification for non-NLP readers: specify SemEval 2013 Task 2 subtasks and state which subtask is addressed (likely contextual polarity) and explain parser constraints.

- Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could be consolidated to improve flow.

- Overall assessment: an interesting and well-written bidirectional recursive variant with promising, state-of-the-art results on contextual sentiment analysis.

- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important NLP problem, proposes a novel inference-feedback model, shows strong experimental results, and introduces useful hybrid word vectors.

- Arguments against acceptance: experimental comparisons and tuning procedures need clarification, naming is potentially misleading, training details are sparse, and theoretical comparison to Irsoy & Cardie is insufficient.
