This paper introduces global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs), extending feedforward recursive networks with a backward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to inform word embeddings and labels, addressing the limitations of purely feedforward architectures in context-dependent tasks like aspect-specific sentiment classification.

The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The model is described in detail and builds upon prior work in recursive neural networks. This work extends the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks, initially introduced by Irsoy and Cardie [12] (note the reference is incomplete). A key contribution is the introduction of hybrid word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised learning. However, experimental comparisons warrant further scrutiny: it is unclear whether the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines also utilized hybrid word vectors. Details regarding the tuning and selection of "best" models – specifically how the single best model was chosen and whether the same ensemble strategy was applied to all models – should be clarified. The use of an ensemble for the GB-RNN but not other methods introduces a potential imbalance in the comparison. The term "global belief" may be misleading; "forward-backward" might be more descriptive.

Section 3.5 (Training) is concise and could benefit from elaboration. The training process likely employs backpropagation through structure [9], and mentioning this, along with the associated challenges of unfolding the recursive model into a deep network for gradient updates, would be valuable. Providing more detail on these practical implementation aspects would strengthen the paper.

Section 3.6 could be more precise in delineating the differences between the proposed model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie. A more direct comparison of the equations and underlying principles would be helpful.

Section 4.1 requires improvement to enhance accessibility for readers outside the NLP community. Clarifying that the SemEval 2013 Task 2 encompasses contextual and message polarity subtasks, and specifying which subtask is addressed (likely contextual polarity), would improve comprehension.  A better description of the task would also contextualize the parser constraints.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could be consolidated for improved flow.

Overall, this paper presents a well-written and interesting variant of the bidirectional recursive model, with promising results on contextual sentiment analysis. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in NLP – capturing contextual information for accurate sentiment analysis.
*   The proposed GB-RNN model offers a novel approach to incorporating feedback during inference.
*   The experimental results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge and outperform baseline methods.
*   The introduction of hybrid word vectors is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental comparison requires further clarification regarding the baseline model configurations and tuning procedures.
*   The term "global belief" is potentially misleading.
*   The training details could be more thoroughly explained.
*   The theoretical comparison to Irsoy and Cardie’s work could be more rigorous.
