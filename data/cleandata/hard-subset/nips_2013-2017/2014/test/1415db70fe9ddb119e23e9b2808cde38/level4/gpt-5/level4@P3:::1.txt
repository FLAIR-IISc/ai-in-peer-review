Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a recursive neural architecture that augments standard bottom-up (feedforward) composition with a single top-down “feedbackward” pass at inference time. The top-down pass propagates information from the root to internal nodes and leaves, enabling word- and phrase-level predictions to be refined by global sentence context. The model concatenates forward and backward node representations before classification, and introduces hybrid word vectors that combine fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a smaller supervised component trained on the task. The method is evaluated on contextual (aspect-specific) sentiment analysis in the SemEval-2013 Task 2 Twitter dataset, showing improvements over a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and previously reported systems; an ensemble of GB-RNNs achieves state-of-the-art performance.

This work builds on recursive neural networks for compositional semantics (e.g., Socher et al.), bidirectional recurrent/recursive architectures (Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie, NIPS DL Workshop 2014), and sentiment-specific embedding work. Relative to bidirectional recursive models, GB-RNN differs in how the top-down vectors are computed (using both forward and backward parent representations) and by coupling with hybrid word vectors and dropout.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: context-dependent labeling (e.g., entity- or aspect-level sentiment) cannot be resolved by purely bottom-up composition or fixed windows.
- Simple, principled extension: a single top-down pass over a parse tree is easy to integrate with existing RNNs.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over a strong feedforward RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline; competitive with SemEval-2013 winners.
- Useful engineering contributions: hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised + small supervised part) and effective use of dropout for RNN training.
- Ablations and analysis: comparisons of single vs ensemble, vector sizing, and additional data.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Terminology: “belief propagation” is potentially misleading in the absence of a graphical-model-style message passing algorithm. Consider “forward–backward recursive neural network” or “top-down recursive refinement.”
- Experimental fairness and clarity:
  - Please make explicit, early in the experimental section and in table captions, that RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines use the same hybrid word vectors, dropout, and tuning protocol as GB-RNN (4.3 mentions this, but it should be emphasized).
  - Clarify “best model” selection: exact cross-validation protocol, number of random restarts, stopping criteria, and whether the held-out dev set guided all hyperparameters (vector sizes, dropout rate, activation function, batch size).
  - Report single-model results alongside ensemble results for all systems where possible, and discuss comparability to SemEval-2013 entries that did not use ensembles.
  - Indicate whether additional external resources (NRC lexicon, distant supervision tweets, movie reviews) were also used for the baselines and bidirectional recursive model; if not, provide a rationale or run matched settings.
- Training details are too brief:
  - Explicitly state that backpropagation through structure (Goller & Küchler, 1996) is used, and describe how the top-down pass is differentiated. Provide pseudocode or a clear computational graph showing forward pass, top-down pass, and gradient flow to embeddings.
  - Discuss depth-related issues (vanishing/exploding gradients), whether gradient clipping or normalization is used, and training/inference complexity and runtime relative to the baseline RNN and the bidirectional recursive model.
  - Clarify the role and dimension of V and W↓, and how nd is selected.
- Section 3.6 comparison to Irsoy & Cardie:
  - The current text is hard to follow and includes stray, unrelated lines (e.g., “Correct FUSION’s 5th General Meeting…”), which appear to be an editing artifact. Please remove and tighten the mathematical comparison. A concise table contrasting parameterization and information flow (which parent vectors are used to compute child ↓-vectors) would help.
  - Ensure the Irsoy & Cardie reference is complete and correctly dated.
- Dataset/task description (4.1):
  - Briefly explain the two SemEval-2013 subtasks and state clearly that you focus on contextual (target-dependent) polarity. Define the evaluation metric (macro-averaged F1 over positive/negative), the split sizes, and differences between tweet and SMS data.
  - Describe the “parser constraints” and how labeled spans are enforced to be subtrees, including any failures or heuristics for spans that are not contiguous or not parseable.
- Analysis and ablations:
  - Provide an ablation isolating the contribution of the top-down pass from that of hybrid embeddings and extra data (e.g., RNN vs GB-RNN with identical word vectors and no external data).
  - Include sensitivity to parsing errors and a brief error analysis to illuminate failure cases (e.g., long-distance dependencies, sarcasm).
  - Report statistical significance of the main improvements.

Quality
- The model is technically sound and well-motivated, but additional training details, clearer comparisons, and removal of editorial artifacts are needed for reproducibility and to fully support the claims.
- The empirical evaluation is appropriate and competitive; further matched ablations and significance tests would strengthen it.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and accessible to NLP readers. Improve Section 3.6, expand Section 3.5, and clarify the task/dataset in 4.1 for broader NIPS readership. Fix incomplete references and remove stray text.

Originality
- The top-down recursive refinement is closely related to prior bidirectional recursive work, but differs in how backward vectors are computed and in coupling with hybrid embeddings and dropout. The combination is novel enough to be of interest.

Significance
- Context-sensitive labeling is important for many applications (aspect sentiment, coreference-sensitive sentiment, WSD). The demonstrated gains and the simplicity of the approach suggest it will be useful and built upon.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of standard recursive models with a simple, effective mechanism.
- Strong empirical results on a public benchmark, with meaningful ablations.
- Practical insights (hybrid embeddings, dropout) likely to transfer to other tasks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete and occasionally confusing exposition (training details, Section 3.6; stray text; incomplete citations).
- Questions about experimental fairness and the contribution of external resources vs the core model change.
- Terminology may mislead; lack of runtime/complexity discussion.

Recommendation
Leaning toward accept, contingent on clarifying the training and comparison details, tightening Section 3.6, and improving the task description and references. The idea is useful, the results are solid, and the required revisions are primarily expository.
