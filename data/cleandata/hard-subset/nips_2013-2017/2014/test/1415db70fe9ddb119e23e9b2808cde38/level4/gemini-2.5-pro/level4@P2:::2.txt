This paper addresses a key limitation of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) in natural language processing: their purely feedforward architecture prevents information from the broader sentence context from influencing the representations and labels of constituent words and phrases. The authors introduce the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), which extends the standard bottom-up, feedforward pass up a parse tree with a subsequent top-down, "feedbackward" pass. This second pass allows the global sentence representation at the root to propagate down the tree, refining the representations of all nodes with contextual information. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, showing that the GB-RNN achieves state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 Task 2 dataset. Secondary contributions include demonstrating the benefits of a hybrid word vector representation (combining unsupervised and supervised vectors) and the successful application of dropout during RNN training.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

*   **Quality & Significance:** The paper presents a technically sound and effective solution to a well-known problem. The performance improvement is substantial; the feedbackward step yields a 3% absolute F1 improvement over the standard RNN baseline, and the final model surpasses the winning system of the SemEval 2013 competition. This is a significant result that demonstrably advances the state of the art in contextual sentiment analysis. The ideas presented are likely to be useful for researchers working on related structured prediction tasks in NLP.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and well-organized. The proposed GB-RNN architecture is explained with precision, and the comparison to prior work, particularly the bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie [12], is detailed and insightful.
*   **Originality:** While the high-level idea of a bidirectional pass in a recursive network has been explored before (e.g., [12]), the specific formulation of the feedbackward computation in the GB-RNN is novel. The authors' method, which uses both the forward and backward parent vectors to compute child representations in the downward pass, is different from prior work and appears to be empirically superior. The proposed hybrid word vectors are also a useful contribution.

**Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors**

The experimental evaluation is strong overall, but a few points could be clarified to further strengthen the paper's claims.

*   **Fairness of Comparison:** The comparison with the bidirectional RNN model from Irsoy and Cardie [12] in Table 2 is critical for establishing the novelty and superiority of the proposed feedbackward mechanism. Could the authors please clarify if this baseline model was implemented with the same enhancements as the GB-RNN? Specifically, was the bidirectional RNN also trained using the proposed hybrid word vectors and the dropout technique? Since these components are shown to improve performance, ensuring they are applied to all comparable models is essential for a fair comparison of the core architectural contributions.
*   **Reporting of Ensemble Results:** In Table 1, the GB-RNN is presented as an ensemble of 5 models, which is then compared against single-model systems from the competition. While the ensemble result is impressive, it is unconventional to directly compare an ensemble against individual models. For a more direct and transparent comparison, it would be valuable to also include the performance of the single best GB-RNN model in Table 1.

**Summary**

This is a well-executed paper that introduces a simple, elegant, and effective extension to Recursive Neural Networks. The proposed Global Belief RNN provides a principled way to incorporate global context into local representations, leading to state-of-the-art results on a challenging sentiment analysis task. The work is clearly presented and technically solid. Addressing the minor questions regarding the experimental comparisons would further solidify its excellent contribution.
