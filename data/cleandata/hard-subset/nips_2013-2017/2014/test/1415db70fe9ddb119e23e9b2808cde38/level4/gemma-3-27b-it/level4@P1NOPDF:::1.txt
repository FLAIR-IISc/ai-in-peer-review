This paper introduces “belief propagation recursive neural networks.” These networks extend standard “feedforward” (bottom-up) recursive networks with a backward (top-down) pass during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels.

Overall, this paper is well written, and the model is well motivated and described. It builds upon the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008) – though the reference to that work is incomplete. A key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors. This may be the most significant distinction from previous work. However, the experimental comparisons aren't entirely fair. Were the RNN and B-RNN models also trained with hybrid vectors? Furthermore, the paper should clarify how the “best” models were tuned and selected, and how the single best model was identified. It's worth noting that the other methods tested did not employ an ensemble approach. These points require further clarification.

The term “belief propagation” is somewhat misleading; “forward-backward” might be a more suitable descriptor. Section 3.5 (Training) is rather brief and could benefit from additional details. I suspect the training process utilizes back-propagation through structure (citing Goller, 1996, would be relevant). This algorithm requires unfolding the recursive model, which, in this case, results in a very deep network to update the word embedding layer. If my understanding is correct, the authors should provide more detail on this important step.

Section 3.6 feels somewhat confusing, and the differences between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie need to be more clearly delineated. 

Section 4.1 could be improved to make it more accessible to readers outside of the NLP community. In SemEval 2013 Task 2, there are two subtasks: contextual and message polarity. I believe this work focuses on the contextual polarity subtask. A clearer presentation of the task would also allow readers to better understand the parser constraints.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could potentially be merged for conciseness.

In summary, this is a well-written paper that describes an interesting and worthwhile variant of the Bidirectional recursive model.
