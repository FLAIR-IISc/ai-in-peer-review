The authors present "belief propagation recursive neural networks," an architecture that enhances standard feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks by incorporating a backward (top-down) pass at inference time. This mechanism enables higher-level phrase representations to refine the embeddings and labels at the word level.

The paper is generally well-written, with a clear motivation and a thorough description of the proposed model. The work builds upon the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks from Irsoy and Cardie (2008), though the citation provided is incomplete. A notable contribution appears to be the use of hybrid word vectors; it would be helpful if the authors clarified whether this is the primary distinction from prior work. The experimental evaluation, however, raises some concerns about fairness. It is unclear if the baseline models (RNN and B-RNN) were also equipped with hybrid vectors. Furthermore, the paper should detail the hyperparameter tuning and model selection process for all compared methods, including how the "single best" model was chosen. The use of an ensemble for the proposed model while comparing against single models for the baselines is another point that weakens the comparison. These aspects of the experimental setup require clarification.

The choice of the term "belief propagation" may be confusing, as "forward-backward" might more accurately describe the architectural flow. Section 3.5 on training is overly condensed and would benefit from additional detail. Presumably, the model is trained using the back-propagation through structure algorithm (citing Goller, 1996 would be appropriate), which involves unfolding the recursive structure. This process would result in a particularly deep network for updating the word-level embeddings. Given the importance of this step, the authors should elaborate on the training procedure.

The discussion in Section 3.6 lacks clarity, and the distinction between the proposed model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie needs to be more explicitly articulated. Similarly, Section 4.1, which describes the experimental task, should be revised to be more accessible to a broader audience unfamiliar with the specifics of NLP benchmarks. For instance, clarification is needed on which of the two Semeval 2013 Task 2 subtasks (contextual or message polarity) was addressed. A more detailed task description would also help readers better appreciate the rationale behind the parser constraints. On a structural note, Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could likely be merged for improved conciseness.

In summary, this is a well-presented paper that introduces an interesting variant of the Bidirectional recursive neural network model.
