- Introduces GB-RNNs: adds a top-down feedbackward pass to recursive networks so root/contextual information flows to leaf nodes for context-dependent sentiment.  
- Addresses feedforward RNN limitation where phrase/word labels lack global sentence context (e.g., "Android" positive in "Android beats iOS").  
- Differs from bidirectional/window methods by using true top-down computation and being parameter-efficient.  
- Builds on compositional semantics literature (Socher et al., Grefenstette et al.) and previous bidirectional recursive nets but with a novel top-down inference.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art on SemEval 2013 Task 2, outperforming the previous winner by ~3% F1.  
- Finds hybrid unsupervised-supervised word vectors combined with dropout yield optimal performance.  
- Paper is technically sound and clearly explained with equations, motivating examples, and architecture description.  
- Strong experimental design: comparisons to strong baselines (standard RNN, bidirectional RNN, logistic regression) and ablation studies isolating dropout, hybrid vectors, and feedbackward pass.  
- Additional analysis shows incremental gains from extra training data (e.g., NRC lexicon).  
- Weakness: Comparison with Irsoy & Cardie’s bidirectional RNN lacks critical details (whether same hybrid vectors, dropout, or extra data were used), raising fairness concerns.  
- Weakness: Ensemble (5-model) result reported alongside single-model results; ensembles should be reported separately to avoid misrepresenting single-model gains.  
- Weakness: Vague wording ("widely used mix of both") about word vector initialization—needs explicit definition of the hybrid approach.  
- Recommendation: Accept — novel, significant, well-evaluated contribution; weaknesses are minor and fixable.  
- Suggested fixes: clarify baseline experimental conditions in Section 3.6/Table 2, separate ensemble results, and precisely describe the hybrid vector method in Section 4.4.
