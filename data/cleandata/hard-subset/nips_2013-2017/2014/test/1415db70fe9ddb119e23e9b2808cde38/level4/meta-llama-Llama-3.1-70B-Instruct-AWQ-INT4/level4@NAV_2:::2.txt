This paper introduces a novel extension of recursive neural networks (RNNs), called global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs), which incorporates a feedbackward step during inference to allow phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The main idea of GB-RNNs is to extend purely feedforward neural networks to include one feedbackward step during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to words. This approach is motivated by the need to capture contextual information in natural language processing tasks, such as aspect-specific sentiment classification. The authors show that the feedbackward step improves F1 performance by 3% over the standard RNN on this task.

The paper is well-written, and the method is clearly presented. The experimental results are excellent, with the GB-RNN outperforming several baseline models and achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge. The authors also propose a hybrid approach to word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised word vector representations, which performs better than either approach alone.

The strengths of the paper include its simplicity, elegance, and effectiveness. The extension of the established RNN-based approach is well-motivated and clearly presented. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors provide a thorough comparison with other models.

One potential weakness of the paper is the experimental setup. It is not clear whether the result for the alternative bidirectional RNN model from [18] reported in Table 2 was obtained using dropout and the additional training data. Additionally, the ensemble result, while impressive, does not really belong in a table comparing the performance of individual models.

In terms of originality, the paper builds upon existing work on RNNs and bidirectional RNNs. However, the computation performed on the downward pass in the paper is different from previous approaches, and the resulting performance appears to be superior.

The significance of the paper lies in its contribution to the field of natural language processing, particularly in the area of contextual sentiment analysis. The GB-RNN model has the potential to be applied to a wide range of NLP tasks, and its ability to capture contextual information makes it a valuable addition to the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the method is clearly presented. The experimental results are excellent, and the authors provide a thorough comparison with other models. The GB-RNN model has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of NLP.
