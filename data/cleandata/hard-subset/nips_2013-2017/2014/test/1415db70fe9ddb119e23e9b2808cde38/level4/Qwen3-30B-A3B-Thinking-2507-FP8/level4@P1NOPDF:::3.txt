

This paper proposes leveraging both the encoder and decoder phrase-level vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification, which the authors term "belief propagation." This terminology is misleading, as the approach does not implement the belief propagation inference algorithm; the misnomer risks confusing readers. While the paper is well-written, its motivation remains vague and lacks clear theoretical grounding.

The most significant weakness lies in the evaluation methodology. Presenting an ensemble result is unjustified in this context, as none of the benchmark datasets or comparisons use ensembles, thereby obscuring the true performance of the proposed BP-RNN model. Critical details about model composition and tuning are missing: How were hyperparameters selected for the BP-RNN and baseline models? How was the ensemble constructed, and how were the best models chosen? Additionally, it is unclear whether the benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned using the same augmented data as the BP-RNN, or whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines also employed hybrid word vectors. Without this information, the second entry in Table 3—reporting the BP-RNN’s standalone performance—appears to be the most directly comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 for evaluating the architecture itself.

Minor concerns include:  
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweet sentiment analysis raises questions about its accuracy on noisy social media text; the authors should report parsing performance on their dataset.  
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie (2014) is incomplete, as are several other references.  

Overall, the model architecture is plausible, and results appear reasonable. However, the lack of a compelling theoretical motivation and insufficient experimental transparency undermine the contribution. The work would benefit substantially from clarifying the evaluation protocol and justifying the proposed terminology.
