This paper proposes a novel extension to recursive neural networks (RNNs), called global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs), to better model contextual information in natural language processing tasks. The GB-RNN architecture includes a feedbackward step during inference, allowing phrase-level predictions and representations to be updated based on the global context of the sentence. This is particularly useful for tasks such as aspect-specific sentiment classification, where the sentiment of a word or phrase can be determined by its context.

The authors provide a clear and well-motivated presentation of their approach, including a detailed description of the GB-RNN architecture and its components. They also provide a thorough analysis of related work, including a comparison to other bidirectional RNN models. The experimental results are impressive, with the GB-RNN achieving state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 Task 2 competition.

The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of their approach. The use of figures and tables to illustrate the GB-RNN architecture and experimental results is also helpful.

One potential concern is that the experimental setup could be more clearly described. For example, it is not clear whether the alternative bidirectional RNN model from [12] was evaluated using the same experimental setup as the GB-RNN, including the use of dropout and additional training data. Additionally, the use of an ensemble of GB-RNN models to achieve the best performance is not clearly motivated, and it is not clear whether this is a necessary component of the approach.

Overall, however, the paper presents a simple and elegant extension to RNNs that achieves impressive results on a challenging NLP task. The approach is well-motivated and clearly presented, and the experimental results are thorough and well-executed.
