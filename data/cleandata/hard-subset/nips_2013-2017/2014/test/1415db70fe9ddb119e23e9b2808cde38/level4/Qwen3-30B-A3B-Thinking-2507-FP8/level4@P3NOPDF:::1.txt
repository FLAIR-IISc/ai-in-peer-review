

This paper proposes "belief propagation recursive neural networks" (BPRNNs), extending feedforward recursive neural networks with a backward (top-down) inference step to enable phrase-level predictions and embeddings to refine word-level representations. The core contribution lies in introducing hybrid word vectors, which represent a significant departure from prior bidirectional recursive models (Irsoy & Cardie, 2008), though the reference to this foundational work is incomplete. The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, making a compelling case for the bidirectional update mechanism.

However, several critical issues require clarification before acceptance. First, the experimental evaluation lacks fairness. The authors compare against RNN and B-RNN baselines but do not specify whether these models also employ hybrid word vectors. Crucially, the hyperparameter tuning and model selection procedures for all methods—particularly how the "best" model is chosen from multiple trials or ensembles—remain opaque. Since the comparison methods do not use ensembles (unlike the proposed BPRNN), this discrepancy undermines the validity of the reported performance gains. The authors must explicitly detail the experimental setup, including whether baselines were tuned identically and how the final model was selected.

The term "belief propagation" is fundamentally misleading, as the algorithm does not implement probabilistic inference. "Forward-backward" would be a more accurate and standard descriptor for the mechanism, avoiding confusion with established techniques in graphical models. This terminology issue should be addressed immediately in the revision.

Section 3.5 (Training) is insufficiently detailed. The authors state the model is trained via backpropagation through structure (BPTS) but omit key methodological specifics. BPTS requires unfolding the recursive network, which creates an extremely deep computational graph to propagate gradients to the word embeddings. The authors should clarify: (1) how the unfolding is implemented, (2) whether the gradient flow is truncated or fully unrolled, and (3) whether the deep structure impacts training stability or convergence. Citing Goller & Kuchler (1996) would strengthen this discussion.

Section 3.6 fails to sufficiently distinguish BPRNNs from Irsoy & Cardie (2008). The paper claims novelty in "hybrid vectors" but does not explicitly articulate how this differs from the bidirectional updates in prior work. A concise comparison table or a dedicated paragraph contrasting the architectures would resolve this ambiguity.

Section 4.1 requires significant improvement for broader accessibility. The Semeval 2013 Task 2 involves two subtasks (contextual and message polarity), but the paper does not specify which one was addressed (contextual polarity is implied). A clearer explanation of the task—particularly the parser constraints and why contextual polarity is the focus—would aid readers outside NLP. For instance, explicitly stating that "the model predicts sentiment for phrases within sentence contexts, as required by Semeval 2013 Task 2a" would greatly enhance clarity.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4, which present ablation studies and results, could be consolidated for better flow. Currently, the separation feels arbitrary; merging these sections would reduce redundancy and strengthen the narrative around the impact of hybrid vectors and bidirectional updates.

Overall, the paper introduces a promising extension to bidirectional recursive networks with a well-motivated mechanism. The core idea of hybrid word vectors represents the most significant contribution, but the current presentation lacks rigor in experimental design, terminology, and methodological detail. Addressing the concerns above—particularly the experimental fairness, training procedure, and clearer differentiation from prior work—would substantially strengthen the contribution. With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable addition to the literature on structured prediction in NLP.
