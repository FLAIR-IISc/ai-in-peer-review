

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel extension to standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) that incorporates a backward (top-down) computation step during inference. The authors argue that standard RNNs, which process sentences in a purely feedforward (bottom-up) manner, fail to capture context-dependent sentiment, as demonstrated in examples like "Android beats iOS" where the sentiment toward "Android" depends on the surrounding sentence context. The GB-RNN addresses this limitation by allowing phrase-level representations to influence word-level representations through a feedbackward pass, thereby enabling context-aware sentiment classification. The model also introduces a hybrid word vector approach combining unsupervised and supervised representations, and demonstrates that dropout improves training performance.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making a strong case for the necessity of context-aware sentiment analysis. The motivation is compelling, particularly in showing how standard RNNs fail to capture context-dependent sentiment in natural language examples. The authors effectively connect their work to the NIPS 2013 literature, especially the Recursive Deep Learning models by Socher et al. and the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks by Irsoy and Cardie (2013), which they properly cite as related work. The paper makes a clear contribution by extending these approaches with a feedbackward mechanism that allows for global context propagation, rather than relying on local context windows.

In terms of quality, the paper presents a technically sound model with a solid experimental validation. The authors compare their approach against multiple baselines including the SemEval 2013 challenge winner, demonstrating a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs and state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 task. The hybrid word vector approach is well-justified and shows clear advantages over purely supervised or unsupervised representations. However, the experimental section could be strengthened by providing more details on model selection and hyperparameter tuning. Specifically, it's unclear whether the standard RNN and Bidirectional RNN baselines used the same hybrid word vectors as the GB-RNN, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain how the "best" models were selected (e.g., whether they used ensemble methods or cross-validation). The paper mentions that the top 5 GB-RNN models were ensembled, but doesn't clarify why this wasn't done for other models.

The clarity of the paper is generally high, though some sections would benefit from additional explanation. The term "belief propagation" is misleading and should be replaced with "forward-backward" or similar to avoid confusion with probabilistic graphical models. Section 3.5 (Training) is too brief and fails to adequately describe how the backpropagation through structure algorithm is applied, which is crucial for understanding the model's implementation. The authors should cite Goller and Küchler (1996) and mention that the recursive model is unfolded during training, as this is a critical technical detail for reproducibility. Section 3.6 (Comparison to Other Models) is particularly confusing and fails to clearly distinguish the GB-RNN from Irsoy and Cardie's work. A more detailed comparison of the mathematical formulations would clarify the novelty.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by introducing the feedbackward mechanism for context propagation, which differs from previous bidirectional approaches that process left-to-right and right-to-left separately. The hybrid word vector approach also represents a novel combination of unsupervised and supervised learning. However, the paper would strengthen its claim of novelty by more explicitly contrasting with the related work, particularly the Irsoy and Cardie bidirectional model, which the authors cite but don't sufficiently differentiate from.

The significance of this work is substantial for natural language processing, particularly for sentiment analysis tasks where context is crucial. The model's ability to accurately predict sentiment toward specific entities in social media text has clear practical applications. The paper advances the state of the art in contextual sentiment analysis by demonstrating superior performance on SemEval 2013, and the approach could potentially generalize to other NLP tasks requiring context-aware compositional semantics.

Strengths: Clear motivation for context-aware sentiment analysis, strong experimental results, well-structured paper, novel hybrid word vector approach, good connection to relevant NIPS literature.

Weaknesses: Misleading terminology ("belief propagation"), insufficient detail about model training and selection, unclear comparison with related work, some sections (3.5, 3.6) need improvement.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to clarify the model's relationship to existing bidirectional approaches, provide more detail about the training procedure, and fix the misleading terminology. The paper makes a valuable contribution to contextual sentiment analysis and would be a strong fit for NIPS.
