

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard recursive neural networks by adding a downward pass through the parse tree during inference. While conventional RNNs process text in a bottom-up manner (from words to phrases to sentences), GB-RNNs incorporate global sentence context by propagating representations top-down, enabling phrase and word representations to be updated based on the full sentence context. The authors demonstrate this approach on contextual sentiment analysis, achieving a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs on the SemEval 2013 task and establishing state-of-the-art results.

The paper makes a well-motivated contribution to compositional semantic modeling. It correctly identifies a critical limitation of standard RNNs: their inability to capture context-dependent sentiment (e.g., "Android" being positive in "Android beats iOS" but neutral in "The Android's screen is better than the iPhone's"). The proposed feedbackward step is elegant, simple, and effectively addresses this limitation. The authors clearly relate their work to previous contributions, particularly comparing their approach to Irsoy and Cardie's bidirectional RNN (2013) and explaining how their downward pass differs and outperforms previous methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The technical description of the GB-RNN architecture, including the hybrid word vector representation (combining unsupervised and supervised vectors), is precise and accessible. The authors provide a thorough theoretical comparison with related work, strengthening their contribution. The motivation for context-dependence is compellingly illustrated through examples like Figure 1, which effectively demonstrates the limitation of current approaches.

In terms of quality, the paper presents robust empirical evidence for the effectiveness of GB-RNNs. The experimental setup is sound, with appropriate baselines (including the SemEval 2013 competition winner) and careful hyperparameter tuning. The authors use dropout to improve training and demonstrate that their hybrid word vector approach outperforms both purely unsupervised and purely supervised methods. The improvements over the standard RNN (5%+ F1) and the bidirectional RNN (as shown in Table 2) are significant and well-supported.

However, there are some concerns about experimental details that need clarification:
1. The comparison between GB-RNN and the bidirectional RNN from Irsoy and Cardie (2013) in Table 2 does not specify whether they used the same experimental settings (dropout, hybrid vectors, additional training data). This is essential for a fair comparison.
2. The ensemble result (which achieves the best performance) should not be included in the model comparison table, as it represents a different approach than the single models.
3. The phrase "widely used mix of both" in Section 4.4 is unclear and should be clarified.

The significance of this work is substantial. Contextual sentiment analysis is a critical problem in NLP, especially for social media analysis where users often focus on sentiment toward specific entities. The GB-RNN approach is generalizable to other NLP tasks requiring context-dependent interpretation, such as word-sense disambiguation.

While the high-level idea of bidirectional processing is not entirely novel, the specific implementation of the feedbackward step in GB-RNNs is original and well-differentiated from previous work. The authors have clearly articulated how their approach differs from Irsoy and Cardie's model, particularly in how they use both forward and backward parent nodes during the downward pass.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured sections that guide the reader through the motivation, methodology, and results. The writing is professional and accessible to NLP researchers.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a meaningful limitation in current RNN approaches
- Simple yet effective solution that builds on established concepts
- Thorough experimental evaluation with compelling results
- Strong theoretical comparison with related work
- Excellent writing and organization

Weaknesses:
- Experimental details require clarification for fair comparison
- Ensemble result should not be included in model comparison table
- Unclear reference to "widely used mix of both" in Section 4.4

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and meaningful contribution to compositional semantic modeling. The method is elegant, the experiments are well-conducted, and the results are compelling. With minor clarifications to the experimental details, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
