This paper proposes a method for sentiment classification using a recursive neural network auto-encoder, leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors. The authors refer to this as "belief propagation," which is a misnomer as the proposed model is not an instance of the well-known inference algorithm, and this terminology is likely to confuse the reader. While the paper is generally well-written, the motivation for the model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification.

The paper's most significant weakness is its experimental evaluation. Presenting an ensemble result is inappropriate in this context, as none of the benchmarks are ensembles. This makes for an unfair comparison and obfuscates the true performance of the proposed architecture. Furthermore, the experimental setup is not described in sufficient detail, making the results difficult to interpret and reproduce. The authors should clarify several key points:

*   How were the hyperparameters tuned and the final models (both the single best and the ensemble) selected?
*   What was the tuning process for the benchmark models presented in Table 2?
*   Were the benchmark models trained on the same augmented data as the BP-RNN?
*   Did the baseline RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models also use the hybrid word vectors?

In the absence of this information, it seems that the second entry in Table 3 is the most direct comparison to the benchmarks in Tables 1 & 2 when assessing the contribution of the BP-RNN architecture itself.

**Minor Points:**

*   The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is surprising. Given the noisy and unconventional nature of Twitter data, it would be valuable to know the parser's accuracy on this specific dataset.
*   Several citations are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie).

In summary, this paper presents a recursive neural network model for classifying phrasal sentiment in context. While the results appear reasonable, the model's motivation needs to be strengthened and the experimental methodology requires significant clarification before the paper's contribution can be fully assessed.
