### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed for contextual classification tasks in natural language processing. The core idea is to extend standard feedforward (bottom-up) Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward (top-down) pass during inference. The initial feedforward pass computes compositional vector representations for phrases in a sentence tree. Subsequently, the feedbackward pass propagates information from the root of the tree back down to the leaves, allowing the representation and classification of each word and phrase to be refined based on the global sentence context. This architecture is motivated by the need to handle phenomena like aspect-specific sentiment analysis, where the sentiment of a word (e.g., "Android") depends on its role in the full sentence (e.g., "Android beats iOS").

The paper makes two additional contributions: it proposes a hybrid word vector representation that combines pre-trained unsupervised vectors with task-specific supervised vectors, and it demonstrates that dropout can effectively regularize RNNs. The authors evaluate the GB-RNN on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, showing that the model achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming previous competition winners and baseline models, including the standard feedforward RNN.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated Model:** The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in NLP: modeling context dependence for semantic interpretation. The proposed two-pass architecture is an intuitive and principled approach for incorporating global sentence information into local predictions, moving beyond the limitations of fixed-size context windows.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The GB-RNN achieves state-of-the-art results on a competitive public benchmark (SemEval 2013, Task 2). The empirical validation is thorough, including comparisons against strong baselines (standard RNN, bidirectional RNN, logistic regression) and ablation studies that demonstrate the clear benefit of the feedbackward step (a 3% F1 improvement) and the hybrid word vector representation.

3.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The model is well-described with helpful illustrations (Fig. 2), and the motivation is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a solid contribution, the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points regarding clarity, experimental details, and comparison to prior work.

1.  **Clarity and Terminology:**
    *   The term "Global Belief Recursive Neural Network" is potentially misleading. The model does not appear to use belief propagation in the sense of probabilistic graphical models. A more descriptive name, such as "Forward-Backward RNN" or "Top-Down Refinement RNN," might be more appropriate and avoid confusion for readers familiar with that literature.

2.  **Comparison to Prior Work:**
    *   The primary novelty of the architecture lies in its distinction from the Bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie (2013). Section 3.6 attempts to clarify this distinction, but the explanation could be more insightful. The authors should provide a clearer intuition for *why* their proposed update rule (Eq. 5), which uses both the forward and backward parent vectors, is superior to the Bidirectional RNN's formulation (Eq. 4). A more detailed discussion of the architectural trade-offs would help solidify the paper's contribution.

3.  **Reproducibility and Experimental Details:**
    *   **Training Procedure:** Section 3.5 on training is too brief. The authors mention using "backpropagation through structure" [9], but more detail is needed for reproducibility. It would be valuable to explain how the recursive model is unfolded for training and to discuss the implications of the resulting network depth, particularly for gradient propagation to the word embeddings.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper states that the best models were selected via cross-validation on the development set. To increase transparency and strengthen the experimental claims, the authors should provide more details on the hyperparameter search space (e.g., ranges for vector sizes, learning rates, regularization) and the specific process used to select the "single best model" for Table 2. While the paper mentions that baselines used the same hybrid vectors and dropout, clarifying that these models were also tuned with comparable effort would further bolster the fairness of the comparison.
    *   **Ensemble Comparison:** In Table 1, the GB-RNN's state-of-the-art performance is achieved with an ensemble of five models. This makes the comparison to the (presumably single-model) systems from the SemEval competition potentially unfair. While ensembling is a standard practice, this should be more explicitly acknowledged as a contributing factor to the final performance. The inclusion of single-model results against baselines in Table 2 is a commendable step towards a fairer evaluation.

4.  **Presentation for a General Audience:**
    *   **Task Description:** Section 4.1 assumes significant familiarity with the SemEval shared tasks. For the benefit of the broader NIPS audience, this section should be improved with a clearer description of the SemEval 2013 Task 2, specifically the contextual phrase polarity subtask. This would help readers better appreciate the problem's challenges and the rationale for the described parser constraints.
    *   **Organization:** The content in Sections 4.3 (baselines) and 4.4 (additional data) is closely related. Merging them could improve the narrative flow of the experiments section.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and effective model for context-dependent classification, supported by strong empirical results. The core idea of a feedbackward pass to refine representations is compelling. The main weaknesses lie in the lack of detail regarding the training procedure and hyperparameter tuning, and a somewhat unclear distinction from closely related prior work. Addressing the points above would significantly strengthen the paper and improve its impact. I believe the work is a valuable contribution to the field.
