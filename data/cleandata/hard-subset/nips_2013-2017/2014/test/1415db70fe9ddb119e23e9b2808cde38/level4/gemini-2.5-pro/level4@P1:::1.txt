This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a model designed to address context-dependent classification tasks in natural language processing. The proposed architecture extends standard feedforward (bottom-up) recursive neural networks by incorporating a feedbackward (top-down) inference step. This second pass allows information from the entire sentence (captured at the root of the parse tree) to propagate back down to individual words and phrases, enabling their predictions to be conditioned on the global context. The authors also introduce a hybrid word vector representation, combining pre-trained unsupervised vectors with task-specific supervised vectors, and demonstrate the effectiveness of dropout for training. The model is evaluated on a contextual sentiment analysis task from SemEval 2013, where it reportedly achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   The paper addresses a well-known and important limitation of standard feedforward recursive models: their inability to capture context beyond the local compositional structure. The motivation for a top-down pass to incorporate global information is clear and intuitive.
*   The model is an interesting and logical extension of existing recursive architectures, and the paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The reported experimental results are strong, showing a significant improvement over the standard RNN baseline and outperforming the winner of a competitive shared task. This suggests the proposed architecture is effective for the problem at hand.
*   The idea of using hybrid word vectors (partially fixed, partially fine-tuned) is a valuable practical contribution that could be useful to other researchers.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection):**

*   **Clarity of Originality and Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper's primary contribution is presented as an extension of the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Network by Irsoy and Cardie (2013). However, the explanation of the differences in Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing and could be clarified. The authors should provide a more direct and intuitive explanation of why their formulation for the backward pass is superior, beyond simply stating the different equations.
*   **Experimental Fairness and Clarity:** The experimental comparisons lack some important details, making it difficult to assess their fairness.
    *   It is crucial to clarify if the baseline models (standard RNN and Bidirectional RNN) were also trained using the proposed hybrid vectors and dropout. The paper implies this is the case, but it should be stated explicitly to ensure the performance gains are attributable to the GB-RNN architecture itself.
    *   The process for hyperparameter tuning and model selection is not described. How were the "best" models for each architecture selected?
    *   The best-performing GB-RNN result is from an ensemble of five models. This should not be directly compared to single models from the baselines or the SemEval competition. A comparison between the single best GB-RNN and the single best baseline model is essential for a fair evaluation of the architecture.
*   **Lack of Detail in Key Sections:**
    *   The training description in Section 3.5 is too brief. The authors should explicitly mention the use of the "backpropagation through structure" algorithm (and cite the original work, e.g., Goller & Küchler, 1996). Unfolding the recursive model for both a forward and backward pass creates a very deep network structure; more detail on how gradients are computed and propagated back to the word embeddings would significantly improve the paper's technical depth and reproducibility.
    *   The description of the SemEval task in Section 4.1 is not sufficiently clear for a general NIPS audience. The authors should specify that they are addressing the contextual polarity subtask and briefly explain it. This context is needed to fully appreciate the motivation for the parser constraints.
*   **Terminology:** The name "Global Belief Recursive Neural Network" is potentially misleading. "Belief Propagation" refers to a specific class of message-passing algorithms in probabilistic graphical models. The mechanism described here is more accurately a two-pass (forward-backward or bottom-up/top-down) inference scheme. Using a more descriptive name could prevent confusion.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  Please clarify the experimental setup: Were hybrid vectors and dropout used for *all* baseline models (RNN, B-RNN)? Please report the performance of your single best model alongside the ensemble result for a fairer comparison.
2.  In Section 3.5, please provide more detail on the training algorithm, explicitly mentioning backpropagation through structure and discussing the implications of the model's depth.
3.  In Section 3.6, please provide a clearer, more intuitive explanation of the key differences between your model's backward pass and that of Irsoy and Cardie. What is the practical advantage of your design?
4.  Please expand Section 4.1 to make the experimental task understandable to readers outside the NLP community.
5.  Consider revising the model's name to something like "Forward-Backward Recursive Neural Network" to more accurately reflect its mechanism.
6.  The reference to Irsoy and Cardie (2013) appears incomplete. Please verify all citations.
7.  A minor organizational point: Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could potentially be merged for better flow.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting model for an important problem. However, the concerns regarding experimental clarity and the lack of technical detail in the training section are significant and should be addressed to strengthen the contribution.
