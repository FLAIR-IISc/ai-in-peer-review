This paper introduces the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), a model designed for contextual sentiment analysis. The core idea is to augment a standard feedforward Recursive Neural Network (RNN) with a top-down "feedbackward" pass. This allows the representation of each word and phrase to be conditioned on the global sentence context, enabling the model to handle cases where sentiment is context-dependent (e.g., "Android beats iOS"). The authors also explore a hybrid word vector representation that combines unsupervised pre-trained vectors with task-specific supervised vectors, and demonstrate that dropout can improve model training. On the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, the proposed model is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of context-dependent sentiment classification, for which standard feedforward models are ill-suited.
*   The proposed GB-RNN architecture, which incorporates a feedbackward pass to refine local predictions with global context, is a novel and intuitive extension to standard RNNs.
*   The empirical results reported on the SemEval 2013 dataset appear to be very strong, outperforming the competition winner.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weaknesses lie in its motivation and, more significantly, its experimental evaluation.

1.  **Vague Motivation:** While the intuition of propagating global information back down the parse tree is appealing, the motivation for the specific architectural choices is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification. It is not obvious why the particular formulation in Equation 3 is optimal or preferable to other possible ways of combining the feedforward and feedbackward information.

2.  **Unclear Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental methodology is not described with sufficient clarity, making it difficult to fairly assess the paper's claims.
    *   **Ensemble Results:** The main result presented in Table 1 is for an ensemble of five GB-RNN models. This is compared against single-model systems from the SemEval competition and other baselines. This is an unfair comparison that obfuscates the true performance gain of the proposed architecture itself. To make a clear scientific point, the primary comparison should be between single models.
    *   **Inconsistent Comparisons:** It is unclear whether the baselines in Table 2 were configured and trained under the same conditions as the GB-RNN. This makes the reported performance gains difficult to interpret. In the absence of this information, one might guess that the most direct comparison for a single GB-RNN model is the "Fixed+Supervised" entry in Table 3. If so, the improvement over the standard RNN is less substantial than the main results suggest.

**Questions for the Authors:**
To properly evaluate the contribution, could you please clarify the following points regarding the experimental setup?
*   How were the hyperparameters for all models (GB-RNN, RNN, Bidirectional-RNN, and other baselines) tuned? Were they all optimized on the same development set?
*   Did the baseline models in Table 2 (RNN, Bidirectional-RNN, etc.) also use the same hybrid word vectors and dropout training scheme as the GB-RNN?
*   Were the baseline models also trained on the same augmented dataset (NRC lexicon, noisily labeled tweets, movie reviews) as the final GB-RNN? A fair comparison requires all models to be trained on the same data.

**Minor Points:**
*   The authors use the term "Global Belief," which may be confusing as the proposed method is not an instance of the belief propagation inference algorithm. The name seems to be a metaphor rather than a technical description, which could be clarified.
*   The use of a standard off-the-shelf parser for Twitter data is surprising, given that tweets often contain non-standard grammar and syntax. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the parser's accuracy on this domain.
*   Several citations in the reference list are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie, 2013).

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a novel and interesting recursive neural network architecture for classifying phrasal sentiment in context. While the empirical results appear strong, the contribution is currently undermined by a lack of clear motivation for the model's specific design and, more critically, an experimental evaluation that is not entirely transparent. The paper would be significantly improved if the authors presented a clear, direct comparison of their single best model against identically configured and tuned baselines. Without these clarifications, it is difficult to confidently assess the significance of the proposed method.
