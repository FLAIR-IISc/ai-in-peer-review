- Term "belief propagation" is misleading—the method does not implement the BP algorithm and risks confusing readers.  
- Paper is well-written but lacks theoretical justification for why combining encoding and decoding phrase vectors should improve sentiment classification.  
- Major evaluation flaw: reporting ensemble results (Table 3) while all other benchmarks (Tables 1–2) show single-model performance.  
- Ensembling obscures whether improvements come from the BP-RNN architecture or from ensembling artifacts.  
- Critical methodological details missing: how models were tuned and how ensemble/best models were selected.  
- Unclear if benchmarks in Table 2 were tuned using the same data augmentation as BP-RNN, compromising comparability.  
- Unclear whether RNN and BiRNN baselines used hybrid word vectors, raising fairness concerns in comparisons.  
- The BP-RNN-only result (Table 3, first entry) is the most comparable, but ambiguity and missing details prevent meaningful comparison.  
- Minor: using an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is surprising—authors should report its accuracy on this domain.  
- Minor: several references (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie 2014) are incomplete, undermining reproducibility.  
- Core contribution (recursive NN for phrasal sentiment) yields plausible results but is weakened by lack of theory, ambiguous evaluation, and missing methodological details.  
- Recommendation: clarify theoretical rationale, remove ensemble obfuscation, and provide full methodological details to make results interpretable and replicable.
