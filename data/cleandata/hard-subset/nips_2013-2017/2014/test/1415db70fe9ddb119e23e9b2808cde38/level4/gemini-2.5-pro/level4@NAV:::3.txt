This paper presents a method for sentiment classification that utilizes phrase representations from both the encoder and decoder of a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The authors term this approach "belief propagation," a potentially confusing choice of name, as the method does not implement the well-known inference algorithm. Overall, the paper is clearly written, yet the rationale for the proposed model is not well-articulated and could benefit from a stronger theoretical justification.

The most significant weakness of this work lies in its experimental evaluation. The inclusion of an ensemble result is questionable, given that none of the benchmark systems are ensembles; this makes a direct comparison difficult and clouds the performance of the core contribution. The specifics of the comparative models are also ambiguous. To properly assess the results, the following should be clarified: what was the procedure for tuning hyperparameters and selecting the best-performing models/ensemble? Were the benchmarks in Table 2 tuned in a similar manner? Did they use the same data augmentation as the BP-RNN? Was the proposed hybrid word vector representation also used by the baseline RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models?

In the absence of this information, the most direct comparison for the proposed BP-RNN architecture against the benchmarks in Tables 1 & 2 appears to be the second entry in Table 3.

Minor points:
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweet data is surprising. It would be valuable to know its performance on this non-standard domain.
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete, along with several others.

In conclusion, this paper introduces a recursive neural network for contextual phrasal sentiment classification. While the reported performance appears plausible, the model itself is weakly motivated, and the experimental methodology lacks the clarity needed for a thorough evaluation.
