In Natural Language Processing, Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) are used to generate representations for snippets of text by recursively combining representations of their constituent words and phrases. This process typically starts with word representations at the leaves of a parse tree and proceeds upwards, culminating in a single representation for the entire snippet at the root node. A key limitation of this unidirectional process is that information cannot propagate down the tree, meaning a node's representation is not informed by the larger context in which it appears.

This paper introduces a bidirectional extension to RNNs that addresses this limitation. The model first performs the standard upward pass through the tree, which is then followed by a downward pass. This second pass augments the representation of each node with contextual information from its parent nodes. The resulting system achieves state-of-the-art performance on Task 2 of SemEval 2012.

The proposed extension to the established RNN framework is simple, elegant, and effective. While the high-level idea is similar to the one proposed in [18], the computation performed during the downward pass is novel, and the resulting performance appears to be superior. The method is well-motivated, the model is presented clearly, and the paper is well-written overall.

The experimental results appear to be excellent; however, I have some concerns about the experimental setup that could impact the fairness of the model comparisons. It is unclear if the result for the alternative bidirectional RNN model from [18], reported in Table 2, was obtained using the same experimental conditions as the proposed model. Specifically, were dropout, the additional training data, and the hybrid vectors also used for this baseline? For a fair comparison, these settings should be consistent across models. Furthermore, while the ensemble result is impressive, including it in a table meant to compare the performance of *individual* models is unconventional and could be misleading. This result would be more appropriately reported separately.

Finally, I have a minor question for clarification: in Section 4.4, what does the phrase "widely used mix of both" refer to?

Despite these minor concerns about the experimental reporting, this is a well-executed paper based on a simple and elegant idea.
