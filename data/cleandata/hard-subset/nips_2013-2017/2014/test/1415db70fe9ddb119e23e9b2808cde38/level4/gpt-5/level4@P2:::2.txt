This paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a bidirectional extension of recursive neural networks for contextual phrase-level classification. Standard RNNs build phrase representations bottom-up along a parse tree, which limits them to context-independent labels. GB-RNN augments the usual upward pass with a top-down “feedbackward” pass that propagates sentence-level context back to every node. Each node’s representation is then the concatenation of forward and backward vectors, which a softmax uses to predict labels (e.g., sentiment). The model is evaluated on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter and SMS sentiment) and reports state-of-the-art results, including an ensemble that surpasses the competition winner. The paper also introduces hybrid word vectors (fixed unsupervised embeddings concatenated with small supervised trainable components) and shows that dropout helps training in this setting.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates GB-RNN among recursive and recurrent architectures, including bi-directional recurrent models and, importantly, bidirectional recursive models such as Irsoy and Cardie (2013) and the inside–outside RNN (Le and Zuidema). The key distinction emphasized here is the specific backward computation: GB-RNN uses both the forward and backward parent vectors and a decomposition matrix W↓ to compute child backward vectors, with an additional transform V at the root, allowing different backward dimensionality and feature specialization. The authors argue this differs from and outperforms prior bidirectional recursive variants on SemEval.

Strengths:
- Simple and elegant extension: One additional top-down pass with a well-defined backward recursion, minimal architectural complexity, and clear motivation for context-dependent labeling (e.g., aspect-specific sentiment).
- Strong empirical results: Consistent gains over a standard RNN and a bidirectional recursive baseline on SemEval 2013 Task 2; the ensemble achieves state-of-the-art F1.
- Practical training choices: Hybrid embeddings (fixed unsupervised + small supervised slice) and dropout applied to node vectors are sensible and well-motivated; two-stage training leverages larger weakly labeled corpora without conflating context-dependent labels.
- Clarity of exposition: The model equations, intuition for V and W↓, and comparison to related architectures are generally clear. The task choice (Twitter/SMS) appropriately stresses robustness to noisy, non-canonical text and parsing.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Fairness of baselines: It is unclear whether the bidirectional recursive baseline was trained with the same hybrid embeddings, dropout, extra lexicon data, and weakly labeled corpora. Please clarify whether Table 2 uses matched resources and training protocols for all models.
- Ensemble vs single-model comparison: Reporting an ensemble alongside single-model baselines is informative, but the main comparative table should focus on single models; the ensemble result could be separated or clearly annotated.
- Data usage transparency: The paper uses NRC lexicon items, a large set of weakly labeled tweets, and movie reviews. More details are needed on how many additional examples were added, their label distributions, and how they were incorporated (pretraining vs joint training), along with an ablation isolating each resource.
- Parsing constraints and robustness: For tweets, constraining the parser so each labeled phrase is a subtree may introduce artifacts. Please discuss how often this constraint fails, whether any examples were discarded or altered, and sensitivity to parsing errors.
- Backward-pass design ablations: The paper motivates V and the choice to exclude the forward node when computing backward vectors. An ablation on (a) setting V to identity or tying dimensions, (b) including/excluding forward vectors in backward computations, and (c) varying backward dimensionality nd would strengthen the architectural claims.
- Efficiency: The backward pass adds parameters (n·nd more than the compared bidirectional model). Some discussion of computational cost, memory, and inference latency would be useful, especially for large-scale or real-time applications.
- Clarity issues: In Section 4.4, “widely used mix of both” is ambiguous—please specify what mixture is meant (e.g., which external resources or labeling heuristics). Also, double-check consistency of the SemEval task/year references throughout.

Quality: The approach is technically sound, with clear forward/backward recursions and training via backpropagation through structure. Results are strong, but the comparative rigor would be improved by ensuring fully controlled baselines (same embeddings, dropout, and external data). Additional ablations on backward design choices would further substantiate the modeling decisions.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Equations are clear and the intuition for the feedbackward pass is conveyed well. Some terminology (“feedbackward”) is unconventional but understandable. A few places need clarification (resource usage, “mix of both,” baseline parity).

Originality: The idea of a top-down recursive pass is related to prior bidirectional recursive work; the novelty lies in the specific backward computation and use of both forward and backward parent vectors with an explicit V transform and flexible dimensionality. The hybrid embedding setup is practical but not novel per se; the combination and empirical validation are valuable.

Significance: Context-dependent labeling is important for aspect-based sentiment and other NLP tasks (e.g., entity-level sentiment, word sense disambiguation). The performance gains and simplicity of the approach suggest it will be useful and extendable. If the superiority over prior bidirectional recursive models holds under controlled conditions, the contribution is meaningful.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated extension that addresses a real limitation of standard RNNs.
- Strong results on a competitive benchmark; practical training recipe.
- Clear comparison to related architectures and good exposition.

Cons:
- Need clearer, controlled comparisons to bidirectional recursive baselines with matched resources.
- Limited ablations on backward design choices and resource contributions.
- Ensemble reporting obscures single-model comparisons; efficiency discussion is limited.

Questions for the authors:
- Were the bidirectional recursive baseline and the standard RNN trained with exactly the same embeddings (including fixed+trainable split), dropout schedule, additional lexicon examples, and weakly labeled corpora?
- Can you provide ablations for V, nd, and inclusion of forward vectors in backward computations?
- How often did the subtree constraint fail in parsing tweets, and how were such cases handled?
- Please clarify “widely used mix of both” in Section 4.4.
- Can you report runtime/memory overhead for GB-RNN versus the compared baselines?

Overall: A well-executed paper with a simple, elegant bidirectional extension to recursive networks and strong empirical results. With clearer baseline parity and a few additional ablations, this would be a compelling contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
