This paper introduces "belief propagation recursive neural networks," a novel extension of the traditional feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks that incorporates a backward (top-down) step during inference. This innovation enables phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels.

The paper is well-written, and the model is well-motivated and clearly described. The authors build upon the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), with one notable contribution being the introduction of hybrid word vectors. This may be the most significant difference between the proposed model and previous work.

However, the experimental comparisons are not entirely fair, and several points require clarification. Specifically, did the RNN and B-RNN models also utilize hybrid vectors? How were the "best" models tuned and selected? How was the single best model chosen? It is also worth noting that the other methods did not employ an ensemble approach. These details should be provided to ensure a comprehensive understanding of the results.

Furthermore, the term "belief propagation" may be misleading, and the term "forward-backward" could be more suitable.

Section 3.5 (Training) is somewhat brief and could benefit from additional details. For instance, it appears that the training process employs the back-propagation through structure algorithm (Goller, 1996), which implies that the recursive model is unfolded. This results in a very deep network to update the word embedding part. The authors should provide more information on this critical step.

Section 3.6 is slightly confusing, and the distinction between the proposed model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie should be clarified.

Section 4.1 could be improved to facilitate understanding by readers outside the NLP community. In the context of Task 2 of SemEval 2013, there are two subtasks: contextual and message polarity. It seems that the authors address the contextual polarity task. A clearer presentation of the task would help readers comprehend the parser constraints.

Consider merging sections 4.3 and 4.4, as they appear to be closely related.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents an interesting variant of the Bidirectional recursive model.
