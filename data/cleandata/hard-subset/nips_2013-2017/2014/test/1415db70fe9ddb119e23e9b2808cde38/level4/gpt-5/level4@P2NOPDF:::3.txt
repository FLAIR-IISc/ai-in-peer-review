This paper proposes using both the encoder and decoder phrase representations from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification. The authors refer to this as “belief propagation,” but the term is misleading, as the method is not an instance of that inference algorithm and the name risks confusing readers. The paper is generally well written, yet the motivation for the model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification.

The primary weakness lies in the evaluation. Reporting ensemble results is unnecessary and obscures the comparison, since none of the baselines are ensembles. Moreover, the exact composition of the compared systems is not sufficiently described. Key details that need clarification include:
- How were hyperparameters tuned, and how were the ensemble and “best” models selected?
- How were the baselines in Table 2 tuned?
- Did all systems use the same augmented training data as the BP-RNN?
- Did the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines also use hybrid word vectors?

Without this information, my impression is that the second entry in Table 3 is the most directly comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when evaluating the BP-RNN architecture.

Minor comments:
- It is surprising that an off-the-shelf parser was used for tweets. Please report its accuracy on this domain or provide relevant diagnostics.
- The Irsoy and Cardie citation is incomplete, as are several others.

In summary, the paper presents a recursive neural model for context-sensitive phrase-level sentiment. The results seem plausible, but the method’s motivation is not strongly articulated and the experimental methodology is insufficiently transparent. Clarifying the naming, providing a stronger rationale for combining encoder and decoder representations, and detailing the training and comparison protocols would substantially strengthen the contribution.
