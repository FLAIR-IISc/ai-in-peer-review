This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment classification by leveraging both encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The term "belief propagation" is used to describe this method, which may cause confusion among readers as it does not align with the traditional definition of this inference algorithm. Overall, the paper is well-written, but the theoretical justification and motivation behind the proposed model are not clearly articulated.

A significant concern with this paper lies in its evaluation methodology. The presentation of ensemble results is unnecessary, as none of the benchmarks used are ensembles, which obscures the outcome. Furthermore, the composition of the compared models is not explicitly stated. To improve the clarity of the evaluation, the authors should provide details on model tuning and selection, as well as the tuning process for the benchmarks in Table 2. Specifically, it is unclear whether the benchmarks utilized the same augmented data as the BP-RNN and whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN employed hybrid word vectors.

In the absence of this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears to be the most comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when assessing the BP-RNN architecture.

Some minor observations include the unexpected use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweet parsing, which raises questions about its accuracy on this specific data. Additionally, the citation for Irsoy and Cardie, among others, is incomplete.

In summary, this paper proposes a recursive neural network model for phrasal sentiment classification, yielding reasonable results. However, the model's motivation and experimental methodology require further clarification to strengthen the paper's overall contribution.
