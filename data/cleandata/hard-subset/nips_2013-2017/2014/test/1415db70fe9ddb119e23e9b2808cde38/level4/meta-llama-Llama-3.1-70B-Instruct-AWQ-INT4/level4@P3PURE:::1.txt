This paper introduces "belief propagation recursive neural networks," which extend the traditional "feedforward" (bottom-up) recursive networks by incorporating a backward (top-down) step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels.

The paper is overall well-written, and the model is well-motivated and clearly described. The work builds upon the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), with notable contributions including the introduction of hybrid word vectors. The use of hybrid vectors may be the most significant difference from previous work.

However, the experimental comparisons are not entirely fair. It is unclear whether the RNN and B-RNN models also utilize hybrid vectors. Furthermore, the paper does not provide sufficient details on how the "best" models are tuned and selected, as well as how the single best model is chosen. It is worth noting that the other methods do not use an ensemble approach. These points require clarification.

The term "belief propagation" may be misleading, and the term "forward-backward" could be more suitable.

Section 3.5 (Training) is somewhat brief and could benefit from more details. For instance, it is likely that the training employs the back-propagation through structure algorithm (Goller, 1996). This algorithm involves unfolding the recursive model, resulting in a very deep network to update the word embedding part. If understood correctly, the authors could provide more information on this crucial step.

Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing, and the difference between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie needs to be clarified.

Section 4.1 requires improvement to be comprehensible to readers outside the NLP community. Specifically, Task 2 of Semeval 2013 consists of two subtasks: contextual and message polarity. It appears that the paper addresses the contextual polarity task. A clearer presentation of the task would facilitate understanding of the parser constraints.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could potentially be merged.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents an interesting variant of the Bidirectional recursive model.
