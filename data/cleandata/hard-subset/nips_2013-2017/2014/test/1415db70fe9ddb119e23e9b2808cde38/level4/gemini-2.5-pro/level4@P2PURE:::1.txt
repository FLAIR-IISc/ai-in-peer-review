This paper introduces "belief propagation recursive neural networks," an extension of feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks that incorporates a backward (top-down) step during inference. This mechanism allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word-level representations.

The paper is generally well-written, and the model is well-motivated and clearly described. It presents an interesting variant of the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Network model. However, several points require clarification and improvement.

A primary concern is the clarity of the model's novelty and its comparison to prior work. The work is presented as an extension of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks (Irsoy and Cardie, 2008), with the introduction of hybrid word vectors being a key contribution. The authors should clarify if this is the main point of novelty. Furthermore, the discussion in Section 3.6 comparing this model to the work of Irsoy and Cardie is confusing and must be revised to better delineate the specific contributions of this paper. Note that the reference to Irsoy and Cardie is also incomplete.

The experimental comparisons also lack sufficient detail to assess their fairness. The authors must clarify the following:
*   Did the baseline RNN and B-RNN models also use the proposed hybrid vectors for a fair comparison?
*   What was the procedure for tuning hyperparameters and selecting the "best" models?
*   How was the single best model selected for the final comparison, particularly as it is compared against baseline methods that do not use an ensemble?

Several sections of the paper require more detail.
*   The training procedure in Section 3.5 is too brief. It is likely that training relies on the back-propagation through structure algorithm (Goller and Küchler, 1996, which should be cited). This would involve unfolding the model into a very deep network to update the word embeddings. Given the importance of this step, the authors should provide a more thorough explanation.
*   Section 4.1 on the experimental setup should be improved to be more accessible to readers outside of the NLP community. For instance, specifying which of the two Semeval 2013 subtasks was addressed (i.e., contextual or message polarity) would help readers better understand the task and the parser constraints.

Finally, a few minor suggestions:
*   The term "belief propagation" may be misleading; a term like "forward-backward" could be more appropriate for the described mechanism.
*   The authors could consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 for a more concise presentation.
