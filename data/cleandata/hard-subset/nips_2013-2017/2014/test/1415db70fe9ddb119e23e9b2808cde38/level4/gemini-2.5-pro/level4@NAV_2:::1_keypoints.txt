- Introduces GB-RNNs: augment bottom-up recursive nets with a top-down feedbackward pass for context-dependent phrase and word-level predictions.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on SemEval 2013 aspect-specific sentiment (contextual polarity) benchmark.  
- Novelty: specific GB-RNN feedbackward formulation and use of hybrid word vectors are presented as new contributions.  
- Significance: tackles the important limitation of feedforward compositional models by incorporating global sentence context.  
- Quality: strong empirical performance reported, outperforming prior systems on a competitive public dataset.  
- Uses hybrid word vectors (unsupervised + supervised) and dropout as training innovations for RNNs.  
- Clarity of contribution versus Irsoy & Cardie (2012) Bidirectional RNN is unclear and needs a precise architectural/theoretical comparison.  
- Unclear whether performance gains come from GB-RNN architecture itself or from hybrid vectors and dropout.  
- Experimental fairness concerns: need to confirm whether baselines used the same hybrid vectors and dropout.  
- Lacks detail on hyperparameter tuning, model selection, and how the "single best model" was chosen; ensemble use should be contextualized.  
- Training section (3.5) is too brief; should explicitly state backpropagation through structure and cite Goller & Küchler (1996).  
- Needs a deeper discussion of training dynamics and challenges given the effectively very deep network from the feedbackward unfolding.  
- The term "Global Belief" may be misleading for readers familiar with belief propagation; a descriptive name like "Forward-Backward Recursive Network" is suggested.  
- Task description (Section 4.1) needs more detail and should explicitly state it addresses SemEval 2013 Task 2 contextual polarity.  
- Reference to Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete in the draft and must be fixed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant limitation, proposes an elegant model with strong empirical results, and provides useful hybrid word vector contributions.  
- Arguments for rejection: baseline comparisons may be unfair, key methodological and related-work details are insufficient, and gains are not clearly disentangled from representation/training improvements.
