

This paper proposes leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder for sentiment classification, labeling this approach "belief propagation." However, this terminology is misleading, as the method does not constitute an instance of belief propagation inference, creating unnecessary confusion for readers. While the paper is well-written, the motivation for the proposed model remains vague and lacks clear theoretical grounding.  

The primary weakness lies in the evaluation methodology. Presenting ensemble results is unjustified, as none of the benchmarks in the study are ensembles, thereby obscuring the findings. The composition of the compared models is also ambiguous. Key clarifications are required: how were the models tuned and the ensemble/best models selected? How were the benchmarks in Table 2 tuned? Were these benchmarks using the same augmented data as the BP-RNN? Did the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models also employ hybrid word vectors? Without this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears to be the most directly comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 for assessing the BP-RNN architecture.  

Minor points:  
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweet processing is surprising; its accuracy on this dataset warrants clarification.  
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie (as well as several others) is incomplete.  

Overall, the paper introduces a recursive neural network model for phrasal sentiment classification in context. The results are reasonable, but the model lacks a compelling motivation, and the experimental methodology requires greater transparency.
