This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending feedforward recursive neural networks with a backward pass during inference. This allows for phrase-level predictions to influence word-level representations, addressing the challenge of context-dependent sentiment analysis. The authors demonstrate improvements on contextual sentiment classification tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated. The core idea of incorporating a feedback mechanism into recursive neural networks is a logical extension of previous work, particularly Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks [Irsoy & Cardie, 2015] (note: the original citation in the paper is incomplete). A key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised learning. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating performance gains over standard RNNs and other baselines. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Contextual sentiment analysis is a challenging problem, and the ability to disambiguate sentiment based on broader context is crucial for many NLP applications.
*   **Novel approach:** The GB-RNN architecture offers a unique approach to incorporating context, differing from traditional recurrent or window-based methods by allowing long-range dependencies to influence local predictions.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements on the SemEval 2013 task and outperforms the competition winner, suggesting the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Hybrid word vectors:** The use of combined unsupervised and supervised word vectors appears to be a valuable contribution, improving performance compared to using either alone.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Comparison:** The experimental comparisons lack sufficient detail. It is unclear whether the RNN and Bidirectional RNN baselines also utilized the hybrid word vectors. The selection process for the “best” models should be clarified, including cross-validation strategies and hyperparameter tuning details. The use of an ensemble in the GB-RNN results, while effective, creates an uneven comparison with single-model baselines.
*   **Terminology:** The term "belief propagation" may be misleading. "Forward-backward" or a similar term might be more descriptive of the model’s mechanism.
*   **Training Details:** Section 3.5, detailing the training process, is somewhat brief. A more thorough description of the training algorithm (likely backpropagation through structure – Goller & Küchler, 1996, should be cited) and the implications of unfolding the recursive structure into a deep network would be beneficial. Addressing potential challenges related to vanishing/exploding gradients in such a deep network would be valuable.
*   **Model Comparison:** The distinction between the proposed model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie (2015) requires further clarification. A more precise theoretical comparison, alongside a detailed discussion of architectural differences and their potential impact, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Task Description:** Section 4.1 could benefit from a more accessible description of the SemEval 2013 task, particularly for readers less familiar with the NLP community. Specifying the exact subtask addressed (likely contextual polarity) and explaining the parser constraints would improve understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to contextual sentiment analysis.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear performance improvement over existing methods.
*   The introduction of hybrid word vectors is a valuable contribution.
*   The paper addresses a significant problem in natural language processing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental comparisons lack sufficient detail, making it difficult to fully assess the significance of the results.
*   The connection to and differentiation from existing work, particularly [Irsoy & Cardie, 2015], requires further clarification.
*   The training details are somewhat limited, and a more thorough discussion of the challenges associated with backpropagation through structure would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of recursive neural networks and contextual sentiment analysis. While some aspects require further clarification and detail, the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the potential impact of the work suggest that it is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding experimental details and model comparison.

**References:**

*   Irsoy, O., & Cardie, C. (2015). Bidirectional recursive neural networks for token-level labeling with structure. *Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics, 3*, 189–201.
