This paper introduces Belief Propagation Recursive Neural Networks (BP-RNNs), which extend conventional feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks by incorporating a backward (top-down) pass during inference. This mechanism allows phrase-level predictions and representations to refine the embeddings and labels at the word level. The paper is generally well-written, and the proposed model is clearly motivated and described. The core idea of incorporating a top-down refinement step is intuitive and presents an interesting direction for recursive architectures.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed to strengthen its contribution.

**Clarity of Contribution and Relation to Prior Work**
The main concern is the clarity of the paper's novelty, particularly in relation to the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Network (Bi-RNN) by Irsoy and Cardie (2008). Section 3.6, which aims to clarify this relationship, is confusing and does not adequately distinguish the two models. The authors should explicitly and precisely delineate the novel aspects of their proposed architecture. For instance, is the introduction of hybrid word vectors the primary contribution? A clearer distinction from prior work is essential to properly situate the paper's contribution.

**Experimental Fairness and Rigor**
The experimental evaluation raises several questions about the fairness and transparency of the comparisons:
*   **Use of Hybrid Vectors:** It is not specified whether the baseline models (RNN and Bi-RNN) also used the proposed hybrid word vectors. For a direct and fair comparison of the architectures, all models should use the same input features, or the impact of this feature engineering choice should be separately ablated.
*   **Model Tuning and Selection:** The paper lacks detail on the hyperparameter tuning and model selection procedures for both the proposed model and the baselines. The process for selecting the "single best" model for comparison is not described, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.
*   **Ensemble vs. Single Model:** The results for the BP-RNN appear to be from an ensemble of models, whereas the baseline methods are presented as single models. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison. To provide a more accurate assessment, the authors should compare their single best model against the single best baseline models, and separately compare their ensemble against other ensemble-based methods if applicable.

**Clarity of Model Description and Terminology**
*   **Terminology:** The term "Belief Propagation" may be misleading, as it has a specific, well-established meaning in the context of graphical models that differs from the mechanism described here. A term such as "Forward-Backward" or "Bottom-Up/Top-Down" inference might describe the model's operation more accurately and avoid confusion.
*   **Training Details:** Section 3.5 (Training) is too brief. It is presumed that training relies on an algorithm like Backpropagation Through Structure (a citation to Goller & Küchler, 1996 would be appropriate). This process involves unfolding the recursive structure, which, given the top-down pass, would result in a very deep network to update the word-level parameters. The authors should provide more details on this critical step, including how gradients are propagated and whether any specific techniques were needed to manage potential issues like vanishing or exploding gradients.

**Presentation of Experimental Setup**
Section 4.1, which describes the experimental task, needs to be improved for a broader audience outside the core NLP community. For example, the paper should explicitly state which of the two Semeval 2013 subtasks is being addressed (presumably the contextual polarity task). A clearer presentation of the task would also help readers better understand the motivation for the parser constraints mentioned.

**Summary and Suggestions**
In summary, this paper presents an interesting and intuitive extension to recursive neural networks. However, its contribution is currently undermined by a lack of clarity regarding its novelty and several methodological concerns in the experimental evaluation. Addressing the points raised above—particularly clarifying the contributions over prior work and ensuring fair experimental comparisons—would substantially improve the quality and impact of this work.

**Minor Suggestions:**
*   Consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4 for a more concise presentation of the results.
*   The reference to Irsoy and Cardie (2008) appears incomplete in the bibliography and should be corrected.
