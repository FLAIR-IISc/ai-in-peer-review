In NLP, recursive neural networks (RNNs) are used to derive representations of text spans by recursively composing pairs of word/shorter-span representations. The process starts from word embeddings and proceeds up a given parse tree until the full-span representation is formed at the root. The one-way nature of this pipeline, however, prevents information from flowing down the tree from larger to smaller contexts. This paper proposes a bidirectional variant in which an upward pass is followed by a downward pass, enriching every node’s representation with information from broader contexts. The resulting system attains state-of-the-art performance on SemEval 2012 Task 2.

The extension of the established RNN approach is simple, elegant, and effective. While the overarching idea resembles that of [18], the downward computation here differs, and the resulting performance appears superior.

The method is well-motivated and clearly presented, and the paper is generally well written. The empirical results look excellent, but I have some concerns about the experimental setup. For the alternative bidirectional RNN from [18] reported in Table 2, were dropout and the additional training data used? Were hybrid vectors also employed in that comparison? Also, although the ensemble result is impressive, it does not really belong in a table intended to compare individual models.

What exactly does the “widely used mix of both” refer to in Section 4.4? A well-executed paper built on a simple and elegant idea.
