

This paper introduces "belief propagation recursive neural networks," extending feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks with a backward (top-down) inference step. This enables phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels. While the core idea builds on Irsoy and Cardie's Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks (2008), the reference to their work is incomplete and requires correction.

The paper is well-written and the model is well-motivated. A key contribution is the introduction of hybrid word vectors, which appears central to the approach. However, the experimental comparisons lack fairness: it is unclear whether the RNN and B-RNN baselines also employed hybrid vectors. Furthermore, the model selection process requires clarification—specifically, how "best" models were tuned, selected, and why a single model was chosen over an ensemble (unlike competing methods). These details are essential for reproducibility and validity.

The term "belief propagation" is misleading. Given the model's forward-backward structure, "forward-backward" would be a more accurate and standard term. Section 3.5 (Training) is insufficiently detailed. The authors should explicitly describe the training algorithm, noting that backpropagation through structure (Goller & Kümmel, 1996) is likely used. This implies the recursive model is unfolded, creating a deeply structured network where word embeddings are updated only after traversing the entire structure. Clarifying this mechanism is critical, as it directly impacts training efficiency and implementation.

Section 3.6 requires significant revision to distinguish this work from Irsoy and Cardie (2008). The relationship and novel contributions relative to prior bidirectional models are currently ambiguous. Section 4.1 also needs improvement for broader accessibility. The Semeval 2013 task description should explicitly state that only the *contextual polarity* subtask was addressed (not the message polarity subtask), and the parser constraints should be clearly explained for non-NLP readers. A concise task overview would greatly enhance clarity.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could be logically merged to streamline the presentation of results and analysis. 

Overall, the paper presents a meaningful extension to bidirectional recursive networks with potential value for structured prediction tasks. However, key methodological details are missing, the experimental evaluation lacks necessary rigor, and several sections require substantial revision for clarity and precision. Addressing the points above—particularly the training procedure, model selection, experimental fairness, and task description—would significantly strengthen the contribution. The core idea is promising, but the current presentation does not fully convey its novelty or robustness.
