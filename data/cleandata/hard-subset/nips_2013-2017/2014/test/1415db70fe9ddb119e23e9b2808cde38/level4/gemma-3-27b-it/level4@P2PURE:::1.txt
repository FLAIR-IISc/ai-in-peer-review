This paper introduces “belief propagation recursive neural networks,” which extend traditional feedforward recursive networks by incorporating a backward (top-down) pass during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels.

The paper is generally well-written, and the model is well-motivated and clearly described. It builds upon the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008) – though the citation to this work appears incomplete. A key contribution is the introduction of hybrid word vectors, and it is possible this is the most significant distinction from prior work. However, the experimental comparisons raise concerns regarding fairness. Specifically, it is unclear whether the RNN and B-RNN baselines also utilized hybrid vectors, and details regarding hyperparameter tuning and model selection – including the criteria for selecting the single “best” model – are missing. The lack of ensembling in the competing methods should also be acknowledged. These points require clarification. 

The use of the term “belief propagation” may be misleading; “forward-backward” might be a more appropriate descriptor. Section 3.5 (Training) is somewhat concise and could benefit from additional detail. It is likely that back-propagation through structure (as described in Goller, 1996) is employed, which would necessitate unfolding the recursive model, effectively creating a very deep network for gradient propagation to the word embedding layer. The authors should elaborate on this crucial training step, if this understanding is correct.

Section 3.6 is currently confusing, and a clearer delineation between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie is needed. Section 4.1 would benefit from improved clarity for readers outside of the natural language processing community. Regarding SemEval 2013 Task 2, it is important to specify whether the reported results pertain to the contextual or message polarity subtask. A more detailed task description would also illuminate the impact of parser constraints.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could potentially be combined for a more streamlined presentation. Overall, this is a well-written paper that describes an interesting and promising variant of the bidirectional recursive model.
