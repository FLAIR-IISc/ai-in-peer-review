- Introduces GB-RNN: a recursive NN with a top-down "feedbackward" pass to condition word/phrase representations on global sentence context.  
- Proposes hybrid word vectors (pre-trained unsupervised + task-specific supervised) and shows dropout improves training.  
- Reports state-of-the-art performance on SemEval 2013 sentiment task.  
- Strength: Tackles context-dependent sentiment classification that standard feedforward models struggle with.  
- Strength: GB-RNN’s feedbackward pass is a novel, intuitive extension to standard RNNs.  
- Strength: Empirical results appear strong, reportedly outperforming the competition winner.  
- Weakness: Motivation is vague and lacks theoretical justification for specific architectural choices (e.g., Equation 3).  
- Weakness: Experimental methodology is insufficiently described, making claims hard to assess.  
- Weakness: Main result is an ensemble of five GB-RNNs compared against single-model baselines—an unfair comparison that obscures single-model gains.  
- Weakness: Unclear whether baselines in Table 2 were configured and trained under the same conditions as GB-RNN.  
- Question: How were hyperparameters tuned across all models and were they optimized on the same development set?  
- Question: Did baseline models also use the hybrid word vectors and dropout scheme used by GB-RNN?  
- Question: Were baselines trained on the same augmented dataset (NRC lexicon, noisy tweets, movie reviews) as GB-RNN?  
- Minor: The term "Global Belief" may be misleading since the method is not belief propagation.  
- Minor: Using an off-the-shelf parser for Twitter is surprising; parser accuracy on that domain should be discussed.  
- Minor: Several references are incomplete (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie, 2013).  
- Conclusion: Novel and interesting architecture but undermined by unclear motivation and non-transparent experiments; needs direct single-model comparisons to identically configured baselines.
