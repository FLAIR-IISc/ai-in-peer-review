This paper proposes using both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors of a recursive neural network auto-encoder in a sentiment classification task. The authors refer to this approach as "belief propagation," although it does not constitute an instance of this inference algorithm, and as such, this name may confuse the reader. In general, the paper is well-written, but the motivation for the proposed model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification.

The key weakness of this paper lies in its evaluation. Presenting an ensemble result in the context of this evaluation is unnecessary, as none of the benchmarks are ensembles, which obfuscates the results. Furthermore, the exact composition of the models compared is unclear. Several key questions remain unanswered: How were the models tuned, and how were the ensemble/best models selected? How were the benchmarks in Table 2 tuned? Were they using the same augmented data as the BP-RNN? Did the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN also utilize hybrid word vectors? 

In the absence of this information, I would speculate that the second entry in Table 3 is the most comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when assessing the BP-RNN architecture.

Some minor points are worth noting. I am surprised that an off-the-shelf parser was used to parse tweets, and I would be interested in knowing its accuracy on this data. Additionally, the Irsoy and Cardie citation is incomplete, as are several others.

This paper presents a recursive neural network model for classifying phrasal sentiment in context. While the results appear reasonable, the model lacks a strong motivation, and the experimental methodology is not entirely clear.
