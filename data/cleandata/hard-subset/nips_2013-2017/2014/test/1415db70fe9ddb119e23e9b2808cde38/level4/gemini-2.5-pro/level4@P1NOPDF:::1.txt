This paper introduces "Belief Propagation Recursive Neural Networks," which extend standard feedforward (bottom-up) recursive networks by incorporating a backward (top-down) step during inference. This mechanism allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to, and thereby refine, the initial word-level embeddings and labels.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed model is clearly motivated and described. The core idea of incorporating top-down feedback to refine bottom-up representations is compelling. However, several key areas require clarification and improvement to strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Novelty and Comparison to Prior Work:** The model is presented as an extension of the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008). However, the paper needs to more clearly articulate its precise novel contributions. For instance, a significant contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vectors. Is this the primary difference from previous work? Section 3.6, which compares the proposed model to that of Irsoy and Cardie, is somewhat confusing and should be revised to provide a more direct and explicit contrast. Clarifying these points is essential for understanding the paper's unique contributions. (As a minor note, the reference to Irsoy and Cardie seems to be incomplete in the bibliography.)

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation raises several concerns about the fairness of the comparisons, which must be addressed for the results to be convincing.
    *   **Feature Sets:** Were the baseline RNN and B-RNN models also trained using the proposed hybrid word vectors? If not, the comparison is not on equal footing, as the performance gains might stem from the input features rather than the model architecture itself.
    *   **Model Selection:** The paper should provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning and model selection process. How were the "best" models for each architecture chosen? Furthermore, how was the *single best* model selected for reporting? This information is crucial for reproducibility and for assessing the robustness of the results.
    *   **Use of Ensembles:** The reported results for the proposed method appear to be from an ensemble of models, whereas the baselines are not presented as ensembles. This is not a direct comparison. The authors should either report the performance of their single best model or compare their ensemble against ensembles of the baseline methods.

3.  **Training Details:** Section 3.5 (Training) is too brief and lacks necessary details. It is likely that the model is trained using an algorithm equivalent to back-propagation through structure (a citation to Goller & Küchler, 1996, might be appropriate here). If so, the recursive model is effectively unfolded into a deep network for gradient computation. Given that the top-down pass updates the initial word embeddings at the lowest level, this unfolding would result in a very deep network, which can present significant optimization challenges (e.g., vanishing/exploding gradients). The authors should provide a more thorough description of this critical training step and discuss any associated challenges and solutions.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  **Terminology:** The term "belief propagation" is potentially misleading. In the graphical models community, this term refers to a specific message-passing algorithm. Since the proposed method does not appear to be a direct application of classical belief propagation, a more descriptive term like "forward-backward" or "bidirectional inference" might be more appropriate and less prone to confusion.

2.  **Clarity for a Broader Audience:** Section 4.1 (Experimental Setup) should be improved to be more accessible to readers outside the NLP community. For example, when discussing Task 2 of Semeval 2013, it should be specified which subtask is being addressed (presumably the contextual polarity subtask, not the message polarity subtask). A clearer presentation of the task would also help readers understand the motivation for the parser constraints.

3.  **Structure:** For better flow and readability, the authors could consider merging Sections 4.3 and 4.4, as they both discuss analyses of the model's behavior.

In summary, this paper describes an interesting and well-motivated variant of the bidirectional recursive model. The core idea is promising, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the concerns raised regarding its positioning relative to prior work, the fairness of the experimental comparisons, and the lack of detail in the training procedure.
