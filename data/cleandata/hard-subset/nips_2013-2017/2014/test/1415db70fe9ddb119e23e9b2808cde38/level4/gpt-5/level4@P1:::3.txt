Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), an extension of standard tree-structured recursive neural networks (RNNs) for contextual sentiment classification. The core idea is to augment the usual bottom-up (feedforward) composition with a single top-down pass at inference time that propagates a “feedbackward” vector from the root to all nodes. Each node’s prediction is then made from the concatenation of its bottom-up and top-down vectors. This is intended to incorporate global sentence context when labeling phrases and entities, addressing the failure of purely feedforward models to capture context-dependent sentiment (e.g., directed sentiment toward Android vs. iOS). The paper evaluates on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), reports gains over a standard RNN and a bidirectional-recursive baseline, and attains state-of-the-art results when using an ensemble. Additional contributions include: (i) a hybrid word representation that concatenates fixed, unsupervised embeddings with small supervised embeddings fine-tuned on the task, and (ii) the use of dropout to improve training with high-dimensional embeddings.

The approach relates to earlier RNN compositional models (Socher et al.) and to bidirectional recurrent/recursive models (e.g., Schuster & Paliwal; Irsoy & Cardie). The top-down pass resembles a decoder over the parse tree and is conceptually close to bidirectional recursive models developed in parallel. The paper also references unfolding in recursive autoencoders but applies the top-down computation at inference time for node prediction rather than solely for training.

Strengths:
- Addresses a real limitation of feedforward recursive models by incorporating global context into node-level predictions.
- Simple architectural change with minimal overhead and clear implementation: one added top-down pass, concatenation, and a single softmax per node.
- Empirical gains over a standard RNN and a bidirectional-recursive baseline on SemEval, with additional gains from the hybrid embeddings and dropout.
- Clear description of the hybrid embedding strategy and evidence that it outperforms purely supervised or purely unsupervised embeddings.
- Practical focus on entity-/aspect-level sentiment in noisy social media text.

Weaknesses and questions (mainly about evaluation transparency and motivation):
- Terminology: calling the approach “global belief” suggests a connection to the belief propagation algorithm in graphical models, which the proposed inference is not. This naming risks confusing readers.
- Motivation is presented largely via examples; a clearer theoretical justification for why a single top-down pass should systematically improve local predictions (beyond bidirectional recursion) would strengthen the paper.
- Evaluation setup lacks clarity on comparability:
  - Ensembles: Reporting an ensemble as the headline result is not directly comparable to single-model baselines from SemEval and obscures the contribution of the architecture. Please report single-model GB-RNN results as primary and reserve ensemble numbers as supplementary.
  - Tuning protocol: How were hyperparameters tuned for the compared models (RNN, bidirectional RNN, GB-RNN)? Were the “best” and “ensemble” models chosen based on dev F1? What random seeds/variance were observed?
  - Resource parity: Did the baselines in Table 2 (RNN and bidirectional RNN) use the same hybrid word vectors and the same augmented resources (NRC lexicon examples, distant supervision tweets, movie reviews)? Were SemEval leaderboard systems in Table 1 allowed the same external data you used?
  - From the current text, the single-model setting with hybrid embeddings (the second entry in Table 3) appears to be the fairest comparison to Tables 1 and 2. If so, please make this explicit and highlight that result.
- Fairness of parsing: An off-the-shelf parser is used on tweets. Please report parser accuracy or at least error rates on this domain, and whether constraints to keep labeled phrases within subtrees introduce bias or errors that favor tree-structured models.
- Related work citation quality: Several references (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) are incomplete; please provide full citations.

Quality:
- The model is technically sound and clearly specified. Training uses backpropagation through structure with AdaGrad and dropout. However, the experimental methodology needs clearer documentation of tuning, data usage, and comparability. Additional ablations (e.g., removing the V transform, varying n vs. nd, using only top-down vectors, or comparing multiple top-down steps) would help isolate the contribution of each component.

Clarity:
- The writing is generally clear. I recommend avoiding the term “belief propagation” and instead using “top-down contextual pass” or “global context feedback.” Please clarify the evaluation protocol, resource usage, and ensemble composition, and fix incomplete citations.

Originality:
- The top-down pass over a parse tree for node labeling is closely related to concurrent bidirectional recursive models. The paper differentiates GB-RNN via the specific backward transformation and parameterization. The hybrid embedding approach is a pragmatic combination of known ideas. The overall combination is a reasonable, incremental contribution.

Significance:
- Context-sensitive phrase labeling is important, and the reported improvements on SemEval indicate practical value. The contribution is likely to be used if results are shown to be robust under fair, single-model comparisons with transparent tuning and resource parity.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a real weakness of feedforward RNNs with a simple, effective architectural change.
- Strong results on a standard benchmark; hybrid embeddings and dropout are effective.
- Clear model description and relation to prior work.

Cons for acceptance:
- Terminology may mislead; motivation lacks deeper theoretical grounding.
- Evaluation methodology needs clearer, fair comparisons; ensemble reporting obscures the core contribution.
- Incomplete citations and limited analysis of parsing reliability on tweets.

Minor comments:
- Please report parser performance on tweets and discuss any preprocessing decisions (e.g., negation tokenization) that may affect tree structures.
- Provide full and correct citations for all related work, including Irsoy & Cardie.
- Consider adding an error analysis and examples where the top-down pass changes predictions, compared to the standard RNN and the bidirectional-recursive baseline.

Overall:
This paper presents a recursive neural network with a top-down contextual pass for phrasal sentiment in context. The results are promising, but the motivation could be sharpened and, more importantly, the experimental methodology should be clarified to ensure fair, single-model comparisons with transparent tuning and resource parity. I encourage the authors to revise accordingly.
