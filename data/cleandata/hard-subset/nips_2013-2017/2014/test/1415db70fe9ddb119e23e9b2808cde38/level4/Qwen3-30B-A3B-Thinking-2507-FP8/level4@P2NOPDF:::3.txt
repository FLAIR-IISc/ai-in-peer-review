

This paper proposes leveraging both encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network autoencoder for sentiment classification, terming the approach "belief propagation." This terminology is misleading, as the method does not implement belief propagation—a well-established inference algorithm—and risks confusing readers. While the paper is well-written, its core motivation lacks theoretical grounding; the authors fail to justify why combining these vectors improves sentiment classification beyond empirical observation.  

The primary weakness lies in the evaluation methodology. Presenting ensemble results (e.g., the second entry in Table 3) is inappropriate, as all benchmarks (Tables 1–2) report single-model performance. This obscures whether the BP-RNN architecture itself drives gains or if ensembling artifacts dominate results. Critical details are missing:  
- How were models tuned, and how were ensemble/best models selected?  
- Were benchmarks in Table 2 tuned using identical data augmentation as BP-RNN?  
- Did RNN and Bidirectional-RNN baselines employ hybrid word vectors?  
Without these, the second entry in Table 3 (reporting BP-RNN with ensembling) cannot be meaningfully compared to Tables 1–2. The most comparable baseline appears to be the BP-RNN-only result (Table 3, first entry), but this remains ambiguous due to insufficient methodological transparency.  

Minor concerns include:  
1. **Parser choice**: Using an off-the-shelf parser for tweet sentiment analysis is surprising. The authors should report its accuracy on this domain to justify its suitability.  
2. **Citations**: Several references (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie, 2014) are incomplete, undermining reproducibility.  

The paper’s core contribution—a recursive neural network for phrasal sentiment classification—yields plausible results. However, the lack of theoretical motivation, ambiguous evaluation, and unresolved methodological gaps prevent a strong endorsement. The proposed "belief propagation" framework is neither novel nor rigorously justified, and the experimental design fails to isolate the model’s true contribution. For the work to be impactful, the authors must clarify the theoretical rationale, eliminate ensemble obfuscation, and provide full methodological details. Until then, the results remain difficult to interpret or replicate.
