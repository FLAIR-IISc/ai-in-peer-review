

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward step during inference to better handle context-dependent sentiment classification. The authors argue that standard RNNs cannot correctly predict phrase or word labels determined by context, as demonstrated in examples like "Android beats iOS," where the sentiment toward specific entities depends on the global sentence structure. The GB-RNN model computes both forward (bottom-up) and feedbackward (top-down) vectors, allowing phrase representations to incorporate global context before making predictions. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 sentiment analysis task, with a 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs.

The paper builds upon prior work in recursive neural networks for NLP, including Socher et al.'s sentiment treebank work [26] and bidirectional recursive models [12, 14]. The authors' key innovation lies in the feedbackward propagation mechanism, which differs from existing approaches by using both forward and backward parent node information when computing backward vectors. The paper also introduces a hybrid word vector representation that combines unsupervised and supervised components, showing this approach outperforms using either alone.

Strengths of the paper include clear technical descriptions of the model architecture, thorough experimental evaluation on a standard sentiment analysis benchmark, and the demonstration that dropout improves training for high-dimensional word vectors. The model's ability to handle context beyond local windows is particularly valuable for sentiment analysis tasks where sentiment depends on global sentence structure.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. The most critical issue concerns the evaluation methodology. The authors present ensemble results without clarifying how the ensemble was formed or how the individual models were selected. This is problematic because the SemEval 2013 competition results presented in Table 1 do not use ensemble methods, making it difficult to assess the true performance gain of the proposed approach. The paper also fails to specify whether the baseline models in Table 2 were trained with the same hybrid word vectors, dropout, and augmented training data as the GB-RNN, which would significantly impact comparability.

The paper's naming convention is confusing: the authors refer to their approach as "global belief" but describe a process that bears no relation to standard belief propagation algorithms. This misleading terminology creates unnecessary confusion for readers unfamiliar with the paper's specific usage.

The motivation for the feedbackward step lacks a strong theoretical foundation. The paper claims that "the meaning of words and phrases can also change once the sentence context is observed," but it doesn't provide a formal argument or empirical evidence to support why the specific feedback mechanism they propose is necessary or optimal for this problem.

Minor issues include:
1. The use of an off-the-shelf parser on Twitter data without reporting parsing accuracy or addressing the challenges of parsing social media text
2. Incomplete citations (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie [12] is cited without full publication details)
3. The claim that "the feedbackward step improves F1 performance by 3% over the standard RNN" appears to be based on a single model configuration, without reporting results across multiple configurations or statistical significance tests

The paper's contributions are somewhat incremental to existing work on contextualized language representations. While the feedbackward mechanism is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how it differs from or improves upon related approaches like bidirectional RNNs [12, 14] beyond the empirical results. The theoretical comparison in Section 3.6 is helpful but could be strengthened with more detailed analysis of the parameter efficiency trade-offs.

Pro:
- Clear description of the GB-RNN architecture and its relationship to existing recursive neural network models
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on a standard benchmark dataset
- Demonstration that hybrid word vectors and dropout improve performance
- State-of-the-art results on SemEval 2013 task

Con:
- Unclear evaluation methodology, particularly regarding ensemble results and baseline comparisons
- Misleading terminology ("global belief" instead of a more standard term)
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the proposed model
- Lack of clarity about experimental details (word vector initialization, hyperparameter tuning)
- Incomplete citations and minor technical inaccuracies

Overall, the paper presents a potentially useful extension to recursive neural networks for contextual sentiment analysis, but the evaluation methodology and presentation are problematic. The authors should clarify their experimental setup and provide a more precise theoretical justification for their approach before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS.
