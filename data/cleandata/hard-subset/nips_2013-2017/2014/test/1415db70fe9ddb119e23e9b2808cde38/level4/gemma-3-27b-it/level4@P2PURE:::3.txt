This paper introduces a method for sentiment classification that leverages both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The authors term this approach “belief propagation,” a label that is misleading as it does not correspond to the standard inference algorithm and may cause confusion. While generally well-written, the paper’s motivation is unclear and lacks robust theoretical grounding.

A significant weakness lies in the evaluation methodology. Presenting an ensemble result is unwarranted given that the benchmarks are single models, and this practice obscures the performance of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the composition of the compared models is not fully specified. Crucial details requiring clarification include the tuning procedures for all models, the selection criteria for the ensemble and best-performing models, and the tuning process for the benchmarks in Table 2. It is also important to know whether these benchmarks utilized the same data augmentation techniques as the BP-RNN and whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models employed hybrid word vectors. 

Without this information, it is reasonable to assume that the second entry in Table 3 represents the most directly comparable result to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 for evaluating the BP-RNN architecture.

Minor points:
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is notable, and information regarding its accuracy on this specific dataset would be valuable.
- The citation for Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete, as are several others throughout the paper.

In summary, this paper presents a recursive neural network model for contextual phrasal sentiment classification with promising results. However, the model’s motivation remains weak and the experimental methodology requires further clarification.
