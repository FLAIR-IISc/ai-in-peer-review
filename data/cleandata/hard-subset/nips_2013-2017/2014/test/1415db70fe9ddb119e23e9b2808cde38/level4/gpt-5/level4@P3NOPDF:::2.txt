This paper proposes a bidirectional extension to recursive neural networks (tree-structured RNNs) for compositional semantics. Standard recursive models compute node representations bottom-up along a parse tree, combining smaller constituents into larger ones until the root is reached. While effective, this unidirectional flow prevents information from larger contexts from influencing representations of intermediate or terminal nodes. The authors address this by adding a downward pass after the upward computation, allowing contextual signals from higher-level constituents to propagate back down the tree. On SemEval 2012 Task 2, the method achieves state-of-the-art performance.

The contribution is simple, elegant, and well motivated. The idea of augmenting bottom-up tree composition with a top-down pass is natural, yet the authors’ specific realization differs from prior work (notably [18]) in how the downward computations are defined. The empirical results suggest the proposed design is more effective than that alternative, delivering strong gains.

The paper is clearly written and the method is presented in a way that makes both the intuition and implementation easy to follow. The experiments appear thorough and the reported numbers are excellent. That said, I have several concerns and requests for clarification regarding the experimental setup and comparisons:

- Fairness of comparisons to [18]. In Table 2, were the results attributed to the alternative bidirectional model from [18] obtained under the same training regime as your model? Specifically:
  - Did that baseline also use dropout?
  - Was the same additional training data included?
  - Were “hybrid vectors” (as used in your best systems) employed for the [18] variant? If so, please specify exactly how they were constructed and integrated; if not, please provide a matched comparison where both models have access to the same features and regularization.

- Ensembles in model comparison tables. The ensemble achieves impressive performance, but mixing ensemble results with single-model results can blur the comparison. I recommend moving ensemble results to a separate row or table, or at least explicitly marking them as such and ensuring that all competing ensemble baselines (if any) are also included.

- Clarification on “widely used mix of both” (Section 4.4). Please define precisely what is being mixed. If this refers to combining different types of embeddings (e.g., pretrained and task-specific, or word- and phrase-level), or mixing supervision signals, specify the components, the mixing mechanism (e.g., concatenation, gating, interpolation), and the proportion/weights used. A brief ablation comparing pure variants versus the mixed configuration would strengthen this section.

- Reporting details. To help assess robustness, please report:
  - Whether results are averaged over multiple random initializations, along with standard deviations.
  - The computational overhead of the downward pass (training and inference time), relative to the baseline upward-only model.
  - Any sensitivity to parse quality (e.g., performance with different parsers or on noisier parses).

- Additional ablations. While the main comparisons are strong, an explicit ablation isolating the impact of the downward pass (same architecture and hyperparameters, with/without the top-down component) would underscore the core contribution.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper based on a simple and powerful idea: enriching tree-structured composition with top-down context. The method is clearly articulated, the motivation is compelling, and the empirical results are strong. Addressing the questions about training parity with [18], clarifying the role of dropout, additional data, and hybrid vectors, and disentangling ensemble versus single-model comparisons will further solidify the claims. With these clarifications, I believe the paper makes a valuable and timely contribution to compositional modeling in NLP.
