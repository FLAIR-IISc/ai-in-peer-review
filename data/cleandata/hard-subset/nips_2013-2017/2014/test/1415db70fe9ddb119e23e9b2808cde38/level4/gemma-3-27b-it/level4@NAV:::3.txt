This paper introduces a method for sentiment classification leveraging both the encoding and decoding vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The authors term this approach “belief propagation,” a label which is misleading as it does not correspond to the standard inference algorithm. While generally well-written, the paper’s motivation remains unclear and lacks robust theoretical grounding.

A significant limitation lies in the evaluation strategy. The inclusion of an ensemble result is difficult to interpret given that the comparative benchmarks are single models, and this choice obscures the presented performance. Further clarification is needed regarding the experimental setup: specifically, the hyperparameter tuning process and the criteria used for selecting the ensemble/best performing models. Details regarding the tuning of the benchmarks reported in Table 2 are also necessary. It is unclear if these benchmarks utilized the same data augmentation techniques as the BP-RNN, and whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models also employed hybrid word vectors. 

Without this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears to offer the most direct comparison to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when evaluating the core BP-RNN architecture.

Minor concerns include:
- The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets raises questions about parsing accuracy on this specific dataset, and a discussion of this would be beneficial.
- Several citations, including Irsoy and Cardie, are incomplete.

In summary, this paper presents a recursive neural network approach to phrasal sentiment classification. The results are promising, but the model's rationale could be strengthened and the experimental methodology requires further clarification.
