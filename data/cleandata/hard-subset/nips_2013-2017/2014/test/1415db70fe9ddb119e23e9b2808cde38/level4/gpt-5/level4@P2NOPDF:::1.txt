This paper introduces “belief propagation recursive neural networks,” which extend bottom-up (feedforward) recursive networks with a top-down step at inference time. This enables phrase-level predictions and embeddings to influence word-level embeddings and labels.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, the model is well motivated, and the approach is described in sufficient high-level detail. It extends the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks proposed by Irsoy and Cardie (the citation appears incomplete). One notable contribution is the use of hybrid word vectors; this may be the primary distinction from prior work, but this should be made explicit.

However, several aspects of the experimental setup and presentation need clarification to ensure fair comparisons and reproducibility:
- Did the RNN and BRNN baselines also use hybrid word vectors?
- How were hyperparameters tuned and model variants selected for all methods, including baselines?
- How was the “single best” model chosen from among candidates?
- Your approach appears to use an ensemble, whereas competing methods do not. Please clarify this discrepancy and, if possible, report single-model results for a fair comparison.

The term “belief propagation” is potentially misleading here, as the method does not implement probabilistic message passing in the classical sense. A term like “forward–backward” or “top-down/bottom-up recursive network” might be more appropriate.

Section 3.5 (Training) is too brief and should include more implementation details. It seems likely that training uses backpropagation through structure (e.g., Goller, 1996), which entails unfolding the recursive model. In that case, gradients must propagate through a very deep computation graph to reach and update the word embeddings. Please describe:
- How the unfolding is performed,
- Any gradient stabilization strategies (e.g., clipping, normalization),
- Initialization choices,
- Computational considerations (time/memory),
- And whether training differs between the bottom-up and top-down passes.

Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing; the differences between your model and the approach of Irsoy and Cardie should be clearly articulated, including architectural, training, and inference distinctions.

Section 4.1 should be made more accessible to readers outside NLP. SemEval 2013 Task 2 includes two subtasks (contextual polarity and message polarity). It appears you address contextual polarity; please state this explicitly and provide a concise task description so that the role of parser-induced constraints is understandable.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could likely be merged to streamline the exposition.

In sum, this paper presents an interesting variant of bidirectional recursive models, with a well-motivated top-down inference step and the introduction of hybrid word vectors. With clearer terminology, more thorough training details, and fairer, better-documented comparisons, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
