Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a bidirectional extension of tree-structured recursive neural networks. Standard RNNs compose word representations bottom-up along a parse tree to form phrase and sentence vectors, and then classify nodes locally; they therefore cannot adjust word/phrase predictions using sentence-level context. GB-RNN augments the bottom-up pass with a top-down “global belief” pass during inference: a transformed root vector is propagated down the tree to produce feedbackward vectors at every node, which are then concatenated with the upward vectors for classification. This enables context-sensitive predictions (e.g., entity- or aspect-targeted sentiment).

The model is evaluated on contextual sentiment classification using the SemEval 2013 Task 2 Twitter/SMS dataset, achieving state-of-the-art F1 and outperforming both a standard RNN and a bidirectional-recursive baseline. The paper also reports two complementary contributions: (i) applying dropout to improve training stability and generalization in recursive models; and (ii) “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed unsupervised embeddings with small supervised, task-specific vectors.

The approach is closely related to prior bidirectional recurrent/recursive models (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie; Le & Zuidema), but differs in the specific top-down computation: GB-RNN’s downward vector for a child depends on both the forward and backward parent, with a parameterization that allows different dimensionalities for upward and downward vectors. The paper positions these differences and reports empirical gains.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for context-sensitive node labeling in trees, with concrete examples where sentence-level context disambiguates local sentiment targets.
- Simple, elegant architectural extension (single top-down pass) that integrates naturally with existing RNNs and classifiers.
- Strong empirical results on SemEval 2013 Task 2, including consistent improvements over a standard RNN and a bidirectional-recursive baseline; the ensemble sets a new state-of-the-art.
- Sensible training innovations: dropout in tree models and hybrid word vectors that mitigate overfitting while retaining task specificity.
- Good discussion of model capacity and parameterization vs. related bidirectional-recursive models.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Experimental controls and fairness: It is unclear whether the bidirectional-recursive baseline (e.g., Irsoy & Cardie) was trained with exactly the same recipe as GB-RNN (dropout, hybrid vectors, additional training data/lexicon, and two-stage pretraining). Please clarify whether Table 2 uses identical settings across models.
- External resources and training schedule: Section 4.4 combines NRC lexicon-derived n-grams, distant supervision tweets, and movie reviews, and uses a two-step training procedure (pretrain standard RNN, then fine-tune GB-RNN). Please detail the exact schedule, data proportions, and whether upward-only pretraining confers an advantage relative to baselines.
- Evaluation details: SemEval scores by averaging positive and negative F1. Please report per-class F1 and statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or approximate randomization) for key comparisons, and separate performance on tweets vs. SMS.
- Parser constraints: You constrain the parser to ensure each labeled phrase forms a subtree. How often is this constraint active, and how does it affect generalization at test time? Please quantify performance with/without this constraint and discuss robustness to parsing errors.
- Ablations: The paper would benefit from additional ablations isolating the top-down pass: (i) vary the downward dimension nd and the presence/absence of the V transform; (ii) remove concatenation at the classifier (use only downward or only upward vectors) to show their respective contributions; (iii) dropout placement (upward only vs. both passes).
- Baselines: While you compare to logistic regression and bigram NB, stronger sequence/tree baselines (e.g., windowed CNN/RNN on the same data) would further strengthen the case that long-range, parse-mediated context is essential.
- Reproducibility: Please state whether code, trained embeddings, and preprocessing (parser settings, subtree constraint) will be released.
- Minor: Section 3.6 contains stray “Correct ...” tweet lines within the equations and several typos (“feedbackward,” “root note,” “shold,” “feedwordard”). Unify terminology (e.g., “top-down” rather than “feedbackward”) and fix formatting.

Quality:
Technically sound and well motivated. The architectural modification is straightforward, the training scheme is reasonable, and empirical results are strong. The main concerns are about experimental control and reporting: ensuring fair comparisons with the bidirectional-recursive baseline and clearly disentangling gains from the model vs. external data and training schedule.

Clarity:
Generally well written and easy to follow, with helpful figures and formulas. Some notational overload (n vs. nd) is justified but could be summarized in a separate notation block. The aforementioned typos/formatting glitches should be corrected. Please clarify the phrase in Section 4.4: what is the “widely used mix of both”? If it refers to combining tweets and SMS or to combining unsupervised and supervised data, please state explicitly.

Originality:
While bidirectional top-down flows in recursive/recurrent architectures have appeared, the specific parameterization—using both forward and backward parent states for the downward computation and decoupling dimensions via V—is a meaningful variant. The hybrid word vector approach is a pragmatic combination rather than a novel representation, but it is well executed.

Significance:
Addressing context-sensitive phrase/word labeling is important in sentiment and beyond (e.g., targeted sentiment, entity-centric analysis, word sense). Demonstrating SOTA on SemEval 2013 with a simple, general method is impactful, and the ideas should transfer to other structured prediction tasks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, elegant idea that is easy to implement and integrates with existing RNNs.
- Strong empirical gains on a standard benchmark; SOTA with an ensemble.
- Useful training practices (dropout in trees, hybrid embeddings) with empirical validation.
- Careful comparison to related bidirectional-recursive work and rationale for design choices.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete clarity on fairness of baseline training (dropout, hybrid vectors, external data).
- Limited ablations isolating the contribution of the top-down pass vs. other factors.
- Some presentation/formatting issues and ambiguous phrasing (Section 4.4).

Detailed questions for authors:
- Were the bidirectional-recursive baseline results in Table 2 obtained with the same dropout probability, hybrid embeddings, additional NRC lexicon data, and two-stage training as GB-RNN?
- Do you apply dropout to both upward and downward vectors? At what locations (node states vs. word embeddings) and rates?
- Please define and quantify “widely used mix of both” in Section 4.4.
- How often does the subtree constraint alter the Stanford parse? What is the performance impact of removing this constraint?
- Can you report per-class F1 (pos/neg/neutral) and significance tests for GB-RNN vs. baselines?

Overall evaluation:
A well-executed paper based on a simple, effective idea that meaningfully advances context-sensitive recursive modeling and achieves strong results. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying the experimental setup and improving the ablation/reporting.
