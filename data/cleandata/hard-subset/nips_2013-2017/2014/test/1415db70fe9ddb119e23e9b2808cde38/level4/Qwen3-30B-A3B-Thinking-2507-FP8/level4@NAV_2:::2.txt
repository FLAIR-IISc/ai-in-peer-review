

The paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard feedforward recursive neural networks (RNNs) with a feedbackward pass during inference to incorporate global sentence context for contextual sentiment analysis. This addresses a key limitation in prior work (e.g., Socher et al., EMNLP 2013; Socher et al., NIPS 2011) where phrase-level predictions could not account for context-dependent sentiment (e.g., "Android" being positive in "Android beats iOS"). GB-RNNs achieve state-of-the-art results on SemEval 2013 Task 2 by propagating root-level representations top-down through the parse tree, enabling phrase predictions to update based on global context. The authors also demonstrate that hybrid unsupervised-supervised word vectors (100 dimensions from unsupervised training + 30 task-specific dimensions) and dropout (p=0.5) improve performance over baselines.

Strengths:  
- **Originality**: The bidirectional extension via feedbackward propagation is novel and effectively solves context-dependent sentiment disambiguation, distinct from prior bidirectional RNNs (Irsoy & Cardie, NIPS 2014) and window-based methods. The model’s ability to leverage context beyond fixed k-neighborhoods is a significant advancement.  
- **Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical motivation (Section 3.1–3.3) and robust empirical validation. The 3% F1 improvement over standard RNNs and outperformance of SemEval 2013 winners (Table 1) are well-supported. The ablation study (Table 2) convincingly isolates the contributions of feedbackward propagation, hybrid vectors, and dropout.  
- **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with precise mathematical formulations (Equations 1–5), intuitive examples (Fig. 2), and clear experimental design (Section 4.1–4.3). The comparison to Irsoy & Cardie’s model (Section 3.6) is particularly thorough.  
- **Significance**: Contextual sentiment analysis is a high-impact task for social media applications (e.g., Twitter), and GB-RNNs provide a scalable, architecture-agnostic solution that advances the state of the art. The model’s generalizability to other NLP tasks (e.g., word-sense disambiguation) further elevates its significance.

Weaknesses:  
- The experimental setup for the bidirectional RNN baseline (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014) in Table 2 lacks critical details: Were dropout, hybrid vectors, and additional training data (Section 4.4) applied to this baseline? Without this, the performance gap cannot be fully attributed to the feedbackward mechanism.  
- The ensemble result (top 5 GB-RNN models) in Table 1 should not be included in the model comparison table, as it conflates model architecture with ensemble techniques. A single-model comparison is more appropriate for assessing the core contribution.  
- The phrase "widely used mix of both" in Section 4.4 is ambiguous (referring to which resources?), and the paper does not clarify whether the hybrid vectors were used in the baseline comparisons.

Pro: Novel context-aware architecture, rigorous validation, clear writing, and strong empirical results.  
Con: Ambiguous baseline details and ensemble inclusion in model comparison.  

The paper makes a high-quality, significant contribution to NLP with clear technical merit. The experimental concerns are minor and resolvable with minor clarifications; they do not undermine the core innovation. Accept.
