

This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), a novel extension of standard recursive neural networks (RNNs) for context-dependent sentiment classification. The key innovation is a "feedbackward" step during inference that propagates global sentence context back to phrase-level representations, enabling the model to correctly identify that "Android" is positive in "Android beats iOS" while "iOS" is neutral. The authors demonstrate that GB-RNNs outperform standard RNNs by 3% F1 on SemEval 2013 Task 2 (surpassing the competition winner), and they introduce a hybrid word vector approach combining unsupervised pre-training with task-specific fine-tuning. The work builds on prior compositional models (e.g., Socher et al. 2011, 2013) and bidirectional RNNs (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014), but distinguishes itself by using a top-down recursive pass rather than window-based context or bidirectional recurrent architectures.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a compelling case for context-dependence in sentiment analysis, clearly illustrating limitations of feedforward RNNs via concrete examples (e.g., Fig. 1). The experimental design is strong, using the SemEval 2013 benchmark with rigorous comparison to multiple baselines, including the competition-winning system. The hybrid word vector approach (combining fixed unsupervised vectors with task-specific trainable components) is well-motivated and empirically validated, showing consistent improvements over pure unsupervised or supervised vectors. The use of dropout for high-dimensional vectors is a practical contribution, and the model achieves state-of-the-art results without heavy reliance on hand-crafted features—a significant advantage over many SemEval entries. The clarity of the model description (Section 3.3) and the effective use of the Stanford Parser for tree construction are notable.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary weakness lies in the *motivation and theoretical justification* for the feedback mechanism. While the paper correctly identifies context-dependence as a problem, it does not establish *why* the specific top-down recursive feedback (vs. alternative approaches like bidirectional RNNs or attention) is theoretically superior. The term "global belief" is misleading—this is not a probabilistic belief propagation algorithm (e.g., as in graphical models), and the naming confuses readers without clarifying the distinction. The authors should explicitly contrast their approach with standard belief propagation and justify the architectural choice.  

The *evaluation methodology requires clarification*. The ensemble results (Table 1, "best model") obscure the core model’s performance, as no other benchmark systems reported ensembles. The tuning procedures for baselines (e.g., RNN, Bidirectional RNN in Table 2) are ambiguous: were they trained with the same hybrid word vectors, dropout, and augmented data (e.g., NRC lexicon)? Section 4.3 states the GB-RNN used "hybrid word vectors with dropout," but it is unclear if this applied to all baselines. If the RNN baseline used purely supervised vectors (as in Table 3), the 5% performance gap is inflated. The authors should explicitly state whether all models used identical hyperparameters and data preprocessing.  

Minor issues include: (1) The parser’s accuracy on noisy Twitter data (Section 4.1) is not reported, raising questions about the reliability of the tree structures; (2) The Irsoy & Cardie citation (Section 3.6) is incomplete; and (3) The claim that "feedbackward" allows context "many words away" is overstated—the model’s context range depends on the parse tree depth, not absolute distance.

**Originality and Significance:**  
The core idea of propagating context via recursive feedback is novel for compositional NLP tasks (though related to unfolding in autoencoders, e.g., Rolfe & LeCun 2013). It directly addresses a well-known limitation in RNN-based sentiment analysis (Socher et al. 2013), and the SemEval results demonstrate clear significance for practitioners. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental given the parallel work on bidirectional RNNs (Irsoy & Cardie, 2014), and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its feedback mechanism from theirs (Section 3.6’s comparison is superficial).

**Recommendation:**  
This is a technically sound and well-executed paper with strong empirical results that advance the state of the art in contextual sentiment analysis. The method is clearly presented, and the hybrid word vector strategy is a valuable contribution. However, the theoretical justification for the feedback mechanism is underdeveloped, and evaluation details are insufficient for full reproducibility. These issues prevent the paper from being a *strong* accept. I recommend **weak accept** with the following revisions:  
1. Replace "global belief" with a more precise term (e.g., "contextual feedback") and clarify its distinction from probabilistic belief propagation.  
2. Explicitly state tuning procedures for all baselines (e.g., "All models used hybrid word vectors, dropout, and identical hyperparameter optimization").  
3. Report parser accuracy on the Twitter dataset.  
4. Strengthen the theoretical argument for the feedback mechanism (e.g., by analyzing its information flow vs. bidirectional RNNs).  

Without these clarifications, the paper’s contribution is slightly overstated, but the empirical results remain compelling. The work is a solid fit for NIPS and would be valuable to the community with minor revisions.
