### **Summary**

This paper addresses a key limitation of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) in natural language processing: their purely feedforward, bottom-up architecture prevents information from the broader sentence context from influencing the representations and labels of constituent words and phrases. To solve this, the authors introduce the Global Belief Recursive Neural Network (GB-RNN), which extends the standard RNN by incorporating a second, "feedbackward" pass. After an initial bottom-up pass computes representations up the parse tree to the root, a top-down pass propagates this global sentence-level information back down to the leaves. The final representation for any given node is a concatenation of its feedforward and feedbackward vectors, allowing for context-dependent predictions.

The authors also show the utility of two additional techniques: the use of dropout to regularize RNN training, and a hybrid word vector representation that concatenates fixed, pre-trained unsupervised vectors with supervised vectors that are fine-tuned for the specific task. The proposed GB-RNN is evaluated on the task of aspect-specific sentiment analysis using the SemEval 2013 dataset, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming the competition winner and several strong baselines, including a standard RNN and a similar bidirectional RNN model from prior work.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is a well-motivated and technically sound extension of existing recursive architectures. The claims of superior performance are substantiated with strong empirical results on a challenging and standard benchmark dataset. The ablation studies and comparisons in Tables 2 and 3 effectively demonstrate the contributions of the key components of the proposed system: the feedbackward pass, the hybrid word vectors, and the use of dropout.

However, the strength of the central claim—that the GB-RNN outperforms the bidirectional RNN of Irsoy and Cardie [12]—depends critically on a fair experimental comparison. While the paper states that the same hybrid vectors and dropout training were used for both models, it is not explicitly clear if the additional training data and the two-stage training procedure described in Section 4.4 were also applied to the bidirectional RNN baseline. Clarification on this point is necessary to fully validate the claimed superiority of the GB-RNN architecture.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for incorporating global context is well-argued with a compelling example in the introduction. The model is described with sufficient mathematical detail, and the comparison to the related bidirectional RNN model [12] in Section 3.6, which highlights the differences in the backward pass computation, is particularly helpful for understanding the paper's specific contribution. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly.

A minor point for improvement would be to provide slightly more detail on the two-stage training procedure mentioned in Section 4.4. For instance, a clearer description of what "training the GB-RNN jointly in this fashion" entails would be beneficial for reproducibility.

#### **Originality**

The paper presents a simple, elegant, and novel approach to integrating top-down contextual information into recursive models. While the high-level idea of a bidirectional, two-pass approach was being explored concurrently by others (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie [12]), the specific mechanism proposed for the feedbackward pass is distinct. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contribution with respect to this related work. The proposed hybrid word vector representation is also a useful, albeit more incremental, contribution that demonstrates a practical way to combine the benefits of unsupervised pre-training and task-specific fine-tuning.

#### **Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant. The work addresses an important and well-understood limitation of a widely used class of models. By demonstrating a substantial improvement on a difficult task like aspect-specific sentiment analysis, the paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The GB-RNN model provides an effective and fairly general mechanism for capturing context, and the core idea of a feedforward-feedbackward process is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers, not only in sentiment analysis but also in other NLP tasks where contextual disambiguation is crucial, such as word sense disambiguation or entity recognition.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Model:** The paper introduces a simple and well-motivated extension to RNNs that effectively addresses the important problem of incorporating global context into local predictions.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The model achieves impressive, state-of-the-art performance on a competitive public benchmark, clearly demonstrating its effectiveness.
3.  **High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and provides a thorough comparison to related work.
4.  **Significant Contribution:** The work provides a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning for NLP and is likely to inspire follow-up research.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**

1.  **Potentially Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The central empirical claim regarding the superiority over the bidirectional RNN model [12] needs stronger support. It is unclear if all experimental advantages (e.g., additional training data) were provided to this key baseline, which slightly weakens the conclusion about the model's architectural superiority. This is a point that could be easily clarified and addressed by the authors.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

This is an excellent paper built on a simple and effective idea. I have only a few minor questions regarding the experimental setup that would strengthen the paper further:

*   In Table 2, you provide a comparison to the bidirectional RNN from Irsoy and Cardie [12]. You mention that this baseline was trained with the same hybrid vectors and dropout. For the sake of a perfectly controlled comparison, could you please clarify whether this baseline was also trained using the additional data sources and the two-stage training procedure described in Section 4.4?
*   In Section 4.2, it is noted that your best model for the SemEval competition is an ensemble. This is perfectly appropriate for that comparison. It is good that the main ablations and baseline comparisons in Table 2 appear to use your single best model; please ensure this is stated explicitly to avoid any confusion.
