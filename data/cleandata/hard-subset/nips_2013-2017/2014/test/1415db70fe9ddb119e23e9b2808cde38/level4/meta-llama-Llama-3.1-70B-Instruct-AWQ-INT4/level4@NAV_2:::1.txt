This paper introduces global belief recursive neural networks (GB-RNNs), which extend feedforward recursive neural networks by including a feedbackward step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to give feedback to word embeddings and labels. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on the task of contextual sentiment analysis and show that it can accurately predict the sentiment of specific entities.

The main idea of the GB-RNN is to propagate information from the root node back to the leaf nodes, allowing all phrases to update their prediction based on the global context of the sentence. This is achieved through a top-down recursive function that computes feedbackward vectors at every level of the parse tree. The authors also introduce a hybrid approach to word vector representations, combining unsupervised and supervised word vector representations, which performs better than either alone.

The paper is well-written, and the model is well-motivated and described. The experimental comparisons with other models, including the bidirectional recursive neural network, are useful, but some details need to be clarified. Specifically, it is not clear whether the RNN and B-RNN models also used hybrid word vectors, and how the "best" models were tuned and selected.

The term "global belief" might be misleading, and the term "forward-backward" could be more suitable. Section 3.5 on training is a bit too short and could provide more details on the back-propagation through structure algorithm used for training. Section 3.6 could be improved to clarify the difference between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie.

Section 4.1 could be improved to provide more context about the task and the dataset used for evaluation. It would be helpful to clarify that the paper addresses the contextual polarity task in the Semeval 2013 challenge.

The paper has several strengths, including the introduction of a new model that extends the state-of-the-art in recursive neural networks, and the demonstration of its effectiveness on a challenging task. The use of hybrid word vector representations is also a significant contribution.

However, there are some weaknesses, including the need for more details on the training algorithm and the comparison with other models. Additionally, some sections could be improved for clarity and readability.
