This paper proposes utilizing both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors of a recursive neural network auto-encoder for a sentiment classification task, which the authors refer to as "belief propagation." However, this term is misleading, as it does not represent an instance of the belief propagation inference algorithm, potentially causing confusion for the reader. In general, the paper is well-written, but the motivation behind the proposed model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification.

The key weakness of this paper lies in its evaluation. Presenting an ensemble result in this context is unnecessary, as none of the benchmarks are ensembles, and this obscures the results. Furthermore, the exact composition of the compared models is unclear. Several crucial questions need to be clarified: How were the models tuned, and how were the ensemble/best models selected? How were the benchmarks in Table 2 tuned? Were they using the same augmented data as the BP-RNN? Were the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN also utilizing hybrid word vectors?

In the absence of this information, it appears that the second entry in Table 3 is the most comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when assessing the BP-RNN architecture.

Minor points worth noting include the use of an off-the-shelf parser to parse tweets, which raises questions about its accuracy on this specific data. Additionally, the Irsoy and Cardie citation is incomplete, as are several other citations.

This paper presents a recursive neural network model for classifying phrasal sentiment in context, yielding reasonable results. However, the model lacks a strong motivation, and the experimental methodology is not entirely clear.
