- Clear writing and an interesting architecture that leverages more information from a recursive auto-encoder.  
- The idea of using both encoding and decoding phrase vectors for classification is intriguing.  
- Terminology problem: calling the method "belief propagation" is misleading and should be renamed to reflect the actual mechanism.  
- Motivation weakness: insufficient theoretical or empirical justification for using the decoding vector alongside the encoding vector.  
- Major methodological flaw: experimental evaluation lacks clarity and rigor.  
- Ensemble results are problematic because baselines are single models, preventing fair comparison; single-model BP-RNN results are needed.  
- Missing experimental details: hyperparameter tuning, model selection, and validation procedures are not described.  
- Unclear whether benchmark models were re-implemented and tuned under identical conditions or results were borrowed from prior work.  
- Unclear whether baselines used the same augmented data and hybrid word vectors, confounding attribution of improvements to the architecture.  
- The most scientifically sound comparison is BP-RNN with hybrid vectors; evaluation should focus on that.  
- Minor: using an off-the-shelf parser on noisy Twitter data is questionable—parser accuracy or error impact should be discussed.  
- Minor: bibliography contains incomplete citations (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie).  
- Conclusion: paper needs stronger motivation, renaming away from "belief propagation," and transparent single-model experimental comparisons against properly configured baselines.
