Summary
The paper proposes Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which augment standard bottom-up recursive neural networks with a single top-down feedback step at inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and representations to inform word-level labels, enabling context-sensitive decisions (e.g., correctly assigning positive sentiment to “Android” in “Android beats iOS”). The model concatenates forward (bottom-up) and backward (top-down) node vectors for classification. The authors also introduce “hybrid” word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a small trainable, task-specific segment, and they report benefits from dropout. On SemEval-2013 Task 2 (Twitter sentiment), GB-RNN outperforms a standard RNN, a bidirectional recursive baseline, and prior systems, achieving state-of-the-art results (with an ensemble of the top five GB-RNN models).

Relation to prior work
The model is closely related to bidirectional recursive neural networks (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie), inside–outside recursive models (Le and Zuidema), and unfolding/autoencoder-based approaches. The paper’s backward step differs in that it uses a transformed root vector and both forward and backward parent information for the top-down recursion, with a dimensionality-reducing transform V for efficiency and separation of roles. The term “belief propagation” is somewhat misleading here; “forward–backward” or “top-down feedback” better captures the mechanism, which is not graphical-model belief propagation.

Quality
- The core idea is technically sound and well-motivated by the need for context-sensitive labeling. The mathematical formulation is mostly clear.
- However, several experimental details limit reproducibility and the fairness of comparisons:
  - Were the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines trained with the same hybrid word vectors, dropout, and hyperparameter search protocol? The text suggests yes in Sec. 4.3, but this should be stated explicitly earlier and consistently.
  - How were “best” models selected (validation metric, early stopping, random seed variance)? How was the “single best” model chosen relative to the ensemble?
  - The ensemble vs. single-model comparison is uneven across systems; most competing methods are single models. Please report single-model GB-RNN results alongside ensembles for a fair comparison in the main table.
- Training details in Sec. 3.5 are too sparse. Please clarify:
  - Use of backpropagation through structure (Goller and Küchler, 1996) and how the tree is unfolded for the top-down pass.
  - Whether gradients are propagated into the supervised segment of the hybrid vector only (and the unsupervised segment is frozen), and how this affects optimization/regularization.
  - Any strategies to mitigate vanishing/exploding gradients in deep trees, the role of dropout in forward vs. backward vectors, learning-rate schedule (AdaGrad details), and computational cost per sentence.
- Sec. 3.6’s comparison with Irsoy & Cardie should be tightened: present the competing equations side by side, define all blocks/matrices once, and clearly articulate the implications of using both forward and backward parent signals vs. only the forward parent (including parameter counts and potential overfitting).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, though several sections would benefit from revisions:
- Terminology: replace “belief propagation” with “forward–backward” or “top-down feedback” to avoid confusion.
- Sec. 3.5: expand training as noted above.
- Sec. 3.6: clarify the mathematical comparison and novelty claim relative to bidirectional recursive models; fix minor typos (“feedbackward,” “feedwordard,” etc.).
- Sec. 4.1: briefly explain SemEval-2013 Task 2 for non-NLP readers, distinguish the contextual vs. message-level polarity subtasks, specify which subtask is addressed, and justify the parser constraints used to ensure labeled spans align to subtrees.
- Consider merging Secs. 4.3 and 4.4 for a unified view of ablations and additional data.

Originality
The top-down feedback step within a tree-structured neural model is related to concurrent bidirectional recursive work; the paper’s specific formulation (use of V, concatenation for classification, and choice not to use the forward child when computing the backward child) is a meaningful variant. The hybrid word-vector strategy is practical and contributes empirically, though similar ideas (combining pre-trained and task-tuned embeddings) have been explored; please clarify how your approach differs and why it is crucial to outperformance.

Significance
The method yields strong gains on a widely used benchmark and addresses an important limitation of purely bottom-up composition—contextual disambiguation at the word/phrase level. The reliance on a parser and use of ensembles may limit deployment simplicity, but the approach is broadly useful for tasks like aspect-based sentiment and potentially sense disambiguation.

Pros
- Clear motivation for context-sensitive labeling in tree models.
- Simple, effective top-down feedback mechanism with strong empirical gains.
- Practical hybrid embeddings and dropout use.
- State-of-the-art results on SemEval-2013.

Cons / Questions
- Fairness and transparency of baseline tuning and model selection need clarification.
- Overuse of the term “belief propagation” may confuse readers.
- Insufficient training details (BPTS, gradient flow, complexity).
- Comparison to Irsoy & Cardie requires a clearer, more rigorous exposition.
- Ensemble advantages should be separated from single-model performance.

Overall
A well-written paper with a useful variant of bidirectional recursive models and promising results for contextual sentiment. With clearer baselines, expanded training details, and a crisper comparison to related work, the paper would make a strong contribution.
