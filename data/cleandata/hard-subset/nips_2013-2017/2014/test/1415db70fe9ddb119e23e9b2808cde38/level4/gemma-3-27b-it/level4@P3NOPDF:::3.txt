This paper introduces a method for sentiment classification leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors derived from a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The authors term this approach “belief propagation,” a designation which is misleading as it does not correspond to the standard inference algorithm of the same name and potentially causes confusion. While the paper is generally well-written, the motivation for the proposed model remains somewhat vague and lacks a strong theoretical grounding.

The most significant weakness of this work lies in its evaluation methodology. The presentation of results as an ensemble is problematic within the context of this comparison. The established benchmarks are single models, and reporting an ensemble performance obscures the true contribution of the proposed BP-RNN architecture. Crucially, the exact composition of the ensemble is not clearly defined. Several critical details regarding the experimental setup are missing: How were the individual models within the ensemble tuned? What criteria were used to select the final ensemble configuration, or to identify the “best” model within it? Furthermore, how were the benchmark models presented in Table 2 tuned—specifically, did they utilize the same data augmentation techniques as the BP-RNN? Were the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN benchmark models also trained with hybrid word vectors? 

Without this information, a fair comparison is difficult to establish. Based on the available information, the second entry in Table 3, representing a single BP-RNN model, appears to be the most directly comparable to the benchmark results in Tables 1 and 2 when evaluating the core BP-RNN architecture's performance. A clearer justification for presenting an ensemble, or a breakdown of individual model performances within the ensemble, is essential.

Several minor points warrant attention. The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is surprising, given the inherent noisiness and unique characteristics of Twitter data. Reporting the parser’s accuracy on this specific dataset would strengthen the analysis. Additionally, several citations, including that of Irsoy and Cardie, are incomplete and should be corrected for a professional presentation.

In summary, this paper proposes a recursive neural network model for contextual phrasal sentiment classification. While the reported results are reasonable, the lack of a compelling motivation, coupled with ambiguities in the experimental methodology, limits the paper's overall impact. Addressing the concerns regarding the evaluation procedure—particularly clarifying the ensemble construction and benchmark tuning—would significantly strengthen the contribution. Further details regarding the parser performance and complete citations are also recommended.
