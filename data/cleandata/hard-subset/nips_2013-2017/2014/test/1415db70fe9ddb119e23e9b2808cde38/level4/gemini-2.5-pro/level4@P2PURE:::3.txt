This paper proposes a model for sentiment classification that utilizes both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors from a recursive neural network auto-encoder. The authors term this approach 'belief propagation,' which is misleading as the model is not an instance of the well-known inference algorithm, potentially causing confusion for the reader. While the paper is generally well-written, the motivation for the proposed model is vague and lacks a clear theoretical justification.

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation. Presenting an ensemble result is not well-justified, as none of the benchmark models are ensembles. This comparison obfuscates the true performance of the proposed architecture. Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks clarity regarding the precise composition of the compared models. The authors should clarify the following:
*   How were the proposed models tuned, and what were the selection criteria for the best single model and the ensemble?
*   How were the benchmark models in Table 2 tuned?
*   Were the benchmarks trained on the same augmented data as the BP-RNN?
*   Did the baseline RNN and Bidirectional-RNN also use the hybrid word vectors?

In the absence of these crucial details, the most appropriate comparison for assessing the contribution of the BP-RNN architecture appears to be the second entry in Table 3 against the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2.

Minor points:
*   It is surprising that an off-the-shelf parser was used for tweets; what is its accuracy on this noisy domain?
*   Several citations are incomplete, including Irsoy and Cardie.

In summary, this paper introduces a recursive neural network for contextual phrasal sentiment classification. While the reported results appear reasonable, the model's contribution is undermined by a weak motivation and an unclear experimental methodology.
