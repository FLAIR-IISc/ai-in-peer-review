This paper introduces “global belief recursive neural networks” (GB-RNNs). This model extends standard “feedforward” (bottom-up) recursive neural networks with a backward (top-down) step during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions and embeddings to inform word embeddings and labels.

The paper is well-written overall, and the model is well-motivated and described. It builds upon the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), though the reference to that paper is currently incomplete. A key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vector representations. It’s worth clarifying whether the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines also utilized these hybrid vectors. Additionally, details on model selection and tuning would strengthen the experimental results. Specifically, how were the “best” models identified, and how does the single best model compare to the ensemble approach used by competing methods?

The term “belief propagation” may be slightly misleading; “forward-backward” might be a more appropriate descriptor. Section 3.5 (Training) could benefit from additional detail. Given the use of recursive computation, it's likely backpropagation through structure is employed (Goller, 1996 could be cited), which unfolds the model into a very deep network to reach the word embedding layer for updates. More detail on this crucial step would be helpful.

Section 3.6 feels somewhat unclear, and the distinction between this model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie needs further clarification. The equations presented comparing the models could be more thoroughly explained.

Section 4.1 could be improved for readers less familiar with the NLP community. SemEval 2013 Task 2 comprised two subtasks: contextual polarity and message polarity. It would be beneficial to explicitly state which subtask this work addresses. Providing this context would also aid understanding of the parser constraints.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could potentially be merged for a more cohesive presentation. 

Overall, this is a well-written paper describing an interesting and potentially valuable variant of bidirectional recursive models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The model addresses a relevant problem – contextual sentiment analysis – and proposes a novel approach to incorporating global context.
*   The experimental results show state-of-the-art performance on the SemEval 2013 challenge, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The introduction of hybrid word vector representations is a potentially valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The explanation of the differences between the proposed model and existing bidirectional recursive neural networks (particularly Irsoy and Cardie, 2008) is somewhat unclear.
*   The details regarding the training process, especially backpropagation through structure, are limited.
*   The experimental comparisons could be more comprehensive and rigorous, with clearer details on model selection and hyperparameter tuning.



References:
Goller, C., & Küchler, A. (1996). Learning task-dependent distributed representations by backpropagation through structure. *In Proceedings of the International Conference on Neural Networks* (pp. 343-350).
