This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), extending standard feedforward recursive networks with a backward pass during inference. This allows phrase-level predictions to influence word representations and labels, addressing the limitation of feedforward models in context-dependent tasks like aspect-specific sentiment classification.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated. The model is well-described and builds upon prior work in recursive neural networks. It notably extends the Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), and a key contribution appears to be the introduction of hybrid word vector representations. However, the experimental comparisons require clarification. It is unclear whether the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines also utilized hybrid word vectors. Further details on the hyperparameter tuning and model selection processes, particularly how the "best" models were chosen, are necessary. The use of an ensemble for the GB-RNN, while potentially beneficial, isn't mirrored in the baseline approaches, impacting the fairness of the comparison.

The term “belief propagation” may be misleading; “forward-backward” might be a more appropriate descriptor. Section 3.5 (Training) is somewhat concise and could benefit from expanded detail. The training process likely employs backpropagation through structure (Goller, 1996), which involves unfolding the recursive model into a deep network. Elucidating this process and its implications, especially concerning reaching the word embedding layer for updates, would strengthen the paper.

Section 3.6 and its comparison to Irsoy and Cardie’s work needs refinement for clarity. The distinctions between the two approaches should be more explicitly delineated. 

Section 4.1 could be improved for readers less familiar with the NLP community. The SemEval 2013 task has two subtasks: contextual and message polarity. Clarifying which subtask is addressed would aid understanding, and context for the parser constraints would be beneficial.

Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could be consolidated for a more streamlined presentation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel extension to recursive neural networks that addresses a known limitation – the inability to model context-dependent meaning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate improved performance on a challenging sentiment analysis task, achieving state-of-the-art results on the SemEval 2013 challenge.
*   The introduction of hybrid word vector representations appears to be a valuable contribution.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed model.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental comparisons aren't entirely fair due to differing configurations and ensemble methods.
*   The explanation of the training process lacks detail, specifically regarding backpropagation through structure.
*   The comparison to Irsoy and Cardie’s work needs further clarification.
*   The terminology ("belief propagation") is potentially misleading.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of recursive neural networks. The proposed GB-RNN addresses a significant limitation of existing models and demonstrates promising results. However, addressing the concerns regarding the experimental setup, training details, and clarity of the model comparison would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
