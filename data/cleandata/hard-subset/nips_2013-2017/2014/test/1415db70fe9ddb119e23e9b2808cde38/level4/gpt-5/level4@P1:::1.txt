This paper introduces Global Belief Recursive Neural Networks (GB-RNNs), which extend standard feedforward (bottom-up) recursive neural networks with a single backward (top-down) step during inference. The backward pass allows phrase-level predictions and representations to feed back into word-level embeddings and labels, enabling context-dependent predictions (e.g., that “Android” is positive in “Android beats iOS”). The authors also propose hybrid word vectors that concatenate fixed, unsupervised embeddings with a smaller supervised component, and they report that dropout improves RNN training. On SemEval 2013 Task 2 Twitter data, GB-RNNs improve F1 by roughly 3% over a standard RNN and achieve state-of-the-art performance, including an ensemble of the top five GB-RNN models. The work is positioned relative to bidirectional recursive models (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie; the reference provided is incomplete) and to recurrent/window-based context models.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for context-dependent composition: purely feedforward recursive models cannot capture aspect-specific sentiment.
- Conceptually simple extension (a single feedbackward step during inference) with empirical gains.
- Hybrid word vectors and the use of dropout are practical contributions that appear to improve performance.
- Strong results on SemEval 2013, with qualitative examples showing contextual disambiguation.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Fairness of comparisons: Were the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines also trained with the same hybrid word vectors, dropout, external resources, and tuning protocol? How were “best” models tuned and selected, and how was the “single best” chosen? It is important to note that competing systems did not use an ensemble; please clarify the apples-to-apples single-model comparison.
- Terminology: “Belief propagation” is potentially misleading in this context; “forward–backward” or “top-down/backward pass at inference” would be more precise.
- Training details are insufficient. Section 3.5 should specify the use of backpropagation through structure (BPTS; Goller and Küchler, 1996) and describe the unfolding, depth, and how gradients reach and update the word embedding parameters. This is a critical step for reproducibility and for understanding optimization challenges in very deep unfolded structures.
- Section 3.6 (Comparison to Other Models) is confusing. The distinctions from Irsoy and Cardie’s bidirectional recursive model should be made explicit and precise, including equations, parameter counts, and the role of forward vs backward representations in computing predictions. The current reference to Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete.
- Section 4.1 should be improved for readers outside NLP. SemEval 2013 Task 2 includes two subtasks (contextual polarity and message polarity). I infer that this work addresses contextual polarity; a clearer task description would help, and would also motivate the parser constraints you impose.
- Organizational suggestion: Sections 4.3 and 4.4 could be merged for a more concise presentation of baselines and the impact of additional training data.

Quality: The model is technically sound and the idea of a single backward inference step is well motivated. However, the experimental methodology needs more transparency. In particular, ensure that baselines share the same word vector setup, hyperparameter search, and data resources; detail the selection of “best” and “single best” models; and separate ensemble vs single-model results. The training description should include BPTS/unfolding specifics.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but the above sections require more detail. Renaming “belief propagation” would prevent confusion. Provide a clear, self-contained description of the SemEval subtask, dataset splits, and the parser constraints you enforce.

Originality: The backward inference step within a recursive architecture is closely related to bidirectional recursive models, but your specific formulation and the hybrid word vector scheme are useful variants. Please articulate what is genuinely novel relative to Irsoy and Cardie (beyond nomenclature) and whether the hybrid vectors are the primary driver of the gains.

Significance: Context-dependent sentiment is important, and the approach appears effective and broadly applicable to other NLP tasks requiring long-range contextual disambiguation. With clearer comparisons and training details, the contribution would be more compelling.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation of feedforward recursive models with a simple, effective backward inference step.
- Strong empirical results on a standard benchmark.
- Practical contributions (hybrid vectors, dropout) with demonstrated benefit.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete methodological transparency and potentially unfair comparisons (ensemble vs single models; unclear use of hybrid vectors in baselines).
- Insufficient training details (BPTS, unfolding) and unclear distinctions from prior bidirectional recursive work.
- Terminology and exposition issues that may confuse readers outside NLP.

Minor comments:
- Correct and complete the Irsoy and Cardie citation.
- Consider renaming the model to avoid confusion with probabilistic “belief propagation.”
- Clarify whether the RNN and bidirectional RNN baselines used exactly the same hybrid word vectors and dropout settings.
