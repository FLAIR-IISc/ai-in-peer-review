This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment classification by leveraging both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors of a recursive neural network auto-encoder, which the authors refer to as "belief propagation". However, this terminology may be misleading, as it does not constitute a true instance of the belief propagation inference algorithm. Despite being well-written, the paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for the proposed model, with the motivation behind it being somewhat vague.

A major weakness of this paper lies in its evaluation methodology. Presenting an ensemble result in this context is not justified, as none of the benchmarks are ensembles, which obscures the results. Furthermore, the exact composition of the compared models is unclear. To improve the clarity of the evaluation, the authors should provide additional information, including the tuning process for the models, the selection criteria for the ensemble and best models, and the tuning process for the benchmarks in Table 2. It is also essential to know whether the benchmarks used the same augmented data as the BP-RNN and whether the RNN and Bidirectional-RNN employed hybrid word vectors. In the absence of this information, the second entry in Table 3 appears to be the most comparable to the benchmarks in Tables 1 and 2 when assessing the BP-RNN architecture.

Some minor points worth noting include the use of an off-the-shelf parser for parsing tweets, which may not be the most accurate approach for this specific data. It would be interesting to know the accuracy of the parser in this context. Additionally, the citation for Irsoy and Cardie is incomplete, as are several other references.

Overall, the paper presents a recursive neural network model for classifying phrasal sentiment in context, with reasonable results. However, the model lacks a strong theoretical motivation, and the experimental methodology could be improved with more detailed information and clearer explanations.
