This paper addresses a key limitation of standard Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) used in NLP. In the conventional approach, representations for text snippets are generated in a purely bottom-up fashion along a parse tree. This unidirectional process prevents information from the broader context (represented by nodes higher up the tree) from influencing the representations of smaller constituents (nodes lower down the tree). The authors propose a simple and elegant solution: a bidirectional RNN that supplements the standard upward pass with a downward pass. This second pass propagates information from parent nodes to their children, thereby augmenting each node's representation with contextual information from the larger snippet it belongs to. The proposed model is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the sentiment analysis task of SemEval 2012 (Task 2).

**Clarity and Quality**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presented, and generally well-written. The proposed extension to the standard RNN is intuitive, and its formulation is described with sufficient detail. The core idea is communicated effectively, making the paper easy to follow.

**Originality and Significance**

The proposed bidirectional extension is a simple, elegant, and effective contribution to the literature on recursive neural models. While the high-level idea of bidirectional processing in tree structures is related to prior work, notably [18], the specific computational model for the downward pass presented here is novel. Furthermore, the empirical results suggest that this new formulation is superior. The strong performance on a standard benchmark task demonstrates the significance of this contribution and its potential for improving representation learning for natural language.

**Concerns and Questions for the Authors**

While the results appear excellent, I have a few concerns regarding the experimental setup that I would like the authors to address:

1.  **Comparison with Baseline [18]:** For the comparison with the alternative bidirectional RNN model from [18] in Table 2, it is not clear if the experimental conditions were identical. To ensure a fair and direct comparison, could the authors clarify whether the reported result for the model from [18] was obtained using the same enhancements as their own model? Specifically, was it trained with dropout, the additional training data, and the hybrid word vectors? A fair comparison is crucial for evaluating the specific contribution of the proposed downward pass mechanism.

2.  **Presentation of Ensemble Results:** The ensemble result reported in Table 2 is impressive. However, including it in a table that primarily compares the performance of individual models can be misleading, as ensemble performance is of a different nature. I suggest that this result be presented separately or more clearly delineated from the single-model comparisons to avoid confusion.

3.  **Minor Clarification:** In Section 4.4, the paper mentions a "widely used mix of both". Could the authors please clarify what this phrase refers to?

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a well-executed paper presenting a simple yet powerful idea. The proposed bidirectional RNN effectively addresses a known limitation of its unidirectional counterpart. The model is well-motivated, clearly explained, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. Pending clarification on the experimental points raised above, I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field.
