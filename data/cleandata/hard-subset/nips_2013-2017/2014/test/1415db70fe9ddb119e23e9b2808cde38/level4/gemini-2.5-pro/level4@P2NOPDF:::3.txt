This paper introduces a recursive neural network auto-encoder for phrasal sentiment classification. The core contribution is the use of both the encoding and decoding phrase vectors as features for the classification task. The authors term their method "belief propagation." However, this terminology is misleading, as the proposed model is not an instance of the well-known belief propagation inference algorithm. This choice of name could cause significant confusion for readers.

Overall, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, its primary weaknesses lie in the motivation for the proposed model and the experimental evaluation. The motivation for using both encoding and decoding vectors is not clearly articulated, and the paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for why this architectural choice should lead to improved performance.

The most significant weakness of the paper is the experimental evaluation, which lacks the clarity and rigor needed for a fair comparison. A major concern is the presentation of an ensemble result in the main comparison table. Since the benchmark models are single systems, comparing them against an ensemble of the proposed models is not an apples-to-apples comparison and serves to obscure the true performance of the standalone BP-RNN architecture.

Furthermore, crucial details about the experimental setup are missing, making it difficult to interpret the results. The authors should clarify the following points:
*   **Tuning and Selection:** What was the procedure for hyperparameter tuning for the proposed models? How were the specific models for the ensemble (or the single best model) selected?
*   **Benchmark Tuning:** How were the benchmark models from Table 2 tuned? Were they re-implemented and tuned on the same validation data, or are the results taken from prior work?
*   **Data Consistency:** Did the benchmark models have access to the same augmented data used by the BP-RNN?
*   **Feature Consistency:** Did the baseline RNN and Bidirectional-RNN models also use the hybrid word vectors, or was this feature unique to the BP-RNN?

Without this information, a reader is left to guess which result is most comparable to the benchmarks. It appears the second entry in Table 3 (the single best model) is the most appropriate comparison, but the lack of clarity on this point undermines the paper's claims.

Minor points:
*   The use of an off-the-shelf parser for tweets is surprising, given the noisy and ungrammatical nature of this domain. Some discussion of the parser's accuracy on this specific dataset would be valuable.
*   Several citations appear to be incomplete (e.g., Irsoy and Cardie). The bibliography should be carefully checked for completeness.

In summary, this paper presents a novel recursive neural network architecture for sentiment analysis. While the proposed model achieves promising results, the paper is hindered by a poorly motivated naming convention, a lack of theoretical justification for the model's design, and an experimental evaluation that is not clear enough to fully support the authors' conclusions. Addressing the methodological questions raised above would significantly strengthen the paper.
