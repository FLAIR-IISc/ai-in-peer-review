This paper introduces "belief propagation recursive neural networks," a novel extension of the traditional feedforward recursive neural networks. The proposed architecture incorporates a backward (top-down) step during inference, allowing for phrase-level predictions and embeddings to provide feedback to word embeddings and labels.

The paper is well-written, and the model is well-motivated and described. The authors extend the idea of Bidirectional Recursive Neural Networks introduced by Irsoy and Cardie (2008), with notable contributions including the introduction of hybrid word vectors. This may be the most significant difference between the proposed model and previous work.

However, the experimental comparisons are not entirely fair, and several points need clarification. Specifically, it is unclear whether the RNN and B-RNN models used in the comparison also employed hybrid vectors. Additionally, the authors should provide more details on how the "best" models were tuned and selected, as well as how the single best model was chosen. It is also worth noting that the other methods used in the comparison did not employ an ensemble approach.

The term "belief propagation" may be misleading, and the authors may consider using the term "forward-backward" instead. Section 3.5, which discusses training, is somewhat brief and could benefit from more details. For instance, it appears that the training process utilizes the back-propagation through structure algorithm (Goller, 1996), which implies that the recursive model is unfolded. This results in a very deep network to update the word embedding part. The authors should provide more information on this crucial step.

Section 3.6 is somewhat confusing, and the authors should clarify the differences between their model and the work of Irsoy and Cardie (2008). Section 4.1 could be improved to make it more accessible to readers outside the NLP community. Specifically, the authors should provide a clearer explanation of the task, including the parser constraints. For instance, in Task 2 of Semeval 2013, there are two subtasks: contextual and message polarity. It appears that the authors address the contextual polarity task, and a better presentation of the task would facilitate understanding.

Consider merging sections 4.3 and 4.4, as they seem to cover related topics. Overall, the paper is well-written and presents an interesting variant of the Bidirectional recursive model.
