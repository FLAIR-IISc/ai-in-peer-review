### **Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"**

#### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the fundamental problem of estimating the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data in the presence of unmeasured confounding. The authors propose a novel methodology that bridges the gap between two prominent paradigms: (1) methods based on the faithfulness assumption, which aim for point identification of causal effects, and (2) methods based on instrumental variables (IV), which provide bounds on the ACE under weaker structural assumptions.

The key idea is to repurpose the "witness" variable concept from Entner et al. (2013). In that work, a witness `W` is used to identify an admissible set of covariates `Z` for back-door adjustment, under the assumption of faithfulness (specifically, that `W ⊥ Y | {X, Z}` implies a corresponding graphical separation). This paper cleverly inverts this logic: instead of assuming faithfulness is perfectly met, it treats the conditional independence as *approximate*. It uses the witness-admissible set pair `(W, Z)` to structure a bounding problem analogous to the classic IV setting.

The degree of "approximate independence" (i.e., the violation of faithfulness) is explicitly parameterized by a set of user-defined relaxation constants (`w, y, x, β, β̄`). These parameters relax the structural assumptions of a perfect instrument. The authors then leverage and extend the linear programming (LP) framework of Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012) to derive bounds on the ACE subject to these relaxed constraints. The paper further contributes a Bayesian framework to account for finite-sample uncertainty and a fast, approximate "back-substitution" algorithm based on derived symbolic bounds, which avoids the full LP optimization for faster exploration.

The work is well-situated in the causal inference literature, synthesizing ideas from causal discovery (Spirtes et al., 2000; Entner et al., 2013), potential outcomes and bounding (Balke & Pearl, 1997; Manski, 2007), and the LP approach to causality (Dawid, 2003; Ramsahai, 2012).

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—reinterpreting a causal discovery criterion as the basis for a relaxed bounding procedure—is highly original and significant. It offers a principled "middle ground" between making the strong, often untestable assumption of faithfulness and resorting to wide, often uninformative IV bounds (or declaring the ACE unidentifiable). This "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) provides a practical framework for sensitivity analysis, allowing researchers to explore how much the conclusions of a faithfulness-based analysis change if the underlying independence tests are only approximately true. This is a crucial and practical problem, as finite-sample statistical tests can never perfectly distinguish a zero from a very small correlation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper demonstrates a strong command of the underlying technical machinery. The extension of the Ramsahai/Dawid LP framework to incorporate the new relaxation parameters is a non-trivial technical contribution. The development of both the exact (but slow) LP/Bayesian method and a fast, symbolic approximation shows methodological depth and a consideration for practical usability. The experimental design is thoughtful, testing the method under "hard" conditions where naive estimators are guaranteed to fail and under conditions where the method's theoretical assumptions are violated (no true witness exists).

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and motivated. The background section provides a concise yet effective overview of the necessary concepts. The central idea is explained intuitively, and the framing of the method as "protecting" a witness from faithfulness violations is both memorable and clarifying.

#### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Guidance on Setting Relaxation Parameters:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a principled discussion on how to set the free parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`). The paper explicitly defers this, stating "we will not discuss in detail how to choose the free parameters." This is a critical omission. The validity and usefulness of the entire framework hinge on these choices. A practitioner is left to wonder: Should `w` be 0.01, 0.1, or 0.2? This choice directly controls the trade-off between assumption strength and bound width, and without guidance, it feels arbitrary. While the supplement may contain more detail, this issue is so central to the method's application that it warrants significant discussion in the main paper. The authors should consider providing heuristics, perhaps based on the sample size and the strength of the observed (conditional) dependencies that are being relaxed.

2.  **Selection Among Multiple Witnesses:** The algorithm can return multiple valid `(W, Z)` pairs, each yielding a different set of bounds. The paper's experimental solution is to select the pair with the highest sum of BDeu scores for the relevant conditional independencies. This is a reasonable heuristic, but it deserves more exploration. What if two different pairs yield strong BDeu scores but produce conflicting, narrow bounds? The paper should discuss the problem of aggregation or selection among multiple candidate bounds.

3.  **Scalability of the Search:** The search for an admissible set `Z` is exponential in the number of potential covariates. The paper acknowledges this and suggests discrete PCA as a potential remedy for high-dimensional `W`. This is a useful suggestion, but the computational complexity of the search remains a practical bottleneck. A more integrated discussion of scalable search strategies (e.g., greedy search, as in other causal discovery algorithms) would strengthen the paper.

#### **Final Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper with a novel and important contribution. It thoughtfully connects two different streams of research in causal inference to produce a practical tool for sensitivity analysis in observational studies. The "Witness Protection Program" is a clever and well-executed idea. The main weakness—the lack of guidance on parameter selection—is significant but addressable. Given the strength and originality of the core idea, I recommend **acceptance**. I strongly urge the authors to use the final version to incorporate a more detailed discussion on the choice of relaxation parameters, as this would substantially increase the impact and usability of their excellent work.
