Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) from observational binary data when standard back-door adjustment is not justified and full-blown instrumental variables (IV) assumptions are too strong. It “inverts” Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a witness W and an admissible set Z to do back-door adjustment (point identification via faithfulness), the paper treats W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z, and introduces bounded violations of faithfulness through sensitivity parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) that cap the strength of direct W→Y paths and W–U associations. Given these relaxations, it computes ACE bounds via a linear programming (LP) approach extending Dawid and Ramsahai’s polytopal methods for binary IVs. To improve scalability and interpretability, it derives algebraic (symbolic) inequality bounds and a back-substitution procedure that is faster though looser than the LP. It also provides a Bayesian implementation with constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to propagate sampling uncertainty, combined with a search over (W, Z) using BDeu scoring. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu vaccine study illustrate the approach, yielding intervals tighter than standard IV in some settings by leveraging approximate independencies.

This bridges two strands of prior work: (i) faithfulness-based graph discovery and admissible set selection (Spirtes et al.; Entner et al.), and (ii) ACE bounds under imperfect compliance/IV (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins). It also relates to recent bounding-with-confounding work (e.g., Manski; Geiger et al.) and inequality constraints for latent variable models.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of bounded violations via parameters (3)–(6) is coherent: setting them to the IV or chain limits recovers known models; relaxing them interpolates between point identification and vacuous bounds. The LP construction follows established convex-analytic methods (extreme points, dual polytopes), extended to handle κ and ω variables when β ≠ β̄ = 1. The symbolic bounds are carefully derived (details in the supplement), and the back-substitution scheme is a reasonable relaxation that preserves feasibility monotonicity and convergence.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper claims tighter-than-IV bounds when approximate conditional independencies are present; both the algebraic reductions and the real-data example support this. It also claims computational gains via back-substitution; while high-level arguments and supplementary evidence are mentioned, more explicit timing/accuracy comparisons in the main text would strengthen the case.
- Empirics: Synthetic studies compare to naive back-door estimators and a faithfulness-based point estimator. The error metric (distance to the true ACE, with intervals scoring zero when they cover) highlights the coverage advantage but underplays interval width. The authors note this trade-off and defer width analyses to the supplement; in the main text, reporting both coverage and mean width would provide a more balanced view. The real-data analysis is sensible, but the choice of sensitivity parameters is not strongly justified beyond defaults; a sensitivity plot would help.
- Limitations acknowledged: The paper is candid about (i) computational cost of exact LP, (ii) reliance on user-chosen relaxation parameters, and (iii) looser nature of algebraic bounds. The Bayesian falsification test (95% rejection threshold) is pragmatic but somewhat ad hoc.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally clear for a causality-savvy audience. The motivation for inverting Rule 1 is well explained, and the connections to IV limits are intuitive (recovering Balke–Pearl bounds at parameter extremes). Notation is heavy (ζ, κ, ω, η, δ), and some steps are deferred to the supplement; adding a small running toy example and a short “map” summarizing how the parameters control each type of violation would improve readability. Algorithm 1 is helpful but leaves the (W, Z) search and model selection largely to prior work; a brief pseudocode block for the back-substitution pass would be useful.

Originality
The key ideas—repurposing witnesses as conditional instruments, formalizing “near-faithfulness” via explicit local bounds, and deriving symbolic inequalities with a message-passing-like refinement—are novel combinations of known ingredients. To my knowledge, algebraic bounds as explicit functions of w, x, y, β, β̄ and the back-substitution scheme have not appeared before. The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet treatment is also a thoughtful way to propagate sampling uncertainty without positing priors over latent structures.

Significance
This is an important contribution to practical causal inference in settings where neither “assume ignorability” nor “assume perfect IV” is credible. The framework provides a principled middle ground that many observational studies need, and the availability of code enhances impact. If adopted, the approach could change how researchers integrate discovery-stage independencies into partial identification analyses. The main potential limiter is the need to select relaxation parameters; offering clearer guidance could broaden adoption.

Pros
- Conceptually fresh bridge between faithfulness-based admissible set selection and IV bounding via controlled relaxations.
- Solid theoretical development, recovering known extremes and introducing new algebraic bounds.
- Practical algorithmic contributions (back-substitution) that improve scalability.
- Bayesian treatment acknowledges finite-sample uncertainty and supports falsification.
- Useful empirical illustrations, including a real dataset, and code availability.

Cons
- Limited guidance on choosing sensitivity parameters; defaults are plausible but not justified by domain knowledge or calibration studies.
- Evaluation emphasizes coverage but not interval width; more comprehensive metrics (coverage, average width, and time) would better situate the trade-offs.
- Baselines could be richer (e.g., compare against standard IV conditional on Z when plausible, or recent confounding-bounds methods).
- Rejection-sampling-based posterior and the 95% rejection rule are pragmatic but could be replaced/augmented by more principled constrained sampling or posterior predictive checks.
- Some reliance on supplement for derivations; main text could benefit from a concise, worked example.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical guidance for setting w, x, y, β, β̄ (e.g., mapping to partial R-squareds, E-value style calibrations, or sensitivity curves showing bound width vs. parameters).
- Report, in the main paper, (i) coverage and mean width across simulations, (ii) runtime and accuracy comparisons of LP vs. back-substitution, and (iii) sensitivity plots for the real data.
- Add an additional baseline using standard IV on candidate (W, Z) pairs when justifiable, and/or compare to recent bounding-with-confounding methods beyond IV.
- Clarify the implications of selecting (W, Z) from the same data used to compute bounds; consider sample-splitting or cross-fitting to mitigate selection-induced bias in finite samples.
- Include a small numerical example illustrating the inequalities and the back-substitution tightening steps.

Overall recommendation
This is a technically sound, original, and potentially impactful contribution to causal inference with partial identification. With stronger guidance on sensitivity parameters and fuller empirical characterization of the width–coverage–compute trade-offs, it would be very compelling. I lean toward acceptance.
