

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new method called the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The authors modify Entner et al.'s work on instrumental variables and admissible sets to provide bounds on ACE that are more robust to violations of faithfulness assumptions. The core idea involves using pairs of variables (witness, admissible set) identified through a modified version of Entner's Rule 1, then applying linear programming with additional relaxation constraints to generate bounds on ACE.

The paper builds on established causal inference approaches, including the instrumental variable framework and the back-door adjustment. However, the authors claim their method provides a "novel compromise" between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. The paper includes a Bayesian learning approach, algebraic bounds, and a "back-substitution" algorithm for computational efficiency.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the causal inference background. The connection to existing work (Entner et al., Ramsahai, Dawid) is well-motivated. The Bayesian approach to handling uncertainty in the constraints is a thoughtful addition. The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss the trade-offs between assumptions and bound tightness.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising motivation, the paper falls short on several critical aspects expected of NIPS submissions:

1. **Lack of rigorous validation:** The paper claims to provide "greater control over violations of faithfulness," but the empirical validation is insufficient. The synthetic experiments (Section 5) compare against only two naive point estimators (NE1, NE2) and the method of Entner et al., but omit comparisons to established causal inference methods like the standard IV bounds (e.g., [1] cited in the paper) or back-door adjustment with causal discovery. The influenza vaccination case study provides limited insight since it doesn't compare WPP to established methods on the same dataset.

2. **Questionable novelty:** The core contribution appears to be a slight modification of existing linear programming approaches for causal bounds (Ramsahai [16], Dawid [5]). The "Witness Protection Program" framework doesn't provide a clear theoretical advantage over existing methods. The relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) are introduced without principled justification or systematic analysis of how they affect bound quality.

3. **Unsubstantiated computational claims:** The paper claims the "back-substitution" algorithm is "an order of magnitude more efficient" than numerical linear programming, but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. The computational complexity of the proposed method isn't thoroughly analyzed, and the paper doesn't compare runtimes against existing methods.

4. **Lack of clear advantages:** The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that WPP provides better bounds than existing methods under realistic conditions. The synthetic experiments show WPP is "stable" but don't quantify how much tighter the bounds are compared to standard IV bounds. In the influenza case study, the authors don't show that WPP's bounds are tighter or more informative than those obtained through standard IV methods.

5. **Overstated claims:** The conclusion claims WPP "should become a standard item in the toolbox of anyone who needs to perform an observational study," but the paper provides no evidence that it outperforms established methods in practice. The paper's main contribution is a slight modification of existing approaches without sufficient evidence of improvement.

**Methodological concerns:** The paper's Bayesian approach to handling the constraints seems unnecessarily complex for the problem. The authors claim to avoid "massive sensitivity to hyperparameter choices," but don't provide evidence for this claim. The "falsification test" in Algorithm 1 is simplistic and doesn't account for the uncertainty in the posterior samples.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in causal inference, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method offers significant advantages over established approaches. The lack of thorough empirical validation against relevant baselines, questionable novelty, and unsubstantiated computational claims undermine the paper's contribution. The authors have not demonstrated that their "Witness Protection Program" provides meaningful improvements over existing methods in terms of bound quality, computational efficiency, or robustness.

For future work, the authors should:
1. Conduct comprehensive comparisons against established causal inference methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets
2. Provide a principled approach for selecting the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄)
3. Demonstrate clear theoretical advantages of their approach
4. Provide empirical evidence for their computational efficiency claims

The paper's approach is a minor variation of existing methods without sufficient evidence of improvement, and thus does not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS.
