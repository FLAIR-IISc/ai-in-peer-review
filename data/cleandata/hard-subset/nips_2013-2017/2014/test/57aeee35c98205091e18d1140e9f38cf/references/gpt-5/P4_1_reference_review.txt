Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a methodology to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y using observational data and a set of pre-treatment covariates W. It reinterprets Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (used to find admissible adjustment sets under faithfulness) to instead generate candidate witness–admissible-set pairs (W, Z) that are treated as “soft” instruments. The key idea is to relax the faithfulness-based conditional independences that justify using a witness as an instrument into bounded violations, parameterized by θ = (w, y, x, β, β̄), which control deviations from: exclusion (W → Y), as-if randomization (W ⫫ U), measurement of X given W, and selection into U given W. Given θ, the authors extend linear programming approaches for IV bounds (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) to compute bounds on ACE, conditioning on Z and averaging over P(Z). They also provide analytical (symbolic) inequalities as functions of θ and a fast “back-substitution” (message-passing-like) procedure to tighten box constraints and approximate the LP solution.

A Bayesian procedure is proposed to account for sampling uncertainty: a constrained Dirichlet prior/posterior over P(Y, X, W | Z) with rejection sampling enforces the inequality constraints, yielding posterior distributions over ACE bounds. The method, termed the Witness Protection Program (WPP), is evaluated on synthetic data (with/without true witnesses) and on a flu-vaccination encouragement dataset. WPP yields intervals that are typically narrower than standard IV bounds and more robust than point estimators that assume exact faithfulness or naive adjustments.

This work builds on: instrumental-variable bounds for binary variables (Balke & Pearl; Ramsahai/Lauritzen), faithfulness-based admissible set discovery (Entner et al.), and graphical/inequality constraints (Evans; Richardson/Robins). The proposed combination—using Rule 1 to produce candidate instruments and encoding bounded departures from faithfulness within an LP and algebraic-inequality framework—appears novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge between faithfulness-based identification and IV-style partial identification. The parameterized relaxations provide interpretable knobs for domain knowledge to control how far to trust conditional independences.
- Technically sound extension of existing LP-based bounding frameworks to incorporate bounded violations via θ and to handle conditioning on Z; use of κ, ω aggregations and dual polytopes is consistent with prior IV literature.
- Analytical inequalities and the back-substitution algorithm offer insight and substantial computational savings, with empirical evidence that the loss in tightness is modest.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty via constrained Dirichlet avoids overconfident plug-in decisions and allows simple falsification checks.
- Empirical results demonstrate stability compared to naive estimators and a realistic application showing improved (narrower) bounds than standard IV.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Choice/calibration of relaxation parameters θ is central but underdeveloped in the main text. Clear guidance, interpretable scales (e.g., mapping to partial correlations or risk differences), and sensitivity plots should be in the paper, not only in the Supplement.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Report average interval widths, coverage, and tightness relative to the full LP across settings. Provide runtime and tightness comparisons between back-substitution and LP. Include additional datasets or simulated regimes with varying degrees of violation, and ablations (e.g., effect of each θ component).
- The falsification rule (“>95% rejections” in rejection sampling) is ad hoc. More principled posterior predictive checks or moment-inequality-based tests (e.g., Andrews–Shi) would strengthen conclusions.
- The Bayesian model assumes independent Dirichlet priors per z; dependence across strata may be more realistic. Rejection sampling in constrained spaces can be inefficient; alternative samplers (e.g., hit-and-run) could improve scalability.
- The method is restricted to binary X, Y and covariates; extensions or discussion for multi-valued/continuous variables would broaden impact.
- Some clarity and presentation issues: heavy notation; missing derivations moved to the Supplement; a small worked example could aid comprehension. Minor typos (e.g., “poymake”), and occasional footnote/notation glitches.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The methodological core is sound and well-grounded in prior LP/IV literature. The algebraic relaxations are conservative by design. Proofs and many details are deferred; bringing key lemmas into the main text would help. The Bayesian layer is reasonable but some choices are heuristic.
- Clarity: Generally well organized but dense. Algorithm 1 is helpful, yet practical guidance on θ and the search over Z is brief. A simplified example and clearer mapping of θ to causal violations would improve readability.
- Originality: The inversion of Rule 1 to generate “soft IVs” and the formalization of bounded-faithfulness violations within an LP and analytical-inequality framework appear novel. The back-substitution scheme is also a useful addition.
- Significance: Addresses a relevant gap between fragile point identification via faithfulness and often-too-wide IV bounds. If adopted, WPP could become a practical tool for observational studies. Impact depends on providing clearer parameter calibration and broader empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled framework that unifies causal discovery cues with partial identification.
- Offers practical control over robustness via interpretable relaxation parameters.
- Provides both exact (LP) and fast approximate (back-substitution) implementations with code.
- Demonstrates improved bounds over standard IV in a real application.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited guidance and empirical calibration for the crucial relaxation parameters.
- Experimental section lacks comprehensive metrics (width, coverage, tightness vs LP) and broader datasets.
- Some methodological choices (falsification threshold, priors) are heuristic without theoretical guarantees provided.
- Restricted to binary variables; scalability and generalization not addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a principled strategy to elicit or calibrate θ (e.g., prior bounds on conditional odds ratios; domain-informed sensitivity curves) and report sensitivity ranges prominently.
- Add tightness/runtime comparisons of back-substitution vs LP and report average interval width and coverage across simulations.
- Use more formal model checking for inequality-constrained models and discuss large-sample properties (consistency of bounds given correct θ).
- Provide an illustrative toy example and streamline notation; fix minor errors.

Overall: A solid and innovative contribution to causal inference with practical potential. Strengthening parameter calibration guidance and empirical evaluation would substantially improve the paper.
