This paper introduces a novel methodology, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), to bound the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s Rule 1, traditionally used for identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment, as a means to find instrumental variables and subsequently derive bounds on the ACE using a modified linear programming approach inspired by Dawid and Ramsahai. The authors address the limitations of existing methods, particularly their sensitivity to faithfulness assumptions, by introducing relaxations and a fast algorithm based on message passing. The work is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental challenge in causal inference – identifying causal effects from observational data.

The paper is clearly structured, beginning with a thorough background section on instrumental variables, witnesses, and admissible sets. The methodology is presented logically, building from the reinterpretation of Entner’s Rule 1 to the formulation of the linear program and its subsequent relaxations. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution algorithm are valuable additions, offering computational efficiency. The experimental evaluation with both synthetic and real data (influenza vaccination) provides a good assessment of the proposed method. The authors appropriately relate their work to prior art, citing relevant papers on causal inference, instrumental variables, and graphical models (e.g., Pearl [14], Entner et al. [6], Dawid [5], Ramsahai [16]). 

The strengths of the paper lie in its originality and technical soundness. The reinterpretation of Entner’s Rule 1 and the subsequent development of the WPP framework represent a novel contribution. The introduction of relaxations to the linear program and the back-substitution algorithm demonstrate a pragmatic approach to tackling computational complexity. The mathematical formulation appears rigorous, and the experiments provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential avenues for future work.

However, several weaknesses should be addressed. While the explanation is generally clear, the level of mathematical detail in some sections could be improved. The jump from the initial parameterization in Table 1 to the subsequent formulations feels abrupt and could benefit from more intermediate steps. The explanation of the Bayesian learning approach in Section 3.1 is relatively concise and could be expanded. The experiments, while illustrative, feel somewhat limited. The influenza data analysis, while interesting, would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison to existing methods applied to the same dataset. The choice of relaxation parameters seems somewhat arbitrary and could be further justified or explored in a more systematic way.  The sensitivity analysis reported in the supplementary material is appreciated, but a more in-depth exploration within the main paper might be beneficial.  The performance claims regarding computational efficiency, particularly of the back-substitution algorithm, require more rigorous benchmarking against the full linear programming approach. Finally, the discussion of the trade-off between interval width and information loss in the experimental section could be elaborated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to bounding ACE.
*   Addresses a significant problem in causal inference.
*   Clear and logical presentation of the methodology.
*   Well-motivated and justified relaxations to address computational complexity.
*   Supportive experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some sections lack sufficient mathematical detail.
*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   Justification for the choice of relaxation parameters is limited.
*   More rigorous benchmarking of the back-substitution algorithm is needed.
*   The discussion of the trade-offs within the results could be expanded.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the noted weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable and original contribution to the field of causal inference. The proposed WPP framework offers a promising approach to addressing the challenges of identifying causal effects from observational data, particularly in the presence of unmeasured confounding. The technical soundness and clarity of the presentation, coupled with the supportive experimental results, outweigh the limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding mathematical clarity, experimental depth, and justification of parameter choices.
