## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies, leveraging covariate sets and relaxing assumptions commonly made in instrumental variable (IV) and backdoor adjustment approaches. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.'s work on identifying admissible sets, framing these sets as providing "witnesses" that protect against faithfulness violations. The authors introduce a linear programming formulation to find these bounds, and, recognizing the computational cost, develop relaxations and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm for scaling up the approach. The paper also explores Bayesian learning within this framework and provides analytical bounds alongside a back-substitution algorithm for efficient computation. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) offers a fresh perspective on causal inference, blending ideas from IV analysis, backdoor adjustment, and faithfulness assumptions.  It offers a pragmatic approach to handling potential violations of faithfulness, a crucial concern in real-world applications.
* **Addressing Faithfulness Concerns:** Explicitly addressing the fragility of faithfulness is a significant strength.  Many existing methods rely heavily on this assumption, and WPP provides a way to obtain bounds even when faithfulness is questionable.
* **Computational Considerations:** The authors are commendable in acknowledging the computational challenges and proposing relaxations and a fast algorithm to address them.  The analytical bounds and back-substitution algorithm further enhance practicality.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes experiments on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the applicability of the proposed method.
* **Clear Motivation & Connections to Prior Work:** The paper thoroughly situates itself within the existing literature on causal inference, particularly highlighting the work of Entner et al., Dawid, Ramsahai, and Pearl.
* **Bayesian Framework:** Incorporation of Bayesian learning and rejection sampling is a solid addition, allowing for a more nuanced handling of uncertainty.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Accessibility:** The paper is dense and requires significant background knowledge in causal inference. While a brief summary of relevant concepts is provided, readers unfamiliar with the area may struggle to follow the methodology.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method appears sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The paper acknowledges this but doesn't provide extensive guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice. The default parameters chosen in the experiments need stronger justification.
* **Computational Scalability (Still a Concern):** While the authors introduce relaxations, the computational cost remains a potential bottleneck, especially for high-dimensional datasets.  More detailed analysis of scalability and runtime performance is needed.
* **Interpretation of Bounds:** The paper acknowledges that the resulting bounds can be wide and doesn’t always pinpoint a specific causal effect. While this is a consequence of the approach’s conservatism, its practical utility might be limited if the bounds are too broad to be informative.
* **Real Data Analysis:** The real data analysis (influenza vaccination) is somewhat limited in scope.  A more in-depth comparison with other existing methods on the same dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Rejection Sampling in Bayesian Framework:** The description of rejection sampling being used if a large proportion of samples are rejected suggests a potential problem with the prior specification. A more detailed discussion of prior sensitivity is warranted.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined methodology and a clear theoretical foundation. The use of linear programming and Bayesian inference demonstrates a strong understanding of optimization and statistical modeling. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the experiments provide some support for the claims.
* **Clarity:** The writing is dense and requires effort from the reader. While the authors attempt to explain the concepts, the complexity of the subject matter makes it challenging to follow.  Improved organization and more illustrative examples could enhance clarity.
* **Originality:** The combination of ideas and the "Witness Protection Program" framework are novel. The paper builds on existing work but introduces a unique perspective on causal inference and addresses a significant challenge – dealing with violations of faithfulness.
* **Significance:** The potential significance of this work is substantial. Providing a method to obtain bounds on causal effects even when faithfulness is in doubt is valuable.  If the computational challenges can be adequately addressed, this approach could be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners in various fields.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to causal inference with a focus on addressing faithfulness violations.
* Sound theoretical foundation and well-defined methodology.
* Inclusion of computational relaxations and a fast algorithm.
* Evaluation on both synthetic and real data.
* Potential for significant impact in observational studies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* High complexity and potential accessibility issues.
* Parameter sensitivity and lack of detailed guidance on parameter selection.
* Potential computational scalability limitations.
* The generated bounds are potentially too wide to be practically useful.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. While the complexity and potential computational limitations are concerns, the novelty of the approach, the thoroughness of the theoretical development, and the attempt to address practical challenges are commendable. 

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  The authors should:

1.  Provide more detailed guidance on selecting the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄).
2.  Include a more thorough discussion of computational scalability and runtime performance.
3.  Expand the real data analysis with a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods.
4.  Elaborate on the prior specification and sensitivity analysis in the Bayesian framework.
5.  Consider adding more illustrative examples to enhance clarity.
