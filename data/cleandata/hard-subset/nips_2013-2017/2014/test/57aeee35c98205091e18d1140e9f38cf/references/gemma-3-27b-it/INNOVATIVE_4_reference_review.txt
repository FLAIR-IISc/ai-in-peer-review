This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal inference with observational data, offering a novel framework – the Witness Protection Program (WPP) – to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE). The core idea of inverting Entner’s Rule 1 to leverage potential instrumental variables, coupled with a linear programming relaxation and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm for scaling, is genuinely inventive. The attempt to move beyond strict faithfulness assumptions by explicitly modeling violations is particularly appealing. While the methodology appears complex, the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the existing literature on instrumental variables, causal discovery, and faithfulness, appropriately citing relevant work (Pearl, Dawid, Entner et al.).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The WPP framework represents a genuinely new approach to causal inference, offering a compelling alternative to existing methods. The relaxation of faithfulness and the corresponding parameterization are particularly creative.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The grounding in linear programming and the connection to existing bounds (Dawid, Ramsahai) suggest a solid theoretical foundation. The analytical approximations and scaling strategies, while not fully detailed, demonstrate a thoughtful consideration of computational challenges.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – causal inference from observational data – is of immense practical importance across numerous fields. The potential to handle violations of faithfulness, a common concern in real-world applications, is a significant advantage.
*   **Comprehensive Exploration:** The paper considers a range of related work and attempts to integrate various techniques to improve robustness. The inclusion of a Bayesian learning component and the development of a fast approximation algorithm demonstrate a comprehensive approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors introduce relaxations, the computational cost of even the approximated linear programming formulation remains a concern. The scaling possibilities based on analytical approximations need further investigation and a more detailed discussion of practical limitations. The back-substitution algorithm is mentioned but lacks a sufficient level of detail regarding its effectiveness.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while presenting promising results, are somewhat limited in scope. The synthetic studies explore a restricted set of scenarios, and the real-world application to the influenza data is brief. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including a comparison to state-of-the-art causal inference methods on various datasets, would strengthen the claims.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of WPP appears sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While the authors acknowledge this, a more systematic exploration of parameter sensitivity and guidelines for selecting appropriate values would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and can be challenging to follow, especially in Sections 3 and 4. The notation is heavy, and the flow of ideas could be improved with more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples. The connection between the symbolic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm isn't fully articulated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful framework for causal inference.
*   The theoretical foundations appear solid, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
*   The attempt to address the limitations of faithfulness assumptions is a significant contribution.
*   Even with current limitations, the approach offers a promising avenue for future research and development.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The computational complexity of the method may limit its practical applicability.
*   The empirical validation is not extensive enough to fully support the claims.
*   The parameter sensitivity and lack of clear guidance for parameter selection are potential drawbacks.
*   The paper’s density and complexity may hinder its broader adoption.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a bold and innovative paper that addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference. While the method has limitations – primarily in computational complexity and empirical validation – the novelty of the approach and the potential for significant impact outweigh these concerns. The paper introduces a new and potentially transformative perspective on causal inference, even if it requires further refinement and development. I lean strongly towards acceptance, prioritizing the novelty and potential of the work over methodological perfection at this stage. The authors have successfully identified a critical gap in the existing literature and proposed a promising solution. Future work focusing on improving computational efficiency, expanding empirical validation, and providing clear guidance for parameter selection would further solidify the contribution of this paper.



**Score:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
