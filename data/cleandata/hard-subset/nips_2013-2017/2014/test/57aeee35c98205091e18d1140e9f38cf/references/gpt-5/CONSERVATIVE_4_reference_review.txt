Summary
The paper proposes “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data using covariates that are not descendants of X or Y. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a witness W and admissible set Z to assert back-door identifiability under faithfulness, treat W as a (conditional) instrument for X given Z, but allow bounded violations of faithfulness. The authors introduce relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that bound deviations from conditional independence and exclusion, and compute ACE bounds via a linear programming formulation inspired by Dawid and Ramsahai, with latent U integrated out through a polytope construction. Because this is computationally heavy, they derive “symbolic” algebraic bounds and a back-substitution procedure to tighten simple box constraints efficiently. They also present a Bayesian approach that places constrained Dirichlet priors on contingency tables P(Y, X, W | Z = z), uses rejection sampling to enforce inequalities, and returns posterior distributions over ACE bounds. Experiments include synthetic studies comparing against naive estimators and a flu-vaccine observational study; WPP yields intervals that are narrower than standard IV bounds yet wider (hence more conservative) than point estimates under faithfulness.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on: (i) IV partial identification and polytope/LP approaches (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai & Lauritzen), (ii) faithfulness-based admissible set discovery (Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes), and (iii) bounding/sensitivity analyses (Manski). The novelty is to use data-driven “witness/admissible set” discovery to justify partial exclusion and independence, while softening faithfulness via bounded violations operationalized by linear inequalities.

Strengths
- Addresses a central and difficult problem: partial identification and sensitivity to faithfulness/unmeasured confounding.
- Conceptually appealing compromise between brittle point identification under faithfulness and very wide IV bounds; the relaxation parameters make assumptions explicit.
- Solid connection to established LP/polytope formulations for bounds; clear reduction to standard IV (w=0, β=β̄=1 etc.) and to chains when y=x=0.
- The symbolic bounds and back-substitution heuristic are practically useful and computationally cheaper than repeatedly solving LPs.
- Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty avoids naive plug-in testing of inequalities; code is promised.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Calibration of relaxation parameters: The methodology hinges on user-specified w, y, x, β, β̄. The paper defers principled selection to the supplement and future work. Without data-driven calibration with error control, different plausible choices can lead to materially different bounds, undermining objectivity and reproducibility.
- Guarantees and operating characteristics: There is no theoretical coverage guarantee or finite-sample behavior analysis (e.g., bounds contain ACE with high probability under specified relaxations). The “falsification test” (reject if >95% posterior samples violate constraints) is ad hoc.
- Use of approximate independencies: In cases where no valid witness should exist, the search still proposes witnesses >90% of the time due to relaxed Bayesian model selection. This raises the risk that the method produces bounds that are inappropriately narrow because they import structure from spurious independencies; no robustness theorem quantifies the resulting bias in bounds.
- Clarity and rigor of the LP/polytope treatment: The main text omits crucial details (Table 1/2, full inequalities, mapping to dual polytope), relying heavily on the supplement. Notation is dense (η, ζ, κ, ω with superscripts/subscripts) and several steps are only sketched.
- Symbolic bounds are not tight by design. While acceptable as a heuristic, the paper does not quantify the additional conservatism introduced versus the exact LP, nor provide runtime/accuracy tradeoffs beyond qualitative statements.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Synthetic setups are tailored; metrics for intervals are unconventional (distance to nearest point; trivial [-1,1] always scores zero), and there is no analysis of coverage, width vs. correctness tradeoffs, or sensitivity curves across relaxation parameters. The real-data study is a single example with no ground truth; the narrower interval than standard IV is unsurprising given added assumptions, but the choice w=y=x=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1 is not justified, and sensitivity is relegated to the supplement.
- Missing comparisons. Beyond NE1/NE2 and faithfulness, there is no comparison to established sensitivity frameworks for unmeasured confounding (e.g., Rosenbaum’s Γ, Imbens’ selection models, VanderWeele–Shpitser bias formulas) or to IV LATE bounds with monotonicity. These are standard baselines for practitioners.

Quality
Technically interesting but incomplete. The bounding machinery is consistent with established LP approaches, but key methodological choices (parameter calibration, falsification, use under approximate independencies) lack theoretical guarantees. Several derivations and algorithmic details are deferred.

Clarity
Moderate. The high-level idea is clear, but notation and organization hinder reproducibility. Important tables/inequalities are missing from the main text; multiple typos and reference inconsistencies reduce confidence.

Originality
Moderate. The combination—using Rule 1 to propose conditional instruments and bounding under controlled unfaithfulness—is novel to my knowledge, although each component (IV bounds, discovery of admissible sets, sensitivity/bounds) is established.

Significance
Potentially useful if better grounded, but current practical impact is limited by the lack of principled calibration and guarantees. The computational heuristics may be of interest to the partial identification community.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a principled mapping from statistical tests of (conditional) independence to relaxation parameters with error control (e.g., translate confidence intervals on conditional odds ratios to bounds on η, δ; deliver coverage guarantees for the ACE bounds).
- Prove monotonicity/consistency properties: as w,y,x→0, β→β̄→1, do bounds shrink appropriately to back-door or IV limits? Are bounds monotone in each parameter?
- Report runtime and constraint counts for LP vs. back-substitution across datasets; quantify the extra width from symbolic bounds relative to exact LP.
- Add comparisons to standard sensitivity analyses and IV LATE bounds under monotonicity; include coverage and width across varying relaxations.
- Clarify the falsification procedure and provide simulation-based Type I/II error analysis.
- Improve exposition: include key inequalities in the main paper, streamline notation, fix typos/URLs, and make the search procedure for (W,Z) explicit.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a conceptually appealing compromise.
- Builds on and connects to established methods; derives usable heuristics.
- Code availability.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on uncalibrated tuning parameters; no guarantees.
- Overuse of approximate independencies; risk of misleadingly narrow bounds.
- Limited empirical validation and missing key baselines.
- Clarity issues and reliance on supplement for core details.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is intriguing and could be impactful, but the current paper lacks principled calibration, theoretical guarantees, and thorough empirical validation against established sensitivity/IV baselines. Strengthening these aspects and improving clarity would make a stronger case for acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: High on methodological concerns; medium on implementation specifics due to missing details.
