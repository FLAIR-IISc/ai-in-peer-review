- Paper introduces Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound ACE from observational data by relaxing faithfulness with interpretable parameters.  
- Reinterprets Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to produce bounds rather than point estimates.  
- Proposes a linear programming framework with relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) controlling faithfulness deviations.  
- Presents a Bayesian approach to handle uncertainty in the bounds.  
- Develops an efficient back-substitution algorithm to avoid full linear programming.  
- Validated on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination study.  
- Novel technical contribution: systematic, interpretable relaxation of faithfulness.  
- Practical algorithm: back-substitution yields substantial computational improvement and is implemented accessibly.  
- Strong empirical validation: outperforms naive estimators and is robust to faithfulness violations.  
- Comprehensive theoretical treatment: algebraic bounds clarify how relaxations affect results.  
- Clear literature positioning between faithfulness-based and IV approaches.  
- Lacks concrete guidance for selecting relaxation parameters; default values are unjustified.  
- No proof of optimality or tightness guarantees for the provided bounds.  
- Claims about computational efficiency lack comparative runtime data.  
- Real-world example unclear: choice of DM as witness over intended IV GRP is not explained.  
- Limited empirical comparisons: missing benchmarks against other causal bound methods (e.g., Geiger et al. 2014).  
- Overall assessment: significant, well-executed contribution that balances robustness and practicality.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Revision suggestions: add guidance/sensitivity analysis for parameter selection.  
- Revision suggestions: provide runtime comparison between full LP and back-substitution.  
- Revision suggestions: clarify why DM was chosen as witness instead of GRP in the influenza study.  
- Revision suggestions: discuss scaling with covariate dimensionality.  
- Revision suggestions: include brief empirical comparison with other causal bound methods (e.g., Geiger et al.).
