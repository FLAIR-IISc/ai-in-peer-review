Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a new framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data using binary variables. The core idea is to invert the use of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (which normally identifies admissible covariate sets under faithfulness to enable point identification via back-door) and instead treat a “witness” variable as a candidate instrumental variable conditional on a (searched) set Z. Rather than requiring exact faithfulness, WPP introduces bounded violations of local independence via relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄), interpolating between the standard IV model (Balke & Pearl) and the fully faithful chain W → X → Y. These relaxations define a partially identified model over the latent-augmented parameterization, leading to linear (and some implicit nonlinear) constraints that can be handled in a modified Dawid/Ramsahai linear programming framework to produce ACE bounds.

To address computational burden and interpretability, the paper derives new analytical, symbolic bounds that explicitly depend on the relaxation parameters and introduces a back-substitution/message-passing style algorithm that quickly refines box constraints on key parameters. For uncertainty quantification and model falsification, the authors adopt a Bayesian approach: constrained Dirichlet priors on P(Y,X,W|Z) and P(Z) with rejection sampling yield posterior distributions over the ACE bounds. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza vaccine dataset illustrate that WPP can produce substantially tighter bounds than standard IV bounds, while being more robust than purely faithfulness-based point estimators.

This work builds on and connects strands in partial identification (Manski), IV bounds (Balke & Pearl), LP formulations (Dawid; Ramsahai), causal discovery under faithfulness (Spirtes et al.; Entner et al.), and IV polytope geometry (Richardson & Robins), offering a novel hybrid: faithfulness-driven structure discovery with controlled, parameterized relaxations feeding into bounding machinery.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual synthesis: repurposing witnesses from admissible-set discovery into conditional IVs, with explicit, tunable relaxations of faithfulness. This creates a principled continuum between point identification and worst-case bounds.
- New technical contributions: (i) linear-program-based bounding under bounded local violations; (ii) symbolic algebraic bounds as functions of relaxation parameters; (iii) an efficient back-substitution algorithm that approximates the full LP while remaining conservative; (iv) a Bayesian sampling scheme for posterior distributions over bounds with a pragmatic falsification test.
- Practicality: The framework acknowledges finite-sample “near-faithfulness” and gives users control via interpretable relaxation parameters. Code availability is a plus.
- Empirical indication of utility: On the flu dataset, WPP yields much tighter bounds than standard IV. Synthetic studies show robustness relative to naive back-door and strictly faithfulness-based estimators, especially when back-door using all covariates is guaranteed to be wrong.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Parameter calibration: The bounds, tightness, and conclusions hinge on choices of (w, x, y, β, β̄). The paper defers principled selection to the supplement. A clearer main-text guideline (interpretation, default heuristics, and sensitivity plots) would substantially improve usability.
- Scope and assumptions: Binary variables only. Discussion of extension to multinomial/continuous variables (e.g., via monotone transforms, discretization, or alternative parameterizations) would broaden impact.
- Theoretical guarantees: It is claimed the WPP bounds are “at least as conservative” as faithfulness-based point estimates and always cover those estimates. Please formalize coverage guarantees and monotonicity with respect to relaxation parameters, and delineate when estimation noise or constraint rejection might violate these guarantees.
- Computational scaling: The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet with rejection sampling and exhaustive search over Z will struggle when |Z| grows. While the back-substitution algorithm helps, more detail on complexity, pruning strategies, or integration with scalable causal discovery (beyond brute-force) would be valuable.
- Experimental breadth: Comparisons could include other partial-identification approaches and sensitivity analyses (e.g., Rosenbaum-style bounds, proximal causal inference where applicable). It would help to report interval widths alongside error metrics systematically, and to clarify how often WPP falsifies models under realistic sample sizes.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation is dense and footnotes bleed into the main text. Equations (3)–(6) would benefit from a clearer semantic explanation (what precisely is bounded, how to interpret y vs x vs w, and β, β̄ as selection bias modifiers). The “posterior over bounds” is easy to misinterpret; a short discussion contrasting it with a posterior over ACE would prevent confusion. Minor typos (e.g., Polymake URL) and edit issues exist.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound overall, with correct connections to prior IV/LP literature and careful attention to identifiability pitfalls under near-faithfulness. Some proofs and algorithmic details are deferred, and empirical validation, while indicative, could be broader.

Clarity: Generally well-organized but dense. Improving interpretability of the relaxation parameters, adding a running example, and tightening the exposition of the Bayesian step would help.

Originality: High. The reinterpretation of witnesses as conditional IVs under bounded faithfulness violations, plus the symbolic/back-substitution machinery, is a fresh and bold direction.

Significance: High. Many observational studies fall between strict faithfulness and vacuous IV bounds. Providing a tunable, computationally viable bridge is impactful for both methodology and practice.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide formal monotonicity and coverage proofs for the bounds as functions of (w, x, y, β, β̄)?
- How sensitive are results to the BDeu prior and independence model selection for identifying (W, Z)? Could cross-validation or stability selection help?
- What are the practical guidelines for setting relaxation parameters in domains like healthcare or economics? Any elicitation templates for domain experts?
- How does WPP behave with weak instruments (conditional on Z)? Any diagnostics?
- Plans for non-binary extensions?

Recommendation: Accept (leaning strong). The paper introduces a genuinely novel and promising framework that advances partial identification in causal inference. While some details (parameter calibration, scalability, broader comparisons) could be strengthened, the conceptual contribution and technical innovations merit inclusion at NeurIPS.
