Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y using observational data and covariates W. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a witness-admissible set pair (W, Z) for back-door adjustment, treat W as an approximate instrument conditional on Z, but protect against violations of faithfulness by imposing bounded relaxations on exclusion, independence, and parameter stability:
- |η?x1 − η?x0| ≤ w (approximate exclusion W → Y),
- |η?xw − P(Y=1|X=x,W=w)| ≤ y and |δ?w − P(X=1|W=w)| ≤ x (approximate stability),
- β P(U) ≤ P(U|W=w) ≤ β̄ P(U) (approximate independence W ⊥ U).
Given these, bounds on ACE are obtained via a linear program over latent-augmented parameters, with generalizations of Balke–Pearl and Dawid–Ramsahai polytopes. Since full LP is costly and opaque, the authors derive looser algebraic (symbolic) bounds and propose a back-substitution “message-passing-like” tightening scheme. They also present a Bayesian workflow using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to propagate uncertainty in P(Y,X,W|Z) and P(Z). Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza encouragement design suggest that WPP can yield narrower bounds than standard IV in some cases and is more robust than naïve back-door estimators when faithfulness is only approximately satisfied.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and connects: (i) point-identification via faithfulness (Entner et al.), (ii) IV partial identification (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins), and (iii) inequality constraints in causal models (Evans, Richardson). The novelty lies in systematically “inverting” a discovery rule to generate candidate instruments and introducing tunable relaxations that interpolate between the chain model and the standard IV.

Strengths
- Conceptually interesting compromise between brittle point-identification via faithfulness and often-wide IV bounds.
- Clear structural mapping: specific relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) correspond to interpretable violations (exclusion, independence, stability).
- Combines LP-based sharp bounds with faster algebraic relaxations and a practical back-substitution tightening algorithm.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty over observed contingency tables avoids pure plug-in and allows model falsification.
- The influenza case study illustrates how WPP can produce substantially narrower bounds than standard IV in one real dataset.

Weaknesses and concerns
Major technical/clarity issues
- Missing elements: The paper repeatedly references Figure 1, Table 1/2, Table 3, and equations (7)–(10), which are not included. This prevents verification of definitions (e.g., η?xw, δ?w, ζ?yx.w), the algebraic bounds, and empirical results.
- Notation is inconsistent and sometimes ambiguous. Symbols with “?” superscripts/subscripts appear without a precise definitional table. The switch between ζ, κ, η, ω without a complete derivation is confusing; the mapping ζyx.w/β̄ ≤ κyx.w ≤ ζyx.w/β is stated but not derived. Equation (2) involves η11, η10, η01, η00 yet the mapping to η?xw is unclear. δ or δ? vs ω uses are inconsistent.
- LP construction details are underspecified. The step “up to 12 points for the case where w > 0” for a 4D polytope is puzzling (a 4D hypercube has 16 vertices); if constraints reduce vertices, provide proof or enumeration. The mapping from extreme points of {δ?}×{η?} to the dual polytope over observables needs a clearer, rigorous derivation.
- The claim “any choice of w, y, x, β, β̄ is guaranteed to provide bounds at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator of [6], which is always covered by the bounds” is strong and unproven in the text. A proof or counterexample conditions should be provided.
- Bayesian section relies on constrained Dirichlet priors sampled via rejection. No discussion of acceptance rates beyond a heuristic “95% rejected ⇒ bad fit,” no choice of M, and no alternative samplers (e.g., hit-and-run, HMC with inequality constraints) are given. Posterior sensitivity to BDeu hyperparameters is asserted but not demonstrated here.
- The back-substitution algorithm is only sketched. No formal guarantee on tightness loss relative to the full LP; no complexity analysis; no proof beyond “bounds never widen.” Referenced inequalities (7)–(10) are missing, precluding reproducibility.
- Experimental evaluation is incomplete in the main text. Table 3 is missing; interval widths, coverage, and fraction of vacuous bounds are not reported. Comparing point estimates to intervals using distance to the interval without penalizing width is biased; while the authors acknowledge this, the paper lacks a principled utility-based comparison (e.g., average width at fixed coverage, regret under minimax loss).
- The choice of relaxation parameters (w=x=y=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1) is ad hoc; no sensitivity plots in the main text; no calibration guidance beyond “see Supplement.” This is critical, as conclusions depend on these values.
- In the influenza analysis, excluding the randomized encouragement GRP from admissible sets is a strong assumption; selecting DM as witness seems nonstandard, and the causal plausibility is not discussed. Narrower bounds than standard IV could reflect model misspecification from approximate independences.

Minor issues and formatting
- Numerous typos and formatting errors: “Polymake” URL misspelled; “inuenza,” “parameterizatios”; dangling footnotes merged into text; inconsistent equation numbering; malformed algorithm pseudocode; “We supress” → “suppress”; “LIV ≤ ACE ≤ UIV” with undefined symbols.
- Citations are inconsistent/incomplete (years missing; some entries truncated; e.g., Balke & Pearl JASA page numbers but no year).
- Scope limited to binary variables; this restriction should be stated up front and its implications discussed.

Quality
Technically promising but currently underspecified and difficult to verify. Missing figures, definitions, and derivations prevent assessment of correctness. The Bayesian and algebraic components need more rigor and clarity.

Clarity
Currently below NIPS standards due to missing references, inconsistent notation, and heavy dependence on the Supplementary for core content.

Originality
The inversion of Rule 1 to construct approximate IVs with tunable relaxations is novel and interesting. The blend of LP bounds, algebraic relaxations, and a Bayesian outer loop is a useful synthesis.

Significance
Potentially significant if made solid; partial identification with user-controllable faithfulness violations is important in practice. As written, the impact is limited by incomplete exposition and evaluation.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and relevant idea bridging discovery-based identification and IV bounds.
- Provides a practical path when faithfulness is only approximately true.
- Potentially useful tools (algebraic bounds, back-substitution) if fully specified.

Cons for acceptance
- Critical missing content (figures/tables/equations) and unclear notation.
- Key claims lack proofs; LP construction and Bayesian procedure not fully justified.
- Experimental section insufficiently reported; parameter sensitivity and interval width/coverage not adequately evaluated.
- Many minor but pervasive presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include complete definitions (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω), all referenced figures/tables/equations, and a worked example mapping from observables to constraints.
- Prove the “coverage of back-door estimate” claim or state conditions.
- Provide a principled method to choose relaxation parameters, with sensitivity analyses in the main text.
- Replace rejection sampling with an efficient constrained sampler; report ESS and acceptance rates.
- Report interval widths, coverage, and fraction of vacuous bounds; compare against Balke–Pearl and Dawid–Ramsahai across settings.
- Clarify the influenza analysis assumptions and compare to LATE/monotonicity bounds as a baseline.

Recommendation: Reject (current form). Confidence: High. Potentially strong paper after major revision.
