Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y using observational data with binary covariates W that are not effects of X or Y. The key ideas are: (i) invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (which, under faithfulness, uses a “witness” W and conditioning set Z to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment) so that (W, Z) is instead used to create conditional instrumental-variable-like bounds; (ii) replace exact faithfulness with bounded violations represented as local constraints on conditional probabilities (parameters w, x, y, β, β̄); (iii) compute bounds via a linear programming formulation extending Dawid and Ramsahai’s LP approach for partial compliance/IV models and derive additional conservative symbolic inequalities enabling a fast back-substitution algorithm; and (iv) embed this in a Bayesian workflow that accounts for estimation uncertainty via constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling, delivering posterior distributions over ACE bounds and simple falsification checks.

This contributes to the literature on partial identification and instrumental inequalities (Balke-Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson et al.) by leveraging structure discovered from conditional independences (Entner et al.) but avoiding brittle reliance on exact faithfulness. The paper aims to bridge point-identification under faithfulness (potentially unstable) and worst-case IV bounds (often wide) by allowing controlled, interpretable relaxations.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Using Rule 1 “in reverse” to generate conditional IV-like bounding problems is elegant and connects discovery with identification in a principled way.
- Flexibility and honesty: The relaxation parameters provide transparent control over faithfulness violations; the method yields bounds rather than potentially misleading point estimates, aligning with Manski’s perspective on partial identification.
- Technical contributions: Extension of LP-based bounding under new constraints; derivation of symbolic (algebraic) inequalities; a simple back-substitution tightening scheme that is much faster than full LP while remaining conservative.
- Bayesian treatment: Constrained Dirichlet priors and posterior predictive falsification acknowledge sampling variability, which is often ignored in IV inequality testing.
- Empirical illustration: Synthetic studies designed to foil naive back-door adjustment; re-analysis of the flu encouragement design showing WPP can provide tighter, data-driven bounds than standard IV in the presence of additional (approximate) independences.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Choice of relaxation parameters: The paper defers principled selection of (w, x, y, β, β̄) to the supplement and future work. These knobs critically control tightness and validity; without guidance, practitioners may struggle to use WPP responsibly. A calibration strategy (e.g., mapping domain beliefs about residual confounding or direct effects into parameter ranges) is needed.
- Theoretical guarantees and transparency: The main text does not clearly state formal theorems guaranteeing that, under the imposed relaxations, the LP bounds are sound (contain the true ACE) and how the symbolic bounds relate to the tight LP bounds (beyond being conservative). Monotonicity and nesting properties of bounds as a function of parameters should be explicit.
- Statistical testing/falsification: The “95% rejection in M proposals” rule and the BDeu-based independence selection are ad hoc. More principled posterior predictive checks, Bayes factors under constrained priors, or frequentist tests with error control would strengthen the evidential treatment.
- Scalability and robustness: The exhaustive search over Z and rejection sampling in constrained spaces will not scale beyond modest Z. While the back-substitution algorithm accelerates bound computation, the end-to-end pipeline (search + posterior sampling + constraint checking) lacks complexity analysis and runtime benchmarks. The suggestion to use discrete PCA to reduce W is sensible but not evaluated.
- Empirical evaluation: Comparisons are mainly to naive estimators and Entner’s faithfulness-based point estimate. It would be valuable to compare to established sensitivity analyses for unmeasured confounding (e.g., Rosenbaum, VanderWeele-Shpitser frameworks), monotonicity-enhanced IV bounds, or recent bounding-with-confounding methods. Coverage properties (how often the true ACE lies within posterior mean bounds) and width-accuracy tradeoffs across parameter settings should be reported.
- Clarity issues: The notation is dense; some key objects (e.g., Table 1/2 parameters) are only in the supplement. Minor typos (e.g., Polymake URL). A clearer roadmap and a compact example tracing (W, Z) through the constraints to final bounds would help readers reproduce the method.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound in spirit and builds on solid prior art. However, some derivations and guarantees are deferred; falsification/testing is somewhat heuristic; and empirical evaluation, while suggestive, is limited in breadth. The conservative nature of symbolic bounds is acknowledged.
- Clarity: Generally well-written but dense. Core ideas are understandable, yet reproduction without the supplement would be difficult. Improve exposition of assumptions (3)–(6), provide a worked toy example, and clarify the relationship between κ, ω, ζ, η throughout.
- Originality: High. The inversion of Rule 1 for bounding, the specific relaxations, and the symbolic/back-substitution machinery constitute a novel and coherent contribution.
- Significance: Potentially high. A practical middle ground between brittle identification and vacuous worst-case bounds is valuable to both ML and causal inference communities. Adoption hinges on parameter calibration guidance and scalable implementations.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled bridge between causal discovery and partial identification.
- Provides actionable and interpretable levers to control faithfulness violations.
- Advances computational tools for bounding (symbolic constraints + back-substitution).
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty in inequality-constrained settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient guidance and validation for choosing relaxation parameters.
- Limited empirical comparisons and scalability assessment.
- Heuristic falsification/testing procedures.
- Some theoretical guarantees not clearly stated in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a section giving default calibration recipes for (w, x, y, β, β̄) tied to domain knowledge, with sensitivity plots.
- State formal theorems on soundness and monotonicity of bounds vs. parameters, and quantify the gap between symbolic and tight LP bounds.
- Replace or augment the ad hoc falsification with posterior predictive checks or Bayes factors under constrained priors; report false-positive/false-negative rates on synthetic data.
- Provide run-time and acceptance-rate benchmarks, and compare to standard IV bounds with/without monotonicity and to mainstream sensitivity analyses.
- Include a small worked example from raw counts to bounds to make the method reproducible from the main paper.

Overall: This is an original and promising contribution. With stronger guidance on parameter selection, clearer guarantees, and broader empirical validation, it would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance if these issues can be addressed.
