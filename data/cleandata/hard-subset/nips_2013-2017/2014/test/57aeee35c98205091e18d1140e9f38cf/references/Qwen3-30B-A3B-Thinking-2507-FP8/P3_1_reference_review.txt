

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using covariates that are not affected by X or Y. The authors build upon Entner et al.'s work on "witness" variables, reinterpreting them as instrumental variables rather than discarding them. The core innovation is a linear programming approach that generates ACE bounds while allowing for controlled violations of faithfulness assumptions. The method includes a Bayesian learning component for uncertainty quantification and a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm for symbolic bounds. The paper demonstrates the approach on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution. The theoretical development is rigorous, with clear derivations of the linear programming constraints and algebraic bounds. The authors carefully address the limitations of faithfulness assumptions, which is a genuine problem in practice. The experimental design is well-justified, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines (NE1, NE2, and Entner's method). The Bayesian implementation is principled, using constrained Dirichlet priors that avoid the sensitivity issues of direct priors on latent variables.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to causal inference literature. While instrumental variable bounds are not new, the specific reinterpretation of Entner's "witness" variables as instrumental variables and the systematic framework for handling faithfulness violations represents a novel approach. The back-substitution algorithm for efficiently computing symbolic bounds is also a useful technical contribution that advances the state of the art in computational causal inference.

**Significance:** The work addresses a critical practical problem in observational studies where faithfulness assumptions often fail. By providing bounds that are robust to faithfulness violations (which can lead to misleading point estimates), the method offers a more reliable tool for practitioners. The influenza vaccine case study demonstrates real-world relevance, showing how the method can yield more informative bounds than standard instrumental variable approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section clearly establishes the problem context, and the methodology is presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The Bayesian learning component and back-substitution algorithm are explained accessibly, with clear connections to the underlying theory.

## Weaknesses

**Parameter Selection Guidance:** While the paper demonstrates the method with specific parameter choices (w = x = y = 0.2, β = 0.9, β̄ = 1.1), it lacks clear guidance on how to select these relaxation parameters in practice. This is a significant limitation for practitioners who need to apply the method to new datasets. The paper mentions "a few choices of default parameters" but doesn't provide principled justification.

**Computational Efficiency Claims:** The back-substitution algorithm is presented as "an order of magnitude more efficient" than full numerical optimization, but the paper doesn't provide concrete benchmarks comparing the two approaches. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of this efficiency gain.

**Comparison with Existing Bounds Methods:** The paper claims to provide a "novel compromise between point estimators given by faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables," but doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing bounds methods like Manski's (1990) or Ramsahai's (2016). A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

**Real-World Justification:** In the influenza case study, the authors reject GRP (the reminder letter) as a witness but don't provide sufficient justification for why DM (diabetes history) with AGE and SEX is a valid witness. More detailed explanation of the causal reasoning would strengthen the application.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to causal inference with observational data. The method addresses a genuine problem (faithfulness violations) with a technically sound approach, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

I recommend **acceptance** at NIPS. The paper is well-written, makes a clear contribution to the field, and has significant practical implications for causal inference in observational studies. The minor weaknesses noted above can be addressed in revision, but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

**Minor Suggestions:**
1. Include a table comparing computational complexity of the back-substitution algorithm versus full linear programming
2. Provide more detailed guidance on selecting relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄)
3. Clarify the distinction between this method and existing bounds-based approaches like Manski's
4. Add more justification for witness selection in the real-world case study

The paper successfully bridges theoretical causal inference with practical observational studies, offering a more reliable tool for researchers who must work with non-randomized data. This represents a meaningful advance in the field of causal inference.
