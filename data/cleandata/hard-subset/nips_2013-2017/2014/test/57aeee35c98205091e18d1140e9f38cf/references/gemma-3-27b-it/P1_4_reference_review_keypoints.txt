- Presents a novel method to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE) by reinterpreting Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to find instruments that bound rather than identify ACE.
- Frames faithfulness violations as bounded local independence violations and uses linear programming (LP) to compute ACE bounds.
- Introduces relaxations and a message-passing-like algorithm to reduce LP computational cost and improve scalability.
- Situates work in causal inference literature, citing Pearl, Spirtes et al., Dawid, Ramsahai, Entner et al., and discusses sensitivity to faithfulness and finite-sample issues.
- Novel reinterpretation of Rule 1 shifts focus from point estimation to robust bounding under uncertainty.
- Explicitly addresses faithfulness concerns, acknowledging and mitigating reliance on the assumption.
- LP framework provides a systematic, mathematically grounded approach to deriving bounds.
- Relaxation techniques and the fast algorithm are important for making the method computationally feasible in higher dimensions.
- Comprehensive experiments include synthetic studies and a real-world influenza vaccination application.
- Paper is well-written, well-structured, and supplemented with useful supplementary material.
- Core LP formulation remains computationally expensive, especially as covariate count grows.
- Method relies on several relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) whose choices materially affect bounds and lack clear selection guidance.
- Relaxations can produce overly conservative (wide) bounds that may limit practical usefulness.
- Bayesian implementation adds complexity and computational burden; rejection sampling is not ideal for high-dimensional settings.
- The influenza example’s exclusion of GRP from admissible sets is insufficiently justified and connection to encouragement design literature is weak.
- Mathematical derivations and LP formulation appear correct, but empirical validation needs broader testing across datasets and parameter settings.
- Some technical sections (LP formulation, back-substitution algorithm) need clearer exposition and visual aids to improve accessibility.
- Contribution is original and potentially significant for robust causal inference in observational studies.
- Potential impact in epidemiology, economics, and public policy given improved robustness to faithfulness violations.
- Main acceptance concerns: scalability, parameter sensitivity, conservative bounds, and added Bayesian complexity.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, including guidance on parameter selection, strategies to mitigate computational complexity, stronger justification for GRP exclusion, better literature connections, and a limitations discussion.
