Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a methodology to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y using observational data and a set of observed covariates W not affected by X or Y. The central idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 for covariate selection under faithfulness: instead of using (W,Z) pairs to justify back-door adjustment and a point estimate, the method treats W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z and computes ACE bounds. To mitigate the brittleness of strict faithfulness, the paper introduces bounded relaxations that allow controlled violations of local independencies through parameters w, y, x, β, β̄. These relaxations yield a family of linear programs that produce bounds on ACE, generalizing prior IV bounds (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) and recovering chain and standard IV models as special cases. Because the LP can be expensive, the paper further derives analytical (symbolic) inequalities and a back-substitution procedure that provides fast, slightly looser bounds. A Bayesian procedure with constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling is used to propagate sampling uncertainty and test model feasibility, delivering posterior distributions over bounds for each (W,Z) pair found by a Rule-1-guided search.

This work sits at the interface of causal discovery under faithfulness (e.g., PC, Entner et al.) and partial identification/IV bounding (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson et al.). It contributes a hybrid approach that (i) uses faithfulness only to suggest instruments/admissible sets, (ii) protects against near-violations of faithfulness via bounded relaxations, and (iii) quantifies uncertainty over bounds. Empirically, it compares against naive back-door adjustments and the Entner et al. faithfulness-based point estimator on synthetic data, and applies to a flu-vaccine encouragement design, producing tighter bounds than the standard IV bounds.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LP formulation extends established IV bounding techniques to include bounded departures from independence via constraints (w,y,x,β,β̄). The construction appears correct and recovers known special cases. The symbolic bounds and back-substitution are conservative relaxations; this is clearly acknowledged. The Bayesian scheme accounts for sampling variability and includes a simple falsification test.
- Assumptions and guarantees: The paper states that any choice of relaxation parameters yields bounds at least as conservative as faithfulness-based back-door estimates; a formal theorem/proof sketch in the main text would strengthen this claim. The feasibility and tightness depend on user-chosen relaxation parameters; guidance is deferred to the supplement.
- Algorithms and computation: The LP pipeline and the back-substitution heuristic are well-motivated. Scalability of the Bayesian rejection sampling with constrained Dirichlet priors is a concern for larger Z; the paper limits scope to modest |Z| and uses posterior-mean tables for the final, tight LP pass.
- Experiments: Synthetic studies are designed to invalidate full W adjustment and explore cases with/without a valid witness, including “hard” and “easy” instances. Evaluation acknowledges the trade-off between interval width and error. The real-data case (flu vaccine encouragement) is informative: WPP rejects the canonical instrument and finds an alternative witness+admissible set, delivering tighter bounds than standard IV. However, more extensive sensitivity analyses to the relaxation parameters and more real datasets would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- The high-level narrative is clear and well-situated in prior work. However, much technical detail (tables, full constraints, proofs, parameter-selection framework) is in the supplement. Some notation and references to missing tables impede self-containment. The description of the search procedure is generic; reproducibility relies on supplemental materials and code.
- Minor issues: typos (e.g., “poymake” vs. “polymake”, “supress”), inline footnotes, and occasional dense passages could be streamlined.

Originality
- The inversion of Rule 1 to generate IV-style bounding problems, the specific bounded-relaxation parameterization, the combination of LP bounds with constrained-Dirichlet Bayesian updating, and the symbolic back-substitution approximations constitute a novel and coherent contribution. The work clearly distinguishes itself from both pure faithfulness-based identification and classical IV bounds.

Significance
- The approach offers a practical compromise between fragile point identification via faithfulness and often-wide IV bounds, with knobs to control allowable violations of independence. This is relevant for many observational studies in ML and the broader causal inference community. Impact will depend on principled selection of relaxation parameters and scalability; the provided code and initial guidance help, but further development is needed.

Pros
- Conceptually compelling bridge between causal discovery and partial identification.
- General framework that recovers known models as special cases and extends IV bounds with interpretable relaxations.
- Provides both exact LP and fast symbolic approximations.
- Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty and model falsification.
- Solid synthetic design; real-data illustration with tighter bounds than standard IV.
- Code availability.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on user-chosen relaxation parameters; limited guidance in the main text.
- Many core technical details deferred to the supplement; clarity and self-containment could be improved.
- Rejection-sampling-based Bayesian learning may not scale; no complexity or runtime analysis is provided.
- Comparisons mix intervals and point estimates; more systematic reporting of interval widths and coverage would aid interpretation.
- Limited real-world validation and sensitivity exploration across datasets and parameter settings.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a formal statement and proof (or sketch) that the back-door point estimate is always covered by WPP bounds for any admissible relaxation parameters.
- Offer principled defaults or elicitation strategies for w,y,x,β,β̄, and discuss diagnostics for non-vacuous bounds.
- Report computational complexity and empirical runtimes for both LP and back-substitution across problem sizes.
- Expand sensitivity analyses (e.g., width vs. coverage trade-offs) and add more real datasets.
- Clarify the search procedure, multiple-testing control when scanning many (W,Z), and the robustness of the BDeu-based independence decisions.

Recommendation
On balance, the paper presents a technically sound and original framework with clear potential significance for robust causal effect assessment from observational data. Despite clarity and practical guidance gaps, the core ideas are valuable and timely. I recommend acceptance, contingent on improving exposition and providing stronger guidance on choosing relaxation parameters and reporting computational costs.
