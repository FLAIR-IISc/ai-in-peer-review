**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data, termed the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP). The core idea is to build upon the covariate selection procedure of Entner et al. [6], which uses conditional independence tests under the faithfulness assumption to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment. Instead of using a discovered (witness, admissible set) pair to generate a point estimate, the authors reinterpret the witness as a candidate instrumental variable (IV). They then relax the strict independence assumptions implied by faithfulness, parameterizing these violations with a set of user-defined constants. This allows them to adapt the linear programming (LP) framework of Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16] to derive bounds on the ACE. To address the computational cost of the LP, the paper also proposes a faster, approximate method based on deriving and solving a system of symbolic algebraic inequalities. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and a real-world study on influenza vaccination.

**Strengths**

The central contribution of this paper—bridging the gap between faithfulness-based causal discovery and IV-based bounding methods—is both novel and significant. The idea of treating faithfulness not as a binary assumption but as a spectrum, controlled by explicit relaxation parameters, is a pragmatic and potentially powerful approach for real-world observational studies where exact conditional independences are rare. This provides a principled compromise between potentially biased point estimates that rely on strong, untestable assumptions and the often-vacuous bounds that result from making no assumptions at all. The application to the influenza dataset, demonstrating a substantial tightening of the classical IV bounds, effectively showcases the potential utility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promise of its core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological detail, and experimental validation. My concerns are numerous and, in aggregate, prevent me from confidently assessing the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work.

1.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The manuscript is exceptionally difficult to follow due to missing critical information. Figures 1(a)-1(e), which are central to the background section, are not included. Table 1, which should define the core parameterization of the model, is missing. Table 2, containing the derived algebraic bounds, is absent. Table 3, which presents the main experimental results, is also missing. Without these elements, key sections of the paper are rendered nearly incomprehensible. For instance, the derivation of the ACE in Equation (2) in terms of the η parameters is opaque without the definitions from Table 1, and it is not immediately clear how it connects to the standard do-calculus definition. The entire discussion of the algebraic bounds in Section 4 is impossible to verify or understand without Table 2.

2.  **Methodological Rigor and Justification:**
    *   **Parameter Selection:** The entire method hinges on the choice of relaxation parameters {w, y, x, β, β̄}. The paper explicitly defers any discussion on how to choose these parameters to supplementary material and future work. This is a critical omission. Without guidance on setting these values, the method is not a practical tool but a theoretical curiosity. The bounds are a direct function of these arbitrary choices, and the paper provides no data-driven way to inform them.
    *   **Ad-hoc Heuristics:** The proposed "falsification test" in the Bayesian procedure—deeming a model a "bad fit" if more than 95% of rejection samples are rejected—is a crude heuristic. This lacks statistical justification and is an arbitrary rule of thumb, not a principled model-checking procedure.
    *   **Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 is a high-level sketch that omits crucial details. The search procedure for admissible sets is "left unspecified," and the falsification step is vague. This is insufficient for reproducibility.
    *   **Back-substitution Algorithm:** The procedure for handling cases where the fast back-substitution algorithm produces an invalid result (i.e., the posterior mean of the contingency table falls outside the feasible region of the full LP) is described as a fallback to simply using the looser bounds. This feels like an ad-hoc patch rather than a robustly designed component of the methodology.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Missing Results:** As noted, Table 3 is missing, so the claims of the method's stability cannot be verified.
    *   **Evaluation Metric:** The choice of "error" as the distance from the true ACE to the estimated interval is non-standard and potentially misleading. It advantages methods that produce wider, less informative intervals, as the trivial [-1, 1] bound always achieves zero error. While the authors acknowledge this, they again defer the crucial discussion of the width-coverage trade-off to the supplement. A proper evaluation must prominently feature interval width as a primary performance metric.

4.  **Minor Issues:** The paper contains several typographical errors ("lowed bounds," "contraints," a misplaced footnote marker in Section 2) and stylistic inconsistencies (e.g., "Naı̈ve Estimator"). Key conceptual points are sometimes relegated to footnotes (e.g., footnote 4, which explains the interpretation of the independence test). The title, while memorable, borders on being overly colloquial for a NIPS publication.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of parameterizing violations of faithfulness to bridge discovery and bounding is highly original and addresses a significant problem in applied causal inference.
*   The method shows promise in the real-world data example, yielding bounds that are more informative than standard IV analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is critically incomplete, missing four essential figures/tables that are required to understand and evaluate the work.
*   The methodology relies on user-specified parameters with no guidance provided on how to set them, severely limiting its practical utility.
*   Several key methodological components, such as the model falsification test and the handling of algorithmic failures, are ad-hoc and lack rigor.
*   The experimental evaluation is unverifiable due to missing results and relies on a potentially misleading primary metric.
*   The overall lack of clarity and meticulousness falls well below the standard expected for a NIPS publication.

**Decision**

While the conceptual contribution is interesting, the execution and presentation of this work are deeply flawed. A paper submitted to a top-tier conference should be a complete, self-contained, and rigorously presented piece of research. This manuscript, in its current state, feels more like a preliminary research summary than a finished paper. The sheer number of omissions and methodological shortcuts overshadows the novelty of the idea. Therefore, I must recommend **rejection**. A significant revision that includes the missing content, provides a principled framework for parameter selection, and shores up the methodological and experimental rigor would be required for this work to be reconsidered.
