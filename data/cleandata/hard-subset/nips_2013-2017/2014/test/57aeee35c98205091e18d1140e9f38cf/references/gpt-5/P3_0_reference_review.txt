Summary
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y from observational data when standard back-door adjustment is unreliable and standard IV assumptions are too strong. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a “witness” W and admissible set Z to point-identify via back-door adjustment under faithfulness, treat W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z and compute bounds on the ACE. To control deviations from faithfulness, the authors introduce slack parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) that bound local violations of certain conditional independences; they then derive ACE bounds via a linear program (extending Balke–Pearl/Dawid–Ramsahai) and develop conservative algebraic bounds with a back-substitution “message passing” algorithm for speed. A Bayesian procedure with constrained Dirichlet priors propagates sampling uncertainty and provides posterior distributions over ACE bounds. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu-vaccine dataset illustrate the approach, with WPP yielding narrower bounds than standard IV in the application.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on: (i) IV bounds for binary models (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins), (ii) faithfulness-based covariate selection (Entner et al.), and (iii) sensitivity/partial identification perspectives (Manski). The idea of “bounded violations” of identifying assumptions is conceptually related to “plausibly exogenous” IV in econometrics (e.g., Conley, Hansen & Rossi, 2012, linear-Gaussian) and to Rosenbaum-style sensitivity analyses in observational studies; the paper extends this spirit to fully nonparametric binary models using polyhedral methods. The constrained-Dirichlet Bayesian treatment connects to inequality-constrained Bayesian modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: WPP is a principled compromise between point identification under faithfulness and worst-case IV bounds, providing control knobs (w, x, y, β, β̄) to interpolate between them.
- Technical contributions: (a) formulation of bounded-faithfulness relaxations within the Dawid/Ramsahai LP framework; (b) derivation of algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that substantially reduces computation while being only mildly conservative; (c) a Bayesian procedure yielding posterior distributions over bounds rather than plug-in estimates, acknowledging finite-sample uncertainty and model falsification.
- Practicality: The framework can leverage standard causal discovery steps (searching for (W, Z) pairs), and the code availability is a plus.
- Experiments: Includes both synthetic stress tests and a real observational study, showing that WPP can meaningfully tighten bounds over standard IV in practice.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Choice of relaxation parameters: The main limitation is the lack of principled guidance for selecting (w, x, y, β, β̄). These govern the strength of faithfulness violations and hidden dependence; results may be highly sensitive to these choices. The paper promises guidance in the supplement, but the main text would benefit from a clearer decision-theoretic or prior-based treatment (e.g., placing hyperpriors on these slacks and reporting robust/posterior sets of ACE), and from a systematic sensitivity analysis (grid over θ with width/coverage trade-offs).
- Interpretability of posterior over bounds: Reporting posteriors over [L, U] is conservative but awkward for decision-making. Consider summarizing via support functions, confidence regions for ACE under a prior over θ, or robust Bayes summaries (e.g., worst-case expected loss under a loss function).
- Validity under approximate independencies: The method uses BDeu to adjudicate Rule 1 with approximate independencies. This can propose witnesses even when no exact solution exists, risking anti-conservative inferences if the slack parameters are too small. A more formal multiple-testing control or Bayes factor thresholding, and a clear separation of model selection from bound computation, would strengthen credibility.
- Scalability and computation: The LP/polytope enumeration is expensive. While the back-substitution algorithm is a nice engineering contribution, quantitative comparisons (tightness vs. exact LP) are only sketched. Please provide systematic benchmarks (runtime, bound width deltas) across problem sizes and θ.
- Evaluation metrics: The “error” metric treats any interval covering the truth as zero error, which favors trivially wide intervals. Although the authors acknowledge this, the main results should also report interval widths, empirical coverage, and a combined score (e.g., expected absolute error to the nearest bound plus width penalty). Synthetic experiments use large samples (n=5000); results at smaller n would be informative.
- Scope limitations: Binary variables only. Discuss extension (or obstacles) to multinomial and continuous settings (e.g., discretization, copula bounds), or clarify the limits.
- Application: In the flu example, compare to standard IV with monotonicity (LATE) assumptions, which can materially tighten bounds, and discuss the plausibility of monotonicity vs. your bounded-faithfulness assumptions. The choice w=x=y=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1 appears ad hoc; provide a sensitivity plot. Also justify excluding GRP from Z and the plausibility that DM, AGE, SEX satisfy the required causal ordering (non-descendants of X and Y).
- Clarity and notation: The notation ζ, κ, η, ω and the mapping between latent and observable quantities are dense; a self-contained example with a single Z level, explicit constraints, and the full LP (or algebraic) inequalities would improve readability. The claim that the WPP bounds always cover the back-door point estimate (when Rule 1 holds) should be explicitly stated as a theorem with assumptions.

Quality
Technically sound overall, leveraging established partial identification machinery with thoughtful relaxations. Some components (model falsification heuristic, parameter selection) are heuristic and would benefit from sharper theory and diagnostics. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution method are solid contributions.

Clarity
Well organized but heavy notation. Several core ideas are deferred to the supplement. Improving exposition around the constraint sets and adding a running example would help reproducibility.

Originality
The inversion of Rule 1 to generate conditional-IV bounds under bounded-faithfulness, and the symbolic/back-substitution approach, are novel and nontrivial. The Bayesian treatment of polyhedral constraints is also useful.

Significance
Addresses an important gap between point-ID methods relying on faithfulness and very conservative IV bounds. If parameter selection and diagnostics are strengthened, the approach could become a practical tool for observational studies.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled framework marrying discovery-based selection with partial identification.
- Nontrivial technical contributions (algebraic bounds, efficient back-substitution).
- Addresses a practically important problem and demonstrates on real data.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient guidance and sensitivity analysis for the key relaxation parameters.
- Heuristic model selection/falsification; potential for anti-conservative use under approximate independencies.
- Limited evaluation metrics and scalability evidence in the main text; binary-only scope.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. I would support acceptance if the authors (i) provide clearer guidance and sensitivity analyses for θ, (ii) strengthen the evaluation with width/coverage and runtime/tightness comparisons, and (iii) clarify the theoretical guarantees and notation.
