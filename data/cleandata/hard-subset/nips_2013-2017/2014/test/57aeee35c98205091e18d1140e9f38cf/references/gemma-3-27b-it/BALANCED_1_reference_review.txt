This paper proposes a novel methodology, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for back-door adjustment, not as a means to estimate the ACE directly, but as a source of instrumental variables for bounding it. This is coupled with a linear programming formulation, relaxed for computational efficiency, and a Bayesian learning approach to handle uncertainty. The authors provide both analytical approximations and an algorithm for scaling up the approach. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial problem in causal inference, and offers a potentially valuable contribution by offering robustness against violations of faithfulness, a common assumption in the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reinterpretation of Entner et al.'s work and the framing of the problem as a bounding exercise, rather than point estimation, is a genuinely novel approach. This offers a different perspective and potentially more robust results in scenarios where faithfulness is questionable.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is grounded in sound causal inference theory, referencing key works by Pearl, Spirtes, and others. The connection to instrumental variable techniques is clearly articulated. The inclusion of algebraic bounds and the discussion of relaxations demonstrate a strong theoretical understanding.
* **Computational Considerations:** Recognizing the computational complexity of the linear programming approach, the authors propose relaxations and an efficient back-substitution algorithm. This is a practical strength, making the method more applicable to real-world datasets.
* **Bayesian Approach:** Employing a Bayesian framework for parameter estimation and uncertainty quantification is a valuable addition, addressing a common critique of frequentist approaches to causal inference.
* **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes synthetic experiments and a real-world application (influenza vaccination), demonstrating the method’s feasibility and providing preliminary evidence of its effectiveness.
* **Clear Presentation:** While the topic is complex, the authors generally present the methodology in a clear and organized manner, with helpful figures and equations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The methodology is highly complex, involving several layers of abstraction (linear programming, relaxations, Bayesian learning, back-substitution). This complexity may make it difficult for practitioners to understand and implement the method. The numerous steps and notations could overwhelm readers.
* **Scalability (Despite Improvements):** While the authors address computational issues, the scalability to high-dimensional datasets with many covariates remains a concern. The brute-force search in the experiments is limited to small problem sizes.  The discussion of discrete PCA as a scaling technique feels underdeveloped.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method likely depends on the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The paper mentions this but lacks a comprehensive sensitivity analysis. Guidance on selecting these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Interpretation of Bounds:** The paper correctly emphasizes that the output is a *bound* on the ACE, not a point estimate. However, the practical implications of these bounds (i.e., how to interpret a wide interval) are not fully explored.  A discussion of the “cost” of wider bounds in terms of information loss would be helpful.
* **Limited Real-World Experiment:** The real-world experiment with influenza data is a good start, but it’s limited to a single dataset. More extensive validation with diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.  The justification for excluding GRP from the admissible set needs further explanation.
* **Overstated Robustness:** The claim of robustness to faithfulness violations, while a key strength, might be overstated. The discussion in Section 5 suggests that even with relaxed constraints, the method can still be sensitive to the quality of conditional independence tests.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to bounding causal effects.
* The method addresses a significant challenge in causal inference: dealing with unmeasured confounding and violations of faithfulness.
* The computational improvements and Bayesian framework enhance the practicality of the approach.
* The experimental results, while preliminary, are encouraging.
* The work aligns well with the NIPS focus on machine learning and statistical inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the methodology may limit its adoption by practitioners.
* The scalability to large datasets is a concern.
* The sensitivity to parameter choices and the interpretation of bounds require further investigation.
* The real-world validation is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. While the complexity and potential scalability issues are concerns, the theoretical foundation, computational improvements, and preliminary experimental results are promising. I believe this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of causal inference.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Provide more detailed guidance on selecting the relaxation parameters.** A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different parameter values would be valuable.
2.  **Elaborate on the interpretation of the bounds.** Discuss the trade-offs between bound width and confidence.
3.  **Expand the real-world validation.** Include results from additional datasets.
4.  **Clarify the scalability limitations.** A more in-depth discussion of computational complexity and potential optimizations would be helpful.
5.  **Simplify the presentation where possible.** Consider providing a more intuitive explanation of the core ideas, perhaps through a running example.
