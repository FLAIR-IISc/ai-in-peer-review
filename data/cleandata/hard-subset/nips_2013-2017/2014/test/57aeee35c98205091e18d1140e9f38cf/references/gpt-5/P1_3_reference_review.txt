Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y using observational data and a set of observed covariates W that are not descendants of X or Y. The key idea is to invert the use of Entner-Hoyer-Spirtes’ Rule 1: instead of using a witness W and an admissible set Z to obtain a back-door point estimate under faithfulness, treat W as a (conditional) instrument given Z and compute bounds on ACE. To balance robustness and informativeness, the authors introduce relaxation parameters that bound violations of faithfulness via local constraints:

- w: bounds heterogeneity of the effect of W on X across U (|η?x1−η?x0| ≤ w),
- y, x: bound deviations between latent response probabilities and observed conditionals (|η?xw − P(Y=1|X=x,W=w)| ≤ y, |δ?w − P(X=1|W=w)| ≤ x),
- β, β̄: bound selection into U by W (β P(U) ≤ P(U|W=w) ≤ β̄ P(U)).

Setting w=0, β=β̄=1, y=x=0 reduces to the chain W→X→Y; w=0, β=β̄=1 recovers standard IV.

Given a candidate (W,Z), they construct bounds via a linear programming approach (building on Dawid and Ramsahai) that maps extreme points of latent response functions to observable constraints and optimizes ACE subject to the relaxed inequalities. Because this can be expensive, the paper contributes: (i) algebraic (symbolic) bounds as functions of the relaxation parameters; (ii) a back-substitution algorithm that iteratively tightens box constraints in a message-passing style, offering major speedups with modest additional looseness; and (iii) a Bayesian learning procedure that places constrained Dirichlet priors on P(Y,X,W|Z=z), uses rejection sampling to account for estimation uncertainty, and returns posterior distributions over ACE bounds while applying simple falsification checks.

Experiments on synthetic DAGs (with |W|=8) compare WPP to two naive point estimators (adjust-all and adjust-none) and to the faithfulness-based point estimator of Entner et al. WPP yields stable intervals with good coverage and lower tail error than the baselines in settings with and without valid witnesses. On an influenza vaccination observational dataset with an encouragement design, standard IV bounds are wide ([-0.23, 0.64]); WPP selects a different witness and admissible set than the randomized encouragement and returns tighter bounds ([-0.10, 0.17]) under modest relaxations.

Relation to prior work
The work connects causal discovery under (approximate) faithfulness (Entner et al.) with partial identification via instrumental variables (Balke-Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai), providing a continuum between point identification under faithfulness and worst-case IV bounds. It complements robust identification and bounding literature (Manski; Geiger et al.) and inequality-based constraints for latent variable models (Evans; Richardson et al.). The algebraic bounds and back-substitution approach provide new computational and analytical tools beyond existing IV bounds.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of bounded violations via (w, y, x, β, β̄) is clear, with recoveries of well-known special cases (chain model, standard IV). The linear programming construction and the κ, ω reparameterization for β-relaxations are consistent with prior convex-analytic treatments of IV models. The algebraic inequalities and back-substitution are conservative relaxations; while not tight, they are useful and computationally appealing. The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach correctly acknowledges estimation uncertainty and model falsifiability.
- Caveats: The semantics and practical calibration of the relaxation parameters are not fully justified; sensitivity analysis is deferred to the supplement. The Bayesian rejection sampling can be inefficient in higher dimensions, and the 95% rejection threshold as a falsification criterion is ad hoc. The paper claims the bounds always cover back-door estimates under [6], but the proof sketch is not shown here; a brief in-paper argument would strengthen the claim. Finally, when no true witness exists, the method often proposes one due to “approximate independencies”; guardrails against spurious inferences are lightly specified.

Clarity
The paper is dense but mostly well-organized. The high-level motivation and special-case recoveries help. However:
- Notation is heavy (ζ, η, δ) and z-conditioning is suppressed in places, which hinders readability; providing a running worked example would help.
- The search stage in Algorithm 1 is unspecified; a concise description of the independence testing and thresholds used (beyond BDeu) and the computational complexity would improve reproducibility.
- The algebraic bounds are relegated to the supplement; including at least one full derivation in the main text would aid understanding.

Originality
High. The inversion of Rule 1 to generate conditional-IV bounds with explicit bounded faithfulness violations, the introduction of parameterized relaxations and their analytic consequences, and the back-substitution algorithm are novel combinations that fill a gap between faithfulness-based point identification and IV-based worst-case bounds.

Significance
Moderate to high. Many observational analyses face model uncertainty between (approximate) conditional independences and hidden confounding. Offering user-controllable, interpretable relaxations that yield nontrivial ACE bounds is valuable for practice and could be adopted in applied fields. The computational contribution (symbolic bounds + back-substitution) is also of broad interest.

Pros
- Bridges two important paradigms (faithfulness-based discovery and IV partial identification) with a tunable, interpretable framework.
- Sound convex-analytic foundation; recovers known special cases.
- Useful algebraic bounds and a fast back-substitution algorithm.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty and falsification.
- Empirical results show robustness vs naive approaches; real-data example yields informative bounds; code available.

Cons
- Limited guidance for choosing relaxation parameters; practical calibration and sensitivity need to be front-and-center.
- Rejection-sampling-based Bayesian step may be inefficient; falsification rule is heuristic.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader (e.g., compare to standard IV bounds conditional on Z, Rosenbaum/Manski-type bounds, or recent robust-IV likelihood methods).
- When no witness exists, the method often proceeds; more stringent model checking and multiplicity control are needed.
- Clarity issues in notation and missing in-paper proofs/details for key claims.

Questions for the authors
1. How should practitioners choose (w, y, x, β, β̄)? Can you provide interpretable mappings (e.g., in terms of maximal odds ratio distortions) and default priors over these parameters?
2. Can you report runtime and acceptance rates for the Bayesian rejection sampler and back-substitution vs full LP across your synthetic settings?
3. When multiple (W,Z) yield contradictory bounds, how should users aggregate or select among them beyond the BDeu scoring heuristic?
4. In the influenza study, why is GRP not validated as a witness? Please elaborate on the diagnostics and sensitivity to parameter choices.
5. Can you provide formal guarantees that WPP bounds always contain any back-door point estimate identified by Rule 1 (with a concise proof in the main text)?

Minor suggestions
- Reintroduce z notation in equations for clarity; add a small worked example.
- Summarize at least one symbolic bound with a derivation in the main text.
- Provide a brief guide for parameter sensitivity in the main paper.
- Clarify the scope: binary variables only; discuss extension challenges.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a thoughtful and timely contribution that is both conceptually and computationally interesting, with promising practical impact. Strengthening guidance on parameter calibration, tightening the falsification and selection procedures, and adding a bit more detail on theory and computation would make this a clear accept.
