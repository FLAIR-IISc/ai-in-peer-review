## Peer Review: Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies, leveraging a set of covariates assumed to be unaffected by the causal relationship of interest. The core idea is a re-interpretation of Entner et al.'s work, shifting from seeking admissible sets for backdoor adjustment to using pairs of variables (witness, admissible set) to establish instrumental variable bounds. The authors propose a linear programming (LP) formulation to achieve this, recognizing its computational cost and subsequently offering relaxations and a faster, message-passing-like algorithm. The paper explores analytical approximations and demonstrates the approach on synthetic and real-world data (influenza vaccination).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly upon the instrumental variables (IV) literature, particularly the work of Dawid and Ramsahai, extending their LP-based bounding techniques. It also acknowledges and differentiates itself from Entner et al.'s approach, focusing on bounds rather than point estimates and relaxing assumptions around faithfulness. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of causal inference, referencing Pearl’s do-calculus and the PC algorithm for causal discovery.  The acknowledgement of challenges regarding faithfulness, and potential sensitivity to sample size, is consistent with ongoing debates in the field (e.g., [20] as cited). The comparison to naive estimators ([15]) provides a useful benchmark.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The “Witness Protection Program” provides a fresh perspective on utilizing observational covariates for causal inference, cleverly bridging the gap between admissible set identification and instrumental variable bounding.
* **Addressing Faithfulness Concerns:**  The method actively attempts to mitigate the sensitivity to violations of the faithfulness assumption by explicitly modeling potential deviations through the introduction of parameters *w, y, x, β, β̄*. This is a significant strength, acknowledging a core limitation of many causal discovery and inference techniques.
* **Computational Considerations:** Recognizing the complexity of the initial LP formulation, the authors demonstrate a proactive effort to develop practical relaxations and a faster algorithm, improving the scalability of the method. The back-substitution algorithm, in particular, shows promise for efficient bound calculation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while relatively limited in scope, provide preliminary evidence of the method's effectiveness in both synthetic and real-world settings. The influenza vaccination example offers a concrete application and highlights the potential for nuanced causal insights.
* **Clear Motivation and Connections:** The paper clearly articulates the motivations behind the approach and its connections to existing literature, making it easier to understand for readers familiar with causal inference.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The introduced parameters *w, y, x, β, β̄* while addressing faithfulness, introduce new parameters requiring justification and selection. The paper acknowledges this, but a more in-depth discussion on parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The choices made in the experiments aren’t fully justified and the impact of different settings remains unclear.
* **Computational Complexity (Despite Relaxations):**  While the relaxations improve scalability, the core LP formulation still seems computationally demanding, particularly for high-dimensional datasets. The paper doesn’t provide a clear indication of the scalability limits of the approach or a detailed complexity analysis.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is somewhat limited in scope. More extensive evaluation with diverse synthetic datasets and real-world applications is needed to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the method. The comparison with other causal inference techniques (e.g., propensity score matching, inverse probability weighting) is missing.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation, especially in Section 3, can be dense and challenging to follow.  A more systematic explanation of the variables and their relationships would improve readability. The use of ζ and η could be more consistently defined.
* **BDeu Score Justification**: The authors' use of the BDeu score feels somewhat arbitrary and requires more justification.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The approach offers a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of causal inference, addressing a key challenge – dealing with observational data and potential violations of assumptions.
* The work is technically sound, with a clear mathematical foundation and a well-defined methodology.
* The authors demonstrate a practical awareness of the computational challenges and actively seek solutions through relaxations and algorithmic optimizations.
* The preliminary empirical results are encouraging and suggest the method holds promise for real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method’s practical applicability is limited by the potentially high computational cost and the need for careful parameter tuning.
* The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently comprehensive to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
* The clarity of the presentation could be improved, particularly regarding the notation and mathematical details.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of causal inference. While some weaknesses remain, particularly regarding computational complexity and empirical evaluation, the novelty of the approach and the effort to address the challenges of faithfulness make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Expand the experimental section:** Include comparisons with other causal inference methods and perform sensitivity analysis with different parameter settings (*w, y, x, β, β̄*).
* **Provide a more detailed complexity analysis:** Quantify the computational cost of the LP formulation and the relaxations.
* **Improve clarity of notation and presentation:**  Provide a more systematic explanation of the variables and their relationships.
* **Justify the choice of the BDeu score:**  Explain the rationale for using this score and consider other potential scoring functions.
* **Discuss limitations:** Explicitly address the limitations of the approach and potential scenarios where it might not perform well.
