

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework called the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on Y using observational data. The authors modify Entner et al.'s (2013) approach to causal inference by reinterpreting their "witness" concept as an instrumental variable rather than discarding it. The WPP framework provides a systematic way to generate candidate instrumental variables and derive bounds on the ACE through a linear programming approach with relaxed constraints, while also introducing a computationally efficient symbolic bound calculation method. The paper connects to foundational causal inference work by Pearl (2009), Entner et al. (2013), and Ramsahai (2016), but provides a novel compromise between faithfulness-based point estimates and IV-based bounds.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-motivated theoretical contributions. The authors provide detailed mathematical derivations of the bounds, including symbolic algebraic expressions that reveal how relaxation parameters interact. The empirical evaluation with synthetic data and the influenza vaccination case study demonstrates practical utility, and the authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., computational expense, sensitivity to relaxation parameters). The Bayesian learning approach to handle uncertainty is particularly well-executed.

**Originality:** The core contribution—the reimagining of Entner's witness concept as a protected instrumental variable—is novel. The paper makes a clear distinction from previous work by providing a systematic framework for relaxing faithfulness assumptions rather than relying on them. The introduction of symbolic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm represents a significant technical contribution that addresses computational limitations of previous approaches.

**Significance:** This work addresses a critical gap in causal inference practice. Many observational studies rely on faithfulness assumptions that may be unstable in practice, while traditional IV methods provide only very wide bounds. The WPP framework provides a practical middle ground that could be widely adopted in observational studies across medical, social, and economic sciences. The influenza vaccination case study demonstrates clear real-world applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with good organization of concepts. The notation is consistent throughout, and the figures help clarify the causal structures. The mathematical derivations, while complex, are well-motivated and explained. The supplemental material provides additional details that would be helpful for reproduction.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Efficiency:** While the back-substitution algorithm is presented as a computational improvement, the paper doesn't provide sufficient comparison to baseline LP approaches in terms of actual runtime. The claim of "order of magnitude more efficient" needs stronger empirical validation.

**Parameter Selection:** The paper acknowledges that choosing relaxation parameters is an open problem, but the current implementation relies on arbitrary defaults (w = x = y = 0.2). The lack of systematic guidance for parameter selection limits the practical utility of the method, as different choices could yield substantially different bounds.

**Theoretical Bound Tightness:** The authors acknowledge their bounds are not tight ("more conservative (i.e., wider)"), but don't quantify how much wider they are compared to alternative methods. A theoretical comparison of bound widths across different parameter settings would strengthen the contribution.

**Real-World Applicability:** The influenza vaccination example is compelling, but the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method would scale to high-dimensional observational studies with hundreds or thousands of covariates, which are common in modern applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to causal inference that addresses an important practical gap between faithfulness-based point estimation and IV-based bounding methods. The paper makes a novel and well-motivated contribution that advances the state of the art in causal inference. While some aspects (parameter selection, computational validation) could be strengthened, the core methodology is sound and potentially impactful for practitioners.

The paper is appropriate for NeurIPS as it combines novel theoretical contributions with practical algorithms and empirical validation, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics. The authors have clearly engaged with the relevant literature and made a significant contribution to causal inference methodology.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to causal inference methodology with clear theoretical foundations, practical algorithms, and empirical validation. While there are some limitations to address, the core contribution is significant and the paper is well-written and technically sound.

## Key Points for Authors

1. Consider providing more detailed empirical comparison of computational efficiency between the back-substitution algorithm and standard LP approaches
2. Expand the discussion on parameter selection, potentially including sensitivity analysis of bounds to different relaxation parameters
3. Clarify how the method would scale to high-dimensional observational studies
4. Consider adding a brief comparison of bound width to alternative methods (e.g., standard IV bounds) to better quantify the trade-off between conservatism and tightness

The WPP framework represents a promising direction for causal inference in practice, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
