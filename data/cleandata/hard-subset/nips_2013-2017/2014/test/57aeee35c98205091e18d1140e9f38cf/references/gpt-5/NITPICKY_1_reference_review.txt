Summary
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of X on Y using observational data with binary variables. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s “Rule 1” for witness/admissible-set discovery: instead of using (W, Z) to identify a back-door adjustment set Z, treat W as a (conditional) instrumental variable given Z, but allow bounded violations of faithfulness/IV assumptions via relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄). The ACE bounds are obtained by a linear programming formulation over a latent mixture parameterization, with additional algebraic relaxations that yield symbolic bounds and a fast “back-substitution” message-passing-style tightening procedure. A Bayesian variant places constrained Dirichlet priors on local contingency tables and uses rejection sampling to propagate uncertainty into ACE bounds. Experiments compare WPP with naive adjustment (all covariates or none) and a faithfulness-based point estimator, and a real-data illustration uses influenza vaccination.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on instrumental variable bounds (Balke & Pearl 1997; Dawid 2003; Ramsahai 2012), faithfulness-based covariate selection (Entner et al. 2013), and partial identification (Manski). The novelty is the specific marriage of (i) Rule-1–driven witness discovery with (ii) parameter-bounded relaxations of faithfulness/IV and (iii) algebraic bound propagation. The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach follows ideas in testing/fitting IV models (Ramsahai & Lauritzen 2011), but here it targets bounds with rejection sampling.

Strengths
- Interesting and practically motivated compromise between brittle point-identification via faithfulness and often-too-wide IV bounds; the relaxation parameters provide a tunable knob.
- The algebraic (symbolic) bounds and back-substitution heuristic are appealing for scalability and provide intuition about parameter effects.
- The framework is modular: any witness search can be plugged in; the LP and the algebraic relaxations are complementary.
- Experiments indicate robustness in scenarios where full back-door adjustment is invalid; the real-data example shows potentially tighter bounds than standard IV.

Weaknesses (technical, clarity, and rigor)
- Notation is confusing and inconsistent. Many key quantities are insufficiently defined in the main text:
  - η?xw, ζyx.w, δ?w, ωxw, κyx.w appear with insufficient explicit definitions, and the “Table 1” parameterization is not included.
  - Equation (2) uses η11, η10, etc., but elsewhere ηxw denotes P(Y=1|do(X=x), W=w, U) or its mixture; the indices/roles of x,w are unclear.
  - The sentence “for w = y = 1, this is the unit hypercube [0,1]^4” conflates the parameter y (relaxation) and the outcome index y.
- The main derivations are deferred to the Supplement, and the core algebraic constraints (e.g., (7)–(10)) are only alluded to; the reader cannot reproduce results from the main text. Claims such as “guaranteed to cover the back-door adjusted point estimator” are not proved in the paper.
- The LP description is vague and contains dimension/counting inconsistencies:
  - “up to 12 points for the case where w > 0” is unclear; the 4-D vs 12-D polytopes are not derived; the mapping ζ?yx.w = (δ?w)x(1−δ?w)(1−x)η?xw is presented without justification or full definitions.
  - The β-relaxations via κ and ω are introduced, but their link to observables and the feasibility constraints are only sketched.
- Choice and interpretation of relaxation parameters are largely deferred (“we will not discuss in detail how to choose…”) despite being central. The default choices (w=x=y=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1) are used in experiments with minimal justification; practical guidance is missing.
- Bayesian component is ad hoc in places:
  - Constrained Dirichlet with rejection sampling can be extremely inefficient; the “>95% rejected ⇒ bad fit” rule is heuristic.
  - Independence decisions via BDeu with ESS=10 are known to be sensitive; sensitivity analyses are relegated to the supplement.
  - Posterior summaries average interval endpoints (“posterior expected interval”), which is not a proper estimator for random sets.
- Experimental design and baselines:
  - Synthetic settings are constructed so that adjusting on all W is always wrong; this may favor WPP. Comparisons with (i) standard IV bounds conditional on Z with averaging over P(Z), (ii) other partial identification methods (e.g., Evans’ graphical constraints, Geiger et al. bounds), or (iii) monotone IV or stochastic dominance assumptions are missing.
  - Computational claims (“order of magnitude more efficient”) lack rigorous runtime/accuracy trade-off measurements; only qualitative statements are given.
  - In the influenza example, the natural IV (GRP) is dismissed and DM with {AGE, SEX} is used; the plausibility of the chosen (w,x,y,β,β̄), and sensitivity of the interval to these values, are not presented in the main text. The narrower bounds than standard IV could reflect strong relaxations rather than data-driven evidence.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues that hinder comprehension:
  - Figures and tables referenced (Figure 1, Table 1, Table 2) are absent; many equations refer to missing numbered constraints.
  - Typos and inconsistencies: “poymake.org” (Polymake URL), “supress”, “upper/lowed”, “Naı̈ve”, capitalization and re-use of W for both a set and an element, collision between variables named x,y and relaxation parameters x,y.
  - References are incomplete, inconsistent, or malformed (missing years/pages; “Myth, J. Pearl, confusion…”; many entries lack full bibliographic info).

Quality
The core idea is promising, but the paper in its current form is not reproducible: central definitions and constraints are missing from the main text, and the derivations are too sketchy. The Bayesian and algorithmic components rely on heuristics that need more theoretical and empirical support. Experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines and rigorous computational assessment.

Clarity
Significant issues with notation, missing figures/tables, and editing errors. The reader cannot implement the method from the main paper. Guidance on parameter choice is largely absent.

Originality
Moderately novel combination: using Rule 1 to propose approximate instruments and bounding under controlled departures is interesting. Some aspects (relaxed IV constraints, LP-based bounds, constrained Bayesian estimation) build on known literature; the message-passing back-substitution for bound tightening seems new.

Significance
Potentially impactful if clarified and strengthened; bounding ACE with interpretable relaxations is useful. As presented, the contribution is weakened by lack of clarity and rigorous evaluation.

Questions for authors
- Provide precise definitions of η, δ, ζ, ω, κ, with indices and conditioning, and include the full parameterization table in the main paper.
- Prove in the paper that the proposed bounds always cover the back-door adjusted point estimator (when Rule 1 holds) and quantify conservativeness.
- Give principled guidelines for choosing (w,x,y,β,β̄); can you link them to testable near-independence measures or domain knowledge?
- Compare against conditional IV bounds averaged over Z and other partial-identification approaches; include runtime and tightness comparisons to the LP solution.
- Clarify the witness selection process and guard against overfitting/cherry-picking in real data; provide ablation/sensitivity analyses in the main text.

Minor comments
- Fix numerous typos/formatting; provide complete references; correct the Polymake URL; avoid notational overloading (indices vs parameters); include all referenced figures/tables; ensure equations are self-contained.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem; practical middle ground between faithfulness point ID and IV bounds.
- Conceptually appealing use of bounded violations.
- Potentially scalable algebraic/back-substitution approach.

Cons
- Core definitions/derivations missing; notation confusion undermines reproducibility.
- Heuristic Bayesian and testing components; parameter choices unjustified.
- Weak baselines and limited computational evaluation.
- Many presentation and formatting errors.

Recommendation: Reject (clear potential, but the paper needs substantial rewriting, full derivations in the main text, stronger empirical validation, and clearer guidance on parameterization before it is ready for NeurIPS).
