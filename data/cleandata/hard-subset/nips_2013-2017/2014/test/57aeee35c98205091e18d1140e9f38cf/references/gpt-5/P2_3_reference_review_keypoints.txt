- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound the ACE of binary X on Y by inverting Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes Rule 1 and treating (W,Z) as an instrumental-like setup.
- Introduces relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) to allow bounded violations of conditional independences and direct effects.
- Adapts LP-based IV bounding (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) to the relaxed setting and conditions on Z with averaging.
- Derives algebraic inequalities and a back-substitution algorithm for fast, scalable bound tightening.
- Proposes a Bayesian procedure using constrained Dirichlet priors with rejection sampling to obtain posterior distributions over ACE bounds and a falsification test.
- Experiments: synthetic comparisons to naive back-door and faithfulness-based point estimates, plus a real influenza-vaccination case showing tighter bounds than standard IV.
- Bridges two research strands: faithfulness-based causal discovery and partial identification via IV bounds, with novelty in parameterized relaxation and conditioning on Z.
- Algebraic bounds and back-substitution offer a new, practical approximation absent in prior IV-bounds work.
- The constrained-Dirichlet Bayesian approach addresses uncertainty in bounds without priors on latent structural parameters.
- Modeling is well-motivated and reduces to known special cases (standard IV and chain models) under specific parameter settings.
- LP reformulation appears sound and the outer-relaxation nature of algebraic bounds is appropriately acknowledged as potentially conservative.
- Major proofs and some derivations are deferred to the supplement, leaving completeness and some validity claims unshown in the main text.
- The Bayesian component is somewhat ad hoc (inefficient rejection sampling, arbitrary 95% falsification threshold) and lacks calibration/coverage analysis.
- Experiments are informative but limited: comparisons are mostly naive, sensitivity to relaxations is not systematically explored, and only one real dataset is used.
- Clarity issues include notational overload, typographical problems, and insufficient intuitive exposition of the relaxation parameters.
- Originality is strong: inverting Rule 1 and the algebraic/back-substitution contributions are novel and practically useful.
- Significance is plausible: offers a pragmatic middle ground between brittle point identification and wide IV bounds, though limited by binary-variable focus and small Z.
- Pros: elegant conceptual bridge, explicit relaxations, scalable algebraic bounds, Bayesian uncertainty quantification, code availability, meaningful real-data tightening.
- Cons: reliance on supplement for proofs, limited guidance on choosing relaxation parameters, inefficient constrained sampling, narrow experiments, binary/Z-size limitations.
- Suggestions: add guidance and sensitivity analysis for relaxation choices, report Bayesian sampler costs/acceptance rates, prove back-substitution yields outer bounds and quantify tightness, compare to strengthened IV assumptions, and improve exposition.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel, technically sound-in-spirit, potentially impactful despite deferred theory and limited empirical evaluation.
