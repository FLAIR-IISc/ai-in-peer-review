

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies, which the authors term the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP). The core idea is to modify Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to generate candidate instrumental variables, then use linear programming with relaxed constraints to provide bounds on the ACE that are less sensitive to violations of the faithfulness assumption.

The paper builds on established causal inference methods, particularly the instrumental variable (IV) approach and the work of Entner et al. The authors introduce relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) to handle potential violations of faithfulness, and propose a Bayesian approach to handle uncertainty in the parameters. They also introduce a "back-substitution algorithm" for more efficient computation of bounds.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- The Bayesian approach to handling uncertainty is a reasonable extension of existing methods.
- The back-substitution algorithm does provide a computationally efficient alternative to the full linear programming approach.
- The real-world influenza vaccination example provides some context for the method's application.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weaknesses lie in its lack of rigorous validation and unsubstantiated claims of novelty. The authors claim their method provides a "novel compromise" between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and IV bounds, but fail to provide compelling evidence that their method actually outperforms existing approaches in meaningful ways.

The experimental evaluation is particularly concerning. The synthetic experiments compare against only two naive point estimators (NE1 and NE2) and the Entner et al. method, but fail to include comparisons against established causal inference methods like the standard IV bounds [1] or the more recent approaches of Geiger et al. [8] or Richardson et al. [18]. The authors claim their method is "more conservative" than the back-door adjusted point estimator, but provide no evidence that this conservatism translates to better performance in real-world scenarios.

The choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is entirely arbitrary in the experiments, with no justification for why the authors chose the specific values they used (w = x = y = 0.2, β = 0.9, β̄ = 1.1). The paper states that "any choice" of these parameters provides bounds at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted estimator, but this is a weak claim that doesn't demonstrate the value of the method. In fact, the authors acknowledge that "the implied bounds are more conservative (i.e., wider)" than tighter bounds that could be obtained with different parameter choices.

The real-world influenza vaccination example is particularly problematic. The authors claim their method "could not validate GRP as a witness," but instead found DM as the highest-scoring witness. However, they don't provide a clear justification for why DM (patient had history of diabetes) is a better witness than GRP (doctor's reminder letter), which was explicitly designed as an instrumental variable. The paper fails to demonstrate that the WPP method provides meaningful improvements over standard IV analysis for this real-world problem.

The paper's claim of novelty is unsubstantiated. The core idea of relaxing faithfulness assumptions to obtain more robust bounds is not new. Ramsahai [16] already explored similar relaxations, and Geiger et al. [8] have developed more comprehensive approaches to bounding causal effects. The paper does not clearly distinguish its method from these existing approaches, and the "Witness Protection Program" name appears to be more marketing than substance.

The paper also fails to address a critical issue: when no valid witness exists (as in the "unsolvable" cases in their experiments), their method still returns bounds, but the authors acknowledge this is a theoretical failure. The paper does not provide a clear rationale for why this approach should be preferred over simply acknowledging that the causal effect cannot be identified.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper proposes a method that appears to be a minor variation of existing causal inference techniques, with insufficient evidence to demonstrate its superiority over established approaches. The lack of rigorous validation against appropriate baselines, the arbitrary choice of relaxation parameters, and the unsubstantiated claims of novelty make this work less compelling than it could be. While the computational efficiency of the back-substitution algorithm is a positive aspect, this alone does not justify publication at NIPS.

I recommend rejection. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its experimental validation by comparing against established causal inference methods, providing clear theoretical justification for the relaxation parameters, and demonstrating concrete improvements over existing approaches to be considered for publication. The current work represents a marginal extension of existing methodology without sufficient evidence of significant advancement.
