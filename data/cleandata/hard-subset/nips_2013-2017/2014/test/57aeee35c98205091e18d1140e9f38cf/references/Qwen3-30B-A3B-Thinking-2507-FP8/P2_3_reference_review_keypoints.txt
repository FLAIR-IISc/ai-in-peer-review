- Introduces Witness Protection Program (WPP), a method to bound the average causal effect (ACE) for binary X→Y using observational data by treating candidate variables as "witnesses" rather than discarding them.  
- Inverts Entner et al.'s Rule 1 and models faithfulness violations as bounded local-independence violations to allow more robust causal inference.  
- Develops a linear programming formulation, algebraic bounds, and a back-substitution algorithm to compute ACE bounds efficiently.  
- Provides a detailed parameterization with relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that control allowed faithfulness violations.  
- Proposes a Bayesian learning approach to handle uncertainty and avoid sensitivity from priors on unidentifiable parameters.  
- Synthetic experiments test both solvable and unsolvable cases, and a real influenza vaccination example demonstrates practical applicability.  
- Clear theoretical grounding and strong technical quality, extending prior work from Entner, Dawid, and Ramsahai.  
- Algebraic bounds give useful insight into how relaxation parameters affect bound tightness.  
- Presentation is generally clear and well-organized, but some parameter definitions (η̄xw, δ?w) and algorithmic descriptions are dense and could use more intuition/examples.  
- Notation can be heavy for readers unfamiliar with causal inference; algorithm steps would benefit from a concrete walkthrough.  
- Novel contribution: principled relaxation of faithfulness assumptions and use of witnesses as informative rather than discardable.  
- Significance is notable for practical observational studies, offering a middle ground between point estimates and worst-case bounds, but currently limited to binary variables and specific structures.  
- Weaknesses include limited guidance on choosing relaxation parameters, insufficient comparison with recent methods, incomplete demonstration of advantages in the real example, and unclear computational complexity of the witness search.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add practical guidance for parameter choice, broader comparisons, and a concrete example illustrating the back-substitution algorithm.
