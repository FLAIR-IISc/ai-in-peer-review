This paper introduces a novel methodology, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.'s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment, using these sets to construct instrumental variables and derive bounds on the ACE via linear programming. The authors acknowledge the computational expense of this approach and introduce relaxations and a fast algorithm based on message passing to scale up the method. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental challenge in causal inference—identifying causal effects from observational data—and builds upon a substantial body of prior work, including Pearl's do-calculus, instrumental variable techniques, and recent advances in causal discovery.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The reinterpretation of Entner et al.'s work and the application of linear programming with relaxations to generate bounds represent a genuinely novel approach. This goes beyond simply applying existing IV methods and attempts to provide more robust bounds when faithfulness assumptions are questionable.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper is firmly rooted in the causal inference literature, clearly articulating the problem, relating it to existing approaches (IVs, backdoor adjustment, faithfulness), and providing a mathematical framework for the proposed methodology. The introduction of parameters to quantify violations of faithfulness is a strong point.
*   **Computational Considerations:** Recognizing the computational complexity, the authors explore relaxations and a fast algorithm, demonstrating an awareness of practical applicability. The development of algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm offer promising avenues for scaling the method.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments, including both synthetic and real data (influenza vaccination), provide a reasonable evaluation of the method's performance. The comparison with naive estimators and the exploration of cases with and without a valid witness are particularly valuable.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper generally presents concepts and methodology clearly, building up from foundational concepts. The algorithmic outline (Algorithm 1) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity & Clarity:** Despite the attempt at clear presentation, the paper is dense and requires significant background knowledge to follow. The introduction of numerous parameters (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω, β) without sufficient intuitive explanation makes it difficult to grasp the core mechanism.  Section 3.1, in particular, feels convoluted.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of WPP appears heavily reliant on the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough sensitivity analysis and guidance on selecting these parameters would be beneficial. The discussion on choosing these parameters feels somewhat relegated to the Supplementary Material, where it deserves more prominence.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Limitations:** The synthetic experiments, while useful, are limited in scope. The parameter settings and graph generation process are not fully detailed, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results. The real-data example, while relevant, is a single case study and doesn't provide strong evidence of widespread applicability. The discussion of the influenza dataset feels brief and lacks a deeper comparison to established methods in that field.
*   **Back-Substitution Algorithm Details:** The description of the back-substitution algorithm is somewhat hand-wavy. A more precise and detailed explanation of the algorithm's steps and convergence criteria would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors address computational cost, the statement that the back-substitution method is "an order of magnitude more efficient" lacks quantitative support. A more rigorous comparison of the computational performance of the full linear programming approach, the relaxed linear program, and the back-substitution algorithm would be valuable.
* **Over-reliance on Supplementary Material:** Critical information like the detailed justification of algebraic bounds, algorithmic details, and comprehensive sensitivity analysis is pushed into the supplementary material.  The core paper should contain more of these details.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The methodology represents a significant advance in the field of causal inference, offering a potential solution to the challenges of identifying causal effects in the presence of unobserved confounding and violations of the faithfulness assumption.
*   The theoretical grounding and careful derivation of bounds demonstrate a high level of technical rigor.
*   The computational considerations and attempts at scaling the method are commendable.
*   The comprehensive experiments, while limited, provide some evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper's complexity and lack of clarity may make it inaccessible to a broader audience.
*   The sensitivity of the method to parameter settings and the lack of guidance on parameter selection raise concerns about its practical usability.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn't provide strong evidence of widespread applicability.
* The heavy reliance on supplementary material detracts from the clarity of the core presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of causal inference. However, its complexity and the limitations of the empirical evaluation raise concerns about its immediate impact.  I lean towards **rejection** but with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity of the presentation, providing more detailed guidance on parameter selection, expanding the empirical evaluation with more diverse datasets and rigorous comparisons, and moving key details from the supplement into the main paper. Addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its chances of acceptance in a future submission. The core idea is strong, but the execution needs refinement to reach the standards expected for NIPS.
