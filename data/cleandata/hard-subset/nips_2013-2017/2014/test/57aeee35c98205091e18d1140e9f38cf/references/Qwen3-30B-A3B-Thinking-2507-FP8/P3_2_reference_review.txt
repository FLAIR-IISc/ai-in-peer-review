

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach called the Witness Protection Program (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between variables X and Y using covariates. The authors address a critical limitation in existing causal inference methods: the fragility of the faithfulness assumption. Building on Entner et al.'s work on "witnesses" and admissible sets, WPP introduces relaxation parameters that allow for bounded violations of faithfulness, providing more robust causal bounds. The method combines linear programming with a faster back-substitution algorithm for computing bounds, and includes a Bayesian framework for model selection and uncertainty quantification. The authors demonstrate the approach on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a meaningful problem**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of faithfulness assumptions in causal inference, a well-documented issue in the literature (e.g., Manski [11]). The authors correctly note that faithfulness violations can lead to substantial errors in point estimates, making the need for robust bounds compelling.

2. **Sound theoretical foundation**: The relaxation framework (with parameters w, y, x, β, β̄) is well-motivated and correctly positioned as a middle ground between point estimation (relying on faithfulness) and purely instrumental variable bounds. The connection to existing work by Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16] is appropriately established.

3. **Practical algorithmic contribution**: The back-substitution algorithm for efficiently computing bounds is a valuable contribution, addressing the computational cost of the linear programming approach. The paper provides a clear explanation of how this algorithm works and demonstrates its efficiency gains.

4. **Thoughtful experimental design**: The synthetic experiments systematically evaluate the method under controlled conditions where the true ACE is known, and the real-world application with influenza data provides a meaningful case study.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear guidance on parameter selection**: The paper's main limitation is the absence of principled methods for choosing the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the current implementation requires users to arbitrarily set these parameters, which significantly affects the tightness of the bounds. Without guidance on how to select these parameters, the method's practical utility is limited.

2. **Limited comparison with existing methods**: The paper compares against naive estimators (NE1, NE2) and Entner's method, but doesn't sufficiently contrast with other causal effect bounding approaches (e.g., Manski's [11] bounds, Geiger et al.'s [8] inequality constraints). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of being a "novel compromise."

3. **Overstated significance of the influenza example**: The influenza case study is underdeveloped. The authors claim WPP identifies "a different witness (DM) than the standard IV approach (GRP)" but don't convincingly demonstrate that this leads to better causal conclusions. The bounds [−0.10, 0.17] are not meaningfully tighter than standard IV bounds [−0.23, 0.64], and the paper doesn't provide evidence that this difference has practical significance.

4. **Theoretical gaps**: The paper states that "any choice of w ≥ 0, y ≥ 0, x ≥ 0, 0 ≤ β ≤ 1 ≤ β̄ is guaranteed to provide bounds that are at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator," but doesn't provide a formal proof. This is a significant claim that should be supported by theory.

## Specific Suggestions

1. **Parameter selection**: Include a section on principled methods for choosing relaxation parameters, possibly using sensitivity analysis or cross-validation techniques. The paper mentions this as future work but should at least provide some heuristic guidelines for practitioners.

2. **Broader comparison**: Add a table comparing WPP with other causal bounding methods (Manski, Geiger, etc.) in terms of assumptions, computational complexity, and bound tightness.

3. **Real-world validation**: For the influenza case, include a sensitivity analysis showing how the bounds change with different parameter choices, and discuss whether the WPP bounds lead to different practical conclusions than standard IV bounds.

4. **Theoretical justification**: Provide a formal proof that the bounds are at least as conservative as back-door adjustment, rather than just stating it.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of causal inference by providing a more robust approach to bounding causal effects. The method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and includes a useful computational algorithm. However, the lack of guidance on parameter selection significantly limits the method's practical utility, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the superiority of WPP over existing methods in realistic scenarios.

The paper's main contribution is the conceptual framework of "witness protection" against faithfulness violations, which is novel and well-executed. The back-substitution algorithm is a valuable computational contribution. But the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the parameter selection issue and providing more compelling evidence of the method's practical advantages.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** (with major revisions)

The paper addresses an important problem and makes a novel contribution to causal inference methodology. However, the lack of guidance on parameter selection and the underdeveloped real-world example prevent it from being a strong NIPS submission at this stage. With revisions addressing these key weaknesses, this could become a valuable contribution to the field.
