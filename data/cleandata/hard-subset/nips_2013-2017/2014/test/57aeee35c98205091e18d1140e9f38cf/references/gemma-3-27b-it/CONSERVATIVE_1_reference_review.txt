This paper proposes a method, the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), to bound the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies, building upon the work of Entner et al. and Dawid/Ramsahai. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner’s Rule 1, originally used for identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment, as a means to find instrumental variables and then leverage linear programming (LP) to establish bounds on the ACE, accounting for potential violations of faithfulness. The authors also present relaxations to the LP for computational efficiency and a Bayesian learning approach.

The paper is technically dense and attempts to address a genuinely challenging problem in causal inference. However, I have significant reservations about its likely impact and the justification for its complexity. While the motivation – providing robustness against faithfulness violations – is sound, the proposed solution feels excessively intricate and lacks convincing evidence of substantial gain over existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Obtaining causal bounds in observational studies with limited assumptions is a crucial goal. The attempt to move beyond strict faithfulness is commendable.
*   **Novel combination of techniques:** The paper integrates concepts from instrumental variable analysis, linear programming, and Bayesian methods in a relatively novel manner.
*   **Thorough exploration of related work:** The authors demonstrate awareness of the relevant literature, citing key papers in causal inference.
*   **Algorithmic detail:** The description of the back-substitution algorithm and Bayesian learning steps is relatively detailed.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity and Lack of Clarity:** The exposition is extremely dense and difficult to follow. The numerous assumptions, parameterizations (η, ζ, ω, etc.), and relaxations obscure the core idea and make it hard to assess the practical utility of the approach. The notation is heavy and quickly becomes overwhelming.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while including both synthetic and real data, are not sufficiently compelling. The synthetic experiments compare against naive estimators, which are weak baselines. A more rigorous evaluation against established bounding methods (e.g., those based on do-calculus with sensitivity analysis, or more recent approaches leveraging tighter IV bounds) is crucial. The real-data example, while interesting, lacks a detailed comparison to standard methods. Reporting only the bounds obtained by WPP without comparing them to, for example, the interval obtained by the standard IV approach [1] feels incomplete.
*   **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the computational expense of the method and introduce relaxations, but the discussion of scalability remains superficial. The reported use of Polymake and SCDD suggests a significant computational burden. The speed-up from the back-substitution algorithm needs to be quantified more rigorously.
*   **Overly Conservative Bounds:** The emphasis on robustness against faithfulness violations seems to come at the cost of overly wide bounds. While conservatism is not inherently bad, it diminishes the practical value of the estimates. The paper doesn't adequately address the trade-off between robustness and precision.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The introduction of numerous parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and their impact on the bounds feels ad hoc. The theoretical motivation for these parameters and their impact on the ACE could be clarified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a hard problem and proposes a novel, albeit complex, approach.
*   The integration of different techniques is potentially valuable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The excessive complexity and lack of clarity hinder understanding and evaluation.
*   The empirical validation is insufficient to demonstrate significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The potential computational cost limits its practical applicability.
*   The overly conservative bounds may reduce its usefulness.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. The paper presents a technically sophisticated approach, but its complexity outweighs its apparent benefits. The empirical validation is weak, and the practical implications remain unclear. The authors need to significantly simplify the presentation, provide a more rigorous comparison against existing methods, and demonstrate tangible gains in terms of bound tightness or computational efficiency. The current form feels like a preliminary exploration of ideas rather than a mature contribution ready for NIPS. A major revision addressing these concerns would be necessary for reconsideration. I believe this paper is more appropriate for a workshop or journal with a less stringent focus on immediate impact.
