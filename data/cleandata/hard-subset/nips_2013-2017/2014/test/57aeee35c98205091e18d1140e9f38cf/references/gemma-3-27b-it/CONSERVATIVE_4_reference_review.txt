This paper proposes a novel methodology, the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret results from Entner et al. [6] to generate candidate instrumental variables and then use a modified linear programming approach (building on Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16]) to establish bounds on the ACE, relaxing faithfulness assumptions. The authors also introduce algorithmic improvements to scale the method, including symbolic bounds and a message-passing-like algorithm.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, providing sufficient background on causal inference and instrumental variables. The motivation for developing methods that are robust to violations of faithfulness is sound, given the challenges of verifying this assumption in practice. The extension of existing linear programming techniques is logically presented. However, despite the authors' efforts, I remain skeptical about the practical significance of this work, primarily due to a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating substantial improvements over existing approaches.

The primary weakness lies in the extensive reliance on assumptions and relaxations. The method hinges on finding suitable “witnesses” and admissible sets, and the authors acknowledge that identifying these can be challenging. The introduction of parameters *w, y, x, β, β̄* to relax faithfulness, while theoretically appealing, feels ad-hoc and lacks clear justification for how to select appropriate values. The paper mentions experimentation with a “few choices of default parameters” but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how these were determined or their impact on the bounds obtained. The analytical approximations and back-substitution algorithm, while presented as scaling improvements, seem to further introduce approximations that could erode the reliability of the bounds. The use of a Dirichlet prior in the Bayesian learning phase, while sensible, adds another layer of complexity and potential sensitivity.

The experimental evaluation is also unconvincing. While the synthetic studies demonstrate the method's ability to provide bounds, the comparisons against naive estimators (NE1, NE2) and the faithfulness-based estimator of Entner et al. [6] are not particularly illuminating. The authors acknowledge that WPP often provides wider bounds than the standard IV model, and the benefits of increased robustness to violations of faithfulness aren’t clearly quantified. The influenza vaccination study, despite utilizing a real dataset, is limited by the inability to validate the assigned witness (DM) and admissible set, and the final reported bounds appear relatively wide. The claim of "empirical robustness" in cases where no solution *should* exist is concerning; it suggests the method may be returning spurious results.

The paper’s originality is moderate. While the combination of Entner's Rule 1 with linear programming and the introduction of relaxation parameters are novel, the underlying concepts (instrumental variables, causal inference, linear programming) are well-established. The paper adequately references related work, but doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement over existing methods.

The significance of this work is questionable. While the problem of causal inference with observational data is undoubtedly important, the WPP, in its current form, appears too complex and reliant on assumptions to be readily adopted by practitioners. The lack of clear guidelines for parameter selection and the potential for wide bounds limit its practical utility. The computational expense, despite the algorithmic improvements, remains a concern.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in causal inference.
*   Presents a novel methodology combining existing techniques in a new way.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Excessive reliance on assumptions and relaxations.
*   Lack of clear justification for parameter selection.
*   Unconvincing experimental evaluation with limited practical demonstration.
*   Limited significance and potential impact compared to existing methods.
*   Complexity and computational cost.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** While the paper presents a technically sound approach, it lacks the compelling evidence and practical demonstration necessary to justify its complexity and overcome my skepticism regarding its usefulness. The authors need to provide a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices, demonstrate substantial improvements over existing approaches on more realistic datasets, and offer clearer guidance for practitioners. The current work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a mature, impactful contribution to the field.
