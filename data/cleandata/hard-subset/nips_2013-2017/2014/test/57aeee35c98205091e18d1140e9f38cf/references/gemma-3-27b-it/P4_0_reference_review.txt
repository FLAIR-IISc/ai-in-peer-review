This paper proposes a “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) to bound the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment as a means to find instrumental variables and relax faithfulness assumptions by introducing bounded violations of local independence. The authors formulate a linear programming (LP) approach to derive these bounds, and introduce relaxations and an efficient algorithm (back-substitution) to scale the method. The work is well-motivated, addressing the limitations of relying on strong faithfulness assumptions in practice.

**Quality:** The technical contributions appear sound, building on existing work in causal inference (Pearl, Entner et al., Dawid, Ramsahai). The LP formulation and the subsequent relaxations are presented logically. The algebraic bounds derived in Section 4 appear to offer analytical insights and computational advantages. However, the experimental evaluation, while including both synthetic and real data, feels somewhat limited in scope. The robustness to parameter choices and the performance gain from the analytical approximations aren’t thoroughly investigated. The claim of robustness in the unsolvable cases (where no witness exists) relies heavily on a relaxed criterion for witness identification and the authors acknowledge this. The reliance on rejection sampling for the Bayesian learning component could be a bottleneck for larger problems, though the authors acknowledge this.  The work is reasonably complete given the novelty of the approach, however, more detailed complexity analysis of the relaxed LP solver would strengthen the argument for scalability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the notation is dense and requires careful reading. The connection between Entner's Rule 1 and the proposed instrumental variable approach could be made more explicit. The descriptions of the relaxations and the back-substitution algorithm are understandable, but could benefit from illustrative examples. The numerous references to supplemental material create a disjointed reading experience; key details should be included in the main text where possible. Section 3 in particular is challenging to follow due to the abstract parameterization.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel combination of ideas: reinterpreting Entner’s results, relaxing faithfulness through bounded violations, and utilizing LP and analytical approximations for scalable computation. While the individual components build on prior work, the synthesis is original.  The specific approach of using the witness concept to generate instrumental variable bounds hasn’t been explored previously, as far as the authors claim, and is a significant contribution. The back-substitution algorithm offers a potentially efficient alternative to full LP optimization.

**Significance:** The proposed method addresses a crucial challenge in causal inference: dealing with unmeasured confounding and violations of faithfulness. The ability to provide bounds on the ACE, rather than relying on point estimates that may be sensitive to model assumptions, is valuable. The potential to scale the method through analytical approximations and the back-substitution algorithm makes it applicable to more complex datasets. The application to a real-world problem (influenza vaccination) demonstrates practical relevance, although a more thorough comparison against existing methods on a wider range of datasets would strengthen this claim. The work's significance lies in providing a more robust and flexible framework for causal effect estimation in observational studies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to bounding ACE in observational studies.
*   Addresses the limitations of relying on strong faithfulness assumptions.
*   Combines ideas from instrumental variables and causal discovery in a new way.
*   Provides analytical approximations and an efficient algorithm for scalable computation.
*   Demonstrates practical relevance with a real-world application.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and doesn't fully demonstrate the scalability and robustness of the method.
*   Dense notation and complex presentation make the paper challenging to follow.
*   Reliance on supplemental material for key details hinders readability.
*   The approach’s true advantage over existing methods isn’t conclusively demonstrated.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The originality and technical soundness of the proposed method are strong. Despite some clarity issues and limitations in the experimental evaluation, the potential significance of the work justifies acceptance, with encouragement for the authors to address the reviewers' comments and provide a more comprehensive evaluation in future work.
