

# Review: Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on a binary variable Y using observational data. The authors build upon the work of Entner et al. [6], who developed a method for identifying admissible sets for back-door adjustment using conditional independence constraints. The key contribution is a reinterpretation of Entner's Rule 1 to generate candidate instrumental variables and a linear programming approach to compute bounds on the ACE under relaxed faithfulness assumptions. The authors demonstrate that their method provides a compromise between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds derived from standard instrumental variable approaches.

The paper is technically sound, with clear mathematical formulations of the problem and a well-structured methodology. The authors provide a detailed explanation of their relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and how they relate to violations of faithfulness assumptions. The Bayesian learning framework for handling uncertainty in model parameters is appropriately motivated and implemented, and the back-substitution algorithm for efficiently computing bounds is a valuable technical contribution. The experimental section includes both synthetic data studies and a real-world application to influenza vaccination data, demonstrating the method's practical utility.

Strengths include:
1. Clear theoretical foundation building on established causal inference concepts (back-door adjustment, instrumental variables)
2. Novel approach to handling faithfulness violations through constrained relaxation
3. Efficient computational algorithm (back-substitution) that avoids full linear programming optimization
4. Comprehensive experimental validation with both synthetic and real data
5. Detailed discussion of the trade-offs between different relaxation parameters
6. Transparent implementation details and availability of code

Weaknesses include:
1. The paper could better motivate why their approach is superior to existing methods in practical terms, beyond theoretical guarantees
2. The experimental comparison with baseline methods (NE1, NE2, and Entner's point estimator) is somewhat limited in scope
3. The real-world influenza application doesn't fully demonstrate the advantages of WPP over simpler approaches
4. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method scales to larger datasets with more variables
5. The discussion of parameter selection (w, y, x, β, β̄) is somewhat superficial, with the authors acknowledging this as future work

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of causal inference by providing a principled way to handle violations of faithfulness assumptions that are common in observational studies. The novelty is well-justified through comparison with previous work, and the paper clearly articulates how it differs from Entner et al. [6] and the standard IV approach [1]. The authors have carefully considered related work, including the relevant NIPS proceedings (e.g., the discussion of faithfulness assumptions in [22]).

The significance of the work is strong, as reliable causal inference from observational data is a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The authors demonstrate that their method provides more robust bounds than existing approaches when faithfulness assumptions might be violated, which is a common practical concern. The Bayesian framework for handling uncertainty in the model parameters is particularly valuable for practitioners.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient mathematical detail for experts to reproduce the results. The authors make appropriate caveats about the limitations of their approach and the trade-offs between the width of the bounds and the assumptions made.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The methodology is technically sound and represents a meaningful contribution to causal inference
- The paper provides clear theoretical justification for the proposed approach
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims
- The approach addresses a practical problem in observational studies
- The back-substitution algorithm provides a computationally efficient implementation

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper could better demonstrate the practical advantages of the method over existing approaches
- The real-world application is limited in scope and doesn't fully showcase the method's benefits
- Some aspects of parameter selection remain underdeveloped

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of causal inference. The methodology is novel, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in observational causal analysis. The authors have carefully considered the relationship to previous work and provided appropriate experimental validation. While there are some limitations in the experimental design and parameter selection discussion, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and should be accepted.
