Summary
This paper tackles partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) from observational binary data when standard back-door adjustment is not justified and pure IV bounds are too loose. The authors invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a (W,Z) pair to point-identify via faithfulness/back-door adjustment, they treat W as a candidate (conditional) IV given Z, but allow bounded violations of faithfulness and IV assumptions. They parameterize deviations from exclusion/independence via relaxation parameters (w,y,x,β,β̄), derive ACE bounds via a linear program following Balke–Pearl/Dawid/Ramsahai, and present further relaxations yielding analytical (symbolic) inequalities and a back-substitution “message-passing-like” algorithm for efficiency. They also propose a Bayesian pipeline with constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to propagate sampling uncertainty and a pragmatic falsification test. Experiments include synthetic studies and one real dataset (influenza vaccine).

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on and combines (i) data-driven admissible set discovery under faithfulness (Entner et al., PC-style reasoning), (ii) partial identification and bounds for binary IV (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai & Lauritzen), and (iii) sensitivity to violations of faithfulness/exclusion akin in spirit to Manski’s partial identification and broader sensitivity analyses. The paper’s novelty is to turn Rule 1 into a generator of candidate conditional IVs and to encode approximate faithfulness as explicit bounded deviations that feed an LP bounding framework, with additional algebraic relaxations for speed.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practically relevant setting: observational studies where neither strong faithfulness-based point identification nor off-the-shelf IV bounds are satisfactory.
- Provides a clear conceptual bridge between constraint-based causal discovery (witness/admissible sets) and partial identification via LP, plus a Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty.
- The relaxation parameters make assumptions explicit and enable sensitivity analyses in principle.
- The symbolic bounds and back-substitution routine are potentially useful for scaling and for insight into how relaxations affect the feasible region.
- Code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness and justification
- The choice of relaxation parameters (w,y,x,β,β̄) is central but largely unspecified. The paper acknowledges this and defers to the supplement/future work. Without principled calibration, the resulting bounds may be arbitrary; different choices can drastically change conclusions.
- Several claims are unproven or deferred. For example, the statement that the bounds are “at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator of [6], which is always covered by the bounds” is not justified in the main text. Tightness properties of the symbolic bounds are also unclear; the authors admit additional relaxations that widen bounds but provide no quantitative gap analysis versus the full LP.
- The Bayesian “falsification test” (reject if >95% of proposals are rejected) and the use of BDeu with fixed equivalent sample size are ad hoc. There is no frequentist coverage guarantee for the bounds under sampling variability; nor is there a principled posterior over ACE, only over bounds.

Validation and comparisons
- Experiments are limited. The synthetic setups are constructed so that using the full W for back-door adjustment is wrong, but many realistic alternatives/baselines are missing. Beyond standard binary IV bounds, there is no comparison to established sensitivity analyses (e.g., Rosenbaum-style Γ sensitivity), plausibly exogenous IV frameworks (e.g., Conley–Hansen–Rossi), or modern partial identification of ACE under bounded confounding.
- The evaluation metric (distance to nearest point in an interval) favors interval methods; the authors note the trade-off but defer width analysis to the supplement. In the main paper, there is insufficient reporting of interval widths and coverage, making it hard to judge practical informativeness.
- The real-data application is a single case; the method rejects the canonical encouragement IV (GRP) and proposes a different witness/admissible set, yielding substantially tighter bounds than standard IV. Given the reliance on approximate independencies and user-chosen relaxations, stronger justification and robustness checks are needed to avoid overconfidence.

Scalability and clarity
- Scalability is a concern: exhaustive search over Z, rejection sampling in constrained Dirichlet posteriors, and repeated LPs may not be feasible for larger W. The back-substitution routine is promising but lacks formal complexity analysis and quantitative accuracy guarantees.
- The manuscript has clarity/organization issues: missing tables/figures referenced in text, typos and broken steps in the LP description, and dense notation. Critical technical details are deferred to the supplement, hindering reproducibility.

Pros
- Timely problem; explicit, interpretable relaxation of assumptions.
- Integrates causal discovery cues with partial identification.
- Derives new algebraic bounds; proposes a faster bounding routine.
- Attempts to handle sampling uncertainty.

Cons
- Core assumptions (relaxation parameters) not principled; sensitivity analysis underdeveloped.
- Limited theoretical guarantees (tightness, coverage); many key details deferred.
- Empirical evaluation modest; baselines and diagnostics incomplete.
- Scalability and clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide principled guidance or data-driven calibration for (w,y,x,β,β̄), e.g., via bounds on conditional odds ratios, partial correlations, or prior-to-posterior calibration targeting desired coverage; report sensitivity curves across a grid.
- Establish finite-sample properties: coverage of the ACE under sampling for given relaxations; conditions under which the Bayesian rejection-sampling procedure yields valid inferences.
- Quantify the gap between back-substitution and full LP, both theoretically and empirically, and analyze time complexity.
- Expand comparisons: standard IV bounds conditional on Z, Rosenbaum Γ sensitivity, plausibly exogenous IV (allowing bounded exclusion violations), and other partial identification methods.
- Add more real datasets and ablations (effect of witness quality, effect of approximate independences, robustness to BDeu hyperparameters).
- Improve exposition and provide a self-contained appendix with all key inequalities and proofs.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea of leveraging data-driven “witness/admissible” structure to tighten partial identification via controlled relaxations is interesting and potentially useful. However, the paper currently lacks principled guidance for its key assumptions, sufficient theoretical guarantees, and a convincing empirical evaluation against established alternatives. With stronger theory, clearer calibration, and more comprehensive experiments, this work could be impactful.
