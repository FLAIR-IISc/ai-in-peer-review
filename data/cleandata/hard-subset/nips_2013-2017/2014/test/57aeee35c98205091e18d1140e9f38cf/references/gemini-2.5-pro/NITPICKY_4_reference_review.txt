**Summary of the Paper**

The paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data, situated as a compromise between methods that rely on the strong, and often brittle, faithfulness assumption for point identification, and instrumental variable (IV) methods that provide bounds under different structural assumptions. The core contribution, which the authors term the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), is to re-purpose the search procedure of Entner et al. [6]. Instead of using a "witness" variable `W` to certify an admissible set `Z` for back-door adjustment, the authors treat `W` as a candidate instrumental variable and `Z` as a conditioning set. Crucially, they do not assume `W` is a perfect instrument. Instead, they introduce a set of five relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`) that quantify the degree to which key independencies (e.g., `W`'s independence from confounders or its lack of a direct effect on `Y`) are violated. These parameterized violations are then integrated into the linear programming (LP) framework of Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16] to derive valid bounds on the ACE. The paper further contributes a fast, approximate algorithm based on algebraic bounds derived from the LP, and a Bayesian framework to manage statistical uncertainty. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and applied to a real-world dataset on influenza vaccination.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of repurposing a witness as a relaxed instrumental variable is highly original and clever. It provides a principled and much-needed bridge between two important but often disconnected approaches to causal inference from observational data: constraint-based discovery (relying on faithfulness) and bounding via structural assumptions (like IVs).
2.  **Addresses a Critical Practical Problem:** Methods relying on faithfulness are known to be sensitive to finite-sample violations or near-unfaithfulness. This paper directly confronts this issue by providing a framework that is robust to such violations, controlled by explicit user-defined parameters.
3.  **Methodological Completeness:** The authors have considered the full lifecycle of the problem, developing not only the core LP formulation but also a faster approximation for scalability and a Bayesian procedure for uncertainty quantification. This demonstrates a thorough consideration of the method's practical application.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, despite its novel core idea, suffers from a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in clarity, methodology, and presentation that collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Lack of Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** The entire framework hinges on the five relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`). However, the paper explicitly defers the crucial question of how to set these parameters, stating, "we will not discuss in detail how to choose the free parameters" and "expect to extend it in follow-up publications." This is a major omission. Without principled guidance, the method's application becomes arbitrary. A practitioner is left to guess values, which could produce any desired result, from a point estimate to the vacuous `[-1, 1]` interval. This makes the work feel incomplete and its current practical value questionable. The arbitrary choice of `w = x = y = 0.2` and `β = 0.9, β̄ = 1.1` in the experiments highlights this weakness.
2.  **Lack of Clarity in the Methodology Section:**
    *   The decision to "supress from our notation" the conditioning on the admissible set `Z` in Section 3 severely harms clarity. The procedure is meant to be applied conditional on each value `z` of `Z` and then averaged. Hiding this complexity makes the core formulation difficult to follow and appears to gloss over potential combinatorial challenges when `Z` is non-trivial.
    *   The description of the "back-substitution algorithm" is procedural and lacks intuition. A more detailed explanation or a simple worked example would have been beneficial. The guarantee of convergence is asserted rather than proven, even informally.
3.  **Issues with Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The chosen evaluation metric—zero error if the true ACE is within the bound—is problematic as it does not penalize interval width. While the paper acknowledges this and refers to the supplement, the main text's results (Table 3) are difficult to interpret without a concurrent analysis of interval width. This metric favors uninformative, wide bounds.
    *   Key details of the synthetic data generation process are relegated to a footnote (Footnote 6), which is inappropriate for such a critical component of the evaluation.
    *   The "falsification test" in Algorithm 1, which rejects a model if more than 95% of rejection samples are rejected, is entirely ad-hoc and lacks any statistical justification.
4.  **Numerous Minor Presentation Flaws:** While individually small, the density of typos and inconsistencies detracts from the paper's professionalism.
    *   Footnote 1: "satisfies1 Rule 1" (missing space).
    *   Footnote 2: "Naı̈ve Estimator2" (missing space).
    *   The URL for Polymake is misspelled as "poymake.org".
    *   Algorithm 1: "upper/lowed bounds" (typo for "lower").
    *   Section 1: The text states the paper assumes familiarity with multiple foundational concepts, which is not standard practice for a self-contained conference paper.
    *   Section 1: Equation (1) defines the ACE for binary `Y`, but this restriction is not explicitly stated in the text, potentially suggesting broader applicability than is supported.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The theoretical idea is sound, but the work is incomplete. The lack of guidance on setting the relaxation parameters is a significant methodological gap. The experimental design has weaknesses in its evaluation metric and ad-hoc statistical tests.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally readable, but key methodological sections are opaque. The suppression of the conditioning set `Z` is a major flaw. The writing could be more precise, and the high number of typos suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
*   **Originality:** High. The central idea is a novel and insightful synthesis of existing work in causal inference. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior contributions by Entner et al., Ramsahai, and others.
*   **Significance:** The potential significance is high. If the methodological gaps were filled, this could become a standard and valuable tool for observational studies. However, in its current form, the method is difficult to apply responsibly, limiting its immediate impact.

**Recommendation: Reject**

I am recommending rejection for this paper, though I do so with some reluctance. The core idea is excellent and addresses an important problem in the field. However, the execution is flawed. The work feels premature for publication due to the critical omission of a methodology for selecting the relaxation parameters, which are central to the entire framework. This, combined with a lack of clarity in key sections, a problematic experimental evaluation, and numerous typographical errors, prevents me from recommending acceptance. I would be very open to seeing a revised and more complete version of this work in the future. The authors should focus on providing a principled approach to parameter selection, clarifying the role of the conditioning set `Z` throughout the methodology, and strengthening their experimental validation.
