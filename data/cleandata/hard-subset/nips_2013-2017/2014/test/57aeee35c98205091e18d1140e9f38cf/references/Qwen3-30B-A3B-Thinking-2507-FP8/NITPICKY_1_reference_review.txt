

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using "witness" variables, building on Entner et al.'s work and instrumental variable approaches. The core idea is to invert the use of Entner's Rule 1 to create a framework that provides bounds on the ACE while being robust to violations of faithfulness. The authors introduce a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) that uses linear programming with relaxed constraints to generate symbolic bounds, along with a Bayesian approach for model selection.

The paper contains several minor but significant issues that undermine its technical quality and clarity, making it difficult to assess the validity of the proposed methodology. Below I detail these concerns.

## Major Technical and Formatting Issues

1. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses "W" to represent both a witness variable and a covariate set, creating confusion. For example, "W ∈ W" (where W is a set) is inconsistent notation that appears multiple times.

2. **Definition of "Witness"**: The paper's definition of "witness" differs substantially from standard causal inference terminology, where a witness is a variable that can identify a causal effect. Here, it's used for bounding purposes, but this distinction is not clearly explained.

3. **Technical Errors**: The paper claims "faithfulness justifies the lack of an edge" (Section 3), but the reverse is true: faithfulness implies conditional independence implies no edge, not the other way around.

4. **Confusing Terminology**: The phrase "witness" is used in a non-standard way, creating confusion with the standard meaning of the term in causal inference. The paper should either justify this terminology shift or use a different term.

5. **Inconsistent Equation Formatting**: Equation numbers are formatted inconsistently (e.g., "(3)" vs. "(3) |η?x1 − η?x0| ≤ w"). The use of "η?xw" instead of proper subscript notation (η_{xw}) appears multiple times.

6. **Critical Missing Details**: The paper claims "WPP returns more than one bound" but doesn't explain how contradictory bounds should be handled or summarized, despite this being a significant practical issue.

7. **Incorrect Statistical Claims**: The paper states "The trivial interval [−1, 1] will always have zero bias" but this is false - the bias would be the difference between the true ACE and the midpoint, which isn't necessarily zero.

8. **Inconsistent Parameter Usage**: The paper refers to "free parameters" but doesn't clearly define them or explain how they affect the bounds, despite this being central to the method.

9. **Typographical Errors**: Numerous typos throughout the paper (e.g., "Naı̈ve" instead of "Naive," extra spaces before punctuation, inconsistent use of "W" vs. "w").

10. **Experimental Ambiguity**: The paper states "WPP could not validate GRP as a witness" but doesn't explain why DM is a better witness than GRP, making the real-world example unconvincing.

## Quality Concerns

The paper's technical quality is compromised by several critical issues:

1. The Bayesian approach lacks justification for the chosen priors and doesn't address how prior sensitivity affects the results.

2. The paper claims "bounds that are at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator" but doesn't define "conservative" in a way that's clear or measurable.

3. The synthetic experiments claim "WPP is quite stable" but don't define what "quite stable" means or provide statistical evidence for this claim.

4. The paper states "The final result is a set of posterior distributions over bounds, possibly contradictory" but doesn't provide a method to handle contradictory bounds, which is a significant practical limitation.

5. The paper claims "the bounds are considerably stricter than in the standard IV model" but provides no evidence or explanation for this claim.

## Clarity and Organization

The paper is poorly organized and lacks clarity in several key areas:

1. The introduction doesn't clearly distinguish the paper's contributions from previous work.

2. Section 3's mathematical derivations are confusingly presented, with inconsistent notation and missing explanations of key steps.

3. The paper doesn't clearly explain the relationship between the relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) and the resulting bounds.

4. The description of the back-substitution algorithm (Section 4) is vague and lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the method.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an interesting problem in causal inference, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, unclear definitions, and formatting issues significantly undermine its quality. These are not trivial matters but rather fundamental flaws that make it difficult to assess the validity of the proposed method. The paper appears to be a position paper rather than a complete scientific contribution, as it lacks sufficient technical detail and justification for key methodological choices.

The paper does not meet NeurIPS's standards for quality, clarity, and technical rigor. The authors have not properly accounted for the issues they raise, and the paper contains a substantial number of minor flaws that collectively make it difficult to evaluate the contribution. I recommend rejection.

The paper might be improved by:
1. Clarifying the definition of "witness" and explaining how it differs from standard terminology
2. Correcting all notational inconsistencies
3. Providing a clear justification for the Bayesian approach and prior choices
4. Defining all key terms and parameters precisely
5. Explaining how contradictory bounds should be handled
6. Providing evidence to support the claim that the bounds are "considerably stricter" than standard IV bounds

Without these corrections, the paper does not present a sufficiently rigorous contribution to warrant publication at NeurIPS.
