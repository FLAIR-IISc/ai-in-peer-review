Summary
This paper tackles partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) from observational binary data by combining constraint-based causal discovery with instrumental-variable (IV) bounds. The core idea is to repurpose pairs (W, Z) that satisfy Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (faithfulness-based admissible set discovery) not for back-door point identification, but as candidates for IV-style bounding conditional on Z. The authors then introduce a set of interpretable relaxation parameters that bound violations of the exclusion and independence conditions (w, x, y, β, β̄), thereby turning hard faithfulness claims into controlled sensitivity assumptions. Bounds are obtained through a linear-programming formulation in the style of Dawid/Ramsahai/Balke-Pearl, but extended to accommodate these bounded violations. To address computational cost and to provide insight, the paper also derives symbolic algebraic bounds and proposes a fast “back-substitution” message-passing-like tightening procedure. Finally, it provides a Bayesian treatment that puts constrained Dirichlet priors on observable contingency tables, performs rejection sampling to reflect uncertainty and model falsification, and aggregates posterior distributions over bounds. Synthetic experiments and an influenza encouragement design case study illustrate the approach.

Relation to prior work
- Balke & Pearl and Dawid/Ramsahai: classic tight bounds for binary IV models and linear-programming formulations. This paper generalizes the setup with bounded violations and conditioning on Z, and offers analytical relaxations and an efficient back-substitution procedure.
- Entner et al.: Rule-based admissible set discovery via faithfulness. The inversion—treating W as a “witness” to generate IV candidates and then protecting against mild unfaithfulness—is a novel conceptual bridge.
- Faithfulness-based discovery (PC and related): the paper acknowledges fragility of hard independence claims and replaces them with bounded deviations.
- Information-inequality and graphical-inequality work (Evans; Geiger et al.): the proposed symbolic bounds complement these directions by providing directly computable constraints tied to interpretable relaxation parameters.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) turns constraint-based discovery into a partial-identification engine with explicit knobs for sensitivity to faithfulness violations. This is a fresh and promising direction.
- Interpretability: The relaxation parameters provide a clear semantics for how much violation of exclusion/exogeneity is tolerated, enabling structured sensitivity analysis rather than all-or-nothing assumptions.
- Methodological breadth: Combines LP-based tight bounds, new analytic inequalities, a fast back-substitution tightening algorithm, and a Bayesian uncertainty treatment with falsification checks.
- Practicality: The algebraic/back-substitution approach is significantly faster than repeatedly solving LPs and offers intuition about how constraints interact. Code availability is a plus.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic studies highlight robustness to approximate independencies; the flu vaccine case study yields substantially tighter bounds than standard IV, which is compelling for practice.

Weaknesses
- Guidance on relaxation parameters: The paper acknowledges that choosing w, x, y, β, β̄ is nontrivial and postpones principled selection to the supplement/future work. More concrete, data-driven guidance in the main text would substantially increase usability.
- Bayesian component scalability: Rejection sampling in a constrained Dirichlet can be inefficient as the feasible polytope shrinks or Z grows. The paper would benefit from discussing alternative constrained-sampling strategies (e.g., hit-and-run, HMC with constraints) and reporting acceptance rates/diagnostics.
- Experimental evaluation: While the synthetic setup is thoughtful, metrics focus on distance-to-interval (which can favor wide bounds). Please report coverage, average width, and tightness gap between algebraic and LP bounds, as well as runtime. The real-data section is interesting but limited to one dataset; additional case studies or stronger sensitivity analysis in the main text would help.
- Witness search/statistical testing: Using BDeu-based independence tests and exhaustive search is reasonable for small W, but the approach to multiplicity and false positives is underdeveloped. In “no witness” scenarios, the method almost always returns some witness; calling this robustness is optimistic without calibration/false discovery control.
- Clarity/notation: The notation around η, ζ, κ, ω and the mapping of constraints to polytopes is dense, with key definitions deferred. A compact, self-contained statement of assumptions (3)–(6), their causal interpretation, and a small worked example in the main text would greatly aid readability.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with credible extensions of known LP formulations and sensible relaxations. The algebraic bounds are conservative by design; proofs and some details are deferred to the supplement. The Bayesian layer is principled but computationally basic. Experiments support claims but could broaden metrics and comparisons.

Clarity
Well organized but notation-heavy. The main ideas are present; however, accessibility would improve with:
- A concise assumptions box with intuitive interpretation of each relaxation parameter.
- A worked toy example showing how a (W, Z) pair yields bounds via both LP and back-substitution.
- A summary of computational complexity and typical runtimes for each component.

Originality
High. The inversion of Rule 1 into a protected IV-bounding framework, coupled with tunable relaxation of faithfulness and efficient algebraic/back-substitution bounds, is a novel and bold contribution.

Significance
High potential. Many practitioners face the tension between fragile point ID via faithfulness and very wide IV bounds. WPP offers a principled middle ground with interpretable sensitivity. If adopted, this could influence how observational studies are conducted and reported.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical guidance for setting w, x, y, β, β̄ (e.g., elicitation templates, defaults tied to conditional mutual informations, or calibration via placebo tests).
- Report coverage-width-runtime and LP vs algebraic/back-substitution tightness across synthetic settings.
- Explore alternative independence tests and address multiple testing/multiplicity in witness search.
- Include additional real-data applications or expanded sensitivity plots in the main text.
- Discuss scalability to larger W and non-binary variables (discretization strategies or extensions).

Recommendation
Accept. This is a creative and impactful contribution that bridges causal discovery and partial identification with interpretable relaxations and practical algorithms. Despite some incomplete validation and clarity issues, the novelty and potential significance are high, and the paper is well within the NeurIPS scope.
