

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces a novel methodology called the Witness Protection Program (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on a binary variable Y using observational data. The authors build upon Entner et al. (2013) who proposed using "witness" variables to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment. The key insight is to invert the usage of Entner's Rule 1, treating witness variables as instrumental variables rather than discarding them, and to provide bounds on the ACE that are robust to violations of the faithfulness assumption. The paper modifies linear programming approaches from Dawid (1979) and Ramsahai (2012) by introducing constraints on causal paths, and develops symbolic bounds and an efficient back-substitution algorithm.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant contribution to causal inference by bridging the gap between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. The core idea of using witness variables as instrumental variables (rather than just for back-door adjustment) is novel and well-motivated. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation with clear derivations of the bounds, and they successfully demonstrate how to handle uncertainty through a Bayesian approach with constrained Dirichlet priors. The computational efficiency of the back-substitution algorithm is a major practical contribution, as it avoids the need for expensive linear programming solutions in many cases. The experimental section shows the method's robustness in synthetic settings and provides a compelling real-world application to influenza vaccination data, where WPP identifies a more meaningful witness (DM with admissible set AGE and SEX) than the standard IV approach.

## Weaknesses

The paper would benefit from more detailed guidance on selecting the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) in practice. While the authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work, the current implementation relies on default values without sufficient justification for their choice. The experimental comparison with existing methods is somewhat limited - the authors compare against two naive point estimators (NE1, NE2) and Entner's method, but omit comparisons with more recent causal inference methods like those in the Geiger et al. (2014) or Richardson et al. (2012) papers, which have appeared in recent NeurIPS conferences. The paper would be strengthened by discussing how WPP relates to other recent work on causal bounds (e.g., the information-theoretic approach of Kocaoglu et al. in NeurIPS 2018).

The theoretical development, particularly the construction of the dual polytope in Section 3, is quite dense and might be challenging for readers without extensive background in linear programming. The authors could improve clarity by providing more intuition about why their relaxation approach works, and by explicitly stating how their method generalizes beyond the specific case of binary variables (as the paper's title suggests broader applicability). While the paper mentions extensions to non-binary variables in the conclusion, the main technical development is limited to binary cases, which constrains the practical applicability.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference with a theoretically sound and practically useful approach. The methodology provides a meaningful compromise between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables, which could become a valuable tool for practitioners in observational studies. The technical contributions are substantial, and the experimental validation demonstrates the method's utility.

The paper meets the quality standard with sound theoretical development, appropriate experimental validation, and honest discussion of limitations. The clarity is generally good, though some technical sections could be made more accessible. The originality is strong, as the witness protection framework represents a novel conceptual shift from previous work. The significance is high, as the method addresses a common practical challenge in causal inference where faithfulness assumptions are questionable but full instrumental variable approaches are too restrictive.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance, but the authors should address the following points in a revision:

1. Provide more detailed guidance on selecting the relaxation parameters, including sensitivity analysis to demonstrate how different choices affect the bounds.

2. Expand the experimental comparison to include more recent causal inference methods (e.g., those from recent NeurIPS papers) and provide more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between bound width and assumption strength.

3. Clarify the connection to recent NeurIPS work on causal inference (e.g., the 2018 papers by Kocaoglu, Shanmugam, and others) to better position the contribution within the current literature.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method might extend to continuous variables, or at least explicitly state the limitations of the binary case.

5. Simplify the presentation of the linear programming details in Section 3 to make it more accessible to a broader NeurIPS audience.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference, and with these minor revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS. The Witness Protection Program framework offers a practical and theoretically grounded approach to causal inference that could become a standard tool in the field.
