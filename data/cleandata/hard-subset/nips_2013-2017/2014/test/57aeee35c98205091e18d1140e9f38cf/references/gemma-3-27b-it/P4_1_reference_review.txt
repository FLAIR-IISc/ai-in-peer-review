This paper presents a novel methodology, the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea builds upon Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment, but reframes the approach to leverage these sets as potential instrumental variables and to explicitly account for violations of the faithfulness assumption. The method employs linear programming, with relaxations and algorithmic optimizations (including a back-substitution algorithm) to tackle computational complexity. The authors provide both theoretical analysis and empirical validation using synthetic and real-world data.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant problem in causal inference – estimating treatment effects when randomized experiments are not feasible. The connection to existing work is clearly articulated, referencing key papers in causal inference (Pearl, Spirtes et al., Dawid, Ramsahai, Entner et al.) and outlining how the proposed method extends or improves upon them. The authors correctly identify the limitations of relying solely on the faithfulness assumption and seek a more robust approach. The introduction of the "Witness Protection Program" is a clever and memorable framing of the contribution. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears theoretically sound, building on established principles of causal inference and linear programming. The relaxations introduced to manage computational complexity seem reasonable, and the back-substitution algorithm is a promising optimization. The careful consideration of violations of faithfulness is a key strength.
*   **Novelty:** The reframing of Entner et al.’s work, combined with the explicit handling of faithfulness violations and the development of the WPP framework, represents a novel contribution. The relaxation techniques and the back-substitution algorithm add further originality.
*   **Clarity:** While the topic is inherently complex, the authors generally present the material in a clear and organized manner. The use of figures (though limited) helps to illustrate key concepts. The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from background to methodology to experiments.
*   **Significance:** The ability to provide bounds on the ACE, even in the presence of unmeasured confounders and potential violations of faithfulness, is practically significant. The method has the potential to be useful in a wide range of observational studies where causal inference is crucial.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** Despite the optimizations, the paper acknowledges that the method can be computationally expensive. The scalability to high-dimensional datasets with many covariates is a concern, and more detailed discussion of this aspect would be beneficial. The conditions under which the approximations become unreliable should be clarified.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method appears to be sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The paper offers some guidance, but a more systematic investigation of parameter sensitivity and potentially adaptive methods for choosing these values would strengthen the work.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the method's performance on synthetic data, the real-world application to the influenza data is limited. A more comprehensive evaluation on diverse real-world datasets would be valuable. It’s also unclear what practical guidance the bounds derived give to a decision maker beyond “the true ACE is in this range”.
*   **Notational Density:** The paper utilizes a fairly dense notation, particularly in Section 3. While mathematically precise, this can make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific notation to fully grasp the details of the methodology. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound methodology for addressing a challenging problem in causal inference.
*   The method offers a valuable compromise between relying solely on strong assumptions (e.g., faithfulness) and employing computationally intractable methods.
*   The empirical results, while not extensive, demonstrate the potential of the approach.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the method may limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
*   The sensitivity to the choice of relaxation parameters requires further investigation.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, with a wider range of real-world applications.
*   The notation is dense and could be simplified for increased readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The WPP framework is a novel and promising approach for bounding treatment effects in observational studies. While some weaknesses remain, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding computational complexity, parameter sensitivity, and empirical validation. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the scalability of their method and provide more guidance on selecting appropriate relaxation parameters. A more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the derived bounds would also be beneficial.
