Summary
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y from observational data using a set of observed binary covariates W that are not effects of X or Y. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (which identifies admissible adjustment sets under faithfulness) and, instead of performing back-door adjustment, treat a “witness–admissible set” pair (W, Z) as the basis for an IV-style bounding exercise. Departures from the ideal graphical assumptions are encoded as bounded violations of local (conditional) independences via relaxation parameters w, x, y, β, β̄. The ACE is then bounded by solving a linear program that extends Dawid and Ramsahai’s polyhedral approach for binary instrumental variables.

Because solving many small LPs is computationally expensive within a Bayesian uncertainty-quantification scheme, the authors derive algebraic (non-tight) symbolic bounds as functions of the relaxation parameters and propose a fast back-substitution procedure that refines box constraints by iteratively propagating inequalities in a message-passing-like fashion. They also present a Bayesian learning strategy with constrained Dirichlet priors for P(Y, X, W | Z) and P(Z), using rejection sampling to respect the inequality constraints, returning posteriors over ACE bounds and providing a simple falsification test.

Experiments include synthetic studies (with and without valid witnesses; hard vs easy cases) comparing WPP to naive estimators (adjust-all covariates and unadjusted) and to the faithfulness-based point estimator of Entner et al., and an application to a well-known influenza encouragement design. In the real data, WPP produces substantially narrower bounds than standard binary-IV bounds under modest relaxations.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on (i) Entner et al. (AISTATS 2013) for data-driven covariate selection and faithfulness-based identification, (ii) Balke–Pearl and Dawid–Ramsahai for IV bounds via polytopes and LP, and (iii) Manski’s partial identification paradigm. It connects to Evans’ inequality constraints and to the geometry of IV models (Richardson et al.). The paper’s novelty lies in (a) reinterpreting Rule 1 pairs as IV-like constructs, (b) introducing tunable bounded violations of faithfulness that interpolate between back-door identification and IV worst-case bounds, and (c) providing algebraic relaxations and a fast back-substitution algorithm.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of relaxations (constraints (3)–(6)) and their integration into the LP framework are reasonable and connect cleanly to prior polyhedral treatments. The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach is conceptually sound for uncertainty propagation and falsification.
- Theoretical support: While several useful lemmas and constraints are referenced to the supplement, the main paper would benefit from a clearer, self-contained statement of the central guarantee: under the specified bounded-violation assumptions, the LP bounds contain the true ACE. The claim that any choice of relaxation parameters yields bounds at least as conservative as the back-door estimate is stated but not formally proved in the main text.
- Algorithms: The back-substitution procedure is a useful contribution; convergence is argued by monotonic tightening, but a bound on suboptimality versus the exact LP would strengthen the contribution.
- Experiments: The synthetic studies are sensible and probe robustness to approximate independencies; however, comparisons are limited to naive estimators and the Entner point estimator. Additional baselines from the sensitivity-analysis literature (e.g., Rosenbaum, VanderWeele–Shpitser), or IV sensitivity methods, would provide a more complete picture. Runtime and scalability results for the LP vs. back-substitution are only qualitatively described.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but several aspects could be clearer:
- The semantics of each relaxation parameter need a more intuitive discussion and guidance for setting them (currently deferred to the supplement). In particular, practical calibration strategies (prior predictive checks, expert elicitation) would help users.
- The LP construction (Steps 1–4) is dense, mixes notation for starred parameters and observables, and relies heavily on the supplement. A small running binary example would improve accessibility.
- The Bayesian pipeline (priors, rejection sampling details, thresholds for “falsification”) should be more explicit to support reproducibility; reporting acceptance rates and computational costs would help.

Originality
- Reinterpreting witness/admissible set pairs as IV-like bounding devices and introducing bounded faithfulness violations is novel and addresses a real limitation of faithfulness-based identification at finite samples.
- The algebraic bounds and back-substitution algorithm represent a pragmatic contribution that will matter in practice.
- The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet treatment for inequality-constrained causal models is not entirely new, but its application here is apt and fills a gap.

Significance
The approach provides a principled middle ground between brittle point identification and very wide IV bounds, offering users control over assumptions via interpretable knobs and delivering conservative, falsifiable conclusions. This is valuable for observational studies and likely to influence practice in causal discovery and estimation with partial identification.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem: robust causal effect bounding under partial knowledge.
- Conceptually elegant bridge between faithfulness-based discovery and IV bounds.
- Practical algorithmic contributions (symbolic bounds, back-substitution) and Bayesian uncertainty handling.
- Demonstrated on synthetic and real data; code availability.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical comparisons to alternative partial-identification and sensitivity-analysis methods; little runtime/scalability evidence.
- Insufficient guidance for choosing relaxation parameters; falsification thresholds feel ad hoc.
- Some theoretical claims are deferred to the supplement or stated informally; more explicit guarantees in the main text would increase confidence.
- Binary-variable restriction is limiting; discussion of extensions is brief.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a clear theorem (with sketch) that the bounds are valid under (3)–(6), and formalize the claim about conservativeness relative to back-door adjustment.
- Provide practical calibration strategies for θ = {w, x, y, β, β̄} (e.g., mapping from observed conditional associations, expert priors, or sensitivity curves), and report sensitivity analyses more prominently.
- Compare against established sensitivity frameworks (e.g., Rosenbaum’s Γ sensitivity, VanderWeele–Shpitser bias functions) and report computational costs and scaling behavior for the LP and back-substitution.
- Include an ablation: full LP vs. back-substitution vs. standard IV; Bayesian vs. plug-in constraints.
- Clarify the influenza analysis assumptions (e.g., DM not a descendant, exclusion of GRP from Z) and discuss domain plausibility.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a useful and original framework that is likely to interest the NeurIPS community working on causal inference and graphical models. With stronger empirical baselining and clearer guidance on setting relaxations, this could be a solid contribution.
