## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies, building upon the work of Entner et al. (2013) and Dawid (1990). The core idea is to reinterpret Entner's Rule 1, traditionally used for identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment, as a mechanism for finding instrumental variables. By relaxing faithfulness assumptions and formulating the problem as a linear program (LP), the authors derive bounds on the ACE. Recognizing the computational cost of the LP, they propose relaxations and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm to approximate these bounds. The method focuses on scenarios where complete knowledge of confounders is unavailable but a set of covariates is assumed to be unaffected by the treatment or outcome.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work bridges the gap between instrumental variable techniques and causal discovery methods based on conditional independence. It directly builds upon Entner et al.'s (2013) work on identifying back-door adjustment sets.  The LP formulation is inspired by Dawid (1990) and Ramsahai (2016), but extends them by incorporating constraints that reflect violations of faithfulness. It also relates to the broader literature on causal inference with imperfect compliance (e.g., Balke & Pearl, 1997) and sensitivity analysis in the presence of unmeasured confounding (e.g., Manski, 2007). The authors position their approach as offering greater control over faithfulness violations compared to relying solely on faithfulness.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The re-interpretation of Entner’s Rule 1 as a mechanism for finding instrumental variables is insightful and potentially powerful.
* **Robustness to Faithfulness Violations:**  Explicitly modeling and bounding the impact of faithfulness violations is a significant strength. Many real-world datasets are likely to deviate from strict faithfulness, making this a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The LP formulation provides a rigorous framework for bounding the ACE.
* **Practical Relaxations:**  Acknowledging the computational cost of the LP, the authors develop practical relaxations and a faster algorithm, increasing the applicability of the method.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents experiments with both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the method's performance against naive estimators and existing techniques.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides sufficient context for understanding the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the LP's complexity, the proposed relaxations still introduce parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that need to be tuned. Guidance on how to choose these parameters beyond the default values tested is limited.
* **Sensitivity to Parameter Choice:**  The bounds are sensitive to the chosen relaxation parameters, as illustrated in the experiments.  The trade-off between tighter bounds and increased conservatism isn’t fully explored.
* **Back-Substitution Algorithm:** The description of the back-substitution algorithm is concise, and more detail about its practical performance and limitations would be beneficial.
* **Empirical Results:** The comparison with the standard IV model in the real-data experiment (influenza vaccine data) is not particularly compelling.  The WPP bounds are similar to those from the standard IV approach, and the justification for the covariate choices (DM and AGE) seems somewhat post-hoc. 
* **Assumptions:**  The assumption that covariates are not effects of X or Y, while common, is still a strong one.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of violating this assumption.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to causal inference that addresses a critical challenge: dealing with violations of faithfulness.
* The method offers a more nuanced approach than simply assuming faithfulness or relying solely on instrumental variables.
* The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation with both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the method’s potential.
* The relaxations and faster algorithm make the method more practical for larger datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity and sensitivity to parameter choice remain significant concerns.
* The real-data example is not particularly convincing.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and their limitations.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of causal inference. While the computational challenges and parameter sensitivity need to be addressed in future work, the novel approach, theoretical rigor, and comprehensive evaluation make this a worthwhile addition to NIPS. The paper is of high quality and addresses an important problem. 

**Rating:**  Accept (with minor revisions). 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Parameter Selection Guidance:** Provide more guidance on how to choose the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), perhaps through a sensitivity analysis or a more detailed discussion of their implications.
* **Back-Substitution Detail:** Expand the description of the back-substitution algorithm, including its computational cost and performance characteristics.
* **Real-Data Analysis:** Provide a more compelling real-data analysis, perhaps by exploring different covariate choices or comparing the results to other causal inference methods.
* **Assumption Discussion:**  Expand the discussion of the assumptions, specifically the assumption that covariates are not effects of X or Y, and discuss the potential implications of violating this assumption.
