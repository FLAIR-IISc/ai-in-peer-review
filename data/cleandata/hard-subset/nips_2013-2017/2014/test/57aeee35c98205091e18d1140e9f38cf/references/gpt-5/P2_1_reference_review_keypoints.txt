- Proposes Witness Protection Program (WPP): invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to form conditional-IV-like bounds for ACE using binary covariates W and relaxations of faithfulness.  
- Replaces exact faithfulness with bounded, interpretable local constraints (parameters w, x, y, β, β̄) to control violations.  
- Computes bounds via an LP extension of Dawid/Ramsahai and derives symbolic inequalities enabling a fast conservative back-substitution algorithm.  
- Embeds the approach in a Bayesian workflow using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to produce posterior distributions over ACE bounds and falsification checks.  

Strengths:
- Conceptually novel: reversing Rule 1 links causal discovery to partial identification in a principled way.  
- Provides transparent, controllable relaxations that favor honest partial identification over brittle point estimates.  
- Technical contribution: LP extension plus symbolic inequalities and a fast conservative back-substitution tightening scheme.  
- Bayesian treatment acknowledges sampling variability in inequality-constrained testing.  
- Empirical illustrations show WPP can yield tighter, data-driven bounds than standard IV under approximate independences.  

Weaknesses / concerns:
- No principled guidance for choosing relaxation parameters; practitioners may struggle to calibrate them.  
- Main text lacks formal theorems guaranteeing soundness of LP bounds under relaxations and clarity on how symbolic bounds relate to tight LP bounds.  
- Falsification and selection rules (95% rejection, BDeu-based selection) are ad hoc and need more principled alternatives.  
- Scalability concerns: exhaustive Z search and rejection sampling may not scale; end-to-end complexity and runtimes are not reported.  
- Limited empirical comparisons: misses established sensitivity analyses and assessments of coverage/width-accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Presentation clarity: dense notation, key parameters relegated to supplement, and minor typos; needs a compact worked example.  

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: promising and grounded in prior work but some derivations, guarantees, and testing remain heuristic or deferred.  
- Clarity: generally understandable but dense and hard to reproduce without the supplement.  
- Originality: high — novel inversion of Rule 1 and symbolic/back-substitution machinery.  
- Significance: potentially high if parameter calibration guidance and scalable implementations are provided.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel bridge between causal discovery and partial identification with interpretable violation controls.  
- Advances computational methods for bounding and treats sampling uncertainty in constrained settings.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Lacks guidance/validation for setting relaxation parameters and has heuristic falsification methods.  
- Limited empirical validation and unclear scalability.  
- Some theoretical guarantees absent from the main text.  

Key suggestions:
- Add calibration recipes and sensitivity plots for relaxation parameters tied to domain beliefs.  
- State formal theorems on soundness and monotonicity of bounds and quantify symbolic vs. tight LP gaps.  
- Replace or augment heuristic falsification with posterior predictive checks, Bayes factors, or controlled frequentist tests and report error rates on synthetic data.  
- Provide runtime/acceptance-rate benchmarks, scalability analysis, and comparisons to standard sensitivity-analysis methods.  
- Include a small worked example from raw counts to final bounds in the main paper for reproducibility.  

Overall verdict:
- Original and promising contribution; recommend acceptance conditional on stronger parameter guidance, clearer guarantees, and broader empirical validation.
