

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a novel approach to bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) framework that modifies Entner et al.'s methodology for causal discovery. The authors invert the use of Entner's Rule 1, treating witnesses as instrumental variables rather than discarding them, and introduce relaxation parameters to handle violations of faithfulness assumptions. The method combines linear programming with Bayesian learning and develops symbolic bounds via back-substitution for computational efficiency.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The authors carefully build upon existing work (Entner et al., Ramsahai, Dawid) and provide clear theoretical derivations. The experimental design is thorough, including synthetic data with controlled bias and a real-world influenza vaccination case study. The paper acknowledges limitations and provides a thoughtful discussion of when the method might fail.

**Originality:** The core insight—using witnesses as instrumental variables rather than as tools for identifying admissible sets—is novel and well-justified. The relaxation framework for handling faithfulness violations is a significant contribution, as is the development of symbolic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from prior work on causal bounds, particularly Ramsahai's work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important in causal inference, where faithfulness assumptions are often problematic. The WPP framework provides a practical compromise between point estimation (based on faithfulness) and interval estimation (based on instrumental variables), which could be valuable for observational studies. The influenza vaccination case study demonstrates potential real-world utility.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper lacks formal proofs demonstrating that the proposed bounds are tighter than existing methods (though this is claimed in the abstract). The Bayesian approach, while described, is not rigorously justified—there's no discussion of how prior choices affect the results, and the "falsification test" in Step 5 is superficially described. The paper claims "novel symbolic bounds" but doesn't clearly establish how these differ from or improve upon existing bounds in the literature.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some technical sections (particularly the Bayesian learning section) would benefit from more intuitive explanations. The pseudo-code for Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail on the search procedure for witness-admissible set pairs, which is a critical component of the method. The connection between the relaxation parameters and their practical interpretation could be clearer.

**Significance:** The method is limited to binary variables, which restricts its applicability to many real-world causal inference problems. The experimental evaluation focuses on small-dimensional problems (|W|=8), which may not reflect the complexity of real-world applications. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how WPP improves upon existing methods in practical scenarios beyond the synthetic examples.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference with a technically sound and novel approach. The WPP framework represents a meaningful contribution to the literature on causal bounds, and the methodological extensions (symbolic bounds, back-substitution) are well-developed. However, the paper would benefit from more rigorous theoretical justification of the bounds, clearer discussion of the practical limitations, and a more thorough comparison with existing methods beyond the synthetic experiments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel inversion of Entner's Rule 1 to treat witnesses as instrumental variables
- Well-constructed relaxation framework for handling faithfulness violations
- Development of efficient symbolic bounds and back-substitution algorithm
- Practical application to influenza vaccination data

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited to binary variables, reducing general applicability
- Lack of formal proofs demonstrating tighter bounds
- Insufficient discussion of practical limitations and parameter sensitivity
- Experimental evaluation restricted to small-dimensional problems

## Recommendations

1. Provide formal proofs demonstrating that the proposed bounds are tighter than existing methods under specific conditions.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include higher-dimensional problems and non-binary variables to better assess practical utility.

3. Clarify the relationship between the relaxation parameters and their practical interpretation, and provide guidance on selecting these parameters.

4. Include a more detailed comparison with existing causal bound methods (e.g., Manski's bounds, Geiger et al.'s approach) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance.

5. Discuss the computational complexity of the method more thoroughly, particularly for larger datasets.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference methodology, but would benefit from addressing these limitations to strengthen its technical foundation and practical relevance. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. As it stands, the paper is promising but requires more rigorous theoretical justification and broader experimental validation before it can be considered for publication.
