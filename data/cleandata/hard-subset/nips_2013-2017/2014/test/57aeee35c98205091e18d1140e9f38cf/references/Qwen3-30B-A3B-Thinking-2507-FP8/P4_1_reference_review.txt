

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on a binary outcome Y. The authors build upon Entner et al.'s [6] work on identifying admissible sets through conditional independence constraints (Rule 1) and modify it to generate candidate instrumental variables rather than point estimates. The key contribution is a linear programming approach that provides bounds on the ACE by introducing constraints on causal paths, motivated as relaxations of the faithfulness assumption. The authors also develop symbolic bounds as a function of relaxation parameters and a fast back-substitution algorithm to improve computational efficiency.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS, particularly the causal inference literature including Pearl's do-calculus [14], Entner et al.'s Rule 1 [6], and the instrumental variable approaches of Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16]. It specifically addresses a limitation in existing approaches: the faithfulness assumption can lead to unstable point estimates in practice when distributions are close to simpler models. The authors provide a clear distinction between their approach and previous work, particularly by framing the problem as a relaxation of faithfulness rather than a complete replacement.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a technically sound approach that addresses a meaningful problem in causal inference. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with careful consideration of how to handle violations of faithfulness through relaxation parameters.
2. The methodology is clearly articulated, with a well-structured algorithm (Algorithm 1) that integrates the search for witnesses and admissible sets with the bounding procedure.
3. The authors provide both analytical insights (Table 2) and practical computational improvements (back-substitution algorithm), making the approach both theoretically and practically valuable.
4. The experimental evaluation demonstrates the approach's utility through both synthetic data (where the authors can control the ground truth) and a real-world influenza vaccination case study.
5. The Bayesian treatment of uncertainty in the model parameters is well-justified and addresses a common limitation in causal inference methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper focuses exclusively on binary variables, which limits its applicability to many real-world causal inference problems where variables are continuous or multi-valued.
2. The choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is not thoroughly addressed in the main paper, with the authors deferring this to supplementary material. The paper lacks clear guidance on how to select these parameters in practice.
3. The computational complexity analysis of the back-substitution algorithm is somewhat limited; the paper states it is "an order of magnitude more efficient" than the numerical solution but does not provide concrete runtime comparisons.
4. The real-world experiment with influenza data is somewhat limited in scope, as the authors exclude the intended instrumental variable (GRP) from consideration, which weakens the demonstration of the method's utility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper makes a clear technical contribution by providing a novel framework for causal inference that handles violations of faithfulness assumptions.
- The methodology is theoretically grounded, with careful analysis of how relaxation parameters affect the resulting bounds.
- The paper provides both analytical insights and practical algorithms, enhancing its applicability to real-world causal inference problems.
- The experimental results demonstrate the approach's superiority in scenarios where faithfulness assumptions fail.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The restriction to binary variables limits the paper's scope and applicability to broader causal inference problems.
- The lack of clear guidance on parameter selection for the relaxation parameters weakens the practical utility of the approach.
- The computational efficiency claims could be better supported with more detailed runtime comparisons.

**Overall assessment:**
The paper makes a solid contribution to causal inference, addressing a meaningful limitation in existing methods through the Witness Protection Program framework. The technical soundness is strong, with careful theoretical development and experimental validation. While the restriction to binary variables and lack of guidance on parameter selection are notable limitations, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in causal inference by providing a more robust approach that bridges the gap between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and purely conservative bounds.

The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written and organized (Clarity), presents a novel modification of existing approaches (Originality), and addresses a problem of practical significance in observational studies (Significance). The methodology could become a valuable tool for practitioners performing observational causal inference.

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The contribution is significant, well-executed, and addresses a meaningful gap in current causal inference methodology.
