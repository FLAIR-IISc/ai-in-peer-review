Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y from observational data when standard back-door adjustment is not justified and standard instrumental variable (IV) assumptions may be violated. The idea inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1 for identifying admissible adjustment sets under faithfulness: instead of using a “witness” W and an admissible set Z to point-identify the ACE via back-door adjustment, WPP treats W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z, and then derives bounds on the ACE while allowing bounded violations of faithfulness and of standard IV assumptions. These violations are parameterized by interpretable tolerance parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that relax independence/exclusion/independence-of-U conditions. The bound computation builds on Dawid’s and Ramsahai’s linear programming approach for partial identification under IV-like models, extended to the conditional-on-Z setting and to the relaxed constraints. Because constructing dual polytopes repeatedly is expensive, the paper also develops algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a fast back-substitution procedure that yields conservative, though not tight, bounds. To account for sampling uncertainty and model falsification, the authors propose a Bayesian scheme with constrained Dirichlet posteriors for P(Y, X, W | Z) and rejection sampling to obtain posterior distributions over ACE bounds. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination encouragement dataset illustrate that WPP often yields informative intervals and can be more robust than naïve back-door estimators or faithfulness-only point estimates.

The work sits at the intersection of causal discovery under faithfulness (Spirtes et al., Entner et al.) and partial identification/bounding under IV (Balke-Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai). It contributes by bridging these lines: replacing binary decisions from faithfulness with inequality relaxations, and propagating them through LP-based bounds, plus proposing computational accelerations and a Bayesian treatment of uncertainty.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation scheme is clearly specified via constraints (3)–(6) and is well-motivated as bounded deviations from faithfulness and IV assumptions. The LP mapping to bounds follows established practice in the IV literature, and the conditional-on-Z extension is natural. The algebraic bounds/back-substitution are correct as conservative relaxations. The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach provides a practical way to incorporate sampling variability and falsification.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments compare WPP with naïve estimators (NE1/NE2) and the faithfulness-based point estimator. The evaluation metric acknowledges the trade-off between bias and interval width, and the paper notes this caveat. The influenza case study demonstrates that WPP can yield materially tighter (and arguably more plausible) bounds than standard IV.
- Caveats: (i) Choice and calibration of relaxation parameters are crucial but largely deferred to the supplement; the default values are somewhat ad hoc. (ii) The falsification criterion (rejecting if >95% posterior proposals are infeasible) is heuristic. (iii) The selection of (W, Z) via exhaustive search/BDeu and the fact that a witness is often proposed even when no exact witness exists raise concerns about multiple testing/post-selection bias; the Bayesian procedure partly mitigates this, but stronger guarantees or corrections would help. (iv) Runtime/scale is discussed qualitatively; more systematic complexity and timing results would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized: the background situates the problem, the methodology is laid out in stages (LP, Bayesian learning, algebraic bounds), and the experiments are described with reasonable detail. However:
- The intuition behind each relaxation parameter (w, y, x, β, β̄) and how they map to concrete “amounts of violation” could be explained more pedagogically, with small numerical examples.
- Several references to figures/tables are essential (e.g., Table 1/2; Fig. 1), so ensuring they are self-contained and legible is important.
- Some notation is heavy (ζ, κ, ω, η, δ), and a consolidated notation table would help.
- Minor typos and broken URLs in references should be fixed.

Originality
- Inverting Entner’s Rule 1 to generate conditional IV-style bounds is novel and insightful.
- The bounded relaxations of faithfulness and IV constraints, and how they are propagated to bounds via LP and via algebraic inequalities/back-substitution, constitute a nontrivial methodological contribution.
- The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet posterior over bounds is also a useful idea for finite-sample uncertainty.

Significance
Partial identification under imperfect assumptions is highly relevant in practice. The proposed framework gives practitioners a continuum between fragile point identification via faithfulness and very wide IV bounds, with knobs to encode domain knowledge about plausible violations. If adopted, it could positively influence observational studies in ML, health, economics, and social science. The main potential barriers to adoption are parameter calibration, computational cost, and the complexity of the workflow; the algebraic bounds and code availability address some of this.

Pros
- Bridges causal discovery (faithfulness) and partial identification (IV bounds) in a principled way.
- Provides both exact (LP) and fast approximate (algebraic/back-substitution) procedures.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty and model falsification is thoughtful.
- Demonstrates robustness on synthetic data and adds value on a real study.
- Code availability is a plus.

Cons
- Limited guidance for choosing relaxation parameters; defaults may feel arbitrary.
- Heuristic falsification and post-selection of (W, Z) could inflate optimism; more rigorous uncertainty accounting over model search would help.
- Analytical bounds are conservative; the practical loss in tightness vs. LP not fully quantified.
- Scalability and runtime not systematically reported; reliance on brute-force search may limit larger W.
- Some exposition/notation issues and minor reference errors.

Suggestions/questions for the authors
1) Provide practical calibration guidance for (w, y, x, β, β̄): e.g., mapping from test statistics, partial R2, or effect-size priors; show how bounds change across a grid, and report width vs. coverage trade-offs.
2) Report average interval widths alongside error metrics, and include coverage of the true ACE to better assess informativeness vs. conservatism.
3) Quantify tightness loss of algebraic/back-substitution bounds relative to LP across a suite of problems.
4) Address multiple-testing/post-selection: e.g., use a split-sample strategy (select (W, Z) on one split, bound on the other), or incorporate model uncertainty over (W, Z) in the posterior summarization.
5) Provide runtime scaling with |W| and |Z|, and compare to standard LP-only pipelines.
6) Clarify the interpretation of parameters: which constraints correspond to exclusion, independence, or weak faithfulness; give toy examples.
7) In the influenza example, justify excluding GRP from Z and report sensitivity of bounds to alternative (W, Z) pairs and parameter choices.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances partial identification under realistic assumptions and connects important strands of causal methodology. With stronger guidance on parameter calibration, clearer handling of model selection uncertainty, and more thorough scalability/tightness evaluations, it would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS. As it stands, I lean toward acceptance due to the conceptual novelty and practical potential, tempered by the need for clearer calibration and evaluation details.
