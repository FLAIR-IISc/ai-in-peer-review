

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel methodology called the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on Y using observational data. The authors address a key limitation of existing causal inference methods: the fragility of faithfulness assumptions in practice. Building on Entner et al.'s work on conditional independence constraints for back-door adjustment, they invert the usage of Rule 1 to treat "witness" variables (W) as instrumental variables rather than discarding them. The WPP framework introduces relaxation parameters to measure violations of faithfulness and provides both a linear programming approach and an efficient back-substitution algorithm for computing bounds on the ACE. The paper includes theoretical derivations, analytical bounds, and experiments on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination dataset.

The work connects to existing NIPS literature on causal inference (e.g., Pearl's causal graphs, Entner et al.'s causal discovery methods) and extends the instrumental variable literature (e.g., Manski, Ramsahai) by providing a more robust framework for handling faithfulness violations.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Contribution**: The core idea of inverting Entner's Rule 1 to use witnesses for bounding rather than back-door adjustment is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation in causal discovery. The relaxation parameters for measuring faithfulness violations provide a practical way to handle the fragility of this assumption.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful mathematical derivations of the bounds. The paper properly builds on existing work while extending it meaningfully. The back-substitution algorithm is particularly elegant and addresses a computational bottleneck.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The synthetic experiments are well-designed to test the method under conditions where faithfulness assumptions might fail. The real-world application to influenza vaccination data demonstrates practical utility, showing how WPP can provide more informative bounds than standard IV methods.

4. **Balanced Discussion**: The authors are honest about limitations (e.g., the need for careful choice of relaxation parameters, the trade-off between bound tightness and assumptions) and provide a thoughtful comparison with existing methods.

5. **Practical Implementation**: The paper includes clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1) and discusses implementation details (e.g., using Polymake or SCDD for R), making it easier for practitioners to adopt the method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Parameter Selection Guidance**: The paper acknowledges that choosing relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is a critical practical issue but provides minimal guidance. In Section 5, they use default values without justification, and the supplemental material only mentions this as future work. This is a significant practical limitation for real-world adoption.

2. **Limited Comparison with Related Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it doesn't sufficiently contrast WPP with other approaches to handling faithfulness violations (e.g., the work by Meek on strong completeness). A more detailed comparison would strengthen the argument for the novelty of their approach.

3. **Theoretical Depth**: While the analytical bounds are presented, the paper could better explain why these particular relaxation parameters were chosen and how they relate to existing measures of faithfulness violation. The connection to the "degree of unfaithfulness" concept would be helpful.

4. **Real-World Impact**: The influenza vaccination application is interesting but limited to a single dataset. More extensive real-world validation would better demonstrate the method's practical value.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to causal inference by providing a practical framework for bounding causal effects that is robust to faithfulness violations. The technical quality is high, the methodology is well-motivated, and the experimental results support the claims. While the parameter selection issue needs to be addressed in future work, this is a minor limitation for a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in causal inference.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the main paper, include a more detailed discussion of how to choose relaxation parameters, perhaps with a brief example of how parameter choice affects bound tightness.

2. Add a short comparison section that explicitly contrasts WPP with other approaches to handling faithfulness violations, particularly Meek's work on strong completeness.

3. In the influenza application, include a brief sensitivity analysis showing how the bounds change with different parameter choices to illustrate the trade-offs.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how WPP might integrate with causal discovery algorithms (e.g., PC algorithm) to automatically identify witness-admissible set pairs.

5. Clarify the computational complexity of the back-substitution algorithm relative to the full linear programming approach, as this is a major practical contribution.

## Final Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to causal inference. The Witness Protection Program framework provides a novel and practical approach to bounding causal effects that is robust to faithfulness violations, which is a significant limitation in current causal discovery methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and includes thorough experimental validation. While the guidance on parameter selection needs improvement, this is a minor issue for a paper of this quality. The work advances the state of the art in causal inference and would be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality at NIPS: it's technically sound, clearly written, original in its approach, and significant for the field of causal inference. I believe it would make a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
