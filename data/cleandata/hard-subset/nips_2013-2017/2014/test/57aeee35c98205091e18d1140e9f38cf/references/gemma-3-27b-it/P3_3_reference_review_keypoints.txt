- Presents a novel framework ("Witness Protection Program") to bound ACE using witnesses and relaxed linear programming rather than point estimation.  
- Reinterprets Entner et al.'s search for instrumental variables and integrates robustness to faithfulness violations via explicit parameters.  
- Connects to core causal inference methods (do-calculus, back-door, IVs) and bridges faithfulness-based and parametric IV approaches.  
- Introduces relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) to model deviations from faithfulness for more robust bounds.  
- Derives theoretical bounds with analytical results and provides supplementary material for deeper understanding.  
- Uses linear programming with computational relaxations and a back-substitution algorithm for efficiency.  
- Empirically validated on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination real-world example showing improved behavior over naive estimators.  
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly organized despite conceptual complexity.  
- Novelty: reframing prior work toward bounding ACE is a genuine contribution.  
- Robustness: explicit modeling of faithfulness violations is a significant practical strength.  
- Theoretical foundation: sound derivations and analytical bounds strengthen credibility.  
- Empirical validation: synthetic and real-data experiments illustrate method effectiveness.  
- Computational complexity: scaling remains a concern, especially with high-dimensional covariates.  
- Parameter sensitivity: lacks guidance and systematic exploration for choosing relaxation parameters; experiments rely on arbitrary defaults.  
- Interpretation of parameters: practical justification of parameter values from domain knowledge is unclear.  
- Limited real-world application: influenza example is simple; scalability to complex, non-binary, high-dimensional datasets is uncertain.  
- Bayesian sampling concerns: reliance on Dirichlet prior and rejection sampling risks inefficiency and failure; convergence and prior choice need more discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, addresses key observational study challenges, and shows empirical promise.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational and parameter-selection issues and uncertain practical applicability to complex problems.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with valuable contribution but needs minor revisions to address computational, parameter, and sampling concerns.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, asking for expanded discussion on parameter selection, computational scaling, Bayesian sampling details, and broader real-world applications.
