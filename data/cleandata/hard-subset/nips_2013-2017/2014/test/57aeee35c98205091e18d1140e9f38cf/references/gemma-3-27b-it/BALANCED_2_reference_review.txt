This paper proposes a novel methodology, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), to bound the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.'s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment as a way to find instrumental variables, and then apply a linear programming framework, further relaxed for computational efficiency, to derive bounds on the ACE. The authors address potential violations of the faithfulness assumption by incorporating parameters that allow for bounded deviations from conditional independence. They provide both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation on synthetic and real data.

The paper tackles a crucial problem in causal inference: estimating causal effects when randomized experiments are not feasible. The motivation is clear and the problem well-defined. The attempt to move beyond strict faithfulness assumptions and provide more robust bounds is commendable. The connection to instrumental variable methods is well-motivated and the development of the linear programming framework, alongside the relaxations for scalability, represents a significant technical contribution. The inclusion of both theoretical development and experimental validation strengthens the work. The presentation of the algorithm in Algorithm 1 is clear and useful. The exploration of algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm provide a tangible pathway to faster computation, which is vital for real-world application. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The sheer number of assumptions and parameters introduced (w, y, x, β, β̄) feels overwhelming and lacks a clear guidance on how to choose them in practice beyond the default choices tested in Section 5. While the authors acknowledge this and point to future work, the lack of practical advice makes the method less immediately accessible. The explanation of the relaxation techniques, while present, is often dense and requires significant effort from the reader to follow. The connection between the relaxation parameters and the resulting conservatism of the bounds could be more clearly articulated. The experiments, while valuable, are somewhat limited in scope. The influenza dataset analysis feels more like an illustration than a rigorous validation. A more comprehensive comparison with existing methods beyond NE1, NE2, and Entner et al.'s approach, especially modern IV methods and other bounding techniques, is needed.  The notation is occasionally cumbersome and could be streamlined. The discussion of the Bayesian learning aspect feels somewhat tacked on and could be better integrated into the overall methodology. The justification for using a Dirichlet prior, while standard, could benefit from a more detailed explanation in this specific context.  There’s a reliance on the Supplemental Material for key details, making it harder to fully grasp the methodology without consulting it. While understandable given space constraints, crucial elements like the derivation of the algebraic bounds should ideally be presented more prominently.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The WPP framework offers a unique perspective on combining ideas from instrumental variables and backdoor adjustment, providing a potentially more robust approach to causal inference.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The development of the linear programming framework and the relaxations for computational efficiency demonstrates technical expertise.
*   **Potential Significance:**  If successfully applied and refined, this method could have a significant impact on observational studies where strict assumptions about causal graphs are difficult to justify.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors address potential limitations related to faithfulness and propose a framework for dealing with violations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The large number of parameters and the density of the theoretical exposition make the method challenging to understand and apply.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope and do not provide a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.
*   **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper lacks clear guidance on how to choose the relaxation parameters in real-world applications.
*   **Heavy Reliance on Supplemental Material:** Critical details are relegated to the supplement, hindering a complete understanding of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and potentially significant contribution to the field of causal inference. However, the complexity of the method and the limited empirical validation raise concerns. While the technical soundness is evident, the lack of practical guidance and the heavy reliance on the supplemental material detract from the paper's overall impact. 

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise. 

The authors should focus on simplifying the presentation of the method, providing more concrete guidance on parameter selection, and expanding the experimental validation with a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods.  Streamlining the notation and integrating key details from the supplement into the main text would also significantly improve the paper's clarity and accessibility. Addressing these concerns could transform this paper into a strong contribution to NIPS.
