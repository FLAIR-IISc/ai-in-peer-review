

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on a binary variable Y using observational data. The authors build upon Entner et al.'s work on instrumental variables and back-door adjustment, introducing the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) as a framework that provides more robust causal bounds by relaxing faithfulness assumptions. The key insight is to reinterpret Entner's Rule 1 to use witness variables as instrumental variables, then introduce constraints on causal paths to generate bounds on the ACE using a linear programming approach. The paper also develops symbolic algebraic bounds and a fast back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency.

The work relates to important NIPS contributions in causal inference, particularly the foundational work by Pearl [14] and the more recent contributions on causal discovery and identification (e.g., Spirtes et al. [22], Entner et al. [6]). The paper extends Ramsahai [16] and Dawid [5] by providing a more flexible framework for handling violations of faithfulness, which is a common practical concern in causal inference applications.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical foundations. The linear programming formulation is well-motivated and the authors carefully explain the relaxation of assumptions. The experimental evaluation on synthetic data demonstrates the method's robustness in cases where faithfulness-based point estimators fail. However, the real-world application to influenza vaccination data is somewhat limited to a single dataset, and the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the trade-offs between different parameter choices for the relaxation (w, y, x, β, β̄) in practical settings. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide sufficient guidance for practitioners on selecting these parameters.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between theoretical development and practical implementation. The background section effectively sets up the problem, and the algorithms (especially Algorithm 1) are well-described. Some technical details in Section 3 (the linear programming formulation) are quite dense and might challenge readers without strong causal inference background. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the relaxation parameters lead to the observed improvements in robustness.

**Originality:** The core contribution of the "Witness Protection Program" represents a meaningful extension of existing causal inference methods. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work by addressing the practical instability of faithfulness assumptions. The symbolic bounds and back-substitution algorithm are novel contributions that provide both theoretical insights and computational advantages. While building on Entner et al. [6], the authors provide a different interpretation and extension that is distinct from previous approaches.

**Significance:** The problem of causal inference from observational data is highly significant for many applications, and the proposed method offers a practical compromise between point estimation (which requires strong assumptions) and purely instrumental variable bounds (which can be overly conservative). The influenza vaccination case study demonstrates real-world applicability. However, the paper's significance would be strengthened by more extensive validation across diverse datasets and clearer articulation of the practical advantages over existing methods in common causal inference scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a meaningful extension to existing causal inference methods that addresses a practical limitation (faithfulness assumptions)
- Develops both theoretical bounds and a computationally efficient algorithm
- Demonstrates empirical robustness in synthetic experiments
- Offers a clear framework for practitioners to apply the method
- Includes a real-world case study with meaningful interpretation

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient guidance on selecting relaxation parameters in practice
- Limited real-world validation beyond a single case study
- The computational efficiency claims need more empirical support
- The connection to recent NIPS work on causal inference could be strengthened
- The trade-off between bound width and assumption strength is not thoroughly explored

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand the real-world validation by applying the method to multiple diverse datasets beyond the influenza case study.
2. Provide more detailed guidance on choosing the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) in practice, perhaps with a sensitivity analysis.
3. Include a more thorough comparison with recent NIPS papers on causal inference (e.g., from the past 2-3 years) to better situate the contribution.
4. Add a section discussing the trade-offs between bound width and the strength of assumptions made, with practical guidance for practitioners.
5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method might extend to non-binary variables, which would broaden its applicability.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference by providing a more robust framework for estimating causal effects from observational data. The WPP approach offers a practical compromise between point estimation and interval estimation that should be useful for practitioners. With the suggested improvements, this work has the potential to become a standard tool in causal inference toolboxes.
