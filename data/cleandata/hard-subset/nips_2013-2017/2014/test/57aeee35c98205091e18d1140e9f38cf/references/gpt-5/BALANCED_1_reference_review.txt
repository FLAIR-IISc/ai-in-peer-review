Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a methodology to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y using observational data with covariates W that are not effects of X or Y. The core idea is to invert Entner et al.’s “Rule 1” (which uses conditional independence tests under faithfulness to identify admissible back-door adjustment sets) and instead treat any discovered (witness, admissible set) pair (W, Z) as the basis for instrumental-variable-like bounds on the ACE. To bridge the gap between strict faithfulness (which enables point identification but can be brittle) and fully nonparametric IV bounds (which can be too wide), the paper introduces bounded relaxations of faithfulness via local constraints on deviations from conditional independences, encoded by parameters w, x, y, β, β̄. These yield a linear programming formulation extending Dawid and Ramsahai’s polytope-based approach for the (binary) standard IV model to settings with approximate independence assumptions.

Computationally, the paper provides: (i) an LP-based method to compute bounds (with a Bayesian treatment that samples from constrained Dirichlet posteriors for P(Y, X, W | Z)); and (ii) looser, symbolic algebraic inequalities combined with a back-substitution “message-passing” algorithm that is much faster and often close to the LP solution. Experiments on synthetic data compare WPP to naive estimators (unadjusted and adjusted on all W) and to the faithfulness-based point estimator of Entner et al.; a real example on influenza vaccination (with an encouragement design) illustrates narrower bounds than classic IV bounds when leveraging conditional independences.

The paper relates to: Pearl’s do-calculus and back-door adjustment; the standard IV literature and nonparametric IV bounds (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins); causal discovery under faithfulness (PC algorithm; Entner et al.); and partial identification and sensitivity analysis (Manski). Its novelty lies in systematically turning discovered (approximate) conditional independences into partial-identification constraints with explicit slack parameters, yielding principled, tunable bounds rather than point estimates.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: Elegant synthesis of causal discovery (faithfulness-based screening) with partial identification via IV-type bounds, addressing known brittleness of faithfulness and width of IV bounds.
- Methodological rigor: Careful extension of polytope-based IV bounds to settings with bounded violations, with clear links to observables via κ and ω parameters and constraints.
- Computational contributions: Practical algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that greatly reduce computation while maintaining reasonable tightness; a Bayesian scheme to propagate uncertainty and avoid plug-in brittleness.
- Practical relevance: Fits common observational scenarios where full confounding control is unlikely; produces honest bounds with interpretable sensitivity parameters; provides code.
- Empirical indication: Synthetic studies show improved robustness relative to naive adjustment and faithfulness-only estimators; real-data example demonstrates potential to meaningfully narrow classic IV bounds.

Weaknesses
- Choice/calibration of relaxation parameters: The method’s conclusions depend critically on w, x, y, β, β̄, but guidance is deferred to the supplement and “future work.” Without principled calibration or priors, applied users may struggle to set these knobs and to interpret resulting bounds.
- Limited empirical breadth: Comparisons focus on two naive baselines and the Entner et al. estimator. Missing comparisons with alternative partial-identification/sensitivity approaches (e.g., Rosenbaum bounds, monotone IV, bias functions/OVB formulas, or recent bounding methods for confounding) and with stronger causal discovery + adjustment pipelines.
- Scalability and search: The witness/admissible-set search is exhaustive over Z and relies on BDeu-based independence testing; scalability and robustness to model misspecification are not well characterized. The Bayesian rejection sampling over constrained Dirichlets may be expensive for higher-dimensional Z.
- Theoretical guarantees: While constraints are designed to be conservative, the paper does not provide finite-sample or asymptotic coverage guarantees for the posterior bounds when the true data-generating process satisfies the relaxed constraints, nor uniformity properties as in robustness literature.
- Clarity and exposition: Many key derivations, constraints, and algorithmic details are deferred to the Supplementary Material. Some notation (e.g., ζ, κ, η, ω) is dense and could be better motivated with small running examples. The connection between faithfulness violations and the chosen slack constraints could be made more transparent.

Comments by review criterion
- Quality: The construction appears technically sound and grounded in established IV polytope methods, with reasonable conservative relaxations. However, correctness and tightness of the symbolic bounds rest on proofs in the supplement; a succinct theorem statement in the main text would help. The Bayesian procedure is reasonable but computationally heavy and lacks formal guarantees.
- Clarity: Overall readable but dense. Algorithm 1 is helpful; clearer guidance on the search and independence-testing choices, and a schematic example walking through constraint construction, would improve accessibility.
- Originality: High. The inversion of Rule 1 to create candidate IV-style constraints with bounded violations, plus the algebraic/back-substitution machinery, is novel and interesting.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for observational studies across ML and applied sciences, offering a middle ground between brittle point identification and overly wide IV bounds. Broader empirical validation and parameter-calibration guidance would enhance practical adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated framework bridging causal discovery and partial identification
- Solid methodological foundation and practical computation
- Honest, tunable robustness to faithfulness violations
- Relevance to widely encountered observational settings; public code

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of principled parameter calibration and sensitivity guidance in the main text
- Limited empirical comparisons and scalability analysis
- Heavy reliance on supplement for key derivations; few theoretical guarantees stated
- Real-data evaluation is thin and relies on potentially debatable witness choices

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical guidance for choosing w, x, y, β, β̄: e.g., elicitation templates, mapping to effect sizes/partial correlations, prior distributions with interpretability, or calibration by posterior predictive checks controlling the implied slack in conditional independences.
- Add theoretical results: conditions under which bounds achieve nominal coverage asymptotically; monotonicity/tightness properties as parameters vary; complexity and convergence of back-substitution.
- Expand empirical evaluation: include alternative partial-identification baselines; stress tests across sample sizes, strength of instruments, degree of confounding, and mis-specified witnesses; ablations comparing LP vs back-substitution vs algebraic-only.
- Improve clarity: a small running binary example with explicit numbers; a diagram clarifying how Rule 1 inversion yields constraints; a table relating each slack parameter to specific independence deviations.
- Discuss extensions beyond binary variables and guidance for continuous/discrete mixtures.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents an original and promising framework with solid technical underpinnings and appealing practical potential. However, the current version leaves crucial questions about parameter calibration, empirical breadth, and theoretical guarantees insufficiently addressed for the NeurIPS bar. With stronger guidance on setting the relaxation parameters, expanded comparisons and scalability analysis, and clearer theoretical statements, I would be enthusiastic about acceptance.
