Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), a framework to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y when confounding is present and classical back-door adjustment is unreliable. The key idea is to invert Entner-Hoyer-Spirtes Rule 1: rather than using a discovered witness W and admissible set Z to produce a point-identified back-door adjustment, treat W (conditional on Z) as a “soft” instrument and compute bounds on ACE under controlled relaxations of the Rule 1 premises. The relaxations are expressed as local sensitivity parameters: (i) w bounds a violation of exclusion (how much Y’s interventional response can depend on W given X), (ii) y and (iii) x bound discrepancies between interventional and observational conditionals P(Y|X,W) and P(X|W), and (iv) β, β̄ bound the strength of dependence between the putative instrument W and latent U (violating independence). When w=0, x=y=0, β=β̄=1, one recovers the chain W→X→Y; with w=0, β=β̄=1, one recovers standard IV; with large relaxations, bounds become vacuous.

Building on Dawid (2002) and Ramsahai (2012), the authors formulate a linear programming (LP) problem over a polytope in response-function space to obtain bounds on ACE conditional on Z and then average over P(Z). They also propose (a) a Bayesian implementation using constrained Dirichlet posteriors with rejection sampling to propagate sampling uncertainty and perform a simple falsification check, and (b) symbolic algebraic (non-tight) inequalities and a fast back-substitution algorithm that approximates the LP solution at much lower computational cost.

Experiments on synthetic DAGs compare WPP to naïve estimators (conditioning on all W or on none) and to the faithfulness-based point estimator of Entner et al. They report that WPP yields stable bounds with lower error tails, including when exact witnesses do not exist but near-independences are found. On influenza data with physician reminder letters (GRP) as an encouragement, standard IV yields [-0.23, 0.64] while WPP—using DM (diabetes) as “witness” and AGE, SEX as Z, with moderate relaxations—yields [-0.10, 0.17].

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge between constraint-based causal discovery (faithfulness) and partial identification via IV bounds. The controlled violations (w, x, y, β, β̄) provide an interpretable sensitivity analysis that interpolates between point identification and worst-case bounds.
- Methodological solidity: the LP formulation follows established potential-outcome/response-function polytopes; the new constraints implement clear relaxations of exclusion and instrument independence.
- Practical contribution: symbolic inequalities and the back-substitution algorithm provide a pragmatic alternative to repeated LP solves, with empirical evidence of good approximation.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty over observed contingency tables and a falsification check addresses finite-sample issues that plague constraint-based decisions.
- Empirical evaluation explores both “hard” and “easy” regimes and a real dataset; the framework appears robust to approximate independences.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Choice and calibration of relaxation parameters: w, x, y, β, β̄ are central yet selection is ad hoc in the main text, with limited guidance. The influenza example’s narrower bounds hinge on a particular choice (w=x=y=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1). Without a principled mapping from substantive knowledge or sensitivity metrics (e.g., partial R2, bounds on odds ratios), users may “tune” to desired widths.
- Validity under search: W and Z are selected via a model selection procedure over many candidates. This induces post-selection uncertainty and potential overfitting; the current Bayesian step conditions on a chosen (W,Z) rather than integrating over model uncertainty across candidates, and the simple “95% rejection” falsification rule is heuristic.
- Interpretation of witnesses: Treating variables such as DM as quasi-instruments is substantively questionable (direct effects on Y beyond X are plausible). While WPP is designed to allow violations, in applied settings the reader needs more guidance on assessing plausibility and on how bounds move under alternative witnesses.
- Clarity/notation: The paper is notation-dense. The starred vs unstarred parameters (η?xw, δ?w, ζyx.w, κ, ω) and their relations could be made clearer up front, ideally with a compact graphical mapping and a worked toy example. Some key proofs and the full list of symbolic inequalities are deferred to the Supplement, which limits reproducibility.
- Scalability: The Bayesian rejection sampler over constrained Dirichlets may be inefficient as constraints tighten or |Z| grows. The algorithm relies on small-dimensional Z; the discrete PCA suggestion is interesting but untested here. Runtime comparisons (LP vs back-substitution) are mentioned but not quantified.
- Tightness guarantees: The algebraic bounds and back-substitution are further relaxations. While empirically close, there is no characterization of the worst-case gap to the LP bounds, nor conditions under which back-substitution attains the LP solution.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide principled guidance for choosing w, x, y, β, β̄. For example, relate them to bounds on log-odds ratios, maximal controlled direct effect, or sensitivity models (e.g., Rosenbaum’s Γ). Include contour plots of bound width as a function of parameters and, if possible, calibrate to plausible domain priors.
- Report coverage properties: in simulations, does the true ACE fall within posterior mean bounds at the target frequency? Consider posterior intervals over bounds (e.g., 90% credible bounds-on-bounds) to reflect sampling uncertainty.
- Quantify computational performance: median runtimes for LP vs back-substitution across settings; acceptance rates for the constrained Dirichlet sampler; any mixing issues.
- Address selection effects: either average bounds across top-k (W,Z) with model weights, or provide a post-selection correction/falsification test akin to overidentification tests in IV.
- For the influenza study, compare WPP to standard IV with auxiliary assumptions (e.g., monotonicity/LATE) and to Rosenbaum-style sensitivity analysis; report how bounds change under alternative plausible witnesses and parameter settings.
- Improve exposition: unify notation for starred/unstarred parameters; include a small numerical example; move key inequalities from the Supplement; clarify the mapping from constraints (3)-(6) to faithfulness violations.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Generally sound and honest about trade-offs. The main methodological claims are well-supported by prior LP-based bounding theory, though some theoretical aspects (tightness, selection) could be strengthened.
- Clarity: Dense but readable for experts; could be improved with clearer notation, examples, and more main-text guidance on parameter choice.
- Originality: Novel synthesis of discovery-driven witnesses with IV-style partial identification using local relaxations; new symbolic bounds and a practical back-substitution algorithm.
- Significance: Addresses an important practical gap between brittle point-identification via faithfulness and often-wide IV bounds. Likely to influence practice in observational studies and to be built upon.

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative and timely bridge between causal discovery and partial identification.
- Practical algorithms with code; empirically useful robustness to near-faithfulness.
- Clear, interpretable sensitivity parameters connecting assumptions to bounds.

Cons for acceptance
- Parameter selection and post-selection inference not fully principled.
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the approximate algorithm’s tightness.
- Exposition and guidance for practitioners need strengthening.

Overall assessment
A strong and thought-provoking contribution to causal inference at NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance after revisions addressing parameter calibration, selection effects, and clarity, as this work advances partial identification in a way that is both principled and practically useful.
