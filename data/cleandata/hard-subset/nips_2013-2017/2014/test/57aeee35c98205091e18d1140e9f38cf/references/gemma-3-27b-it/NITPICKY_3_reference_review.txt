This paper proposes a method, the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using observational data and a set of covariates. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment, framing these sets as providing instrumental variables and utilizing linear programming (LP) relaxations to handle faithfulness violations. The authors also introduce algorithmic improvements to scale the LP approach and a Bayesian learning framework.

While the motivation for addressing the challenges of faithfulness in causal inference is sound and the problem setting relevant, the paper suffers from a concerning level of detail-oriented flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of compelling evidence for the practical benefit of its contributions relative to its substantial complexity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Causal inference with observational data is a critical area.
*   **Novel Interpretation:** Reinterpreting Entner et al.’s work in the context of instrumental variables is a potentially useful perspective.
*   **Technical Depth:** The paper presents a detailed, mathematically-grounded approach.
*   **Algorithmic Considerations:** The introduction of relaxations and a fast algorithm demonstrate attention to computational feasibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The paper’s notation is frequently inconsistent. For example, the meaning of subscripts in η and δ variables shifts without clear explanation. The use of ζ and κ is also confusing.
*   **Missing Definitions:** Several key terms are referenced without sufficient definition, forcing the reader to refer back to cited papers (e.g., “BDeu score”). While referencing is appropriate, essential concepts should be briefly explained for clarity within the paper itself.
*   **Overly Complex Presentation:** The presentation is dense and difficult to follow. Equations are often presented without sufficient context or explanation of their components. The jump between the LP formulation and the back-substitution algorithm feels abrupt.
*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experiments presented are limited in scope and lack strong justification for parameter choices (w, y, x, β, β̄). The comparison to simpler methods (NE1, NE2, faithfulness-based estimator) isn’t convincing and is presented in a rather disorganized fashion within Table 3. The justification for choosing DM and AGE in the influenza study is weak.
*   **Unclear Scaling Benefits:** The analytical approximations are mentioned as a route to scaling up the approach, but their practical impact isn't demonstrated convincingly. The claim of a significant speedup from the back-substitution algorithm isn’t supported by concrete timing results.
*   **Relaxation Without Justification:** The authors repeatedly mention "relaxations," but the rationale behind *why* these particular relaxations are reasonable isn’t always clear. What are the consequences of these relaxations on the accuracy of the bounds?
*    **Minor Errors:** There are several minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper. For instance, in the introduction, “interest of space” should be “interest of brevity”.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   Section 3 lacks sufficient justification for the introduced assumptions (equations 3-6). Why are these assumptions necessary, and under what conditions are they reasonable?
*   The discussion of the Bayesian learning approach in Section 3.1 is vague. How does the Dirichlet prior handle the constraints implied by the polytopes? What are the computational challenges of rejection sampling in high dimensions?
*   The description of the back-substitution algorithm in Section 4 is difficult to follow. More details on the lexicographical ordering and tightening of bounds would be helpful.
*   Table 3 presents results for only one parameter setting (w=x=y=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1). This limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   The conclusion feels overly optimistic given the complexity of the method and the limitations of the empirical evaluation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference.
*   The proposed method offers a novel perspective on combining instrumental variables and faithfulness assumptions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is overly complex and difficult to follow.
*   The notation is inconsistent and the presentation lacks clarity.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn't provide strong evidence for the practical benefits of the method.
*   The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The authors do not sufficiently address the potential consequences of the relaxations they introduce.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential novelty of the approach, the paper, in its current form, is not suitable for acceptance at NIPS. The technical details are overly complex, the presentation lacks clarity, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient. The numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies further detract from the paper's quality. While the core ideas are intriguing, they are obscured by a lack of rigor and a failure to demonstrate practical advantages. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, focusing on clarity, consistency, and a more thorough empirical evaluation. Specifically, they should provide a more intuitive explanation of the method, clarify the notation, and conduct experiments with a wider range of parameters and datasets. A rigorous comparison to existing methods, with a clear demonstration of the benefits of their approach, is essential.
