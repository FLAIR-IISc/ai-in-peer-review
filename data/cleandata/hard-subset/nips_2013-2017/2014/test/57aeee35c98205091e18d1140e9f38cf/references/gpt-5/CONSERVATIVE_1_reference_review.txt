Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y from observational data with binary covariates W. The core idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 for discovering admissible adjustment sets: instead of using a “witness” W to justify back-door adjustment with Z, the pair (W,Z) is used to construct conditional instrumental-variable-type bounds. The method allows controlled departures from faithfulness via bounded violations of local independences, parameterized by (w,y,x,β,β̄) in constraints (3)–(6), and casts the problem as a linear program over a polytope formed by latent response type parameters {η?xw,δ?w}. The authors also propose: (i) a Bayesian approach with constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to propagate sampling uncertainty; (ii) algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a fast back-substitution “message passing-like” algorithm as a computationally lighter relaxation; and (iii) a search procedure (Algorithm 1) to identify candidate (W,Z) pairs and return posterior distributions over bounds.

The work is positioned between faithfulness-based point identification (Entner et al., Rule 1) and classical IV partial identification (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai). Compared to conditional IV bounds, the novelty is to incorporate bounded violations of faithfulness and hidden confounding via (3)–(6) and to propagate uncertainty. The paper relates to partial identification (Manski) and inequality constraints in IV models (Balke-Pearl; Richardson & Robins) and to confounder-selection criteria (VanderWeele & Shpitser).

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: robust causal effect estimation under model uncertainty and partial identification.
- Conceptual bridge between faithfulness-based discovery and IV bounds is interesting and may be practically useful when back-door fails or is fragile.
- Mathematical formulation is careful, grounded in prior LP-based partial identification literature; the proposal to encode bounded violations via (3)–(6) is clear.
- The symbolic bounds and back-substitution heuristic offer practical speedups and some intuition about how relaxation parameters affect bounds.
- Includes both synthetic and a real data example (influenza encouragement design); publicly available code is a plus.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- The key relaxation parameters (w,y,x,β,β̄) drive the results but are left essentially ad hoc. The main text defers choosing them to the supplement and “future work.” Without a principled, data-driven calibration (e.g., sensitivity curves, prior elicitation tied to domain observables, or falsification tests with nominal error control), conclusions can be arbitrarily wide or optimistic.
- The Bayesian component is superficial: a constrained Dirichlet prior with rejection sampling is used, but (a) the “falsification test” (reject if >95% rejections) is ad hoc; (b) posterior bounds are often summarized by running the LP only on posterior-mean contingency tables, which undermines full posterior propagation; and (c) the paper acknowledges cases where posterior means lie outside the feasible region, then falls back to heuristic bounds. This weakens the claimed Bayesian rigor.
- The algebraic/back-substitution bounds are acknowledged to be looser than the numerical LP, but there is no theoretical approximation guarantee (e.g., bound inflation factor) nor a systematic empirical comparison beyond anecdotal timing; it is unclear how much tightness is lost on average.
- The linear-program construction and polytope manipulations depend heavily on supplementary material and external tools; the main text lacks enough detail to reproduce the dual polytope/inequality derivation.
- The claim that WPP bounds are “at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator of [6], which is always covered by the bounds” is asserted but not proved in the main text; a formal statement and proof (including conditions) would be helpful.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and sometimes hard to follow. Several important steps are relegated to the supplement (e.g., derivation of constraints from (3)–(6), dual polytope construction). Some notation is introduced quickly (ζ,κ,ω), and the switch to treating κ,ω as unknowns could be explained more carefully.
- The search procedure for (W,Z) and the decision rules for conditional independence (BDeu choices, thresholds) could be described more clearly; currently the design choices appear somewhat arbitrary.

Originality
- Inverting Rule 1 to generate conditional IV-style bounds and adding bounded-faithfulness constraints is a novel combination. However, the conditional IV idea and LP-based partial identification are well-established. The incremental novelty lies in the particular parameterization (3)–(6) and the symbolic/back-substitution heuristic.

Significance/empirical validation
- Experiments are limited. Synthetic studies are tailored to scenarios where adjusting for all covariates is harmful, and evaluation uses an error metric that makes interval estimators incomparable to point estimators (the paper itself notes that [−1,1] would get zero error). Coverage rates, interval widths, and tightness relative to ground truth should be reported systematically. Ablations (full LP vs back-substitution; with/without Bayesian sampling; sensitivity to (w,y,x,β,β̄)) are needed.
- The real-data application is a single, well-known dataset; the bounds become narrower than standard IV after choosing a particular (W,Z). The justification for excluding GRP from admissible sets and the robustness of the chosen witness/admissible set should be examined; alternative (W,Z) choices and resulting bounds are not explored.
- Scalability is unclear. The approach assumes modest |Z|, binary variables, and relies on rejection sampling with constrained Dirichlets; it is not evident how it performs for higher-dimensional covariates or non-binary variables.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a principled procedure to choose or calibrate (w,y,x,β,β̄), e.g., elicitation tied to observed near-independences, sensitivity plots over grids, or empirical Bayes calibration via posterior predictive checks.
- Report coverage, average interval width, and fraction of vacuous intervals across simulations; include ablations comparing LP vs symbolic bounds and showing computational/runtime trade-offs.
- Clarify whether WPP always contains the faithfulness-based point estimate and under what conditions.
- Discuss extension beyond binary variables and the computational implications.
- Compare against other partial identification methods (e.g., Balke-Pearl conditional on covariates, more recent inequality-based approaches) on the same datasets.

Pros
- Timely contribution at the interface of causal discovery and partial identification.
- Combines established LP-based bounds with a novel relaxation scheme and a faster heuristic.
- Provides code and some empirical evidence.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on uncalibrated relaxation parameters; limited guidance on setting them.
- Bayesian treatment is ad hoc and not fully integrated with the LP bounds.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and uses a problematic metric; lacks comprehensive sensitivity/ablation analyses.
- Clarity and reproducibility suffer from extensive dependence on supplemental derivations.

Overall assessment
Interesting idea with potential, but the current submission lacks the methodological calibration, empirical rigor, and clarity expected for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the suggestion to strengthen parameter calibration, provide thorough sensitivity/coverage analyses, and sharpen the theoretical and empirical guarantees for the symbolic/back-substitution bounds.
