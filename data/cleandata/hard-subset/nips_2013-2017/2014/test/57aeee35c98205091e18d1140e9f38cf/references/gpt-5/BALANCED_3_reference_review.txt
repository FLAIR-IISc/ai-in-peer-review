Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y from observational data with possibly unmeasured confounding. It proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), which inverts Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes’ Rule 1 for covariate selection: instead of using a “witness” W and admissible set Z to justify back-door adjustment under faithfulness, WPP treats W|Z as a near-instrument and derives bounds on ACE that allow bounded violations of the faithfulness implications. These violations are encoded by interpretable relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that bound direct effects W→Y, dependence between W and latent U, and discrepancies between observable and latent conditional probabilities. The bounds are obtained by modifying LP-based approaches for the binary IV model (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai), replacing exact IV constraints with relaxed ones. To improve scalability/insight, the paper derives algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a fast “back-substitution” procedure that tightens box constraints iteratively, trading tightness for speed. To handle sampling uncertainty, a Bayesian approach places constrained Dirichlet priors on P(Y,X,W|Z=z) with rejection sampling, yielding posterior distributions over bounds. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu vaccine dataset illustrate the method; code is provided.

The work is positioned at the intersection of causal discovery under faithfulness (PC/Rule 1) and partial identification via IV bounds (Balke–Pearl, Dawid, Ramsahai), with additional connections to Manski’s partial identification and related inequality-based approaches (Evans; Geiger et al.). Compared to standard IV, WPP leverages conditional independences to tighten bounds; compared to faithfulness-based point identification, it hedges against near-unfaithfulness by parameterized relaxations.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A compelling bridge between causal discovery (witness/admissible sets) and partial identification (IV bounds), providing a continuum between point identification and robust bounds.
- Methodological contribution: A general LP framework for relaxed near-IV constraints, plus practical algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that are insightful and computationally efficient.
- Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty with constrained Dirichlet priors is principled and avoids ad hoc plug-in testing of inequalities.
- Sensible use case: observational studies where back-door sets are uncertain and pure IVs rare; the approach lets users encode limited tolerance to violations and see how bounds react.
- Code availability and a real-data example enhance reproducibility and relevance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Assumptions and guarantees: The paper asserts that for any relaxation parameters the bounds cover the faithfulness-based point estimate and are “at least as conservative,” but the main text lacks formal statements/proofs of soundness, monotonicity (bounds tighten as w,y,x→0 and β,β̄→1), and continuity. Please add a theorem summarizing these properties with proof sketches (or bring key results from the supplement into the main text).
- Choice and interpretation of relaxation parameters: The practical meaning of w, y, x, β, β̄ needs clearer guidance and elicitation strategies. Provide concrete calibrations (e.g., mapping to correlation or risk differences), sensitivity curves of bounds versus each parameter, and a decision recipe (e.g., domain-informed priors or worst-case analyses across plausible ranges).
- Statistical validity under search: You select (W,Z) using the same data used to compute bounds, with a simple BDeu-based model selection and a 95% rejection threshold. This risks selection bias and mis-calibration. Consider sample splitting/cross-fitting (discover (W,Z) on fold A, bound on fold B), or hierarchical Bayes that integrates over (W,Z). Quantify the frequency coverage of the bounds after selection in simulations.
- Baselines: The empirical comparison lacks other partial-identification/sensitivity methods (e.g., Rosenbaum sensitivity, Geiger–Janzing–Schölkopf bounding confounding, VanderWeele–Shpitser confounder selection; LATE/monotonicity if applicable). Add these or justify why they are inapplicable here.
- Scalability and computation: Rejection sampling from constrained Dirichlet may be inefficient as constraints tighten or |Z| grows. Consider reporting acceptance rates, runtimes, and using polytope-aware samplers (e.g., hit-and-run) or specialized truncated-Dirichlet MCMC. Provide a complexity and runtime comparison across LP vs. back-substitution on synthetic benchmarks.

Clarity
- The notation (ζ, κ, ω, η) and constraint pipeline are dense. A small running binary example, with numbers, showing how parameters map to inequalities and how bounds are computed would help. Summarize key constraints in the main text (not only in the supplement), and explicitly define all symbols at first use.
- More intuition for each relaxation parameter (which causal paths it binds, operational meaning) would aid adoption.
- Describe the search for Z beyond brute force, and discuss integration with PC/FCI/Y-structures, and the impact of conditioning on collider paths.

Originality
- The idea to invert Rule 1 and use bounded violations to yield near-IV bounds appears novel and well-motivated. The algebraic/back-substitution technique is also a useful engineering contribution.

Significance
- If strengthened, the method could become a practical tool for analysts dealing with uncertain back-door sets. Current limitations: binary-only variables, single real dataset, limited baseline comparison, and guidance for parameter selection. Discuss extension to multi-valued and continuous variables (e.g., via discretization or other relaxations), and to non-binary ACE-like functionals.

Empirical evaluation
- Synthetic design is informative but somewhat tailored (e.g., ensuring NE1 is biased). Add scenarios where back-door adjustment truly suffices and where IV is valid, to show WPP gracefully recovers tight bounds. Report (i) average width, (ii) coverage of the true ACE, (iii) rejection/acceptance rates, and (iv) runtime. On the real data, provide sensitivity plots over parameter ranges and compare to standard IV bounds and other sensitivity methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely conceptual bridge between faithfulness-based discovery and IV-based partial identification.
- Solid methodological development with both exact (LP) and fast approximate (algebraic/back-substitution) implementations.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty; code available; promising empirical indications.

Cons for acceptance
- Important details deferred to the supplement; clarity issues in the main exposition.
- Limited guidance on tuning/eliciting relaxation parameters; limited baseline comparisons and real-data breadth.
- Potential selection bias from using the same data to select (W,Z) and compute bounds; no coverage guarantees under selection.
- Scalability and MCMC efficiency not fully characterized.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept). The paper introduces a useful and original framework with clear potential impact for causal inference in observational ML settings. Strengthening clarity, adding principled guidance for relaxation parameters, broadening empirical comparisons, and addressing post-selection validity would make it a strong contribution. Even if not all additions fit in the page limit, moving core guarantees to the main text and expanding the evaluation would significantly improve the paper.
