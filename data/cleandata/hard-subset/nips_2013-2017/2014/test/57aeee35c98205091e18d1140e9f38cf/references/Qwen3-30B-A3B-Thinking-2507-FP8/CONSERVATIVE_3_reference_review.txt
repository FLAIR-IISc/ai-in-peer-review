

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new method, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The authors build on Entner et al.'s work on witness variables and instrumental variables, introducing a framework that relaxes faithfulness assumptions through parameterized constraints. The approach uses a linear programming formulation with symbolic bounds and a "back-substitution" algorithm for computational efficiency.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear and well-structured background on causal inference, properly contextualizing the problem within established literature. The mathematical formulation is technically sound, and the authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of faithfulness assumptions. The Bayesian approach to handling model uncertainty is appropriately applied to the constrained problem. The paper also demonstrates reasonable care in discussing the limitations of their method.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising foundation, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that undermine its contribution.

First, the experimental validation is insufficient. The authors present only synthetic data experiments with a limited number of cases (100 datasets across four scenarios) and a single real-world example (influenza vaccination). Crucially, they fail to demonstrate that their method provides substantially better bounds than existing approaches. For instance, in the influenza example, they obtain an interval of [-0.10, 0.17], but don't show how this compares to standard IV bounds or other established methods. The paper claims to provide "novel symbolic bounds," but the comparative advantage over existing bounds (e.g., those from Ramsahai [16]) is not convincingly demonstrated.

Second, the paper overstates its novelty. The core methodology is a modification of existing linear programming approaches for causal bounds, with the main innovation being the parameterized relaxation of faithfulness assumptions. The paper cites relevant prior work but doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from or improves upon the standard IV bounding approaches that already handle similar violations. The "Witness Protection Program" name appears to be more marketing than substantive.

Third, the computational claims lack proper justification. The paper claims their "back-substitution algorithm" is "an order of magnitude more efficient" than numerical linear programming, but provides no computational complexity analysis or runtime comparisons. The "symbolic bounds" are described as providing "insight" but don't clearly show how they improve practical performance.

Fourth, the paper fails to adequately validate the claimed advantages in real-world scenarios. The influenza example is particularly problematic: they discard the obvious instrumental variable (GRP) and instead propose a witness (DM) with an admissible set (AGE and SEX), but don't demonstrate why this is superior to the standard IV approach that would use GRP. The paper admits this alternative solution "does not mean the vaccine is more likely to be bad (positive ACE) than good," which undermines the practical value of their approach.

**Significance assessment:** While the problem of handling faithfulness violations is important, the paper doesn't demonstrate that WPP provides a meaningful advance over existing methods. The proposed solution appears to be a minor extension rather than a significant contribution to the field. The real-world example doesn't convincingly show the method's superiority, and the synthetic experiments don't provide sufficient evidence of the method's advantages.

**Overall assessment:** The paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to causal inference. While the methodology is well-developed and the background is well-explained, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and practical advantage. The experimental validation is inadequate, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that WPP provides substantially better bounds than existing approaches. The authors have taken a reasonable approach to handling model uncertainty but haven't provided sufficient evidence that their specific modification is worth adopting over established methods.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be stronger with more comprehensive experimental validation against established baselines, clearer demonstration of the method's advantages, and a more modest claim about its novelty. As it stands, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its proposed method as a meaningful advance over existing causal inference techniques.
