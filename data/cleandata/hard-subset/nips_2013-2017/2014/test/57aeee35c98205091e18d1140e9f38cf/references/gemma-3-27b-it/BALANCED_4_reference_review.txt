This paper presents a new methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using a “Witness Protection Program” (WPP). The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for back-door adjustment and instead use these sets to derive instrumental variable (IV) bounds, even in the presence of faithfulness violations. The authors propose a linear programming formulation and relaxations to make the computation tractable, along with a fast algorithm based on message passing. They demonstrate the method on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination dataset.

The paper addresses a crucial problem in causal inference: estimating treatment effects from observational data. The motivation is strong, as obtaining valid bounds on ACEs is essential in many applications. The WPP framework is a novel and potentially valuable contribution. Reinterpreting Entner’s rule to focus on IV bounds is a clever idea. The authors effectively connect the work to existing literature on IVs, faithfulness, and causal discovery, correctly positioning their contribution within the broader field. The inclusion of analytical approximations and a fast algorithm are practical considerations that increase the potential impact of the work. The experimentation, including a real-world application, adds to the credibility of the proposed method. The detailed discussion of the connection to existing methods, such as those by Dawid and Ramsahai, demonstrates a thorough understanding of the literature.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the presentation is dense and difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the introduction of numerous concepts (witnesses, admissible sets, relaxations, linear programming) in quick succession makes it challenging for the reader to grasp the overall approach. While the authors acknowledge the complexity and provide a short summary of background concepts, it is not sufficient for readers unfamiliar with the nuances of causal inference. Second, the computational complexity, despite the relaxations, remains a concern. The authors mention that the linear programming approach is expensive and that the fast algorithm involves trade-offs, but a more detailed discussion of the scalability of the method would be helpful. The practical limitations of applying the algorithm to high-dimensional datasets with many covariates are not fully explored. Third, the experiments, while present, are not entirely convincing. The synthetic studies lack sufficient detail regarding the specific data generating processes and the choice of parameters. It's also difficult to assess the improvement over existing methods without a more thorough comparison, especially in scenarios where faithfulness is violated. Finally, the connection to the influenza example, while interesting, is not fully developed. The authors claim that WPP could not validate GRP as a witness but found DM and AGE as a better combination, but a more detailed explanation of why this result is meaningful in the context of the vaccination study is needed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bounding ACEs, offering a compromise between faithfulness-based estimates and IV bounds.
*   Solid theoretical foundation, building on established work in causal inference.
*   Addresses a practically important problem with real-world relevance.
*   Includes analytical approximations and a fast algorithm to improve computational feasibility.
*   Demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and difficult-to-follow presentation.
*   Potential computational scalability issues.
*   Limited detail in the experimental setup and comparison with existing methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of the practical implications of the influenza example.
*   The multiple relaxations introduced introduce a level of abstraction that obscures clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to causal inference, but its impact is currently limited by its complexity and lack of thorough evaluation. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the presentation and experimental validation need to be significantly improved. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity:** Rewrite the paper with a focus on clarity and accessibility. Provide more intuitive explanations of the core concepts and reduce the reliance on dense mathematical notation. Consider adding visual aids (e.g., diagrams) to illustrate the methodology.
*   **Scalability:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the method, and discuss strategies for improving scalability. Consider providing runtime benchmarks on datasets of different sizes.
*   **Experiments:** Expand the experimental evaluation with more comprehensive comparisons to existing methods, including scenarios with varying degrees of faithfulness violation. Provide detailed information about the data generating processes used in the synthetic studies.
*   **Real-world Application:** Elaborate on the influenza example and explain the practical implications of the WPP results. Discuss the limitations of the analysis and potential areas for future research.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the relaxation parameters and their impact on the bounds. Investigate methods for automatically selecting optimal parameter values.
*   **Simplify notation:** Unify the notation across the paper, ensuring consistency and minimizing confusion.
