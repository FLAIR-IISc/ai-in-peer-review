## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the challenging field of causal inference, specifically addressing the problem of bounding average causal effects (ACE) in observational studies. The authors propose a thoughtful modification of existing methods, cleverly framing the problem through a “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) analogy. While the paper is dense and the notation can be challenging, the core ideas are compelling and the experimental results, though limited in scope, are promising. I believe this work has the potential to be highly influential, though significant improvements in clarity and presentation are needed. I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the fundamental problem of identifying causal effects from observational data. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional approaches like back-door adjustment, which rely on complete knowledge of confounders. The authors build upon the work of Entner et al. [6] on identifying admissible sets using conditional independence constraints, but crucially reinterpret their approach to generate instrumental variable bounds rather than point estimates. This reinterpretation, combined with a linear programming framework (drawing from Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16]), allows the authors to establish bounds on the ACE even in the presence of unmeasured confounders and potential violations of faithfulness.  The introduction of relaxations to the linear program and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm addresses the computational challenges inherent in the full approach.

The work is well-situated within the broader context of causal inference. It acknowledges the importance of instrumental variables (IV) and the standard IV bounds [1], while aiming to provide a more robust method less sensitive to faithfulness assumptions. The paper correctly highlights the limitations of relying solely on faithfulness [13, 20] and seeks to provide a more practical approach for real-world data analysis.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The "Witness Protection Program" framing is clever and provides a fresh perspective on utilizing conditional independence constraints for causal inference. Reinterpreting Entner et al.'s work to focus on bounding rather than point estimation is a significant insight.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The combination of linear programming and relaxations provides a rigorous mathematical framework for deriving bounds on the ACE. The introduction of parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) to quantify deviations from faithfulness is a valuable contribution.
* **Addressing Faithfulness Concerns:** The method explicitly addresses the limitations of the faithfulness assumption, offering a more robust approach in scenarios where faithfulness might not hold.
* **Computational Considerations:** The authors recognize the computational challenges and propose both analytical approximations and a fast algorithm to improve scalability, demonstrating awareness of practical implementation concerns.
* **Clear Connection to IV Literature:** The work builds directly on the established IV literature, making it accessible to researchers in that area.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is *very* dense and challenging to follow. The notation is heavy, and the explanations can be terse. The flow of ideas could be significantly improved with more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples.  Section 3, in particular, feels rushed and lacks sufficient motivation for each step.
* **Experimental Validation:** While the synthetic experiments demonstrate the method's performance in controlled settings, the real-world experiment with the influenza data is limited.  It would be beneficial to see results on a more diverse range of datasets and with a more thorough comparison to existing causal inference methods.
* **Parameter Selection:** The choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is crucial, but the paper provides limited guidance on how to select these values in practice. The discussion of this in Section 3.1 and 4 feels incomplete.  Further exploration of default parameter settings or a principled approach to parameter selection is needed.
* **Computational Complexity:** Despite the proposed relaxations, the computational complexity of the full linear programming approach remains a concern, especially for high-dimensional datasets. A more detailed analysis of the computational scaling and comparison to other methods in terms of runtime would be valuable.
* **Lack of Discussion on Identifiability:** While the paper focuses on bounding the ACE, it doesn't adequately address the issue of identifiability. Under what conditions is the bound tight? What information is needed to achieve tighter bounds?




**4. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  The core idea is genuinely novel and has the potential to significantly advance the field of causal inference.
*  The theoretical framework is rigorous and well-motivated.
*  The authors demonstrate an awareness of the practical challenges and propose reasonable solutions.
*  The experimental results, while limited, are promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The paper is difficult to understand and requires significant effort from the reader.
*  The experimental validation is insufficient.
*  The guidance on parameter selection is lacking.
*  The computational complexity remains a concern.



**5. Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Notation:**  Simplify the notation where possible.  Provide a more comprehensive glossary of terms.
* **Section 3:** Expand the explanation of the methodology.  Break down the steps into smaller, more digestible chunks. Provide concrete examples to illustrate the concepts.
* **Section 4:** Provide a more detailed explanation of the back-substitution algorithm and its computational advantages.
* **Section 5:**  Expand the experimental validation to include a wider range of datasets and comparison to other causal inference methods.
* **Parameter Selection:**  Provide more guidance on how to select the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). Consider a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of different parameter settings.
* **Identifiability:** Discuss the conditions under which the bounds are tight and what information is needed to achieve tighter bounds.

**6. Related Work Check:**

The paper adequately references related work in causal inference, instrumental variables, and graphical models.  The citations to Pearl [14], Spirtes et al. [22], Entner et al. [6], Dawid [5], and Ramsahai [16] are particularly relevant and appropriate.


**Conclusion:**

Despite its current shortcomings, I believe that "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program" represents a valuable contribution to the field.  The authors have developed a novel and theoretically sound approach to bounding causal effects in observational studies. With significant improvements in clarity, presentation, and experimental validation, this paper has the potential to become a landmark contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
