

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) framework that generalizes instrumental variable (IV) approaches. The authors claim to provide a compromise between faithfulness-based point estimators and IV-based bounds.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper builds on Entner et al. [6], who proposed a search procedure to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment using conditional independence constraints. The authors reinterpret this procedure to generate candidate instrumental variables, then modify the linear programming approach of Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16] to generate bounds on the ACE. They introduce relaxation parameters to handle violations of faithfulness assumptions.

The core contribution is the WPP framework, which aims to provide more robust bounds against faithfulness violations than standard IV approaches. However, the paper's relationship to prior work on IV bounds (e.g., [1, 16]) is not clearly articulated, and the novelty is somewhat obscured by the paper's presentation.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine problem in causal inference: faithfulness assumptions can lead to misleading point estimates when violated. The authors correctly note that "distributions which lie close to (but not on) a simpler model may in practice be indistinguishable from distributions within that simpler model at finite sample sizes." The approach of bounding the ACE rather than providing point estimates is a valuable contribution to causal inference practice.

The Bayesian approach to handling uncertainty in the bounds is well-motivated and represents a thoughtful extension to existing IV bounding methods.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Presentation
- The paper references figures (e.g., "Figure 1") and tables (e.g., "Table 1") that are not included in the manuscript. This is a major issue for a paper that relies on graphical representations.
- There are inconsistent mathematical notations throughout (e.g., η?xw vs ηxw, ζ?yx.w vs ζyx.w).
- The paper contains typographical errors: "Naı̈ve Estimator" should be "Naive Estimator" (with a standard 'i' not 'ı̈').
- The paper claims to provide "symbolic bounds" but doesn't show them in the main text, only referring to the Supplemental Material.

### Methodological Issues
- The paper states "Rule 1: If there exists a variable W ∈ W and a set Z ⊆ W\{W} such that: (i) W \⊥ Y |Z (ii) W ⊥ Y |Z ∪ {X}." This contains a critical typo: it should be "W ⊥ Y |Z" for (i), not "W \⊥ Y |Z" (which would mean "W is not independent of Y given Z").
- The paper claims to generalize Entner et al. [6] but doesn't clearly explain how their method differs from the "Rule 1" procedure beyond the "witness" concept.
- The relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) are not properly defined or motivated. The paper states "we will not discuss in detail how to choose the free parameters of the relaxation," which is a significant omission for a method that depends on these choices.
- The paper claims "the resulting constraints are now implicitly non-linear in P(Y = y,X = x |W = w)," but doesn't explain why this is problematic or how they handle it in practice.

### Technical Weaknesses
- The paper states "Setting w = 0, β = β̄ = 1 recovers the standard IV structure," but doesn't define what w represents in this context.
- The Bayesian approach is described but not thoroughly evaluated. The paper claims "the constrained Dirichlet prior does not suffer from massive sensitivity to the choice of hyperparameters," but provides no empirical evidence for this claim.
- The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described as "the outline of the Witness Protection Program algorithm," but the search procedure for admissible sets is left unspecified, making it unclear how the method would be implemented in practice.
- The paper claims to provide a "fast algorithm that sidesteps the full linear programming optimization," but doesn't explain how this algorithm works or provide evidence of its speed advantage.

### Experimental Evaluation
- The synthetic experiments compare against "naive" point estimators (NE1 and NE2), but don't compare against more sophisticated causal inference methods that might be more appropriate for the problem.
- The real-world influenza vaccination example is interesting, but the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate why WPP provides advantages over standard IV bounds in this case.
- The paper states "WPP could not validate GRP as a witness, instead returning as the highest-scoring pair the witness DM (patient had history of diabetes prior to vaccination) with admissible set composed of AGE and SEX." However, it doesn't explain why GRP (the randomization variable) wasn't selected as the witness, which would be the standard IV assumption.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference but suffers from multiple critical flaws. The methodological description contains significant errors and inconsistencies, particularly in the key Rule 1 statement. The paper fails to properly define and motivate its relaxation parameters, which are central to the proposed method. The lack of figures and tables, along with numerous formatting inconsistencies, severely undermines the paper's credibility.

While the general idea of providing bounds against faithfulness violations is valuable, the paper's presentation and technical execution fall short of the standards expected at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address the numerous inconsistencies and clarify the methodological contributions.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper contains fundamental methodological errors, lacks clear presentation of key concepts, and fails to properly define its central contributions. While the problem addressed is important, the paper does not provide a sufficiently rigorous or clear solution to warrant acceptance. The minor flaws identified here are not just "minor" but represent critical issues that prevent the reader from understanding or evaluating the method properly.
