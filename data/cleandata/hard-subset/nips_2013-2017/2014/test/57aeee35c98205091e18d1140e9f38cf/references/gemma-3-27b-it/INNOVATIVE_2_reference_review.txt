This paper tackles the challenging problem of causal inference with observational data, proposing a novel “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE). The core idea – inverting Entner’s Rule 1 to use potential witnesses as instrumental variables and relaxing faithfulness assumptions via linear programming – is genuinely intriguing and represents a potentially significant departure from existing methods. The authors cleverly connect this to existing IV bounding procedures and demonstrate a pathway towards scalability through algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm. While the presentation is dense and the validation limited, the boldness and novelty of the approach are compelling.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of inverting Entner’s Rule and interpreting witnesses as providing instrumental variable-like bounds is highly original. It offers a fresh perspective on dealing with unmeasured confounding.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon a solid foundation in causal inference, referencing relevant work by Pearl, Entner et al., and Dawid/Ramsahai. The attempt to formalize violations of faithfulness within a linear programming framework is commendable.
* **Scalability Considerations:** The exploration of algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm demonstrates awareness of computational challenges and a proactive effort to address them.
* **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the inherent conservatism of the bounds and the potential for the method to return wide intervals. They also openly discuss the sensitivity to parameter choices and the challenges of posterior sampling.
* **Potential for Broad Impact:** Successful development of this line of work could provide a more robust and less assumption-heavy approach to causal inference in observational studies, which is highly valuable across numerous domains.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is incredibly dense and requires a strong background in causal inference to fully grasp. The notation is heavy, and the flow of ideas could be improved. Several sections feel rushed.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while present, are somewhat limited in scope. The synthetic data setups, while designed to test specific scenarios, may not fully capture the complexities of real-world datasets. The influenza data analysis is intriguing but relies on assumptions regarding the IV structure. A more thorough empirical evaluation, ideally on a wider range of datasets, is needed.
* **Computational Complexity:** Despite efforts to address scalability, the computational cost of the linear programming formulation remains a significant concern. The effectiveness of the relaxations and back-substitution algorithm needs to be rigorously demonstrated on larger datasets.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of WPP appears sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The paper lacks a detailed discussion of how to select these parameters in practice.
* **BDeu Score Justification:** The use of the BDeu score to select the "best" (W,Z) pair feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a strong theoretical justification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to causal inference that has the potential to overcome limitations of existing methods.
* The theoretical framework is well-developed and grounded in established causal inference principles.
* The authors demonstrate an awareness of the computational challenges and propose practical solutions.
* The potential impact of this work is significant, as it could lead to more robust and reliable causal estimates in observational studies.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The paper is difficult to understand due to its density and heavy notation.
* The empirical validation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in realistic settings.
* The computational complexity remains a concern.
* The sensitivity to parameter choices and the justification for the BDeu score are weaknesses.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a high-risk, high-reward submission. While the presentation and validation could be significantly improved, the novelty and potential significance of the approach outweigh its current shortcomings. The authors are tackling a very difficult problem with a bold and innovative solution. I lean towards **acceptance**, with a strong recommendation for the authors to prioritize clarity, expand the empirical evaluation, and provide more guidance on parameter selection in a revised version. This work has the potential to open up new avenues for research in causal inference, and I would be excited to see it further developed. I would encourage the authors to release their code to facilitate wider adoption and experimentation.
