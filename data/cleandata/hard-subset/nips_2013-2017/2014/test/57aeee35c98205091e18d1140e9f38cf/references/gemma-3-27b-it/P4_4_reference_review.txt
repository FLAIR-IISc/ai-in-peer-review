This paper introduces a method, the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment as a means to find instrumental variables, and then leverage a linear programming (LP) approach, enhanced with relaxations and a fast algorithm, to calculate bounds on the ACE. The method attempts to provide more robust bounds than standard instrumental variable techniques by allowing for violations of faithfulness. The paper includes synthetic experiments and an application to influenza vaccination data.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem in causal inference – estimating treatment effects when randomized trials are infeasible and confounders are present. The authors correctly identify the limitations of relying solely on faithfulness assumptions and seek a method that is more robust to violations of this assumption. The combination of ideas – repurposing Entner’s work, leveraging LP, and introducing relaxations for computational efficiency – is a reasonable approach. The paper clearly outlines the methodology and provides a detailed explanation of the underlying assumptions. The connection to existing work on instrumental variables, causal graphs, and related LP formulations is well-established through citations.  The experiments, while limited, provide some preliminary validation of the proposed method.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses.  The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, even for readers familiar with the field. While the paper states it assumes familiarity with concepts like causal graphs and the do-operator, the explanation of the methodology itself feels quite technical and requires careful attention. The introduction of numerous parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and constraints within the LP framework adds to the complexity. While the authors attempt to provide intuition for these parameters, the link between their values and the resulting bounds isn't always clear. 

The computational complexity is a significant concern. Despite the introduction of relaxations and a fast algorithm, the paper acknowledges that the method can be computationally expensive. The description of the back-substitution algorithm (Section 4) is brief, and it’s unclear how much performance improvement it provides in practice.  The experiments only consider small dimensional problems, which limits the ability to assess the scalability of the method.  

The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. The synthetic studies are well-designed to test the method under different conditions, but the results are presented somewhat sparsely.  The application to the influenza data is interesting, but the interpretation of the results is cautious, and it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the obtained bounds. The comparison with existing methods, especially standard instrumental variable techniques, could be more thorough.  Specifically, how much *wider* are the bounds generated by WPP, and is this increase in width justified by the added robustness to faithfulness violations?

The paper’s novelty lies in the combination of existing techniques and the framing of Entner’s work in a new light. However, the core ideas – using LP for causal inference and relaxing faithfulness assumptions – are not entirely novel. The strength of the paper is in its attempt to integrate these concepts into a cohesive framework. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in causal inference.
*   Proposes a novel combination of existing techniques.
*   Provides a theoretically sound framework for bounding the ACE.
*   Demonstrates preliminary validation through synthetic experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   High technical complexity and dense notation.
*   Computational concerns regarding scalability.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and sparse results.
*   Incremental novelty—builds on established ideas rather than presenting a breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the ideas are sound and the technical development is rigorous, the high complexity, computational concerns, and limited experimental evaluation raise concerns. The paper makes a worthwhile contribution to the field, but it needs further refinement and more extensive evaluation to fully demonstrate its practical utility. The authors should consider simplifying the notation and providing more detailed computational analysis, as well as expanding the experimental evaluation to include larger datasets and a more thorough comparison with existing methods.
