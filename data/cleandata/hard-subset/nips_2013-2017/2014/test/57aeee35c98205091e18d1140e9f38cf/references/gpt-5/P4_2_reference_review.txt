Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y from observational data when ACE is not identifiable. It proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), which leverages sets of observed covariates W that are not effects of X or Y. Instead of using faithfulness purely to find admissible back-door sets (Entner et al., AISTATS 2013), the paper inverts that approach: it treats a variable W that satisfies Entner’s Rule 1 with respect to a set Z as a “witness” used instrumentally, and then bounds ACE by combining (i) instrumental-variable-type bounding (Balke & Pearl, 1997; Dawid, 2003; Ramsahai, 2012) conditional on Z, with (ii) explicit, bounded relaxations of faithfulness.

Formally, WPP encodes bounded violations of conditional independence via parameters w, x, y, β, β̄ that constrain deviations between “structural” and observed conditionals (equations (3)–(6)). Setting these parameters to special values recovers standard IV (w=0, β=β̄=1) and the chain W→X→Y (additionally x=y=0). Given these relaxations, the paper formulates a linear program over latent response-function parameters to produce bounds on ACE, following ideas from Dawid and Ramsahai, but adapted to the relaxed constraints. To improve scalability and interpretability, the authors derive conservative algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a back-substitution “message passing-like” algorithm that yields fast, approximate box constraints for the optimization.

A Bayesian learning scheme places constrained Dirichlet priors over P(Y,X,W|Z=z) and P(Z), using rejection sampling to obtain posterior samples of bounds; a simple falsification step rejects models where constraints are rarely satisfied under the posterior. Experiments include synthetic data with and without valid witnesses and a real influenza vaccine dataset (encouragement design), where WPP yields narrower bounds than standard IV by exploiting conditional independencies among additional covariates.

Relative to prior work: (i) it reinterprets Entner et al.’s discovery rule as a generator of instrument-like witnesses; (ii) it extends linear-program bounding frameworks (Balke-Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) to bounded-faithfulness settings; (iii) it provides algebraic (though conservative) inequalities and a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm; and (iv) it offers a Bayesian framework for uncertainty in constraints. The work also relates to partial identification (Manski) and recent inequality/graphical constraint literature (Evans; Geiger et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge between causal discovery (faithfulness-based admissible sets) and partial identification via IV bounds, enabling controlled relaxation of faithfulness rather than an all-or-nothing assumption.
- Clear parameterization of bounded violations (w, x, y, β, β̄) with interpretable limiting cases (standard IV, chain).
- Integrates linear programming with explicit relaxations and introduces fast algebraic bounds/back-substitution that reduce computation and offer qualitative insight.
- Bayesian treatment acknowledges sampling uncertainty in testing inequalities and model selection for witnesses/admissible sets.
- Empirical results demonstrate robustness relative to naïve back-door estimators and faithfulness-only point estimates, including a real-world application where WPP yields nontrivial, tighter bounds than standard IV.
- Code availability increases reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Guidance for selecting relaxation parameters is deferred to the supplement and not empirically systematized; practical use may hinge on this choice.
- The algebraic bounds and back-substitution are conservative and not proven tight; there is no characterization of when they approximate the LP bounds well.
- The falsification rule (95% rejection threshold) and use of BDeu-based independence testing are heuristic; finite-sample coverage and Type I/II error properties of the overall pipeline are not theoretically analyzed.
- Scalability is limited: exhaustive search over Z is used; dimensionality is assumed modest; complexity of polytope computations is not characterized for larger Z or multi-valued variables.
- Experimental comparisons are limited: no head-to-head with alternative partial-identification approaches (e.g., monotonicity/LATE, sensitivity analysis frameworks, recent inequality-based bounds beyond IV). The evaluation metric zeroes error for wide intervals; while discussed, a more thorough informativeness vs. coverage analysis would help.
- Some clarity issues: heavy notation (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω) without the referenced table in the main text; occasional elisions to the supplement for crucial derivations; minor presentation typos.

Quality
- The methodological construction appears sound, building on established LP-based bounding frameworks and adding well-defined relaxations. However, key proofs and constraint derivations are deferred to the supplement; tightness/optimality are not established.
- The Bayesian approach appropriately accounts for uncertainty but relies on rejection sampling under inequality constraints; efficiency and mixing are not detailed. The falsification step is pragmatic but ad hoc.
- Empirics support the claims qualitatively; more comprehensive baselines and coverage analyses would strengthen the evidence.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized with clear motivation and connections to prior work. Notation is dense; including the parameter table and an illustrative, fully-worked small example would improve readability. Guidance for setting relaxation parameters and interpreting bounds would benefit practitioners.

Originality
- The inversion of Entner’s Rule 1 for generating instrumental witnesses under bounded faithfulness, and the combination with LP bounds plus algebraic back-substitution, is a novel and substantive contribution.

Significance
- Addressing the gap between strict faithfulness-based point identification and conservative IV bounds is important in observational causal inference. If adopted, WPP can provide more informative, honest inferences in many practical studies. Broader impact depends on parameter selection guidance and scalability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide principled strategies for choosing relaxation parameters (e.g., calibration via held-out tests, sensitivity curves, domain-informed priors).
- Theoretically analyze coverage and informativeness as functions of (w, x, y, β, β̄), and characterize when algebraic/back-substitution bounds approximate LP bounds.
- Report computational complexity and scaling behavior; explore heuristic search over Z and comparisons to score-based constraint discovery (e.g., FCI variants).
- Compare against alternative partial identification methods (e.g., LATE/monotonicity, robust bias-aware estimators) and report width/coverage trade-offs.
- Improve clarity by including the parameter table and a small end-to-end example in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated framework that blends causal discovery signals with partial identification.
- Methodologically sound extension of LP-based bounds with interpretable relaxations.
- Practical algorithms (algebraic bounds, back-substitution) and a Bayesian uncertainty treatment.
- Promising empirical results and available code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees (tightness, coverage) and heuristic components (falsification, parameter selection).
- Scalability and generality beyond binary/small-Z settings are unclear.
- Experimental evaluation lacks broader baselines and deeper informativeness–coverage analysis.

Overall, the paper offers a substantial and timely contribution to causal inference from observational data, with room for strengthening theoretical and empirical validation.
