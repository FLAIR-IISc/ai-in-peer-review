

# NIPS Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel approach to bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The authors address a key limitation of existing causal inference methods: their sensitivity to the faithfulness assumption, which can lead to unreliable point estimates when violated. WPP provides bounds on ACE by relaxing faithfulness through bounded violations of local independence constraints, using a "witness" variable identified via Entner et al.'s Rule 1. The method combines linear programming for bounding with a computational shortcut (back-substitution) for efficiency. The paper demonstrates the approach through synthetic experiments and a real-world influenza vaccination study.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Contribution to Causal Inference**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a well-known limitation of causal discovery methods (sensitivity to faithfulness assumptions). The WPP framework provides a principled way to quantify uncertainty due to potential faithfulness violations, which is a significant gap in current practice.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The mathematical formulation is rigorous, with clear definitions of the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and their relationship to faithfulness violations. The back-substitution algorithm for efficiently computing bounds is a particularly valuable technical contribution that makes the method computationally feasible.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate WPP's stability compared to naive estimators (NE1, NE2) and the faithfulness-based approach of Entner et al. The real-world influenza study, while limited in scope, provides a concrete application where WPP identified a different witness (DM with admissible set AGE and SEX) than the expected IV (GRP), yielding tighter bounds.

4. **Honest Assessment of Trade-offs**: The paper acknowledges the fundamental trade-off between interval width and assumptions made (citing Manski's work), which is a refreshing contrast to many causal inference papers that overstate their assumptions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Existing Bounds Methods**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare against other methods for bounding causal effects (e.g., [8], [7], [16]). Without such comparison, it's difficult to assess whether WPP provides meaningful improvements over existing approaches. The claim that WPP is "at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator" is trivial since all bounds should be at least as conservative.

2. **Arbitrary Relaxation Parameters**: The choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is largely arbitrary in the paper. While the authors mention future work on choosing these parameters, the current implementation lacks a principled method for setting them, which undermines the practical utility of the approach. The paper's claim that "any choice of w ≥ 0, y ≥ 0, x ≥ 0, 0 ≤ β ≤ 1 ≤ β̄ is guaranteed to provide bounds that are at least as conservative" suggests the method could be used with any parameters, which weakens the contribution.

3. **Weak Real-World Validation**: The influenza study doesn't convincingly demonstrate WPP's advantage over standard IV methods. The paper notes that WPP "could not validate GRP as a witness" (the expected IV), but doesn't explain why this is a benefit or how it relates to the true causal effect. The resulting interval [-0.10, 0.17] is not substantially tighter than standard IV bounds, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that this interval is more reliable.

4. **Overstated Bayesian Approach**: The Bayesian model checking approach (using rejection sampling with an arbitrary 95% rejection threshold) is ad hoc and lacks theoretical justification. The paper doesn't address how this might interact with the relaxation parameters or how it compares to other model checking approaches.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand the Comparison Section**: Add comparisons against established methods for bounding causal effects (e.g., the methods of [8] and [16]) to demonstrate WPP's relative advantages.

2. **Provide Guidance for Parameter Selection**: Include a more detailed discussion of how to choose the relaxation parameters, perhaps with sensitivity analysis or a principled approach for specific applications.

3. **Strengthen the Real-World Validation**: In the influenza study, better explain why WPP's identified witness (DM) is preferable to the expected IV (GRP), and how the resulting bounds improve upon standard IV methods.

4. **Clarify the Computational Advantage**: The paper claims the back-substitution method is "an order of magnitude more efficient" than the numerical solution, but provides no empirical evidence for this claim in the main text (it's only mentioned in the Supplemental).

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to causal inference that addresses a genuine limitation of existing methods. The WPP framework provides a valuable tool for practitioners who want to quantify uncertainty due to potential faithfulness violations. However, the paper's contribution is somewhat diminished by the lack of comparison to existing bounding methods, the arbitrary nature of the relaxation parameters, and the limited real-world validation.

While the paper makes a meaningful contribution to causal inference, it doesn't quite reach the level of significance expected for NIPS, primarily due to the lack of compelling evidence that WPP provides substantially better results than existing approaches. The method would be much stronger with more thorough comparisons and a principled approach to parameter selection.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses in a revised version, particularly by adding comparisons to other bounding methods and providing better guidance for parameter selection).
