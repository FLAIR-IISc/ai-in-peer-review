Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data when standard identifiability is not guaranteed. The key ideas are:
- Inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using witness/admissible-set pairs (W, Z) to claim point identifiability via faithfulness, WPP treats W as a conditional instrument given Z and produces bounds on ACE.
- Relaxed faithfulness: violations of faithfulness are modeled as bounded deviations in local conditional relationships via parameters w, x, y and β, β̄, interpolating between chain models and standard IVs.
- Bounding via linear programming: extends Dawid and Ramsahai’s LP approach to incorporate these relaxations, introducing auxiliary variables (κ, ω) to handle dependence of U on W.
- Scalable approximations: derives novel algebraic bounds symbolic in the relaxation parameters and a fast back-substitution/message-passing-like procedure that avoids full LP solves.
- Bayesian treatment: uses constrained Dirichlet posteriors with rejection sampling to account for sampling uncertainty and to falsify models when the generated inequalities are incompatible with the data.
Experiments include synthetic studies (with and without valid witnesses) and a reanalysis of a classic influenza encouragement dataset; WPP yields intervals that are narrower than standard IV bounds and robust to approximate independencies.

Relation to prior work
Builds directly on: (i) selection of admissible sets via faithfulness (Entner et al.), (ii) ACE bounds under (binary) IV assumptions (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson–Robins), and (iii) partial identification (Manski). The key novelty is repurposing witness/admissible sets as conditional IV structures with controlled violations of faithfulness, plus new symbolic bounds and an efficient back-substitution algorithm.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Turning witness/admissible-set discovery into a generator of conditional IV bounding problems with tunable relaxations is fresh and impactful. It bridges two influential but often disjoint paradigms—faithfulness-based discovery and IV-based partial identification.
- Methodological contributions: Clear parameterization of bounded deviations from faithfulness; extension of LP-based bounding to handle β, β̄ constraints; new algebraic (symbolic) bounds as explicit functions of relaxation parameters; a practical, fast back-substitution procedure; and a Bayesian framework for uncertainty and model falsification.
- Practical relevance: Offers an honest, conservative alternative to fragile point-identification in observational studies, with code and a real-data case.
- Robustness emphasis: The framework explicitly addresses near-unfaithfulness and finite-sample indistinguishability issues that plague rule-based selection.

Weaknesses
- Guidance on relaxation parameters: The choice of w, x, y, β, β̄ crucially shapes the bounds. The paper postpones principled selection to the supplement/future work. Practitioners will need clearer defaults, interpretations (e.g., mapping to measurable violations), and sensitivity workflows.
- Clarity and notation burden: The paper is dense and sometimes hard to follow. Multiple symbol sets (ζ, κ, ω, η, δ) and lifted polytopes appear with limited intuitive exposition. A small running binary example would help.
- Bayesian procedure scalability: Constrained Dirichlet with rejection sampling will not scale when Z is larger or data are sparse. The falsification rule (>95% rejected) is ad hoc. Discussing alternative samplers (e.g., hit-and-run, HMC with inequality constraints) or guaranteed termination would strengthen the approach.
- Tightness and guarantees: The algebraic bounds are acknowledged to be non-tight; interplay between the fast back-substitution and exact LP bounds is empirical. Formal approximation guarantees or worst-case width inflation bounds would be valuable.
- Empirical scope: Synthetic experiments are thoughtful but tuned; the real-data analysis is a single case study. Comparisons to more recent partial identification/bounding approaches (e.g., proximal causal inference, graphical inequalities) are limited.

Quality
Technically sound at a conceptual and construction level; the LP formulation and relaxations are carefully motivated. Proofs are deferred, and some steps are conservative, but the claims are appropriately scoped. The Bayesian treatment is reasonable but could use more rigorous discussion of computational diagnostics and prior sensitivity.

Clarity
Readable but compact. Organization is reasonable; however, readers would benefit from:
- A one-page worked toy example tracing Rule 1 inversion → relaxations → constraints → bounds.
- A table summarizing all symbols and their meanings.
- A short “practical guide” for choosing relaxation parameters and interpreting them.

Originality
High. The reinterpretation of witnesses as conditional instruments with bounded faithfulness violations, plus symbolic bounds and back-substitution, are distinctive contributions likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Significance
High potential. The community needs robust, intermediate options between fragile point-ID and overly loose IV bounds. If parameter selection is systematized and scalability improved, WPP could become a standard tool.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, original reframing with clear potential impact on observational causal inference.
- New algebraic bounds and an efficient algorithmic alternative to LP.
- Honest handling of near-unfaithfulness and uncertainty; code available.

Cons against acceptance
- Incomplete guidance on crucial relaxation parameters.
- Non-tightness and limited theoretical guarantees for approximations.
- Scalability and ad hoc choices in the Bayesian component; limited empirical breadth.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide principled defaults or elicitation strategies for w, x, y, β, β̄ (e.g., mapping to effect-size or correlation bounds)?
- Any theoretical guarantees on the gap between back-substitution and exact LP bounds?
- Could you discuss alternative constrained sampling methods to reduce rejection rates?
- How sensitive are conclusions in the flu study to parameter choices and to the choice of witness/admissible set?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a novel and promising direction that bridges important strands in causal inference. Despite some incompleteness in parameter guidance and scalability, the conceptual contribution and new tools merit presentation at NeurIPS.

Confidence
Moderate. I am familiar with causal discovery and partial identification, though not with all recent advances in inequality-based constraints.
