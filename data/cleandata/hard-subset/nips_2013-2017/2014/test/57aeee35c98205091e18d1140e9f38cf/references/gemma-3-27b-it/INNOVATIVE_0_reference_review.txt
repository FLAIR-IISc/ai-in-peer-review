This paper tackles a challenging problem – causal inference with observational data – and proposes a novel framework, the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), to bound the average causal effect (ACE). The core idea of inverting Entner’s Rule 1 to focus on instrumental variable bounding, combined with a relaxation of faithfulness assumptions through bounded violations of local independence, is a genuinely interesting and potentially impactful contribution. It moves beyond simply seeking *a* back-door path and acknowledges the inherent uncertainty in real-world observational studies. The attempt to provide analytical bounds and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm to scale the approach is also commendable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The WPP framework is a refreshing departure from standard instrumental variable and faithfulness-based approaches. The re-interpretation of Entner's work and the introduction of bounded faithfulness violations represent a significant conceptual leap.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of linear programming and the attempt to derive analytical bounds (even if relaxed) demonstrate a strong theoretical grounding. The connection to existing work (Dawid, Ramsahai, Entner et al.) is clearly established.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The authors recognize the computational complexity and proactively address it with relaxations and a faster algorithm. This is crucial for the method’s usability.
*   **Honest Assessment:** The paper acknowledges the limitations of the approach and the trade-offs involved, particularly regarding the width of the resulting bounds.
*   **Well-Defined Problem:** The motivation for addressing the shortcomings of existing methods, especially in scenarios with potential faithfulness violations, is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity (despite relaxations):** While relaxations are introduced, the description of the full linear programming approach still feels computationally intensive. The scalability of the back-substitution algorithm needs further justification.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The guidance on choosing these parameters is somewhat limited, and the experiments provide only a single configuration.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The synthetic experiments, while helpful, are relatively simple. The real-world application to the influenza data is a good starting point but could benefit from more diverse datasets and comparisons against other state-of-the-art causal inference methods. The explanation of *why* DM and AGE were chosen as witness/admissible set, and not GRP is limited.
*   **Clarity in Notation:** While the paper is generally well-written, the mathematical notation can be dense and challenging to follow at times. More intuitive explanations of key concepts would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper touches upon the limitations of the faithfulness assumption but doesn’t fully explore the implications of relaxing it. A deeper discussion of the conditions under which the bounded violations are most appropriate would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a long-standing problem in causal inference.
*   The theoretical framework is well-developed and offers a potentially powerful way to handle uncertainty in observational data.
*   The practical considerations and attempts to address computational complexity are commendable.
*   Even with its limitations, the WPP framework represents a significant step forward and could inspire further research in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity might limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
*   The parameter sensitivity and lack of comprehensive experimental validation raise concerns about its robustness.
*   The notation and some of the theoretical explanations could be clearer.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a high degree of novelty. While the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity are valid concerns, the authors have made a commendable effort to address them. The framework's potential to advance the field of causal inference, particularly in scenarios where faithfulness assumptions are questionable, is significant. I believe the strengths of this paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the full linear programming approach and the scalability of the back-substitution algorithm.
2.  Offer more guidance on choosing the relaxation parameters and explore the sensitivity of the results to different parameter configurations.
3.  Expand the experimental validation with more diverse datasets and comparisons against other causal inference methods.
4.  Clarify the notation and provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
5.  Elaborate on the assumptions underlying the bounded violations of faithfulness and discuss their implications.
