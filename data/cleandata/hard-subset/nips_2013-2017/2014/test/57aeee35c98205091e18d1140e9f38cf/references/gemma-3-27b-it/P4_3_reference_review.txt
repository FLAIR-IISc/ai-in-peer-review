This paper introduces the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), a methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) using observational data with covariates. The core idea is to reinterpret results from Entner et al. [6] and Dawid [5] through the lens of instrumental variables, relaxing faithfulness assumptions to provide more robust bounds. The method leverages a linear programming formulation, with further relaxations and a fast algorithm to improve computational efficiency. The authors also propose a Bayesian learning approach and analytical approximations. The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of causal inference in observational settings where identifying back-door adjustment sets is difficult.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel approach to bounding causal effects by inverting the traditional use of Entner’s Rule 1 and incorporating a relaxation of faithfulness. The combination of linear programming, relaxations, and a fast algorithm appears to be a substantial contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology builds on established causal inference concepts (do-calculus, instrumental variables) and provides a clear mathematical formulation. The extension of Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16] to accommodate violations of faithfulness is well-explained.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions of key concepts and a logical flow of ideas. The authors provide sufficient background information for readers familiar with causal inference. The introduction of the “Witness Protection Program” metaphor is helpful.
*   **Completeness:** The paper addresses both theoretical and computational aspects of the proposed method. The inclusion of analytical approximations and a fast algorithm suggests a practical focus. Experiments with both synthetic and real data provide empirical validation.
*   **Addressing Weaknesses:** The authors acknowledge the challenges associated with faithfulness assumptions and explicitly address them through relaxations. They also discuss potential limitations and future work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The method is complex, involving a combination of linear programming, relaxations, Bayesian inference, and back-substitution. This complexity could make it difficult for practitioners to implement and interpret.
*   **Computational Cost:** Despite the introduction of a fast algorithm, the computational cost may still be significant for high-dimensional datasets, especially for the full Bayesian approach.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method may be sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While the authors acknowledge this and provide some guidance, a more systematic investigation of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Interpretation of Bounds:** The bounds produced by the method may be wide, making them less informative in some cases. The paper discusses this trade-off, but it is important for users to understand the implications.
*   **Limited Real-Data Analysis:** The real-data analysis is limited to a single example (influenza vaccination) with a relatively small dataset. More extensive evaluation on diverse real-world datasets would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant advancement in causal inference methodology, offering a novel approach to bounding causal effects in the presence of unmeasured confounding.
*   The technical contributions are substantial, building on established concepts and providing a rigorous mathematical framework.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility and potential effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the method may limit its practical applicability.
*   The computational cost could be prohibitive for large datasets.
*   The sensitivity to relaxation parameters and the potential for wide bounds require careful consideration.
*   The real-data analysis is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of causal inference. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated. While the complexity and computational cost are potential concerns, the authors have made efforts to address these challenges. The experimental results are promising, and the paper provides a valuable addition to the toolkit for causal inference in observational settings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and computational scalability, and perhaps expand the real-data analysis.
