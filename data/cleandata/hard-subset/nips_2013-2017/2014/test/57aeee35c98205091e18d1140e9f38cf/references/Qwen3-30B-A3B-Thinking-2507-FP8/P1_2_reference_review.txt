

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on Y using observational data. The authors build on Entner et al.'s work on "witnesses" for causal discovery but reframe their use to generate causal bounds rather than point estimates. The key insight is to relax the faithfulness assumption through carefully parameterized deviations from ideal causal structures, providing more robust bounds that are less sensitive to faithfulness violations than standard instrumental variable approaches.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a reinterpretation of Entner's Rule 1 for bounding rather than point estimation, (2) a linear programming framework with relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that control the degree of faithfulness violation, (3) a Bayesian learning approach for handling uncertainty in the bounds, and (4) an efficient "back-substitution algorithm" that avoids full linear programming optimization. The method is validated on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study.

This work relates to several important strands of causal inference research, extending the instrumental variable literature (Balke & Pearl, 1997; Manski, 1990) while connecting to causal discovery approaches (Spirtes et al., 2000; Entner et al., 2013). It provides a practical middle ground between faithfulness-based point estimators and purely theory-driven bounds.

## Strengths

**1. Novel Technical Contribution:** The paper's core contribution—systematically relaxing faithfulness assumptions through interpretable parameters—is both conceptually novel and practically valuable. The authors provide a clear mathematical framework for how faithfulness violations affect causal bounds, which is a significant gap in current causal inference literature.

**2. Practical Algorithm Development:** The WPP algorithm is well-motivated and well-executed, with the back-substitution algorithm providing a substantial computational improvement over standard linear programming approaches. The implementation details (e.g., using Polymake) make the method accessible to practitioners.

**3. Strong Empirical Validation:** The synthetic experiments demonstrate WPP's robustness across different problem setups, outperforming naive point estimators (NE1, NE2) and the faithfulness-based approach when faithfulness is violated. The real-world influenza vaccination study provides a compelling application that demonstrates the method's practical utility.

**4. Comprehensive Theoretical Treatment:** The derivation of algebraic bounds (Table 2) provides valuable theoretical insights into how relaxation parameters affect the resulting bounds, which is particularly useful for understanding the method's behavior. The paper carefully discusses the trade-offs between bound width and robustness.

**5. Clear Positioning in Literature:** The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contribution relative to prior work, acknowledging both the limitations of faithfulness-based approaches and the conservativeness of standard IV bounds.

## Weaknesses

**1. Parameter Selection Guidance:** The paper lacks concrete guidance on selecting the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While the authors mention they'll address this in future work, this is a critical gap for practical application. The experiments use default values (w = x = y = 0.2, β = 0.9, β̄ = 1.1) without justification.

**2. Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** While the paper provides algebraic bounds, it doesn't prove they're optimal or quantify how much wider they are than theoretically possible bounds. The authors acknowledge the bounds are conservative but don't provide tightness guarantees.

**3. Computational Efficiency Claims:** The paper claims the back-substitution algorithm is "an order of magnitude more efficient" but doesn't provide comparative runtime data with the full linear programming approach or alternative methods.

**4. Real-World Application Explanation:** The influenza study results are intriguing but lack clear explanation for why DM (diabetes history) was selected as the witness rather than GRP (reminder letter), which was the intended IV. This undermines the practical demonstration of the method.

**5. Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The synthetic experiments compare only to NE1, NE2, and the faithfulness-based approach. A comparison with other causal bound methods (e.g., Geiger et al., 2014) would strengthen the empirical claims.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant technical contribution to causal inference by providing a method that bridges the gap between faithfulness-based point estimators and purely theory-driven bounds. The WPP framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and provides a valuable tool for observational studies where faithfulness assumptions may be questionable.

The paper's strengths in conceptual novelty, practical implementation, and empirical validation outweigh its weaknesses. The identified limitations (particularly regarding parameter selection) are important but not deal-breakers for a NIPS submission. The authors have clearly articulated the trade-offs between robustness and bound width, which is crucial for causal inference practitioners.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference that is well-executed and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The weaknesses identified are important but can be addressed in a revision.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Provide more guidance on choosing relaxation parameters, perhaps with a sensitivity analysis showing how different parameter choices affect bound width in the influenza study.

2. Add a brief comparison of runtimes between the full linear programming approach and the back-substitution algorithm.

3. Clarify the reasoning behind the choice of DM as the witness in the influenza study, and explain why GRP (the intended IV) wasn't selected.

4. Include a brief discussion of how the method scales with the dimensionality of the covariates, as this is crucial for real-world applications.

5. Consider adding a brief comparison with Geiger et al.'s (2014) causal bound method to strengthen the empirical validation.

This paper represents an important step toward more robust causal inference in observational studies, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
