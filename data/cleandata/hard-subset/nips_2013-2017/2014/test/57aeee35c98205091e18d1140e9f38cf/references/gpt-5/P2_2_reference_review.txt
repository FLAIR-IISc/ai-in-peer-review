Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), a methodology to partially identify the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y using observational data and a set of pre-treatment covariates W. It inverts the use of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (faithfulness-based discovery of admissible sets) by treating any discovered (W, Z) pair not as a license for back-door adjustment but as evidence to construct a “near-IV” after conditioning on Z. The key idea is to relax the structural implications of Rule 1 by bounding violations of the relevant conditional independences with parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄). These relaxations induce linear (and some implicitly nonlinear) inequality constraints on latent-response parameters, enabling linear-programming-based bounds on ACE, extending the classic Balke-Pearl IV bounds and related LP formulations by Dawid and Ramsahai. The paper further provides: (i) a Bayesian procedure that places constrained Dirichlet priors on observable contingency tables P(Y, X, W | Z=z) and samples posteriors of bound endpoints via rejection sampling; (ii) algebraic, symbolic (non-tight) inequalities that enable a fast “back-substitution” algorithm yielding conservative outer bounds without running the full LP; and (iii) experiments on synthetic data and an influenza encouragement dataset, showing WPP can produce narrower bounds than standard IV under modest relaxation settings.

This sits at the intersection of causal discovery (PC, faithfulness), partial identification (Manski; Balke-Pearl; Ramsahai), and IV/noncompliance inference. Relative to prior work, WPP’s novelty is in using discovered “witness/admissible set” pairs to define a family of relaxed-IV models with tunable parameters, then bounding ACE within that family, rather than committing to exact faithfulness (point-ID) or to the very weak (often wide) IV bounds.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The LP construction builds correctly on known binary-IV machinery: latent-response parameterization, extremal point enumeration, and dual polytope inequalities. The relaxation parameters (w, x, y controlling deviations in η and δ, and β, β̄ bounding selection on unobservables) are clearly interpretable as bounded departures from the conditional independences implied by Rule 1. Treating κ and ω as “weighted” versions under β-interval selection and linking to observables via inequalities is reasonable. The symbolic bounds and back-substitution are explicitly acknowledged as not tight, but are argued to be conservative. However, two aspects need strengthening:
  - Soundness guarantees: It would help to state a formal theorem that, under assumptions (3)-(6), the LP bounds are valid outer bounds on ACE; and that the back-substitution procedure returns an outer relaxation of those LP bounds (so no false precision).
  - Model falsification and uncertainty: The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach is sensible to propagate sampling uncertainty, but the rejection-sampling-based “95% rejection ⇒ falsify” criterion is ad hoc. Acceptance rates, diagnostics, and sensitivity of posterior bounds to priors would improve confidence.
- Experiments: The synthetic studies are carefully designed to make back-door on all W invalid and to vary whether true witnesses exist. Reporting error distributions is useful, but additional metrics are needed:
  - Interval width and coverage: Please report empirical coverage of the true ACE under your specified relaxation parameters, and average interval widths, to complement the distance metric (a trivial wide interval always has zero error).
  - Baselines: Consider comparisons to other partial-identification methods that exploit covariates (e.g., monotonicity with IV; Rosenbaum-type sensitivity analyses; Geiger et al.’s “bounding confounding”), and to front-door when applicable.
  - Sensitivity to relaxation parameters: The chosen defaults (w=x=y=0.2, β=0.9, β̄=1.1) are plausible but subjective. Show how bounds vary with these, and provide guidelines for practitioners.
- Real data: On the influenza dataset, WPP yields bounds [−0.10, 0.17], substantially narrower than Balke-Pearl [−0.23, 0.64]. Given that WPP leverages conditional independences in the data, this is plausible, but the credibility hinges on robustness to parameter settings and on diagnostics that the chosen (W,Z) pair is not spurious. Please include sensitivity plots and acceptance rates (you say they are in the supplement) and discuss possible violations of exclusion-like restrictions in the chosen witness DM.

Clarity:
The paper is dense but generally well organized. The intuition behind the relaxation parameters and the inversion of Rule 1 is explained clearly. However:
- Some key inequalities (e.g., those in Table 2) are only sketched; bringing a minimal set into the main text would help.
- The notation (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω) is heavy; a compact glossary would aid readability.
- The Bayesian algorithm would benefit from a brief complexity analysis and a discussion of practical acceptance rates for constrained Dirichlet sampling as |Z| grows.

Originality:
High. The conceptual inversion of discovery-driven admissible sets into relaxed-IV bounding models, coupled with tunable local-independence relaxations, is novel. The algebraic back-substitution procedure is a useful algorithmic contribution, even if conservative.

Significance:
Potentially high. Many observational studies face the dilemma between untestable point-ID assumptions (faithfulness/back-door) and very wide IV bounds. Providing a principled middle ground with interpretable sensitivity parameters and practical algorithms could see uptake in both research and applied domains. The current empirical section is promising but should more thoroughly demonstrate robustness and practical guidance on setting relaxations.

Pros:
- Novel framework bridging causal discovery and partial identification.
- Solid grounding in established IV LP machinery; clear interpretability of relaxations.
- Practical algorithmic contribution (back-substitution) and Bayesian uncertainty quantification.
- Code availability; real-data illustration.

Cons:
- Choice and calibration of relaxation parameters are underdeveloped; more sensitivity and guidance needed.
- Back-substitution bounds are not tight; need explicit guarantees they are outer bounds.
- Bayesian rejection sampling criterion and model falsification are ad hoc; scalability unclear.
- Empirical evaluation lacks coverage/width reporting and broader baselines.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a creative and principled approach, and extends the toolkit for observational causal inference. With stronger empirical validation (coverage and sensitivity), clearer guidance on parameter calibration, and explicit soundness guarantees for the relaxed algorithms, this could be a strong contribution.
