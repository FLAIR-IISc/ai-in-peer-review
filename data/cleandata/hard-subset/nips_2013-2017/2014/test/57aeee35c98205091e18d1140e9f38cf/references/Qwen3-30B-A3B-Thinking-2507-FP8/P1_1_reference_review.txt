

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on Y using observational data. The authors build on Entner et al.'s [6] work by inverting the usage of their "Rule 1" to use witness variables (W) for instrumental variable bounding rather than back-door adjustment. The key innovation is interpreting violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence constraints, then using linear programming with relaxed constraints to generate conservative bounds on the ACE. The paper also develops symbolic algebraic bounds and a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm.

This work extends Ramsahai [16] and Dawid [5] by providing a systematic way to incorporate bounded violations of faithfulness assumptions, offering a middle ground between point estimates based on strong faithfulness assumptions and conservative IV bounds. The approach relates to NIPS work on causal inference (e.g., [14, 22, 7, 8]) but provides a new perspective on handling faithfulness violations.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formalizing a relaxation of faithfulness assumptions and deriving bounds that account for potential violations. The algebraic bounds (Table 2) provide valuable intuition about how different relaxation parameters affect the bounds.

2. **Practical Framework**: The WPP framework offers a principled approach to causal inference when faithfulness assumptions may be violated. The authors correctly identify that the "worst-case scenario" analysis of faithfulness violations is important for practical applications.

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The back-substitution algorithm provides a significant computational advantage over full linear programming, making the method feasible for larger problems. The paper provides evidence that this approach is "an order of magnitude more efficient" with reasonable accuracy.

4. **Experiments**: The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the method's robustness in cases where other approaches fail (e.g., when conditioning on the full covariate set produces biased estimates). The real-world influenza vaccination application shows practical utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Novelty Claims**: While the paper presents a new framework, the core techniques (linear programming for causal bounds, Bayesian model selection) are not entirely novel. The "novel symbolic bounds" are not sufficiently differentiated from existing work like Ramsahai [16]. The paper would benefit from a clearer comparison of how these bounds differ from or improve upon existing analytical bounds.

2. **Computational Claims**: The claim that the back-substitution algorithm is "an order of magnitude more efficient" lacks concrete evidence. The paper mentions "evidence in the Supplementary Material" but doesn't provide key benchmarks in the main text (e.g., runtime comparisons with full linear programming).

3. **Parameter Selection**: The paper acknowledges that choosing relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is an important open problem but provides only minimal guidance. The experiments use fixed values (w = x = y = 0.2, β = 0.9, β̄ = 1.1) without justification, making it difficult to assess how sensitive results are to these choices.

4. **Bayesian Approach Justification**: The Bayesian model validation approach (using Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling) is somewhat ad hoc. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific Bayesian approach is preferable to other methods for handling uncertainty in the constraints.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical derivations and reasonable experimental validation. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the paper could strengthen its claims with more concrete evidence on computational efficiency and parameter sensitivity.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good background context. The mathematical derivations are thorough, though some sections (particularly the Bayesian framework) could be clearer.

**Originality**: The WPP framework is novel in its systematic approach to handling faithfulness violations, but the specific techniques build substantially on existing work. The paper could better articulate the precise conceptual advances over previous methods.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is highly significant in causal inference. The WPP framework provides a practical middle ground between point estimation and conservative IV bounds, which is valuable for applied researchers. The real-world application demonstrates tangible utility.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to causal inference, particularly in providing a principled way to handle faithfulness violations. The main areas for improvement are:

1. Strengthen the novelty claims by more clearly differentiating the symbolic bounds from existing work (e.g., Ramsahai [16]).
2. Provide concrete evidence for the computational efficiency claims (e.g., runtime comparisons in the main text).
3. Justify the choice of relaxation parameters used in experiments or provide more guidance for parameter selection.

The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference with a methodologically sound approach that should be useful to practitioners. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the practical utility and clear exposition make it a valuable contribution to the field.

## Final Recommendation
**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to causal inference methodology, providing a useful tool for practitioners dealing with potential faithfulness violations. The WPP framework offers a well-justified compromise between point estimation and conservative bounds, and the computational improvements make the approach more practical. With minor revisions to strengthen the novelty claims and provide more evidence for computational efficiency, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
