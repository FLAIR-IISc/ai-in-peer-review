

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) by leveraging witness variables and relaxing the faithfulness assumption. The authors aim to provide a compromise between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds derived from instrumental variable approaches. While the paper addresses an important problem in causal inference, it suffers from numerous technical, methodological, and presentation flaws that significantly undermine its contribution.

## Major Technical and Methodological Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, including η?xw vs ηxw, "naı̈ve" vs "naive," and inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "P (Y = 1 | do(X = 1))" vs "P(Y=1|do(X=1))"). This makes the paper difficult to follow and suggests a lack of attention to detail.

2. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The claim that "we know of no previous analytical bounds as an algebraic function of w" is unsubstantiated. The authors should provide a clear comparison with existing work on analytical bounds for causal effects.

3. **Incomplete Mathematical Development**: The paper describes a "back-substitution algorithm" for efficiently computing bounds but fails to provide a clear mathematical formulation or proof of correctness. The description of how to "back-substitute inequalities" is vague and lacks necessary details.

4. **Misuse of Statistical Concepts**: The paper states that "the error of the interval [L,U] is zero if the true ACE lies in this interval," but this metric is problematic as it makes the trivial interval [-1,1] always have zero error, which the authors acknowledge but don't properly address.

5. **Inadequate Experimental Validation**: The paper claims "WPP is quite stable" based on simulations, but provides no statistical evidence for this claim (e.g., no p-values, confidence intervals, or comparisons of variances). The experimental setup is also poorly described, with missing details about parameter values and simulation conditions.

## Presentation and Formatting Issues

1. **Missing Figures and Tables**: The paper references "Table 1" and "Table 2" but these tables are not included in the provided text. Similarly, Figure 1 is referenced but not provided.

2. **Incomplete Citations**: Several citations are incomplete (e.g., [1], [5], [16] lack years, volumes, or page numbers), which is unacceptable for a scientific paper.

3. **Inconsistent Section Numbering**: The paper has a section "3.1 Bayesian Learning" but the content appears to be a continuation of Section 3 rather than a new subsection.

4. **Terminology Confusion**: The paper uses "witness" inconsistently to refer to both the instrumental variable and the admissible set, creating confusion. The definition of "witness" in Section 2 is not maintained throughout the paper.

5. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent formatting for mathematical expressions, variable names, and section headings, suggesting a lack of careful proofreading.

## Theoretical and Conceptual Flaws

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty**: The paper claims to "modify the results of Entner et al. [6]" but fails to clearly articulate how their approach differs from Entner's. The connection to prior work is weak and underdeveloped.

2. **Misunderstanding of Faithfulness**: The paper states "Even if unfaithful distributions can be ruled out as pathological under seemingly reasonable conditions [13]" but misrepresents the meaning of faithfulness in causal inference. The faithfulness assumption is not about pathological distributions but about the relationship between conditional independences and the causal graph.

3. **Unjustified Relaxations**: The paper introduces multiple relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) without justification for their selection. The claim that "any choice of w ≥ 0, y ≥ 0, x ≥ 0, 0 ≤ β ≤ 1 ≤ β̄ is guaranteed to provide bounds that are at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator" lacks proof.

4. **Misinterpretation of IV Structure**: The paper claims "Setting w = 0, β = β̄ = 1 recovers the standard IV structure" but fails to properly define what this structure is or how it differs from other structures.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths**: The paper addresses a genuine problem in causal inference (the fragility of faithfulness assumptions) and proposes a potentially useful framework. The idea of using witness variables as instrumental variables is conceptually interesting.

**Weaknesses**: The paper's methodological flaws, inconsistent presentation, and unsubstantiated claims significantly undermine its contribution. The experimental evaluation is inadequate, the theoretical development is incomplete, and the writing is riddled with errors.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent presentation, and lack of rigorous theoretical development make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues, including:

1. Complete mathematical development with proper proofs
2. Consistent notation throughout
3. Detailed experimental setup and statistical validation
4. Clear comparison with existing work
5. Proper formatting and citation of all references

The current version of the paper is not technically sound, lacks clarity, and does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. While the underlying idea has potential, the execution is poor, and the paper does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor and presentation. I recommend rejection.
