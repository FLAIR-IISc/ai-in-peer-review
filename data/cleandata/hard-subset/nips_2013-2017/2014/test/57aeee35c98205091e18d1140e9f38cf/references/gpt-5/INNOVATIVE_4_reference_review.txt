Summary
This paper tackles partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data when standard back-door adjustment is unreliable and the instrumental variable (IV) structure is only approximately satisfied. The core idea is to invert Entner et al.’s “witness/admissible set” Rule 1: instead of using (W, Z) to point-identify via back-door, treat W as a conditional instrument given Z, and then relax faithfulness by bounding local violations of conditional independences. These relaxations are parameterized by interpretable constants (w, x, y, β, β̄) that bound deviations from key IV and exclusion assumptions. Given these bounds, the authors formulate a linear program (extending Dawid and Ramsahai) to compute ACE bounds, and show how to incorporate Bayesian uncertainty via constrained Dirichlet posteriors with rejection sampling.

To improve scalability and interpretability, they derive new algebraic (symbolic) bounds as explicit functions of the relaxation parameters and present an efficient “back-substitution” algorithm that tightens box constraints in a message-passing-like fashion, avoiding repeated full LP solves. Experiments on synthetic data compare against naive back-door estimators and a faithfulness-only estimator; a real influenza vaccination dataset illustrates that WPP can yield substantially tighter bounds than standard IV bounds when conditional independences suggest attenuated confounding.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on and bridges (i) IV bounds (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins), (ii) discovery via faithfulness and Rule 1 (Entner et al.), and (iii) partial identification and sensitivity analysis (Manski). The novelty is in using conditional independences to generate “plausibly valid conditional instruments” and then deliberately relaxing those independences in a controlled, bounded way to interpolate between point-identification and worst-case IV bounds.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: Repurposes witnesses from faithfulness-based discovery into conditional IVs, and systematically relaxes faithfulness to produce robust yet informative bounds. This is a fresh and bold angle on combining discovery with partial identification.
- Interpretable sensitivity parameters: The w, x, y, β, β̄ knobs give a transparent way to encode “how much” violation of exclusion/independence is tolerated, unifying faithfulness and IV perspectives.
- New analytical contributions: Algebraic bounds as explicit functions of relaxation parameters and the back-substitution algorithm provide insight and efficiency beyond black-box LP solvers.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty: Constrained Dirichlet posteriors acknowledge sampling variability and allow falsification of overly tight models.
- Practical potential: The method often yields substantially narrower intervals than standard IV while remaining honest about uncertainty induced by unmeasured confounding. Code is promised.

Weaknesses
- Guidance for relaxation parameters: The paper defers principled selection/elicitation of w, x, y, β, β̄ to the supplement, yet these choices critically determine informativeness vs robustness. Concrete mappings from domain knowledge or observed measures (e.g., partial R2 or bounds on direct effects) would enhance usability.
- Validation depth: Simulations are helpful but limited. There is no systematic coverage analysis to show that the bounds achieve nominal containment under data generated with known relaxation parameters; width vs validity trade-offs are not quantified.
- Binary-only scope: The framework is currently restricted to binary variables. Applicability to multi-valued or continuous data (discretization aside) is not addressed.
- Model selection and multiplicity: Searching over many (W, Z) pairs and then reporting the “best” bound raises concerns about selection-induced optimism. There is no correction (e.g., split-sample validation or multiplicity-aware scoring).
- Ad hoc falsification rule: The “95% rejection” cutoff for rejecting constrained Dirichlet proposals is heuristic, and the impact on inference and calibration is unclear.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is dense; key ideas (e.g., the precise meaning of the η, δ, ζ, κ, ω parameterizations and their relationships) are hard to follow in the main text. The back-substitution algorithm is only sketched; an explicit pseudo-code and a small worked example would help. Several claims are deferred to the supplement.

Quality
Technically solid with reasonable extensions of known LP-based partial identification. The algebraic bounds/back-substitution are interesting and appear correct as conservative relaxations, though more proof detail in the main text would help. Empirical evaluation is promising but could better characterize calibration (coverage) and sensitivity to parameter choices and near-unfaithfulness.

Clarity
Readable but overly compact. Suggestions:
- Include a running toy example that walks through witness selection, parameter setting, and bound computation (LP and back-substitution).
- Provide pseudo-code for back-substitution and a complexity discussion.
- Centralize notation (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω) in a concise table and keep Z explicit instead of suppressing it.
- Offer a short “practitioner’s guide” for choosing relaxation parameters.

Originality
High. The inversion of Rule 1 into a bounded-IV framework and the algebraic/message-passing treatment of partial identification represent a genuine, creative direction.

Significance
High potential impact for robust causal inference from observational data. The approach fills an important gap between brittle point-ID via faithfulness and overly conservative IV bounds, and could influence practice in epidemiology, econometrics, and ML.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide principled schemes for setting/eliciting w, x, y, β, β̄ (e.g., tie to prior bounds on direct effects or partial correlations), and study robustness across a grid of plausible values.
- Report empirical coverage of ACE bounds under data generated with known relaxation parameters; quantify width inflation from back-substitution vs full LP.
- Address multiplicity in witness/admissible set search (e.g., use sample splitting or hierarchical modeling over (W, Z)).
- Clarify whether back-substitution converges to an order-invariant fixed point; any guarantees on tightness relative to LP?
- Discuss extensions beyond binary variables and to unknown causal ordering (the Y-structure direction is promising—outline concrete steps).
- Compare against other sensitivity/partial-ID methods (e.g., Rosenbaum-type bounds, recent confounding-robust methods) to position WPP more broadly.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold, original contribution that opens a new and useful path between structure discovery and partial identification. While some empirical and expository polish is needed, the conceptual advance and the methodological toolkit (LP formulation with bounded faithfulness violations, algebraic bounds, and efficient back-substitution) merit presentation at NeurIPS.
