

# Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary variable X on Y using a set of binary covariates. The authors build on Entner et al.'s [6] work on admissible sets and instrumental variables, but invert the interpretation of their Rule 1 to use "witnesses" as instrumental variables rather than discarding them. The key innovation is introducing bounded violations of faithfulness through relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), which allows for more robust causal bounds when faithfulness assumptions are violated. The paper also introduces symbolic algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm to improve computational efficiency over standard linear programming approaches.

The work sits at the intersection of causal inference, graphical models, and robust statistics. It relates to Manski's work on partial identification [11], the instrumental variable literature [1, 5, 16], and the growing body of work on causal discovery under weak assumptions [6, 22]. While the paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference methodology, it could better distinguish itself from existing robust causal inference approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper's core insight—using witnesses as instrumental variables with bounded violations of faithfulness—is novel and addresses a genuine limitation of current methods. The relaxation framework (Equations 3-6) provides a principled way to handle faithfulness violations, which is a significant advancement over purely point-estimation approaches.

2. **Computational Innovation**: The back-substitution algorithm for generating symbolic bounds (Section 4) is a substantial computational contribution. The paper demonstrates it's orders of magnitude faster than standard LP approaches while maintaining reasonable bound tightness, making the method more practical for real-world applications.

3. **Bayesian Framework**: The Bayesian approach to handling uncertainty (Section 3.1) is well-motivated and provides a more principled way to handle model uncertainty than simple plug-in estimators. The rejection sampling approach for constrained Dirichlet priors is clever and appropriate for the problem.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The synthetic experiments (Section 5) effectively demonstrate the method's robustness when faithfulness assumptions are violated, and the real-world influenza vaccination case study provides a compelling practical application.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing robust causal inference methods. For example, Manski [11] and Geiger et al. [8] have developed similar bounded approaches without the witness framework. The authors should clarify how their method improves upon or differs from these approaches.

2. **Overstated Claims**: The claim that "this should become a standard item in the toolbox" is premature without more extensive validation across diverse datasets. The paper only tests on one real-world dataset, and the synthetic experiments are limited to specific graph structures.

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail on how to choose the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While they mention these will be addressed in follow-up work, the paper should at least provide practical guidance for practitioners. The default parameters used in experiments (w = x = y = 0.2, β = 0.9, β̄ = 1.1) seem arbitrary.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper doesn't fully address when the witness-based approach will outperform standard instrumental variable methods. The theoretical guarantees for the bounds (e.g., how tight they are compared to the true ACE) are not thoroughly analyzed.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of causal inference. The core idea of using witnesses as instrumental variables with bounded faithfulness violations is novel and addresses a genuine problem in causal discovery. The computational improvements via symbolic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm are particularly valuable for practical applications.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) More detailed comparison to existing robust causal inference methods (Manski, Geiger, etc.)
2) Better justification for the choice of relaxation parameters
3) More extensive empirical validation across diverse datasets
4) Clearer theoretical characterization of the bounds' tightness

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference methodology that would be useful to researchers and practitioners. The main issues are not fundamental flaws but rather opportunities to strengthen the paper's presentation and context within the existing literature. The authors should address the limitations noted above in the revision, particularly by better distinguishing their approach from existing robust causal inference methods and providing more concrete guidance on parameter selection.

The paper's strengths in theoretical contribution, computational innovation, and empirical validation outweigh its current limitations, especially given the importance of robust causal inference in real-world applications. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
