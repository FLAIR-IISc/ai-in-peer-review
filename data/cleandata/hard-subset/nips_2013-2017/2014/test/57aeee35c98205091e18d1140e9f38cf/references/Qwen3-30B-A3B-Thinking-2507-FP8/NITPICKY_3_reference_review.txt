

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a new method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) by modifying Entner et al.'s approach to identify admissible sets. The core idea is to use witness variables to generate bounds on ACE, relaxing faithfulness assumptions through carefully parameterized constraints. While the problem addressed is important in causal inference, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation issues that undermine its credibility and clarity.

## Major Technical Concerns

The paper's central claim is that it "modifies the results of Entner et al. [6]" but fails to clearly articulate the specific modifications. The relationship between the proposed method and Entner's Rule 1 is obfuscated. The paper states that "W will play the role of an instrumental variable, instead of being discarded," but doesn't explain why this represents a meaningful modification rather than a simple reinterpretation.

The notation throughout is inconsistent and confusing. The use of "η?xw" (with a question mark) instead of standard subscript/superscript notation (e.g., η_xw) creates unnecessary ambiguity. The paper states "Setting w = 0, β = β̄ = 1 recovers the standard IV structure," but β = β̄ = 1 is not standard for IV; the standard IV doesn't involve β parameters at all. This suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of instrumental variable theory.

The paper claims to provide "novel symbolic bounds" but fails to present them in the main text, instead deferring to supplementary material. The referenced Table 2 (which isn't provided) is crucial for evaluating the claimed novelty, but the paper's description of these bounds is insufficient to assess their contribution.

## Presentation and Formatting Issues

The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent typography. The repeated use of "Naı̈ve Estimator" with unusual diacritics (instead of the standard "Naive") is unprofessional and creates confusion. The phrase "in not implied" should be "is not implied," demonstrating carelessness in proofreading.

The paper claims "WPP is quite stable" based on experimental results but provides insufficient detail about the experimental setup and results. The description of the synthetic study is vague ("we analyze two variations" but doesn't specify what these variations are), and the comparison against baseline methods lacks necessary statistical details.

The reference to "the influenza data discussed by [9, 18]" is problematic because [18] is not properly cited in the reference list (it appears as "Transparent parameterizatios of models for potential outcomes, T. Richardson, R. Evans, J. Robins, J. Bernardo, M. Bayarri, J. Berger, A. Dawid, D. Heckerman, A. Smith, and M. West, editors, Bayesian Statistics 9, pages 569–610. Oxford University Press, ," which is actually reference [18] in the paper, but the paper incorrectly references it as [18] when discussing the influenza study).

## Incomplete Presentation

The paper states "We develop a framework for choosing relaxation parameters in the Supplemental, and expect to extend it in follow-up publications." This is a major red flag for a conference paper, as the main contribution should be self-contained. The paper's claims of novelty and utility depend on these relaxation parameters, yet the paper provides no guidance on how to choose them in practice.

The description of the Bayesian approach in Section 3.1 is technically problematic. The paper states that "we parameterize P(Y,X,W|Z) as a full 2×2×2 contingency table" but doesn't explain why this is appropriate given the binary nature of the variables. The approach to handling uncertainty through Bayesian methods is not well-justified for this specific problem.

## Clarity and Organization

The paper's structure is confusing. The transition from the background to the methodology is abrupt, and the relationship between the witness concept and instrumental variables isn't clearly established. The paper uses "witness" in multiple contexts without consistent definition, making it difficult to follow the argument.

The description of the linear programming approach is particularly unclear. The paper states "We now introduce the following assumptions" but doesn't explain why these assumptions are necessary or how they relate to the problem. The reference to "the following linear programming" is misleading as no linear program is actually presented.

## Minor but Cumulative Flaws

The paper contains numerous minor but significant issues that collectively undermine its credibility:
- Inconsistent use of "ACE" (sometimes capitalized, sometimes not)
- Missing spaces in phrases like "in not implied" (should be "is not implied")
- Inconsistent use of parentheses around "do(X = x)"
- The phrase "a so-called Naı̈ve Estimator" is unprofessional
- The paper refers to "Figure 1(c)" without describing what the figure shows
- The paper states "We provide a short summary for context in Section 2" but the summary is not provided

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an important problem in causal inference, the numerous technical, presentation, and organizational flaws make it difficult to assess the validity and novelty of the proposed method. The paper fails to clearly articulate how its approach differs from prior work, contains inconsistent notation, and relies on supplementary material for key contributions. The grammatical errors and formatting issues suggest a lack of careful editing, which is particularly concerning for a technical paper.

The paper's claims of providing "novel symbolic bounds" and a "new methodology" are not substantiated in the main text. The experimental results are not presented with sufficient detail to evaluate the method's performance. The paper's dependence on supplementary material for its main contributions is a serious flaw for a conference paper.

Given these issues, I recommend rejection. While the topic is important, the paper does not meet the standards of technical rigor and clarity expected at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to clarify the relationship to prior work, fix the notation issues, provide the key results in the main text, and ensure proper technical accuracy before it could be considered for publication.
