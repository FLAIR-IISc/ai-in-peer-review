- Introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework that bounds average causal effect (ACE) using witnesses and relaxation parameters to handle faithfulness violations.  
- Modifies Entner et al.'s witness-based approach to produce conservative ACE bounds rather than unstable point estimates.  
- Combines instrumental-variable ideas with linear programming and symbolic algebra to generate bounds, plus a Bayesian method for uncertainty quantification.  
- Theoretical development is solid, building on Pearl, Dawid, Ramsahai, and Entner et al., with detailed derivations and supplementary material.  
- Experiments are thoughtful: four synthetic scenarios (hard/easy, with/without theoretical solutions) and a real-world influenza vaccination study.  
- Demonstrates WPP yields more robust (though wider) bounds than naive point estimators when faithfulness is questionable.  
- Bayesian learning using constrained Dirichlet priors is well-executed for handling conditional probability uncertainty.  
- Authors transparently discuss limitations: higher computational cost for full LP and non-tight symbolic bounds.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and mathematically clear; Algorithm 1 and notation are consistent.  
- Suggests adding a brief glossary to aid readers less familiar with causal-inference terminology.  
- Original contribution: using witnesses to produce bounds (not point estimates) and introducing relaxation parameters to quantify faithfulness violations.  
- Symbolic algebraic bounds are innovative, clarifying how relaxation parameters affect bounds beyond numeric evaluations.  
- Significant practical impact: addresses instability of point estimates under violated assumptions and provides a principled trade-off between precision and robustness.  
- Weaknesses: computational cost may hinder adoption, symbolic bounds are loose, sensitivity to relaxation-parameter choice not fully explored, and some hard synthetic cases may be contrived.  
- Recommendation: accept — the work is original, significant, well-executed, and relevant to practitioners in healthcare and social sciences.
