- Proposes WPP: invert Rule 1 to construct “near-IVs” from discovered (W,Z) pairs and bound ACE via parameterized relaxations and LP-based partial identification.  
- Extends Balke-Pearl and related LP formulations by introducing interpretable relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) linking faithfulness violations to observable inequalities.  
- Provides a Bayesian procedure using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to propagate sampling uncertainty into bound endpoints.  
- Introduces algebraic symbolic (non-tight) inequalities and a fast back-substitution algorithm to compute conservative outer bounds without full LP.  
- Synthetic and real-data experiments (influenza encouragement) show WPP can yield substantially narrower bounds than standard IV under modest relaxations.  
- Technical construction is soundly built on binary-IV latent-response parameterization, extremal point enumeration, and dual polytope inequalities.  
- Relaxation parameters are interpretable as bounded departures from Rule 1 conditional independences and linked reasonably to observables via inequalities.  
- Paper should state formal soundness theorems: LP bounds are valid outer bounds under (3)–(6), and back-substitution returns an outer relaxation (no false precision).  
- The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet rejection-sampling “95% rejection ⇒ falsify” rule is ad hoc; report acceptance rates, diagnostics, and sensitivity to priors.  
- Experiments need additional metrics: empirical coverage of true ACE under relaxations and average interval widths (not just distance).  
- Compare against more baselines: other partial-identification methods using covariates, monotonicity-IV, Rosenbaum-type sensitivity analyses, and front-door where applicable.  
- Provide sensitivity analyses showing how bounds vary with relaxation parameters and offer practical guidelines for choosing them.  
- Real-data credibility depends on robustness to parameter settings and diagnostics that (W,Z) pairs are not spurious; include sensitivity plots and acceptance rates.  
- Clarity issues: include key inequalities (e.g., Table 2) in main text, provide a compact glossary for heavy notation (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω).  
- Bayesian algorithm needs brief complexity analysis and discussion of practical acceptance rates as |Z| grows.  
- Originality: high — novel inversion of discovery-driven admissible sets into a family of relaxed-IV models with tunable parameters.  
- Significance: potentially high, offering a principled middle ground between point-ID faithfulness and wide IV bounds if robustness and guidance are strengthened.  
- Pros: novel framework, strong grounding in IV LP machinery, interpretable relaxations, practical algorithmic back-substitution, Bayesian uncertainty quantification, code and real-data example.  
- Cons: underdeveloped calibration/guidance for relaxations, back-substitution not tight without explicit outer-bound guarantees, ad hoc Bayesian falsification and scalability concerns, empirical evaluation lacks coverage/width and broader baselines.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept pending stronger empirical validation (coverage and sensitivity), clearer parameter calibration guidance, and explicit soundness guarantees for relaxed algorithms.
