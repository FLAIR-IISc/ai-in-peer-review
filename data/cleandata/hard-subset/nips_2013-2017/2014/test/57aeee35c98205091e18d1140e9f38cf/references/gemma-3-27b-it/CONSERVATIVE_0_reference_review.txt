This paper proposes a “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) for causal inference, aiming to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets for backdoor adjustment as a means to find instrumental variables, and then to use linear programming, combined with relaxations for scalability, to derive bounds on the ACE. The authors also introduce a Bayesian learning approach and a back-substitution algorithm to speed up computation.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a central problem in causal inference – estimating causal effects from observational data. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard approaches, such as reliance on faithfulness and the difficulty in identifying complete sets of confounders. The proposed method attempts to provide more robust bounds by explicitly accounting for potential violations of faithfulness. The connection to instrumental variable theory is also well-established and relevant.

However, despite the novelty of the approach, I find myself unconvinced of its practical significance or theoretical advancement. The method appears overly complex, with numerous parameters and relaxations introduced, making it difficult to assess its robustness and interpretability. The reliance on linear programming, even with the proposed relaxations and back-substitution algorithm, raises concerns about computational scalability, especially for problems with a larger number of covariates. The authors acknowledge this, but the proposed solutions feel ad-hoc and potentially introduce further inaccuracies.

My primary concern is the lack of convincing evidence demonstrating a substantial improvement over existing methods. The experimental results, while presenting a comparison to naive estimators and Entner et al.’s approach, do not clearly showcase a compelling advantage for WPP. The synthetic experiments, although well-designed, lack the complexity of real-world datasets. The real-world experiment with influenza data is similarly limited – the authors admit the method couldn’t validate the initially proposed IV and relied on a different variable set. While this demonstrates flexibility, it doesn't prove superior performance. The comparison to the standard IV bounds from [1] is limited, and it’s unclear whether the wider bounds obtained by WPP provide any meaningful information.

Furthermore, the paper’s notation is dense and occasionally obscures the core ideas. While the authors provide background, the rapid introduction of numerous definitions and constraints makes the methodology challenging to follow. The extensive use of Supplementary Material, while common, feels necessary here to even begin to grasp the details of the approach, hindering accessibility. The Bayesian aspect, while theoretically sound, adds another layer of complexity without a clear demonstration of its benefit over simpler alternatives. The justification for the choice of Dirichlet priors is cursory.

The authors spend considerable effort attempting to address potential faithfulness violations. However, the reliance on bounding these violations via parameters 'w', 'y', 'x', 'β', and 'β̄' feels somewhat arbitrary. The choice of these parameters seems to lack a strong theoretical justification and could significantly impact the resulting bounds.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to causal inference that explicitly addresses faithfulness violations.
*   Connection to established theories of instrumental variables.
*   Potential for providing more robust bounds on ACEs in complex observational settings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Overly complex methodology with numerous parameters and relaxations.
*   Lack of compelling evidence demonstrating significant improvement over existing methods.
*   Computational concerns regarding scalability, even with proposed optimizations.
*   Dense notation and challenging readability.
*   Arbitrary choices in the parameterization of faithfulness violations.
*   Limited practical demonstration in real-world settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejection**. While the paper tackles an important problem and presents a novel approach, the complexity of the method, coupled with the lack of convincing empirical evidence, outweigh its potential benefits. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a fully developed and validated contribution. The authors need to significantly simplify the methodology, provide stronger empirical results demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods, and offer a more compelling justification for the choices made in the parameterization of faithfulness violations before this work would be suitable for NIPS. The current presentation gives the impression of adding complexity for complexity's sake, rather than providing a genuinely useful and practical tool for causal inference.
