Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a methodology to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y using observed binary covariates W that are not descendants of X or Y. It inverts and relaxes Entner et al.’s “Rule 1” (which uses faithfulness to obtain point identification via back-door adjustment): instead of discarding witness variables W that satisfy conditional independencies relative to some Z, WPP treats such pairs (W, Z) as candidate “conditionally valid instruments” and computes bounds on the ACE that explicitly allow bounded violations of faithfulness. The violations are encoded via interpretable slack parameters:
- w bounds the direct effect of W on Y (exclusion restriction slack),
- x and y bound deviations between structural parameters and observed conditionals P(X|W), P(Y|X,W),
- β, β̄ bound dependence between the latent confounders U and W.

Given (W, Z) and a choice of slack parameters, the paper adapts the linear programming framework of Dawid and Ramsahai to compute bounds on the ACE, conditioning on each value of Z and averaging over P(Z). It introduces a computationally cheaper “back-substitution” approach based on a family of algebraic (non-tight but fast) inequalities that approximate the LP solution and provide intuition on how the slack parameters tighten or loosen bounds. To address sampling uncertainty, the paper proposes a Bayesian procedure using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling over P(Y, X, W | Z), producing posterior distributions over ACE bounds and a simple falsification test.

Experiments include synthetic studies comparing WPP to naive back-door adjustments (using all W or none) and to Entner et al.’s faithfulness point estimator, and an application to influenza vaccination using an encouragement design. In the latter, WPP identifies a different witness (DM) with Z = {AGE, SEX} and yields substantially narrower bounds [−0.10, 0.17] than the standard IV bounds using the randomized encouragement [−0.23, 0.64].

Relation to prior work
- Builds on instrumental variable bounds for binary variables (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) and their LP formulations.
- Uses causal discovery via conditional independencies (Spirtes et al.; Entner et al.) but relaxes faithfulness rather than relying on it for point identification.
- Connects to partial identification (Manski) and to inequality constraints in causal graphs (Evans; Richardson & Robins).
- The idea of tunable relaxations bridging IV and faithfulness-based back-door adjustment appears novel in this form.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: an appealing middle ground between brittle faithfulness-based point estimates and often-wide IV bounds, with interpretable slack parameters that make assumptions explicit.
- Methodological breadth: LP bounds, algebraic approximations, and a message-passing-like back-substitution algorithm that greatly improves computational efficiency.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty with constrained Dirichlet modeling and model falsification provides a pragmatic workflow.
- Empirical demonstration on both synthetic data (showing stability when back-door is misspecified) and a real encouragement-design dataset where WPP yields sharper bounds than standard IV.
- Code availability and a clear path for practitioners to use the method.

Weaknesses
- Choice and interpretation of relaxation parameters are central but under-specified. Without principled guidance or calibration (e.g., to domain knowledge, sensitivity indices analogous to Rosenbaum’s Γ), users could unintentionally tune assumptions to desired conclusions.
- Some technical exposition is hard to follow: several definitions (η, ζ, κ, ω) and constraints (3)–(6) appear without full derivations or intuitive explanation in the main text; notation is heavy; references to supplementary material are frequent and essential.
- The algebraic bounds are acknowledged to be non-tight, and the paper reports cases where back-substitution outputs fall outside the feasible LP region. While expected, this undermines trust without a quantitative characterization of the typical tightness gap and failure rates.
- Empirical evaluation focuses on scenarios partly constructed to disadvantage naive back-door adjustment. Comparisons to other partial identification/sensitivity approaches (e.g., Rosenbaum’s sensitivity analysis, VanderWeele–Shpitser/Geiger–Janzing–Schölkopf bounding confounding) are missing.
- Practical scalability: exhaustive search over Z is exponential; the approach assumes low-dimensional Z. Guidance on scalable Z-selection beyond brute-force or on using existing causal discovery algorithms at scale is limited.
- The Bayesian falsification rule (95% rejection in constrained-Dirichlet sampling) and the use of BDeu with ESS=10 are ad hoc; sensitivity to these choices is not studied.

Quality
- Largely sound and well grounded in prior theory on IV bounds and LP formulations. The new constraints and relaxations are sensible, and the mapping to LP is appropriate. However, some claims (e.g., “bounds are at least as conservative as backdoor-adjusted point estimator”) would benefit from a precise theorem and proof in the main paper. The stochastic search/falsification choices could be justified more rigorously.

Clarity
- The paper is dense. Improve explanations of the slack parameters’ causal meaning, the roles of ζ, κ, ω, and the mapping from structural to observed quantities. A small running example would help. Fix minor typos and provide a clear algorithmic complexity discussion. Summarize key inequalities from the supplement in a more self-contained way.

Originality
- The inversion of Rule 1 to generate candidate IVs conditioned on Z, coupled with bounded violations of faithfulness and LP-based bounds, is novel. The back-substitution algorithm and algebraic bounds are also new to my knowledge.

Significance
- Useful contribution for observational causal inference where neither strong faithfulness nor strong IV assumptions are fully credible. The tunable, transparent trade-off between assumptions and informativeness is valuable for practice. If better parameter-calibration guidance is added, impact could be considerable.

Empirical evaluation
- Synthetic setup is informative but tailored; include scenarios with smaller n, different strengths of instruments, and mis-specified witnesses/Z. Report coverage and width statistics, and computational times for LP vs back-substitution. In the flu data, compare directly to standard sensitivity analyses and report sensitivity of bounds to the chosen slack parameters.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide principled strategies to set or elicit w, x, y, β, β̄ (e.g., mapping to bounds on direct effects, prior knowledge, or sensitivity grids akin to Γ).
- Quantify the tightness gap and failure probability of back-substitution vs LP across many draws, and give practical guidance when to trust the fast method.
- Add more baselines from partial identification and sensitivity analysis literature; discuss relations to weak-IV diagnostics and overidentification tests.
- Clarify the search over Z (scalable heuristics; use of constraint-based discovery with stability selection).
- Strengthen clarity with a running binary example, explicit mapping between parameters and DAG semantics, and a consolidated list of the most informative inequalities.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely contribution bridging faithfulness and IV bounds with interpretable relaxations.
- Practical algorithmic advances (fast back-substitution) and Bayesian uncertainty handling.
- Demonstrates potential to sharpen bounds in real data.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient guidance on setting relaxation parameters; risk of subjective tuning.
- Heavy reliance on supplement; clarity and rigor could be improved.
- Limited comparisons and scalability discussion.

Overall: A solid and promising contribution to partial identification in causal inference. With clearer exposition and stronger empirical comparisons, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance after revision focusing on parameter calibration, clarity, and broader baselines.
