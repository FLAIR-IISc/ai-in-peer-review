## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies. It builds upon the work of Entner et al. (2013) by reinterpreting their search procedure for admissible sets as a means of identifying instrumental variables. The core idea is to relax faithfulness assumptions and represent violations as bounded deviations, allowing for more robust ACE bounds via a linear programming formulation. To address the computational complexity, the authors propose further relaxations and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm for approximating the bounds. The paper demonstrates the approach through synthetic experiments and an analysis of influenza vaccination data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper situates itself within the extensive literature on causal inference, particularly focusing on instrumental variables and back-door adjustment (Pearl, 2009). It directly addresses limitations of existing methods relying on strict faithfulness assumptions, referencing challenges highlighted by Dawid (1990), Ramsahai (2016) and others. The connection to Entner et al. (2013) is central, and the authors correctly position their work as a refinement and generalization. The referencing is comprehensive and covers key work in causal discovery (Spirtes et al., 1993), and related areas.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The "Witness Protection Program" provides a compelling framework for addressing ACE estimation in the presence of potential unfaithfulness. The idea of explicitly modeling deviations from faithfulness as bounded violations is innovative.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper offers a strong theoretical basis for its methodology, grounding it in the established literature on causal inference and linear programming. The algebraic bounds (presented in the supplementary material) offer valuable analytical insight.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the computational challenges and propose practical relaxations to make the method scalable. The back-substitution algorithm is a notable contribution to address this issue.
* **Empirical Evaluation:**  The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments provides valuable validation of the approach. The influenza vaccination example offers a concrete application.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the need for methods that are robust to violations of faithfulness, a common concern in real-world observational studies.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:**  Despite the relaxations and the back-substitution algorithm, the overall method remains complex, potentially limiting its accessibility to practitioners without a strong background in causal inference and linear programming.  The numerous steps and parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) require careful consideration and justification.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper discusses the trade-off between bound width and robustness, the sensitivity of the bounds to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is not fully explored in the experiments. More detailed sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.
* **Computational Cost (even with relaxations):** Although the back-substitution algorithm improves efficiency, the computational cost, particularly for larger datasets with many covariates, could still be substantial. More quantitative analysis of the runtime is needed.
* **Interpretation of Bounds:** The paper focuses heavily on obtaining bounds, but less on how these bounds should be interpreted and used in practice. Providing guidance on how to leverage the width and location of the bounds to make informed decisions would be beneficial.
* **Bayesian implementation detail:** The mention of rejecting 90% of samples during the Bayesian approach raises concerns about efficiency and potential bias. It is unclear what the implications of this rejection rate are.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in causal inference.
* The methodology offers a valuable extension to existing techniques, providing robustness to violations of faithfulness.
* The empirical results demonstrate the potential of the method in both synthetic and real-world settings.
* The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the method may limit its widespread adoption.
* The sensitivity to relaxation parameters and computational cost require further investigation.
* The interpretation and practical application of the bounds could be better addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to advance the field of causal inference. While the method is complex and requires further refinement, the core idea is innovative and the theoretical foundation is solid. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in observational studies and offer a valuable contribution to addressing them. The weaknesses are not fatal, and could be addressed through future work. 

**Rating:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions**

**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Expand Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of different relaxation parameter settings on the bounds.
* **Runtime Analysis:** Provide quantitative analysis of the runtime of the back-substitution algorithm and the full linear programming approach for datasets of varying sizes.
* **Guidance on Interpretation:**  Include a discussion on how to interpret the bounds in practice and how to use them to inform decision-making.
* **Clarify Bayesian Implementation:** Provide more detail about the rationale for the 95% rejection threshold in the Bayesian implementation, and its potential impact on the results.
* **Consider a simplified case study:** Including an additional, very simple example alongside the influenza data, may improve understanding of the method for a broader audience.
