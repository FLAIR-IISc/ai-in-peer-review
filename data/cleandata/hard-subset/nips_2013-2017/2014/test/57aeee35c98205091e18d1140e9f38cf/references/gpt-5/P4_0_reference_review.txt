Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y using observational data and available covariates W that are not effects of X or Y. Building on and inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (which uses conditional independence tests under faithfulness to identify admissible covariate sets for back-door adjustment), the authors treat a discovered witness–admissible set pair (W, Z) as a generator of candidate instrumental-variable-like structures. Instead of insisting on exact faithfulness, they introduce bounded relaxations that allow limited violations of independence along specific causal pathways, controlled by parameters w, x, y, β, β̄. These bounds are then propagated through a linear programming (LP) formulation that extends prior LP-based partial identification for binary instrumental variables (Dawid; Ramsahai; Balke-Pearl).

The contribution is threefold:
- A methodology (“Witness Protection Program”, WPP) that converts witness/admissible-set discoveries into IV-style bounds on ACE, parameterized by interpretable relaxation degrees for faithfulness violations, and solvable via LP over a latent U.
- A Bayesian framework that places constrained Dirichlet priors on observable contingency tables conditioned on Z and uses rejection sampling to obtain posterior distributions over ACE bounds, including a simple falsification heuristic when constraints are largely violated.
- Algebraic, symbolic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that avoid full LP construction at each posterior draw, improving computational efficiency while producing slightly looser (more conservative) bounds.

This work connects to: (i) classical IV bounds in binary models (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins), (ii) faithfulness-based causal discovery (Spirtes et al.; Entner et al.), and (iii) recent interest in bounding approaches under imperfect compliance or unmeasured confounding (Manski; Geiger et al.). The main novelty is the principled combination of structure learned from conditional independences with a tunable relaxation away from strict faithfulness, producing data-driven partial identification rather than point identification.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LP construction leverages established ideas for binary IV models and adapts them with relaxation parameters that bound local deviations between observable and latent-conditioned probabilities, plus sensitivity to imbalance of U across W via β, β̄. The mapping to κ and ω to accommodate β ≠ β̄ is coherent. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution method are conservative relaxations of the exact LP and are correctly positioned as such. The Bayesian treatment correctly acknowledges uncertainty in estimated contingency tables and the fact that the implied feasible region occupies a strict subset of the probability simplex.

- Assumptions: The approach requires (a) a set of covariates not downstream of X or Y, (b) discovery of witness–admissible set pairs via approximate conditional independences, and (c) user-chosen relaxation parameters. The paper is explicit that the bounds widen as assumptions are weakened, and that choices of relaxation parameters control the trade-off.

- Empirical evaluation: Synthetic experiments are designed so that full adjustment on W is invalid and include cases with and without a true witness pair. Metrics compare interval estimates to the true ACE. WPP exhibits stability across settings relative to naive estimators and faithfulness-only point estimates. A real-world influenza vaccination dataset is analyzed, yielding substantially narrower bounds than standard IV bounds when using an alternative witness and admissible set. While illustrative, the empirical section would benefit from broader sensitivity analyses to θ = {w, x, y, β, β̄}, sample size scaling, and comparisons to other bounding frameworks.

- Limitations: Guidance for choosing relaxation parameters is deferred to the supplement and not fully operationalized; the falsification criterion (95% rejection in rejection sampling) is heuristic; the use of BDeu and exhaustive search for Z may be brittle or costly for larger W; frequentist coverage or calibration of the posterior over bounds is not studied; theoretical properties of the back-substitution algorithm beyond monotone tightening are not characterized.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear in motivation and high-level flow but dense in notation. The main assumptions and parameters would benefit from clearer, earlier definitions and an intuitive explanation of their causal meaning. The LP pipeline is summarized but relies heavily on the supplement for derivations and the exact inequalities. The Bayesian section is concise but leaves practical implementation details (e.g., acceptance rates, effective sample sizes, computational cost) largely to the supplement. The back-substitution algorithm is described clearly enough to grasp its intent; additional illustrative examples would help.

Originality
The key ideas—turning witness/admissible-set discoveries into IV-like bounding with tunable faithfulness relaxations, plus symbolic constraints and back-substitution—appear novel. Prior work either enforces strict faithfulness to get point identification or uses IV bounds without leveraging conditional independences in the observed graph. The Bayesian posterior over bounds with constrained Dirichlet on observables is also a useful and relatively underexplored perspective.

Significance
Partial identification with realistic relaxations is important for observational studies where strict assumptions are dubious. The framework can make bounds substantially tighter than generic IV bounds while maintaining robustness to near-unfaithfulness, which is a practically meaningful contribution. Adoption will hinge on scalable search for Z, principled selection of relaxation parameters, and clearer guidance on interpretation. If these are addressed, the proposed toolbox could see use in applied causal analyses.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap between faithfulness-based point identification and conservative IV bounds.
- Provides a tunable, interpretable framework with explicit sensitivity parameters and linear programming implementation.
- Introduces efficient algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that can substantially reduce computation.
- Bayesian treatment accounts for sampling uncertainty and offers model falsification.
- Demonstrates utility on both synthetic and real data, with code availability.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited guidance and empirical calibration for choosing relaxation parameters; practical deployment choices remain ad hoc.
- Heuristic falsification and reliance on approximate independence tests (BDeu) may yield spurious witnesses in finite samples.
- Scalability concerns: exhaustive search over Z and rejection sampling may not scale; complexity and runtime are not fully characterized.
- Theoretical guarantees on tightness, coverage, and behavior under misspecification are not developed.
- Many technical details and proofs are relegated to the supplement, which hampers standalone reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide principled procedures for selecting θ (e.g., prior elicitation, cross-validated predictive checks, or calibration to near-independence magnitudes), and report sensitivity curves for bounds vs θ.
- Add theoretical results on monotonicity and continuity of bounds in θ, and finite-sample coverage properties under the Bayesian procedure.
- Include scalability experiments (vary |W|, |Z|, and sample size), report computational cost, and compare back-substitution vs full LP in accuracy/runtime.
- Compare against alternative bounding approaches (e.g., Balke-Pearl, Manski-style bounds, or recent confounding-bounds methods) across a range of data-generating scenarios.
- Clarify the role of temporal ordering assumptions and discuss robustness when W or Z may include descendants of X or Y.
- Expand the real-data analysis with multiple witnesses/admissible sets and a thorough sensitivity analysis to θ.

Overall assessment
Technically sound and novel in combining structure discovery with partial identification via tunable relaxations, with promising empirical behavior. Clarity is acceptable but could be improved, and practical guidance and theoretical calibration of the relaxation parameters are needed to maximize impact. The work is within NeurIPS scope (causal inference, structure learning, optimization) and has potential significance if the above issues are addressed.
