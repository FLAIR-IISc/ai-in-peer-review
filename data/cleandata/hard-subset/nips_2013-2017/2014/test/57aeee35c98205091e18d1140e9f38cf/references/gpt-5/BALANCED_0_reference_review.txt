Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y from observational data when full back-door adjustment is not justified and instrumental variables (IV) may be imperfect. Building on Entner et al.’s Rule 1 (faithfulness-based discovery of admissible adjustment sets), the authors invert its use: rather than discarding a “witness” W after finding an admissible set Z, they treat W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z, and derive bounds on ACE. Key ideas include:
- Introducing explicit relaxation parameters that quantify bounded violations of faithfulness/conditional independence and of the IV assumptions: w for the effect of W on Y via X, y and x for discrepancies between structural and observed conditional probabilities of Y|X,W and X|W, and β, β̄ to bound the strength of selection on latent U by W.
- Framing the resulting constraints as a linear program over a latent-variable parameterization (following Dawid and Ramsahai) to obtain ACE bounds that interpolate between standard IV bounds (Balke–Pearl) and point identification under back-door adjustment.
- A Bayesian strategy to propagate sampling uncertainty: a constrained Dirichlet prior/posterior over P(Y,X,W|Z) (and P(Z)), with rejection sampling to respect the inequality constraints, and a simple “falsification” check based on posterior rejection rates.
- Derivation of algebraic (symbolic) bounds as functions of the relaxation parameters, and a “back-substitution” (message-passing-like) algorithm that yields fast, though conservative, bounds without solving many LPs.
Empirically, they study synthetic settings (with/without a valid witness; “hard”/“easy” cases) and a real influenza vaccination dataset. The method often yields informative intervals that are tighter than standard IV bounds and more robust than naive back-door adjustments.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling framework is thoughtful and connects several strands of work (IV bounds; inequality constraints for causal models; discovery under faithfulness). The LP formulation and the symbolic relaxations are reasonable and the inclusion of β, β̄ is a neat way to model limited selection on U. However, several aspects would benefit from stronger guarantees and empirical checks:
  - Coverage: There is no theorem or systematic simulation study demonstrating that, under the relaxed model (bounded violations governed by w, x, y, β, β̄) and finite samples, the posterior bounds achieve near-nominal coverage of the true ACE. The falsification test (reject if >95% of proposals are rejected) is ad hoc.
  - Hyperparameter calibration: The bounds depend critically on w, x, y, β, β̄. The paper acknowledges this and defers to the supplement, but the main text lacks a principled, data-informed or domain-informed calibration strategy (e.g., mapping observed near-independences to plausible parameter ranges).
  - Estimation and selection effects: Searching over many (W,Z) pairs with BDeu to detect approximate independencies, then conditioning on the highest-scoring pair to report bounds, can induce bias. There is no correction for multiplicity or model selection uncertainty in the final reported interval.
  - Efficiency: Rejection sampling for constrained Dirichlet posteriors may be inefficient in higher dimensions; convergence diagnostics are not discussed.
- Experiments: Synthetic setups are useful, but they are crafted so that adjustment on all covariates is wrong and a witness often exists; comparisons are limited to NE1/NE2 and a faithfulness-based point estimate. Missing are baselines such as: (i) standard IV bounds conditional on Z (the same conditioning the method uses), (ii) alternative partial identification/sensitivity analyses (e.g., Manski-style or bounds that cap confounding), and (iii) the full Dawid/Ramsahai LP without the proposed relaxations to isolate the contribution of WPP. Coverage and width–bias trade-offs under controlled deviations from assumptions would be very informative. The single real dataset is helpful but limited.

Clarity
The paper is dense and leans heavily on the supplement. Some notation is hard to follow (η, ζ, κ, ω with ? superscripts; missing equations referenced as (7)–(10)). Algorithmic details (search over Z, scoring thresholds, handling multiple witnesses, posterior summarization of intervals) are under-specified in the main text. A small running example would help.

Originality
The conceptual inversion of Rule 1 to generate candidate instruments and the quantitative relaxation of faithfulness/IV assumptions are novel. The algebraic bounds and the back-substitution procedure are also interesting contributions that could be useful beyond this specific framework.

Significance
If refined, WPP could become a practically appealing bridge between discovery (faithfulness-based CI tests) and partial identification (IV bounds), offering analysts a tunable spectrum between point estimates and worst-case IV bounds. The idea of bounding controlled violations is timely and relevant. However, the current paper falls short on guidance, validation, and clarity needed for broad adoption.

Pros
- Clear, relevant problem: bounding ACE with limited, data-driven structure.
- Novel and principled relaxation of faithfulness/IV assumptions with interpretable parameters.
- Integration of LP-based bounds with symbolic approximations and a fast bounding algorithm.
- Bayesian treatment to propagate sampling uncertainty.
- Open-source code.

Cons
- Heavy dependence on user-chosen relaxation parameters; limited guidance or calibration.
- No coverage guarantees or systematic sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Model selection over (W,Z) with approximate independencies risks invalid bounds; no multiplicity control.
- Experimental baselines and diagnostics are insufficient; limited real-data validation.
- Exposition is hard to follow; many crucial details deferred to the supplement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical or empirical coverage studies under controlled violations governed by (w, x, y, β, β̄); report width vs coverage curves.
- Propose principled calibration strategies linking observed near-independences to parameter ranges (e.g., mapping observed |P(Y|X,W=w)−P(Y|X,W=w′)| to w, and similarly for x, y), and/or elicit domain priors.
- Compare against stronger baselines: conditional IV bounds given Z; Dawid/Ramsahai LP without relaxations; recent confounding-bounds methods.
- Address model selection: aggregate bounds across plausible (W,Z) with posterior weights, or report union bounds to account for multiplicity.
- Improve clarity: define all symbols in-line; include the key inequalities in the main text; add a didactic toy example; detail the back-substitution algorithm and its complexity.
- Discuss scalability and rejection rates; consider alternative constrained posterior sampling methods (e.g., hit-and-run, HMC with constraints).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper proposes an interesting and potentially impactful framework with genuinely new ideas, but the current version lacks sufficient validation, guidance on hyperparameters, stronger baselines, and clarity. With the suggested additions, it could be a strong submission in a future round.
