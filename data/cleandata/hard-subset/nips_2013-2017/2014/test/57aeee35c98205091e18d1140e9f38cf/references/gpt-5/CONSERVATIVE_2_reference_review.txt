Summary
This paper tackles partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) when neither back-door adjustment nor a clean instrumental variable (IV) is available. The authors propose the Witness Protection Program (WPP): invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 so that (W,Z) pairs discovered from conditional independencies are treated as “protected” quasi-IVs. They introduce bounded relaxations of faithfulness via constraints on local conditional distributions (parameters w, x, y, β, β̄), and derive ACE bounds by linear programming that extend Dawid/Balke-Pearl/Ramsahai style formulations. They also present (i) a Bayesian approach with constrained Dirichlet priors to account for sampling variability and model falsification, and (ii) analytical, symbolic bounds and a back-substitution/message-passing algorithm that approximates the LP more efficiently. Experiments include synthetic studies and an influenza encouragement-design dataset.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on and connects several strands: Entner et al.’s faithfulness-based admissible set discovery, classical IV bounds (Balke–Pearl), Dawid/Ramsahai LP formulations for binary IV models, and faithfulness/causal discovery literature (PC). The novelty lies in inverting Rule 1 to generate candidate IV-like settings and relaxing faithfulness via bounded violations, then obtaining bounds under these relaxations. The algebraic back-substitution method is a pragmatic contribution.

Strengths
- Important problem: robust causal inference from observational data when neither full adjustment nor clean IVs are plausible.
- Bridging ideas: unifies conditional independence-based discovery with partial identification/bounding, offering a continuum between “pure faithfulness point estimates” and “conservative IV bounds.”
- Methodological toolbox: LP formulation with explicit relaxation parameters, a Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet treatment to reflect sampling uncertainty and detect infeasible models, and faster algebraic/back-substitution bounds.
- Code availability and some empirical illustration.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Choice and calibration of relaxation parameters: The core assumptions (3)–(6) introduce user-chosen tolerances (w, x, y, β, β̄). The paper provides little principled guidance for setting these, limited sensitivity analyses, and no coverage guarantees conditioned on specific choices. Without calibration or elicitation procedures (expert/prior, falsifiable tests, empirical calibration), the practical meaning of the bounds is unclear.
- Validation against established baselines: Experiments compare mainly to naïve estimators and Entner et al.’s method. Missing are systematic comparisons to:
  - Standard IV bounds applied per stratum of Z then averaged (a baseline the method explicitly subsumes).
  - Other partial identification approaches (e.g., Manski-style monotonicity or monotone IV, where applicable).
  - Graphical/inequality-constraint methods that yield testable implications.
  Reporting coverage and width across these would be informative.
- Post-selection inference and multiple testing: W and Z are discovered from the same data using BDeu model selection, then used to produce bounds on the same sample. There is no sample splitting, cross-fitting, or post-selection correction. The ad-hoc falsification rule (reject if >95% of posterior proposals are infeasible) lacks theoretical justification. This raises risks of overfitting and anti-conservative conclusions.
- Bayesian component: The constrained Dirichlet/rejection sampler ignores the difficulty of high-dimensional constraints and may be inefficient; more importantly, the constraints themselves depend on estimated quantities, again blurring the line between prior and data-driven constraint-setting. No analysis of posterior calibration (e.g., frequentist coverage of resulting bounds) is given.
- Back-substitution algorithm: While computationally appealing, it adds another relaxation layer. The paper asserts the additional widening is modest but provides limited quantitative comparisons to the exact LP (no formal bounds on approximation error or systematic empirical evaluation across settings).
- Experimental design and metrics: The core accuracy metric (“distance to interval”) can favor trivially wide intervals. Although the paper acknowledges this, the evaluation does not systematically report coverage-width trade-offs, calibration, or decision-relevant summaries (e.g., average interval width at fixed coverage). Synthetic setups appear tailored (e.g., NE1 guaranteed wrong), but robustness across broader graph classes and confounding strengths is not shown. Real-data analysis (influenza) demonstrates narrower bounds than standard IV but relies on data-driven witness/admissible set selection and additional assumptions; domain justification and placebo/stability checks are limited.
- Claims and clarity: The claim that WPP bounds are “at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted point estimator of [6]” needs a precise formal statement and proof in the main text. Several key derivations and inequalities are deferred to the Supplement; references to equations (7–10) and tables not shown hamper readability. Guidance on scalability when |Z| is larger than “modest” is limited.

Quality
The approach is technically interesting and mostly sound as a proposal, but key aspects (parameter calibration, post-selection validity, calibration/coverage of bounds, and rigorous empirical comparisons) are underdeveloped. Theoretical guarantees directly tying choices of (w, x, y, β, β̄) to guaranteed coverage would substantially strengthen the work.

Clarity
The paper is dense and at times hard to follow without the Supplement. More self-contained exposition of the main inequalities, explicit statement of the optimization problems, and clearer discussion of the computational pipeline and its approximations would help.

Originality
Moderately high: inverting Rule 1 to produce IV-like bounds under bounded faithfulness violations, plus the algebraic/back-substitution machinery, is novel to my knowledge.

Significance
Potentially useful if the calibration and validation issues are addressed. As it stands, the practical impact is limited by the difficulty of choosing and justifying relaxation parameters and by the lack of post-selection validity and comprehensive baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide principled procedures for choosing (w, x, y, β, β̄): e.g., expert elicitation protocols, sensitivity curves, or data-driven calibration with coverage guarantees under stated assumptions.
- Add post-selection safeguards: sample splitting or cross-fitting for witness/admissible set selection; control for multiple testing; report stability across random splits.
- Report frequentist coverage and width across a grid of relaxation parameters on diverse synthetic graphs (including cases where no valid witness exists).
- Compare against standard IV bounds conditional on Z, Manski-type assumptions where applicable, and recent inequality-based methods; report calibration and informativeness.
- Quantify the approximation gap of back-substitution to exact LP across regimes.
- Make the main inequalities and proofs more self-contained; clarify the “conservativeness” claim.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents interesting ideas and a potentially valuable bridge between faithfulness-based discovery and IV bounding, but the empirical and theoretical validation is not yet convincing enough, particularly given the central role of user-chosen relaxation parameters and post-selection issues. I encourage a revision focusing on calibration, coverage, and rigorous comparisons. Confidence: high.
