- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to bound average causal effects (ACE) while allowing limited departures from faithfulness via relaxation parameters and linear programming.  
- Topic is timely and of broad interest.  
- Method is principled: explicitly quantifies mild unfaithful interactions and uses familiar optimization to obtain ACE bounds.  
- Novel synthesis of known techniques and related to recent work, potentially cross-pollinating multiple lines of research.  
- Practical relevance is unclear because the plausibility of the unfaithful instances parameterized by the relaxations is unspecified.  
- Many relaxation choices produce very wide intervals, limiting practical utility.  
- WPP intentionally avoids committing to a point estimate, but readers may nonetheless treat intervals as point estimates.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: the “bias” metric compares fundamentally different quantities and is not a meaningful baseline comparison.  
- Influenza application yields an ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64), which is essentially non-actionable.  
- Technically sound overall, but many details are deferred to the supplement (not verified by reviewer).  
- Guidance on choosing relaxation parameters is postponed, making practical deployment difficult to anticipate.  
- Empirical evaluation lacks balance, robustness, and sensitivity analyses (e.g., smaller sample sizes).  
- Writing is generally clear, but the conclusion and some passages are problematic or emotive.  
- Algorithm variants and parameterizations are hard to track; main text may be insufficient for implementation without the supplement.  
- Need better intuition and interpretation for the relaxation/constraint parameters to avoid misreading them as implying only weak interactions.  
- Some implicit assumptions (e.g., causal sufficiency or W→X) should be made explicit.  
- Connection between the search phase (witness/admissible Z selection) and bound computation under relaxations needs clearer explanation.  
- Clarify whether “at most 10 free parameters” refers to parameters describing Z rather than number of covariates.  
- Prior related work (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf UAI 2014) should be cited and discussed.  
- The back-substitution step for tightening bounds may have limited practical impact; quantify its effectiveness empirically.  
- Multiple admissible sets can produce multiple bounds—clarify how this arises (e.g., multiple witnesses).  
- Concerns that reported WPP “stability” may reflect excessively loose bounds rather than meaningful robustness.  
- Emphasize that intervals are not confidence bands around a best guess; interpretation must be carefully communicated.  
- Numerous minor wording, clarity, and typographical issues throughout (specific page/line comments provided).
