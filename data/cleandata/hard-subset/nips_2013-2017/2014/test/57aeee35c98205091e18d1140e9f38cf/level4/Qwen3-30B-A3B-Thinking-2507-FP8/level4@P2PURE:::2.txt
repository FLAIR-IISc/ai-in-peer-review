

Quality  
This paper proposes a novel approach for deriving bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) from variable X to Y. The method relies on three key principles: (1) applying Entner's Rule 1 in its inverse direction, (2) relaxing the faithfulness assumption to permit path cancellation, and (3) incorporating inequality constraints as additional assumptions. These principles enable the formulation of a linear programming-based computation scheme for ACE bounds. Although the authors have not yet optimized the algorithm for bound derivation, the resulting bounds are remarkably tight and accurate compared to state-of-the-art approaches.  

The paper establishes a novel method for deriving tighter ACE bounds than existing techniques by combining feasible principles, yielding theoretically interesting contributions. However, two critical gaps require clarification. First, the validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not sufficiently justified. The authors must explicitly assume the structural configuration illustrated in Figure 2 and discuss its necessity. Second, the paper lacks generic criteria for selecting relaxation parameters, which significantly impact bound quality. The principle for determining these parameters should be rigorously investigated and incorporated.  

Clarity  
The theoretical exposition is largely comprehensive, except for the unresolved issues noted above. The experimental section suffers from inadequate description: the algorithm implementation details are incomplete, and the parameters referenced in Table 1 (likely intended as Table 1, not Table 5) are ambiguously defined. Additionally, the following typos require correction:  
- "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1" (p. 6) contradicts the absence of an algorithm table.  
- "Results are summarized in Table 5" (p. 8) appears to reference Table 1 instead.  

Originality  
While the problem setting aligns with prior work (as acknowledged by the authors), the paper's scope lacks strong originality. The unification of three distinct ideas into a cohesive framework contains some novelty, but this combination's validity remains inadequately justified, as previously highlighted.  

Significance  
The method demonstrates significance in deriving tighter ACE bounds, yet its performance is evaluated only through limited experiments. Theoretical generality and empirical robustness across diverse settings have not been rigorously assessed. Although the problem of bounding causal relationship strength is important, the paper must strengthen both theoretical justification and experimental validation to substantiate its claims.
