This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel algorithm for computing bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) while accounting for potential violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The core idea is to introduce a set of relaxation parameters that constrain the extent of unfaithful interactions. A linear programming framework is then employed to solve for the upper and lower bounds on the ACE, conditioned on an appropriate set of covariates identified subject to these constraints.

The problem of relaxing the faithfulness assumption is receiving considerable attention, making the paper both timely and relevant. The approach is commendable for its principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of unfaithful interaction patterns, leveraging well-established mathematical optimization tools.

Despite the interesting premise, the paper has several significant weaknesses, primarily concerning the practical utility and interpretation of the results, as well as an insufficient experimental evaluation.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Interpretability and Practical Utility of the Bounds:** A fundamental challenge lies in the interpretation of the algorithm's output. WPP produces a distribution of bounds that are a function of user-defined 'unfaithful relaxations.' However, the paper provides little guidance on how to assess the likelihood or realism of these relaxations, which in turn makes it difficult to judge the relevance of the resulting bounds. As the authors' own experiments show, many relaxation settings lead to bounds that are too wide to be practically informative. For instance, the result on the influenza dataset, ACE ∈ (-0.23, 0.64), is so broad that it precludes any meaningful causal conclusion. While this may be the most "honest" assessment possible under the relaxed assumptions, it questions the algorithm's utility for practitioners seeking actionable insights. Furthermore, the method does not commit to a preferred point estimate within the derived interval, yet there is a high risk that readers will misinterpret the bounds as a confidence interval around a central estimate. This critical point about interpretation is mentioned but should be more heavily emphasized.

2.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is not robust enough to demonstrate the value of the proposed method.
    *   The choice of 'bias' as a comparison metric is inappropriate. WPP computes bounds on the ACE, whereas the competing methods generate point estimates. Comparing them based on the bias of a point estimate is not a meaningful evaluation of WPP's contribution, as they are designed to solve different inferential tasks. This is highlighted by the trivial example where a method that always outputs zero would have the lowest bias, demonstrating the metric's inadequacy.
    *   The presentation of results is unclear. It is impossible to gauge the benefit of using WPP over a standard point estimate from Table 1.
    *   The claim that "WPP is quite stable" on unsolvable problems seems to be a euphemism for the fact that the algorithm produces very wide, uninformative bounds in those cases. This is not a desirable feature.
    *   The experiments are conducted on datasets with 5000 data points. Many real-world applications, especially in medicine, have far smaller sample sizes. It is crucial to understand how the width of the WPP bounds behaves with less data, as they are likely to become even wider and less useful.

**Quality and Clarity**

The technical approach appears sound, although key details are deferred to the supplementary material, which was not checked in detail for this review. The core ideas are introduced reasonably well, but the overall context is sometimes lost. A critical aspect—the selection of the relaxation parameters (θ)—is relegated to future work, which makes it difficult to fully assess the method and its potential pitfalls.

The paper is decently written, but the clarity could be improved. The description of the algorithmic steps, with their many subtly different parameters, is difficult to follow. A clearer explanation of how the search for admissible sets Z depends on the relaxation parameters would be beneficial. The paper also contains abrupt shifts in the level of abstraction (e.g., p4/172-180) without sufficient intuition for what the constraint parameters represent, making sections hard to follow for readers not already deeply familiar with the subject.

**Originality and Significance**

The method represents a novel combination of existing techniques and is sufficiently different from related recent work on this problem. The significance of the paper lies more in the ideas and direction it proposes than in its current implementation. It successfully demonstrates how optimization techniques can be integrated to tackle this challenging problem. While the WPP algorithm in its current form seems unlikely to be a finished tool for practitioners, its core ideas may prove influential and stimulate further research at the intersection of causal inference and sensitivity analysis.

**Summary of Recommendations**

This paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem with a technically interesting method. However, its contribution is significantly undermined by a weak and poorly justified experimental evaluation and serious open questions regarding the practical utility and interpretation of its output.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1.  Provide a more thorough and appropriate experimental evaluation. This should involve comparing the WPP bounds to credible intervals from Bayesian methods or confidence intervals from other approaches, rather than using an inappropriate 'bias' metric. The evaluation should also explore the method's sensitivity to sample size.
2.  Elaborate significantly on the interpretation of the bounds and the choice of relaxation parameters. Without this guidance, the method remains a theoretical curiosity rather than a practical tool.
3.  Improve the clarity of the algorithmic description and provide more intuition behind the mathematical formulations.
4.  Revise the language in the conclusion to be more objective and scientific, avoiding emotive phrases like "risky Bayesian approaches" or "more honest" inference, which suggest a biased perspective.

Overall, the paper presents a promising research direction, but the current manuscript does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of practical value.
