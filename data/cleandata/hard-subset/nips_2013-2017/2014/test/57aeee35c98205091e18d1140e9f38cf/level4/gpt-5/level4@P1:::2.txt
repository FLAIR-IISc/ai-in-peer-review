Summary
This paper proposes a method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y in observational studies. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a (W, Z) pair to justify back-door adjustment for point identification, the paper treats W as an IV-like “witness” and uses linear programming to produce ACE bounds conditional on Z. Faithfulness is relaxed to allow bounded violations (path cancellations), encoded as inequality constraints on local conditional distributions. The method integrates (i) IV bounding ideas (Balke–Pearl), (ii) linear programming formulations (Dawid; Ramsahai), and (iii) Bayesian inference over constraints to account for sampling uncertainty. For scalability, the authors derive symbolic algebraic bounds and introduce a back-substitution procedure that approximates the LP while retaining much of its tightness. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu vaccine dataset illustrate the approach.

Quality
Strengths:
- Conceptually sound compromise between strict faithfulness-based point estimation and IV-style worst-case bounds.
- Clear technical integration of existing building blocks (IV bounds, LP formulations) with a novel “witness protection” reinterpretation of Rule 1.
- Bayesian treatment that acknowledges uncertainty in testing conditional independences and in the induced constraints.
- Symbolic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm offer practical computational benefits and qualitative insight.

Weaknesses:
- The validity of using Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not adequately justified. To apply it this way, the paper needs to state explicit structural assumptions (e.g., a graph where W has no direct effect on Y given Z, and bounded dependence between W and unobservables that affect X and Y). This is essentially assuming the structure depicted in Fig. 2; this should be made explicit and carefully discussed, including when the inversion fails or becomes overly conservative.
- The paper does not provide generic criteria for choosing the relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄). Since these directly control the tightness of the bounds and encode the degree of faithfulness violation one is willing to tolerate, practical guidance, diagnostics, or priors for these parameters are essential.
- Some implementation details are insufficiently described. The definitions of the parameters in Table 1 (or Table 5, as referenced) are not clearly given. The Bayesian rejection-sampling procedure over constrained Dirichlet posteriors would benefit from more detail (e.g., acceptance rates, convergence diagnostics).
- The experimental evaluation, while suggestive, is limited. More comprehensive comparisons across a wider range of data-generating regimes and sensitivity analyses for the relaxation parameters would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- The theoretical development is generally clear, but the core justification for the inverted use of Rule 1 needs a dedicated, formal treatment, ideally with theorems specifying sufficient conditions under which the resulting bounds are valid and non-vacuous.
- The experimental section is not well documented. Key parameter definitions (Table 1/Table 5) are unclear, and the algorithmic steps are not fully traceable.
- There are presentation issues:
  - “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1” (p. 6): I do not see such a table. It appears to be Algorithm 1 in the main text.
  - “Results are summarized in Table 5” (p. 8): I do not see Table 5 (perhaps this refers to Table 1).
Please correct these references and ensure all tables/figures are present and labeled consistently.

Originality
- The problem is well studied, and the paper builds on established ideas (faithfulness, IV bounds, LP formulations, causal discovery rules). The primary novelty is the unified framework that (i) repurposes Rule 1 to generate IV-like bounding problems, (ii) relaxes faithfulness via bounded violations encoded as inequalities, and (iii) couples LP-based bounds with a Bayesian posterior over constraints and symbolic approximations. This combination has some originality, though the paper should more clearly delineate what is new versus adapted from prior work.

Significance
- Tight, robust bounds are valuable for observational studies, and the proposed approach could become a useful tool, especially when neither strong faithfulness assumptions nor pure IV assumptions are tenable. However, the current evidence base is limited: broader experiments and clearer parameter-selection guidance are needed to assess practical impact and reproducibility.
- If the authors can (a) formalize the conditions for the inverted Rule 1, (b) provide principled defaults or data-driven strategies for setting relaxation parameters, and (c) expand empirical validation, the method’s significance would be considerably higher.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal treatment of the inverted Rule 1: assumptions, sufficient conditions, and failure modes; relate to standard IV-identification conditions and to bounds under partial violations.
- Develop principled guidance for choosing relaxation parameters, including sensitivity analyses and possibly hierarchical priors or empirical Bayes strategies.
- Clarify all parameter definitions (especially those in the tables) and fully specify the Bayesian inference procedure (priors, constraints, proposal distributions, acceptance rates).
- Fix all table/figure references and ensure the algorithm is clearly presented.
- Expand experiments: include ablations (LP vs. back-substitution), robustness to approximate independencies, and comparisons with more recent bounding methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a thoughtful and practically motivated framework.
- Promising empirical tightness of bounds; scalable approximations and Bayesian uncertainty quantification.
- Potential to influence practice in observational studies.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient justification for the core inversion of Rule 1.
- Lack of guidance on selecting relaxation parameters.
- Limited experimental scope and some clarity/presentation issues.
