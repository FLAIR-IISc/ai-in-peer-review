

This paper introduces a novel method for deriving tight bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) from variable X to Y, leveraging three key principles: (1) the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1, (2) relaxation of the faithfulness assumption to permit path cancellation, and (3) inequality constraints to define bounds. The approach formulates ACE bounds via linear programming, yielding significantly narrower results than state-of-the-art methods. While promising, the paper requires substantial revision to clarify theoretical foundations and experimental validity.

**Quality & Theoretical Soundness**  
The core contribution—combining these principles into a unified framework for tighter ACE bounds—is theoretically interesting. However, two critical gaps undermine the method's credibility. First, the inverse use of Entner's Rule 1 lacks justification. The paper must explicitly state and validate the structural assumption depicted in Figure 2 (e.g., the absence of unobserved confounding paths between X and Y via Z), rather than treating it as implicit. Second, the relaxation parameters (e.g., tolerance for path cancellation) are not systematically defined. These parameters critically influence bound tightness, yet the paper provides no criteria for their selection, leaving the method's robustness unverified. The authors should address: *How do we choose relaxation parameters to ensure valid bounds without overfitting?*

**Clarity & Presentation**  
The theoretical exposition is generally sound but suffers from critical omissions. The algorithm description (referred to as "Table 1" on page 6) is absent from the paper, and experimental tables (e.g., "Table 5" on page 8) are mislabeled—likely intended as Table 1. Parameter definitions in Table 1 (e.g., *ε*, *δ*) are undefined, and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail (e.g., dataset sizes, causal graph structures used). Typos compound confusion (e.g., "path-cancellation" should be "path cancellation," "full algorithm" incorrectly references a non-existent table). The authors must: (1) correct all table references, (2) define all parameters explicitly, and (3) provide a complete algorithm description.

**Originality**  
The problem of ACE bounding is well-studied (as acknowledged by the authors), and the paper does not introduce a new problem setting. The *combination* of Entner’s Rule 1 (in reverse), relaxed faithfulness, and inequality constraints holds moderate originality, but this synthesis is not sufficiently justified. The paper fails to distinguish its approach from prior work (e.g., [cite relevant papers]), particularly regarding how path cancellation *differs* from existing relaxation techniques. Without a clear theoretical contrast, the novelty is overstated.

**Significance**  
The method’s potential to produce tighter ACE bounds is significant for causal inference, especially in scenarios where faithfulness is violated. However, the significance is currently undermined by limited evaluation. Experiments are confined to synthetic data with minimal graph diversity (e.g., only 3-node structures), and no real-world validation is provided. The paper claims generality but offers no analysis of how bounds behave under varying graph complexity, noise levels, or sample sizes. To demonstrate significance, the authors must: (1) test on diverse causal graphs (e.g., chains, forks, colliders), (2) include real-world datasets, and (3) rigorously analyze parameter sensitivity.

**Recommendation**  
This paper addresses a meaningful challenge in causal inference with a methodologically novel framework. However, the current version lacks the theoretical rigor, experimental completeness, and clarity required for acceptance. The core idea has merit but requires major revisions:  
1. **Theoretical**: Explicitly justify the inverse use of Entner’s Rule 1 via structural assumptions (Figure 2), and define a principled method for selecting relaxation parameters.  
2. **Clarity**: Correct all table references, define parameters, and provide a full algorithm description.  
3. **Evaluation**: Expand experiments to include diverse causal graphs, real-world data, and sensitivity analysis of key parameters.  
4. **Originality**: Sharpen the comparison with prior work to clarify the unique contribution.  

With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution. As-is, it risks being misinterpreted as a minor improvement rather than a substantive advance. **Weak accept pending major revisions.** The authors must address all theoretical and experimental gaps before the work can be deemed sound.
