Summary
This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is not fully specified. It builds on a rule from Entner et al. (2013) intended to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths, a rule originally justified only under the faithfulness assumption. The paper proposes to use this rule to produce bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE and develops a method following Ramsahai (2012).

Evaluation
Although I have not fully digested all the technical details, the main idea appears sound, interesting, and potentially useful. However, I am puzzled about the role of the conditions in Entner et al.’s rule within this framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, those conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that, if verified, the conditions help inform the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets that issue aside. As a result, it seems the machinery does not crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.’s rule. I also wonder whether the method’s apparent robustness in the experiments is related to this limited dependence. Clarification on this point would be appreciated.

Minor comments
1. First paragraph of Section 3: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” reads oddly. Unfaithfulness usually involves allowing additional independences, not violating independences. It seems the proposed framework is intended to allow or express “almost violations” of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.
2. In the experiments, it would be helpful to report the performance of Entner et al.’s method using their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as yielding uninformative bounds.
3. In the second paragraph on p. 8, if X=1 means the patient received a flu shot and Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized, why does a negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine?
4. “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is cited as “Table 5” in the text; please reconcile the numbering.

Overall
This is an interesting paper, though the details are not always easy to follow. Further clarification of the role of Entner et al.’s conditions—especially in the absence of faithfulness and in relation to the choice of relaxation parameters—would strengthen the contribution.
