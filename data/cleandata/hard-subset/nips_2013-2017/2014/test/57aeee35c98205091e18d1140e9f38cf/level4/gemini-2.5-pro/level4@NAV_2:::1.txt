This paper introduces a novel methodology to estimate bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies where the causal structure is not known. The approach builds upon the work of Entner et al. (2013), which proposed a rule based on the faithfulness assumption to identify a set of covariates sufficient for back-door adjustment. Rather than yielding a point estimate that is sensitive to faithfulness violations, this paper repurposes the rule's output to identify candidate instrumental variables. The authors relax the faithfulness assumption by modeling "path cancellations" as bounded violations of conditional independence. This formulation allows them to derive a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE using a linear programming framework that extends the work of Ramsahai (2012) and Dawid (2003).

**Strengths:**
- The paper tackles a fundamental and challenging problem in causal inference: estimating effects from observational data when key assumptions like faithfulness may not hold.
- The core idea of providing robust bounds on the ACE by relaxing, rather than strictly adhering to, the faithfulness assumption is a novel and significant contribution. It offers a practical and well-reasoned compromise between assumption-laden point estimators and potentially uninformative bounds from standard instrumental variable analyses.
- The proposed "Witness Protection Program" framework is theoretically sound and appears useful for applied researchers. The development of a computationally efficient "back-substitution" algorithm based on symbolic bounds further enhances the method's practical applicability.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear justification for using Entner et al.'s Rule 1 as the search heuristic in a setting where the faithfulness assumption is explicitly relaxed.
- The conditions in Rule 1 (W \⊥ Y |Z and W ⊥ Y |Z ∪ {X}) have clear implications for the causal structure only under faithfulness. Once this assumption is dropped, the rationale for using these specific conditional independence tests to find promising (W,Z) pairs is not obvious. The paper defers the choice of relaxation parameters, but the motivation for the search procedure itself needs to be strengthened. Could the authors provide a more detailed theoretical or intuitive argument for why this heuristic remains effective?
- The method shows strong empirical performance, which is encouraging. However, it is difficult to determine if this robustness is a direct result of the proposed search heuristic or a feature of the general bounding machinery, which might be robust to the choice of candidate (W,Z) pairs. Clarifying this relationship would help in understanding the method's properties.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  The phrasing in Section 3, "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is potentially confusing. Unfaithfulness typically implies the existence of an independence relation despite a connecting path in the graph. The proposed framework seems designed to allow for weak dependencies where faithfulness would imply strict independence. It might be clearer to frame this as relaxing the strict independence constraints implied by a simpler model.
2.  The experimental comparison could be made more comprehensive by including the original Entner et al. method as a baseline, using their suggested stringent thresholds. Cases where their method returns "I don't know" could be treated as the uninformative interval [-1, 1], providing a clearer contrast with the bounds produced by the proposed method.
3.  In the description of the influenza study, there appears to be a contradiction. If X=1 denotes receiving a flu shot and Y=1 denotes *not* being hospitalized, a desirable vaccine would be associated with a positive ACE. The text states that a negative ACE indicates a desirable vaccine. Please clarify the definition of the outcome variable Y and the corresponding interpretation of the ACE.
4.  There appears to be a typo on page 8, where "Table 1" is referred to as "Table 5".

**Overall Assessment:**
This is an interesting paper with a well-motivated and potentially impactful contribution to the practice of observational causal inference. The primary concern is the justification for the search procedure in the absence of the faithfulness assumption. If the authors can convincingly address this point and the minor issues raised, this would be a strong paper for the conference.
