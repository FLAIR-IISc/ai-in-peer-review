The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, designed to compute bounds on the inferred average causal effect (ACE) while accounting for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The method achieves this by incorporating relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and then employing a linear programming framework to determine upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusting for a suitable set of covariates subject to these constraints.

This is a timely and interesting contribution, as the problem of robustness to unfaithfulness is currently receiving considerable attention. The approach is principled, representing a deliberate attempt to quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns using established mathematical tools for optimization. 

However, the practical interpretability of the results is questionable. The output is a distribution over bounds that are functions of the ‘unfaithful relaxations’ relative to a given causal model, but the likelihood of these unfaithful instances – and consequently, the relevance of the resulting bounds – remains unclear. Many relaxations yield wide bounds, potentially diminishing their utility. Furthermore, the method does not commit to a specific point estimate within the calculated interval, despite the likelihood that users will interpret it as such.

The experimental evaluation is weak. The ‘bias’ metric used to compare WPP with competing methods is not meaningful, as they assess fundamentally different quantities. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output represents a valuable addition for practitioners or a complex and ultimately unproductive complication. The performance on the influenza dataset is particularly concerning, with an ACE in the range of (-0.23, 0.64) offering limited actionable conclusions, although this may be a fair reflection of the inherent uncertainty.

**Quality:**

The technical foundation of the proposed approach is sound, with many details provided in the supplementary material (which I have not fully reviewed). The ideas are well-introduced, although the broader context is occasionally lost. Crucially, the selection of relaxation parameters – a key aspect of the method – is deferred to a future work, hindering a complete assessment of potential issues. The experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written (with the exception of the conclusion, see comments below). However, the algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving numerous parameters were difficult to follow, partly due to a lack of detailed explanation. While I believe I understand the overall process, a complete implementation would likely require the supplementary material.

**Originality:**

The method appears to be a novel combination of existing techniques, and while related to recent work addressing the same problem, it is sufficiently distinct to potentially stimulate cross-fertilization of ideas.

**Significance:**

The significance of this work lies primarily in the ideas and direction it offers for tackling this challenging problem, demonstrating how to integrate other techniques into the solution. In its current form, it is unlikely to be a definitive tool for practitioners, although the concepts employed in the WPP algorithm may prove influential.

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem. It presents an interesting method that will likely attract attention. While current applications are limited, the approach has the potential to extend to more general cases. The writing is reasonably clear, though improvements are possible. The technical solutions are interesting, but the experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
