The paper describes the so-called Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which calculates the limit based on an assumed average causal effectiveness (ACE). This algorithm considers the potential for violating the standardized reliability assumption of causal inference.

It works by introducing some relaxation parameters, which limit untrustworthy interactions. Then, by adjusting a suitable conjugate set, these limits, subject to these constraints, use a linear programming framework to solve the resulting high and low limits of the ACE for a specific link.

This problem is currently gaining considerable attention, so this article is both timely and interesting. I liked the methods and concepts presented in the article: it is a principled attempt to clearly measure the impact of (weak) untrustworthy interaction patterns, combined with well-known mathematical tools for solving related optimization/minimization problems.

Whether they have found the right method to capture this effect remains to be seen. The resulting output is difficult to interpret from a conceptual perspective: a distribution over the limits as a function of a specific model's relative 'untrustworthy relaxations'... but how likely these untrustworthy examples are, and at the same time, how relevant/realistic these limits are, is still unclear. Many relaxations create limits that are so broad that they may become less useful. The method is not committed to estimating a preferred point within the interval... although people will probably interpret it that way.

Experimental evaluation weak: The 'bias' evaluation metric is meaningless when comparing the WPP method with its competitors, as they literally measure two completely different things. As a result, whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or simply a complex confusion is unclear. Also, practical performance on influenza datasets is disturbingly weak: (-0.23,0.64) does not indicate any relevant conclusion... although this may be the fairest evaluation of them all.

Quality
The method presented in the paper is technically sound, many details are explained in the supplementary file (I did not examine any details there). The concepts are well presented, although the overall context is sometimes lost. Important aspects such as choosing relaxation parameters have been moved to future articles, which makes it difficult to get an idea of potential problems with this part of the method. Experimental evaluation is not strong enough or strong enough.

Clarity
Well written enough (see comments below, except for the conclusion). It was quite difficult to follow the algorithmic steps and follow the subsequent changes in terms of many subtly different parameters, partly due to the lack of some details in the method. (I think I understand what is happening, but I am not sure that I can fully implement it based only on this description; the supplement may be sufficient.)

Originality
As far as I can tell, the method is a new combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related, but quite different from recent work on the same problem. For this reason, it may stimulate the fruitful exchange of several methods on the subject.

Importance
The significance of the paper mainly lies in the concepts and directions, which show how other techniques can be incorporated to solve this very challenging problem. In its current implementation, it is unlikely to present a finished article for practitioners, although some concepts used in the WPP algorithm may eventually play an important role.

Summary:
Relevant and very topical problem. Interesting method that will undoubtedly find an audience. Current application is limited, but likely to expand to other, more general cases. Reasonably well written, although could be improved. Technically interesting solution. Experimental evaluation weak. Relevance and correct interpretation of the output distribution over the limits is unclear.
