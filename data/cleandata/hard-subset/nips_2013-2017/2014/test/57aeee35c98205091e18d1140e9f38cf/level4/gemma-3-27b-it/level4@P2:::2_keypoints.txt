- Proposes a novel method to bound the average causal effect (ACE) by inverting Entner’s Rule 1 and relaxing faithfulness to allow path cancellations while permitting confounding.  
- Frames the problem as a linear program constrained by inequalities representing the relaxed assumptions.  
- Demonstrates tighter ACE bounds than existing methods on synthetic and one real-world dataset.  
- Computationally intensive but offers relaxations and an efficient back-substitution algorithm to improve tractability.  
- Solid theoretical motivation and thoughtful combination of instrumental variables, Entner’s covariate selection, and faithfulness relaxations.  
- Key strength: demonstrated tightness of resulting ACE bounds.  
- Major theoretical concern: inversion of Entner’s Rule 1 lacks rigorous justification and an explicit statement of required causal structure.  
- Method introduces multiple relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) but provides no principled guidance for choosing them.  
- Lacks thorough sensitivity analysis and strategies for selecting relaxation parameters.  
- Experimental description is under-detailed: algorithm implementation and parameter settings (e.g., in Table 3) are not adequately explained.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited in breadth and does not assess generalizability across different confounding levels or causal structures.  
- Scalability to high-dimensional datasets is not demonstrated and remains a concern.  
- Minor clarity issues and typographical errors (incorrect table references) detract from presentation.  
- Recommendation: weak accept pending clearer justification of the inverted Entner’s Rule 1 and more comprehensive empirical validation.
