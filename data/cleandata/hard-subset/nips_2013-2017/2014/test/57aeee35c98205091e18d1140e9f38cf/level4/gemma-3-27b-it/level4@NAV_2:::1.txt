This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in the presence of unobserved confounding, relaxing the reliance on fully specified causal structures. It builds upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) for identifying sufficient covariate sets to block back-door paths, typically justified under the faithfulness assumption. The authors propose to leverage this rule for bounding the ACE, rather than obtaining point estimates, and develop a method rooted in the work of Ramsahai (2012).

The core idea – utilizing conditional independence constraints to derive bounds on the ACE, even in the presence of potential violations of faithfulness – is both interesting and potentially valuable. The approach offers a pragmatic compromise between strict faithfulness-based assumptions and purely theory-driven analyses. I found the development of the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) framework and the discussion of relaxations within the linear programming formulation to be conceptually sound. 

However, a central point requires further clarification. While the paper explicitly builds upon Entner et al.’s Rule 1, the role of the conditions within that rule – specifically regarding conditional independencies – isn’t entirely clear given the relaxation of the faithfulness assumption. Without faithfulness, these conditions don't directly imply specific characteristics of the causal structure. The paper mentions a connection to the choice of relaxation parameters, but largely postpones detailed discussion of this aspect. It is unclear whether the method’s performance is critically dependent on satisfying these conditions, or whether the robustness observed in the experiments is independent of them. Elaborating on this relationship would significantly strengthen the paper. 

Several minor points could improve clarity: 
1. In Section 3, the phrasing “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…” could be refined. Unfaithfulness concerns *additional* independence relationships, rather than violations of existing ones. The framework seems to allow for “near violations” of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependencies.
2. The experimental comparison would benefit from including Entner et al.’s original method, using their stringent thresholds for Rule 1 and reporting “uninformative bounds” (i.e., a very wide interval) when the criteria aren't met. This would provide a direct baseline for assessing the advantage of the proposed approach.
3. On page 8, the description of the influenza vaccination experiment seems to have an inconsistency: if Y=1 indicates *not* being hospitalized, then a negative ACE would suggest a *harmful* vaccine, not a desirable one.
4. There’s a citation error on page 8: “Table 1” is referenced as “Table 5” in the text.

Despite these points, this is a worthwhile contribution. The technical details are complex, and full comprehension requires careful study, but the paper presents a compelling approach to a difficult problem in causal inference. The potential for providing more robust and informative estimates of causal effects, particularly in observational settings, is significant. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to bounding causal effects under relaxed assumptions.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem in observational studies.
*   Presents a well-defined framework (WPP) with potential for further development.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on synthetic and real data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection between Entner et al.’s Rule 1 and the proposed method needs further clarification.
*   Some technical details are difficult to follow and could benefit from increased clarity.
*   Minor inconsistencies and errors in the presentation.
