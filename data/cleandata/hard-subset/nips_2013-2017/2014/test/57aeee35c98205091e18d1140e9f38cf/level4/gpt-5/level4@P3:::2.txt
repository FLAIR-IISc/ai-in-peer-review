Summary
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary treatment X on a binary outcome Y in observational studies when confounding may be present. The approach reverses the use of Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using (W, Z) pairs to assert back-door admissibility and return a point estimate under faithfulness, the paper treats W as an “instrument-like” variable conditional on Z and derives bounds on the ACE. Faithfulness is relaxed via bounded “approximate independences,” encoded as inequalities on local conditional probabilities with relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The bounds are obtained via linear programming in the spirit of Dawid and Ramsahai, generalizing the Balke–Pearl IV bounds; an analytical back-substitution method provides a faster approximation. A Bayesian treatment with constrained Dirichlet priors yields a posterior over bounds, accounting for sampling uncertainty. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination dataset suggest that WPP can yield tighter bounds than standard IV while being more conservative than purely faithfulness-based point estimators.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on: (i) IV bounds for binary models (Balke & Pearl; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins), (ii) causal discovery and faithfulness (Spirtes–Glymour–Scheines; PC), (iii) admissible set discovery (Entner et al.), and (iv) partial identification and conservative inference (Manski). The contribution is to blend faithfulness-driven conditional independencies with IV-style bounding, thereby interpolating between point identification under strong assumptions and very wide bounds under IV alone.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Turning a “witness” into an instrument-like variable conditional on Z is an elegant inversion of Rule 1 that pragmatically acknowledges approximate, not exact, independencies.
- Controlled relaxation of faithfulness: The parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) offer interpretable knobs for bounding violations, mapping cleanly to known special cases (standard IV; chain W → X → Y; back-door).
- Sound optimization setup: The LP formulation extends known techniques and is technically well-motivated. The algebraic/back-substitution bounds provide intuition and substantial computational gains.
- Bayesian uncertainty quantification: Using constrained Dirichlet priors respects the partially identified nature of the problem and avoids fragile priors on latent variable models.
- Empirical indications: On synthetic and influenza data, WPP produces bounds narrower than standard IV while remaining conservative relative to naive back-door adjustments.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
Quality (technical soundness)
- Inverse use of Rule 1: The paper should more formally justify treating a (W, Z) pair that satisfies Rule 1 as defining an “approximately valid” conditional IV. What assumptions beyond causal order and “W, Z not descendants of X or Y” are required? Under what conditions can small violations in Rule 1’s premises be translated into specific ranges for (w, y, x, β, β̄)? Please provide a proposition spelling out the sufficient conditions and a discussion of false positives when approximate independences are used.
- Choice and calibration of relaxation parameters: The results are highly sensitive to (w, y, x, β, β̄). Beyond the Supplementary discussion, the main text should provide principled guidance (e.g., mapping from effect sizes of near-zero partial correlations to parameter ranges; elicitation templates for domain experts; default priors; and a sensitivity analysis protocol). Monotonicity of bound width with respect to each parameter should be stated and proven or illustrated.
- Guarantees and tightness: Please state formal properties: (i) reduction to standard IV and to back-door under the appropriate limiting parameters; (ii) coverage guarantees under the stated relaxations; (iii) a characterization of the tightness gap between the LP and the algebraic/back-substitution bounds; and (iv) conditions under which back-substitution may accept infeasible points or reject feasible ones.
- Model falsification: The “95% rejection” heuristic for the constrained posterior sampling is ad hoc. Clarify its operating characteristics (type I/II error) and contrast with established tests (e.g., Ramsahai & Lauritzen likelihood methods). Report how often falsification occurs in experiments and what is concluded in those cases.

Clarity (presentation)
- Notation and definitions: Clearly define all parameters appearing in Table 1 (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω), and the role of Z when “suppressed” in notation. Distinguish observed cell probabilities from latent/aggregated quantities. Explain the mapping steps in the LP (extreme points → dual polytope) at a higher level and reference explicit constraints in the Supplementary.
- Algorithm references and table numbering: The paper refers to “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1” and “Results are summarized in Table 5,” but the main text appears to show Algorithm 1 and not Table 5. Please fix numbering and ensure all referenced tables/algorithms/figures are present.
- Implementation details: Provide pseudocode for the Bayesian pipeline (priors, proposal, rejection sampling, falsification test) and for the back-substitution routine. Report computational complexity and typical runtimes.

Originality
- The problem is well-studied, but the unification—reinterpreting witnesses as conditional IVs with explicit, tunable relaxations and a posterior over bounds—has clear novelty. Strengthen the comparison to closely related “bounding confounding” approaches (e.g., Geiger et al., 2014) and admissible set criteria (VanderWeele & Shpitser).

Significance
- The approach is promising for practice, offering a principled compromise between optimistic point estimates and pessimistic IV bounds. However, the current empirical evaluation is limited. Please add:
  - Coverage and width: Report empirical coverage of the true ACE and average interval width across settings; include near-faithful and unfaithful regimes.
  - Sensitivity: Systematically vary (w, y, x, β, β̄) and show width/coverage trade-offs.
  - Scalability: Benchmarks for LP vs back-substitution on larger Z and on multiple (W, Z) candidates.
  - Additional baselines: Compare against standard covariate selection heuristics and recent partial-identification methods.
  - Flu case study: Explain why GRP fails as a witness under WPP and discuss plausibility of DM/AGE/SEX as Z; report posterior distributions over bounds, not only expectations, and compare to naive adjusted ACE.

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative synthesis of causal discovery signals with partial identification.
- Practical and transparent control of faithfulness violations.
- Bayesian treatment appropriate for partially identified settings.
- Evidence of tighter bounds than IV and robustness to approximate independencies.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the inverse use of Rule 1 and for parameter calibration.
- Clarity issues (notation, table/algorithm references) and sparse implementation details.
- Limited empirical validation and sensitivity analysis.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and timely contribution that could impact observational causal inference practice. I would support acceptance if the authors strengthen the theoretical justification for the inverted Rule 1, provide principled guidance and sensitivity analyses for the relaxation parameters, fix clarity/notation issues, and expand the empirical evaluation (coverage, width, sensitivity, runtime).
