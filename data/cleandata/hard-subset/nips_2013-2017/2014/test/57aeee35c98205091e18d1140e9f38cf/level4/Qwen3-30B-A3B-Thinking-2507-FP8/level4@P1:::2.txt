

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel framework for estimating bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies. The authors build upon Entner et al.'s Rule 1 by reversing its application to generate candidate instrumental variables rather than admissible sets, while relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for partial path cancellations. This approach generates posterior distributions over bounds on ACE through linear programming and Bayesian inference, providing a middle ground between point estimates based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds derived from instrumental variables.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a reinterpretation of Entner's Rule 1 for generating candidate instrumental variables, (2) a relaxation of faithfulness to allow bounded violations of local independence, and (3) a linear programming framework for deriving ACE bounds that incorporates these relaxations. The authors demonstrate their approach through both analytical bounds and computational algorithms, with experiments on synthetic data and influenza vaccination data.

Quality: The paper presents a theoretically sound approach that successfully combines several existing concepts into a coherent framework. The relaxation of faithfulness through bounded violations is well-motivated, and the linear programming formulation for bounding ACE is technically correct. However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive justification of why the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1 is valid without the causal graph structure shown in Figure 1(c). The authors should explicitly state their assumption about the underlying causal structure when applying Rule 1 in reverse. Additionally, the criteria for selecting relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) are not sufficiently developed. Since these parameters significantly affect the tightness of the bounds, the authors should provide more guidance on their principled selection rather than treating them as arbitrary hyperparameters.

Clarity: The theoretical exposition is generally clear, though some sections would benefit from greater precision. For instance, the authors state "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1" (page 6), but Algorithm 1 is presented as a pseudocode algorithm, not a table. Similarly, "Results are summarized in Table 5" (page 8) appears to be a typo, as the paper does not contain a Table 5. The parameters referenced in "Table 1" (which appears to be Table 2 in the paper) are not sufficiently explained in the main text. The paper would be significantly clearer if these parameters were defined more explicitly when first introduced.

Originality: While the problem of bounding ACE has been addressed by prior work (e.g., Dawid [5], Ramsahai [16]), the authors' approach of combining Entner's Rule 1 with a relaxation of faithfulness represents a meaningful extension. However, the paper could better clarify how this differs from the approaches of Entner et al. and Ramsahai. The authors acknowledge this work but do not sufficiently distinguish their contribution from previous methods that also use conditional independence constraints to bound causal effects. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with related work, particularly with respect to how their relaxation parameters differ from existing approaches.

Significance: The WPP framework addresses an important practical problem in observational studies where faithfulness assumptions may be violated. The method provides a valuable tool for researchers to quantify uncertainty in causal estimates when unmeasured confounding is suspected. The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate that WPP can provide tighter bounds than naive approaches, particularly in cases where the faithfulness assumption is violated. However, the empirical validation is limited to a single real-world dataset (influenza vaccination), and the paper does not demonstrate the method's performance across a wider range of causal structures or data types. The authors should provide more thorough experimental validation to support their claim that this approach should become "a standard item in the toolbox."

Strengths: The paper provides a well-motivated theoretical framework that bridges the gap between point estimates based on faithfulness and bounds derived from instrumental variables. The Bayesian implementation is particularly valuable as it properly accounts for estimation uncertainty. The analytical bounds derived in Section 4 offer valuable theoretical insights into how the relaxation parameters affect the bounds, which is a significant contribution beyond the computational method.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient justification for the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1 without the causal structure. The criteria for selecting relaxation parameters are underdeveloped, which is critical for practical implementation. The experimental section is limited in scope and does not adequately demonstrate the method's generalizability. The paper's claim that this approach should be "used in regular practice" is premature given the limited validation.

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to causal inference that could be valuable for practitioners. The technical content is sound, but the paper would benefit from greater theoretical justification of key assumptions, more comprehensive experimental validation, and clearer explanations of implementation details. With these improvements, WPP could make a significant contribution to the field of causal inference.
