Summary
This paper proposes a method to compute tight bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) of X on Y by combining three ideas: (i) using Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction, (ii) relaxing faithfulness to allow limited path-cancellation, and (iii) imposing additional inequality constraints. The resulting bound estimation is framed as a linear program. Although the algorithmic optimizations are not fully developed, the reported bounds appear narrower and more accurate than prior approaches.

Quality
The paper tackles an important and well-studied problem and presents a thoughtful synthesis of existing principles to improve ACE bounds. The linear-programming formulation is appropriate and, based on the reported results, appears empirically effective.

However, several foundational assumptions are insufficiently justified. Most critically, the validity of applying Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction is not established. If the reverse use requires the causal structure in Fig. 2 (or a close variant), this should be made explicit, with formal conditions and counterexamples clarifying when the inference is valid or unsafe. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the tightness of the bounds is due to sound causal information or to unacknowledged structural assumptions.

Second, the relaxation of faithfulness is only partially specified. Because the degree of allowed path-cancellation directly governs the tightness and correctness of the bounds, the paper needs principled guidance on how to choose, interpret, and validate these relaxation parameters. At minimum, a sensitivity analysis should illustrate how the bounds vary with these parameters, and practical rules of thumb (or data-driven procedures) should be offered.

Clarity
The theoretical presentation is largely clear, aside from the missing justification for the inverse application of Entner’s Rule 1 and the lack of concrete guidance on relaxation parameters.

The experimental section is underdeveloped and sometimes confusing:
- The algorithmic details are not sufficiently described to ensure reproducibility. Please provide full pseudo-code or a clear step-by-step description of the linear program, including variables, constraints, and objective.
- Parameter definitions and ranges (e.g., those referenced in Table 1 / Table 5) are unclear. Each parameter should be defined, motivated, and linked to the corresponding constraint or relaxation.
- There are inconsistencies in the tables and references: “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1” (p. 6) does not match the content; “Results are summarized in Table 5” (p. 8) appears to refer to a table that is missing or misnumbered. Please standardize figure/table numbering and ensure all references resolve.

Originality
The high-level problem has been addressed by prior work, and the paper builds on known ideas. The novelty lies in the particular combination of (reverse) Entner’s Rule 1, partial-faithfulness relaxation, and inequality constraints within a linear-programming framework. This combination is promising, but the originality is limited by the lack of a rigorous treatment of when the reverse use of Entner’s Rule 1 is justified and how the relaxation parameters should be set.

Significance
If the assumptions can be made explicit and validated, the method could be practically valuable, offering tighter ACE bounds than current alternatives. At present, the significance is constrained by:
- Limited empirical evaluation. The experiments seem narrow in scope, making it hard to gauge robustness, generality, and sensitivity.
- Lack of ablations disentangling the contributions of each component (reverse Entner’s Rule 1, relaxation, inequality constraints).
- Absent or unclear comparisons to strong baselines across diverse graph structures, sample sizes, and noise regimes.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Theoretical justification:
  - State precisely the structural assumptions under which Entner’s Rule 1 may be applied in reverse. Provide proofs or at least propositions and counterexamples.
  - Clarify the identifiability implications: under which graphs does the reverse application constrain ACE, and how do hidden confounders affect this?
- Relaxation parameters:
  - Define each parameter, its causal meaning, and how it bounds path-cancellation.
  - Provide guidelines for setting them (domain knowledge, data-driven calibration, or validated defaults).
  - Include sensitivity analyses and robustness checks; report how bounds widen as relaxations increase.
- Algorithmic clarity and reproducibility:
  - Present complete pseudo-code or a clear LP specification: decision variables, all constraints, and the objective.
  - Report computational complexity and runtimes; discuss scalability and any solver-specific considerations.
- Experiments:
  - Expand benchmarks to include synthetic and semi-synthetic settings with known ground truth across varied DAGs, confounding levels, and sample sizes.
  - Compare against established baselines and recent methods; report both tightness and coverage when ground truth ACE is known.
  - Provide ablations isolating the effect of each modeling ingredient.
- Presentation fixes:
  - Correct table/figure numbering and cross-references.
  - Ensure all parameters in tables are defined in the text and tied to equations or constraints.

Overall Evaluation
This is an interesting and potentially impactful approach that promises tighter ACE bounds via a coherent optimization framework. However, key assumptions—especially the reverse use of Entner’s Rule 1—and the handling of faithfulness relaxation parameters require much clearer justification. The experimental section also needs substantial strengthening for reproducibility and breadth. With these issues addressed, the contribution could be significant; as written, the paper is promising but not yet fully convincing.
