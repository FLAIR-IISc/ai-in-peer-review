

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in causal inference: estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between binary variables X and Y when confounding variables may be unobserved. The authors build upon Entner et al.'s (2013) rule for identifying admissible sets for back-door adjustment, but propose a novel approach that generates bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates. The key innovation is relaxing the faithfulness assumption typically required for causal discovery, allowing for "bounded violations" of conditional independence to generate more robust causal bounds. The authors develop a framework called the Witness Protection Program (WPP) that combines linear programming with Bayesian inference to produce posterior distributions over ACE bounds.

The paper makes a significant contribution to causal inference by providing a method that bridges faithfulness-based approaches and instrumental variable methods. Previous work (e.g., Ramsahai, 2012) has focused on point estimates under faithfulness assumptions or bounds under strict instrumental variable assumptions. The WPP framework offers a middle ground, providing more reliable causal estimates when faithfulness may be violated in practice. This is particularly relevant for observational studies where unobserved confounding is common, as the authors demonstrate with both synthetic and real-world influenza vaccination data.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution: The paper successfully extends the causal inference framework by formalizing a relaxation of the faithfulness assumption, allowing for more practical causal estimation in real-world settings.
2. Practical utility: The method provides a concrete tool for practitioners to complement existing causal inference methods, with a clear implementation strategy using Bayesian inference and linear programming.
3. Experimental validation: The experiments demonstrate the method's robustness in synthetic settings and provide a compelling real-world application with influenza vaccination data, showing how WPP can yield more informative bounds than standard instrumental variable approaches.
4. Clear methodology: The paper provides a well-structured algorithm (Algorithm 1) and detailed technical development of the WPP framework.

Weaknesses:
1. Conceptual clarity: The paper's connection between Entner et al.'s rule and the proposed framework could be strengthened. The role of the rule's conditions in the WPP framework is not sufficiently explained, particularly regarding how the conditions relate to the relaxation parameters. The phrase "bounded violations of local independence" is conceptually confusing, as unfaithfulness involves unexpected conditional independences, not violations of independence.
2. Experimental presentation: The paper could better contextualize the performance of Entner et al.'s method under standard thresholds, which would help readers understand the advantages of the WPP approach. The claim that "a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine" requires clarification, as the description of Y=1 (hospitalization) creates confusion about the interpretation of negative effects.
3. Technical exposition: The paper's technical details are dense, particularly in Sections 3 and 4. More intuitive explanations of the relaxation parameters and their relationship to the causal structure would improve accessibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper addresses a significant challenge in causal inference with practical relevance to observational studies.
- The proposed WPP framework represents a meaningful theoretical advancement beyond existing approaches.
- The method provides a more robust alternative to faithfulness-based causal estimation when assumptions may be violated.
- The experimental results demonstrate the method's utility in both synthetic and real-world settings.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The conceptual connection between Entner's rule and the WPP framework requires clarification, particularly regarding how the rule's conditions are utilized in the proposed method.
- Some technical terms are confusingly presented (e.g., "bounded violations of local independence").
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the relaxation parameters relate to the causal structure.

Overall, this paper makes an important contribution to causal inference by providing a more robust framework for estimating causal effects in observational studies. The WPP approach offers a practical middle ground between faithfulness-based point estimation and instrumental variable bounds. While the paper could be strengthened by clarifying the connection between Entner's rule and the proposed framework, and improving the presentation of technical concepts, the methodological contribution is substantial and the experimental results are compelling. The paper advances the state of the art in causal inference by providing a more realistic approach to handling unobserved confounding.

The authors should address the conceptual connection between Entner's rule and the WPP framework, clarify the terminology regarding faithfulness violations, and better explain the interpretation of negative ACE in the influenza vaccination example. With these clarifications, the paper would make a strong contribution to the causal inference literature and provide practitioners with a valuable tool for observational studies.

Given the paper's significant theoretical contribution, practical utility, and compelling experimental validation, I recommend acceptance. The work is well-grounded in existing literature, addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference, and provides a method that should become a standard tool in the causal inference practitioner's toolbox.
