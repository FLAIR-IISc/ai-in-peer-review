This paper discusses the problem of estimating average causal effectiveness (ACE) between two (binary) variables, where the causal structure between the variables is not fully specified. It considers a rule proposed by Antner et al. (2013), which aims to select a set of observed variables sufficient to block all hidden pathways. However, for Antner et al., the rule is only fair under the assumption of fidelity. This paper proposes using the rule to provide bounds instead of point estimates of ACE and creates a method following Ramaswamy (2012).

I must admit that I couldn't digest all the technical details, but the main idea seems understandable and appealing and useful to me. However, what puzzled me was that I didn't see the role of the conditions in Antner et al.'s rule as much in this method. Without the fidelity assumption, those conditions have no clear impact on the causal structure. My best guess is that if the conditions are verified, they are relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper clearly removes that point. I think this process is not particularly dependent on the conditions in Antner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if there is any connection to it with the apparent robustness of the testing method. I would expect a clarification on this matter.

Some minor comments:
1. The first paragraph of section 3, "...violates fidelity as a violation of local independence..." sounds strange. Isn't infidelity more about granting too much freedom than violating freedom? I think the proposed framework is actually intended to allow / reveal "almost" violations of fidelity by limiting the degree of dependence.
2. It might be helpful to report the effectiveness of Antner et al.'s method in testing, where the "don't know" answer is considered an informative boundary.
3. In the second paragraph of page 8, if "X=1 means the patient has had a flu shot, Y=1 indicates that the patient has not been hospitalized", then why does a negative ACE suggest a desired vaccine?
4. "Table 1" on page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

It is an interesting paper, although the details are not easy to follow.
