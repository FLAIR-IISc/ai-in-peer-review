This paper proposes a new method for deriving bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two variables, X and Y. The approach is built on three core principles: the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1, the relaxation of the faithfulness assumption to allow for a degree of path cancellation, and the introduction of new assumptions formulated as inequality constraints. These principles are unified into a computational scheme that uses linear programming to calculate the ACE bounds. The preliminary results suggest that this method can produce significantly narrower and more accurate bounds compared to state-of-the-art approaches, even though the algorithm has not yet been fully optimized.

### **Quality and Technical Soundness**

The primary strength of this paper is its novel attempt to establish a more accurate method for bounding the ACE. The synthesis of several plausible principles into a unified framework is compelling, and the initial success in producing tighter bounds makes the work highly interesting.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses in its theoretical justification and exposition:

1.  **Justification for Inverse Rule Application:** A central component of the proposed method is the use of Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction. The validity of this step is not clearly discussed or justified. For this inverse application to hold, specific structural assumptions (such as the one depicted in Figure 2) are likely required. The authors should explicitly state these assumptions and provide a rigorous argument for why the inverse application is sound under those conditions. Without this, a core pillar of the methodology is left unsubstantiated.

2.  **Guidance on Parameter Selection:** The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption introduces new parameters that control the degree of allowed path cancellation. These parameters have a significant impact on the resulting ACE bounds. The paper fails to provide generic criteria or a principled methodology for choosing appropriate values for these parameters. A more thorough investigation into how to set them, or at least a sensitivity analysis, is necessary for the method to be practically useful and its results to be credible.

### **Clarity**

While the theoretical explanation is generally comprehensible, its clarity is undermined by the critical omission of the justifications mentioned above. The presentation of the experimental results is particularly weak and requires substantial improvement.

*   The description of the algorithm's implementation is not comprehensive enough to allow for replication of the experiments. Key details are omitted.
*   The parameters presented in Table 1 are not clearly defined, which makes it difficult for the reader to interpret the results and understand their context.
*   The manuscript contains several typographical errors that detract from its professionalism. For instance, on page 6, it states, "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1," but no such algorithm table is present. Similarly, on page 8, the text refers to "Table 5" for a summary of results, which appears to be an incorrect reference (perhaps meant to be Table 1). These errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

### **Originality**

The problem of bounding causal effects is a well-established area of research, so the problem setting itself is not highly original. The paper's originality lies in the specific combination of the three core ideas (inverse rule, faithfulness relaxation, inequality constraints) into a single computational framework. However, as noted earlier, the validity of this combination is not yet clearly established, which tempers the novelty of the contribution.

### **Significance**

The paper addresses the important and practical problem of quantifying the strength of causal relationships from observational data. In demonstrating a potential path to deriving tighter ACE bounds, the method shows considerable promise.

However, the significance of the work in its current form is limited. The performance has only been evaluated in a very limited set of experiments, and its generality has not been rigorously assessed either theoretically or empirically. For the paper to be impactful, the authors must strengthen the theoretical foundations of their approach and provide a more comprehensive and well-described experimental validation across a wider range of conditions.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting idea with promising initial results, but its contributions are undermined by a lack of theoretical rigor and poor presentation of its empirical validation.
