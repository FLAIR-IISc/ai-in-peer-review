This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is unknown. The approach builds on a rule from Entner et al. (2013) for selecting a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. While the original rule relies on the faithfulness assumption to yield a point estimate of the ACE, the key contribution here is to relax this assumption. By interpreting the conditional independencies suggested by the rule as evidence of "weak" rather than non-existent causal paths, the authors derive bounds on the ACE using a linear programming framework adapted from Ramsahai (2012).

The core idea of relaxing the faithfulness assumption to produce bounds instead of point estimates is compelling and potentially very useful. The proposed "Witness Protection Program" offers a pragmatic compromise between methods that make strong, often unverifiable, assumptions and those that may produce uninformatively wide bounds.

However, the main weakness of the manuscript is the lack of a clear connection between the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule and the proposed bounding methodology. Once the faithfulness assumption is dropped, the observational conditional independencies that form the basis of the rule no longer have a direct, unambiguous implication for the underlying causal structure. The paper appears to use these observed (near) independencies to motivate placing bounds on certain causal path strengths via a set of relaxation parameters. Yet, the crucial question of how to select these parameters based on the data is explicitly deferred. This makes it difficult for the reader to understand how the strength of an observed statistical association (or lack thereof) translates into specific, principled bounds.

This raises a fundamental question: does the proposed machinery truly depend on Entner et al.'s rule, or is the rule simply a heuristic for identifying promising candidate variables for the bounding procedure? It is possible that the method's strong empirical performance stems from the robustness of the bounding framework itself, rather than from the specific witness/admissible set selection criteria. Clarifying how the selection rule concretely informs the choice of relaxation parameters would greatly strengthen the paper's argument and practical utility.

Some minor comments:
1.  The phrasing in Section 3, "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is potentially confusing. A violation of faithfulness typically refers to an independence relation that holds in the data but is not entailed by the graph (e.g., due to parameter cancellations). The proposed framework seems to address the converse problem: modeling weak dependencies that might be mistaken for independencies in a finite sample. It might be clearer to state that the method models *near-unfaithful* distributions by constraining the magnitude of dependencies that faithfulness would assume to be exactly zero.

2.  The experimental comparison could be enhanced by including the original method from Entner et al. (2013) using their recommended stringent thresholds. In cases where their method returns "I don't know," this could be treated as returning the uninformative interval [-1, 1] for the ACE. This would provide a more direct benchmark for the trade-offs between the two approaches.

3.  In the description of the influenza study (Section 5, page 8), please clarify the definition of the outcome variable. If "Y=1 indicates the patient was hospitalized," then a negative ACE correctly suggests a desirable vaccine. However, if Y=1 were to mean the patient was *not* hospitalized, a negative ACE would imply a harmful effect. Explicitly confirming the variable definitions would prevent any potential ambiguity for the reader.

4.  There appears to be a typo in the text where Table 1 is referred to as "Table 5."

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a novel and promising idea. The primary concern is the lack of a clear, principled connection between the data-driven selection rule and the subsequent parameterization of the bounds. Addressing this point and the minor comments above would significantly improve the clarity and impact of the work.
