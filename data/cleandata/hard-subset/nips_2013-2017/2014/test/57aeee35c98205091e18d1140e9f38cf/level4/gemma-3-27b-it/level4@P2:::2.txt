This paper introduces a novel approach for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between variables X and Y in observational studies. The core idea centers on inverting Entner’s Rule 1, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for “path cancellations” without precluding confounding, and formulating a linear programming approach constrained by inequalities representing these relaxations. The authors demonstrate, through synthetic data and a real-world example, that their method can produce tighter bounds on the ACE compared to existing techniques. While computationally intensive, they also propose relaxations and an efficient back-substitution algorithm to mitigate this.

**Quality**

The paper tackles an important problem in causal inference: estimating causal effects in the presence of unobserved confounders. The proposed methodology represents a thoughtful combination of existing ideas – instrumental variables, Entner’s work on covariate selection, and relaxations of the faithfulness assumption – and demonstrates potential for improving the accuracy of ACE bounds. The demonstrated tightness of the bounds is a key strength. 

However, the justification for inverting Entner’s Rule 1 warrants further elaboration. The paper alludes to a required causal structure (akin to Fig. 2) but doesn't explicitly state this as a necessary assumption for the inversion to be valid. Clarifying this structural requirement would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation. Furthermore, the method introduces several relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and while the paper acknowledges the importance of their selection, it lacks a clear principled guide for choosing appropriate values. A more in-depth investigation into sensitivity analysis and strategies for parameter selection would enhance the robustness and practical applicability of the method.

**Clarity**

The theoretical background and motivation are generally well-explained, providing sufficient context for readers familiar with causal inference concepts. The authors appropriately summarize relevant prior work (Pearl, Spirtes et al., Entner et al.). However, the description of the experimental results lacks sufficient detail. The implementation of the algorithm is only briefly mentioned, and critical aspects such as the parameter settings used in Table 1 (which is actually Table 3, a minor error) and the justification for their choices are not adequately explained. 

Minor typographical errors distract from the overall clarity. For example, the reference to “Table 1” on page 6 is incorrect (it should be Table 3), and the reference to “Table 5” on page 8 is also inaccurate. Correcting these inconsistencies would improve readability.

**Originality**

While the problem of bounding causal effects is well-studied, the specific combination of techniques presented in this paper – particularly the inversion of Entner’s Rule 1 coupled with the relaxation of faithfulness – offers a novel contribution. The development of the linear programming formulation and the subsequent relaxations to improve computational efficiency also demonstrate originality. However, the degree of novelty is somewhat tempered by the reliance on established principles. A more explicit discussion of how this work differentiates itself from, and improves upon, existing approaches would further solidify its originality.

**Significance**

The potential to obtain tighter bounds on the ACE is a significant contribution, as it directly addresses a central challenge in observational causal inference. However, the evaluation of the method's performance remains limited. The experiments, while illustrative, lack breadth and do not thoroughly assess the method's generalizability. The authors should consider evaluating their method on a wider range of datasets, including those with varying degrees of confounding and different causal structures. Furthermore, exploring the computational scalability of the method, particularly for high-dimensional datasets, is crucial for establishing its practical significance. The paper addresses a challenging and important problem, but a more comprehensive empirical validation is needed to fully demonstrate the impact of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of established causal inference techniques.
*   Potential for tighter ACE bounds compared to existing methods.
*   Clear theoretical motivation and formulation.
*   Demonstrated performance on synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a rigorous justification for inverting Entner’s Rule 1.
*   Insufficient guidance for selecting relaxation parameters.
*   Limited empirical evaluation and scalability analysis.
*   Minor clarity issues and typographical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to bounding causal effects. The proposed methodology is theoretically sound, and the preliminary results are encouraging. However, the lack of a complete theoretical justification for key assumptions and the limited empirical evaluation raise concerns. I recommend **weak acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly providing a clearer explanation of the inverted Entner's Rule 1 and conducting a more thorough empirical validation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field.
