The paper describes the so-called Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute bounds on an inferred average causal effect (ACE) that takes possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference into account. It does so by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, and then uses a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link by adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates, subject to these constraints.

This problem is currently receiving considerable attention, making the paper both timely and interesting. I appreciate the approach and ideas introduced – it is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, combined with well-known mathematical tools to solve the resulting optimization problems.

However, the interpretation of the resulting output remains a challenge. A distribution over bounds, reflecting “unfaithful relaxations” relative to a given model, lacks clear practical meaning. The potential for wide bounds, rendering them less useful, is a concern. The method does not commit to a preferred point estimate within the interval, despite the likelihood that users will interpret it as such. 

The experimental evaluation is weak. The ‘bias’ metric used to compare WPP with competitors is inappropriate, as they measure fundamentally different things. Consequently, it's unclear whether WPP’s output represents a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated abstraction. Furthermore, the results on the influenza dataset are uninformative – an ACE interval of (-0.23, 0.64) provides little actionable insight, even if it is a fair assessment given the data.

**Quality:**

The approach is technically sound, with details explicated in the supplementary material. The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. The lack of discussion regarding how to choose the relaxation parameters—relegated to future work—hinders a complete assessment of potential issues with this crucial aspect of the method. The experimental evaluation is not robust enough to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the approach.

**Clarity:**

The paper is decently written, although the algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving numerous parameters are challenging to follow, in part due to a lack of detailed explanation. While I believe I understand the overall process, reproducing the implementation solely from the description would be difficult. The supplement may be sufficient for that purpose.

**Originality:**

As far as I can tell, the method represents a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently distinct from, recent work on the same problem, and may stimulate cross-fertilization between different approaches.

**Significance:**

The significance of the paper lies primarily in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate various techniques to address this highly challenging area of causal inference. In its current form, it may not be immediately applicable for practitioners, although some of the algorithmic components could prove valuable in future developments.

**Summary:**

Relevant and timely problem. Interesting method with potential. Current application is limited, but likely to extend to more general cases. Reasonably well-written, with room for improvement. Technically interesting solutions. Experimental evaluation is weak. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output – a distribution over bounds – remain unclear.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to addressing the challenges of causal inference under violations of faithfulness.
*   Principled combination of established techniques (linear programming, instrumental variables) with a novel relaxation framework.
*   Potentially useful contribution to the development of more robust causal inference methods.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and motivation for the proposed solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Weak experimental evaluation that fails to demonstrate practical benefits.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the interpretation and practical application of the output (bounds).
*   Omission of crucial details regarding the selection of relaxation parameters.
*   Potential for wide bounds that may render the method less useful in practice.

*   p8/406-408: The language ("risky," "honest," "compromise") indicates a biased evaluation.
*   p8/421+: The framing of WPP as a “compromise” is misleading; it addresses a different aspect of the inference problem.



I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The ideas are promising, but the lack of a compelling experimental validation and the challenges in interpreting the output limit its immediate impact. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and justify its inclusion in NIPS.
