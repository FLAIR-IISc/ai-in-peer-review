The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which computes bounds on the inferred average causal effect (ACE) while accounting for possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. To achieve this, the algorithm incorporates relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and utilizes a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE. This is done by adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates, subject to these constraints.

The problem addressed in the paper is timely and interesting, and I appreciate the approach and ideas presented. The method is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of weak unfaithful interaction patterns, combining well-known mathematical tools to solve the corresponding maximization/minimization problems. However, it remains to be seen whether the authors have found the right method to capture this impact.

The resulting output is challenging to interpret from a conceptual point of view: it provides a distribution over bounds that are a function of 'unfaithful relaxations' relative to a given model. However, the likelihood of these unfaithful instances and the relevance/realism of these bounds themselves remain unclear. Many relaxations lead to wide bounds, which can become less useful. Furthermore, the method does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, although people may interpret it that way.

The experimental evaluation is poor. The 'bias' evaluation metric is meaningless when comparing the WPP method with its competitors, as they measure two completely different things. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza dataset is also weak, with an ACE in the interval (-0.23, 0.64), implying that there may be no possible relevant conclusion. Although this could be the fairest assessment of all.

Quality:
The approach introduced in the paper is technically sound, with many details explicated in the supplementary file (which I did not thoroughly check). The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as choosing the relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article, making it difficult to understand potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough.

Clarity:
The paper is decently written, except for the conclusion. The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving subtly different parameters were hard to follow, partly due to the lack of explanation of certain details of the method. I think I understand what happens, but I am not sure I could fully implement it from just this description; the supplement may be sufficient.

Originality:
As far as I can tell, the method is a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently different from, recent work on the same problem. As such, it may stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of several approaches on the subject.

Significance:
The significance of the paper mainly lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, showing how to incorporate other techniques in solving this highly challenging problem. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to represent the finished article for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm could well end up playing an important role.

Summary:
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem. The method is interesting and will undoubtedly find an audience. However, the current application is limited, and it is likely to extend to other, more general cases. The paper is reasonably well-written, though it could be improved. The experimental evaluation is poor, and the relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
