This paper addresses the problem of estimating the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two binary variables in settings where the causal relationships between covariates are not fully known. The authors build upon a covariate selection rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013), designed to identify a sufficient set of observed covariates to block back-door paths. While Entner et al.’s rule relies on the faithfulness assumption, this work leverages it to construct bounds – rather than point estimates – for the ACE, utilizing a methodology inspired by Ramsahai (2012). 

The core idea is sound and potentially valuable, though a complete understanding of the technical details eluded me during this review. My primary concern relates to the role of the conditions specified in the Entner et al. (2013) rule within this bounding framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions lack clear implications regarding the underlying causal structure. I suspect they may inform the selection of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly postpones a discussion of this connection. Consequently, it appears the presented machinery doesn’t fundamentally depend on satisfying Entner et al.’s conditions, leading me to question whether the observed robustness in the experimental results is coincidental or attributable to another factor. Clarification on this point would be greatly appreciated.

Several minor points warrant attention:

1. The phrasing in the first paragraph of Section 3, “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…”, is somewhat counterintuitive. Unfaithfulness typically implies *extra* independence relationships, rather than a violation of existing ones. It seems the framework more accurately allows for – and constrains the magnitude of – *near* violations of faithfulness, effectively permitting “almost independence” rather than strict violations.

2. In the experimental evaluation, including a comparison against Entner et al.’s method, employing their original stringent thresholds, would be informative. Reporting “don’t know” as the outcome when their criteria aren't met could provide a baseline for evaluating the bound tightness achieved by the proposed method.

3. The explanation provided in the second paragraph of p. 8 – where X=1 represents receiving a flu shot and Y=1 indicates non-hospitalization – presents a potentially misleading interpretation. A negative ACE in this context suggests the flu shot *increases* the likelihood of hospitalization, which contradicts the claim that it indicates a desirable vaccine effect. This section requires careful re-examination.

4. There is an inconsistency in table referencing. “Table 1” is mentioned on top of p. 8, but subsequently cited as “Table 5” within the text.

Despite these points, this is an interesting and potentially significant contribution. While the technical details demand careful study, the overall approach holds promise for causal inference in complex settings.
