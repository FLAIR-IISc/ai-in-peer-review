This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel algorithm for computing bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data. The core idea is to account for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption, which is a common but potentially fragile cornerstone of many causal discovery methods. WPP addresses this by introducing a set of user-defined relaxation parameters that quantify the degree of permissible unfaithfulness. It then employs a linear programming framework, building on the work of Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012), to derive upper and lower bounds on the ACE, conditional on an appropriately chosen set of covariates. The problem of relaxing the faithfulness assumption is both timely and significant, and this paper makes an interesting contribution.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of "near-unfaithful" data generating processes. By combining ideas from covariate selection (Entner et al., 2013), instrumental variables, and linear programming for bounds estimation, the authors present a novel synthesis of existing techniques. The conceptual framework is intriguing and may stimulate further research at the intersection of these areas.
*   **Technical Approach:** The use of linear programming to derive bounds under these relaxed assumptions is technically sound. The development of algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm is a clever addition that provides both computational efficiency and some insight into the behavior of the bounds.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Interpretability and Practical Utility:** Despite its novelty, the practical utility of the proposed method is not yet clear, primarily due to issues with the interpretation of its output. WPP produces a posterior distribution over bounds, which are themselves a function of the chosen relaxation parameters. It remains unclear how a practitioner should interpret these bounds or assess their relevance, as this depends on the plausibility of the assumed relaxation. The bounds can also be very wide, potentially to the point of being uninformative. For instance, the analysis of the influenza dataset yields an ACE interval of `[-0.23, 0.64]`, which offers little conclusive evidence. While the authors rightly note that the method does not commit to a point estimate, there is a risk that users will misinterpret the output as a simple confidence interval.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness of the paper. The chosen "error" metric is not suitable for comparing the interval-based WPP with point-estimate methods. An interval that contains the true value is assigned zero error, which means a trivial, uninformative interval like `[-1, 1]` would always be rated as perfect. This makes the comparisons in Table 3 difficult to interpret and does not convincingly demonstrate the value of WPP over alternatives. The claim that "WPP is quite stable" could be interpreted as the bounds being so wide that they are insensitive to whether a true witness exists. Furthermore, the experiments are conducted on datasets with 5000 samples; the performance on smaller datasets, which are common in practice, is not explored and remains a concern.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper's clarity could be improved. The methodology, with its many interacting parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), is difficult to follow. The paper would benefit from more intuition about what these parameters represent and how they interact. A key aspect of the method—how to choose the relaxation parameters—is deferred to future work, which makes the proposed framework feel incomplete and difficult to evaluate as a self-contained contribution.
*   **Related Work:** Regarding originality, the claim that "We know of no previous analytical bounds as an algebraic function of w" seems to overlook recent, highly related work, such as "Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding" by Geiger et al. (UAI 2014). The authors should discuss the relationship between their approach and this line of research.

**Summary:**
This paper addresses a highly relevant problem in causal inference and proposes an interesting and technically novel method. The ideas presented are likely to be of interest to the community. However, the current manuscript is hampered by a weak experimental evaluation, significant open questions regarding the practical interpretation and utility of its output, and a lack of clarity in its methodological description. While the core ideas are promising, the work in its current form seems more like a proof of concept than a finished, practitioner-ready tool.

**Additional Comments for Improvement:**
*   p1: The term "unstable" assumptions is imprecise. Consider rephrasing to "assumptions that may be violated in practice."
*   p2: "cartoon representation" could be replaced with the more standard "schematic diagram."
*   p3: The claim that the method "gives greater control over violations of faithfulness" is vague and should be substantiated with a more concrete explanation of what this control entails.
*   p3: It should be clarified that the witness W is used to *identify* the admissible set Z but is not itself needed for the back-door adjustment computation.
*   p4: The sudden transition to the parameterization in Table 1 is jarring. Please provide more intuition for what these parameters represent and why this specific parameterization was chosen.
*   p5: The sentence "Background knowledge... can be used here" is unclear. How should it be used?
*   p6: The back-substitution algorithm is an interesting idea, but its practical efficacy is not demonstrated. Could you provide some analysis in Section 5 of how much this step tightens the bounds in practice?
*   p8: The tone of the conclusion could be more objective. Phrases like "risky Bayesian approaches" and "keeps inference more honest" are unnecessarily emotive and detract from the scientific contribution. Similarly, characterizing other work as "purely theory-driven analyses that refuse to look at competing models" is overly dismissive.
