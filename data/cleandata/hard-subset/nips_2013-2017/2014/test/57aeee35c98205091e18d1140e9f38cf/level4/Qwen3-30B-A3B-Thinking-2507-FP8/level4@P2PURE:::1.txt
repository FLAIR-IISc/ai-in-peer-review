

This paper addresses the estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is partially specified. It adapts a rule from Entner et al. (2013), which selects covariates sufficient for blocking all back-door paths under the faithfulness assumption, to provide bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates. The approach follows Ramsahai (2012), though the role of Entner et al.'s conditions in this bounding framework remains unclear. Without faithfulness, these conditions lack causal interpretability, yet the paper does not explain how they inform the method. I suspect the conditions may relate to relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this aside. Consequently, it appears the method's robustness in experiments may not critically depend on Entner et al.'s conditions, and the authors should clarify this connection.  

Minor comments:  
1. Section 3, first paragraph: "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is misleading. Unfaithfulness involves *extra* independencies (not violations of independence), so the framework should instead express "near-faithfulness" by constraining dependence magnitudes.  
2. In experiments, report Entner et al.'s method performance using their stringent thresholds, treating "don't know" outputs as uninformative bounds.  
3. Page 8, second paragraph: If X=1 denotes flu shot receipt and Y=1 denotes hospitalization, a negative ACE implies the vaccine reduces hospitalization, making it desirable. The phrasing should clarify this causal direction.  
4. Table 1 (top of page 8) is cited as "Table 5" in the text.  

This is an interesting contribution, though the technical exposition requires greater clarity.
