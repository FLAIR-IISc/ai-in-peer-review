This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the full causal graph over the covariates is unknown. The authors build upon a rule from Entner et al. (2013) designed to identify a sufficient set of covariates for blocking all back-door paths between a treatment and an outcome. A key limitation of the original rule is its reliance on the faithfulness assumption to guarantee a valid point estimate of the ACE.

The central contribution of this work is to adapt this rule to a setting where faithfulness may be violated. Instead of providing a point estimate, the proposed method computes bounds on the ACE, leveraging a framework for sensitivity analysis developed by Ramsahai (2012). The core idea of deriving bounds on the ACE under violations of faithfulness is both interesting and practically significant. It addresses a well-known brittleness in causal discovery algorithms and could make causal inference more robust in real-world applications.

While I find the overall approach compelling, I had difficulty understanding a crucial aspect of the methodology, and I would appreciate the authors' clarification. My main confusion concerns the precise role of the conditions from Entner et al.'s rule within the proposed bounding framework. The original justification for these conditions rests on the faithfulness assumption. When this assumption is relaxed, as is the goal of this paper, the implications of these conditions for the underlying causal structure are no longer obvious.

I initially conjectured that satisfying these conditions might inform the choice of the relaxation parameters that quantify the degree of unfaithfulness. However, the paper explicitly states that the selection of these parameters is outside its current scope. This leaves me wondering: does the bounding machinery fundamentally depend on Entner et al.'s conditions? Or could the framework be applied more generally, with the connection to Entner et al. being primarily a motivating example? Clarifying this is important, as it could explain the method's apparent robustness in the experiments. If the bounding procedure is effective largely independently of whether Entner et al.'s specific conditions are met, this would be a significant finding in its own right and should be highlighted.

In summary, this is a promising paper that tackles an important and difficult problem in causal inference. The main idea is compelling, though the technical details are dense. My primary reservation concerns the clarity of the connection between the proposed bounding method and the specific conditions from Entner et al. If the authors can clarify this central point and address the minor issues raised below, I believe the paper would be a strong contribution to the conference.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrasing "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is potentially misleading. Unfaithfulness typically refers to the presence of conditional independencies that are not entailed by the graph structure (e.g., due to parameter cancellations), rather than a violation of independence. It seems the framework is designed to allow for "near-unfaithful" distributions by placing bounds on the strength of dependencies that would otherwise be assumed to be exactly zero. Revising this phrasing would improve clarity.

2.  For the experimental evaluation, the comparison to Entner et al. could be strengthened. I suggest also reporting the performance of the original Entner et al. method using their stringent thresholds. When the method abstains from providing a point estimate (i.e., outputs "don't know"), this could be treated as reporting uninformative bounds (e.g., [-1, 1]). This would provide a more direct benchmark for the utility of the proposed bounding approach.

3.  There appears to be a small error in the interpretation of the results on page 8. In the second paragraph, the variables are defined as X=1 (patient received a flu shot) and Y=1 (patient was not hospitalized). Under this definition, a positive ACE would indicate a protective effect of the vaccine, whereas the text states that a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine. This should be corrected.

4.  There is a minor citation error on page 8: "Table 1" is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.
