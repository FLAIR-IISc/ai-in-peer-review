- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute ACE bounds under potential faithfulness violations using relaxation parameters and linear programming.
- Timely and interesting conceptual contribution that integrates optimization tools into robust causal inference; memorable name.
- Practical interpretation of the output is unclear: WPP returns a distribution over bounds from relaxed models whose realism and relevance are not established.
- Many relaxation settings yield excessively wide bounds, reducing practical usefulness.
- Method does not produce a point estimate, risking users misinterpreting interval outputs as single best values.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: the “bias” metric is misleading because it compares fundamentally different outputs across methods.
- Influenza dataset results (e.g., ACE in (-0.23, 0.64)) are too wide to be actionable and highlight current limitations.
- Technically sound overall; supplementary material seems to provide necessary details but was not fully reviewed.
- Major limitation: no practical guidance on selecting relaxation parameters, hindering sensitivity analysis and usability.
- Clarity issues: algorithmic steps and parameter explanations are sometimes hard to follow; reproducing the method from the paper alone may be difficult.
- Originality: novel combination of existing techniques with potential to stimulate further research.
- Significance: strong conceptual advance in accounting for unfaithfulness, but current implementation is unlikely to be a definitive practitioner tool.
- Request to clarify causal assumptions (e.g., causal sufficiency and W → X) and explicitly state witness role (used to identify Z, not required for ACE computation).
- Need clearer intuition and interpretation for the relaxation parameters and how they constrain unfaithful interactions.
- Explain relationship between the search component and bounds computation, and how admissible sets Z depend on relaxation parameters θ.
- Provide reference to related work (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf UAI 2014) and link earlier introductions of used concepts.
- Doubts about efficacy of the back-substitution refinement step — provide evidence of its effectiveness.
- Clarify that multiple bounds arise from different admissible Z for different witnesses and that bounds are not confidence intervals around a point estimate.
- Discuss sensitivity to smaller sample sizes and whether observed stability simply reflects loose bounds.
- Numerous requested textual/typo fixes and tone adjustments (replace emotive language, correct typos, clarify phrasing) to improve clarity and neutrality.
