- Proposal: Invert Entner’s Rule 1, relax faithfulness to allow partial path cancellations, and use linear programming under inequality constraints to bound the ACE; shows tighter bounds but is computationally intensive.  
- Technical soundness: Method appears technically sound and a thoughtful integration of techniques, but the reverse use of Entner’s Rule 1 needs stronger justification.  
- Structural assumption: Connection to the specific structural assumption (Fig. 2) is alluded to but must be explicitly stated and discussed.  
- Relaxation parameters: No clear principle for choosing (w, y, x, β, β̄); selection is crucial and currently unsupported.  
- Sensitivity analysis: Paper lacks analysis of how bounds depend on the relaxation parameters.  
- Practical applicability: Reliance on user-set parameters without guidance limits real-world usability.  
- Clarity: Theoretical background is clear for causal-inference readers, but experimental and implementation details (LP, Bayesian learning) are insufficiently described.  
- Presentation errors: Incorrect table references (Table 1 vs Table 5) and minor typos reduce clarity.  
- Originality: Novel combination of inverse Entner’s Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness, and LP formulation, but needs more nuanced comparison to existing bounding methods.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments are limited to small synthetic datasets and one real example, reducing claims about generality and robustness.  
- Significance: Potentially important for tighter ACE bounds, but impact depends on demonstrating scalability and broader applicability.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel technique mix, potential for tighter bounds, clear theoretical framing, new handling of faithfulness violations.  
- Cons against acceptance: Insufficient justification for inverting Entner’s Rule 1, no parameter guidance, limited experiments, unclear implementation, and minor editorial issues.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing the Entner justification, parameter selection/sensitivity, implementation clarity, expanded experiments, and fixing typos.
