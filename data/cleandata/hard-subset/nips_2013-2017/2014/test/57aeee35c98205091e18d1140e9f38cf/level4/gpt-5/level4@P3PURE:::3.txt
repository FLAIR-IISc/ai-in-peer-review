The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm for computing bounds on an inferred average causal effect (ACE) that account for possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. It does so by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and then using a linear programming framework to solve for upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates subject to these constraints.

The topic is timely and interesting, and I like the overall approach: it is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, combined with well-known mathematical tools to solve the resulting optimization problems. Whether the paper has found the right way to capture this impact remains to be seen. The output is conceptually difficult to interpret: it amounts to a distribution over bounds that are functions of “unfaithful relaxations” relative to a given model. However, how likely those unfaithful instances are—and, with that, how relevant or realistic the bounds themselves are—remains unclear. Many relaxations lead to very wide bounds, to the point where they can be of limited practical use. The method also does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, even though practitioners are likely to interpret it that way.

The experimental evaluation is weak. The “bias” metric is not meaningful for comparing WPP against its competitors, as they measure fundamentally different quantities. As a result, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza dataset is also worryingly weak: ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) implies essentially no actionable conclusion—although that could be the fairest assessment available.

Quality
The approach appears technically sound, with many details explained in the supplementary file (I did not check those details). The core ideas are well introduced, though the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects—such as how to choose the relaxation parameters—are deferred to future work, which makes it difficult to assess potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough.

Clarity
The paper is decently written (with the exception of the conclusion; see comments below). The algorithmic steps and their modifications via several subtly different parameters are hard to follow, in part due to missing explanations of key details. I think I understand the approach, but I am not sure I could fully implement it from the description alone; the supplement may be just sufficient.

Originality
As far as I can tell, the method is a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently different from, recent work on the same problem. As such, it may stimulate fruitful cross-fertilization across approaches in this area.

Significance
The significance lies primarily in the ideas and direction the paper brings to the problem, showing how to incorporate other techniques into this challenging setting. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to be the finished tool for practitioners, although some ideas in WPP could end up playing an important role.

Summary
- Relevant and highly topical problem.
- Interesting method that will likely find an audience.
- Current application is limited but may extend to more general cases.
- Reasonably well written, though clarity could be improved.
- Technically interesting solutions.
- Experimental evaluation is poor.
- The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
