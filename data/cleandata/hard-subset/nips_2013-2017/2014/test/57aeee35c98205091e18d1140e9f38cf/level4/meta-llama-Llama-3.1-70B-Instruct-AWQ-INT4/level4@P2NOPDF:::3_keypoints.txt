- Introduces WPP, a novel LP-based algorithm to bound ACE while allowing controlled violations of faithfulness via relaxation parameters.  
- Problem is timely and approach is principled and interesting but it's unclear if the method correctly captures unfaithful interaction patterns.  
- Output is hard to interpret: WPP returns a distribution over bounds tied to unfaithful relaxations relative to a model.  
- The likelihood and relevance of the unfaithful instances (and thus the bounds) are unclear and not assessed.  
- Many relaxation settings produce very wide bounds, limiting practical usefulness.  
- Method does not provide or prefer a point estimate within intervals, risking misinterpretation.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak and unbalanced, making empirical claims hard to trust.  
- The 'bias' metric used for comparison is meaningless because methods estimate different objects.  
- Practical performance on the influenza dataset is poor, yielding an ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64) that conveys little actionable insight.  
- Technical development is sound with detailed supplementary material, but key choices (e.g., selecting relaxation parameters) are deferred to future work.  
- Important practical aspects like choosing relaxation parameters are insufficiently addressed, complicating assessment.  
- Writing is generally decent but the conclusion is problematic and some algorithmic steps lack clear explanation.  
- Unclear whether a reader could fully implement the method from the paper alone despite the supplement.  
- The method is an original combination of known techniques and is sufficiently different from recent related work.  
- Significance lies in the conceptual direction and ideas rather than being a ready-to-use tool for practitioners.  
- The WPP name is catchy and memorable.  
- The term "unstable" is misused; interactions are stable but may not hold, so wording should be corrected.  
- The "cartoon representation" is unclear and should be improved.  
- The assumption of causal sufficiency or known W -> X is not explicitly stated and should be clarified.  
- The witness W is used to identify Z but is not needed to compute the ACE, which should be clarified.  
- The claim that WPP "gives greater control over violations of faithfulness" is vague and needs precision.  
- Section 4 has a sudden abstraction jump that may confuse less familiar readers.  
- The link between the search procedure and bounds computation should be briefly explained.  
- Identification of admissible sets Z depends on relaxation parameters theta and this dependency should be explained.  
- Several sentences/phrases are unclear or misleading and need rephrasing (e.g., "Background ... used here", "max. 10 free parameters").  
- The claim of novelty ignores closely related recent work (e.g., Geiger et al., UAI 2014) and should acknowledge it.  
- Supplementary material was not checked in detail by the reviewer, leaving some verification open.  
- The efficacy of back-substitution in tightening bounds is questionable and needs clarification.  
- The bias comparison is meaningless and the provided trivial optimal example highlights this flaw.  
- Intervals like (-1, 1) remain too wide, indicating limited inferential value; small sample sizes (e.g., n=500) may worsen this.  
- Benefit of WPP over standard faithfulness-based point estimates is unclear from reported results (Table 1).  
- The statement that "WPP is quite stable" is misleading since WPP yields loose bounds that do not hinge on solvability.  
- Interpreting bounds as a confidence interval around a best guess is crucial but problematic and difficult in practice.  
- Conclusion language is emotive and may reflect biased or erroneous interpretation; several phrases should be reworded or removed.
