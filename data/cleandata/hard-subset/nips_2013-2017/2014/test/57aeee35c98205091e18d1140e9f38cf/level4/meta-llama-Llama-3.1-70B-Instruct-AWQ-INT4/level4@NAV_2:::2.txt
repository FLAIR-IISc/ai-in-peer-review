This paper proposes a novel approach to estimate the average causal effect (ACE) from a variable X to another Y by exploiting observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors relax the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The paper builds upon previous work by Entner et al., who exploit conditional independence constraints to obtain point estimates of the ACE. The authors modify the results of Entner et al. to generate candidate instrumental variables and introduce a linear programming approach to generate bounds on the ACE by introducing constraints on some causal paths. The new setup can be computationally expensive, so the authors introduce further relaxations to the linear program to generate novel symbolic bounds and a fast algorithm that sidesteps the full linear programming optimization with some simple, message passing-like, steps.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a novel methodology for estimating the ACE, which the authors call the Witness Protection Program (WPP). The WPP provides a compromise between point estimators given by the faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the WPP through experiments with synthetic and real data, showing that it can provide tighter bounds on the ACE than other approaches in the state of the art.

Quality
The paper is technically sound, and the authors have made a significant effort to establish a novel method for estimating the ACE. The combination of Entner's Rule 1, the relaxation of the faithfulness assumption, and the introduction of assumptions represented by inequality constraints is innovative and well-motivated. However, some criteria to define the bounds are not comprehensively explained, particularly the validity of the use of Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction. The authors should explicitly assume the structure depicted in Fig.2 and clearly explain and discuss its use in the inverse direction.

Additionally, the generic criteria to choose appropriate parameters for relaxation are not provided. Since these affect the result of the bounds significantly, the principle to determine the parameters should be more investigated and included in the work.

Clarity
The explanation of the paper on the theoretical part is comprehensive, except for the essential discussion mentioned above. However, the demonstration of the experimental results is not well described. The actual implementation of the algorithm is not comprehensively explained, and the definition of the parameters shown in Table 1 is not clearly given. Furthermore, there are some typos in the paper, such as the incorrect reference to Table 1 as the full algorithm.

Originality
The problem setting of this paper has been addressed by some earlier work, as the authors pointed out. In this regard, the scope of this paper does not have very strong originality. However, the idea to combine the three ideas in a unified shape contains some originality. As mentioned earlier, the validity of the combination is not very clearly discussed.

Significance
In terms of deriving tighter bounds of ACE, this method shows some significance. However, its performance has been checked by quite limited experiments. Its generality in both theory and wide experiments has not been strictly assessed. The paper addresses an important problem, which is to evaluate the bound of the strength of causal relations. However, the validity of the presented idea should be more well described in both theory and experiments.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to estimate the ACE, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The WPP provides a compromise between point estimators given by the faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the WPP in providing tighter bounds on the ACE than other approaches.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper lacks a comprehensive explanation of the criteria to define the bounds, particularly the validity of the use of Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction.
* The generic criteria to choose appropriate parameters for relaxation are not provided, which affects the result of the bounds significantly.
* The demonstration of the experimental results is not well described, and the actual implementation of the algorithm is not comprehensively explained.
* The paper has some typos and incorrect references to tables.
