- Paper proposes WPP: uses relaxation parameters and linear programming to bound ACE under possible violations of faithfulness.
- Problem is timely and interesting; method is a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithful interactions.
- Resulting output (a distribution over bounds conditioned on relaxations) is conceptually hard to interpret and its practical meaning is unclear.
- The method does not assess how likely unfaithful instances are, so the relevance of the derived bounds is uncertain.
- Many relaxation settings produce very wide bounds, often too loose to be useful.
- WPP provides intervals but no preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation by practitioners.
- Experimental evaluation is weak and unbalanced; the "bias" metric used is meaningless for comparing WPP to competitors.
- Influenza dataset results (ACE in (-0.23,0.64)) are uninformative and suggest poor practical performance.
- Approach is technically sound and detailed in supplementary material, but the paper omits guidance on choosing relaxation parameters.
- Algorithmic description and multiple parameter variants are hard to follow; may be difficult to implement from the paper alone.
- Method is novel as a combination of familiar techniques and related but sufficiently different from recent work.
- Significance lies in ideas and direction rather than immediate practical readiness for practitioners.
- Concern that the back-substitution refinement step may be of limited practical efficacy; effectiveness should be quantified.
- Comparisons across methods are not straightforward; some presented comparisons are misleading or trivialize differences.
- Sample-size sensitivity is a concern—results shown for 5000 points may not hold for typical smaller medical datasets.
- Several clarity, wording, and typo issues throughout the manuscript that need fixing.
- Many technical points need more intuition and explanation (interpretation of relaxation parameters, relation between search and bounds, admissible set dependence on theta).
- Some evaluative language is emotive or biased and should be toned down for accurate interpretation.
