This paper studies estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over observed covariates is only partially known. Building on the selection rule of Entner et al. (2013), which identifies covariate sets sufficient to block back-door paths under the faithfulness assumption, the authors propose using that rule to derive bounds rather than point estimates of ACE, adapting techniques from Ramsahai (2012). The core idea—replacing strong structural assumptions with partial-testable constraints and obtaining bounds—is compelling and practically relevant.

Strengths
- The problem is important: in many applications the causal graph is not fully identified, and bounds can be more defensible than point estimates.
- The paper advances the literature by turning a selection rule that is brittle under faithfulness violations into a tool for partial identification, potentially improving robustness.
- The empirical results suggest the method can be informative in realistic settings where strict assumptions would fail or yield “don’t know.”

Main concerns and requests for clarification
- Role of Entner et al.’s conditions without faithfulness. The paper motivates its approach via Entner et al.’s rule, but once faithfulness is relaxed, the logical link between those conditions and the underlying causal structure is unclear. As written, it is difficult to see whether the rule is essential or merely one heuristic among many for generating constraints. In particular:
  - Are the Entner conditions used only to propose candidate adjustment sets, with the bounds then computed regardless of their causal validity?
  - Do observed (approximate) independences inform the choice of relaxation parameters (e.g., upper bounds on dependence) in a principled way? The paper states that parameter selection is set aside, but then it is not clear how the Entner conditions guide the actual computation.
  - How would the results change if the Entner rule were replaced by other structure-lean procedures (e.g., testing a different set of conditional independences, or using all small subsets up to a size limit)? An ablation illustrating the contribution of the rule itself versus the Ramsahai-style bounding machinery would be very helpful.
  - More generally, are the derived bounds guaranteed valid irrespective of whether the Entner-selected set is causally sufficient, provided the relaxation constraints are satisfied? A clear statement of conditions under which the bounds are sound (and when they may be vacuous) would strengthen the contribution.

- Interpretation of robustness in experiments. The apparent robustness may stem from the bounds machinery rather than any special virtue of the Entner conditions. Please clarify which parts of the pipeline drive performance by:
  - Reporting results with and without invoking the Entner selection step.
  - Demonstrating sensitivity of the bounds to the relaxation parameters and to thresholding choices in conditional independence tests.
  - Including cases where Entner’s conditions fail or return “don’t know,” and showing the corresponding bounds.

Clarity and presentation
- Some technical sections are dense. A brief, self-contained overview of how the Ramsahai (2012) approach is instantiated here (variables, constraints, optimization objective, and guarantees) would improve accessibility.
- It would help to explicitly map each testable (approximate) independence to a corresponding relaxation parameter and constraint in the bounding program, even if parameter selection is deferred.

Suggestions and minor comments
- Section 3 phrasing: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing. Unfaithfulness typically entails additional independences beyond those implied by the graph, not violations of independence. The intended idea seems to be allowing small departures from the conditional independences expected under faithfulness by bounding the magnitude of dependence (e.g., via covariance or risk difference constraints). Please rephrase.
- Baseline in experiments: Include Entner et al. with their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as yielding uninformative bounds. This would contextualize the informativeness of your bounds against a conservative alternative.
- Sign of ACE in the vaccine example (p. 8): If X=1 denotes receiving a flu shot and Y=1 denotes not being hospitalized, a desirable vaccine should increase P(Y=1|do(X=1)) relative to do(X=0), i.e., a positive ACE under the usual definition ACE = E[Y|do(X=1)] − E[Y|do(X=0)]. Please clarify the sign convention or correct the text.
- Table numbering: “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is cited as “Table 5” in the text; please fix the inconsistency.
- Provide practical guidance, even if informal, on selecting relaxation parameters (e.g., linking them to test power, sample size, or domain knowledge), or at least include a sensitivity plot so readers can gauge robustness.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and timely paper. Conceptually, turning a faithfulness-dependent selection rule into a source of constraints for partial identification is a valuable direction, and the empirical results are promising. However, the paper needs a clearer account of how Entner et al.’s conditions matter once faithfulness is relaxed, what guarantees hold for the bounds under the proposed constraints, and how sensitive the results are to the choice of relaxation parameters and test thresholds. Addressing these points, adding the suggested baselines/ablations, and improving a few clarity issues would materially strengthen the paper.
