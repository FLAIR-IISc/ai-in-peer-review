This paper addresses the problem of estimating average causal effects (ACE) between two (binary) variables when the causal structure is not fully specified at the associate. It considers a rule proposed by Antonor et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of sufficient observed associates to block all confounding. However, for Antonor et al.'s purpose, this rule is only valid under the assumption of confounding. This paper proposes using the rule to provide a threshold rather than point estimates of ACE, and develops a method to follow Ramsohai (2012).

I must admit that I did not understand all the technical details, but I understand the main idea and find it interesting and useful. However, what puzzled me was that I did not really see the role of the conditions in Antonor et al.'s rule in this approach. Without the assumption of confounding, those conditions have no clear implication for the causal structure. My best guess is that if the conditions are verified, they are relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper clearly sets that case aside. I think the mechanism does not significantly depend on the conditions in Antonor et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the explicit robustness of the method has anything to do with this. I would appreciate clarification on this issue.

A few minor comments:
1. The first paragraph of Section 3, "...expresses confounding violations as bounded violations of local independence..." seems odd. Isn't confounding a matter of allowing additional independence rather than violating independence? I think the proposed framework is actually intended to allow/express "assumed violations" of confounding by limiting the magnitude of confounding.
2. Reporting the performance of Antonor et al.'s method in experiments can also be helpful when their strict threshold is used, taking the answer "not known" as reporting a non-informative boundary.
3. In the second paragraph on page 8, if "X=1 means the patient received a flu vaccine, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized", why does negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine?
4. "Table 1" at the top of page 8 is quoted in the text as "Table 5".

This is an interesting paper, although the details are not easy to follow.
