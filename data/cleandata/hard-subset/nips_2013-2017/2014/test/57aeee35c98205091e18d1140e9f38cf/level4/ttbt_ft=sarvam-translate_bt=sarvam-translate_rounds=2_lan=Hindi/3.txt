This paper describes the so-called Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which calculates the limits of approximate average causal effects (ACE). This algorithm takes into account potential violations of the standard fidelity assumption in causal inference.

The process begins by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to limit unreliable interactions, and then, subject to these constraints, uses a linear programming framework to solve the resulting upper and lower bounds on ACE for a suitable set of associates.

This problem is currently gaining considerable attention, making this paper both timely and interesting. I like the approach and ideas presented in the paper: it is a theoretical attempt to clearly measure the impact of (weak) unreliable interaction patterns, as well as using well-known mathematical tools to solve related maximization/minimization problems.

How much of an impact it has remains to be determined. It is difficult to understand the resulting output from a conceptual perspective: a distribution on the limits that is a function of 'unreliable relaxation' relative to a given model... but how likely these unreliable examples are, and also, how relevant/realistic these limits are themselves, is still unclear. Many relaxations lead to broad limits, where they may be less useful. This method is not committed to any preferred point estimate within the interval... even if people are likely to understand it in the same way.

The experimental evaluation is poor: 'bias' evaluation is meaningless when the WPP method is compared to its competitors because they literally measure two completely different things. As a result, it is not clear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition to practitioners or just a complex distraction. Also, the practical performance on influenza data sets is worryingly weak: ACE at (-0.23,0.64) means there is no potentially relevant conclusion... although this may be the fairest assessment of them all.

Quality
The approach presented in the paper is technically sound, with many details clarified in the supplementary file (I didn't see any details there). The ideas are well presented, although the overall context is sometimes lost. Important aspects such as choosing relaxation parameters are assigned to a future article, making it difficult to experience potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or strong enough.

Clarity
Well written (except for the conclusion, see comments below). The algorithm steps and subsequent modifications were quite difficult to follow, in part due to the lack of explanation of some details of the method in terms of several subtly different parameters. (I think I understand what happens, but I'm not sure I can fully apply it from this description; the supplement may be sufficient).

Originality
As far as I can tell, the method is a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related, but quite different from recent work on the same problem. Thus it may stimulate fruitful cross-fertilization of several approaches on the subject.

Importance
The importance of the paper mainly lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, showing how other techniques can be incorporated into solving this highly challenging problem. In its current implementation, it is unlikely to represent a ready-made article for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm may eventually play an important role.

Summary:
Relevant and highly topical problem. Interesting method that will undoubtedly attract viewers. Current application is limited, but likely to be extended to other, more general cases. Quite well written, although it could be improved. Technically interesting solution. Experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution on the limits is unclear.
