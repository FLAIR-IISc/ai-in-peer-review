This paper proposes a new method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y from observational data. The core idea is to repurpose Entner's Rule 1: instead of using a "witness" variable to identify an admissible set for back-door adjustment, the authors use it as a candidate instrumental variable (IV). The method then relaxes the strict faithfulness assumption by allowing for bounded violations of conditional independence, which are parameterized by a set of user-defined "relaxation" parameters. These ideas are integrated into a linear programming framework to derive bounds on the ACE. The authors also present a Bayesian method for handling sampling uncertainty and a faster, approximate "back-substitution" algorithm for improved scalability. The experiments on synthetic and real-world data suggest that the proposed bounds can be significantly tighter than those from standard IV analysis.

**Quality and Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is its novel approach to a fundamental problem in causal inference. By providing a principled compromise between methods that rely on strict (and often unrealistic) faithfulness assumptions and standard IV methods that can yield very wide bounds, the paper carves out a valuable middle ground. The idea of parameterizing violations of faithfulness is compelling, allowing practitioners to express degrees of belief about the underlying causal structure rather than making a binary choice. The combination of ideas from causal discovery, instrumental variables, and linear programming is creative and well-motivated.

The experimental results, though limited, are promising. The proposed Witness Protection Program (WPP) appears to produce significantly tighter and more accurate bounds compared to standard baselines, demonstrating the potential utility of the method in practice.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the core idea is interesting, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed.

1.  **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical justification for repurposing Entner's Rule 1 could be strengthened. The paper suggests using a witness W as an instrumental variable, but the conditions under which this is a valid or well-motivated choice are not fully elaborated. A more explicit discussion of the assumed causal structures (e.g., relating to the standard IV model in Figure 1d) that this "inversion" of the rule relies upon would make the foundations of the method clearer.

2.  **Parameter Selection:** A significant practical limitation is the lack of clear guidance on how to set the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). These parameters directly control the trade-off between the strength of the assumptions and the width of the resulting bounds. Without principled criteria for their selection, a user is left to choose them arbitrarily, which could undermine the objectivity of the analysis. The paper would be much stronger if it included a more thorough investigation into setting or eliciting these parameters from domain knowledge.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The clarity of the paper could be improved in several areas. The description of the experimental setup and the implementation details of the algorithm are somewhat brief, which may hinder reproducibility. For example, the criteria for selecting the final reported bound when multiple (W,Z) pairs are found could be explained more clearly. The definitions of the parameters in Table 1 are not fully self-contained within the main text.

4.  **Typos:** There are several typographical errors that detract from the paper's professionalism. For example:
    *   On page 6, the text says, "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1," but it refers to Algorithm 1.
    *   On page 8, the text refers to "Table 5," but this table does not exist (it is likely meant to be Table 3).

**Originality and Significance**

The problem of estimating causal effects under unmeasured confounding is of central importance. While building on existing work (notably Entner et al. and the IV literature), the synthesis of these ideas is novel. The approach of using observed conditional independencies not to make a hard decision about the causal graph, but rather to justify a *relaxation* of a stronger model (the IV model), is an original and significant contribution. If the methodological weaknesses regarding parameter selection can be addressed, this technique has the potential to become a valuable tool for applied causal inference. However, its generality needs to be demonstrated with more extensive experiments.

In summary, the paper addresses an important problem with a creative and promising approach. However, its impact is currently limited by a lack of clarity in its theoretical foundations, a crucial missing discussion on parameter selection, and several presentational issues.
