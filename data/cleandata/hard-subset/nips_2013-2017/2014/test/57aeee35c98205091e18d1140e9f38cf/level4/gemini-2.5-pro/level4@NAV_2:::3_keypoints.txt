- Introduces Witness Protection Program (WPP) to compute bounds on ACE by relaxing the faithfulness assumption.  
- Reinterprets Entner et al.'s admissible-set search to identify "witness" variables used in an IV-style bounding framework.  
- Introduces relaxation parameters to quantify near-independencies and uses linear programming (Dawid & Ramsahai) to derive ACE bounds.  

Strengths:
- Principled approach that models and quantifies weak/approximate conditional independencies instead of treating faithfulness as binary.  
- Novel combination of causal discovery, instrumental-variable ideas, and linear-program bounding that may spur new research.  
- Addresses an important practical problem: robustness to violations of faithfulness with finite data.  

Weaknesses:
- Output is a posterior over bounds and gives no guidance on the ACE distribution within bounds, making practical interpretation and decision-making difficult.  
- Experimental evaluation is flawed: the error metric favors wider intervals and prevents fair comparison with point estimators.  
- Practical utility is limited: real-data example (influenza) still yields wide intervals that do not provide actionable conclusions.  

Detailed evaluation:
- Quality: Method appears technically sound but leaves key choices (e.g., selection of relaxation parameters) unresolved and experiments insufficient.  
- Clarity: Paper is dense with many parameters and algorithmic stages; supplement likely needed for reproducibility.  
- Originality: Approach is original, combining existing ideas into a novel framework for bounding under relaxed assumptions.  
- Significance: Conceptually significant for opening new directions, but current implementation limits immediate practical impact.  

Summary:
- WPP is an interesting, technically novel framework for causal inference under unfaithfulness, but interpretation and empirical validation are inadequate for it to be an off-the-shelf tool.  

Additional actionable comments to authors:
- Clarify early that witness W identifies admissible set Z but is not used in back-door adjustment.  
- Add intuition for each relaxation parameter (w, y, x, β) in terms of the causal graph.  
- Explain how relaxation parameters θ influence the (W, Z) search and admissible-set identification.  
- Provide a concrete example of how users would apply background knowledge during the search.  
- Compare claims about algebraic bounds in w to related work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014).  
- Report how much the back-substitution heuristic tightens bounds in practice.  
- Study sensitivity to sample size (e.g., N=500) since many real studies are smaller than N=5000.  
- Soften subjective language in the conclusion and rephrase claims in neutral scientific terms.  
- Fix typo: "an useful" → "a useful".
