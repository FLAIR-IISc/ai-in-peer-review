Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) under possible violations of the faithfulness assumption. The approach inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1: a variable W that would be used as a “witness” for an admissible adjustment set Z is instead treated as an instrument conditioned on Z. The authors introduce relaxation parameters that bound departures from conditional independences implied by faithfulness, yielding a family of linear programs that produce upper and lower bounds on the ACE. They also present a Bayesian scheme to obtain a posterior distribution over these bounds, analytic inequalities that provide insight and speed, and a back-substitution routine to tighten box constraints efficiently. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza encouragement design illustrate the method.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects causal discovery under faithfulness (e.g., PC), admissible set identification via Rule 1 (Entner et al.), and bounds in instrumental variable models (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai). It is timely given recent interest in robust causal inference and bounding approaches and is related to other work on bounding confounding and inequality constraints in graphical models.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge between faithfulness-based identification and IV bounds via controlled relaxations of conditional independences.
- Clear mathematical formulation using LP, together with analytic bounds and a faster back-substitution heuristic.
- Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty for bounds rather than point estimates.
- Addresses an important practical concern: near-unfaithfulness and model misspecification in observational studies.
- Public code and attention to computational scalability.

Weaknesses
- Interpretation is challenging: the primary output is a posterior over intervals driven by user-chosen relaxation parameters, with limited guidance on how to set or justify these parameters. Without this, the practical relevance of the bounds can be unclear and bounds may become too wide to be useful.
- The method provides no default point estimate within the interval, which many practitioners expect, and readers may misinterpret the interval as a confidence interval around a best-guess.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. The “bias/error” metric used to compare intervals and points is not meaningful for assessing practical utility. Results rely on relatively large sample sizes and few datasets, and the influenza example still yields broad intervals.
- Some presentation is dense. Several key steps and parameter roles would benefit from clearer intuition and a more explicit link between the search phase and the bounding phase.

Quality
Technically sound and grounded in established tools (IV bounds, LP, Dirichlet posteriors). The relaxation framework is well-motivated, and the computational contributions are interesting. However, the paper defers critical choices (relaxation parameters) to future work, and the empirical evaluation does not adequately demonstrate practical performance or calibration (e.g., coverage vs. width, sensitivity to parameter choices, small-n behavior).

Clarity
Generally readable but at times difficult to follow due to abrupt shifts in abstraction and heavy notation. The roles of the relaxation parameters and their interpretation need clearer, early intuition. The relationship between identifying (W,Z) and computing bounds, and how this depends on the relaxation vector, should be explained more explicitly. Tone in the conclusion should be more neutral.

Originality
A novel synthesis: repurposing Rule 1 for IV-style bounding under controlled faithfulness violations, combined with new algebraic bounds and a practical back-substitution algorithm. It is distinct from, though related to, recent work on bounding confounding and inequality constraints.

Significance
Promising direction that could inform robust causal analysis in observational studies. As presented, it is more a methodological framework than a ready-to-use tool for practitioners; stronger guidance on parameterization, interpretability, and empirical validation would increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a central and timely problem with a principled relaxation framework.
- Provides new analytic inequalities and a computationally efficient heuristic.
- Could catalyze cross-fertilization between causal discovery and IV bounding.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited guidance for choosing relaxation parameters and interpreting the resulting intervals.
- Empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate practical utility or robustness.
- Presentation could be clearer, and comparisons to closely related recent work could be expanded.

Other comments and suggestions
- Title and framing: great, memorable name; consider adding a concise schematic early to explain how W, Z, and the relaxation parameters interact.
- Clarify that W serves to identify Z but is not included in the final back-door adjustment when one exists.
- Replace “unstable” with “may not hold in finite samples” when referring to faithfulness-based inferences.
- Avoid “cartoon” and use “schematic” or “illustrative” for figures.
- Explicitly state assumptions needed (e.g., causal ordering W → X or exogeneity conditions) when invoking Rule 1.
- Provide intuition for each relaxation parameter (w, x, y, β, β̄) before formal definitions, and relate them back to the ACE expression.
- Explain how the search for (W,Z) interacts with the chosen relaxation vector θ, and how violations deemed “acceptable” influence admissible sets and bounds.
- Clarify that the posterior is over bounds, not over the ACE; emphasize the difference from confidence intervals or credible intervals for a point estimate.
- Comparisons: include coverage and expected width of intervals, sensitivity to θ, and sample-size scaling; consider comparisons to recent bounding-confounding approaches.
- Back-substitution: offer a quantitative summary of how much it tightens bounds in Section 5.
- Influenza example: briefly discuss why GRP fails as a witness under WPP and how allowing controlled dependence can still yield tighter bounds than standard IV.
- Tone: avoid emotive phrases such as “risky” and “more honest”; use neutral language.

Summary
An interesting and technically solid contribution that relaxes faithfulness to produce ACE bounds via LP, with analytic and computational refinements. The main limitations are interpretability, parameter selection, and a weak empirical demonstration. With clearer guidance on setting relaxations and stronger evaluation, WPP could become a useful component of the causal inference toolkit.
