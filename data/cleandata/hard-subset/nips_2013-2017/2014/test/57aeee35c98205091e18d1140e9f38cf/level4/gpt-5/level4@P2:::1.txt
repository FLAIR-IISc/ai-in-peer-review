This paper studies estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure among covariates is unknown. Building on Entner et al. (2013), which uses conditional independence tests (Rule 1) under faithfulness to select admissible covariates for back-door adjustment, the authors invert that logic: they treat the identified (W, Z) pairs as instruments-with-controls, relax faithfulness by allowing bounded departures from conditional independences, and derive bounds—not point estimates—on the ACE. They formulate the problem via linear programming in the spirit of Ramsahai (2012), extend it with a Bayesian treatment that yields a posterior over bounds, and propose fast symbolic (algebraic) bounds with a back-substitution procedure to improve scalability. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu-vaccine dataset illustrate the approach.

Overall, the main idea is coherent, interesting, and potentially useful: it offers a principled middle ground between faithfulness-based point identification and standard instrumental-variable (IV) bounds, with tunable relaxations that acknowledge near-unfaithfulness. The engineering of both a full LP-based solution and a faster algebraic approximation is a practical strength. However, I found the role of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 under relaxed faithfulness insufficiently explained: without assuming faithfulness, it is unclear what structural guarantees the Rule 1 conditions provide, and thus how critical they are to the validity or tightness of the proposed bounds. The paper defers the choice of relaxation parameters, which seems central to connecting Rule 1 evidence to constraint strength. Clarifying this dependence would help interpret the empirical robustness seen in the experiments.

Quality:
- The technical framework appears sound, and the connection to prior IV-bounding work is appropriate. The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach to propagate uncertainty is a thoughtful addition.
- Key methodological choices (especially how to set relaxation parameters and how evidence for Rule 1 influences them) are postponed, limiting practical guidance.
- Derivations are dense, and some proofs are deferred to the supplement; more intuition in the main text would improve accessibility.

Clarity:
- The paper is thorough but hard to follow in places. A clearer exposition of how Rule 1, bounded deviations from independence, and the resulting constraints interact would be valuable.
- Minor inconsistencies (noted below) should be corrected.

Originality:
- The inversion of Rule 1 from covariate selection for point ID to witness/admissible-set discovery for bounding, combined with bounded violations of faithfulness and a posterior-over-bounds perspective, is novel to my knowledge.
- The algebraic bounds and back-substitution routine contribute a practical and original computational angle.

Significance:
- As a conservative alternative to fragile point-ID procedures, the method could be broadly useful in observational studies.
- Its impact will depend on offering concrete guidance for choosing relaxation parameters and demonstrating that bounds are informative across diverse settings.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- What is the precise theoretical role of Rule 1 when faithfulness is relaxed? Does satisfying Rule 1 (approximately) yield specific inequalities or parameter range restrictions, or is it mainly a heuristic for proposing (W, Z) pairs?
- How should evidence strength for the premises of Rule 1 map to the relaxation parameters? Is there a principled calibration (e.g., via Bayes factors or effect-size estimates) rather than fixed defaults?
- In cases where no exact witness exists, how sensitive are the bounds to “approximate” witnesses? Can you characterize when approximate independences still yield informative bounds?

Pros:
- Conceptually compelling compromise between faithfulness-based identification and IV bounds.
- LP framework extended with a Bayesian posterior over bounds.
- Efficient algebraic bounds and back-substitution improve scalability.
- Empirical results suggest robustness and, in the flu example, tighter bounds than standard IV.

Cons:
- Dependence on Rule 1 under relaxed faithfulness is not fully justified.
- Limited guidance on setting relaxation parameters.
- Dense presentation; some notational and editorial issues.
- Comparisons to Entner et al. under standard thresholds are limited.

Minor comments:
1) Section 3 phrasing: “violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing. Faithfulness violations typically mean extra independences implied by parameter cancellations. Here, you bound departures from independence (i.e., constrain dependence magnitudes). Consider rephrasing to “bounded departures from conditional independence (relaxations of faithfulness).”
2) In experiments, please also report Entner et al.’s method using their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as yielding uninformative bounds (e.g., [−1, 1]). This would contextualize the trade-off between informativeness and robustness.
3) p. 8: If X=1 denotes vaccination and Y=1 denotes “not hospitalized,” why does a negative ACE indicate a better vaccine? With that coding, a positive ACE would seem desirable. Please clarify the coding or the interpretation.
4) The item labeled “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is referred to as “Table 5” in the text; please unify.

This is an interesting and promising paper; improving the explanation of how Rule 1 informs the relaxed constraints and providing guidance on parameter selection would substantially strengthen it.
