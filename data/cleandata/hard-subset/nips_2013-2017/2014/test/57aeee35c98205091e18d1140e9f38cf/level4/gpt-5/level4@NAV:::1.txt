This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal relations among covariates are not fully specified. It builds on a rule from Entner et al. (2013) intended to select a set of observed covariates that suffices to block all back-door paths. While Entner et al.’s justification depends on the faithfulness assumption, this work proposes using the rule to obtain bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, and develops a method in the spirit of Ramsahai (2012).

I have not digested all the technical details, but the central idea is coherent, interesting, and potentially useful. What I found unclear is the role of Entner et al.’s conditions within this framework. Without assuming faithfulness, those conditions do not have straightforward implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that, when verified, they could inform the choice of relaxation parameters; however, the paper explicitly sets that aside. It appears that the machinery does not rely critically on Entner et al.’s conditions, and I wonder whether the method’s apparent robustness in the experiments is related to this. Clarification would be appreciated.

Some minor comments:
1. First paragraph of Section 3: “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…” reads oddly. Unfaithfulness typically entails permitting additional independences rather than violating independence. It seems the proposed framework is meant to allow “near-violations” of faithfulness by constraining the strength of dependence.
2. In the experiments, it would be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.’s method with their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as yielding uninformative bounds.
3. In the second paragraph on p. 8, if X=1 denotes a flu shot and Y=1 denotes not being hospitalized, why would a negative ACE indicate a desirable vaccine?
4. The “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is cited as “Table 5” in the text.

Overall, this is an interesting paper, though the details are not easy to follow.
