Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure among observed covariates is unknown. It builds on Entner et al. (2013), who propose a rule (Rule 1) to identify an admissible set for back-door adjustment under the faithfulness assumption. Rather than using Rule 1 to produce point estimates, this paper inverts the role of the “witness” and uses it to generate instrumental-variable-like constraints, then relaxes faithfulness via bounded deviations from conditional independences. Bounds on the ACE are obtained through a linear programming approach in the spirit of Dawid (2002) and Ramsahai (2012), with additional computational relaxations yielding symbolic algebraic bounds and a faster back-substitution procedure. A Bayesian treatment places constrained Dirichlet priors on contingency tables to propagate sampling uncertainty and yields posterior distributions over ACE bounds. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination study illustrate that the proposed Witness Protection Program (WPP) can provide informative bounds that are often tighter than standard IV bounds (Balke & Pearl, 1997) while being more conservative than point estimates based solely on faithfulness.

Strengths
- Originality: The idea of “inverting” Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to produce IV-style bounds rather than point adjustment, combined with explicit, tunable relaxations of faithfulness encoded as local bounded deviations, is novel. The derivation of symbolic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm offer additional methodological contributions.
- Quality: The formulation using polytopes and linear programs follows established work on causal bounds, and the Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach appropriately captures sampling uncertainty without placing priors directly on unidentifiable latent structures. The empirical section compares against naïve estimators and the faithfulness-based point estimator.
- Significance: Bounding approaches are valuable in observational studies when identification is fragile. The method appears robust in synthetic settings and yields considerably tighter bounds than standard IV in the flu example, which could make it a useful complement to existing tools.
- Clarity (partial): The high-level motivation is clear, and the paper provides both numerical and approximate analytical machinery.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Role of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 without faithfulness: The paper relies on Rule 1 to propose witness/admissible-set pairs but then explicitly relaxes faithfulness. Without faithfulness, the conditions W ⊥ Y | Z ∪ {X} and W ⊥̸ Y | Z have no straightforward structural implications. It is therefore unclear why satisfying Rule 1 (approximately) should be central to the bounding procedure rather than, say, any heuristic for proposing candidate instruments and covariates. My best guess is that Rule 1 informs which relaxations are plausible and how to choose the relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄), but the paper defers parameter selection. As a result, it is difficult to see whether the method’s empirical robustness stems from Rule 1 per se or from the subsequent bounding machinery. Please clarify precisely how the Rule 1 tests influence (i) which pairs (W, Z) are considered; (ii) which constraints are imposed; and (iii) how relaxation parameters should be set or prioritized.
- Parameter selection: The performance and informativeness of the bounds will hinge on w, x, y, β, β̄. While the supplement reportedly discusses this, the main paper would benefit from principled guidance, sensitivity analyses, and, ideally, default choices linked to diagnostics (e.g., strength of conditional dependence/independence tests).
- Scope and generality: The approach is developed for binary variables and modest |Z|. Discussion of scalability, runtime, and how the back-substitution bounds degrade versus the full LP would help readers assess practical applicability.
- Clarity (technical): Some notation is heavy and the dependence of constraints on ζ, κ, η, ω could be summarized more intuitively (e.g., a diagram of the mapping and where relaxations enter). A brief worked example would help.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance (per NIPS criteria)
- Technical soundness: The approach appears sound and consistent with prior bounding literature. I could not verify every derivation, but the framework is coherent and the numerical procedures are standard for this class of problems.
- Clarity: The exposition is dense. Greater emphasis on the conceptual role of Rule 1 under relaxed faithfulness, clearer guidance on parameter choice, and tighter alignment between main text and supplement would improve readability.
- Originality: The combination of inverted witness use, bounded faithfulness violations, posterior over bounds, and symbolic constraints is novel.
- Significance: Likely to be useful for practitioners seeking honest inference in observational studies, especially where IVs are weak and full identification is doubtful.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually interesting compromise between faithfulness-based identification and IV bounds.
- Methodologically solid, with both exact and approximate computational tools.
- Demonstrates practical gains in a real dataset.

Cons:
- Insufficient clarity on the necessity and role of Entner’s conditions once faithfulness is relaxed.
- Limited guidance on choosing relaxation parameters and interpreting sensitivity.
- Exposition and notation could be streamlined; some inconsistencies/typos remain.

Requests and minor comments
1) Section 3 phrasing: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing. Faithfulness violations typically mean additional independencies beyond those entailed by the graph. Here you seem to allow small dependencies where faithfulness would require zero; i.e., bounded deviations from independence. Please rephrase to avoid confusion.
2) Experiments: Please also report performance of Entner et al.’s method under their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as uninformative bounds, to isolate the effect of your relaxations versus their decision thresholds.
3) Flu example: There seems to be an inconsistency. If X=1 denotes vaccination and Y=1 denotes not hospitalized, then a negative ACE would imply vaccination increases hospitalization, which is undesirable. If, as I believe, Y=1 denotes hospitalized (as stated elsewhere), then a negative ACE indicates benefit. Please reconcile the definitions and the sign interpretation.
4) Table numbering: “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is cited as “Table 5” in the text; please fix.
5) Practical guidance: Include a brief section on computational cost (LP vs back-substitution), typical runtimes, and how close the approximate bounds are to the exact ones.
6) Sensitivity plots: For the real data, provide sensitivity of bounds to w, x, y, β, β̄ to help readers calibrate the trade-off between assumptions and informativeness.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution. With clearer articulation of the role of Rule 1 under relaxed faithfulness and more guidance on choosing relaxation parameters, the paper would be significantly stronger.
