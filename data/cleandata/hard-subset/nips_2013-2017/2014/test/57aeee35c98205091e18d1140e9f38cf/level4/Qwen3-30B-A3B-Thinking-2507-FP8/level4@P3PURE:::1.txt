

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is incompletely specified. It extends a rule from Entner et al. (2013), which selects covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths, by using it to derive bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates, following Ramsahai (2012). While the core idea is conceptually sound and potentially valuable, I have concerns regarding the role of Entner et al.'s conditions in the proposed framework.  

The paper justifies Entner et al.'s rule under faithfulness, but the approach explicitly avoids this assumption. Without faithfulness, Entner et al.'s conditions lack clear causal interpretation. I question their necessity in the bound derivation: the method appears to depend only on the covariate selection mechanism, not the specific conditions. The apparent robustness in experiments may stem from this independence, but the paper does not clarify whether the conditions influence relaxation parameters (as suggested in the draft). A detailed explanation of this relationship is essential.  

Minor comments:  
1. Section 3, first paragraph: The phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is confusing. Unfaithfulness arises from *extra* independencies, not violations; the framework likely aims to model *bounded deviations* from faithfulness by constraining dependence magnitudes.  
2. Experiments: Reporting Entner et al.'s method performance under stringent thresholds (treating "don't know" as uninformative bounds) would strengthen comparisons.  
3. Page 8, second paragraph: If \(X=1\) denotes flu vaccination and \(Y=1\) denotes hospitalization, a *negative* ACE implies vaccination *increases* hospitalization risk—contradicting the claim that it "suggests a desirable vaccine." This should be corrected.  
4. Page 8: Table 1 is cited as "Table 5" in the text; this must be fixed.  

The paper presents an interesting approach, though the technical exposition is challenging to follow. Clarification on the role of Entner et al.'s conditions is critical for assessing the method's theoretical foundation.
