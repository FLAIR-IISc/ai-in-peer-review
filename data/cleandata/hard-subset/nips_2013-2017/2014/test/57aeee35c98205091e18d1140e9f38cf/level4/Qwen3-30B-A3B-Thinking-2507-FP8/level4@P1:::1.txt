

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is not fully specified. The authors build on Entner et al.'s (2013) rule for identifying admissible sets to block back-door paths, but instead of using it for point estimates (which rely on the faithfulness assumption), they propose to use it to derive bounds on the ACE. The approach extends Ramsahai (2012) and introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) to provide more robust causal estimates by relaxing the faithfulness assumption.

The main contribution is a method that generates posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE using linear programming and Bayesian inference, rather than point estimates. This addresses a practical concern in observational studies where faithfulness may not hold, offering a more conservative and reliable alternative to existing methods. The paper is well-motivated, as practitioners often rely on point estimates that can be unreliable when faithfulness is violated.

The technical approach is generally sound, though the connection between Entner et al.'s rule and the proposed framework requires clarification. Specifically, without the faithfulness assumption, the conditions in Entner's rule (W ⊥ Y | Z ∪ {X} and W ⊥ Y | Z) do not necessarily provide information about the causal structure. The authors state they "leave the choice of relaxation parameters aside," but the paper does not sufficiently explain how these conditions relate to the relaxation parameters or why they are necessary for the proposed method. This is a significant concern, as it appears the method's robustness might derive from not critically depending on these conditions, which weakens the paper's core argument.

The paper's main strength is its practical focus on providing usable bounds for observational studies rather than relying on potentially fragile assumptions. The experiments with synthetic data and the influenza vaccine case study demonstrate the method's utility in real-world settings. The WPP framework would be particularly valuable for practitioners who want to be more cautious about untestable assumptions.

However, I have several concerns about the paper's presentation and technical clarity:

1. The phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" (Section 3) is misleading. Unfaithfulness typically involves unexpected independences rather than violations of independence. The authors appear to intend to express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining dependence magnitudes, but this requires clearer explanation.

2. In the influenza vaccine example (Section 5), the paper states "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was hospitalized," but claims "a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine." This is inconsistent; if Y=1 indicates hospitalization, a negative ACE would mean vaccination is associated with *lower* hospitalization rates, which is desirable. The paper should clarify this to avoid confusion.

3. The paper cites "Table 1" as "Table 5" on page 8, which is a clear typographical error.

4. The authors should include a comparison with Entner et al.'s method using their stringent thresholds, where "don't know" answers are treated as providing uninformative bounds (e.g., [-1,1]). This would provide a more complete picture of how the proposed method compares to the baseline.

The paper makes an interesting contribution to causal inference by providing a more robust alternative to faithfulness-based methods. However, the unclear connection between Entner's rule and the proposed framework weakens the paper's argument. The authors should clarify how the conditions in Entner's rule relate to the relaxation parameters and why they are necessary for the proposed method.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, addresses a relevant problem, and provides a useful tool for causal inference practitioners. With improved clarity regarding the relationship between Entner's rule and the proposed method, this would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

Strengths:
- Practical focus on providing robust bounds rather than fragile point estimates
- Clear motivation for the problem and relevance to observational studies
- Demonstrated utility through synthetic and real-world experiments
- Well-structured methodology with clear Bayesian and linear programming components

Weaknesses:
- Unclear connection between Entner's rule conditions and the proposed framework
- Confusing phrasing about "violations of faithfulness"
- Inconsistent explanation of the influenza vaccine example
- Typographical errors in table references

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to clarify the relationship between Entner's rule and the proposed method.
