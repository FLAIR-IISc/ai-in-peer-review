This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully specified. It considers a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, Entner et al.'s rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. The authors propose using this rule to provide bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, and develop a method following Ramsahai (2012).

Although I have not fully digested all the technical details, the main idea appears sensible, interesting, and useful. Nevertheless, I am puzzled by the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that the conditions, if verified, are relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this matter aside. It seems to me that the machinery does not crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the apparent robustness of the method in the experiments has anything to do with this. I would appreciate clarification on this issue.

Some minor comments:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the statement "… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …" seems unusual. Isn't unfaithfulness a matter of allowing extra independence rather than violating independence? It appears to me that the proposed framework is actually intended to allow/express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the experiments, it may be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when their stringent thresholds are used, treating the answer of "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds.

3. In the second paragraph on page 8, if "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized", why does a negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine?

4. "Table 1" on top of page 8 is cited as "Table 5" in the text.

This is an interesting paper, although the details are not easy to follow.
