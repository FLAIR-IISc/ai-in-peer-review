This paper addresses the problem of average causal effect (ACE) estimation between two binary variables in settings where the causal structure relating covariates is not fully specified. The authors build upon a covariate selection rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013), originally justified under the faithfulness assumption, and adapt it to provide bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates. Their approach leverages techniques from Ramsahai (2012) to achieve this.

I found the core idea of utilizing the Entner et al. (2013) rule for bounding the ACE to be both interesting and potentially useful, despite not having fully digested all the technical details. However, a key point of confusion for me concerns the role of the conditions outlined in the Entner et al. (2013) rule within this framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions lose their direct implications regarding the causal structure. While I speculate that these conditions *might* inform the selection of relaxation parameters, the paper explicitly postpones discussion of this matter. Consequently, it appears the method’s machinery does not critically depend on satisfying the Entner et al. (2013) conditions. I am curious whether the observed robustness in the experimental results is, at least in part, attributable to this decoupling. Clarification on this point would significantly strengthen the paper.

Specifically, I am unsure *why* adhering to the Entner et al. (2013) rule – beyond potentially influencing relaxation parameters – is beneficial in this bounds-estimation setting if faithfulness cannot be assumed. Does the rule still serve as a heuristic for identifying ‘good’ sets of covariates even in the absence of faithfulness?

I have several minor comments that could further improve the paper:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrasing “… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …” is somewhat counterintuitive. Unfaithfulness typically manifests as *allowing* additional independence relationships, rather than violating existing ones. The proposed framework seems to better capture “near-violations” of faithfulness by constraining the *strength* of dependence, rather than describing a violation of independence itself. 

2. The experimental section would benefit from including a performance comparison with the original Entner et al. (2013) method, employing their strict thresholds for covariate selection. When the method returns “don’t know” due to failing to meet those thresholds, this could be interpreted as reporting uninformative bounds, providing a valuable baseline for comparison.

3. On page 8, in the second paragraph, the statement that “X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized” and that a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine is potentially misleading. A negative ACE in this context would imply that receiving the flu shot *increases* the probability of hospitalization, which would clearly be an undesirable outcome. This requires careful rewording to avoid confusion.

4. There is an inconsistency in the table citations. “Table 1” is mentioned on top of page 8, but is referred to as “Table 5” within the text. This should be corrected for clarity.

Overall, this is a promising paper that tackles an important problem. While the technical details require further study, the presented approach appears to offer a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference, particularly in scenarios where full causal knowledge is unavailable. Addressing the clarification request regarding the role of the Entner et al. (2013) conditions and resolving the minor comments would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
