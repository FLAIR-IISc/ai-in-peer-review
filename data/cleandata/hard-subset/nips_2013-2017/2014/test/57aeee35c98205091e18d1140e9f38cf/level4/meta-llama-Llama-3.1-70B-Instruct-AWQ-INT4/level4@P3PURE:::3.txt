The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute bounds on the inferred average causal effect (ACE) while considering possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. This is achieved by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, which are then used within a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link. These bounds are obtained by adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates, subject to the introduced constraints.

The problem addressed in the paper is both timely and interesting, and I appreciate the approach and ideas presented. The WPP algorithm represents a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, leveraging well-known mathematical tools to solve the corresponding maximization/minimization problems. However, whether this method is the most suitable for capturing this impact remains to be seen.

A major concern with the WPP algorithm is that its output is challenging to interpret from a conceptual standpoint. The method produces a distribution over bounds that are a function of 'unfaithful relaxations' relative to a given model. Nevertheless, the likelihood of these unfaithful instances and the relevance/realism of the resulting bounds remain unclear. Furthermore, many relaxations lead to wide bounds, which can become less useful in practice. The method does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, although users may interpret it that way.

The experimental evaluation in the paper is poor. The 'bias' evaluation metric used to compare the WPP method with its competitors is meaningless, as it measures two completely different things. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output provides a valuable addition to practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. Moreover, the practical performance of the WPP algorithm on the influenza dataset is worryingly weak, with the ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64) implying that no relevant conclusions can be drawn. Although this could be the fairest assessment of all.

In terms of quality, the approach introduced in the paper is technically sound, with many details explicated in the supplementary file (which I did not thoroughly check). The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as choosing the relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article, making it difficult to understand potential issues with this part of the method. Unfortunately, the experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough.

The paper is reasonably well-written, except for the conclusion. The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving subtly different parameters are quite hard to follow, partly due to the lack of explanation of certain details of the method. While I think I understand the overall process, I am not sure I could fully implement it from just this description; the supplement may be sufficient.

As far as I can tell, the method is a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently different from, recent work on the same problem. As such, it may stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of several approaches on the subject.

The significance of the paper mainly lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate other techniques to solve this highly challenging problem. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to represent the finished article for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm could well end up playing an important role.

In summary, the paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem, presents an interesting method that will undoubtedly find an audience, and is reasonably well-written. However, the current application is limited, and the experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
