This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between variables X and Y in observational studies. The core idea involves inverting Entner’s Rule 1, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to accommodate potential path cancellations, and formulating constraints – expressed as inequalities – within a linear programming framework to derive these bounds. The authors demonstrate promising results, suggesting tighter bounds compared to existing state-of-the-art approaches, despite acknowledging current computational limitations.

**Quality:**
The authors present a technically sound approach to a challenging problem in causal inference. Combining established concepts – instrumental variables, Entner’s Rule, and linear programming – in a novel way is a significant contribution. The proposed method aims to address the limitations of relying solely on the faithfulness assumption, offering a more nuanced approach to handling potential confounding. However, the justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in reverse requires further clarification. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the underlying causal structure (as depicted in Fig. 2) necessary for this inverse application. Additionally, the criteria for selecting appropriate relaxation parameters are not sufficiently detailed. Given the significant influence of these parameters on the derived bounds, a more thorough investigation and justification for their selection is needed. While the reported bounds appear narrow, the paper lacks a rigorous analysis of sensitivity to parameter choices.

**Clarity:**
The theoretical exposition is generally well-structured and comprehensible, with the exception of the aforementioned point regarding the inverse application of Entner’s Rule 1. However, the presentation of the experimental results lacks sufficient detail. The implementation of the algorithm is not fully explained, and the definitions of parameters presented (e.g., those referenced in what appears to be Table 1, but potentially Table 5) are unclear. Several minor typographical errors were noted (e.g., the reference to “Table 1” on page 6, where no algorithm table is present, and the reference to “Table 5” on page 8, which likely refers to Table 1). These detract from the overall clarity.

**Originality:**
The problem of bounding causal effects has been extensively studied, as acknowledged by the authors. Therefore, the novelty of this work lies not in the problem itself, but in the specific combination of techniques and the relaxation of the faithfulness assumption. While this combination represents a novel approach, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how it differs from, and improves upon, existing methods in specific scenarios. A clearer articulation of the unique contribution is needed to firmly establish the originality of the work.

**Significance:**
The potential to derive tighter bounds on the ACE is a significant contribution to the field of causal inference. However, the current evaluation is limited by the scope of the experiments. While the synthetic studies are valuable, the generalizability of the method remains uncertain. A more comprehensive assessment, including a wider range of datasets and comparisons with established methods, is required to fully demonstrate the significance of this work. The paper tackles an important problem – accurately estimating causal effects in observational studies – but the validity and robustness of the proposed method need to be more thoroughly established through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of established techniques to address a challenging problem.
*   Potential for tighter bounds on the ACE compared to existing methods.
*   Addresses a core limitation of relying solely on the faithfulness assumption.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient justification for the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1.
*   Lack of clear criteria for selecting relaxation parameters.
*   Limited experimental validation and concerns about generalizability.
*   Clarity issues related to the implementation details and some typographical errors.
