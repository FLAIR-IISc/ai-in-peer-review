The paper proposes WPP, an LP-based algorithm that computes bounds on the ACE by introducing relaxation parameters to model violations of faithfulness.

Addresses an important, topical problem in causal inference where faithfulness may be violated.

Presents a principled formalization of unfaithful/weak interactions via explicit relaxation parameters.

Novelly applies linear programming to derive ACE bounds under these relaxed assumptions.

Has potential to inspire further research and cross-fertilize methods for inference under unfaithfulness.

Primary concern: the WPP output depends on user-chosen relaxation parameters, with little guidance on how to set them or assess plausibility.

Many relaxations produce very wide, practically uninformative bounds (e.g., influenza data ACE in (-0.23, 0.64)).

Avoiding a point estimate is principled but risks practitioner misinterpretation of intervals as confidence intervals or implicit point estimates.

Experimental evaluation is weak: using "bias" as a metric is inappropriate for comparing interval outputs to point-estimate methods.

Table 1 and reported results fail to show clear benefits of WPP over standard faithfulness-based point estimates.

Claimed "stability" on real data may indicate bounds are too loose and insensitive to data specifics, not robustness.

Algorithmic description is hard to follow due to many similar parameters and lack of intuition for each parameter's role.

Procedure for selecting relaxation parameters is deferred to future work, limiting assessment of practical utility.

Ambiguity: clarify that witness W is a tool to find adjustment set Z and is not required for final ACE computation.

Request clarification on phrases and assumptions (e.g., "unstable", "cartoon representation", causal sufficiency, W not a descendant of X).

Need clearer explanation of parameters theta/alpha/beta and how admissible set search depends on theta.

Clarify ambiguous statement about "a maximum of 10 free parameters" describing the joint distribution over Z.

Paper overlooks related prior work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014) and should discuss connections.

Back-substitution refinement is interesting but its practical efficacy appears limited and needs empirical discussion.

Experiments use large n=5000; authors should discuss impact of smaller, realistic sample sizes on bound width.

Reiterate that ACE is not uniformly distributed within bounds—practitioners need guidance on what conclusions are warranted.

Conclusion contains emotive/biased language and should adopt a more neutral, precise tone.

Minor issues: typos (e.g., "an useful" -> "a useful", "inuenza" -> "influenza") and several phrasing/parenthesis clarifications needed.
