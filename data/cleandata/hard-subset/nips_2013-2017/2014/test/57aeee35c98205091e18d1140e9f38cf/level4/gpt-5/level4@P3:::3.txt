Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) under possible violations of faithfulness in observational studies. The method inverts Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes Rule 1: rather than using a “witness” W and an admissible set Z to justify back-door adjustment, WPP treats W as an approximate instrument conditional on Z, and bounds the ACE via linear programming subject to relaxations of faithfulness. These relaxations are encoded by parameters controlling (i) deviations from zero direct effect W → Y given X,Z, (ii) deviations from independence between W and latent confounding of X and Y, and (iii) discrepancies between observed and latent conditional probabilities. The paper offers two computational routes: (a) a linear program derived from Dawid and Ramsahai’s polyhedral approach to IV bounds, adapted to the relaxed setting; and (b) faster, looser algebraic bounds with a back-substitution procedure. A Bayesian treatment places constrained Dirichlet priors on observed contingency tables, producing a posterior distribution over ACE bounds. Experiments include synthetic data and an influenza vaccination study, with comparison to naïve estimators and a faithfulness-based point estimator.

This work sits between faithfulness-based identification (e.g., PC algorithm; Spirtes, Glymour, Scheines) and classical IV bounding (Balke and Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai), and relates to recent efforts to bound effects under partial or approximate assumptions (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, and Schölkopf, UAI 2014). It advances the idea that conditional independencies can justify soft constraints rather than all-or-nothing structural claims.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus: estimating causal effects under unmeasured confounding with realistic departures from faithfulness.
- Principled use of inequality constraints and LP geometry to propagate relaxations into ACE bounds.
- Bayesian treatment of uncertainty in observed frequencies with constrained posteriors.
- Practical computational contributions (algebraic bounds and back-substitution) that can scale better than repeated LPs.
- Open-source implementation and a topical application (influenza encouragement design).

Weaknesses
- Interpretability: the primary output is a posterior over intervals whose width depends on user-specified relaxations. Without guidance on setting these parameters, it is hard to assess how informative or credible the bounds are for a given application.
- Evaluation: the comparison metric (distance from the interval to the true ACE) is not directly comparable to point estimators, and can favor trivially wide bounds. More suitable metrics include coverage under misspecification, average width vs. coverage trade-offs, decision loss under plausible priors, and falsification rates of the relaxed constraints. Sensitivity to sample size (e.g., n=500) would also be valuable.
- Scope and assumptions: binary variables and modest Z are assumed; computational cost and tightness under higher-dimensional Z are not fully characterized. The “greater control over violations” claim would be stronger with data-driven heuristics or elicitation protocols for the relaxation parameters and a sensitivity analysis summary.
- Clarity: transitions into the parameterization and the role of each relaxation parameter are abrupt. Some terminology and claims (e.g., “control,” “risky Bayesian approaches”) should be toned down or clarified.

Quality
Technically sound overall and well grounded in the literature on IV bounds and inequality constraints. The geometric construction and Bayesian constrained posterior are appropriate. However, empirical validation of the back-substitution method’s tightness vs. LP (e.g., average inflation of widths, runtime) is only briefly discussed; quantifying this trade-off in Section 5 would strengthen the paper. The reliance on the supplement for key derivations is acceptable, but the main text should provide more intuition for the relaxations and their practical interpretation.

Clarity
Generally readable, but the presentation would benefit from:
- Early, intuitive explanations of the relaxation parameters and their mapping to violations of faithfulness.
- A clearer statement that the witness W is used to propose Z and to justify constraints, not to adjust directly.
- A brief overview of how the search for (W,Z) interacts with the bounding step and how acceptance depends on the relaxation θ.
- More explicit guidance on interpreting the posterior over bounds (not a confidence or credible interval for ACE).

Originality
A novel and useful synthesis: inverting Rule 1 to generate conditional-IV-like constraints, softening faithfulness, and propagating these via LP and algebraic bounds is original. The relation to recent bounding approaches (e.g., Geiger et al., UAI 2014) should be discussed more explicitly to position the contribution.

Significance
Conceptually significant: it offers a middle ground between brittle identification and very weak worst-case bounds. As a practical tool, it is promising but incomplete; the lack of parameter-setting guidance and evaluation calibrated to decisions limits immediate applicability. With stronger empirical validation and user guidance, WPP could become a valuable addition to observational study workflows.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, current topic (causal inference under weak assumptions).
- Introduces a thoughtful and technically grounded framework combining discovery, relaxations, and bounding.
- Provides computational innovations and open-source code that others can build on.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate practical utility, tightness, or robustness relative to alternatives.
- Limited guidance on choosing relaxation parameters; interpretability and decision relevance of the posterior over bounds remain unclear.
- Presentation gaps (abrupt technical transitions; some overstatements) and reliance on the supplement for key insights.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Clarify that W serves to find an admissible Z; W itself is not used for adjustment. Define “witness” and “admissible set” early.
- Provide intuition for each relaxation: which structural violations do w, x, y, β, β̄ represent? Include small toy examples mapping parameter values to graph motifs (e.g., small W → Y direct effect).
- Explain how the search over (W,Z) depends on θ, and how the falsification step uses the inequalities.
- Report, for back-substitution vs. LP: average runtime, mean width inflation, and frequency of infeasible expected tables (now mentioned only in passing).
- Evaluation: add coverage and width summaries, sensitivity to n (e.g., 500 and 1000), and decision-oriented metrics. For the “no witness” cases, report falsification rates and how often WPP rejects models.
- Influenza study: emphasize that bounds are assumption-dependent; include a sensitivity plot over θ to show how conclusions vary. Avoid language suggesting that the bounds reflect a posterior for ACE.
- Tone down emotive phrases (“risky,” “more honest”), and replace with neutral descriptions. Avoid “cartoon” for figures; “schematic” is preferable. Fix minor typos (e.g., “influenza,” “an useful” → “a useful,” “unstable” → “may not hold”).
- Cite and compare to Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf (UAI 2014) and related inequality-based bounding work to better situate the contribution.

Overall
An interesting and timely contribution that advances bounding under approximate faithfulness. With clearer guidance on parameter selection, stronger empirical validation, and improved exposition, this approach could have meaningful impact. As it stands, the paper’s main contribution is conceptual and methodological; the empirical case for practical utility needs strengthening.
