This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in a setting where the causal structure over the covariates is not fully known. The authors build upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) for selecting a sufficient set of observed covariates to block all back-door paths. While the original rule from Entner et al. is justified only under the faithfulness assumption, this paper cleverly adapts it to provide bounds on the ACE, rather than point estimates, thereby relaxing this strong assumption. The proposed method for developing these bounds follows the framework of Ramsahai (2012).

While I have not been able to verify all the technical details, the central idea is intuitive, interesting, and potentially very useful. The primary question that arose during my reading, however, concerns the specific role of the conditions from Entner et al.'s rule within this new framework. The original justification for these conditions rests on the faithfulness assumption. Without this assumption, the conditions no longer have obvious implications for the underlying causal structure. My best guess is that satisfying these conditions might inform the choice of the relaxation parameters in the bounding method, but the paper explicitly sets this issue aside. Consequently, it appears the core machinery of the proposed method might not critically depend on the Entner et al. conditions. I wonder if the method's apparent robustness in the experiments is related to this. A clarification on the precise role and necessity of these conditions would significantly strengthen the paper and help readers understand its core contribution.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is potentially misleading. Unfaithfulness is typically characterized by the presence of extra conditional independencies, not by the violation of them. It seems the proposed framework is actually intended to express "near-unfaithfulness" by constraining the magnitude of statistical dependence, which is a subtle but important distinction. Clarifying this language would improve precision.

2.  The experimental evaluation would be more comprehensive if it included a direct comparison to the original method of Entner et al. For this comparison, instances where their method returns "don't know" due to its stringent thresholds could be treated as reporting uninformative bounds (e.g., [0, 1]).

3.  In the second paragraph on page 8, there seems to be a contradiction in the interpretation of the ACE. If "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot" and "Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized," a desirable vaccine should result in a positive ACE (i.e., increase the probability of not being hospitalized), not a negative one as stated in the text.

4.  There is a minor typo on page 8: the text refers to "Table 5" when it appears to be referencing the table labeled "Table 1" at the top of the page.

In summary, this is a thought-provoking paper on an important topic. While the technical presentation is dense, the core idea is promising. Addressing the central question about the role of the Entner et al. conditions would greatly improve the clarity and impact of the work.
