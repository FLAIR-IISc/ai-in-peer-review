

This paper introduces a novel method for deriving bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) from variable X to Y. The approach leverages Entner's Rule 1 in its inverse direction, relaxes the faithfulness assumption to permit path-cancellation at specified degrees, and incorporates inequality constraints as key assumptions. The ACE bounds are computed via linear programming under these principles. While the authors have not yet optimized the algorithm for bound derivation, the resulting bounds demonstrate notable narrowness and accuracy relative to state-of-the-art methods.

Quality  
The authors present a novel framework for obtaining tighter ACE bounds than existing approaches, successfully integrating multiple feasible principles. The paper's content is highly interesting in this regard.  
However, critical aspects remain inadequately explained. Specifically, the validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 inversely is not thoroughly justified. The authors must explicitly justify this application by assuming the structural configuration illustrated in Figure 2, and this justification should be clearly articulated and discussed.  
Additionally, the paper lacks generic criteria for selecting appropriate relaxation parameters. Given their significant impact on bound results, the principles governing parameter selection require deeper investigation and inclusion in the work.

Clarity  
The theoretical exposition is generally comprehensive, except for the unresolved points noted above.  
The experimental results, however, are poorly described. The algorithm implementation details remain insufficiently explained, and the parameters referenced in Table 1 (likely intended to be Table 5) are not clearly defined.  
Minor typographical errors are present, including: "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1" (p. 6, where no algorithm table appears) and "Results are summarized in Table 5" (p. 8, where Table 1 appears to be referenced instead).

Originality  
While the problem addressed has been explored in prior work (as acknowledged by the authors), the paper's scope lacks strong originality.  
The integration of three distinct concepts into a unified framework possesses some originality. However, the validity of this combination is not sufficiently substantiated, as highlighted in the Quality critique.

Significance  
The method demonstrates significance in deriving tighter ACE bounds. Nevertheless, its performance has been evaluated only on limited experimental settings. Theoretical generality and broad experimental validation remain unassessed. Although the paper tackles an important problem—quantifying causal relationship strength—the theoretical and experimental validity of the proposed approach must be more thoroughly substantiated.
