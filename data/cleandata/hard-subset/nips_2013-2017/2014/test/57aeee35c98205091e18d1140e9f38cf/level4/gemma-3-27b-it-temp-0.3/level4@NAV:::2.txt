This paper introduces a novel method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) from variable X to Y, leveraging a reversed application of Entner's Rule 1, a relaxation of the faithfulness assumption to allow for path cancellation, and the introduction of inequality constraints. A linear programming scheme is formulated to compute these ACE bounds. While algorithmic optimization is not yet addressed, the resulting bounds appear tighter and more accurate compared to existing state-of-the-art approaches.

**Quality:**

The authors attempt to establish a new method for deriving more accurate ACE bounds than currently available. Their combination of established principles successfully yields tighter bounds, making the work inherently interesting. 

However, the justification for certain bounding criteria remains insufficiently detailed. Specifically, the application of Entner's Rule 1 in reverse requires clearer discussion and explicitly relies on the structural assumptions depicted in Fig. 2, which should be stated. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks guidance on selecting appropriate parameters for the relaxation step. Given the significant impact of these parameters on the resulting bounds, a more thorough investigation and inclusion of parameter selection principles is needed.

**Clarity:**

The theoretical exposition is generally comprehensive, aside from the critical points noted above. 

However, the presentation of experimental results is lacking in detail. The algorithmic implementation is not fully explained, and the parameters defined in Table 1 (potentially a reference error, as it's cited as Table 5) are not clearly defined. 

Minor typographical errors are present; for example, the statement "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1" on page 6 is inaccurate, and the reference to "Table 5" on page 8 may be a mistake (likely referring to Table 1).

**Originality:**

The problem addressed in this paper has been previously investigated, as acknowledged by the authors. Consequently, the overall scope doesn’t represent a substantial leap in originality.

The novelty lies in the unified combination of the three aforementioned ideas. However, the validity of this combination requires more robust justification, as previously discussed.

**Significance:**

This method demonstrates potential significance in deriving tighter ACE bounds. However, its performance evaluation is limited by a relatively small number of experiments. A more rigorous assessment of its generality, both theoretically and through broader experimentation, is necessary. The paper tackles an important problem – evaluating the strength of causal relationships – but the validity of the proposed approach needs further elaboration in both theoretical and experimental contexts.
