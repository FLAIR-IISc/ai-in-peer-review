- Introduces WPP algorithm to compute ACE bounds under potential faithfulness violations using relaxation parameters and linear programming.  
- Timely and relevant problem with a principled, compelling optimization-based approach.  
- Technical approach appears sound with many details in the supplement.  
- Practical utility is unclear because the output (bounds over relaxations) is difficult to interpret.  
- No guidance on how to choose relaxation parameters or a preferred point estimate within bounds.  
- Many relaxation settings yield bounds that are too wide to be informative.  
- The likelihood of unfaithful instances is not assessed, undermining the realism of the bounds.  
- Experimental evaluation is a major weakness and lacks balance and robustness.  
- The bias metric used for comparison is inappropriate because competitors estimate different quantities.  
- Influenza dataset result (ACE interval -0.23 to 0.64) is too wide to draw meaningful conclusions.  
- Algorithmic steps and numerous parameters are hard to follow; main paper may not suffice for implementation.  
- Key methodological details (e.g., how admissible sets Z depend on relaxations) need clearer explanation.  
- Back-substitution for refining bounds may have limited practical efficacy and needs empirical justification.  
- The claim of no previous analytical bounds may be overstated given related prior work.  
- W is only a tool to identify adjustment sets and not needed in final ACE computation—this should be emphasized.  
- Some parameter interpretations and wording (e.g., “unstable,” “conditional”) are imprecise or incorrect.  
- Conclusions contain emotive and biased language and overstate comparisons to other methods.  
- The paper’s main significance is conceptual and potentially influential, but not yet a practical solution for practitioners.  
- Minor issues: typos, unclear phrases (e.g., “cartoon representation”), and unclear use of background knowledge.  
- Concerns about sample size sensitivity (results shown with 5000 points may not generalize to typical smaller datasets).
