This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the underlying causal structure over the covariates is unknown. The authors build upon a rule from Entner et al. (2013), which aims to select a set of covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, the validity of Entner et al.'s rule depends on the faithfulness assumption. The key contribution of this work is to adapt this rule to provide bounds on the ACE, rather than point estimates, thereby relaxing the need for the faithfulness assumption. The proposed method is developed based on the framework of Ramsahai (2012).

While I was not able to verify every technical detail, the central idea is intuitive, interesting, and potentially very useful. My main question, however, concerns the precise role of the conditions from Entner et al.'s rule within the proposed framework. Once the faithfulness assumption is dropped, it is no longer clear what these conditions imply about the causal structure. I suspect that these conditions might inform the choice of the relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly states that this topic is beyond its current scope. This suggests that the core machinery of the proposed method may not critically depend on Entner et al.'s conditions. This raises the question of whether the method's apparent robustness in the experiments is a consequence of this independence. I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify this point.

Some minor comments:

1.  The phrasing in the first paragraph of Section 3, "… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …" seems counter-intuitive. Unfaithfulness typically implies the presence of extra conditional independencies, not the violation of an independence condition. It appears the framework is actually designed to express *near*-faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of statistical dependence, thus allowing for small but non-zero dependencies where strict faithfulness would require them to be zero.

2.  For the experiments, it would be informative to include a comparison with the original method of Entner et al. using their stringent thresholds. Cases where their method abstains ("don't know") could be treated as returning uninformative bounds, providing a more direct baseline for performance.

3.  In the second paragraph on page 8, the interpretation of the flu shot experiment seems incorrect. If X=1 represents receiving a flu shot and Y=1 represents *not* being hospitalized, a desirable vaccine should correspond to a positive ACE (i.e., getting a shot increases the probability of not being hospitalized), not a negative one as stated.

4.  There is a typo on page 8: the table at the top of the page is labeled "Table 1" but is cited as "Table 5" in the text.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with a promising direction, although the technical presentation is dense in places and could be challenging for some readers to follow.
