The paper describes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute bounds on an inferred average causal effect (ACE) that accounts for potential violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. It does so by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, and then employs a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates subject to these constraints.

This is a timely and interesting problem, receiving considerable attention currently. I appreciate the approach and the ideas presented: it’s a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, using well-established mathematical tools to solve the corresponding maximization/minimization problems. 

However, it remains to be seen whether this is the right method to capture that impact. The resulting output is conceptually challenging to interpret: a distribution over bounds that are a function of ‘unfaithful relaxations’ relative to a given model. Crucially, the likelihood of these unfaithful instances – and consequently, the relevance and realism of the resulting bounds – remains unclear. Many relaxations lead to wide bounds that can become practically unhelpful. Moreover, the method does not commit to any specific point estimate within the interval, even though it’s likely that users will interpret it as such.

The experimental evaluation is weak. The ‘bias’ evaluation metric is misleading when comparing WPP with competitors, as they measure fundamentally different quantities. As a result, it’s unclear whether the WPP output constitutes a valuable addition for practitioners or simply a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza dataset is also concerning: an ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) yields little in the way of meaningful conclusions, although this may be the most honest assessment.

**Quality**

The approach is technically sound, with many details explicated in the supplementary material (which I haven’t fully verified). The ideas are generally well introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as selecting the relaxation parameters, are deferred to a future article, making it difficult to assess potential issues with this crucial aspect of the method. The experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness.

**Clarity**

The paper is reasonably well written, though the conclusion could be improved (see comments below). The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving numerous parameters were difficult to follow, in part due to a lack of explanation for certain method details. (I believe I understand the general process, but I am unsure if I could fully implement it solely from this description; the supplement may be sufficient).

**Originality**

To the best of my knowledge, the method represents a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently distinct from, recent work on the same problem, potentially fostering cross-fertilization between different approaches.

**Significance**

The paper's significance lies primarily in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate existing techniques to address this challenging area. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to be the definitive solution for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm could well become important.

**Summary:**

This addresses a relevant and highly topical problem. It presents an interesting method that will undoubtedly attract an audience. Current applications are limited, but likely to extend to more general cases. It is reasonably well written, though improvements are possible. The solutions are technically interesting, but the experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output – a distribution over bounds – remains unclear.
