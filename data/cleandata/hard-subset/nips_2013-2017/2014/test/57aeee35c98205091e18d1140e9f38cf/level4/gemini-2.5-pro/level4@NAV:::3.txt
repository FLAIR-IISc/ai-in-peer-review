This paper presents the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a method for computing bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) that accounts for potential violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The methodology achieves this by introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and then employs a linear programming framework. This framework solves for the upper and lower ACE bounds for a given causal link by adjusting for an appropriate covariate set, subject to the specified constraints.

The problem addressed is receiving considerable attention, making the paper both timely and relevant. The approach and the ideas presented are commendable; it is a principled effort to explicitly quantify the effects of weak or unfaithful interaction patterns, leveraging well-established mathematical optimization tools.

However, it is an open question whether this is the optimal method for capturing the impact of unfaithfulness. The output is conceptually challenging to interpret: a distribution over bounds that are a function of "unfaithful relaxations" relative to a given model. The likelihood of these unfaithful instances, and therefore the practical relevance of the bounds themselves, remains unclear. Many relaxation settings result in bounds that are too wide to be useful. The method also refrains from providing a preferred point estimate within the interval, although it is likely to be interpreted as such by practitioners.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The "bias" metric used for evaluation is inappropriate for comparing the WPP method with its competitors, as they are fundamentally measuring different quantities. This ambiguity makes it difficult to determine if the WPP output is a valuable tool for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. Furthermore, the performance on the real-world influenza dataset is concerningly poor, yielding an ACE interval of (-0.23, 0.64) that permits virtually no meaningful conclusion—though this may indeed be the most honest possible assessment of the data.

**Quality**
The proposed approach is technically sound, with numerous details provided in the supplementary material (which I have not checked in detail). The core ideas are introduced effectively, though the broader context is sometimes lost. Critical aspects, such as the selection of relaxation parameters, are deferred to future work, which makes it challenging to assess potential issues with this component of the method. The experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness.

**Clarity**
The paper is reasonably well-written, with the exception of the conclusion (see comments). The algorithmic steps and their subsequent modifications, involving many nuanced parameters, were difficult to follow. This was partly due to an insufficient explanation of certain methodological details. (While I believe I grasp the overall process, I am not confident I could implement it solely from this description; the supplement might provide the necessary details).

**Originality**
To my knowledge, the method represents a novel synthesis of existing techniques. It is closely related to but distinct from other recent work on this problem. As such, it has the potential to stimulate a productive cross-fertilization of ideas in the field.

**Significance**
The paper's primary significance lies in the ideas and direction it contributes, demonstrating how to integrate different techniques to tackle this challenging problem. In its current form, the implementation is unlikely to be a finished tool for practitioners. Nevertheless, some of the concepts employed in the WPP algorithm could prove to be important in future developments.

**Summary**
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem. It proposes an interesting method that is likely to find an audience and could be extended to more general cases. The technical solutions are notable. The manuscript is reasonably well-written but could be improved. The experimental evaluation is poor, and the practical relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
