This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully specified. It considers a rule proposed in Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, Entner et al.'s rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. The authors propose using this rule to provide bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, and develop a method following Ramsahai (2012).

The main idea of the paper seems interesting and useful, although I have not fully digested all the technical details. However, I am puzzled by the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that the conditions, if verified, are relevant to the choice of the relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly puts this matter aside. It appears that the machinery does not crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the apparent robustness of the method in the experiments is related to this. I would appreciate a clarification on this issue.

Some minor comments:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the statement "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" seems unusual. Isn't unfaithfulness a matter of allowing extra independence rather than violating independence? It appears that the proposed framework is intended to allow or express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the experiments, it may be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when their stringent thresholds are used, taking the answer of "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds.

3. In the second paragraph on page 8, if "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized", why does a negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine?

4. The table on top of page 8 is cited as "Table 5" in the text, but it is labeled as "Table 1".

Overall, this is an interesting paper, although the details are not easy to follow.
