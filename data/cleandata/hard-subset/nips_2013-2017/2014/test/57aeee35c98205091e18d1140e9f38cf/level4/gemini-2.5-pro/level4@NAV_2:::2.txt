This paper presents a novel methodology for estimating bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data where unmeasured confounding is a concern. The central idea is to reinterpret the output of the witness-based search procedure for admissible sets developed by Entner et al. [6]. Instead of using a witness variable `W` and a covariate set `Z` to justify a point estimate of the ACE via back-door adjustment, the authors propose using the pair `(W, Z)` to motivate an instrumental variable (IV) framework. The primary contribution lies in relaxing the strict faithfulness assumption by allowing for bounded violations of conditional independencies. These relaxations are then integrated as constraints into a linear programming formulation, building on the work of Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16], to derive posterior distributions over the ACE bounds. The paper also introduces an algebraic approximation of the bounds to improve computational efficiency.

**Quality**
The paper's main strength is its technically sound and interesting approach, which thoughtfully combines concepts from causal discovery, instrumental variables, and linear programming to tackle the fundamental problem of ACE estimation. The proposed "Witness Protection Program" offers a principled and appealing compromise between relying on the strong, and potentially fragile, faithfulness assumption and resorting to the often wide and uninformative bounds produced by standard IV analysis.

However, the theoretical justification for the core modeling decision—treating a witness `W` as a candidate instrument—could be strengthened. Using Entner's Rule 1 in this inverted manner seems to implicitly rely on an underlying causal structure that is not explicitly stated or justified. A more thorough discussion of this foundational assumption would improve the paper's rigor. Furthermore, a significant concern is the selection of the relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`). These parameters are critical as they directly control the trade-off between assumption strength and bound width, yet the paper offers little guidance on how to choose them in a principled way. The work would be substantially stronger if it included a methodology or at least a detailed sensitivity analysis for setting these values.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written, and the theoretical exposition in Sections 2 and 3 is clear and largely self-contained. However, the clarity of the experimental section could be improved. The description of the algorithm's implementation and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to allow for full understanding or reproduction. For example, the definitions for the parameters in Table 1 are not explained clearly within the text. There are also a few minor typographical errors that could be easily corrected in a revision. For instance, the text on page 6 refers to the algorithm being in "Table 1" when it is presented in "Algorithm 1," and the text on page 8 refers to results in "Table 5," which appear to be in "Table 3."

**Originality**
While the problem of ACE bounding is well-established, the paper's originality lies in its novel synthesis of existing techniques. The idea to connect a search procedure for admissible sets to the generation of candidate instrumental variables, and to quantify violations of faithfulness within a flexible bounding framework, represents a creative and new contribution to the field. This novel combination distinguishes the work from prior art. As noted under the Quality section, however, the originality of this combination would be more impactful with a clearer justification of its theoretical underpinnings.

**Significance**
The paper addresses a problem of high importance across many scientific disciplines that rely on observational data. If its utility can be more broadly established, the proposed method could become a valuable tool for practitioners, offering a way to derive tighter and more realistic bounds on causal effects than existing alternatives. The current significance of the contribution, however, is somewhat limited by the scope of the empirical validation. The experiments, while promising, are conducted on a relatively narrow set of synthetic problems and a single real-world dataset. A more extensive evaluation across a wider range of data-generating processes and real-world applications is needed to convincingly establish the general utility and robustness of the method.
