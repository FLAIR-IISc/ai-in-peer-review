This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a method for computing bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) while accounting for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption. The approach introduces relaxation parameters to quantify the degree of unfaithfulness. These parameters serve as constraints within a linear programming framework, which is then used to solve for the upper and lower bounds of the ACE after adjusting for a valid set of covariates.

The paper addresses a timely and significant problem in causal inference. The proposed approach is commendable for its principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of unfaithful conditional independencies. The use of a linear programming framework to solve the resulting optimization problem is a sound and well-motivated choice.

However, the practical utility of the method is less clear. A key challenge lies in the interpretation of the output: a distribution of bounds corresponding to different faithfulness relaxation parameters. The paper does not provide a mechanism for assessing the likelihood of these relaxations, making it difficult to determine the practical relevance of the resulting bounds. In many cases, these relaxations result in bounds that are too wide to be informative. Furthermore, while the method correctly avoids providing a point estimate within the computed interval, this presents a potential pitfall, as practitioners may be tempted to misinterpret the bounds as a confidence interval around a central estimate.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness of the paper. The 'bias' metric used for comparison with competing methods is ill-suited for this task, as WPP produces bounds while other methods produce point estimates; they are fundamentally different quantities. Consequently, the experiments fail to demonstrate whether WPP provides a valuable tool for practitioners or an overly complex one. The performance on the influenza dataset is also concerning: the resulting ACE interval of (-0.23, 0.64) is so wide that it precludes any meaningful causal conclusion, although this may indeed be the most honest assessment possible given the data and assumptions.

**Quality**
The technical approach appears sound, with many details provided in the supplementary material (which I did not check in detail). The core ideas are introduced effectively, though the broader context is sometimes lost. Crucial aspects, such as the methodology for choosing the relaxation parameters, are deferred to future work, which makes it difficult to assess potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation lacks robustness and balance.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written, with the exception of the conclusion. However, the algorithmic steps and the introduction of numerous, subtly different parameters can be challenging to follow, partly due to a lack of detailed explanation for certain components. While I believe I grasp the overall procedure, I am not confident I could implement it solely from the main paper's description; the supplement may be necessary.

**Originality**
To my knowledge, the method is a novel combination of existing techniques. It is closely related to, but distinct from, other recent work on this problem. As such, it has the potential to stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of different approaches in this area.

**Significance**
The paper's primary significance lies in the ideas and direction it offers for tackling this challenging problem, demonstrating how to integrate different techniques. In its current form, it seems unlikely to be a ready-to-use tool for practitioners, but some of the concepts employed in the WPP algorithm could prove to be important in future developments.

**Summary**
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem. It presents an interesting method that is likely to find an audience and could potentially be extended to more general cases. The technical solutions are interesting, and the paper is reasonably well-written. However, the experimental evaluation is poor, and the relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
