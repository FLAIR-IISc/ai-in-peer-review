

This paper addresses the estimation of average causal effects (ACE) between two binary variables when the full causal structure among covariates is unknown. It leverages Entner et al.'s (2013) rule for selecting covariates sufficient to block back-door paths, but shifts from point estimation to deriving bounds on the ACE, following Ramsahai (2012). While the core idea of using such rules to provide robust bounds is conceptually appealing and potentially useful, a critical gap in justification undermines the approach's foundation.

The central issue lies in the role of Entner et al.'s conditions. These conditions are only valid under the faithfulness assumption, which the paper explicitly avoids. Without faithfulness, the conditions do not imply any meaningful constraints on the causal structure. The paper states it "puts aside" the relationship between these conditions and relaxation parameters, yet the method's machinery appears to rely on them implicitly. This creates a tension: if the conditions are irrelevant without faithfulness, why are they central to the proposed bound derivation? The apparent robustness observed in experiments may stem precisely from the method not depending on these conditions—suggesting the approach might be robust *despite* the rule, not *because* of it. The authors must clarify whether the conditions are necessary for the bound derivation or merely a red herring, and how the method avoids dependence on faithfulness.

Regarding technical phrasing:  
1. Section 3’s claim that the framework "expresses violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is misleading. Unfaithfulness involves *additional* independences, not violations of independence. The framework instead bounds *departures* from faithfulness by constraining dependence magnitudes—a nuance critical for clarity.  
2. For experimental comparison, it would strengthen the analysis to include Entner et al.’s method with their stringent thresholds (treating "don’t know" as uninformative bounds), enabling a fairer assessment of robustness against their approach.  
3. The interpretation of the flu vaccine example (p. 8) requires precision: If "Y=1 indicates no hospitalization," a *negative* ACE (where X=1 = flu shot) implies the vaccine *reduces* hospitalization risk—making the negative ACE desirable. The current phrasing ("negative ACE suggests desirable vaccine") is confusing without explicitly linking Y=1 to the positive outcome (avoiding hospitalization).  
4. Table 1 is cited as "Table 5" in the text; this typo must be corrected.

The paper’s shift from point estimation to bounds is a valuable contribution, but the justification for using Entner’s rule without faithfulness is insufficient. The method’s robustness hinges on this unresolved point. Without clarifying how the conditions interact with the bound derivation in the absence of faithfulness, the proposed approach lacks a solid theoretical foundation. The experiments demonstrate promise, but their interpretation depends on resolving this core issue.

While the technical details are intricate, the paper’s clarity suffers from the unresolved tension between Entner’s conditions and the faithfulness assumption. The authors should explicitly: (a) state whether the conditions are necessary for the bound derivation, (b) explain how the method avoids faithfulness dependence, and (c) address the robustness question directly. Without this, the approach risks being perceived as ad hoc rather than principled.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting direction for causal inference under partial structure knowledge. However, the theoretical justification requires significant strengthening to support the method’s claimed robustness. The minor points above are easily addressable, but the core conceptual gap must be resolved for the contribution to be credible. With revisions clarifying the role of Entner’s conditions and the faithfulness assumption, this work could make a meaningful impact. As it stands, the argument is compelling but incomplete.
