The paper presents the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) while allowing specified violations of the standard faithfulness assumption. It introduces relaxation parameters that constrain departures from faithfulness, and then formulates a linear program to compute upper and lower ACE bounds for a given exposure–outcome link by adjusting for a suitable set of covariates under those constraints.

The topic is timely and of broad interest. I like the overall strategy: a principled attempt to quantify the impact of weak unfaithfulness coupled with standard optimization tools to perform the resulting max/min calculations. Whether this is the right way to capture that impact remains an open question. The outputs are conceptually hard to interpret: the method yields a distribution over bounds as a function of “unfaithful relaxations” relative to a model, but the likelihood of those unfaithful instances—and thus the relevance or realism of the bounds—is not addressed. Many relaxations produce very wide intervals that are of limited practical use. The method also does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, even though many readers may implicitly interpret the output that way.

The experimental evaluation is weak. The “bias” metric is not meaningful for comparing WPP to competitors, because they quantify fundamentally different quantities. Consequently, it is unclear whether WPP’s output provides added value to practitioners or is primarily a complicated distraction. The empirical results on the influenza dataset are also concerning: ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) leaves almost no actionable conclusion—though it may, admittedly, be the most honest assessment.

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound, with many details in the supplement (which I have not verified in detail). The ideas are well introduced, but the broader context is occasionally lost. Critical elements—such as how to choose the relaxation parameters—are deferred to future work, making it hard to assess potential pitfalls of this component. The experimental evaluation is neither balanced nor robust enough.

Clarity
- Generally well written (except for the conclusion; see below). The algorithmic steps and numerous subtly different parameters are hard to follow, in part because some details are under-explained. I believe I follow the overall method, but I am not confident I could implement it from the main text alone; the supplement may suffice.

Originality
- As far as I can tell, the method is a new combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to—but sufficiently distinct from—recent work on the same problem, and could spur useful cross-fertilization across approaches.

Significance
- The paper’s significance lies primarily in its ideas and the direction it sets for tackling this difficult problem, showing how to integrate other techniques into causal bounding under unfaithfulness. In its present form, it is unlikely to be a finished solution for practitioners, although several WPP components may ultimately prove important.

Summary
- Addresses a relevant, topical problem. Proposes an interesting method that will likely find an audience. Current practical impact is limited but could extend to broader cases. Writing is reasonable but could be improved. Technically interesting solutions. Experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
