This paper proposes a new method for deriving bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two variables, X and Y. The approach is based on three main principles: the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1, a relaxation of the faithfulness assumption to allow for a degree of path-cancellation, and the introduction of new assumptions formulated as inequality constraints. The authors formulate the computation of the ACE bounds as a linear programming problem. Although the algorithm is not yet fully optimized, the preliminary results suggest that the method can produce significantly narrower and more accurate bounds compared to current state-of-the-art approaches.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of accurately bounding causal effects from observational data. The core contribution—combining several principles to achieve tighter bounds—is compelling and shows considerable promise. The formulation of the problem as a linear program provides a clear and computationally tractable framework. If the underlying theoretical assumptions are sound, this method could represent a significant advance in the field.

However, the paper's main weakness lies in its justification of the core theoretical components. Several key criteria and assumptions that underpin the bounds are not sufficiently explained or validated.

1.  **Validity of Inverse Entner's Rule 1:** The most critical concern is the use of Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction. This is a non-standard application, and its validity is not self-evident. It appears to depend on a specific causal structure, such as the one depicted in Figure 2. The authors must explicitly state this structural constraint as a necessary assumption and provide a rigorous discussion or proof of the conditions under which this inverse application is valid. Without this justification, the foundation of the proposed method remains unsubstantiated.

2.  **Guidance on Relaxation Parameters:** The method's performance is highly sensitive to the parameters that control the relaxation of the faithfulness assumption. Yet, the paper offers no principled guidance, generic criteria, or sensitivity analysis for selecting these parameters. Since these choices significantly affect the final bounds, a discussion on how to set these values—perhaps based on domain knowledge or a data-driven procedure—is essential for the method to be practically applicable and for its results to be reproducible.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The method's performance has been evaluated on a limited set of experiments. While the results are encouraging, the generality of the approach has not been established. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more extensive empirical evaluation across a wider variety of causal graphs and data-generating processes to demonstrate its robustness.

**Clarity and Originality**

The explanation of the theoretical framework is generally understandable. However, the lack of discussion on the critical points mentioned above detracts from the overall clarity. The presentation of the experimental results is particularly weak; the implementation details of the algorithm are sparse, and the definitions of the parameters shown in Table 1 are not provided, making the experiments difficult to interpret and reproduce.

The problem setting itself is not new, as it has been addressed by prior work cited by the authors. The originality of this paper lies in the novel combination of the three core ideas (inverse rule, relaxed faithfulness, and inequality constraints) into a unified framework. However, this originality is diminished by the insufficient justification for the validity of this combination.

**Minor Issues**

There appear to be several typos and incorrect references in the manuscript:
*   On page 6, the text states, "The full algorithm is shown in Table 1," but no algorithm is presented in a table.
*   On page 8, the text refers to "Table 5" summarizing results, but this table does not exist. It is likely a typo for Table 1.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and potentially significant method for deriving tighter ACE bounds. The core idea is innovative. However, the paper is not yet ready for publication due to major weaknesses in its theoretical justification and empirical validation. The validity of the central theoretical claims must be more rigorously established, and the practical aspects of the method, particularly parameter selection, need to be thoroughly investigated. With substantial revision to address these critical issues, the paper could make a valuable contribution.
