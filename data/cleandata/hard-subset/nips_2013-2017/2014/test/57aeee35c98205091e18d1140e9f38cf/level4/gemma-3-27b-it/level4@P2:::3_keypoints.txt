- Introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to bound the average causal effect (ACE) under potential faithfulness violations using relaxation parameters and linear programming.  
- Timely and principled approach that combines existing mathematical tools to quantify weak faithfulness violations.  
- Main interpretability concern: WPP outputs a distribution over bounds, which can be very wide and lacks a preferred point estimate, reducing practical usefulness.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak and unconvincing; using “bias” as a metric is misleading because competitors measure different quantities.  
- Influenza dataset example yields a very wide ACE interval (−0.23, 0.64), offering limited practical insight.  
- Technical quality: method is mathematically sound and well-detailed in the supplement, but selecting relaxation parameters is deferred and limits evaluation.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but algorithmic steps and parameter modifications are hard to follow and hard to replicate without the supplement.  
- Originality: appears to be a novel combination of techniques and distinct from recent related work.  
- Significance: introduces important ideas and direction for future robust causal inference, but not yet a complete practitioner-ready solution.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel handling of faithfulness violations, technical soundness (LP-based), potential to stimulate further research, addresses an important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak experiments, interpretability and usability issues, lack of guidance on choosing relaxation parameters, limited practical demonstration.  
- Specific critique: “unstable” is a poor term for the assumptions; recommend more formal descriptions instead of “cartoon” representations.  
- Specific critique: missing clarity on causal sufficiency assumptions and the role of witness W (W identifies Z but is not used directly for ACE).  
- Specific critique: multiple typos and vague claims (e.g., “gives greater control over violations of faithfulness”) need tightening.  
- Specific critique: abrupt abstraction shift in parameter discussion; need more intuition and explicit interpretation of constraint parameters.  
- Specific critique: clarify relationship between searching admissible sets and bounds computation, and confirm what “10 free parameters” refers to.  
- Specific critique: suggest citing related work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014) on bounding confounding; reviewer couldn’t fully verify supplement.  
- Specific critique: quantify effectiveness and frequency of the back-substitution process and how often it helps.  
- Specific critique: multiple bounds come from different (W,Z) pairs; comparisons to other methods are problematic because targets differ.  
- Specific critique: assess effect of smaller datasets on bound width, Table 1 doesn’t convincingly show WPP’s advantage, and bounds may be so wide they’re insensitive to problem specifics.  
- Tone critique: avoid emotive or dismissive language (e.g., “risky,” “more honest”) and unwarranted dismissal of theory-driven analyses.
