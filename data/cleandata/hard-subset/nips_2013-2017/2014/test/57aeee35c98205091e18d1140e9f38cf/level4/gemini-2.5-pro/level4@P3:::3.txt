### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel methodology for computing bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies. The core idea is to address the limitations of the faithfulness assumption, which is often invoked in causal discovery but can be fragile. Instead of assuming faithfulness holds perfectly, the authors propose a framework that allows for controlled violations. The method leverages the witness-based covariate selection rule of Entner et al. [6] not to find a single admissible set for point estimation, but to identify candidate instrumental variables. It then introduces a set of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that quantify the degree to which faithfulness might be violated (e.g., weak confounding paths or direct effects that were missed). These parameters define a set of constraints within a linear programming (LP) framework, adapted from Dawid [5] and Ramsahai [16], to derive upper and lower bounds on the ACE. The final output is a posterior distribution over these bounds, obtained via Bayesian inference. The paper also presents an algebraic approximation to speed up computation.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach that thoughtfully combines several established concepts: covariate selection via conditional independence tests, instrumental variable (IV) analysis, and linear programming for bounding causal effects. The core contribution—a principled method to relax the faithfulness assumption and quantify the resulting uncertainty in the ACE—is well-motivated.

However, the quality of the work is undermined by two significant issues:
1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is not compelling. The primary metric used to compare WPP with point estimators (NE1, NE2, faithfulness) is the "error," defined as the distance from the true ACE to the estimated interval (which is zero if the true value is inside). This metric is fundamentally unsuited for comparing an interval-based method with a point-based one. By this logic, the trivial interval [-1, 1] would always achieve a perfect score of zero error, despite being uninformative. This makes it impossible to assess from the results whether the WPP provides a genuinely useful trade-off between correctness and informativeness, or if it simply produces wide, unhelpful bounds.
2.  **Completeness:** A crucial aspect of the methodology—how to set the relaxation parameters {w, y, x, β, β̄}—is relegated to supplementary material and future work. Without clear guidance on choosing these parameters based on domain knowledge or data, it is difficult for a practitioner to apply the method or for a reader to assess its practical utility and potential for misuse. This makes the current work feel more like a position paper than a complete, self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the motivation is clear. The background section provides a good summary of the relevant concepts. However, the methodology section becomes dense and difficult to follow. The introduction of numerous parameters for the LP formulation and the subsequent algebraic relaxations could benefit from more intuition and step-by-step explanation. While the supplementary material may contain these details, the main paper should be clear enough for an expert to grasp the core mechanics without needing to consult it extensively. It is not clear I could implement the method solely from the description in the main paper.

**Originality:** The approach is novel and represents an interesting synthesis of existing techniques. While others have worked on bounding causal effects in the presence of unobserved confounders (e.g., using IVs), the specific idea of starting from a faithfulness-based witness search and systematically relaxing the implied independence constraints is, to my knowledge, new. It builds directly upon and extends the work of Entner et al. in a creative way, connecting it to the bounding literature. This connection could stimulate fruitful cross-fertilization between the causal discovery and causal inference communities.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and challenging problem: how to perform reliable causal inference when our assumptions (like faithfulness) are likely to be only approximately true. The ideas and direction presented are significant and could inspire future research into more robust causal estimation methods.

However, the immediate practical significance of the proposed method is questionable. The experiments, particularly the real-world influenza data example, show the method producing very wide bounds (e.g., [-0.10, 0.17] for the vaccine effect), which may be too wide to support decision-making. While the authors rightly argue this is the price of making fewer assumptions, it also highlights the central challenge: the interpretability of the output. The result is a distribution over bounds, with no guidance on how a practitioner should interpret a point within that bound. This ambiguity may limit its adoption in its current form.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Topical Problem:** Addresses the important and practical problem of causal inference under violations of the faithfulness assumption.
*   **Novel Approach:** Proposes an interesting and principled synthesis of techniques from causal discovery, instrumental variables, and linear programming.
*   **Conceptual Contribution:** The idea of explicitly parameterizing and bounding the effect of faithfulness violations is a valuable conceptual step forward.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The paper could stimulate new research directions on robust causal inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The chosen evaluation metric is flawed, making the comparison to other methods difficult to interpret and inconclusive.
*   **Unclear Practical Utility:** The method produces wide bounds and a complex output (a distribution over bounds) whose practical interpretation and usefulness for decision-making are unclear.
*   **Incomplete Methodology:** The crucial step of selecting the relaxation parameters is not adequately addressed in the main paper, making the method difficult to apply and evaluate.

### **Detailed Comments for the Authors**

1.  **p1, Abstract:** The name "Witness Protection Program" is clever and memorable.
2.  **p1, line 47:** The word "unstable" may not be the best choice to describe the assumptions in Entner et al. [6]. The point estimates are stable functions of the distribution, but the underlying faithfulness assumption itself may not hold true. Consider "unverifiable" or "potentially violated."
3.  **p3, line 127:** It would be helpful to explicitly state that the witness W is used to *identify* the admissible set Z, but W itself is not part of the set used for back-door adjustment in the original formulation of Entner et al.
4.  **p4, lines 172-180:** This section introduces the core parameterization very abruptly. It would greatly improve clarity to provide more intuition on what each parameter (η, δ) and each constraint (3-6) represents in terms of the underlying causal graph and potential faithfulness violations.
5.  **p5, line 239:** The sentence "Background knowledge... can be used here" is vague. How can it be used? To set the relaxation parameters? Please be more specific.
6.  **p6, line 304:** The claim "We know of no previous analytical bounds as an algebraic function of w" may be too strong. This work seems related to other efforts in bounding confounding, and you may want to discuss the connection to papers like "Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding" by Geiger, Janzing, & Schölkopf (UAI 2014).
7.  **p7, line 357:** You correctly state the "comparison is not straightforward," but the subsequent explanation reveals that the chosen "error" metric is not just difficult, but inappropriate for this comparison. A better evaluation might involve assessing the trade-off between the width of the WPP interval and its coverage frequency (i.e., how often it contains the true ACE).
8.  **p7, line 369:** The experiments use 5000 data points. Many real-world studies (e.g., in medicine) have far smaller sample sizes. It would be valuable to discuss or show how the width of the bounds is affected by smaller sample sizes (e.g., N=500), as one might worry they would become completely uninformative.
9.  **p8, Table 3:** It is very difficult to draw a clear conclusion from this table. For instance, in the "hard" cases, WPP has a low average error (because it contains the true ACE) but the bounds are presumably wide. Faithfulness has a high error. This doesn't tell us if WPP is "better," only that it's more conservative.
10. **p8, lines 421+ (Conclusion):** The conclusion uses some emotive language ("risky Bayesian approaches," "WPP keeps inference more honest," "purely theory-driven analyses that overlook competing models"). This language detracts from the scientific objectivity of the paper. It would be stronger to state the advantages of your method (e.g., it avoids strong, untestable assumptions required by some point-estimation methods) in more neutral terms. WPP is not a "compromise" but rather an alternative approach that prioritizes robustness over precision.
