This paper proposes a novel approach to estimate the average causal effect (ACE) from a variable X to another Y, leveraging observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors relax the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, building upon the ideas of Entner et al. and Ramsahai. The authors provide a new methodology to bound the ACE, which is a significant contribution to the field of causal inference. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear summary of the background and methodology.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. However, some criteria to define the bounds are not comprehensively explained, particularly the validity of using Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction. The authors should explicitly assume the structure depicted in Fig.2 and clearly explain and discuss its implications. Additionally, the generic criteria to choose appropriate parameters for relaxation are not provided, which affects the result of the bounds significantly.

Clarity:
The explanation of the paper's theoretical part is comprehensive, except for the essential discussion on the validity of using Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction. The demonstration of the experimental results is not well described, and the actual implementation of the algorithm is not comprehensively explained. The definition of the parameters shown in Table 1 is not clearly given. There are also some typos in the paper.

Originality:
The problem setting of this paper has been addressed by earlier work, as the authors pointed out. However, the idea to combine the three ideas in a unified shape contains some originality. The authors provide a new perspective on estimating the ACE, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Significance:
The paper addresses an important problem in evaluating the bound of the strength of causal relations. The proposed method shows some significance in deriving tighter bounds of ACE. However, its performance has been checked by quite limited experiments, and its generality in both theory and wide experiments has not been strictly assessed. The paper's significance lies in its potential to provide a more accurate estimate of the ACE, which is crucial in many real-world applications.
