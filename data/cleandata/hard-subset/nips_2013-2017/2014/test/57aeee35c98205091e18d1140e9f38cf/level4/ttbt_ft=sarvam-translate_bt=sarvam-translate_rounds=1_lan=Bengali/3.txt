The paper describes the so-called Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which calculates limits based on an assumed average causal effect (ACE). This algorithm considers the possibility of violating the standard fidelity assumption in causal inference.

It works by introducing some relaxation parameters, which limit unreliable interactions. Then, adjusting for an appropriate conjugate set, this limits, subject to these constraints, uses a linear programming framework to solve the resulting upper and lower bounds of the ACE for a specific link.

This problem is currently gaining considerable attention, so this article is both timely and interesting. I liked the methods and concepts presented in the article: it is a principled attempt to clearly measure the impact of (weak) unreliable interaction patterns, combined with well-known mathematical tools for solving related optimization/minimization problems.

Whether they have found the right method to capture this effect remains to be seen. The resulting output is difficult to interpret from a conceptual point of view: a distribution over the limits as a function of 'unreliable relaxations' relative to a specific model... But how likely these unreliable examples are, and at the same time, how relevant/realistic these limits are, is still unclear. Many relaxations create limits that are so broad that they may become less useful. The method is not committed to estimating a preferred point within the interval... although people will probably interpret it that way.

Experimental evaluation weak: The 'bias' evaluation metric is meaningless when comparing the WPP method with its competitors, as they literally measure two completely different things. As a result, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or simply a complex confusion. Also, practical performance on the influenza dataset is disturbingly weak: the ACE at (-0.23,0.64) does not indicate any relevant conclusion... although this may be the fairest evaluation of them all.

Quality
The method presented in the paper is technically correct, many details are explained in the supplementary file (I did not examine any details there). The concepts are well presented, although the overall context is sometimes lost. Important aspects such as choosing relaxation parameters have been moved to future articles, which makes it difficult to get an idea of the potential problems with this part of the method. Experimental evaluation is not sufficiently robust or strong.

Clarity
Well written enough (except for the conclusion, see the comments below). It was quite difficult to follow the algorithmic steps and subsequent changes in terms of many subtly different parameters, partly due to the lack of some details in the method. (I think I understand what is happening, but I am not sure that I can fully implement it based only on this description; the supplement may be sufficient.)

Originality
As far as I can tell, the method is a new combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related, but quite different from recent work on the same problem. For this reason, it may stimulate the fruitful exchange of several methods on the subject.

Importance
The significance of the paper mainly lies in the concepts and directions, which show how other techniques can be incorporated to solve this very challenging problem. In its current implementation, it is unlikely to present a finished article for practitioners, although some of the concepts used in the WPP algorithm may ultimately play an important role.

Summary:
Relevant and very topical problem. Interesting method that will undoubtedly find an audience. Current application is limited, but likely to expand to other, more general cases. Reasonably well written, although could be improved. Technically interesting solution. Experimental evaluation weak. Relevance and correct interpretation of the output distribution over the limits is unclear.
