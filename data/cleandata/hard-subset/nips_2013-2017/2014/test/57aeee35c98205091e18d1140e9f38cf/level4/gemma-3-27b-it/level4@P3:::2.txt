This paper proposes a novel approach to bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y. The core idea is to invert Entner’s Rule 1, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for partial path cancellations, and formulating a linear programming approach to derive these bounds under inequality constraints. While computationally intensive, the authors demonstrate promising results, achieving tighter bounds compared to existing methods.

**Quality:** The paper tackles an important problem in causal inference – estimating causal effects in the presence of unmeasured confounding. The proposed methodology appears technically sound and offers a potentially significant contribution by providing tighter bounds on the ACE. The combination of inverting Entner’s Rule 1 with a relaxation of faithfulness, and leveraging linear programming is a thoughtful approach. However, the justification for using Entner’s Rule 1 in reverse requires further clarification. The authors allude to a specific structural assumption (akin to Fig. 2), but this connection needs to be explicitly stated and thoroughly discussed. Furthermore, the selection of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is crucial, yet the paper lacks a clear principle for choosing appropriate values. While the authors acknowledge this and mention future work, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the bounds to these parameters is needed. The reliance on these parameters without guidance leaves the practical applicability somewhat unclear.

**Clarity:** The theoretical background and core methodology are generally well-explained, with sufficient context provided for readers familiar with causal inference concepts. However, the description of the experimental results is less clear. The implementation details, particularly regarding the computational aspects of the linear programming and the Bayesian learning steps, could benefit from more detail. Table 1 is referenced as containing the algorithm, but this is inaccurate. The notation and definitions of parameters, particularly in Table 5 (likely a typo and should be Table 1), are not fully transparent. Several minor typos were observed ("Table 1" referring to an absent table, "Table 5" when Table 1 is intended) and should be addressed.

**Originality:** The problem of bounding causal effects is well-studied, as the authors acknowledge through their citations. The originality of this work lies in the specific combination of techniques: the inverse application of Entner’s Rule 1, the relaxation of faithfulness, and the linear programming formulation. While each component has precedents, their integration represents a novel approach. However, the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this work differs from, and improves upon, existing methods beyond simply stating that it achieves tighter bounds. A more detailed comparison to alternative bounding techniques would strengthen the originality argument.

**Significance:** The potential to derive tighter bounds on the ACE is significant, as it could lead to more informed decision-making in observational studies. However, the paper's significance is currently limited by the scope of the experiments. The validation is performed on relatively small, synthetic datasets and a single real-world example. The generality of the method, both theoretically and empirically, requires further investigation. While the influenza example is a good starting point, applying the method to a broader range of datasets and causal structures would bolster the claims of its practical utility. The paper addresses a crucial problem – accurately estimating causal effects – and the proposed method shows promise, but its true impact hinges on demonstrating its robustness and scalability.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of existing techniques to address a fundamental problem in causal inference.
*   Potential to provide tighter bounds on the ACE compared to current methods.
*   Clear presentation of the theoretical framework.
*   Provides a new approach to handling violations of the faithfulness assumption.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Justification for inverting Entner’s Rule 1 is not fully explained.
*   Lack of guidance on choosing appropriate relaxation parameters.
*   Limited scope of experimental validation.
*   Implementation details are not sufficiently clear.
*   Minor typos and inconsistencies in referencing tables.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a promising new approach to bounding causal effects. While the method has technical strengths and potential significance, several areas require further clarification and investigation before it can be considered a major contribution. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the justification for inverting Entner’s Rule 1, providing guidance on parameter selection, elaborating on the implementation details, and conducting more extensive experiments. The clarity of the presentation can be improved by correcting the typos and ensuring accurate referencing of tables and figures.
