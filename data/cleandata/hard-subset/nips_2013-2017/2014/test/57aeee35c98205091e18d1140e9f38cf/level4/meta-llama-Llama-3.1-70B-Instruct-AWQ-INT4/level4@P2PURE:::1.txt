This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully specified. It considers a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, this rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. The paper proposes using this rule to provide bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, developing a method that follows Ramsahai (2012).

Although I have not fully digested all the technical details, the main idea appears to be sensible, interesting, and useful. However, I am puzzled by the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that the conditions, if verified, are relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this matter aside. It seems that the machinery does not crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the apparent robustness of the method in the experiments is related to this. I would appreciate clarification on this issue.

Some minor comments:
1. The first paragraph of Section 3 states that the framework "… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …" This phrasing is unusual. Isn't unfaithfulness a matter of allowing extra independence rather than violating independence? It appears that the proposed framework is actually intended to allow/express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.
2. In the experiments, it would be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when their stringent thresholds are used, treating the answer of "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds.
3. In the second paragraph on p. 8, if "X=1" means the patient received a flu shot and "Y=1" indicates the patient was not hospitalized, it is unclear why a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine.
4. The table on top of p. 8 is cited as "Table 5" in the text, but it is labeled as "Table 1."

This is an interesting paper, although the details are not easy to follow.
