- Proposes Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound ACE of binary X→Y using W as an instrument-like variable conditional on Z, relaxing faithfulness via bounded approximate independences.
- Reverses Entner et al.’s Rule 1 by treating witnesses as conditional IVs and derives LP-based bounds generalizing Balke–Pearl IV bounds, with a faster algebraic back-substitution approximation.
- Introduces relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) to encode approximate independences and interpolate between IV bounds and back-door identification.
- Provides a Bayesian approach with constrained Dirichlet priors to produce a posterior over bounds and account for sampling uncertainty.
- Empirical results (synthetic and influenza data) indicate WPP yields tighter bounds than standard IV while being more conservative than faithfulness-based point estimators.
- Builds on prior work in IV bounds, causal discovery/faithfulness, admissible set discovery, and partial identification, contributing a unifying framework.
- Conceptual novelty: elegant inversion of Rule 1 to pragmatically accept approximate independences.
- Relaxation parameters are interpretable and map to known special cases (standard IV, chain, back-door).
- LP formulation is sound and technically well-motivated; algebraic bounds offer computational gains.
- Bayesian uncertainty quantification respects partial identification and avoids fragile latent-variable priors.
- Empirical indications support practical usefulness, producing narrower but conservative bounds.
- Lacks formal justification for treating Rule 1 (W,Z) pairs as approximately valid conditional IVs and needs a proposition on sufficient conditions and false positives.
- Results are highly sensitive to relaxation parameters; requires principled guidance, calibration methods, and a sensitivity-analysis protocol.
- Needs formal guarantees: reduction to IV/back-door limits, coverage under relaxations, tightness characterization between LP and algebraic bounds, and conditions for back-substitution feasibility errors.
- The 95% rejection heuristic for constrained posterior sampling is ad hoc; requires analysis of type I/II errors and comparison to established falsification tests.
- Notation and definitions are unclear (parameters in Table 1, suppressed Z); must distinguish observed vs latent quantities and clarify LP mapping steps.
- Paper has incorrect references to tables/algorithms and missing numbered items; fix numbering and ensure all referenced figures/tables/algorithms exist.
- Missing implementation details: pseudocode for Bayesian pipeline and back-substitution, computational complexity, and typical runtimes.
- Novelty is solid but should strengthen comparisons to related bounding/confounding approaches and admissible set criteria.
- Empirical evaluation is limited; needs coverage and width reporting, systematic sensitivity analyses, scalability benchmarks, and additional baselines.
- Flu case study needs clearer justification for witness choices, reporting of posterior bound distributions, and comparison to naive adjustments.
- Pros for acceptance: innovative synthesis, transparent control of faithfulness violations, appropriate Bayesian treatment, and promising empirical tightness vs IV.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient theoretical justification, clarity/notation and implementation gaps, and limited empirical validation.
- Overall: promising contribution conditional on stronger theoretical justification for inverted Rule 1, principled parameter guidance, clarified presentation, and expanded empirical evaluation.
