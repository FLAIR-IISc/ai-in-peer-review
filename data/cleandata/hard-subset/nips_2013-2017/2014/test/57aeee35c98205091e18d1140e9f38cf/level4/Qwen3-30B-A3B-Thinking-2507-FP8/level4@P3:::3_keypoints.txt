- Introduces Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound causal effects under possible faithfulness violations using conditional independence "witnesses" and admissible covariates.  
- Builds on Entner et al. (witness identification) and Ramsahai (linear-programming causal bounds) to compute ACE bounds via relaxed constraints.  
- Generalizes faithfulness with relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that quantify deviations from conditional independence.  
- Implements both numerical linear programming and a faster back-substitution algorithm that yields symbolic bounds.  
- Proposes a Bayesian framework to perform posterior inference over the resulting bounds, capturing estimation uncertainty.  
- Technical content and mathematical derivations are sound and well-documented in main text and supplements.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited and inadequate, comparing mainly to naive estimators and Entner's method.  
- Error metric (Euclidean distance to true ACE) is inappropriate for interval estimates; interval coverage should be used instead.  
- Influenza vaccination case study shows application but does not convincingly demonstrate added value over standard IV bounds.  
- Clarity issues: motivation and interpretation of relaxation parameters need strengthening, and the LP-to-back-substitution transition lacks intuitive explanation.  
- Claim that "WPP is quite stable" is misleading because uninformative wide bounds can reduce to trivial [-1,1], undermining practical usefulness.  
- Terminology: distinction between "witness" and "admissible set" should be clarified earlier.  
- Originality: novel integration of existing ideas (repurposing Entner’s Rule 1, relaxation parameters, symbolic bounds) representing a meaningful but incremental contribution.  
- Significance: tackles an important problem (faithfulness violations) but practical impact is limited due to typically wide bounds.  
- Strengths: technically solid, thoughtful extension of prior work, Bayesian uncertainty treatment, and an efficient back-substitution algorithm.  
- Weaknesses: inadequate experiments, often overly wide bounds, inappropriate comparison metric, weak connection to related work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014), and unclear practical interpretation of output distributions.  
- Recommendation: reject; paper should demonstrate cases with narrower bounds than IV, adopt proper interval evaluation metrics, clarify interpretation of bound distributions, and focus presentation on scenarios where WPP adds clear value.
