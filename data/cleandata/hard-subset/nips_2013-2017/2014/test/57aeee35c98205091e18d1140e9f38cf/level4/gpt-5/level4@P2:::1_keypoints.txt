- Paper proposes bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables by inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1: treat identified (W,Z) pairs as instruments-with-controls and allow bounded departures from conditional independences.  
- Formulates ACE bounds via linear programming (LP) in the style of Ramsahai (2012) and derives bounds rather than point estimates when faithfulness is relaxed.  
- Introduces a Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach to produce a posterior over bounds, propagating sampling uncertainty into interval estimates.  
- Develops fast symbolic (algebraic) bounds and a back-substitution routine as scalable approximations to the full LP solution.  
- Experiments on synthetic data and a flu-vaccine dataset demonstrate practicality and suggest robustness; algebraic bounds can be tighter than standard IV in the application.  
- Main conceptual contribution: a principled middle ground between faithfulness-based point identification and standard IV bounds, with tunable relaxations for near-unfaithfulness.  
- Strength: combining full LP solution and a faster algebraic approximation is an engineering and computational plus.  
- Strength: Bayesian posterior-over-bounds is a thoughtful method to report uncertainty in bounds.  
- Weakness: Role and theoretical guarantees of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 under relaxed faithfulness are insufficiently explained.  
- Weakness: Choice and calibration of relaxation parameters (which are central) are deferred and lack practical guidance.  
- Weakness: Derivations are dense, some proofs relegated to supplement, reducing accessibility.  
- Weakness: Some notational and editorial inconsistencies; presentation can be hard to follow in places.  
- Open question: Precisely what inequalities or parameter restrictions does (approximate) satisfaction of Rule 1 imply when faithfulness is relaxed?  
- Open question: How should strength of evidence for Rule 1 map to relaxation parameters—are there principled calibrations (e.g., Bayes factors or effect-size based)?  
- Open question: How sensitive are bounds when no exact witness exists—when do approximate independences still yield informative bounds?  
- Suggestion: Rephrase “violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” to clarify you mean bounded departures from conditional independence.  
- Suggestion: Report Entner et al.’s method with stringent thresholds (treating “don’t know” as uninformative bounds) to contextualize trade-offs between informativeness and robustness.  
- Minor corrections requested: clarify coding/interpretation of ACE in flu example (sign confusion) and fix table numbering inconsistency (Table 1 vs Table 5).
