This paper addresses the problem of estimating the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully known. The authors build upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) for selecting a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, the justification for Entner et al.'s rule relies on the faithfulness assumption to provide a point estimate. This paper proposes a novel approach: instead of seeking a point estimate, it uses the rule to derive bounds on the ACE, developing a method that follows the work of Ramsahai (2012).

While I have not been able to verify all technical details in depth, the central idea of the paper is intuitive, interesting, and potentially very useful. What puzzled me, however, was the precise role that the conditions from Entner et al.'s rule play in the proposed framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions no longer have obvious implications for the underlying causal structure. My best guess is that satisfying these conditions might inform the choice of the relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this topic aside. It appears that the core machinery of the proposed method does not crucially depend on the conditions from Entner et al.'s rule. I wonder if this apparent decoupling is related to the method's robustness observed in the experiments. I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify this issue.

Some minor comments:

1.  In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "... express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence ..." is potentially misleading. Unfaithfulness typically implies the presence of extra conditional independencies, rather than a violation of independence. It seems the framework is actually designed to express "near-violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of a statistical dependence. A more precise phrasing would be helpful.

2.  In the experiments, it would be beneficial to also report the performance of the original Entner et al. method as a baseline. When their method produces a "don't know" outcome due to its stringent thresholds, this could be treated as reporting uninformative bounds for a more direct comparison.

3.  In the second paragraph on page 8, the interpretation of the ACE seems incorrect. If X=1 means the patient received a flu shot and Y=1 indicates the patient was *not* hospitalized, then a negative ACE would suggest the vaccine *increases* the probability of hospitalization, making it an undesirable vaccine, not a desirable one.

4.  The table on the top of page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text but is labeled "Table 1".

This is an interesting paper, though some of the details are not easy to follow. The clarification requested above would significantly improve my understanding of the paper's contribution.
