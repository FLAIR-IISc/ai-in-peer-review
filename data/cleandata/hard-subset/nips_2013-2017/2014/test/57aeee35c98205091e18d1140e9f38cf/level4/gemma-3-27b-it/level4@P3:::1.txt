This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in the presence of unobserved confounding, relaxing the strong faithfulness assumption commonly required for identification. The authors propose a novel methodology that leverages a rule from Entner et al. (2013) – originally reliant on faithfulness for identifying sufficient adjustment sets – to instead construct bounds on the ACE. This is achieved by interpreting violations of faithfulness as bounded deviations from local independence, and formulating a linear programming problem (building upon the work of Dawid (1990) and Ramsahai (2012)) to determine these bounds. The paper further introduces relaxations to improve computational efficiency and a Bayesian learning approach for parameter estimation.

The core idea is appealing and potentially valuable. The motivation for providing bounds, rather than point estimates, in the face of uncertainty regarding the causal structure is well-articulated. The proposed Witness Protection Program (WPP) framework seems like a pragmatic approach to combine elements of causal discovery and instrumental variable techniques, offering a middle ground between strict assumptions and purely observational analyses. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness through both synthetic and real-world (influenza vaccination) data.

However, a significant weakness lies in the somewhat unclear connection between the Entner et al. (2013) rule and the subsequent methodology. While the paper states the goal is to “invert” the usage of the rule, it doesn’t convincingly explain *how* the conditions of Rule 1 are actively utilized beyond motivating the parameter space for the linear program. Without faithfulness, those conditions don’t inherently imply anything specific about the causal graph, and the authors acknowledge that the framework doesn’t critically depend on them. A clearer explanation of how the conditions influence the choice of relaxation parameters, or a justification for their relative independence from the core approach, would strengthen the paper considerably.  The notion of "protecting" the witness against faithfulness violations is intriguing, but the precise mechanism through which this occurs needs elaboration.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of using a rule initially based on faithfulness to derive bounds under relaxed assumptions is a creative contribution.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed relaxations and the Bayesian learning framework address the computational challenges associated with the linear programming formulation.
*   **Soundness:** The method builds upon established theoretical foundations in causal inference (do-calculus, instrumental variables, linear programming).
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments with both synthetic and real data demonstrate the potential of the method and compare its performance with existing techniques.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the need for methods that are robust to violations of the faithfulness assumption.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity regarding Entner et al.’s Rule:** The role of the conditions in Entner et al.’s Rule 1 remains unclear and under-explained.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While relaxations are introduced, the computational cost, particularly with increasing covariate dimensionality, remains a concern.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the bounds to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is acknowledged, but a more thorough investigation of parameter selection strategies would be beneficial.
*   **Ambiguous Language:** Phrases like “bounded violations of local independence” (Section 3.1) could be more precise.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem in causal inference – estimation of causal effects with unobserved confounding and limited assumptions.
*   The proposed methodology is novel and potentially offers a valuable addition to the toolkit of causal inference methods.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method and its ability to provide more informative bounds than existing techniques.
*   The paper is well-written (despite some minor clarity issues) and clearly presents the underlying ideas and technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The somewhat tenuous connection between Entner et al.'s rule and the core methodology weakens the theoretical justification.
*   The computational complexity and sensitivity to parameter choices raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method.
*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential biases of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has merit and presents a promising approach to causal inference. However, the lack of clarity regarding the role of Entner et al.’s rule and the potential computational challenges prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form.  I recommend a *weak accept* contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the role of Entner et al.’s rule. A clarification that demonstrates a meaningful link, or a justification for its independence, would significantly strengthen the paper. 

**Minor Comments:**

2.  The experiments would benefit from including a comparison against Entner et al.'s method with the "don't know" answer interpreted as uninformative bounds.
3.  Page 8, second paragraph – Correct the logic regarding the interpretation of a negative ACE in the context of the influenza vaccination study. A negative ACE would suggest the vaccine *reduces* hospitalization risk, making it desirable.
