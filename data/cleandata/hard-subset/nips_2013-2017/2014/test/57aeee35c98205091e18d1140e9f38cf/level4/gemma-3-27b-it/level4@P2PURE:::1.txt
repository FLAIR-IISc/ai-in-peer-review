This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in scenarios where the causal structure of the covariates is not completely known. It builds upon a covariate selection rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013), designed to identify a sufficient set of observed covariates for blocking back-door paths. While Entner et al.’s rule relies on the faithfulness assumption, this work proposes leveraging it to derive bounds—rather than point estimates—for the ACE, utilizing a methodology inspired by Ramsahai (2012). 

Although I haven't fully verified all the technical details, the core idea appears sound, interesting, and potentially valuable. However, the role of the conditions specified in Entner et al.’s rule within this framework is unclear to me. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions lack readily apparent implications regarding the causal structure. My understanding is that, if satisfied, the conditions might inform the selection of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly avoids addressing this aspect. Consequently, the presented machinery doesn’t appear critically dependent on Entner et al.’s conditions, and I question whether the observed robustness in the experiments is related to this. Clarification on this point would be greatly appreciated.

Minor comments:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase “… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …” is somewhat awkward. Unfaithfulness typically allows for *extra* independence relationships, rather than violating existing ones. It seems the proposed framework actually aims to accommodate or represent “near violations” of faithfulness by bounding the magnitude of dependence.
2. In the experiments, including a performance evaluation of Entner et al.’s method with their original, stricter thresholds would be beneficial. Treating cases where Entner et al.’s method reports “don’t know” as providing uninformative bounds would offer a useful point of comparison.
3. In the second paragraph on p. 8, given that "X=1 represents receiving a flu shot and Y=1 indicates not being hospitalized," a negative ACE suggests the vaccine is *not* effective, not desirable.
4. “Table 1” on top of p. 8 is referenced as “Table 5” within the text. 

Overall, this is an interesting paper, despite the technical details being challenging to follow.
