

This paper introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies by providing bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) rather than point estimates. The authors build on Entner et al. (2013), which proposed a rule for identifying admissible sets for back-door adjustment under the faithfulness assumption. Their key contribution is reinterpreting this rule to generate instrumental variables for bounding the ACE while relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. The method employs a linear programming framework to generate bounds, augmented by a Bayesian implementation for uncertainty quantification and a more efficient back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference by addressing a meaningful limitation of current practice: the reliance on faithfulness assumptions that may be violated in real-world data. The authors provide a theoretically grounded framework for bounding causal effects that bridges the gap between point estimation under faithfulness and traditional instrumental variable approaches. The experimental validation on both synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination dataset demonstrates the practical utility of the approach, showing that the method provides meaningful bounds even when no valid witness exists.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, with clear theoretical development of how the approach differs from previous work. The Bayesian implementation properly accounts for uncertainty in constraint estimation, which is a significant strength. The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption is well-motivated by the practical limitations of assuming perfect faithfulness. However, the theoretical connection between Entner et al.'s rule conditions and the relaxation parameters used in the bounds calculation remains somewhat opaque, which weakens the paper's theoretical foundation.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with excellent contextualization of the work within the causal inference literature. The explanation of the Witness Protection Program algorithm (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the practical implementation. The connection between the method and previous work is well-established. The paper would benefit from clearer explanation of how the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule relate to the relaxation parameters used in the bounds calculation, as this is central to understanding the method.

Originality: This work makes a genuinely novel contribution by reinterpreting Entner et al.'s rule to generate bounds rather than point estimates, while relaxing the faithfulness assumption. The Bayesian framework for uncertainty quantification and the back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency are also valuable additions. The paper distinguishes itself from previous work by providing a practical method that gives greater control over violations of faithfulness, which is an important gap in current causal inference toolkits.

Significance: The proposed method has significant potential impact for applied researchers working with observational data. By providing a method that gives greater control over faithfulness violations, the authors address a real limitation of current practice. The influenza vaccination case study demonstrates how the approach can provide more informative bounds than traditional instrumental variable methods, making it immediately relevant to applied researchers. The method could become a standard tool in observational study toolkits, complementing existing approaches.

Strengths:
- Addresses a meaningful limitation of current causal inference practice
- Provides theoretically grounded bounds on causal effects
- Offers a practical implementation with Bayesian uncertainty quantification
- Demonstrates utility on both synthetic and real-world data
- Introduces a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm

Weaknesses:
- The relationship between Entner et al.'s rule conditions and the relaxation parameters needs clearer explanation
- The interpretation of the influenza vaccination results could be improved (Y=1 as "hospitalized" should be explicitly connected to vaccine benefit)
- The paper could better clarify how their method differs from traditional instrumental variable approaches

Specific comments:
1. The phrase "bounded violations of local independence" (Section 3) is confusing. Unfaithfulness involves allowing extra conditional independencies, not violating them. The authors should clarify that their approach allows for "almost" violations of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the influenza vaccination example, the authors state "a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine" but define Y=1 as "hospitalized." This is correct (negative ACE indicates vaccination reduces hospitalization), but the connection between the definition and the interpretation could be made clearer.

3. The paper should report Entner et al.'s method performance using their stringent thresholds (as suggested in their original paper), treating "don't know" answers as uninformative bounds for comparison.

4. The paper cites "Table 5" in the text but refers to "Table 1" on page 8. This should be corrected.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to causal inference. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and has clear practical applications. With some clarification on the theoretical connections and minor improvements to the presentation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Pro: Well-motivated problem, technically sound approach, strong experimental validation, useful practical contribution.
Con: Some theoretical connections could be clearer, minor presentation issues.
