This paper describes the so-called Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which calculates the limits on the estimated average causal effect (ACE). This algorithm takes into account potential violations of the standard fidelity assumption in causal inference.

The process begins by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to limit unfaithful interactions, and then, subject to these constraints, uses a linear programming framework to solve the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a suitable set of associates.

This problem is currently gaining considerable attention, making this paper both timely and interesting. I like the approaches and ideas presented in the paper: it is a theoretical attempt to clearly measure the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, as well as using well-known mathematical tools to solve related maximization/minimization problems.

How much of an impact it has remains to be determined. Understanding the resulting output from a conceptual perspective is difficult: a distribution on the limits that is a function of 'unfaithful छूट' relative to a given model... but how likely these unfaithful examples are, and at the same time, how relevant/realistic these limits are themselves, is still unclear. Many छूट lead to broad limits, where they may be less useful. This method is not committed to any preferred point estimate within the interval... even if people are likely to understand it in the same way.

The experimental evaluation is poor: 'bias' evaluation metric is meaningless when comparing the WPP method to its competitors because they literally measure two completely different things. As a result, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition to practitioners or just a complex distraction. Also, the practical performance on influenza data sets is worryingly weak: the ACE at (-0.23,0.64) means there is no possible relevant conclusion... although this may be the fairest assessment of them all.

Quality
The approach presented in the paper is technically sound, with many details clarified in the supplementary file (I didn't check any details there). The ideas are well presented, although the overall context is sometimes lost. Important aspects such as choosing relaxation parameters are assigned in a future article, making it difficult to experience potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or strong enough.

Clarity
Well-written (except for the conclusion, see comments below). It was quite difficult to follow the algorithm steps and subsequent revisions in terms of several subtly different parameters, partly due to the lack of explanation of some details of the method. (I think I understand what happens, but I'm not sure I can fully apply it from this description alone; the supplement may be sufficient).

Originality
As far as I can tell, the method is a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related, but quite different from recent work on the same problem. Thus it may stimulate fruitful cross-fertilization of several approaches on the subject.

Importance
The importance of the paper mainly lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, showing how other techniques can be incorporated into solving this highly challenging problem. In its current implementation, it is unlikely to represent a ready-made article for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm may eventually play an important role.

Summary:
Relevant and highly topical problem. Interesting method that will undoubtedly appeal to the audience. Current application is limited, but likely to be extended to other, more general cases. Fairly well-written, although it could be improved. Technically interesting solution. Experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution on the limits is unclear.
