The paper describes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute bounds on an inferred average causal effect (ACE) that takes possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference into account. 
It does so by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, and then uses a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link by adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates, subject to these constraints.
This problem currently receives a fair amount of attention, so the paper is both timely and interesting. I like the approach and ideas introduced in the paper: it is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, in combination with well-known mathematical tools to solve the corresponding maximization/minimization problems.
Whether or not the method correctly captures this impact remains to be seen. The resulting output – a distribution over bounds reflecting ‘unfaithful relaxations’ relative to a given model – is hard to interpret conceptually, and the relevance and realism of these bounds are unclear. Many relaxations lead to wide bounds, potentially rendering them less useful. The method does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, though users are likely to interpret it as such. 
Experimental evaluation is limited: the ‘bias’ evaluation metric is not meaningful when comparing WPP with its competitors, as they measure different things. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. Furthermore, the practical performance on the influenza data set is weak: an ACE in (-0.23,0.64) provides little conclusive information, though this may be the most honest assessment.
Quality
The approach is technically sound, with many details explicated in the supplementary material (which I have not fully reviewed). Ideas are well introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as choosing the relaxation parameters, are relegated to future work, making it difficult to assess potential issues with this part of the method. Experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness. 
Clarity
The paper is decently written (with exceptions noted in the conclusion). The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications, involving numerous subtly different parameters, were challenging to follow, in part due to a lack of explanation for certain methodological details. (I believe I understand the process, but am unsure if I could fully implement it solely from this description; the supplement may suffice).
Originality
As far as I can tell, the method represents a novel combination of familiar techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently distinct from, recent work on the same problem, potentially stimulating fruitful cross-fertilization of approaches.
Significance
The significance of the paper primarily lies in the ideas and direction it provides, demonstrating how to incorporate other techniques to address this highly challenging problem. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to represent a finished product for practitioners, though some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm could eventually play an important role. 
Summary:
Relevant and highly topical problem. Interesting method that will likely find an audience. Current application is limited, but may extend to more general cases. Reasonably well written, though could be improved. Technically interesting solutions. Experimental evaluation is weak. Relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
