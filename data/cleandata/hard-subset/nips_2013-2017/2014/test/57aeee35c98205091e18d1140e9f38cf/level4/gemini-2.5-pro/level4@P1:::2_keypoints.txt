- Proposes repurposing Entner’s Rule 1 to treat a “witness” variable as a candidate instrumental variable (IV).  
- Relaxes strict faithfulness by introducing user-defined relaxation parameters that bound conditional-independence violations.  
- Integrates these relaxations into a linear programming framework to derive bounds on the average causal effect (ACE).  
- Adds a Bayesian approach to handle sampling uncertainty.  
- Introduces a faster approximate “back-substitution” algorithm for improved scalability.  
- Experiments on synthetic and real data show substantially tighter bounds than standard IV methods.  
- Strength: novel, principled compromise between strict-faithfulness approaches and wide IV bounds.  
- Strength: parameterizing faithfulness violations lets practitioners express graded beliefs about causal structure.  
- Strength: creative synthesis of causal discovery, IV methods, and linear programming.  
- Weakness: theoretical justification for using a witness as an IV is underdeveloped and needs clearer assumptions/causal-structure discussion.  
- Weakness: no principled guidance for selecting relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), leaving users to choose arbitrarily.  
- Weakness: clarity and reproducibility issues—experimental details, algorithm implementation, and selection criteria for reported bounds are insufficiently explained.  
- Weakness: presentational problems and typos (misreferenced tables/algorithms) that reduce professionalism.  
- Originality/significance: novel and potentially impactful synthesis, but broader applicability depends on better parameter-selection strategies and more extensive experiments.
