The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), an algorithm for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in the presence of possible violations of the faithfulness assumption. The method introduces relaxation parameters that quantify allowed deviations from faithfulness (e.g., bounded “path cancellations”), then formulates a linear program to compute ACE bounds conditional on covariate sets identified via a reversal of Entner et al.’s Rule 1. It further provides a Bayesian procedure that yields a posterior over bounds, and develops algebraic (symbolic) bounds with a back‑substitution routine to speed computation. Conceptually, WPP bridges instrumental‑variable bounds (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) with faithfulness‑based discovery (PC/Rule 1) by turning candidate witnesses into IV‑like constructs subject to controlled violations.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant problem: causal effect estimation under unmeasured confounding with uncertain faithfulness.
- Principled formulation that explicitly models degrees of unfaithfulness through interpretable relaxation parameters, linked to linear programming constraints.
- Clear connection to prior work (Entner et al.; Dawid; Ramsahai; Pearl), but with a distinct inversion of Rule 1 and a novel integration of symbolic bounds/back‑substitution.
- The Bayesian treatment acknowledges sampling uncertainty in conditional independence tests and in bound computation.

Weaknesses
- Interpretability and practical relevance of a posterior over bounds remain unclear: the method gives distributions over intervals that depend on user‑specified relaxations, yet offers little guidance on how likely specific relaxations are or how to select them. Many settings yield very wide bounds that may be of limited practical use.
- The method does not commit to point estimates, but readers may still interpret bounds as confidence intervals around a “best guess.” The paper should emphasize how to use such bounds in practice.
- Experimental evaluation is weak. The “bias” metric used to compare WPP (intervals) against point estimators is not meaningful for cross‑method comparison; as shown, trivial wide intervals can dominate this metric. The influenza application yields broad intervals (e.g., −0.23 to 0.64), offering little actionable insight—although arguably this may be the fairest assessment.
- Key choices (e.g., setting relaxation parameters) are deferred to future work, making it difficult to assess robustness and practical deployment.
- Some algorithmic steps and parameter roles are hard to follow from the main text alone; reproducibility may rely heavily on the supplement.

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound, with many details in the supplement (not fully checked). The linear programming and Bayesian components are standard and appropriately applied. However, the experimental design and evaluation metrics do not convincingly demonstrate practical utility or superiority over alternatives.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but the narrative occasionally loses context. Explanations of the relaxation parameters and their causal interpretation should be expanded where they first appear. The transitions between search, constraint construction, and bounding are abrupt and would benefit from added intuition. The conclusion uses overly emotive language.

Originality
- A novel combination of established ideas: turning witnesses into IV‑like devices under bounded unfaithfulness, deriving algebraic bounds, and using back‑substitution. Related to contemporary work on bounding confounding, but sufficiently distinct to contribute to the literature.

Significance
- The main contribution is conceptual and methodological: providing a structured way to incorporate “approximate faithfulness” into ACE bounds. While the current implementation may be premature for practitioners, elements of WPP (relaxation‑based constraints, symbolic bounds) are promising and could inform future, more general methods.

Arguments for acceptance
- Important, topical problem; principled modeling of unfaithfulness.
- Thought‑provoking integration of causal discovery with LP‑based bounds.
- Potential to stimulate follow‑up work on parameter selection and tighter, more interpretable bounds.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and unconvincing empirical evaluation; metrics not comparable across methods.
- Practical guidance on selecting relaxation parameters is missing.
- Resulting bounds are often too wide to be actionable; interpretability remains problematic.
- Some methodological details are hard to follow without the supplement.

Other comments and suggestions
- p1/5: “Witness Protection Program” — memorable name.
- p1/47: “unstable” is not accurate; better: “may not hold in practice.”
- p2/74: “cartoon representation” sounds informal; consider “schematic diagram.”
- p2/93–94: This implicitly assumes causal sufficiency or knowledge that W is not an effect of X.
- p3/127: Clarify that the witness W is used to identify Z; W itself is not required for ACE computation once Z is chosen.
- p3/143: Typo: “an useful” → “a useful.”
- p3/144: “gives greater control over violations of faithfulness” is vague; specify what is controlled (e.g., magnitude of allowed deviations via w, x, y, β, β̄).
- p4/164: “might” → “can.” Also remove “(conditional)” or clarify; W generally depends on U given X.
- p4/172–180: The abstraction level jumps; add intuition for parameters (what w, x, y, β, β̄ bound causally).
- p4/183: Link back to ACE definition in (1) for orientation.
- p4/185–189: Emphasize that these parameters bound departures from conditional independencies implied by faithfulness, not “weak interactions.”
- p5/221: Briefly describe how the search phase (witness/admissible set) feeds into the bounds computation.
- p5/221/idem: Explain how identifying Z depends on the relaxation parameters θ used in testing approximate independencies.
- p5/239: “Background… used here.” — clarify how background knowledge informs parameter choices or admissible sets.
- p5/259: Clarify that “dimensionality 10” refers to the number of free parameters for Z’s distribution, not necessarily the number of variables.
- p6/304: Acknowledge related work (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf, UAI 2014) and clarify differences.
- p6/319–332: The back‑substitution idea is appealing; provide empirical evidence (Section 5) on how often it tightens bounds in practice.
- p7/350: “more than one bound” arises from multiple (W, Z) pairs; please state explicitly.
- p7/357: The bias‑based comparison is not meaningful across interval vs point estimators; consider metrics that penalize width and coverage jointly.
- p7/360: Intervals still covering (0, 0.6) imply “from no effect to substantial positive effect”; highlight implications for decision‑making.
- p7/369: Many real datasets are smaller (e.g., n ≈ 500); discuss impact on bound width and stability.
- p8/384: Table 1 does not clearly demonstrate WPP’s benefit over faithfulness‑based point estimates.
- p8/391: “WPP is quite stable” could reflect bounds being loose; quantify stability vs informativeness.
- p8/406–408: Strongly emphasize that bounds are not confidence intervals around a preferred point and do not imply any distribution within the interval; provide guidance for practitioners.
- p8/421+: Avoid emotive phrasing (“risky Bayesian approaches,” “more honest”); rephrase neutrally. WPP is complementary rather than a “compromise,” and the critique of “purely theory‑driven analyses” reads as dismissive.
- p9: Typo in ref 9: “inuenza.”

Overall assessment
A relevant, technically interesting paper with a novel and principled direction. However, unclear parameter selection, weak empirical evaluation, and interpretability concerns limit its current practical impact. With stronger experiments, clearer guidance on relaxation parameters, and improved exposition, it could make a valuable contribution.
