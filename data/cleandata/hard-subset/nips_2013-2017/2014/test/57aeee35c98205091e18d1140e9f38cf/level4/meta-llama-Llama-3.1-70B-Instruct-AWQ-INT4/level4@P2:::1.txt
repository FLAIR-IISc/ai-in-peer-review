This paper is concerned with the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully given. It considers a rule proposed in Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates that is sufficient to block all back-door paths. For Entner et al.'s purpose, however, the rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. This paper proposes to use the rule to provide bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, and develops a method following Ramsahai (2012).

The main idea of the paper is to invert the usage of Entner's rule, so that pairs (W,Z) provide an instrumental variable bounding method instead of a back-door adjustment. This approach is interesting and useful, as it allows for the estimation of ACE without making strong assumptions about the causal structure.

However, I have some concerns regarding the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, those conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. It seems to me that the machinery does not crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the apparent robustness of the method in the experiments has anything to do with this. I would appreciate a clarification on this issue.

Some minor comments:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is misleading. Isn't unfaithfulness a matter of allowing extra independence rather than violating independence? It seems to me that the proposed framework is actually intended to allow/express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the experiments, it may be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when their stringent thresholds are used, taking the answer of "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds.

3. In the second paragraph on p. 8, there seems to be a typo. If "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized", then a negative ACE would suggest a desirable vaccine. However, the text states that Y=1 indicates the patient was hospitalized.

4. There is a typo in the citation of "Table 1" on top of p. 8, which is cited as "Table 5" in the text.

Overall, this is an interesting paper, though the details are not easy to follow. I would appreciate some clarification on the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule and some minor corrections to the text.
