

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure among covariates is partially unknown. It builds on a rule from Entner et al. (2013) for selecting covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths, but proposes using this rule to derive bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates, following Ramsahai (2012). While the core idea appears promising and potentially useful, I have a key concern regarding the role of Entner et al.'s conditions in the proposed approach. The rule is justified under the faithfulness assumption for Entner et al.'s purpose, but the paper explicitly sets aside faithfulness. Without this assumption, the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule do not directly imply structural constraints. I was unclear about their role here: if verified, do they inform the choice of relaxation parameters (as I suspect), or is their inclusion merely incidental? The method’s apparent robustness in experiments may depend on this, yet the paper does not clarify the connection. A more explicit discussion of how these conditions interact with the relaxation framework would be valuable.

Minor comments:
1. Section 3, first paragraph: "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." This phrasing is misleading. Unfaithfulness arises from *additional* independences, not "violations" of independence. The framework should instead model *near-faithfulness* by bounding the magnitude of dependence, not "violations."
2. In the experiments, it would be helpful to include Entner et al.’s method’s performance under their stringent thresholds, treating "don't know" answers as reporting uninformative bounds (e.g., [−1, 1]).
3. On p. 8, second paragraph: If X=1 denotes receiving a flu shot and Y=1 indicates hospitalization, a *negative* ACE (E[Y|X=1] − E[Y|X=0]) would imply lower hospitalization after vaccination. Thus, a negative ACE suggests the vaccine is beneficial—why does the paper state it "suggests a desirable vaccine"? Clarify the interpretation.
4. Table 1 is cited as "Table 5" on p. 8. Please correct the reference.

The paper presents an interesting approach, though the technical exposition is dense and the role of Entner et al.’s conditions requires further clarification. The method’s potential utility is evident, but the connection between the rule’s conditions and the relaxation mechanism needs strengthening.
