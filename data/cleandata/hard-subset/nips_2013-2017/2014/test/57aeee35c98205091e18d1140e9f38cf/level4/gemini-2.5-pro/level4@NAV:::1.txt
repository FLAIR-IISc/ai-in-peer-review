This work addresses the challenge of estimating the Average Causal Effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal graph over covariates is incompletely known. It builds upon a rule from Entner et al. (2013) designed to identify a sufficient covariate set for blocking all back-door paths. Whereas the Entner et al. rule's justification relies on the faithfulness assumption for point estimation, this paper adapts it to derive bounds on the ACE, employing a methodology based on the framework of Ramsahai (2012).

While I have not been able to verify every technical derivation, the central concept of the paper is intuitive, compelling, and appears to be of significant practical value. A point of confusion for me, however, was the precise role that the conditions of the Entner et al. rule play within this new bounding framework. In the absence of the faithfulness assumption, the causal implications of these conditions are not immediately clear. I surmised that these conditions might inform the selection of the relaxation parameters, but the authors explicitly state that this topic is beyond the current scope. This leads me to question whether the proposed machinery is fundamentally reliant on the Entner et al. conditions, and I wonder if the method's strong empirical performance is partly due to this potential robustness. I would welcome the authors' clarification on this matter.

Some minor points for consideration:
1.  In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is counterintuitive. Unfaithfulness typically implies the presence of conditional independencies not entailed by the graph, rather than the violation of them. The framework seems better described as allowing for "near-violations" of faithfulness by placing bounds on the strength of dependencies.
2.  To provide a more direct baseline, the experiments would be strengthened by including the performance of the original Entner et al. method. The cases where it returns "don't know" could be treated as it providing uninformative bounds.
3.  In the second paragraph on page 8, the interpretation of the ACE appears to be inverted. If X=1 represents receiving a flu shot and Y=1 represents not being hospitalized, a positive ACE would indicate a beneficial vaccine effect, not a negative one.
4.  There is a typo on page 8: the table labeled "Table 1" is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

Overall, this is a stimulating paper on an important problem, even if some of the technical arguments are dense.
