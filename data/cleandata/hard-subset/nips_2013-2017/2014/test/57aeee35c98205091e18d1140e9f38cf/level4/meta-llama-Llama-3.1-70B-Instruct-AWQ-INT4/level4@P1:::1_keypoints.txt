- Proposes a novel method to estimate bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when covariate causal structure is partially unknown.
- Builds on Entner et al. (2013) rule for selecting covariates that block back-door paths, but replaces point estimation with bounded estimates.
- Recognizes Entner’s rule requires faithfulness and introduces relaxations to allow conditional independencies from path cancellations.
- Uses the rule to generate candidate instrumental variables (IVs) despite incomplete causal graphs.
- Applies a linear programming approach (from Ramsahai 2012) to compute bounds on the ACE given candidate IVs.
- Integrates Bayesian inference to produce a posterior distribution over ACE bounds under the relaxation assumptions.
- Experiments on synthetic data show the method yields more stable and robust results than naive point estimators and Entner et al.’s point estimator.
- Real-data application estimating influenza vaccination effect on hospitalization produces a tighter interval than a standard IV model.
- Strength: novel combination of LP and Bayesian methods to obtain principled bounds, improving over traditional point estimates.
- Strength: clear, well-written exposition with well-designed experiments demonstrating robustness.
- Weakness: limited guidance on choosing relaxation parameters and how they influence results.
- Weakness: computational complexity and runtime details are not sufficiently discussed or compared to alternatives.
- Originality: significant improvement by shifting from point estimates to bounded inference under relaxed assumptions.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept, with minor revisions to address parameter choice insight and complexity analysis.
