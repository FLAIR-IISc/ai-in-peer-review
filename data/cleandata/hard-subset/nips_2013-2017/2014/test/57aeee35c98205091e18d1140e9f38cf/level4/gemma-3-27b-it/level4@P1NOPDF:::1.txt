This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure relating the covariates is not fully specified. It considers a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) designed to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, the validity of Entner et al.’s rule relies on the faithfulness assumption. This paper proposes leveraging this rule to derive bounds – rather than point estimates – for the ACE, using a method inspired by Ramsahai (2012).

I admit I haven't fully grasped all the technical details, but the core idea seems sensible, interesting, and potentially useful. My primary concern relates to the role of the conditions outlined in Entner et al.’s rule within this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, those conditions don't appear to have clear implications for the underlying causal structure. My understanding is that, if satisfied, these conditions might inform the selection of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this consideration aside. It seems the proposed machinery doesn't critically depend on these conditions, and I wonder if the observed robustness of the method in the experiments is connected to this. I would appreciate clarification on this point.

Here are some minor comments:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…” feels awkward. Isn't unfaithfulness characterized by *allowing* extra independence, rather than *violating* independence? It appears the framework aims to allow or express “near” violations of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the experiments, it might be informative to also report the performance of Entner et al.’s method when their stricter thresholds are applied, treating the “don’t know” outcome as an uninformative bound.

3. In the second paragraph on page 8, if "X=1 means the patient received a flu shot and Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized," a negative ACE would suggest the vaccine *is* effective, not undesirable. 

4. "Table 1" is referenced as "Table 5" in the text on page 8.

Overall, this is an interesting paper, although the details can be challenging to follow.
