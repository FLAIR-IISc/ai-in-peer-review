The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to estimate bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) while accounting for potential violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. This is achieved by introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and employing a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusted for a set of covariates and subject to these constraints.

The problem addressed in the paper is timely and relevant, receiving significant attention in the field. The approach is interesting and principled, as it attempts to explicitly quantify the impact of weak unfaithful interaction patterns using well-known mathematical tools to solve the corresponding maximization/minimization problems. However, it remains to be seen whether the authors have found the right method to capture this impact.

The output of the WPP algorithm is difficult to interpret from a conceptual standpoint, resulting in a distribution over bounds that are a function of 'unfaithful relaxations' relative to a given model. The likelihood and relevance of these unfaithful instances, and thus the bounds themselves, remain unclear. Many relaxations lead to wide bounds, making them less useful. Furthermore, the method does not provide a preferred point estimate within the interval, although it is likely that people will interpret it as such.

The experimental evaluation is poor, with the 'bias' evaluation metric being meaningless when comparing the WPP method to its competitors, as they measure different things. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza dataset is also concerning, with the ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64) implying that no relevant conclusions can be drawn.

Quality:
The approach introduced in the paper is technically sound, with many details explained in the supplementary file. The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as choosing the relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article, making it challenging to understand potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough.

Clarity:
The paper is decently written, except for the conclusion. The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications, involving subtly different parameters, are hard to follow due to the lack of explanation of certain details. While the supplement may provide sufficient information, it is unclear whether one could fully implement the method from the description alone.

Originality:
The method appears to be a novel combination of familiar techniques, closely related to but sufficiently different from recent work on the same problem. As such, it may stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of approaches on the subject.

Significance:
The significance of the paper lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate other techniques to solve this challenging problem. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to represent the finished article for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm could play an important role.

Summary:
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem, proposing an interesting method that will likely find an audience. However, the current application is limited, and the experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.

Other comments:

* The name 'Witness Protection Program' is catchy and memorable.
* The term 'unstable' is not accurate; the interactions are stable but may not hold true.
* The 'cartoon representation' is unclear and could be improved.
* The assumption of causal sufficiency or knowledge W -> X is not explicitly stated.
* The role of the witness W in identifying the set Z could be clarified.
* The phrase 'gives greater control over violations of faithfulness' is too vague and could be rephrased.
* The transition in level of abstraction in Section 4 is sudden and may be difficult for less familiar readers to follow.
* The link to the original introduction in (1) could be provided for clarity.
* The constraint parameters could be better explained to avoid misinterpretation.
* The relation between the search part and the bounds computation could be briefly described.
* The dependence of the identification of admissible sets Z for W on the relaxation parameters theta could be clarified.
* The sentence 'Background ... used here' is unclear and could be rephrased.
* The phrase 'max. 10 free parameters to describe the entire set Z' could be clarified to indicate that it refers to the number of parameters, not variables.
* The statement 'We know of no previous analytical bounds' is not accurate, as recent papers such as 'Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding' (Geiger, Janzing, Scholkopf, UAI2014) seem closely related.
* The efficacy of the back-substitution process in refining bounds could be discussed in more detail.
* The reason for having 'more than one bound' could be clarified to indicate that it is due to different possible admissible sets for different witnesses.
* The comparison based on the bias metric is meaningless, as shown by the subsequent trivial optimal example.
* The ACE interval (-1, 1) still covers a wide range, implying that the bounds are not informative.
* The impact of the dataset size on the bounds could be discussed, as typical medical trials often have less data available.
* The benefit of using WPP over the standard point estimate using faithfulness is unclear from Table 1.
* The statement 'WPP is quite stable' could be rephrased to indicate that the bounds derived by WPP are loose and do not depend on whether the problem is solvable or not.
* The interpretation of the WPP results is crucial, as many casual readers may read the bounds as a confidence interval around the best guess value.
* The language used in the conclusion is emotive and suggests a biased evaluation and/or erroneous interpretation of results.
* The statement 'WPP is not a compromise but focuses on an additional aspect of the overall inference problem' could be rephrased for clarity.
* The phrase 'and purely theory-driven analyses that refuse to look at competing models' is unclear and could be rephrased or removed.
