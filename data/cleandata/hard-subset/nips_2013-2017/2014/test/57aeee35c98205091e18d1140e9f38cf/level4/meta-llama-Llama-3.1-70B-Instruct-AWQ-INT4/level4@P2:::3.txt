The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute bounds on an inferred average causal effect (ACE) that takes possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference into account. This problem is both timely and interesting, and the approach is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns.

The paper is well-organized, and the algorithmic steps are clearly described. However, some details of the method are not fully explained, making it difficult to fully implement the algorithm from just this description. The supplementary material may provide sufficient details, but it would be beneficial to include more explanations in the main text.

The paper is technically sound, and the ideas are well-introduced. However, the overall context is lost on occasion, and important aspects like choosing the relaxation parameters are relegated to a future article. This makes it difficult to get a feel for potential issues with this part of the method.

The experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough. The 'bias' evaluation metric is meaningless in comparing the WPP method with its competitors, as they literally measure two completely different things. The practical performance on the influenza data set is also worryingly weak, with the ACE interval being too wide to draw any meaningful conclusions.

The significance of the paper mainly lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem. It is a novel combination of familiar techniques and may stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of several approaches on the subject. However, in its current implementation, it seems unlikely to represent the finished article for practitioners.

One of the main concerns is the interpretation of the output distribution over bounds. The method does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, and it is unclear how likely these unfaithful instances are and how relevant/realistic these bounds themselves are. Many relaxations lead to wide bounds, which can become less than useful.

The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the relaxation parameters and their impact on the results. Additionally, the experimental evaluation should be more comprehensive, including a comparison with other methods and a more detailed analysis of the results.

In conclusion, the paper introduces an interesting and timely method for computing bounds on an inferred average causal effect. However, the experimental evaluation is poor, and the interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remains unclear. With some revisions to address these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
