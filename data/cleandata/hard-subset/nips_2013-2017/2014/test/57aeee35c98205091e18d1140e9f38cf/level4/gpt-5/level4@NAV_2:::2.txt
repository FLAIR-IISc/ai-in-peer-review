This paper presents a new method to derive bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) from a variable X to a variable Y. The approach relies on three key ideas: using Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow degrees of path cancellation, and introducing a set of inequality constraints. The ACE bounds are computed via a linear programming formulation under these principles. Although the algorithm has not yet been fully optimized, the resulting bounds appear quite narrow and accurate compared to existing approaches.

Quality
The authors aim to develop a novel method that yields tighter ACE bounds than current alternatives. By combining several plausible principles, they succeed in obtaining more stringent bounds, making the contribution interesting.
However, some of the criteria that determine the bounds are not fully elaborated. In particular, the justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in reverse is not clearly articulated. Since these parameters strongly influence the resulting bounds, principles for choosing them should be further developed and included.

Clarity
The theoretical exposition is largely clear, aside from the essential discussion noted above. However, the presentation of the experimental results is not well detailed. The practical implementation of the algorithm is not thoroughly described, and the definitions of the parameters in Table 1 (Table 5?) are not clearly specified.
There are also some typographical and cross-referencing issues. For example:
- “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1.” on page 6 => There is no algorithm table.
- “Results are summarized in Table 5.” on page 8 => Table 5 is not present (perhaps it should be Table 1).

Originality
While the problem setup has been studied in earlier work (as acknowledged by the authors), limiting the paper’s scope for strong originality, the idea of unifying the three components in a single framework does offer some originality. That said, as noted above, the validity of combining these elements is not argued with sufficient clarity.

Significance
The method shows promise in producing tighter ACE bounds, which is significant. Nonetheless, the empirical evaluation is limited, and the generality of the approach—both theoretically and across broader experimental settings—has not been rigorously assessed. The paper addresses an important problem, namely bounding the strength of causal relationships, but the soundness of the proposed ideas should be substantiated more thoroughly in both the theoretical development and the experimental validation.
