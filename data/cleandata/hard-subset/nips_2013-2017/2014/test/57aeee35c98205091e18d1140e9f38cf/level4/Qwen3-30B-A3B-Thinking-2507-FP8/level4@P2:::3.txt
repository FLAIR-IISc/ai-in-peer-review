

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel approach to causal inference that addresses violations of the faithfulness assumption in observational studies. The authors propose a framework that generates bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) by relaxing the faithfulness condition through a set of parameters, then uses linear programming to compute these bounds while accounting for potential unfaithful interactions. The method builds on Entner et al.'s work on witness variables and instrumental variable approaches, extending them to incorporate uncertainty about faithfulness violations. The paper makes a timely contribution to a field receiving significant attention, as practitioners increasingly recognize the limitations of strict faithfulness assumptions in real-world causal inference.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with thorough mathematical derivations and a well-structured algorithmic framework. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail to verify the core methodology. However, the paper could benefit from more complete discussion of parameter selection, which is currently relegated to future work. The experimental evaluation lacks robustness, particularly in the comparison metric for bias, which conflates point estimates with interval estimates in a way that renders the comparison meaningless. The influenza vaccination case study produces bounds that are too wide to be practically useful ([-0.23, 0.64]), and the paper fails to adequately explain why WPP's narrower bounds ([−0.10, 0.17]) should be considered an improvement in this context.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written, though some sections suffer from abrupt shifts in abstraction level that make the technical content difficult to follow. The relationship between witness variables, admissible sets, and the relaxation parameters is not clearly explained. Key terms like "unfaithful relaxations" require more intuitive interpretation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) lacks sufficient explanation of how witness selection interacts with the bound computation. The conclusion contains vague statements about "honest inference" that would benefit from more precise technical justification.

**Originality**: The paper presents a novel combination of existing techniques, particularly the adaptation of Entner's Rule 1 to create a framework that explicitly accounts for faithfulness violations. The method differs significantly from recent work on causal bounds (e.g., Geiger et al. 2014) by incorporating Bayesian learning and symbolic algebraic bounds. The authors successfully demonstrate how their approach extends previous work while addressing its limitations.

**Significance**: The paper makes an important conceptual contribution by providing a principled way to quantify the impact of faithfulness violations in causal inference. The theoretical framework advances our understanding of how to incorporate uncertainty about causal structure into estimation. However, the practical significance is limited by the current implementation's inability to produce meaningful bounds in many realistic scenarios. The paper would benefit from stronger evidence that WPP provides actionable insights beyond the trivial interval [-1, 1] that always contains the true ACE.

**Strengths**: The core idea of using relaxation parameters to model faithfulness violations is conceptually elegant and addresses a genuine limitation in current causal inference practice. The Bayesian approach to handling uncertainty in the bounds is well-motivated and avoids the pitfalls of direct priors on unidentifiable parameters. The symbolic bounds derivation provides valuable theoretical insights that complement the numerical approach.

**Weaknesses**: The experimental evaluation fails to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, particularly when the bounds are too wide to be practically useful. The paper does not adequately address how practitioners should interpret the distribution over bounds or how to choose relaxation parameters in real applications. The influenza case study's results suggest that WPP may not consistently outperform simpler approaches in real-world settings.

**Recommendations**: The authors should strengthen the experimental evaluation by comparing methods using metrics appropriate for interval estimates (e.g., interval width and coverage rates). They should provide clearer guidance on parameter selection and interpretation of the bounds. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when WPP provides meaningful advantages over standard approaches, and when it might be preferable to use simpler methods.

**Overall**: The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach that represents a meaningful contribution to causal inference methodology. While the current implementation has practical limitations, the theoretical framework has significant potential for future development. The reviewers recommend acceptance, though the authors should address the concerns about experimental evaluation and practical interpretation in revision. The method is conceptually interesting and could become a valuable tool for causal inference practitioners if the implementation challenges are addressed.
