This paper addresses the challenge of average causal effect (ACE) estimation between two binary variables in scenarios where the causal relationships among covariates are incompletely specified. The work builds upon a covariate selection rule from Entner et al. (2013), originally designed to identify a sufficient set of observed covariates for blocking back-door paths, and which relies on the faithfulness assumption for justification. This paper innovatively reframes the rule to generate bounds – rather than point estimates – for the ACE, employing a method inspired by Ramsahai (2012).

While I haven’t fully verified all technical aspects, the core concept appears sound, promising, and practically relevant. However, the precise function of the conditions outlined in Entner et al.’s rule within this bounding approach is unclear. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions lack a direct connection to the underlying causal structure. My understanding is that these conditions, if met, *might* influence the selection of relaxation parameters, but the paper deliberately avoids detailed discussion of this connection. Consequently, it seems the developed machinery doesn't fundamentally depend on these conditions, leading me to question whether the observed robustness in the experiments is related to this independence. I would greatly appreciate clarification on this point.

Several smaller points warrant attention:

1. In Section 3, the phrasing “… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …” is somewhat counterintuitive. Unfaithfulness typically corresponds to *allowing* additional independencies, rather than a violation of existing ones. The proposed framework appears to accommodate “near-violations” of faithfulness by limiting the degree of dependence, rather than characterizing violations.
2. Including a performance comparison with the original Entner et al. method, utilizing their conservative thresholds and treating “don’t know” results as uninformative bounds, would enhance the experimental evaluation.
3. On page 8, the example where X=1 represents receiving a flu shot and Y=1 indicates non-hospitalization makes the interpretation of a negative ACE as indicating a desirable vaccine counterintuitive.
4. The table cited as “Table 1” above page 8 is actually referenced as “Table 5” in the main text. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution, despite its technical complexity.
