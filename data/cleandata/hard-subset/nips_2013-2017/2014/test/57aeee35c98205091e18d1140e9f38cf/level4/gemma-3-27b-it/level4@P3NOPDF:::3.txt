## Review of Witness Protection Program for Causal Inference

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a novel approach to computing bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) that explicitly accounts for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The algorithm achieves this by introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, formulating the problem as a linear program to derive upper and lower bounds on the ACE, adjusted for appropriate covariates. 

This is a timely and interesting contribution. The problem of robust causal inference, particularly in the face of unfaithful graphical models, is receiving considerable attention. The WPP approach is a principled attempt to quantify the impact of such violations, leveraging established mathematical tools for optimization. The name “Witness Protection Program” is particularly memorable and apt.

However, the practical interpretation and utility of the resulting bounds are questionable. The output is a distribution over bounds defined by ‘unfaithful relaxations’ relative to a given causal model, and the relevance or likelihood of these relaxed instances – and, consequently, the realism of the derived bounds – is unclear. Many relaxation settings lead to excessively wide bounds, diminishing their practical value. Crucially, the method does not commit to a point estimate within this interval, despite the risk that users will interpret it as such.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The use of a “bias” metric is misleading, as it compares fundamentally different quantities produced by WPP and competing methods. This makes it difficult to assess whether WPP provides a valuable addition to the practitioner’s toolkit or simply introduces unnecessary complexity. Furthermore, the practical performance on the influenza dataset is concerning; an ACE in the range (-0.23, 0.64) offers little actionable insight, and may in fact represent the most honest assessment of the method’s current limitations.

**Quality:** The approach is technically sound, and the supplementary material (which I have not exhaustively reviewed as a late submission reviewer) appears to provide necessary details. The ideas are generally well-introduced, though the broader context is occasionally lost. A key limitation is the deferral of guidance on selecting the relaxation parameters to future work, hindering a complete evaluation of the method’s sensitivity and potential pitfalls. The experimental evaluation lacks robustness and balance.

**Clarity:** The paper is reasonably well-written, although the algorithmic steps and parameter adjustments can be challenging to follow, particularly due to a lack of detailed explanation in certain areas. While I believe I understand the overall process, a complete implementation based solely on the provided description would be difficult. The supplementary material may address this concern.

**Originality:** The method represents a novel combination of existing techniques, distinct from but related to recent work addressing the same problem. It has the potential to stimulate valuable cross-pollination of ideas within the field.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper lies primarily in its conceptual contribution – demonstrating how to incorporate considerations of unfaithfulness into causal inference through optimization techniques. Its current implementation is unlikely to provide a definitive solution for practitioners, but the ideas underlying WPP could prove influential.



**Detailed Comments:**

*   p1/47: Replace "unstable" with "do not necessarily hold." Stability refers to the model itself, not the assumption's truth.
*   p2/74: Clarify "cartoon representation."
*   p2/93-94: Explicitly state the assumption of causal sufficiency or knowledge of W – > X.
*   p3/127: Explain that the witness W is only used to *identify* the covariate set Z, and is not required for the actual ACE computation.
*   p3/143: Correct typo: "an useful" -> "a useful".
*   p3/144: "gives greater control over violations of faithfulness" is too vague. Specify *how* control is exerted.
*   p4/164: Replace "might" with "can."
*   p4/164: Remove "(conditional)" or replace with "(unconditional)," as W is always dependent on U given X.
*   p4/172-180: Provide more intuition on the meaning of the relaxation parameters. This section feels like a sudden shift in abstraction and is difficult to interpret without further explanation. Avoid phrasing that suggests these parameters represent "weak interactions."
*   p4/183: Include a link to the original introduction of the concepts in reference (1).
*   p4/185-189: Further interpret the constraint parameters.
*   p5/221: Briefly describe the relationship between the search component and the bounds computation.
*   p5/221: Explain how the identification of admissible sets Z for W depends on the relaxation parameters theta.
*   p5/239: Clarify the purpose of "Background ... used here." Is this step crucial to the paper’s contribution?
*   p5/259: Confirm that the maximum of 10 free parameters describes the entire set Z, not a set of 10 individual variables.
*   p6/304: Note the related work "Estimating Causal Eff ects by Bounding Confounding" (Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf, UAI2014).
*   p6/319-332: The efficacy of the back-substitution process for refining bounds is doubtful. Provide some indication of its effectiveness in Section 5.
*   p7/350: Clarify that multiple bounds arise from different admissible sets Z for different witnesses.
*   p7/357: The subsequent example demonstrating the trivial optimality of the bias metric clearly shows its meaninglessness for comparing WPP to other methods.
*   p7/360: A bound of (0, 0.6) still allows for a potentially strong causal effect.
*   p7/369: Consider the impact of smaller sample sizes (e.g., 500 records), common in medical trials, on bound size.
*   p8/384: Table 1 provides insufficient information to assess the benefit of WPP over a standard faithfulness-based point estimate.
*   p8/391: The stability of WPP may simply reflect the looseness of the derived bounds.
*   p8/406-408: Emphasize that the bounds should *not* be interpreted as a confidence interval around a best-guess value. This is a critical point for proper interpretation and highlights the practical challenges of using WPP results.
*   p8/421+: The use of emotive language ("risky Bayesian approaches," "WPP bounds keep inference more honest") suggests a biased evaluation. Avoid such phrasing. Reframe "providing a compromise" as WPP focusing on an additional, important aspect of the inference problem. The final statement regarding "theory-driven analyses" is unclear and dismissive.
*   p9: Correct typo in reference 9: "inuenza" -> "influenza".



In summary, this paper presents a technically interesting and conceptually valuable approach to robust causal inference. However, the lack of clarity in interpretation, limited experimental evaluation, and potentially impractical bound widths currently limit its application. Further work addressing these issues is warranted.
