Summary
This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of X on Y from observational data with binary variables. The key ideas are: (i) invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to treat a “witness” W and admissible set Z as the basis for IV-style bounds rather than back-door adjustment; (ii) relax faithfulness by allowing bounded deviations (path cancellations) encoded as inequality constraints on local conditionals; and (iii) compute bounds via a linear programming formulation that yields a posterior distribution over ACE bounds under a constrained Bayesian model. The approach connects to and extends prior work on instrumental-variable bounds (Balke–Pearl), linear-programming formulations (Dawid; Ramsahai), faithfulness-based covariate selection (Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes), and causal discovery (PC). The authors also present algebraic/symbolic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that accelerates testing and bounding relative to full LP solves. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu vaccination dataset show that WPP can yield substantially tighter bounds than standard IV when conditional independencies provide partial protection against unobserved confounding.

Quality
Strengths:
- Technically interesting combination of causal discovery constraints with IV-style bounding. The relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) interpolate between the standard IV model and a chain model W→X→Y, providing a controlled way to trade assumptions for informativeness.
- The LP formulation and the posterior over bounds are principled and, in simulations, produce intervals that appear narrower than standard IV bounds while maintaining robustness to confounding.
- The algebraic bounds and back-substitution procedure add computational insight and a practical speedup.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The justification for using Rule 1 “in reverse” needs to be made explicit. Rule 1 is derived under faithfulness for identifying admissible back-door sets. When repurposed to construct IV-style constraints, what structural assumptions must hold (e.g., W not a descendant of X or Y; W excluded from Y except via X conditional on Z; no Z affected by X or Y)? The paper should clearly state and defend the graphical conditions under which the inversion is valid, ideally with a figure and a lemma/theorem.
- Choice of relaxation parameters is critical but under-specified. Guidance for selecting (w, x, y, β, β̄) is minimal, yet these parameters largely determine bound width and potential model misspecification. Please provide a principled selection strategy (e.g., prior calibration, sensitivity grids, data-driven diagnostics) and report sensitivity results systematically.
- Some constraints and parameterizations are not fully defined in the main text. For example, the roles and interpretations of ζ, κ, η, δ, and ω, and the mapping between observable and latent-averaged quantities, would benefit from a concise, self-contained summary with consistent notation.
- The Bayesian implementation relies on constrained Dirichlet priors with rejection sampling and a pragmatic falsification test (>95% rejection). This can be computationally heavy and somewhat ad hoc. Discuss computational complexity, convergence diagnostics, and robustness to prior choices (e.g., BDeu ESS, independence priors).
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Beyond comparisons to naive estimators and the faithfulness-based point estimator, it would help to benchmark against standard IV bounds, monotonicity variants, and recent partial-identification methods, reporting both coverage and average width.

Clarity
- Theoretical exposition is generally clear, but the paper should explicitly state the structural assumptions required for the inverted Rule 1 and the precise meaning of “bounded” faithfulness violations.
- There are issues with presentation and cross-referencing: the “full algorithm” is referred to as a table but appears as an algorithm; table/figure numbering is inconsistent (e.g., “Table 5” not found; “Table 1” vs. “Algorithm 1”). Please correct numbering and ensure parameter definitions (e.g., those listed in the table) are clearly given in the main text.
- Provide a compact glossary of symbols and a schematic of the parameter flow (observed contingencies → constraints → LP → bounds), including the conditional-on-Z case.

Originality
- The problem of bounding ACE under noncompliance/unmeasured confounding is well-studied, and components of the approach (IV bounds, LP formulations, faithfulness) are established. The novel contributions are (a) inverting Rule 1 to generate witness/admissible sets for bounding, (b) formalizing bounded deviations from faithfulness within an LP framework, and (c) deriving algebraic bounds with a back-substitution algorithm. Overall, the combination is original enough to be of interest, though incremental relative to the individual literatures.

Significance
- If validated and better documented, WPP could become a useful tool to complement default practices in observational studies, offering tighter bounds than standard IV when conditional independencies are informative. The potential impact is practical and methodological.
- Current evidence is promising but limited. Stronger significance claims would require broader empirical validation (more datasets, ablations, runtime analysis), clearer parameter-selection principles, and more extensive sensitivity studies demonstrating coverage and informativeness across settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing bridge between faithfulness-based discovery and partial identification.
- Method can yield meaningfully tighter bounds than standard IV in realistic scenarios.
- Algebraic bounds and back-substitution provide computational and interpretive value.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient justification and clarity for the inverted use of Rule 1 and the required structural assumptions.
- Lack of principled guidance for setting relaxation parameters, with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Presentation issues (notation, table/figure references) and incomplete algorithmic specification.
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow.

Suggestions for improvement
- State and prove (or cite) conditions under which (W, Z) from Rule 1 can be validly used for IV-style bounding; add a clear figure depicting the assumed structure.
- Provide a principled framework for choosing (w, x, y, β, β̄), including default priors/ranges, diagnostics, and sensitivity plots (interval width vs. parameter values).
- Consolidate notation and define all parameters in the main text; fix numbering and cross-references.
- Report standard IV bounds alongside WPP bounds in all experiments; include coverage and average width, not only point error metrics.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability; compare LP vs. back-substitution trade-offs.
- Expand experiments and, if possible, include non-binary extensions or discuss how the framework generalizes.
