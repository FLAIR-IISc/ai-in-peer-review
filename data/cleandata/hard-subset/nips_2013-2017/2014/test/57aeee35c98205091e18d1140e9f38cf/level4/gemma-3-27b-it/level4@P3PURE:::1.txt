This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully specified. It considers a covariate selection rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) designed to identify a sufficient set of observed covariates for blocking all back-door paths. While Entner et al.’s rule relies on the faithfulness assumption, this work proposes to leverage the rule to derive bounds – rather than point estimates – for the ACE, utilizing a method inspired by Ramsahai (2012). 

I find the central idea of this paper to be sound, interesting, and potentially useful, although I confess I haven’t fully absorbed all the technical details. However, I was puzzled by the role of the conditions specified in Entner et al.’s rule within this framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions do not possess clear implications regarding the underlying causal structure. My understanding is that, if satisfied, the conditions *might* be relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly defers discussion of this point. It appears the methodology doesn’t critically depend on these conditions, and I wonder if the observed robustness of the method in the experiments is related to this. I would appreciate clarification on this aspect.

Several minor points could be addressed:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…” is somewhat unusual. Unfaithfulness typically allows for *additional* independence relationships, rather than violating existing ones. It seems the framework instead aims to allow for – or express – “near violations” of faithfulness by bounding the magnitude of dependence.
2. In the experiments, including the performance of Entner et al.’s method using their original, stringent thresholds would be informative. Treating the “don’t know” result as an uninformative bound could provide a useful baseline for comparison.
3. In the second paragraph on p. 8, if “X=1 means the patient received a flu shot, and Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized,” a negative ACE suggests the vaccine is *undesirable* – it implies the flu shot *increases* hospitalization risk.
4. “Table 1” is referenced on top of p. 8, but cited as “Table 5” within the text. 

Overall, this is an interesting paper, though the technical details present a challenge for quick comprehension.
