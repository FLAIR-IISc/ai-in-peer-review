Summary
This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), an algorithm for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) while allowing for controlled violations of the faithfulness assumption. The method specifies a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful dependencies and uses linear programming to compute upper and lower ACE bounds for a target link after adjusting for a suitable covariate set. The topic is timely and important, and the paper offers a principled attempt to quantify the impact of weak violations of faithfulness using familiar optimization tools.

Strengths
- Clearly motivated problem: quantifying robustness of causal conclusions under violations of faithfulness is both relevant and under active investigation.
- Principled formulation: relaxation parameters provide a transparent way to encode departures from ideal assumptions; the linear programming approach is appropriate for computing bounds.
- Potential for cross-fertilization: the approach connects constraint-based causal discovery, sensitivity analysis, and partial identification, and may inform future developments.

Weaknesses
- Interpretability and practical relevance: the output is a distribution over bounds as a function of unspecified relaxation parameters. Without guidance on how likely particular relaxations are, or how to elicit/choose them, it is unclear how to interpret or act on the bounds. Many plausible relaxations yield very wide intervals that are difficult to use.
- No preferred point estimate: while the goal is to report bounds, many users will still seek a central estimate; the paper does not offer calibrated summaries within the interval (e.g., robust centroids, minimax estimators, or decision-theoretic guidance).
- Experimental evaluation: the comparison metric (bias) is not commensurate across methods that output intervals versus point estimates; conclusions from this comparison are not meaningful. The influenza case study yields extremely wide bounds (e.g., ACE in [-0.23, 0.64]), limiting practical conclusions—possibly the correct outcome, but it underscores the need for interpretation guidance.
- Parameter selection and robustness: the paper defers the choice/elicitation of relaxation parameters to future work, leaving a major practical component unaddressed. The frequency and magnitude with which back-substitution tightens bounds is also unclear.

Quality
The technical framework appears sound, and the supplementary material reportedly contains detailed derivations (not fully verified by this reviewer). The main ideas are sensible and potentially impactful. However:
- The paper does not provide a defensible procedure for selecting relaxation parameters or for assessing their plausibility in real applications.
- The empirical section does not provide balanced or robust comparisons. In particular, bias is unsuitable for comparing a bounding method to point estimators, and the benefits over standard faithfulness-based estimates are not convincingly demonstrated.
- The practical effectiveness of the back-substitution step in tightening bounds is not quantified (e.g., how often it helps, by how much, and under what conditions).

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but several sections are difficult to follow due to abrupt changes in abstraction level and insufficient intuition for key parameters and constraints. The sequence of algorithmic steps and the relationships among the many closely related parameters are challenging to track. With improved exposition (and possibly a worked example), the method would be much easier to implement from the text; as written, one may need to rely on the supplement.

Originality
The approach is a novel synthesis of established tools and is distinct from closely related contemporaneous work on bounding causal effects under limited confounding or faithfulness violations. It adds useful ideas to the literature and may encourage integration across methods.

Significance
The main contribution lies in the conceptual framing and tools introduced. As a practical solution, the current instantiation seems premature for routine use, but elements of the WPP approach could prove important in future, more general frameworks for robust causal inference.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled approach to selecting or eliciting relaxation parameters (e.g., prior beliefs, sensitivity curves, expert elicitation, or calibration to detectable violations).
- Report how often back-substitution tightens bounds and by how much across scenarios, including small-n settings (e.g., n = 200–1000).
- Compare against directly relevant bounding methods (e.g., recent work such as “Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding,” UAI 2014) and classical bounds (e.g., Balke-Pearl), using metrics appropriate for intervals: coverage, average/median width, regret under simple utility models, and whether the interval excludes clinically relevant thresholds.
- Provide case studies demonstrating how practitioners should interpret and use the bounds in decision-making, including when bounds remain wide.
- Clarify the relationship between the witness, the admissible set Z, and the bounds computation, and explicitly show how admissible sets depend on the relaxation parameters.
- Temper language in the discussion to avoid emotive or dismissive phrasing, and clarify the intended role of WPP relative to Bayesian and theory-driven approaches.

Overall verdict
- Contribution: Interesting and technically sound; conceptually valuable.
- Writing: Generally good but needs clearer exposition of parameters and procedures.
- Experiments: Currently weak; metrics and scenarios should be revised.
- Impact: Promising direction; not yet a practitioner-ready solution.
