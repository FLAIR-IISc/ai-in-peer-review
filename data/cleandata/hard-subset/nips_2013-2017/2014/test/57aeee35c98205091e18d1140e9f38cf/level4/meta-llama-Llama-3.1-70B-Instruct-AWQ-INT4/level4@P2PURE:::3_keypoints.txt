- Paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute ACE bounds by relaxing faithfulness and solving linear programs.
- Method introduces relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and produces distributions over upper/lower ACE bounds conditional on covariates.
- Problem addressed is timely and the approach is a principled use of known mathematical tools to quantify weak unfaithful interactions.
- It is unclear whether the WPP method is the correct or best way to capture the impact of unfaithfulness.
- The output (a distribution over bounds as a function of relaxations) is conceptually hard to interpret for practitioners.
- The likelihood and practical relevance of the unfaithful instances considered (and hence the bounds) are not established.
- Many relaxation settings yield very wide bounds, reducing practical usefulness.
- The method provides intervals but no preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation.
- Experimental evaluation is weak and unbalanced, especially in comparisons using the “bias” metric that measure different things.
- It is unclear whether WPP adds practical value or is an unnecessary complication given current comparisons.
- Performance on the influenza dataset is concerning; the ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64) affords limited actionable conclusions.
- The approach is technically sound with many details in the supplement, but some contextual and practical choices are underexplained.
- Key practical aspects (e.g., how to choose relaxation parameters) are deferred to future work, making assessment difficult.
- The paper is decently written overall, but the conclusion and some algorithmic explanations are unclear or hard to follow.
- Subtly different parameters and algorithmic steps are hard to track; the supplement may be needed to implement the method.
- The method is an original combination of familiar techniques and is sufficiently different from recent related work to be interesting.
- The paper’s significance is in the ideas and direction rather than in being a finished tool for practitioners.
- Some WPP ideas may be useful in future work, but the current implementation is unlikely to be adopted by practitioners as-is.
- The name “Witness Protection Program” is catchy.
- Several wording and terminology issues: misuse of “unstable,” unclear “cartoon representation,” and vague phrasing about control over faithfulness violations.
- Implicit assumptions need clarification (e.g., causal sufficiency or W -> X), and the role of witness W in identifying Z should be explicitly stated.
- Multiple typos and phrasing fixes are needed (e.g., “an useful” → “a useful”; “might” → “can”; reference typo “inuenza” → “influenza”).
- Transition to higher abstraction (parameters) is abrupt; readers need intuition and a link to the original introduction.
- Constraint parameters require clearer interpretation to avoid misreading them as indicating weak interactions.
- Clarify relation between the search stage and bounds computation, and rephrase unclear sentences (e.g., “Background ... used here”).
- Clarify that “maximum of 10 free parameters” refers to describing set Z, not to Z containing 10 variables.
- The claim of no previous analytical bounds overlooks related recent work (e.g., UAI2014 paper mentioned).
- Doubts about the practical efficacy of the back-substitution refinement; empirical evidence of its effectiveness should be provided.
- Comparisons across witnesses and admissible sets complicate the “more than one bound” claim and make bias-based comparisons meaningless.
- Data size concerns: results use 5000 points, but many applications (e.g., medical trials) have much less data, affecting bound widths.
- Table 1 and related statements do not convincingly show WPP’s benefit over standard point estimates based on faithfulness.
- Warnings needed: bounds are not confidence intervals around a best guess and can be misinterpreted; some language in the paper appears emotive or biased.
