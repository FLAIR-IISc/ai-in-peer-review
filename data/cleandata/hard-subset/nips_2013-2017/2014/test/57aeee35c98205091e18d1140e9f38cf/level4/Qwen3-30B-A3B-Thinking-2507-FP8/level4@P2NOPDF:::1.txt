

This paper addresses the challenge of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure among covariates is partially unknown. It builds on Entner et al.'s (2013) rule for selecting covariates sufficient to block back-door paths, but repurposes it to provide bounds on ACE rather than point estimates, following Ramsahai (2012). While the core idea is sound and potentially valuable for robust causal inference, the precise role of Entner et al.'s conditions in the proposed framework remains unclear.

The authors justify their approach without relying on the faithfulness assumption, which is critical for Entner et al.'s original rule. However, without faithfulness, Entner's conditions do not inherently constrain the causal structure. The paper explicitly sets aside how these conditions relate to relaxation parameters, yet it is unclear whether the method's observed robustness in experiments stems from this dependency. If the conditions are merely used as a heuristic for parameter selection without causal justification, the claimed robustness might not be theoretically grounded. Clarification on this point is essential to assess the method's validity.

Minor points for improvement:  
1. Section 3, first paragraph: "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is misleading. Unfaithfulness involves *additional* independencies, not violations of existing ones. The framework likely aims to model *near-faithfulness* by bounding dependence magnitudes; the phrasing should be revised accordingly.  
2. Experiments: Include results for Entner et al.'s method using their original stringent thresholds (treating "don't know" as uninformative bounds) to better contextualize the proposed approach's advantages.  
3. Page 8, second paragraph: If *X=1* indicates flu vaccination and *Y=1* indicates hospitalization, a *negative* ACE implies vaccination reduces hospitalization risk. Thus, a negative ACE *does* indicate a desirable vaccine effect (as stated), but the phrasing could be clearer to avoid confusion.  
4. Page 8: Table 1 is cited as "Table 5" in the text; correct this typographical error.  

Overall, the paper presents an interesting shift from point estimation to bounding under partial causal knowledge. The technical depth is substantial, and the experimental results suggest practical utility. However, the lack of explicit justification for using Entner et al.'s conditions without faithfulness weakens the theoretical foundation. Resolving this ambiguity would significantly strengthen the contribution. The minor comments above are straightforward to address and would enhance clarity. While the paper is promising, the current presentation requires greater precision regarding the interplay between the rule’s conditions and the method’s robustness claims.
