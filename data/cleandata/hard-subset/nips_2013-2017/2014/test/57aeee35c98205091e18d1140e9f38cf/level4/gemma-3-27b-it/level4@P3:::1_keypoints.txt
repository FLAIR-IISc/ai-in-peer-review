- Tackles estimation of average causal effect (ACE) between binary variables under unobserved confounding without assuming strict faithfulness.  
- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) that repurposes Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to derive bounds on ACE instead of point identification.  
- Interprets faithfulness violations as bounded deviations from local independence and formulates a linear program (building on Dawid and Ramsahai) to compute bounds.  
- Introduces relaxations to reduce computational cost and a Bayesian learning approach for parameter estimation.  
- Demonstrates method on synthetic and real (influenza vaccination) datasets, showing improved informative bounds over some existing techniques.  
- Novel: creatively uses a faithfulness-based rule to derive bounds under relaxed assumptions.  
- Practicality: relaxations and Bayesian estimation address computational challenges.  
- Theoretical grounding: leverages established causal inference tools (do-calculus, IVs, linear programming).  
- Clear motivation: rightly emphasizes need for methods robust to faithfulness violations.  
- Main weakness: unclear and under-explained connection between Entner et al.’s Rule 1 and the implemented methodology.  
- The paper does not convincingly show how Rule 1 conditions are actively used beyond motivating the LP parameter space.  
- The “protecting” mechanism for witnesses against faithfulness violations is intriguing but insufficiently elaborated.  
- Computational complexity remains a concern, especially as covariate dimensionality grows.  
- Bounds are sensitive to relaxation parameter choices (w, y, x, β, β̄), with limited guidance on selecting them.  
- Some language is ambiguous (e.g., “bounded violations of local independence”) and needs tightening.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, offers a novel and potentially useful method, and provides supportive empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: tenuous theoretical link to Entner et al., practical issues with complexity and parameter sensitivity, and insufficient discussion of limitations and potential biases.  
- Overall assessment: promising contribution but currently warrants only a weak accept pending clearer justification of Rule 1’s role or a justification of its independence.  
- Minor fixes: rephrase Section 3.1 wording on near-faithfulness, add comparison to Entner et al.’s method with “don’t know” as uninformative bounds, correct interpretation of negative ACE in influenza example, and fix table citation errors.
