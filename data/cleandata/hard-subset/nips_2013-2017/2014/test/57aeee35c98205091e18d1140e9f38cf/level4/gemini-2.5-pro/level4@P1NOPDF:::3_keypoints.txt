- Introduces WPP, an LP-based algorithm that computes ACE bounds under controlled violations of faithfulness via relaxation parameters.
- Timely and interesting problem; principled attempt to quantify impact of unfaithful interactions using optimization tools.
- Technically sound approach with detailed supplementary material, though the main paper omits some core explanations.
- Novel combination of existing techniques; sufficiently distinct from recent work and likely to stimulate follow-up research.
- Major conceptual challenge: unclear how to assess the likelihood of the unfaithful relaxations, making the relevance of the produced bounds hard to judge.
- Many relaxations produce intervals that are too wide to be practically useful; no guidance or preferred point estimate is provided.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: the chosen "bias" metric is inappropriate for comparing fundamentally different methods.
- Influenza dataset result (ACE interval -0.23 to 0.64) illustrates bounds that are too wide to support meaningful conclusions.
- Important methodological details (e.g., how to choose relaxation parameters) are deferred to future work, reducing immediate applicability.
- Algorithmic presentation is hard to follow due to many subtly different parameters and insufficient intuition in the main text.
- Unclear relationship between the search for admissible sets and bounds computation, and how admissible set identification depends on relaxation parameters.
- Skepticism about practical efficacy of the back-substitution refinement step; requests experimental evidence of its usefulness.
- Paper overlooks related prior work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014) when claiming novelty of analytical bounds.
- Concerns about sample-size sensitivity: experiments use N=5000 but many real-world studies have far fewer records, likely widening bounds further.
- Table and results do not demonstrate any clear practical benefit of WPP over standard point estimates.
- Some wording and presentation issues: inappropriate terms ("unstable"), missing assumptions (causal sufficiency), abrupt abstraction transitions, and ambiguous claims (e.g., "maximum of 10 free parameters").
- Conclusion uses emotive and biased language; should be rewritten to be more objective and collegial.
