

This paper addresses the challenge of estimating average causal effects (ACE) between binary variables when the full causal structure of covariates is unknown. It extends Entner et al.'s (2013) rule for identifying admissible covariate sets, but shifts from providing point estimates (which rely on faithfulness assumptions) to generating bounds on ACE through a modified linear programming approach inspired by Ramsahai (2012). The authors propose a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) that relaxes the faithfulness assumption by allowing bounded violations of local independence, providing posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates.

The paper's main contribution is compelling: it offers a practical middle ground between faithfulness-based point estimators and instrumental variable-based bounds, which could be valuable for observational studies where faithfulness assumptions may not hold. The method's potential to complement existing tools in causal inference practice represents a significant contribution to the field.

However, I found the paper challenging to fully digest due to its technical depth, particularly regarding the role of Entner et al.'s conditions in the proposed framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, it's unclear how the conditions from Entner et al. directly inform the causal structure, and I'm uncertain whether the method's apparent robustness in experiments stems from these conditions or other aspects of the approach. The paper explicitly sets aside the question of how to choose relaxation parameters, which is a significant practical consideration for implementation.

Specific comments:
1. The phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" (Section 3) appears misleading. Unfaithfulness typically involves the presence of unexpected conditional independencies, not violations of independence. The framework seems to model "approximate" faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence, which would be more accurately described as allowing "almost violations" of faithfulness.

2. For experimental comparison, it would be beneficial to report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when using their stringent thresholds, treating "don't know" responses as providing uninformative bounds rather than excluding them from analysis.

3. In the influenza vaccination case study, the text states "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was hospitalized," yet claims a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine. If Y=1 indicates hospitalization, a negative ACE would suggest vaccination reduces hospitalization (which is desirable), but the explanation should clarify this relationship to avoid confusion.

4. Table 1 on page 8 is cited as "Table 5" in the text, creating a reference inconsistency.

This is an interesting contribution with potential practical utility, though its technical complexity and certain ambiguities regarding the role of Entner et al.'s conditions warrant clarification. The method provides a valuable approach for practitioners who want to avoid over-reliance on faithfulness assumptions while still leveraging conditional independence constraints.
