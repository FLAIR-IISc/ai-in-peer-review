

The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a method for estimating causal effects in observational studies that accounts for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption. The authors propose a framework that leverages conditional independence constraints to identify "witness" variables and admissible covariate sets, then computes bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) through linear programming with relaxed constraints. This approach builds on Entner et al.'s work on instrumental variables and extends Ramsahai's linear programming framework for causal bounds.

The paper makes several technically sound contributions. The authors generalize the faithfulness assumption by introducing relaxation parameters that quantify deviations from conditional independence, then use these to derive bounds on the ACE. The method is implemented through both a numerical linear programming approach and a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm that provides symbolic bounds. The authors also propose a Bayesian framework for posterior inference over the bounds, which addresses uncertainty in the estimation process.

**Quality.** The technical content is sound and well-motivated. The authors provide sufficient mathematical details in the main text and supplemental material to verify the correctness of their approach. The method represents a logical extension of existing work on causal bounds (e.g., Ramsahai, Dawid), though the connection to recent work on bounding confounding (Geiger et al., UAI 2014) could be more explicitly discussed. The experimental evaluation is limited, however. The synthetic studies compare WPP against naive estimators (NE1, NE2) and Entner's method, but the evaluation metric—Euclidean distance between the true ACE and the estimate—is problematic for interval estimates. For WPP, the error metric should account for interval coverage rather than pointwise distance, as the paper acknowledges ("error of the interval [L,U] is zero if the true ACE lies in this interval"). The influenza vaccination case study demonstrates the method's application but fails to convincingly show the added value over standard instrumental variable bounds.

**Clarity.** The paper is generally well-written with clear mathematical exposition. However, several aspects would benefit from clarification. The motivation for the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and their interpretation could be strengthened. The transition between the linear programming approach and the back-substitution algorithm is abrupt and would benefit from more intuitive explanation. The statement "WPP is quite stable" (p. 8) is misleading—WPP's bounds are so wide in unsolvable cases that they become virtually indistinguishable from the trivial [-1,1] interval, which undermines the practical utility of the method. The distinction between "witness" and "admissible set" should be clarified earlier in the paper (e.g., p. 3, line 127).

**Originality.** The paper presents a novel combination of existing techniques: it repurposes Entner's Rule 1 as a witness identification procedure rather than a back-door adjustment method, introduces relaxation parameters to handle faithfulness violations, and develops symbolic bounds via back-substitution. While the core ideas build on prior work (Entner et al., Ramsahai), the specific integration of these elements into a unified framework for bounding causal effects represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The method differs significantly from recent approaches that focus on point estimation under faithfulness assumptions.

**Significance.** The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference—handling potential faithfulness violations in observational studies. The method provides a principled way to quantify uncertainty about causal effects when the faithfulness assumption might not hold. However, the current implementation has limited practical utility due to the wide bounds it produces. As the authors acknowledge, "the price for making fewer assumptions" (Manski) is wider intervals. The paper's significance would be substantially enhanced if the authors could demonstrate cases where WPP provides meaningful bounds that are narrower than standard instrumental variable bounds, or where the method provides actionable insights that point estimation cannot.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution to causal inference, provides a well-motivated extension of existing methods, and offers a Bayesian framework for uncertainty quantification. The back-substitution algorithm is a clever computational improvement over the full linear programming approach.

**Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation is inadequate for demonstrating practical utility. The comparison metric is inappropriate for interval estimates. The method often produces overly wide bounds that provide little actionable insight. The connection to recent work on causal bounds (Geiger et al., UAI 2014) is underdeveloped. The practical interpretation of the output (a distribution over bounds) remains unclear to practitioners.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting technical approach to a meaningful problem, the current implementation lacks practical utility due to the wide bounds it produces, and the experimental evaluation fails to demonstrate added value over simpler methods. The method would be significantly strengthened by (1) demonstrating cases where WPP provides narrower bounds than standard IV approaches, (2) providing a more compelling evaluation metric for interval estimates, and (3) clarifying the interpretation of the output distribution over bounds. The paper would benefit from a more focused presentation that emphasizes the specific scenarios where WPP provides meaningful advantages over existing methods.
