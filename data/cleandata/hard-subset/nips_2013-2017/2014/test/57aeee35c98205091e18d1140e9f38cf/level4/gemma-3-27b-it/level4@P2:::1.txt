This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in observational studies where the full causal structure, particularly concerning confounding variables, is unknown. The authors build upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) for identifying covariate sets sufficient for back-door adjustment, but relax the reliance on the faithfulness assumption. Instead of seeking a single point estimate, they aim to derive bounds on the ACE, leveraging a method inspired by Ramsahai (2012).

The core idea – using conditional independence constraints to establish bounds rather than point estimates in the presence of potential unfaithfulness – is appealing and potentially valuable. The paper’s contribution lies in providing a framework that offers a degree of robustness to violations of the faithfulness assumption, a common concern in causal inference. The introduction of a “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) metaphor is memorable and aptly describes the methodology's intent. 

However, a key point of confusion for me concerns the precise role of Entner et al.’s rule within the proposed framework. While the authors leverage the rule, its foundational justification rests on faithfulness. Without this assumption, the conditions within the rule (W ⊥ Y | Z and W ⊥ Y | Z ∪ {X}) don’t inherently constrain the causal structure. I’m unclear how satisfying these conditions directly informs the selection of relaxation parameters, or why they are essential to the approach. The paper acknowledges this issue implicitly, stating the matter is “put aside,” which is unsatisfying. It seems the method’s success might not be fundamentally reliant on these conditions, and clarification would strengthen the paper.

The paper is generally well-organized, though some sections are dense and require careful reading. The inclusion of algebraic bounds (Section 4) and the back-substitution algorithm represents a significant effort to improve computational efficiency and provides analytical insights. The experiments, including a real-world application to influenza vaccination data, are valuable and demonstrate the method’s potential.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Causal inference with unmeasured confounding remains a central challenge.
*   **Novel approach:** The bound-based framework offers a pragmatic alternative to strict faithfulness assumptions.
*   **Technical sophistication:** The combination of linear programming, Bayesian inference, and algebraic manipulation demonstrates a strong technical foundation.
*   **Empirical validation:** Experiments with synthetic and real data provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
*   **Computational considerations:** Addressing computational complexity through relaxations and the back-substitution algorithm is a practical strength.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Unclear connection to Entner et al.’s rule:** The justification for using the rule's conditions without relying on faithfulness is not adequately explained.
*   **Density and complexity:** Some sections are difficult to follow, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   **Minor inconsistencies:** The reference to “Table 1” in the text as “Table 5” is a minor error.
*   **Clarification needed in experimental section:** The explanation regarding the interpretation of a negative ACE in the influenza vaccination experiment (p. 8) is confusing as written. The statement that Y=1 indicates *not* being hospitalized seems counterintuitive.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental problem in causal inference and offers a novel approach with potential practical benefits.
*   The technical contributions are substantial and demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature.
*   The experiments provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness, even in challenging scenarios.
*   Despite some clarity issues, the core idea is sound and the potential impact is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of a clear justification for the role of Entner et al.’s rule weakens the theoretical foundation of the method.
*   The density and complexity of the presentation might limit its accessibility and reproducibility.
*   The minor inconsistencies and confusing explanations detract from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has merit and presents a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. However, addressing the concerns regarding the connection to Entner et al.’s rule and improving the clarity of the presentation would significantly strengthen the paper. I lean towards **acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing these concerns in a revised version.



**Minor Comments:**

1.  Section 3, "… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …" This phrasing is potentially misleading. It might be more accurate to state that the framework allows for "near violations" of faithfulness by constraining the strength of dependencies.
2.  Section 5, consider including results from Entner et al.’s method with stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” responses as uninformative bounds. This would provide a more complete comparative analysis.
3.  Section 5, clarify the interpretation of Y=1 in the influenza vaccination example.
4.  Correct the citation of “Table 1” on p. 8.
