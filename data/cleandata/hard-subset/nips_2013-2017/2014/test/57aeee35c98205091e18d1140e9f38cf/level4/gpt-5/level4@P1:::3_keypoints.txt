- Proposal: Witness Protection Program (WPP) bounds ACE under possible faithfulness violations via relaxation parameters and LP-based interval computation, plus a Bayesian posterior over bounds and symbolic back‑substitution for speed.
- Strength: Addresses a timely problem—causal effect estimation with unmeasured confounding and uncertain faithfulness.
- Strength: Principled formulation that explicitly parameterizes degrees of unfaithfulness with interpretable relaxation parameters tied to LP constraints.
- Strength: Clear ties to prior work (Entner et al., Dawid, Ramsahai, Pearl) with a novel inversion of Rule 1 and integration of symbolic bounds/back‑substitution.
- Strength: Bayesian treatment accounts for sampling uncertainty in independence testing and bound estimation.
- Weakness: Posterior-over-bounds interpretability is unclear and offers little guidance on how to choose or assess relaxation parameters.
- Weakness: Many settings produce very wide, potentially uninformative bounds, limiting practical utility.
- Weakness: No point estimates provided, risking misinterpretation of bounds as confidence intervals around a “best guess.”
- Weakness: Experimental evaluation is weak; the bias metric comparing intervals to point estimators is inappropriate and can be gamed by trivially wide intervals.
- Weakness: Influenza application yields broad, non‑actionable intervals, illustrating practical limitations.
- Weakness: Key practical choices (e.g., setting relaxation parameters) are deferred to future work, hindering assessment of robustness.
- Weakness: Some algorithmic steps and parameter roles are unclear in the main text, making reproducibility reliant on the supplement.
- Quality: Method appears technically sound; LP and Bayesian components are standard and correctly applied, but empirical claims are not convincing.
- Clarity: Generally readable but needs better early explanation of relaxation parameters, smoother transitions, and less emotive language in the conclusion.
- Originality: Novel combination of established ideas—turning witnesses into IV‑like constructs under bounded unfaithfulness and deriving algebraic bounds.
- Significance: Conceptual and methodological contribution by formalizing “approximate faithfulness”; promising but not yet practitioner‑ready.
- For acceptance: Addresses an important problem, offers principled modeling of unfaithfulness, and could stimulate follow‑up work on parameter selection and tighter bounds.
- Against acceptance: Insufficient empirical evidence, missing practical guidance for parameter choice, often too‑wide bounds, and reliance on supplement for key details.
- Suggested evaluations: Provide empirical evidence for back‑substitution tightening bounds and use metrics that penalize both interval width and coverage.
- Suggested exposition fixes: Clarify the roles of W and Z, link parameters to causal interpretation, explain how search and relaxation parameters interact, and avoid emotive phrasing.
- Minor edits: Numerous wording, clarity, and typo fixes across the paper (e.g., “unstable” → “may not hold in practice,” “cartoon representation” → “schematic diagram,” typo in ref 9).
- Overall assessment: Relevant and technically interesting with a novel direction, but needs stronger experiments, clearer guidance on relaxation parameters, and improved exposition to be practically impactful.
