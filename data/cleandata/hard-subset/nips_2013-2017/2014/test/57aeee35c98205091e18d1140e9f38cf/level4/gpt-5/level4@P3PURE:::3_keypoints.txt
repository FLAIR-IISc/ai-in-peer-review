- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute ACE bounds under possible faithfulness violations using relaxation parameters and linear programming.
- Timely and interesting idea that principledly quantifies weak unfaithful interactions with familiar mathematical tools.
- Output is conceptually hard to interpret: yields a distribution over bounds that depend on unspecified unfaithful relaxations.
- Unclear how likely the unfaithful instances are, so the practical relevance of the resulting bounds is uncertain.
- Many relaxation choices produce very wide bounds, limiting practical usefulness and actionability.
- WPP does not provide a preferred point estimate within intervals, though practitioners may expect one.
- Experimental evaluation is weak and not persuasive about WPP’s practical benefit.
- The “bias” metric used for comparison is not meaningful because competitors estimate fundamentally different quantities.
- Influenza dataset results produce very wide ACE intervals (e.g., (-0.23, 0.64)), offering little actionable conclusion.
- Method appears technically sound with detailed supplementary material, but the reviewer did not fully verify all details.
- Key methodological choices (e.g., how to select relaxation parameters) are deferred to future work, hindering assessment.
- Clarity issues: algorithmic steps and multiple parameter variants are hard to follow and may impede reproducibility.
- The paper is a novel combination of familiar techniques and related but sufficiently different from recent work.
- Significance lies in introducing new ideas/direction rather than delivering a ready-to-use practitioner tool.
- Back-substitution refinement step’s practical efficacy is questioned and needs empirical support.
- Need clearer explanation of how the witness W identifies admissible sets Z and how Z depends on relaxation parameters θ.
- Several vague or misleading claims and word-choice problems throughout (e.g., “unstable”, “gives greater control”).
- Concerns about sample-size sensitivity—bounds may remain large with realistic small datasets (e.g., 500 records).
- Table 1 and other comparisons do not allow meaningful assessment of WPP versus standard point estimates assuming faithfulness.
- Tone issues: emotive phrasing (e.g., “risky Bayesian approaches”, “more honest”) suggests biased evaluation.
- Missed or insufficiently acknowledged related work (e.g., Geiger et al. 2014) and some typos in text and references.
