This paper addresses the challenge of average causal effect (ACE) estimation between two binary variables in scenarios where the causal relationships among covariates are incompletely specified. It builds upon a covariate selection rule from Entner et al. (2013), originally justified under the faithfulness assumption, by adapting it to produce bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates, leveraging techniques similar to those in Ramsahai (2012). 

While I haven’t fully verified all technical aspects, the core concept appears sound, novel, and potentially valuable. My primary concern relates to the role of the conditions defined in the Entner et al. (2013) rule within this framework. Without relying on the faithfulness assumption, the implications of those conditions for the underlying causal structure are unclear. I suspect they might influence the selection of relaxation parameters, but the paper does not explicitly address this connection. Consequently, it seems the method’s functionality isn’t fundamentally dependent on satisfying the Entner et al. conditions, and I question whether the observed experimental robustness might be related to this independence. Clarification on this point would be greatly appreciated.

Several minor points warrant attention:

1. In Section 3, the phrasing “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…” is somewhat counterintuitive. Unfaithfulness typically introduces *additional* independence relationships, rather than violating existing ones. The framework appears to accommodate “near” violations of faithfulness by limiting the strength of dependence, rather than characterizing violations directly.
2. The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including the performance of the original Entner et al. (2013) method, utilizing their strict thresholds and treating “don’t know” responses as uninformative bounds.
3. On page 8, the explanation that a negative ACE (where X=1 represents vaccination and Y=1 indicates non-hospitalization) implies a beneficial vaccine effect is unclear and requires further justification.
4. There is an inconsistency in table referencing; “Table 1” on page 8 is cited as “Table 5” in the main text.

Overall, this is a promising paper, although the technical details require careful consideration.
