This paper proposes a new approach to derive bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) from a variable X to a variable Y. The main principles are: using Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow some degree of path cancellation, and introducing assumptions represented as inequality constraints. Under these principles, the authors formulate a linear programming scheme to compute ACE bounds. Although the algorithm for deriving the bounds has not been optimized, the resulting bounds appear narrow and accurate compared with the current state of the art.

Quality
The paper aims to establish a novel method that yields more accurate ACE bounds than existing approaches. By combining several feasible principles, the authors succeed in producing tighter bounds, which makes the paper interesting.
However, several criteria used to define the bounds are not comprehensively explained. In particular, the validity of using Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not clearly discussed. In addition, there are no general criteria for choosing appropriate relaxation parameters. Because these parameters significantly affect the resulting bounds, the principles for determining them should be further investigated and included in the paper.

Clarity
The theoretical exposition is generally comprehensive, except for the essential discussion noted above.
However, the presentation of the experimental results is not well described. The actual implementation of the algorithm is not comprehensively explained, and the definitions of the parameters shown in Table 1 (Table 5?) are not clearly given.
There are also typos and inconsistencies:
- “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1.” on page 6: there appears to be no table containing the algorithm.
- “Results are summarized in Table 5.” on page 8: there appears to be no Table 5 (perhaps this refers to Table 1).

Originality
As acknowledged by the authors, the problem setting has been studied in earlier work, so the scope of the paper is not highly original. The idea of combining the three principles in a unified framework contains some originality, but, as noted above, the validity of this combination is not clearly discussed.

Significance
The method shows significance in deriving tighter ACE bounds. However, its performance has been evaluated on a quite limited set of experiments. Its generality—both in theory and across a broader range of empirical settings—has not been strictly assessed. The paper addresses an important problem, namely evaluating bounds on the strength of causal relations, but the validity of the proposed ideas should be more thoroughly substantiated in both theory and experiments.
