This paper discusses the problem of estimating average causal effects (ACE) between two (binary) variables, where the causal structure between the variables is not fully specified. It considers a rule proposed by Antner et al. (2013), which aims to select a set of observed variables that are sufficient to block all hidden paths. However, for Antner et al., the rule is only fair under the assumption of faithfulness. This paper proposes using the rule to provide bounds instead of point estimates of ACE and creates a method following Ramaswamy (2012).

I must admit that I could not digest all the technical details, but the main idea seems understandable and appealing and useful to me. However, what puzzled me was that I did not see the role of the conditions in Antner et al.'s rule as much in this method. Without the faithfulness assumption, those conditions have no clear effect on the causal structure. My best guess is that if the conditions are verified, they are relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper clearly removes that point. I think this process is not particularly dependent on the conditions in Antner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if it has any connection with the apparent robustness of the method in the experiment. I would expect a clarification on this matter.

Some minor comments:
1. The first paragraph of section 3, "... violates faithfulness as a violation of local independence..." sounds strange. Isn't infidelity more about giving too much freedom than violating freedom? I think the proposed framework is actually intended to allow/express "almost" violation of faithfulness by limiting the degree of dependence.
2. In experiments, it might be helpful to report the effectiveness of Antner et al.'s method when using a strict threshold, where the "don't know" answer is considered an informative boundary.
3. In the second paragraph of page 8, if "X=1 means the patient has had a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient has not been hospitalized", then why does a negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine?
4. "Table 1" on page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

This is an interesting paper, although the details are not easy to follow.
