- Paper introduces WPP: an algorithm that computes ACE bounds under relaxed faithfulness using relaxation parameters and linear programming over adjusted covariate sets.  
- The problem is timely and interesting, and the approach is a principled, novel combination of familiar techniques.  
- It’s unclear whether the chosen method is the right way to capture the impact of weak unfaithful interactions.  
- The output (a distribution over bounds parameterized by relaxations) is conceptually hard to interpret and its practical relevance is unclear.  
- The likelihood and realism of the unfaithful instances considered are not assessed, making many produced bounds potentially irrelevant.  
- Many relaxations yield wide bounds that may be useless in practice, and the method does not provide a preferred point estimate within intervals.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak and unbalanced, undermining claims about practical utility.  
- The “bias” metric used to compare WPP to competitors is meaningless because the methods measure different quantities.  
- The influenza data example yields a wide ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64), offering little actionable conclusion.  
- The approach is technically sound and detailed in the supplement, but some contextual and methodological aspects are underexplained.  
- Choosing relaxation parameters is deferred to future work, leaving a crucial practical issue unresolved.  
- Paper clarity is adequate overall but the conclusion and several algorithmic/parameter explanations are hard to follow; implementation from the paper alone may be difficult.  
- The method is original enough to stimulate cross-fertilization with related recent work.  
- The paper’s significance lies in its ideas and direction, but the current implementation is unlikely ready for practitioner use.  
- Back-substitution refinement may have limited practical efficacy; empirical evidence of its effectiveness is requested.  
- Multiple admissible sets lead to multiple bounds; this should be clarified and related to the search procedure.  
- The relation between the search step and bound computation, and dependence of admissible sets Z on relaxation parameters θ, need clearer explanation.  
- Several wording, clarity, and typographical issues throughout the paper should be fixed (many specific line comments provided).  
- Some constraint parameters are poorly interpreted in the text and can be misread as indicating weak interactions; they need clearer interpretation.  
- The claim of having no previous analytical bounds overlooks closely related prior work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014).  
- Concerns about sample size: using 5000 points may not reflect typical smaller-sample (e.g., medical) settings and the impact on bound width should be examined.  
- Table 1 and related comparisons do not convincingly demonstrate benefit of WPP over standard faithfulness-based point estimates.  
- Statements implying WPP is “stable” or a “compromise” are misleading—stability may reflect overly loose bounds, and WPP addresses a different aspect rather than serving as a compromise.  
- Important interpretive warning: WPP bounds are not confidence intervals around a best-guess estimate and can be misread by casual readers.  
- Some evaluative language (e.g., “risky Bayesian approaches”) reads emotive or biased and should be neutralized.  
- Minor remarks: the algorithm name is good, and a typo exists in reference 9 (“inuenza”).
