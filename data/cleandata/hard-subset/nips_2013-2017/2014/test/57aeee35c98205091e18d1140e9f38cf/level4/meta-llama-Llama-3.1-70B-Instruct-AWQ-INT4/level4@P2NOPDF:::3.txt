The paper presents the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a novel approach to computing bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) that accounts for potential violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. By introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and employing a linear programming framework, the algorithm solves for upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusted for a set of covariates and subject to these constraints.

The problem addressed in the paper is timely and relevant, and the approach is principled and interesting. However, whether the authors have found the right method to capture the impact of unfaithful interaction patterns remains to be seen. The output of the algorithm is challenging to interpret conceptually, as it provides a distribution over bounds that are a function of 'unfaithful relaxations' relative to a given model. The likelihood and relevance of these unfaithful instances, and consequently the bounds themselves, are unclear. Furthermore, many relaxations lead to wide bounds, making them less useful. The method does not commit to a preferred point estimate within the interval, which may lead to misinterpretation.

The experimental evaluation is poor, with the 'bias' evaluation metric being meaningless for comparing the WPP method with its competitors, as they measure different things. The practical performance on the influenza data set is also weak, with the ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64) implying limited relevant conclusions.

Quality:
The approach is technically sound, with many details provided in the supplementary file. However, important aspects, such as choosing the relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article, making it difficult to assess potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough.

Clarity:
The paper is decently written, except for the conclusion. The algorithmic steps and modifications are hard to follow due to a lack of explanation of certain details. While the supplement may be sufficient, it is unclear whether a reader could fully implement the method from the provided description.

Originality:
The method appears to be a novel combination of familiar techniques, closely related to but sufficiently different from recent work on the same problem. This may stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of approaches on the subject.

Significance:
The paper's significance lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate other techniques to solve this challenging problem. However, in its current implementation, it is unlikely to be the finished article for practitioners, although some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm may play a crucial role in the future.

Summary:
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem with an interesting method that will undoubtedly find an audience. However, the current application is limited, and the relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear. The experimental evaluation is poor, and the paper could be improved with more robust and balanced evaluation metrics.

Other comments:

* The name "Witness Protection Program" is catchy and memorable.
* The term "unstable" is misused; the interactions are stable but may not hold true.
* The "cartoon representation" is unclear and could be improved.
* The assumption of causal sufficiency or knowledge W -> X is not explicitly stated.
* The witness W is not needed in the computation of the ACE but is used to identify the set Z.
* The phrase "gives greater control over violations of faithfulness" is too vague and could be clarified.
* The transition in level of abstraction in Section 4 is sudden and may be challenging for less familiar readers.
* The relation between the search part and the bounds computation could be briefly described.
* The identification of admissible sets Z for W depends on the relaxation parameters theta, which could be explained.
* The sentence "Background ... used here" is unclear and could be rephrased.
* The phrase "max. 10 free parameters" refers to the entire set Z, not a set of 10 variables.
* The statement "We know of no previous analytical bounds" is closely related to recent papers, such as "Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding" (Geiger, Janzing, Scholkopf, UAI2014).
* The supplementary material has not been checked in detail.
* The practical efficacy of the back-substitution process in refining bounds is questionable and could be clarified.
* The comparison based on the bias metric is meaningless, and the subsequent trivial optimal example demonstrates this.
* The interval (-1, 1) still covers a wide range of possible values, implying limited relevant conclusions.
* The impact of the size of the bounds for smaller data sets, such as 500 records, could be a concern.
* The benefit of using WPP over the standard point estimate using faithfulness is unclear from Table 1.
* The statement "WPP is quite stable" may be misleading, as the bounds derived by WPP are loose and do not depend on the solvability of the problem.
* The interpretation of the bounds as a confidence interval around the best guess value is a crucial statement that highlights the difficulty in using the WPP results in practice.
* The language used in the conclusion is emotive and suggests a biased evaluation and/or erroneous interpretation of results.
* The statement "WPP is not a compromise but focuses on an additional aspect of the overall inference problem" could be rephrased for clarity.
* The phrase "and purely theory-driven analyses that refuse to look at competing models" is unclear and could be rephrased or removed.
