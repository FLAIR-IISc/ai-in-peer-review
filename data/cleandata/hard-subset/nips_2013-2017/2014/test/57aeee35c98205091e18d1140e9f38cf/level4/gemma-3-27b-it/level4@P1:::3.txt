The paper describes the so-called Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute bounds on an inferred average causal effect (ACE) that takes possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference into account. 
It does so by introducing a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, and then uses a linear programming framework to solve for the resulting upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link by adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates, subject to these constraints.
This problem currently receives a fair amount of attention, so the paper is both timely and interesting. I like the approach and ideas introduced in the paper: it is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of (weak) unfaithful interaction patterns, in combination with well-known mathematical tools to solve the corresponding maximization/minimization problems.
Whether or not the method provides a practically useful way to capture this impact remains an open question. The resulting output – a distribution over bounds reflecting the degree of ‘unfaithful relaxation’ from a given model – is conceptually challenging to interpret.  The width of these bounds can be substantial, potentially diminishing their usefulness. Furthermore, the method does not commit to a preferred point estimate within the interval, despite the likely tendency of users to interpret it as such. 
Experimental evaluation is limited. The ‘bias’ evaluation metric is not well-suited for comparing WPP to its competitors, as they measure fundamentally different quantities. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output represents a valuable addition for practitioners or simply a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza data set is also weak; an ACE interval of (-0.23, 0.64) offers limited actionable insight, though this may ultimately be the most honest assessment.
Quality
The approach is technically sound, with many details explicated in the supplementary material (which I have not examined in detail). The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context can be lost at times. Important aspects, such as choosing the relaxation parameters, are deferred to future work, which makes it difficult to fully assess potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness. 
Clarity
The paper is generally well-written (with the exception of the conclusion – see comments below). The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving numerous parameters were challenging to follow, partially due to a lack of explanation for certain details. (I believe I understand the overall process, but am unsure if I could fully implement it based solely on the description; the supplement may be sufficient.)
Originality
As far as I can tell, the method represents a novel combination of established techniques. It is sufficiently different from recent work on the same problem to potentially stimulate fruitful cross-fertilization of approaches.
Significance
The significance of the paper lies primarily in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate various techniques to address this challenging area. In its current form, it seems unlikely to become a finished product for practitioners, though some of the ideas employed in the WPP algorithm could well play an important role in future developments. 
Summary:
Relevant and highly topical problem. Interesting method that will likely find an audience. Current application is limited, but has potential to extend to more general cases. Reasonably well written, though could be improved. Technically interesting solutions. Experimental evaluation is insufficient. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
