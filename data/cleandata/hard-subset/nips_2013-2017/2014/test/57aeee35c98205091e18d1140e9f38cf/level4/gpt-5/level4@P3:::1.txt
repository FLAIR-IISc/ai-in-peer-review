Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in observational settings when the causal graph over measured covariates is unknown and unmeasured confounding may be present. Building on Entner et al. (AISTATS 2013), who use faithfulness-based conditional independencies to identify admissible adjustment sets yielding point estimates, the authors invert that logic: they use pairs (W, Z) discovered via Entner’s Rule 1 not to adjust, but to construct instrumental-variable-like bounds on ACE, conditional on Z. They relax faithfulness by allowing bounded deviations from the conditional independencies suggested by Rule 1 via parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) that control departures from zero direct effects and from independence between W and latent U.

Methodologically, they adapt and extend linear-programming bounds for binary IV models (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai) to include these bounded violations, yielding a feasible set in terms of η, δ, and κ-type parameters, and then optimize ACE over that set. They provide two computational routes: (i) a Bayesian approach that places constrained Dirichlet priors on observed contingency tables P(Y, X, W | Z = z) and propagates uncertainty to posterior intervals for ACE via rejection sampling of the inequalities; and (ii) a fast “back-substitution” algorithm that derives algebraic (symbolic) bounds as functions of the relaxation parameters and applies message-passing-like tightening to produce box constraints, which approximate the linear-program solution. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza reminder-letter dataset suggest that, for reasonable relaxations, WPP can produce narrower bounds than standard IV while being more robust than faithfulness-based point estimation.

Strengths
- Novel compromise between faithfulness-based point identification and IV-based worst-case bounds. Using conditional independencies to generate candidate (W, Z) and then bounding ACE with controlled violations is original and well-motivated.
- General framework that includes standard IV and chain models as limiting cases; the relaxation parameters offer interpretable knobs for sensitivity analysis.
- Incorporation of uncertainty via constrained Dirichlet posteriors avoids overconfidence from plug-in tests of inequality constraints.
- The algebraic bounds and back-substitution provide useful intuition and practical speedups.
- Empirical results show stability when faithfulness is only approximately satisfied and improvements over naive adjustments commonly used in practice.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Role of Rule 1 conditions: The paper leverages (W ⟂ Y | Z ∪ {X}) and (W ⧧⟂ Y | Z) to nominate (W, Z), but, once faithfulness is relaxed, it is unclear what precise graphical content these conditions guarantee. Are they used only as heuristics to choose candidates, or do they formally justify particular ranges for the relaxation parameters (e.g., y, x, w, β, β̄)? The paper defers parameter selection, leaving a conceptual gap. Please clarify the logical pathway from Rule 1 to non-vacuous bounds under relaxation, and whether the empirical robustness stems from the bounding machinery rather than from Rule 1 per se.
- Sensitivity to relaxation choices: Although sensitivity is mentioned, the main text should provide clearer guidance and diagnostics (e.g., coverage-width curves as a function of y, x, w; how β, β̄ practically tighten/loosen bounds).
- Bayesian computation: Rejection sampling over constrained Dirichlet posteriors may scale poorly as constraints tighten or |Z| grows. Provide acceptance rates, runtime, and a discussion of alternatives (e.g., hit-and-run, elliptical slice sampling under linear constraints).
- Multiple (W, Z) outputs: The method returns several posterior distributions over bounds. The paper could better specify how to aggregate or select among them, and how often the falsification step discards candidates.
- Experimental comparisons: Baselines are limited. Including Entner et al.’s estimator with their stringent thresholds, interpreting “don’t know” as returning uninformative bounds, would strengthen the empirical case. Also report coverage of the true ACE and average interval widths alongside the “error” metric to make interval vs point comparisons more interpretable.
- Clarity and presentation: Several notational shifts (ζ, κ, ω) and footnotes hinder readability, and some references/tables appear misnumbered (see minor comments). A schematic figure summarizing the pipeline (search for (W, Z) → relaxation → LP/back-substitution → Bayesian propagation) would help.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound and connects to established IV bounds via a principled relaxation. Some derivations are deferred and the Bayesian inference section would benefit from more algorithmic detail and diagnostics. Experiments are promising but limited in scope.
- Clarity: The paper is dense and at times difficult to follow. Tightening notation, fixing cross-references, and expanding the discussion on the role of Rule 1 and parameter selection would improve clarity substantially.
- Originality: High. The inversion of Rule 1 for bounding and the parameterized relaxation framework, together with algebraic bounds and back-substitution, are novel contributions.
- Significance: Potentially significant. If adopted, WPP could become a standard complement to back-door adjustment and standard IV, especially in large observational studies where faithfulness is approximate and instruments are weak.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Innovative framework bridging two important causal paradigms.
- Clear practical relevance; provides honest uncertainty quantification via bounds.
- Useful computational contributions (symbolic bounds, back-substitution).

Cons:
- Insufficient clarity on the formal role of Rule 1 under relaxation and on selecting relaxation parameters.
- Limited empirical baselines and diagnostics; scalability and acceptance rates under the Bayesian scheme not fully addressed.
- Presentation/notation issues that impede readability.

Minor comments
- Section 3 phrasing: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing. Consider rephrasing to “allow bounded departures from the conditional independencies implied by faithfulness (i.e., constrain the magnitude of dependence).”
- Please report Entner et al.’s method with their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as uninformative bounds (e.g., [−1, 1]).
- Influenza example: If X = 1 denotes vaccination and Y = 1 denotes hospitalization, then a negative ACE indicates reduced hospitalization and thus a beneficial vaccine. If instead Y = 1 denotes not hospitalized, adjust the sign discussion accordingly.
- Table/figure numbering: “Table 1” on p. 8 is referred to as “Table 5” in the text; please correct.
- Notation and reproducibility: Consolidate ζ, κ, ω notation early; give explicit defaults for (w, x, y, β, β̄) and report sensitivity; provide acceptance rates for the constrained-Dirichlet rejection sampler; specify independence test priors/thresholds used in the (W, Z) search; ensure the Polymake URL is correct.
