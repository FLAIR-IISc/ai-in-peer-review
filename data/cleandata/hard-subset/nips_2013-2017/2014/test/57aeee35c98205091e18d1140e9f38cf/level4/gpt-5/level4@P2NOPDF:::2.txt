This paper presents a new method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of X on Y. The approach (i) applies Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction, (ii) relaxes the faithfulness assumption to allow a controlled degree of path cancellation, and (iii) introduces additional assumptions encoded as inequality constraints. Under these principles, the authors formulate a linear programming scheme to compute ACE bounds. Although the algorithm has not been fully optimized, the reported intervals appear notably tight and competitive with the current state of the art.

Quality
The paper aims to produce tighter ACE bounds than existing methods, and the proposed combination of ideas is promising. The results suggest that the approach can yield substantially narrower bounds, making the contribution interesting.

However, several key elements are insufficiently justified or explained:
- The validity of using Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction is not clearly established. To use the rule this way, the paper appears to require the specific structural assumptions illustrated in Fig. 2. These assumptions should be explicitly stated, their necessity discussed, and their implications analyzed.
- The paper does not provide general criteria for selecting the relaxation parameters that modulate the degree of path cancellation and constraint strength. Because these parameters can materially affect the tightness and correctness of the bounds, principled guidance (e.g., identifiability conditions, sensitivity analyses, or data-driven heuristics) is needed.

Clarity
The theoretical exposition is largely clear, aside from the essential issues noted above. In contrast, the experimental description and implementation details are under-specified:
- The algorithmic steps are not described with sufficient precision to enable replication. In particular, the mapping from assumptions to linear constraints and the handling of parameter relaxations need clearer, self-contained descriptions.
- The definitions of the parameters listed in Table 1 (possibly misreferenced as Table 5) are unclear, and their roles in the linear program should be stated explicitly.

There are also inconsistencies and possible typos in the manuscript:
- “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1.” (p. 6): No algorithm table is present.
- “Results are summarized in Table 5.” (p. 8): Table 5 is missing; this may refer to Table 1.

Careful proofreading and consistent cross-referencing are required.

Originality
While the problem of bounding ACE has been addressed in prior work, the paper’s attempt to unify three ideas—reverse application of Entner’s Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness with partial path cancellation, and inequality-based assumptions within a linear program—has some originality. That said, the novelty is tempered by the lack of a clear, rigorous justification for combining these components, particularly the reverse use of Entner’s Rule 1 and the principled selection of relaxation parameters.

Significance
The potential to produce tighter ACE bounds is significant for causal inference, especially when point identification is impossible. The preliminary results are encouraging. However, the empirical evaluation is limited in scope, and the generality of the method—both theoretically (e.g., conditions under which the bounds remain valid) and empirically (e.g., across diverse graph structures, sample sizes, noise levels, and degrees of path cancellation)—has not been thoroughly assessed. Stronger theoretical guarantees and broader experiments would considerably strengthen the paper’s impact.

In summary, this is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution that demonstrates promising empirical tightness of ACE bounds via a linear programming framework. To be suitable for publication, the paper should: (1) explicitly justify the reverse use of Entner’s Rule 1 and the structural assumptions it entails (e.g., those in Fig. 2); (2) provide principled guidance for setting relaxation parameters and analyze sensitivity; (3) clarify algorithmic and implementation details sufficient for replication; and (4) correct table references and improve the clarity of experimental reporting.
