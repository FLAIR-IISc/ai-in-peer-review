The paper presents the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a novel approach to estimating bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) while accounting for potential violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. By introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions, the algorithm utilizes a linear programming framework to derive upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusted for a suitable set of covariates. This problem has garnered significant attention, making the paper both timely and interesting.

I appreciate the approach and ideas presented in the paper, which represent a principled attempt to quantify the impact of weak unfaithful interaction patterns using established mathematical tools. However, it remains to be seen whether this method accurately captures this impact. The resulting output, a distribution over bounds as a function of 'unfaithful relaxations' relative to a given model, is challenging to interpret conceptually. The likelihood and relevance of these unfaithful instances, and consequently the bounds themselves, are unclear. Many relaxations lead to wide bounds, rendering them less useful. The method does not provide a preferred point estimate within the interval, although users may interpret it as such.

The experimental evaluation is subpar: the 'bias' evaluation metric is meaningless when comparing the WPP method to its competitors, as they measure different aspects. Consequently, it is uncertain whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza dataset is concerning, with ACE bounds of (-0.23, 0.64) implying limited relevant conclusions.

Quality
The approach presented in the paper is technically sound, with many details explained in the supplementary file (which I did not thoroughly review). The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as choosing relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article, making it difficult to assess potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness.

Clarity
The paper is decently written (except for the conclusion). The algorithmic steps and subsequent modifications involving subtly different parameters were challenging to follow, partly due to the lack of explanation of certain method details. While I think I understand the method, I am not confident I could fully implement it based solely on this description; the supplementary material may be sufficient.

Originality
As far as I can tell, the method represents a novel combination of familiar techniques, closely related to but sufficiently different from recent work on the same problem. This may stimulate fruitful cross-fertilization of approaches on the subject.

Significance
The paper's significance lies primarily in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate other techniques to solve this challenging problem. In its current implementation, it seems unlikely to be the finished article for practitioners, although some ideas employed in the WPP algorithm may play an essential role in the future.

Summary:
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem, presenting an interesting method that will likely find an audience. However, the current application is limited, and the relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear. The paper is reasonably well-written, with technically interesting solutions, but the experimental evaluation is poor.

Other comments:

* The name "Witness Protection Program" is catchy and fitting for the algorithm.
* The term "unstable" is misused; the interactions are stable but may not hold true.
* The "cartoon representation" is unclear.
* The assumption of causal sufficiency or knowledge W -> X is technically implicit.
* The role of witness W in identifying set Z should be explained.
* The phrase "gives greater control over violations of faithfulness" is too vague.
* The transition in level of abstraction in section 4 is abrupt and challenging to interpret.
* The relation between the search part and bounds computation should be briefly described.
* The identification of admissible sets Z for W depends on the relaxation parameters theta, which should be explained.
* The sentence "Background ... used here" is unclear and lacks purpose.
* The phrase "max. 10 free parameters" refers to the entire set Z, not a set of 10 variables.
* The claim "We know of no previous analytical bounds" is closely related to recent papers, such as "Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding" (Geiger, Janzing, Scholkopf, UAI2014).
* The supplementary material was not thoroughly reviewed.
* The practical efficacy of the back-substitution process in refining bounds is doubted.
* The comparison based on the bias metric is meaningless.
* The bounds derived by WPP are so loose that they don't depend on whether the problem is solvable or not.
* The statement "WPP is quite stable" is misleading.
* The interpretation of the WPP results is crucial, as many readers may misinterpret the bounds as a confidence interval around the best guess value.
* The language used in the conclusion, such as "risky Bayesian approaches" and "WPP bounds keep inference more honest," is emotive and suggests a biased evaluation.
* The WPP is not a compromise but rather focuses on an additional aspect of the overall inference problem.
* The statement "and purely theory-driven analyses that refuse to look at competing models" is unclear and sounds unduly dismissive.
* There is a typo in reference 9: "inuenza".
