This paper introduces a new method to derive bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) from a variable X to a variable Y. The core ideas are: using Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow a controlled degree of path cancellation, and incorporating assumptions expressed as inequality constraints. Under these principles, the authors formulate a linear programming scheme to compute ACE bounds. Although the algorithm has not yet been optimized, the resulting bounds appear quite tight and accurate relative to state-of-the-art methods.

Quality
The authors aim to produce more precise ACE bounds than existing approaches and succeed by combining several plausible principles, resulting in tighter intervals. In this sense, the paper is compelling.
However, some of the criteria used to define the bounds are not thoroughly justified. In particular, the soundness of applying Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction is insufficiently discussed. Moreover, there is no general guidance for selecting the relaxation parameters. Because these choices materially influence the resulting bounds, the rationale and procedure for determining them should be further developed and documented.

Clarity
The theoretical exposition is largely clear, aside from the critical discussion noted above.
By contrast, the presentation of the experiments is under-specified. As mentioned, the actual algorithmic implementation is not described in sufficient detail, and the parameters in Table 1 (Table 5?) are not clearly defined.
I also noticed several issues with the text, for example:
- “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1.” on page 6 => There is no algorithm table present.
- “Results are summarized in Table 5.” on page 8 => Table 5 is missing (possibly meant Table 1).

Originality
While the problem has been studied in prior work, as acknowledged by the authors, the paper’s overall scope is not highly original.
The unification of the three ideas carries some novelty, but, as noted, the justification for their combined use is not sufficiently clarified.

Significance
The method shows promise in yielding tighter ACE bounds, which is significant. However, the evaluation is based on relatively limited experiments. Its generality—both theoretically and empirically—has not been rigorously established. The paper addresses an important task, namely bounding the strength of causal relationships, but the validity of the proposed approach needs stronger support in both theory and experimentation.
