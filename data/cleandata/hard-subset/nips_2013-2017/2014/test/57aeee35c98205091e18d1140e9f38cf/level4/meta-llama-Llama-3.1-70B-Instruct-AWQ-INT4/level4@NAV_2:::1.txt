This paper is concerned with the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two (binary) variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully given. It considers a rule proposed in Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates that is sufficient to block all back-door paths. For Entner et al.'s purpose, however, the rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. This paper proposes to use the rule to provide bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, and develops a method following Ramsahai (2012). 

The main idea makes sense and seems interesting and useful, but some aspects require clarification. The role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach is not entirely clear. Without the faithfulness assumption, those conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. It is possible that the conditions, if verified, are relevant to the choice of the relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly puts that matter aside. The machinery does not seem to crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and it is unclear whether the apparent robustness of the method in the experiments has anything to do with this. Further clarification on this issue would be appreciated.

In terms of technical soundness, the paper appears to be well-executed, but some minor points require attention. The first paragraph of Section 3 contains a statement that sounds strange: "… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …" Unfaithfulness is typically a matter of allowing extra independence rather than violating independence. It seems that the proposed framework is actually intended to allow/express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

The experiments could be improved by also reporting the performance of Entner et al.'s method when their stringent thresholds are used, taking the answer of "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds. Additionally, in the second paragraph on p. 8, there appears to be a mistake: if "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized", why does a negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine? Finally, "Table 1" on top of p. 8 is cited as "Table 5" in the text.

Overall, the paper is interesting, but the details are not easy to follow. Addressing the above points would improve the clarity and technical soundness of the paper.

Strengths: 
- The paper proposes a novel approach to estimating the ACE by providing bounds rather than point estimates.
- The method is developed following Ramsahai (2012) and appears to be well-executed.
- The experiments demonstrate the apparent robustness of the method.

Weaknesses: 
- The role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach is not entirely clear.
- The paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule are used in the method.
- Some minor points require attention, such as the statement in the first paragraph of Section 3 and the mistake in the second paragraph on p. 8.

Originality: 
- The paper proposes a novel approach to estimating the ACE by providing bounds rather than point estimates.
- The method is developed following Ramsahai (2012), but the application to the problem at hand is new.

- The proposed method has the potential to be widely applicable in various fields where causal inference is necessary.
