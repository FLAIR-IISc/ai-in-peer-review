- Introduces WPP: an algorithm computing ACE bounds that models faithfulness violations via relaxation parameters and uses linear programming for upper/lower bounds.
- Addresses a timely problem and offers a principled, appealing way to quantify unfaithfulness effects by combining causal inference with optimization.
- Main practical concern: results’ interpretability is limited because bounds depend on chosen relaxations and the plausibility of those unfaithful scenarios is not assessed.
- Many relaxation settings produce extremely wide bounds, reducing practical utility.
- Method avoids providing a point estimate, risking users misinterpreting bounds as confidence intervals.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: the “bias” metric used for comparisons measures different quantities and is fundamentally flawed.
- Influenza dataset result (ACE interval -0.23 to 0.64) is very wide and offers little actionable insight, though it may reflect the data.
- Technical foundation appears sound and supplementary material contains technical details (not fully vetted by reviewer).
- Crucial shortcoming: methodology for selecting relaxation parameters is deferred to future work, preventing full assessment.
- Experimental evaluation lacks robustness, balance, and sufficient comparisons to demonstrate practical benefit.
- Paper is generally well-written, but several methodological steps and parameter explanations are unclear, making reproduction difficult without the supplement.
- The approach is original—a novel combination of established techniques building on recent work.
- Significance is primarily conceptual; unlikely to be a standalone practitioner tool in current form but could influence future work.
- Overall summary: interesting and topical method with limited current practical application and inadequate experimental validation.
- Praise: “Witness Protection Program” is a memorable, effective name.
- Wording suggestions: replace “unstable” with “do not necessarily hold,” fix typos (e.g., “an useful” → “a useful”), and clarify ambiguous phrasing throughout.
- Clarify “cartoon representation” and several vague sentences that lose broader context.
- Explicitly acknowledge implicit assumptions, e.g., causal sufficiency or knowledge of W – X.
- Explain that witness W is used only to identify covariate set Z and not directly in ACE computation.
- Specify how the method “gives greater control over violations of faithfulness” instead of vague claims.
- Replace “might” with “can” in places where stronger wording is appropriate.
- Clarify conditional vs. unconditional statements where W always depends on U | X.
- Provide more intuition and interpretation for the relaxation/constraint parameters; current exposition is abstract.
- Link the parameter concepts to their original introduction in cited prior work.
- Explain the relationship between the search process and bounds computation, and how admissible Z sets depend on relaxation parameters (theta).
- Clarify an unclear sentence about “Background … used here” and confirm that the limit of 10 parameters refers to the entire Z set, not 10 individual variables.
- Correct an incorrect claim of novelty by citing related work (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf UAI 2014).
- Reviewer hasn’t fully examined the supplementary material—results depend on that unseen content.
- Question the practical efficacy of the back-substitution process for refining bounds and request an empirical assessment in Section 5.
- Note that multiple bounds arise from different admissible Z sets for different witnesses and clarify this.
- Emphasize that the bias metric comparison is not straightforward and can be misleading.
- Consider the effect of smaller sample sizes (e.g., 500 records) on bound width, as many studies have limited data.
- Table 1 lacks sufficient information to assess WPP’s benefit over standard faithfulness assumptions.
- Claim that “WPP is quite stable” may instead indicate bounds are loose and insensitive; clarify intent.
- Strongly emphasize that derived bounds are not confidence intervals to avoid common misinterpretation.
- Avoid emotive or dismissive language (e.g., “risky Bayesian approaches,” “keeps inference more honest”) and vague claims about being a “compromise.”
- Fix typographical errors in references (e.g., “inuenza” → “influenza”).
