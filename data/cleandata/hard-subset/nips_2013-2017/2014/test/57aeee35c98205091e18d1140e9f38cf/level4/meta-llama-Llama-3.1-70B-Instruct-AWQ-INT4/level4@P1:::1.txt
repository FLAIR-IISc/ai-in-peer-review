This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over the covariates is not fully given. The authors build upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, this rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. The authors modify this rule to provide bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, leveraging a method developed by Ramsahai (2012).

The main idea of the paper is to use the rule to generate candidate instrumental variables and then apply a linear programming approach to obtain bounds on the ACE. The authors introduce a set of assumptions to relax the faithfulness condition, allowing for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. These assumptions enable the estimation of a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments with synthetic and real data. They compare their method against naı̈ve point estimators and the point estimator of Entner et al. (2013), showing that their approach provides more stable and robust results. The authors also apply their method to a real-world problem, estimating the effect of influenza vaccination on hospitalization, and obtain a more precise interval estimate compared to the standard IV model.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference, providing a novel approach that complements existing methods. The authors' use of a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference to estimate bounds on the ACE is a significant improvement over traditional methods that rely on point estimates. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology and results.

One minor comment is that the authors could provide more insight into the choice of relaxation parameters and how they affect the results. Additionally, the authors may want to consider including more details on the computational complexity of their approach and how it compares to existing methods.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The authors' approach has the potential to be widely applicable in various fields where causal inference is necessary.
