This paper studies estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal relations among covariates are not fully specified. It builds on Entner et al. (2013), who propose a rule for selecting an admissible set that blocks all back-door paths and, under faithfulness, yields a point estimate. Here, the authors repurpose that rule to produce bounds rather than a point estimate of the ACE and develop a linear-programming-based approach along the lines of Ramsahai (2012).

While I could not verify every technical detail due to the density of the material, the central idea appears sound, interesting, and potentially useful. One aspect that remains unclear is the precise role played by Entner et al.’s rule in this framework. Without invoking faithfulness, the rule’s conditions do not have obvious structural implications. My impression is that these conditions, when approximately met, might guide the choice or calibration of the relaxation parameters, yet the paper explicitly sets parameter selection aside. As a result, the procedure seems not to rely critically on the rule’s conditions, which raises the question of whether the method’s apparent robustness in the experiments is largely independent of them. Clarification on how the rule informs the relaxed constraints (beyond suggesting candidate (W, Z) pairs) would be helpful.

Some minor comments:
1. Section 3, first paragraph: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” reads oddly. Unfaithfulness typically corresponds to additional conditional independences not entailed by the graph, not to the violation of an independence. The proposed framework seems instead to constrain the magnitude of dependence, thereby permitting “near-violations” of faithfulness (i.e., small deviations from the independences suggested by the rule). Consider rephrasing.
2. In the experiments, it would be informative to also report Entner et al.’s method with their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” outcomes as yielding uninformative bounds, to better contrast identification via faithfulness-based screening versus the proposed relaxed-bounds approach.
3. Page 8, second paragraph: please reconcile the outcome coding with the sign of the ACE. If X=1 denotes vaccination and Y=1 denotes not hospitalized, then a negative ACE would not imply a beneficial vaccine; if Y=1 denotes hospitalized, then a negative ACE would. The text seems inconsistent on this point.
4. The “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is referenced as “Table 5” in the text.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution, though several technical sections are hard to follow.
