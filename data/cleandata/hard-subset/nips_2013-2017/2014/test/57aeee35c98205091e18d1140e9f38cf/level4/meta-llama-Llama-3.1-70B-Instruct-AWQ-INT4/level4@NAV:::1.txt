This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure among covariates is not fully specified. It builds upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013), which aims to identify a set of observed covariates that can block all back-door paths. Although the rule is originally justified under the faithfulness assumption, this paper proposes using it to derive bounds for the ACE, rather than point estimates, by employing a method inspired by Ramsahai (2012).

The core idea of the paper is clear and seems both interesting and useful, although I have not fully grasped all the technical details. However, I am puzzled by the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule within this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, the implications of these conditions for the causal structure are not immediately apparent. My interpretation is that the conditions, if verified, might be relevant to choosing the relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this issue aside. It appears that the methodology does not fundamentally rely on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the observed robustness of the method in the experiments is related to this. I would appreciate clarification on this point.

Some minor comments are as follows:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" seems unusual. Isn't unfaithfulness about allowing additional independence rather than violating independence? It appears that the proposed framework is actually designed to allow or express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the experiments, it would be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when using their stringent thresholds, treating the answer "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds.

3. In the second paragraph on page 8, if "X=1" indicates that the patient received a flu shot and "Y=1" indicates that the patient was not hospitalized, why does a negative ACE suggest a desirable vaccine?

4. The table on top of page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text, but it is actually labeled as "Table 1".

Overall, this is an interesting paper, although the details can be challenging to follow.
