

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel algorithm for computing bounds on average causal effects (ACE) under potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The approach uses relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and employs linear programming to derive ACE bounds, adjusting for covariates subject to these constraints. The problem is highly relevant, and the method represents a principled attempt to quantify the impact of weak unfaithful interactions using established mathematical tools.  

**Strengths:** The technical framework is sound, with sufficient detail provided in the supplementary material. The core idea—explicitly modeling unfaithfulness through relaxations—offers a fresh perspective on a challenging problem. The algorithmic structure is well-motivated, and the connection to existing work (e.g., bounding confounding) is appropriately contextualized. The "Witness Protection Program" moniker is apt and memorable.  

**Critical Concerns:**  
1. **Interpretability of Results:** The output—a distribution over bounds parameterized by relaxation values—lacks clear conceptual grounding. The paper fails to address *how likely* specific relaxation scenarios are, rendering the bounds practically uninterpretable. For instance, excessive relaxation often yields vacuous intervals (e.g., ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) for influenza data, which spans from strong negative to strong positive effects, implying no actionable insight). Crucially, the method does not commit to a point estimate, yet practitioners may misinterpret the bounds as confidence intervals around a single value—a critical ambiguity.  

2. **Flawed Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation metric ("bias") is fundamentally incommensurate across methods. WPP bounds are compared against point estimates from faithfulness-based approaches, but these measure entirely different quantities (e.g., interval coverage vs. point error). This invalidates comparisons, leaving the practical value of WPP unclear. The influenza analysis exemplifies the issue: the reported bounds are so wide as to be uninformative, yet the paper claims "stability" (p. 8), which likely reflects bound looseness rather than robustness.  

3. **Technical Gaps and Clarity:**  
   - Relaxation parameters (e.g., θ) are under-specified; their selection and impact on bound quality lack justification (e.g., why 10 parameters? p. 5, 259).  
   - Key steps (e.g., witness W’s role in identifying admissible sets Z, p. 3, 127; the "back-substitution" refinement in Section 5) are poorly explained, hindering reproducibility.  
   - The transition to high-level abstractions (p. 4, 172–180) lacks intuitive scaffolding, confusing readers unfamiliar with the subject.  
   - Critical clarifications are missing: e.g., "unstable" (p. 1, 47) should be "unfaithful"; "cartoon representation" (p. 2, 74) is vague; constraints are mischaracterized as "weak interactions" (p. 4, 185–189), not relaxation parameters.  
   - The claim "no previous analytical bounds" (p. 6, 304) overlooks closely related work (Geiger et al., UAI 2014).  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper tackles a significant problem with a technically interesting contribution. However, its current form suffers from three major limitations: (1) the output lacks practical interpretability due to unaddressed likelihood of relaxation scenarios; (2) the experimental evaluation is methodologically flawed; and (3) critical implementation details are omitted or unclear. While the *ideas* could inspire future work (e.g., refining bound computation under structured unfaithfulness), the *current implementation* does not deliver actionable insights for practitioners. The influenza results—where bounds encompass near-zero ACE—highlight the gap between theoretical novelty and real-world utility.  

**Recommendations for Revision:**  
- **Clarify the relaxation framework:** Explicitly justify parameter choices and quantify the plausibility of relaxation scenarios (e.g., via sensitivity analysis or prior distributions).  
- **Revise evaluation:** Compare methods on *interpretable* metrics (e.g., bound tightness under known unfaithfulness patterns, not "bias").  
- **Improve exposition:** Add intuitive explanations for abstract steps (e.g., "Why do relaxation parameters affect admissible sets Z?"), clarify W’s role (p. 3, 127), and fix all noted technical errors (e.g., "an useful" → "a useful").  
- **Reframe significance:** Emphasize the method’s *potential* for future extensions rather than current practicality.  

**Final Verdict:**  
The paper is conceptually novel and technically sound but currently limited in practical value due to interpretability and evaluation flaws. It would be a strong contribution if the authors address the gaps above. As-is, it risks being perceived as a theoretical curiosity rather than a useful tool. **Recommendation: Reject (with strong encouragement for resubmission after major revisions).**
