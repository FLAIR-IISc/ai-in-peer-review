

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel framework for estimating bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) that explicitly accounts for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The approach relaxes the standard faithfulness condition by introducing bounded constraints on "path cancellations," allowing the derivation of posterior distributions over ACE bounds via linear programming and Bayesian inference. WPP identifies candidate instrumental variables (witnesses) through a modified version of Entner et al.'s Rule 1, then computes conservative bounds by incorporating relaxation parameters that quantify deviations from faithfulness. The method is theoretically grounded, with analytical bounds derived in the supplementary material and a fast back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency.

**Strengths**  
The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a critical limitation in causal inference: the fragility of faithfulness-based methods under unfaithful distributions. The WPP framework provides a principled, quantifiable way to handle unfaithfulness through interpretable relaxation parameters (e.g., *w*, *x*, *y*, *β*, *β̄*), moving beyond ad hoc point estimates. The integration of Bayesian inference for uncertainty propagation over bounds (rather than point estimates) is conceptually compelling and aligns with a more honest reporting of causal effects. The analytical bounds and back-substitution algorithm offer valuable computational insights, and the method’s flexibility—allowing users to trade off bound width against assumptions—represents a meaningful advance. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the influenza vaccination case study demonstrates practical applicability despite the method’s current limitations.

**Weaknesses**  
The primary weakness lies in the *interpretability* and *practical utility* of the output. WPP produces a posterior distribution over bounds (e.g., [-0.10, 0.17] for the influenza data), but the review fails to clarify how practitioners should act on these intervals. Crucially, the bounds are not confidence intervals around a point estimate; the paper correctly notes that "the posterior distribution is over bounds, not over points," yet this distinction is not emphasized enough to prevent misinterpretation. The evaluation metric (Euclidean distance to the true ACE) is problematic: it treats interval estimates (WPP) and point estimates (e.g., Entner’s method) as directly comparable, which is misleading. For instance, WPP’s "error" is zero if the true ACE lies within its interval—a trivial property that does not reflect the method’s practical value. The synthetic experiments show WPP’s bounds are "stable" even in unsolvable cases (where no witness exists), but this stability stems from overly wide intervals (e.g., [-1, 1]), which offer little actionable insight. The influenza case study’s WPP bounds [-0.10, 0.17] are narrower than the standard IV interval [-0.23, 0.64], but the paper does not sufficiently justify why this is meaningful given the inherent uncertainty. The experimental section lacks analysis of how bound width scales with data size (e.g., 500 vs. 5000 samples), which is critical for real-world adoption.

**Quality & Clarity**  
The technical approach is sound, with clear connections to foundational work (e.g., Dawid, Ramsahai, Entner). However, the exposition of the relaxation parameters (*w*, *x*, *y*, *β*, *β̄*) is initially opaque. While the paper defines them as constraints on unfaithfulness, the motivation for specific values (e.g., *w* = 0.2) is underdeveloped. The supplementary material addresses this, but the main text should clarify that these parameters represent *user-defined tolerances* for violations of faithfulness (e.g., *w* bounds the maximum deviation in conditional effects). The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is functional but lacks intuitive guidance on choosing the relaxation parameters *θ*, which is a major hurdle for practitioners. The conclusion’s claim that WPP "keeps inference more honest" is overly emotive; the method’s value lies in its *explicit* handling of unfaithfulness, not in contrast to "risky" approaches.

**Originality & Significance**  
WPP is sufficiently distinct from prior work (e.g., Entner et al., Ramsahai) by reframing witness identification as a relaxation of faithfulness rather than a strict assumption. It bridges instrumental variable bounds and faithfulness-based methods in a novel way, offering a spectrum of inference strategies. The significance is high for causal inference methodology, as it provides a structured path to incorporate uncertainty about causal assumptions—a gap in current practice. However, the paper’s current implementation is not yet a drop-in replacement for standard tools; the need for user-specified *θ* and the computational overhead of Bayesian inference limit immediate applicability. That said, the framework’s potential to inspire more robust causal tools is clear, especially as parameter selection methods mature.

**Recommendation**  
The paper presents a technically rigorous and conceptually important contribution to causal inference. While the experimental evaluation underestimates the challenges of interpreting interval-based outputs and lacks robustness checks (e.g., on small datasets), the core methodology is novel and well-motivated. I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions to clarify the interpretation of bounds and the role of relaxation parameters. The authors should explicitly address how practitioners should use the output (e.g., "a bound [L, U] implies the ACE could be as large as U or as small as L, but not outside this interval") and justify the choice of *θ* in experiments. The influenza case study should emphasize why the narrower WPP bounds are practically meaningful relative to the standard IV interval. The "Witness Protection Program" name is apt and memorable—retaining it adds to the paper’s appeal.  

*Summary of key concerns for authors*:  
- **Interpretation**: Clarify that bounds are not confidence intervals; emphasize they represent *conservative* ranges under specific unfaithfulness relaxations.  
- **Evaluation**: Replace the Euclidean distance metric with a metric comparing bound widths (e.g., average interval length) against true ACE coverage.  
- **Parameter selection**: Discuss default *θ* choices (e.g., *w* = *x* = *y* = 0.2) as a pragmatic starting point, acknowledging this requires future work.  
- **Practicality**: Address the impact of small datasets on bound width (e.g., "for 500 samples, bounds widen by X%").  

This work is a valuable step toward more transparent causal inference. With minor clarifications, it will significantly influence how researchers handle unfaithfulness in observational studies.
