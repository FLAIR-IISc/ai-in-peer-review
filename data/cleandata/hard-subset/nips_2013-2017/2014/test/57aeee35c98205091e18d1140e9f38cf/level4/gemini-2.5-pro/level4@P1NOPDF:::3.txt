This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), an algorithm designed to compute bounds on an inferred Average Causal Effect (ACE) by taking into account possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption. The method introduces a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and then employs a linear programming framework to solve for the upper and lower bounds on the ACE. This is achieved by adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates, subject to the specified constraints.

The problem of inference under violations of faithfulness is receiving significant attention, making this paper both timely and interesting. The approach and ideas presented are commendable; it is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of unfaithful interaction patterns, leveraging well-established mathematical tools to solve the corresponding optimization problems.

### Quality and Significance

The technical approach appears sound, with many details explicated in the supplementary material (which I have not checked in detail). However, the paper's overall significance is more in the ideas and direction it proposes rather than its immediate practical applicability. While the WPP algorithm introduces techniques that could play an important role in future work, the current implementation seems unlikely to be the final word for practitioners.

A key conceptual challenge lies in the interpretation of the output. The algorithm produces a distribution over bounds that are a function of "unfaithful relaxations" relative to a given model. However, it remains unclear how to assess the likelihood of these unfaithful instances, and consequently, how relevant or realistic the computed bounds are. Many relaxations result in bounds that are too wide to be useful. Furthermore, the method does not provide a preferred point estimate within the interval, though end-users are likely to interpret it as a confidence interval around a best guess, which the authors rightly caution against. This interpretive ambiguity is a major hurdle for practical application.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The "bias" metric is not a meaningful way to compare WPP with its competitors, as they are designed to measure fundamentally different things. As a result, it is unclear whether the WPP's output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. The performance on the influenza dataset is also concerning: an ACE interval of (-0.23, 0.64) is too wide to support any meaningful conclusion, although this might be the most honest assessment possible under the relaxed assumptions.

### Clarity

The paper is reasonably well-written, though some sections could be improved. The algorithmic steps, involving many subtly different parameters, were difficult to follow, partly due to a lack of detailed explanation for certain aspects of the method. While the supplementary material might clarify these points, the main paper should be more self-contained. Important methodological details, such as how to choose the relaxation parameters, are relegated to future work, making it difficult to assess potential issues with this core part of the method. The conclusion, in particular, uses emotive language that detracts from the paper's objectivity (see detailed comments).

### Originality

To the best of my knowledge, the method is a novel combination of existing techniques. It is closely related to, but sufficiently distinct from, recent work on the same problem. As such, it has the potential to stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of different approaches to this challenging subject.

### Summary

The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem with a technically and conceptually interesting method that is likely to extend to more general cases. The approach is novel and may inspire further research. However, the experimental evaluation is poor and does not adequately demonstrate the method's practical value. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output—a distribution over bounds—remain unclear, limiting its current applicability.

### Other Comments

*   **p1, l.47:** "unstable" is not the appropriate word for assumptions; perhaps "violated" or "may not hold" would be better.
*   **p2, l.74:** What is meant by a "cartoon representation"?
*   **p2, l.93-94:** The reasoning here seems to implicitly assume causal sufficiency or prior knowledge that W is not a descendant of X. This should be stated.
*   **p3, l.127:** It would be helpful to explicitly state that the witness W is only a means to identify the admissible set Z and is not itself required for the computation of the ACE.
*   **p4, l.164:** The transition in this section (l.172-180) to a high level of abstraction is abrupt. It would greatly improve readability to provide some intuition on what the constraint parameters represent for readers less familiar with the subject.
*   **p5, l.221:** Please clarify the relationship between the search for admissible sets and the bounds computation. It is also unclear how the identification of admissible sets Z for a witness W depends on the relaxation parameters θ.
*   **p5, l.259:** To be clear, do you mean a maximum of 10 free parameters to describe the entire joint distribution involving Z, not a set Z containing 10 variables?
*   **p6, l.304:** The claim "We know of no previous analytical bounds" seems to overlook closely related work, such as "Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding" (Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf, UAI 2014).
*   **p6, l.319-332:** I am skeptical about the practical efficacy of the back-substitution process for refining bounds. It seems rare to be able to exploit such constraint violations in practice. Could you provide evidence from the experiments in Section 5 on how effective this step was?
*   **p7, l.357:** Stating that the "comparison is not straightforward" is an understatement. The subsequent example shows that comparison based on the chosen bias metric is meaningless.
*   **p7, l.369:** The experiments use 5000 data points. Many real-world studies, particularly in medicine, have far less data. The paper should address the expected impact on the width of the bounds for smaller sample sizes (e.g., 500 records).
*   **p8, Table 1:** It is impossible to gauge any meaningful benefit of using WPP over a standard point estimate from this table.
*   **p8, l.391:** Describing WPP as "quite stable" seems to be a euphemism for the fact that the derived bounds are so loose they are insensitive to whether the underlying problem is identifiable or not.
*   **p8, l.421+:** The language in the conclusion, such as "risky Bayesian approaches," "WPP bounds keep inference more honest," and the dismissive reference to "purely theory-driven analyses," is overly emotive and suggests a biased evaluation of competing research directions. WPP is not a "compromise" but rather a method focused on an additional aspect of the inference problem. This section should be rewritten to be more objective and collegial.
