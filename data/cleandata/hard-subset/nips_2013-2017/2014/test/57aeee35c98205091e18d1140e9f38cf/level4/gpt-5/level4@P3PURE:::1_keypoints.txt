- Paper proposes bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables using Entner et al.’s covariate-selection rule and a Ramsahai (2012)–style bounding method.  
- Main idea is sound, interesting, and potentially useful, but some technical details are not fully digested by the reviewer.  
- Key concern: the role and implications of Entner et al.’s conditions are unclear without the faithfulness assumption.  
- The reviewer suspects the conditions might inform the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly leaves this connection unexplored.  
- Because the paper sets that issue aside, the presented machinery may not crucially depend on Entner et al.’s conditions, raising questions about why the rule is needed.  
- The reviewer wonders whether the method’s empirical robustness is related to this limited dependence on Entner et al.’s conditions.  
- Minor: rephrase “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” since unfaithfulness usually means extra independences rather than violated independences.  
- Minor: report Entner et al.’s method performance using their stringent thresholds and treat “don’t know” as uninformative bounds.  
- Minor: clarify the flu-shot example—if X=1 is vaccinated and Y=1 is not hospitalized, explain why a negative ACE indicates a desirable vaccine.  
- Minor: fix table numbering (Table 1 cited as “Table 5” in text).  
- Overall: interesting contribution but needs clearer explanation of Entner et al.’s conditions’ role (especially without faithfulness) and how they relate to choosing relaxation parameters.
