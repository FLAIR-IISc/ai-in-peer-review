This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), an algorithm for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) under possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption. The method specifies a set of relaxation parameters to limit the extent of unfaithfulness and uses linear programming to compute lower and upper ACE bounds for a target link, given an adjustment set that satisfies these constraints. The problem is timely and important. I find the overall direction appealing: the paper offers a principled attempt to quantify the impact of weak violations of faithfulness and leverages well-established optimization tools to solve the resulting extremization problems.

That said, it is unclear whether the chosen parameterization fully captures the relevant departures from faithfulness. The output is also difficult to interpret: the method yields a distribution over bounds as a function of relaxation choices, but we lack a way to assess how plausible different unfaithful scenarios are, and hence how informative the resulting bounds might be. Many relaxation settings produce very wide intervals, limiting practical utility. The procedure does not commit to any point estimate within the interval—yet readers may still interpret the bounds as supporting a “best guess.” The experimental evaluation is weak: the “bias” metric is not comparable across methods that return intervals versus point estimates, making the head-to-head comparisons largely uninformative. On the influenza dataset, the interval ACE ∈ (−0.23, 0.64) offers almost no actionable conclusion—though it may well be the most honest assessment.

Quality
- The approach appears technically sound, and many details are deferred to the supplement (which I did not verify). The core ideas are well motivated, but the broader context is sometimes lost.
- Crucial elements—especially how to select or calibrate the relaxation parameters—are pushed to future work, leaving readers unable to gauge how sensitive the method is to these choices.
- The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently balanced or robust to establish practical value.

Clarity
- Generally well written, though the conclusion section needs revision (see comments below).
- The algorithm and its many parameter variants were hard to track. From the main text alone I am not confident I could implement the full method; the supplement may suffice.

Originality
- As far as I can tell, the paper combines familiar ingredients in a novel way. It is close to, but distinct from, recent literature on related bounding problems and could stimulate useful cross-pollination.

Significance
- The main contribution is conceptual and directional: it shows how to incorporate relaxation of faithfulness and optimization-based bounding into causal inference pipelines. In its current state, it is unlikely to be a finished tool for practitioners, but some of its ideas may become influential components in future methods.

Summary
- A relevant and topical problem. An interesting, technically thoughtful approach likely to attract attention. Current practical impact seems limited, but the ideas may extend to broader settings. Writing is decent but could be clearer. Experimental evaluation is weak. The meaning and practical interpretation of the distribution over bounds remain unclear.
