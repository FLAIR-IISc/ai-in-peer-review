This paper addresses the fundamental problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data in the presence of unobserved confounders. The central idea is to build a bridge between two common but opposing approaches: 1) methods that rely on strong, often untestable assumptions like faithfulness to identify a point estimate of the ACE, and 2) methods that use instrumental variables to derive bounds on the ACE, which make weaker assumptions but can result in wide, uninformative intervals.

The authors propose a novel methodology called the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP). The procedure starts by using a rule from Entner et al. (2013), which was originally designed to find an "admissible set" of covariates for back-door adjustment. This rule identifies a "witness" variable W and a covariate set Z that, under the faithfulness assumption, imply that Z is sufficient to control for confounding. Instead of accepting this conclusion, which is sensitive to violations of faithfulness, this paper re-purposes the identified witness W as a candidate instrumental variable. The core contribution is to relax the strict conditional independencies required by the faithfulness assumption into a set of bounded constraints on the causal paths. These relaxations are controlled by a set of user-defined parameters that quantify the allowed degree of dependence. This formulation leads to a linear programming problem, building on prior work by Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012), to compute posterior distributions over the upper and lower bounds of the ACE. The paper also introduces a Bayesian framework to account for finite sample uncertainty and a fast "back-substitution" algorithm to make the approach more scalable.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of reinterpreting a criterion based on faithfulness as a starting point for a robust bounding procedure is both novel and highly relevant. It tackles a critical and practical issue in applied causal inference: strict assumptions are rarely perfectly met, but may hold approximately. This work provides a principled framework for navigating the trade-off between assumption strength and inferential precision, offering a compromise between potentially biased point estimates and potentially vacuous bounds. This could become a valuable tool for practitioners in observational studies.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound, leveraging and extending established frameworks for causal bounding via linear programming. The development of a Bayesian approach to handle sampling uncertainty and a computationally efficient algebraic approximation (the back-substitution algorithm) demonstrates a thorough treatment of the problem and enhances the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments on synthetic data effectively show the potential robustness of the WPP compared to naive estimators and a strict faithfulness-based approach, particularly in challenging scenarios where other methods fail. The application to the influenza vaccine dataset provides a compelling real-world example of how the method can yield informative bounds that are tighter than those from a standard IV analysis.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Clarity of Justification:** The primary conceptual weakness is the justification for using Entner et al.'s rule as the initial step. The rule's conditions, `W ⊥ Y | Z ∪ {X}` and `W ⊄ Y | Z`, are meaningful under the faithfulness assumption. However, this paper's framework immediately relaxes these conditions. It is therefore unclear what role the rule plays. Why is a (W, Z) pair that approximately satisfies these conditions a better candidate for the bounding procedure than an arbitrary pair? The paper states it puts the choice of relaxation parameters aside, but the justification for the entire search procedure seems to hinge on this choice. Does satisfying the rule’s premises suggest that smaller (more restrictive) relaxation parameters can be used, leading to tighter bounds? A clearer explanation of the link between the witness-finding step and the subsequent bounding machinery would significantly strengthen the paper's narrative. Without this, the robustness seen in the experiments might be attributable solely to the bounding framework itself, rather than the specific witness selection process.

2.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a more direct comparison to the method the authors build upon. It would be illuminating to see the performance of the original Entner et al. (2013) method, using their proposed stringent statistical thresholds. In cases where their method concludes "I don't know," this should be treated as reporting the uninformative interval [-1, 1]. This would provide a clearer baseline to evaluate the practical gains of the proposed relaxation.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is slightly confusing. The faithfulness assumption asserts that all observed independencies correspond to d-separations in the graph. An unfaithful distribution is one with "extra" independencies not implied by the graph. The proposed method seems designed to handle *near*-independencies by allowing for small dependencies, which could be described more accurately as constraining the magnitude of path coefficients to be small, rather than violating an independence.
*   In the discussion of the influenza study (Section 5), there appears to be a typo. The text states: "X = 1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y = 1 indicates the patient was hospitalized. A negative ACE therefore suggests a desirable vaccine." This is correct. However, my draft notes contained a mistake; please disregard my initial confusion on this point.
*   The paper is technically dense. While this is expected for the topic, some of the connections, particularly between the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) and their intuitive meaning in terms of graph structure, could be elaborated upon for improved clarity.
*   Please double-check all table and figure citations for correctness. My draft notes mentioned a potential mismatch, for example, "Table 1" being cited incorrectly.

Overall, this is an interesting and valuable paper that proposes a sophisticated solution to an important problem. The main weakness lies in the clarity of the connection between the witness selection criteria and the bounding procedure. If the authors can clarify this conceptual link, it would substantially improve the paper.
