The paper "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program" introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies. The method, called the Witness Protection Program (WPP), aims to address the issue of confounding variables by exploiting observational conditional independencies to identify "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors relax the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths.

The WPP algorithm uses a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference to obtain a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect. The method is based on the idea of using a set of covariates to block all back-doors and identify an admissible set for back-door adjustment. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the methodology, including the assumptions and constraints used to derive the bounds on the average causal effect.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the technical details. However, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the results are not convincing. The authors compare the WPP method with two naive point estimators and the point estimator of Entner et al., but the comparison is not straightforward. The evaluation metric used, Euclidean distance, is not suitable for comparing the WPP method with point estimators.

The significance of the paper lies in its ideas and direction, showing how to incorporate other techniques in solving the challenging problem of causal inference. However, the current implementation of the WPP algorithm is unlikely to represent the finished article for practitioners. The method is technically interesting, but its practical efficacy is unclear.

The paper raises several questions and concerns. For example, how likely are the unfaithful instances, and how relevant/realistic are the bounds themselves? Many relaxations lead to wide bounds, which can become less than useful. The method does not commit to any preferred point estimate within the interval, but people are likely to interpret it that way.

In terms of quality, the approach is technically sound, but the experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough. The paper is reasonably well-written, but some aspects, such as the choosing of relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article. The originality of the method is its novel combination of familiar techniques, but its significance is mainly in its ideas and direction.

To improve the paper, the authors should provide a more robust experimental evaluation, including a comparison with other methods and a more suitable evaluation metric. They should also clarify the interpretation of the output distribution over bounds and provide more insight into the practical efficacy of the WPP algorithm.
