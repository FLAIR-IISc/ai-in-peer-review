

The paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a novel approach to computing bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) that accounts for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. WPP achieves this by introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and employing linear programming to derive upper and lower ACE bounds for a given covariate adjustment set under these constraints. The problem is timely and relevant, and the method represents a principled effort to explicitly quantify the impact of weak unfaithful patterns using established mathematical tools.

The core contribution—combining familiar techniques to handle unfaithfulness—is conceptually interesting and likely to stimulate further research. However, the practical utility of WPP remains questionable. The output consists of a distribution over bounds parameterized by "unfaithful relaxations," but the method provides no guidance on the likelihood of these relaxation scenarios or their real-world relevance. Consequently, many relaxations yield overly wide bounds (e.g., ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) for influenza data), rendering them practically uninformative. Critically, WPP offers no point estimate within the interval, yet this omission risks misinterpretation as a confidence interval around a single estimate.

The experimental evaluation is severely deficient. The proposed "bias" metric is ill-defined, as it compares fundamentally different quantities (WPP’s interval bounds vs. competitors’ point estimates), making the reported comparisons meaningless. This undermines the claim that WPP provides "valuable additions" to practitioners. The influenza results exemplify this weakness: the near-zero-effect interval offers negligible actionable insight. Further, the paper fails to demonstrate how WPP’s bounds improve upon standard faithfulness-based approaches in real-world scenarios, as evidenced by Table 1’s inability to convey any meaningful benefit.

Technically, the method is sound, with sufficient detail provided in the supplementary material (though not fully verified). The ideas are well-motivated, though the context occasionally becomes obscured. Key practical challenges—such as selecting relaxation parameters—are deferred to future work, hindering assessment of potential pitfalls. Clarity suffers from insufficient explanation of subtle parameter nuances (e.g., the role of relaxation parameters in defining admissible sets *Z* for witness *W*), making implementation difficult without the supplement. The algorithmic steps are hard to follow due to abrupt shifts in abstraction (e.g., p.4, lines 172–180).

The paper’s significance lies in its conceptual framework for incorporating unfaithfulness into causal bounds, which could inform future work. However, its current implementation is not ready for routine practitioner use. While the core ideas may prove influential, the specific WPP algorithm lacks practical robustness. The claim that WPP "keeps inference more honest" (p.360) is misleading; the bounds’ looseness (e.g., covering 30% of (-1,1) but including both strong and null effects) does not inherently improve interpretability. The assertion that WPP "provides a compromise" (p.369) is inaccurate—it addresses a distinct aspect of causal inference, not a trade-off.

Minor issues require attention:  
- The "Witness Protection Program" name is apt (p.1/5).  
- "Unstable" (p.1/47) should be "untrue" (patterns are stable but may not hold).  
- "Cartoon representation" (p.2/74) is misleading.  
- Causal sufficiency (knowledge of *W* → *X*) must be clarified (p.2/93–94).  
- *W* is not used in ACE computation (p.3/127).  
- "An useful" (p.3/143) → "a useful."  
- "Gives greater control" (p.3/144) is vague; specify *how*.  
- "Might" → "can" (p.4/164); "(conditional)" → "(unconditional)" (p.4/185).  
- Parameter interpretations (p.4/172–180, p.5/185–189) need intuitive explanations.  
- Clarify that "max. 10 free parameters" refers to *Z*’s description, not 10 variables (p.5/259).  
- Cite related work (e.g., Geiger et al., UAI 2014) regarding analytical bounds (p.6/304).  
- Address the impracticality of the "back-substitution" refinement step (p.6/319–332).  
- Explain why "more than one bound" arises (p.7/350).  
- Correct the misstatement about the bias metric (p.7/357).  
- Clarify the impact of small sample sizes (e.g., 500 records) on bound width (p.7/369).  
- Remove emotive language (e.g., "risky Bayesian approaches," p.8/421+) and dismissive remarks (e.g., "purely theory-driven analyses," p.8/421+).  
- Fix typo: "inuenza" (p.9).

In summary: WPP presents a theoretically novel approach to causal bounds under unfaithfulness, but its experimental evaluation is inadequate, and the practical relevance of its output is unclear due to overly wide intervals and lack of interpretational guidance. While conceptually compelling, the method does not yet deliver actionable insights for practitioners. The writing is generally clear but requires refinement in technical explanations and interpretation. The contribution is significant for future work but not yet robust for current applications.

**Recommendation: Reject**  
*(Due to poor experimental evaluation, unclear practical relevance, and unresolved interpretational challenges.)*
