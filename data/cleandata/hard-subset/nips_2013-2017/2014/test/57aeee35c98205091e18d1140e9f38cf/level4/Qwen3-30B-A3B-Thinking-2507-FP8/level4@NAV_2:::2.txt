

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel method for deriving bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) when confounding is present in observational data. The approach inverts Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to treat witnesses as instrumental variables rather than discard them, relaxes the faithfulness assumption to allow controlled path cancellations, and formulates bounds via constrained linear programming and Bayesian inference. It builds directly on prior NIPS work by Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2012) but extends their point-estimation framework to provide rigorous bounds that remain valid under faithfulness violations. The method is validated on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study, demonstrating tighter bounds than standard instrumental variable approaches while maintaining computational feasibility through symbolic algebraic relaxations and a back-substitution algorithm.

Quality  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by formalizing a relaxation of faithfulness that enables conservative ACE bounds under unmeasured confounding. The Bayesian framework for posterior inference over bounds—using constrained Dirichlet priors to avoid sensitivity to hyperparameters—addresses a key limitation in prior causal inference methods. However, the connection between the proposed inverse application of Rule 1 and the specific causal structure in Figure 1(d) requires clearer justification; explicitly linking the "witness protection" mechanism to the graph in Figure 2 would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Additionally, while the paper provides default parameter choices (e.g., $w = x = y = 0.2$), a principled methodology for selecting relaxation parameters based on data characteristics or prior knowledge would enhance reproducibility and applicability.

Clarity  
The theoretical exposition is largely comprehensive, though minor inconsistencies require correction: Algorithm 1 is referenced as "Table 1" on page 6 (it is an algorithm), and "Table 5" on page 8 should be "Table 3" (the results table). The definition of parameters in Table 1 (now Table 2 in the paper) should be clarified to distinguish $\zeta_{yx.w}$ from its marginalized counterparts $\kappa_{yx.w}$ and $\omega_{xw}$. The explanation of the back-substitution algorithm (Section 4) is particularly valuable for computational efficiency, though the transition from numerical LP to symbolic bounds could be more explicitly motivated. The real-world influenza case study (Section 5) effectively demonstrates practical utility.

Originality  
The core innovation—leveraging conditional independencies via an inverted Rule 1 to generate bounds rather than point estimates—represents a meaningful extension beyond Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2012). While the problem of causal effect bounding has been addressed previously (e.g., Manski 1990, Ramsahai 2012), WPP’s relaxation of faithfulness to control path cancellations and its computational efficiency via symbolic bounds are novel. The paper adequately contextualizes these contributions against prior NIPS work, though a brief comparison to recent causal discovery methods (e.g., Geiger et al. 2014) would further highlight its distinctiveness.

Significance  
The method addresses a critical gap in observational studies by providing robust, interpretable bounds on causal effects when faithfulness is questionable—a common challenge in real-world data. The influenza vaccination case study demonstrates tangible utility, yielding narrower bounds than standard instrumental variable approaches ($[-0.10, 0.17]$ vs. $[-0.23, 0.64]$) while acknowledging the posterior uncertainty over bounds (not point estimates). The computational improvements (back-substitution algorithm) enable scalability to practical settings, and the open-source implementation (via R/Polymake) supports reproducibility. While the synthetic experiments are thorough (four case studies with 100 datasets each), expanding the real-world validation to additional domains would further strengthen the significance claim. Overall, WPP offers a promising addition to the causal inference toolkit with clear potential for adoption in applied settings.
