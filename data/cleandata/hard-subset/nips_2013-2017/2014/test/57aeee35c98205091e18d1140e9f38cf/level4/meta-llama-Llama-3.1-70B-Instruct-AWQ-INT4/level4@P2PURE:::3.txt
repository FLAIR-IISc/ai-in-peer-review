The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which computes bounds on the inferred average causal effect (ACE) by considering possible violations of the standard faithfulness assumption in causal inference. This is achieved by introducing relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions and utilizing a linear programming framework to solve for upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a given link, adjusted for a set of covariates and subject to these constraints.

The problem addressed in the paper is timely and interesting, and the approach is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of weak unfaithful interaction patterns using well-known mathematical tools. However, it remains to be seen whether the authors have found the right method to capture this impact. The resulting output, a distribution over bounds as a function of 'unfaithful relaxations' relative to a given model, is difficult to interpret conceptually. The likelihood and relevance of these unfaithful instances, and thus the bounds themselves, are unclear. Furthermore, many relaxations lead to wide bounds, rendering them less useful. The method does not provide a preferred point estimate within the interval, which may lead to misinterpretation.

The experimental evaluation is poor, particularly in the comparison of the WPP method with its competitors using the 'bias' evaluation metric, which measures two completely different things. Consequently, it is unclear whether the WPP output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complicated distraction. The practical performance on the influenza dataset is also concerning, with the ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64) implying limited relevant conclusions.

Quality:
The approach is technically sound, with many details provided in the supplementary file. The ideas are well-introduced, although the overall context is occasionally lost. Important aspects, such as choosing relaxation parameters, are relegated to a future article, making it difficult to assess potential issues with this part of the method. The experimental evaluation is not balanced or robust enough.

Clarity:
The paper is decently written, except for the conclusion. The algorithmic steps and modifications involving subtly different parameters are hard to follow, partly due to the lack of explanation of certain details. While the supplement may provide sufficient information, it is unclear whether a reader could fully implement the method from the description alone.

Originality:
The method appears to be a novel combination of familiar techniques, closely related to but sufficiently different from recent work on the same problem. This may stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of approaches on the subject.

Significance:
The paper's significance lies in the ideas and direction it brings to the problem, demonstrating how to incorporate other techniques to solve this challenging problem. However, in its current implementation, it is unlikely to represent the finished article for practitioners, although some ideas employed in the WPP algorithm may play an important role in the future.

Summary:
The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem with an interesting method that will likely find an audience. However, the current application is limited, and the experimental evaluation is poor. The relevance and proper interpretation of the output distribution over bounds remain unclear.
