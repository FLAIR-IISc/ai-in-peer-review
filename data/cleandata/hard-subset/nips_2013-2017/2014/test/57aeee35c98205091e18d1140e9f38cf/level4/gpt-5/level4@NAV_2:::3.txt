The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a procedure for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) while explicitly allowing controlled deviations from the standard faithfulness assumption. It does this by introducing relaxation parameters that quantify permissible departures from conditional independences and then formulates a linear program to compute upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a target causal link after conditioning on suitable covariates. The topic is timely and relevant. I find the overall direction compelling: the method provides a principled way to model “near-faithful” situations and leverages established optimization tools to obtain worst-case bounds. That said, it remains uncertain whether the specific relaxation scheme best captures practical departures from faithfulness. The resulting output—a posterior over bounds determined by these relaxations—is difficult to interpret conceptually: it is not obvious how plausible the posited departures are, and hence how informative the derived bounds will be in realistic settings. Many relaxations yield very wide intervals, potentially limiting utility. The procedure does not select or recommend any point within the interval, yet users may be tempted to read it as such. The empirical evaluation is also unsatisfying: the “bias” metric is not a meaningful basis for comparing interval-valued outputs with point estimators, making it hard to judge practical benefit. On the influenza application, the obtained bounds are extremely broad (ACE in (-0.23, 0.64)), offering little actionable guidance—perhaps appropriately cautious, but not practically decisive.

Quality
The approach appears technically sound, with substantial derivations deferred to the supplement (I did not verify those details). The main ideas are well motivated, though the broader context is occasionally hard to follow. Important choices—especially selection of relaxation parameters—are postponed to future work, making it difficult to assess robustness and sensitivity. The experimental study is neither balanced nor sufficiently rigorous to support strong conclusions.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable (the conclusion is an exception; see comments below). The algorithm and its variants, along with multiple closely related parameters, are difficult to track, partly due to missing intuition for some steps. I believe the high-level method is understandable, but it is not clear one could implement it reliably from the main text alone; the supplement may suffice.

Originality
To the best of my knowledge, the method represents a novel integration of known components. It is closely connected to, yet distinct from, concurrent work on bounding approaches, and could catalyze cross-pollination among related lines of research.

Significance
The significance lies primarily in the framing and methodological direction: it shows how to incorporate relaxed independence assumptions and optimization for bounding causal effects in challenging observational settings. As presented, the method seems premature for routine practice, but some of its components may become influential as the area matures.

Summary:
A relevant and current problem. An interesting methodology likely to attract attention. Present utility is limited, but the framework could extend to broader settings. Writing is reasonable but can be improved. Technical contributions are of interest. Empirical evaluation is weak. The meaning and practical use of the posterior over bounds remain unclear.
