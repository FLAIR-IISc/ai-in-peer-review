

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies by relaxing the faithfulness assumption. The authors invert Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to treat potential witnesses as instrumental variables rather than discarding them, then introduce bounded violations of local independence to model path cancellations. This yields posterior distributions over ACE bounds via linear programming and Bayesian inference, with symbolic bounds derived through algebraic relaxation. The work builds on Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2012), but extends their point-estimation approach to provide uncertainty-aware bounds that remain valid under faithfulness violations—a critical gap in existing methods.

**Quality**  
The technical contribution is sound and well-motivated. The relaxation of faithfulness through bounded path cancellations (Eqs. 3–6) provides a principled alternative to the "all-or-nothing" faithfulness assumption, and the linear programming formulation with Bayesian posterior sampling is rigorously derived. The authors correctly note that their bounds are at least as conservative as Entner's point estimator, which is a necessary property. However, the paper lacks sufficient justification for the *inverse application of Rule 1* (e.g., why the witness structure in Fig. 1(d) is valid for this inversion). The theoretical argument would benefit from explicitly stating the causal graph assumptions (e.g., "We assume the causal graph contains no edges W → Y given Z, as implied by Rule 1's premises"). Additionally, the absence of a principled method to select relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) is a significant weakness. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the current implementation relies on ad hoc defaults (e.g., w=x=y=0.2 in experiments), which undermines the method's reliability. A discussion of how these parameters relate to data characteristics (e.g., sample size, confounding strength) would strengthen the contribution.

**Clarity**  
The exposition of the core methodology is generally clear, though minor inconsistencies hinder readability. For instance, the claim that "the full algorithm is shown in Table 1" (p. 6) is incorrect—Algorithm 1 is presented in the text, not a table. Similarly, "Results are summarized in Table 5" (p. 8) should reference Table 3 (as per the paper's actual tables). The experimental section lacks detail on parameter choices: it is unclear how the "brute-force search for Z" (p. 8) handles high-dimensional covariates, and the description of the influenza study omits key assumptions (e.g., why GRP was excluded from admissible sets). The supplementary material appears essential for full comprehension, but the main paper should clarify critical implementation details. Typos also persist (e.g., "Naı̈ve Estimator" on p. 4).

**Originality**  
The paper’s core innovation—using witness variables as instrumental variables under relaxed faithfulness—represents a meaningful extension of Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2012). While the use of linear programming for causal bounds is not novel (Dawid, 1979; Ramsahai, 2012), the *combination* of this with the inverse Rule 1, bounded path cancellations, and Bayesian posterior sampling for bounds is original. The symbolic algebraic bounds (Section 4) further distinguish the work, as prior methods lack closed-form expressions in terms of relaxation parameters. However, the paper does not sufficiently contrast its approach with related work (e.g., Geiger et al., 2014; Manski, 2007), which also derives bounds under weaker assumptions. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance**  
The method addresses a pressing need in observational causal inference: providing reliable bounds when faithfulness is questionable. The empirical results (Table 3) demonstrate that WPP produces tighter, more stable bounds than naive back-door adjustment (NE1/NE2) and Entner’s point estimator, particularly in "hard" cases where confounding is severe. The influenza application shows practical utility, as WPP’s interval [−0.10, 0.17] is significantly narrower than the standard IV bound [−0.23, 0.64]. However, the experimental scope is limited to synthetic data with |W|=8 and a single real-world dataset. Broader validation—e.g., on high-dimensional data or with varying confounding structures—is needed to establish generalizability. Despite this, the framework’s potential to become a standard tool in observational studies (as claimed in the conclusion) is compelling, especially for practitioners wary of faithfulness.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference by offering a robust, computationally feasible method for bounding causal effects under relaxed assumptions. Its strengths lie in the principled relaxation of faithfulness, the integration of Bayesian uncertainty quantification, and the practical symbolic bounds. To address the weaknesses, the authors should: (1) clarify the causal graph assumptions for the inverse Rule 1 usage; (2) provide guidance on selecting relaxation parameters; and (3) expand experimental validation. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the technical merit is high, but the current presentation requires refinement to fully realize its significance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.
