This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables in settings where the causal structure over the covariates is unknown. The authors build upon a rule from Entner et al. (2013), which was designed to select a sufficient set of observed covariates for blocking all back-door paths. However, the validity of this rule in the original work relies on the faithfulness assumption. This work relaxes this requirement by proposing to use the rule to derive bounds on the ACE, rather than a point estimate. The proposed method is developed based on the framework of Ramsahai (2012).

While I have not been able to verify all the technical details, the central idea of the paper is intuitive, interesting, and appears useful. A key point that remains unclear to me, however, is the precise role of the conditions from Entner et al.'s rule within the proposed bounding framework. Specifically, when the faithfulness assumption is dropped, these conditions no longer have clear implications for the underlying causal structure. I suspect that satisfying these conditions might inform the choice of the relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly defers this topic. This raises the question of whether the proposed machinery is largely independent of Entner et al.'s conditions. If so, this might explain the method's apparent robustness in the experiments. Clarification on this point would be greatly appreciated.

Some minor comments:
1.  In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrasing "...express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence..." is confusing. Unfaithfulness typically refers to the presence of conditional independencies not entailed by the graph structure, rather than a violation of independence. It seems the framework is designed to model "near-unfaithful" distributions by constraining the magnitude of dependencies that would otherwise be zero under faithfulness.

2.  The experiments would be more compelling if they included a comparison with the original method of Entner et al. (using their stringent thresholds). The "don't know" output from their method could be treated as returning uninformative bounds for a direct comparison.

3.  On page 8, the interpretation of the ACE appears contradictory. If X=1 represents receiving a flu shot and Y=1 represents *not* being hospitalized, a negative ACE would imply the vaccine *increases* the probability of hospitalization. The text, however, suggests a negative ACE indicates a desirable effect. Please clarify this.

4.  There is a typo on page 8: the table labeled "Table 1" is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

Overall, this is an interesting paper on an important topic, though its technical presentation is challenging to follow in parts.
