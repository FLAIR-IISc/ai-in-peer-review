This paper studies estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is only partially known. It builds on a rule from Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. In Entner et al., however, the rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. This paper instead uses the rule to derive bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE and develops a method following Ramsahai (2012).

I have to admit I have not absorbed all of the technical details, but the main idea seems sound, interesting, and practically useful. What puzzled me is the role that the conditions in Entner et al.’s rule actually play in this framework. Without faithfulness, those conditions do not have clear implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that when those conditions are verified, they might inform the choice of relaxation parameters; yet the paper explicitly sets that issue aside. It therefore appears that the machinery does not critically depend on the Entner et al. conditions, and I wonder whether the apparent robustness in the experiments is related to this. Clarification on how, specifically, the Entner conditions are used (or not used) in the bounding procedure would be helpful.

Minor comments:
1. First paragraph of Section 3: the phrase “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” reads oddly. Unfaithfulness typically means allowing additional independences, not violating independences. It seems the framework is meant to allow “near-violations” of faithfulness by constraining the strength of dependences, rather than by positing violations of independence per se. Please clarify the intended meaning.
2. In the experiments, it would be informative to also report the performance of Entner et al.’s method using their original, stringent thresholds, treating a “don’t know” outcome as yielding uninformative bounds. This would give a clearer baseline comparison.
3. On p. 8, second paragraph: if X=1 denotes receiving a flu shot and Y=1 denotes not being hospitalized, why would a negative ACE indicate a desirable vaccine? A negative ACE would seem to decrease the probability of Y=1 (i.e., increase hospitalization), which appears undesirable. Please check the sign convention or the interpretation.
4. The table at the top of p. 8 is labeled “Table 1” but is cited as “Table 5” in the text. Please correct the inconsistency.

Overall, this is an interesting and promising paper, though some details are difficult to follow. A more explicit account of how the Entner et al. conditions interact with the relaxation parameters and the bounding results would strengthen the presentation.
