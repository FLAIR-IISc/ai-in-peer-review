- Introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute ACE bounds while allowing controlled violations of faithfulness via relaxation parameters and linear programming.  
- Principled and timely approach that quantifies impact of weak unfaithful interactions using established optimization tools.  
- Practical relevance is questionable because the output is a distribution over bounds without indicating how likely the unfaithful scenarios are.  
- Many relaxation settings produce excessively wide bounds that are practically uninformative.  
- Method does not provide a preferred point estimate within the interval, though practitioners may expect one.  
- Experimental evaluation is inadequate: the "bias" metric compares fundamentally different quantities and is therefore meaningless.  
- Influenza dataset results yield a near-useless ACE interval (-0.23, 0.64), raising concerns about practical usefulness.  
- Technical approach is sound and detailed (supplement contains extra material), but the supplement was not independently verified.  
- Main-text exposition is unclear: algorithmic steps, parameter choices, and subtle modifications are hard to follow.  
- Selection and interpretation of relaxation parameters are deferred, preventing assessment of real-world pitfalls.  
- Back-substitution for refining bounds is questioned; paper should analyze its practical efficacy.  
- Claims about novelty overlook closely related prior work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014).  
- Originality: novel combination of established techniques with potential for cross-fertilization.  
- Significance: conceptually important but not yet immediately useful to practitioners; implementation needs improvement.  
- Critical clarification needed: WPP bounds are not confidence intervals around a point estimate and should not be treated as such.  
- Paper contains several clarity/editing issues and typos (terminology, abrupt abstraction shifts, specific phrasing and spelling errors).  
- Some claims/emotive language (e.g., "risky Bayesian approaches," "compromise") are misleading or dismissive and should be revised.  
- Overall: relevant and interesting method but hampered by poor experimental validation, unclear interpretation of outputs, and insufficient methodological clarity.
