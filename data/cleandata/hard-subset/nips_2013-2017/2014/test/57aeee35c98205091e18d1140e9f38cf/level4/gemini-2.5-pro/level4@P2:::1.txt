This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data when the underlying causal graph is unknown. The authors build upon the work of Entner et al. (2013), which proposed a rule for identifying a sufficient set of covariates for back-door adjustment. However, that rule's validity rests on the strong and often untestable faithfulness assumption. The key innovation of this paper is to repurpose this rule as a heuristic for generating bounds on the ACE, rather than a method for obtaining a single point estimate. By relaxing the strict independence conditions that faithfulness implies and framing the problem within a linear programming and Bayesian inference framework (drawing on methods from Dawid, 2003 and Ramsahai, 2012), the paper develops a procedure that is potentially more robust to the near-violations of faithfulness common in finite datasets.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength is its novel and pragmatic approach to a fundamental problem in causal inference. It thoughtfully navigates the trade-off between methods that produce precise point estimates by relying on strong assumptions and those that offer wide, often uninformative, bounds with minimal assumptions. By treating faithfulness as a condition that can be relaxed by degrees, the proposed "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) provides a valuable new tool for observational studies. The development of a Bayesian framework for inference on these bounds and the derivation of algebraic approximations for computational efficiency are also significant contributions.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

While the overall idea is compelling, a central point requires clarification: the precise role and justification of Entner et al.'s rule within this relaxed framework. The original rule, which identifies a "witness" `W` and an "admissible set" `Z` based on specific conditional independencies, is powerful precisely because of the strong structural implications it has under the faithfulness assumption.

Once faithfulness is relaxed, it is not immediately clear what inferential power these conditions retain. The paper uses the rule to find candidate `(W,Z)` pairs to initialize the bounding procedure. However, the bounding machinery itself does not seem to fundamentally depend on these conditions holding true, which raises the question of why this selection criterion is used at all. My best guess is that the satisfaction of these conditions (even approximately) should inform the choice of the relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`), but the paper explicitly sets aside the methodology for choosing these parameters.

Could the authors elaborate on why a `(W,Z)` pair satisfying Entner's conditions is a better starting point for this bounding procedure than an arbitrary pair? The impressive robustness of the method shown in the experiments is a key result. It would be helpful to understand if this robustness is due to the selection rule successfully identifying approximately correct structures, or if the bounding method is so robust that it works well even with a weak heuristic. Clarifying this would strengthen the paper's central argument.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on "Violations of Faithfulness":** The phrasing "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" (Section 3) could be confusing. Unfaithfulness typically refers to exact conditional independencies that arise from parameter cancellations, not from the graph structure. The proposed framework seems designed to handle *approximate* independencies (i.e., weak dependencies), which is a related but distinct concept. Clarifying this terminology would help readers better grasp the conceptual underpinnings of the method.

2.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are well-designed. To provide a more complete picture, it would be beneficial to also report the performance of the original Entner et al. method, using their proposed stringent thresholds for independence tests. In cases where their method returns "I don’t know," this outcome could be treated as producing the uninformative bounds [−1, 1]. This would allow for a more direct comparison of robustness and informativeness against the WPP.

3.  **Minor Corrections:** Please consider the following minor points:
    *   In the influenza study description (Section 5), the definition "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was hospitalized" correctly implies that a negative ACE is desirable. Please ensure this is stated unambiguously to avoid potential misinterpretation.
    *   Please check all table citations for correctness. For instance, in the draft I reviewed, there appeared to be a reference to "Table 5" that may have intended to be "Table 1".

**Conclusion:**

This is an interesting and valuable paper that proposes a promising methodology for a difficult and important problem. The core idea of relaxing the faithfulness assumption to derive robust bounds on causal effects is a significant contribution to the field. The main weakness is a lack of clarity regarding the precise justification for the initial covariate selection step. Addressing this point and the minor suggestions above would substantially improve an already strong and thought-provoking paper.
