- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute ACE bounds under relaxed faithfulness using relaxation parameters and linear programming.  
- Addresses a timely and important problem with a principled, mathematically grounded approach.  
- Concerns whether the method truly captures the impact of unfaithfulness; output is conceptually hard to interpret.  
- Unclear how likely the assumed unfaithful instances are, making the realism and relevance of bounds uncertain.  
- Many relaxation settings yield very wide bounds that may be practically unhelpful.  
- WPP produces intervals but no point estimate, risking user misinterpretation as a single best estimate.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak and unbalanced, reducing confidence in practical usefulness.  
- The “bias” metric used for comparison is misleading because competitors measure different quantities.  
- Influenza dataset results are inconclusive (e.g., ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) gives little actionable insight).  
- Method is technically sound with supplementary details, but key decisions (e.g., choosing relaxation parameters) are deferred and under-specified.  
- Paper clarity is adequate overall but algorithmic steps and many parameters are difficult to follow and may prevent reimplementation without the supplement.  
- The method is an original combination of familiar techniques and related to recent work, offering potential cross-fertilization.  
- Significance lies in the ideas and direction rather than being a ready-to-use practical solution.  
- Doubts about the practical efficacy of the back-substitution refinement step and how often it meaningfully tightens bounds.  
- Concerns about sample size sensitivity (e.g., typical trials with few records may produce much larger bounds).  
- Many detailed editorial and technical comments: wording/typo fixes, clearer interpretation of parameters, link to prior work, explain relation between search and bound computation, and clarify parameterization limits.  
- Critique of emotive or biased language in discussion; calls for more measured interpretation of results.
