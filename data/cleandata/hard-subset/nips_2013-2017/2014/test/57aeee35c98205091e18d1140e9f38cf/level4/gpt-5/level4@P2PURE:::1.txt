This paper studies estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure among covariates is only partially known. It revisits the rule of Entner et al. (2013), which is intended to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. In Entner et al., the rule is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. This paper instead uses the rule to produce bounds, rather than point estimates, on the ACE, and develops a method building on Ramsahai (2012).

I have not fully worked through all technical details, but the main idea seems sound, interesting, and useful. What I find puzzling is the role of Entner et al.’s conditions in this approach. Without assuming faithfulness, those conditions do not have clear implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that, when they hold, they are meant to inform the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets that choice aside. This makes it appear that the machinery does not critically rely on Entner et al.’s conditions, and I wonder whether the method’s apparent robustness in the experiments is related to this. Clarification on this point would be helpful.

Minor comments:
1. Section 3, first paragraph: “… express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence …” reads oddly. Unfaithfulness typically involves additional independences beyond those implied by the graph, rather than violations of independence. It seems the proposed framework is actually meant to allow “near violations” of faithfulness by constraining the strength of dependence.
2. In the experiments, it would be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.’s method using their original stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as yielding uninformative (vacuous) bounds.
3. Page 8, second paragraph: if X=1 denotes receiving a flu shot and Y=1 denotes not being hospitalized, why would a negative ACE indicate a desirable vaccine?
4. The table labeled “Table 1” at the top of p. 8 is cited in the text as “Table 5.”

Overall, this is an interesting paper, though the technical development is not easy to follow.
