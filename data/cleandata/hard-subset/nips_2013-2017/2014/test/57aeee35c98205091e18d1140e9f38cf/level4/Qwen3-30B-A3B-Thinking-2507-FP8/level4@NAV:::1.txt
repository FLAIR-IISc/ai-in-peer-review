

This paper addresses the estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is partially unknown. It adopts a selection rule from Entner et al. (2013) for identifying covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths, but notes this rule relies on the faithfulness assumption for its justification. The authors propose applying this rule to derive bounds on the ACE rather than point estimates, leveraging methods from Ramsahai (2012).  

While I have not fully parsed all technical details, the core concept appears sound, novel, and potentially valuable. However, I remain puzzled by the role of Entner et al.’s conditions in this framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions do not clearly constrain the causal structure. My tentative interpretation is that verified conditions might inform relaxation parameters, though the paper explicitly dismisses this connection. It seems the method’s robustness in experiments may not critically depend on these conditions, and I would appreciate clarification on this point.  

Minor comments:  
1. Section 3, first paragraph: "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is misleading. Unfaithfulness involves *additional* independence, not violations; the framework likely aims to model *bounded deviations* from independence.  
2. In experiments, reporting Entner et al.’s method performance under their stringent thresholds (treating "don’t know" as uninformative bounds) would provide useful context.  
3. On p. 8, second paragraph: If X=1 denotes flu shot receipt and Y=1 denotes no hospitalization, a negative ACE would indicate reduced hospitalization post-vaccination, implying efficacy. The current phrasing ("desirable vaccine") is confusing.  
4. Table 1 (top of p. 8) is cited as Table 5 in the text.  

This is an interesting contribution, though the technical details are challenging to follow.
