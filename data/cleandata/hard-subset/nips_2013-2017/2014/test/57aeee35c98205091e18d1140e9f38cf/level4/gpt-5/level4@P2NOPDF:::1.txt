This paper addresses estimation of the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over covariates is only partially known. It builds on a selection rule from Entner et al. (2013) intended to identify covariate sets that block all back-door paths—a rule that, in the original work, is justified only under the faithfulness assumption. Here, the authors repurpose that rule to produce bounds rather than point estimates of the ACE, following an approach in the spirit of Ramsahai (2012).

I have not fully worked through every technical detail, but the central idea appears sound, interesting, and practically useful. What remains unclear to me is the precise role of Entner et al.’s conditions in the proposed framework. Without faithfulness, those conditions do not obviously constrain the causal graph. My best guess is that, when they hold, they could guide the choice of relaxation parameters; however, the paper explicitly sets that issue aside. Consequently, it seems that the core machinery may not materially depend on Entner et al.’s conditions. If so, could the observed robustness in the experiments be largely independent of those conditions? A clarification of how, if at all, the Entner rule influences the method (and its guarantees) would be helpful.

Minor comments:
1. Section 3, first paragraph: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” reads oddly. Unfaithfulness typically involves additional conditional independences, not violations of independence. It seems the framework instead aims to allow “near-violations” of faithfulness by bounding the magnitude of dependencies. Please clarify the phrasing.
2. Experiments: It would be useful to also report Entner et al.’s method with their stricter thresholds, treating “don’t know” as yielding uninformative bounds, to provide a clearer comparison.
3. Page 8, second paragraph: If X=1 means the patient received a flu shot and Y=1 means the patient was not hospitalized, why would a negative ACE indicate a desirable vaccine? Please clarify the sign convention and interpretation.
4. The table at the top of p. 8 is labeled “Table 1” but is cited as “Table 5” in the text. Please fix the mismatch.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with a promising approach, though the exposition is dense in places. Clearer discussion of how the Entner rule interacts with the relaxation parameters and where faithfulness is used or relaxed would improve readability and interpretability.
