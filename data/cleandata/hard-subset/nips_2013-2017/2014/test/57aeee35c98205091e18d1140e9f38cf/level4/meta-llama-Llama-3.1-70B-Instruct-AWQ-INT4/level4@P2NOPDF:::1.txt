This paper addresses the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure of the covariates is not fully specified. It builds upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) that aims to select a set of observed covariates sufficient to block all back-door paths. However, Entner et al.'s rule is justified under the faithfulness assumption. The authors of this paper propose using the rule to derive bounds for the ACE rather than point estimates and develop a method following Ramsahai (2012).

The main idea of the paper is intriguing and appears to be useful, although some technical details remain unclear to me. What I find puzzling is the role of the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule in this approach. Without the faithfulness assumption, these conditions do not have obvious implications for the causal structure. My best guess is that the conditions, if verified, are relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly sets this matter aside. It seems that the methodology does not crucially depend on the conditions in Entner et al.'s rule, and I wonder if the apparent robustness of the method in the experiments is related to this. I would appreciate clarification on this issue.

Some minor comments are as follows:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" sounds unusual. Isn't unfaithfulness a matter of allowing extra independence rather than violating independence? It appears that the proposed framework is intended to allow or express "almost violations" of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependence.

2. In the experiments, it may be helpful to also report the performance of Entner et al.'s method when their stringent thresholds are used, treating the answer "don't know" as reporting uninformative bounds.

3. In the second paragraph on page 8, the statement "X=1 means the patient got a flu shot, Y=1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized" seems to imply that a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine. However, this is unclear.

4. "Table 1" on top of page 8 is incorrectly cited as "Table 5" in the text.

This is an interesting paper, although the details can be challenging to follow.
