This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables when the causal structure over observed covariates is uncertain. It builds upon a rule proposed by Entner et al. (2013) for selecting a sufficient set of covariates to block back-door paths, but relaxes the faithfulness assumption required for the original justification of that rule. The authors propose using the rule to derive bounds on the ACE, employing a method inspired by Ramsahai (2012).

I found the central idea to be both sensible and potentially valuable. While I admit not fully grasping every technical detail, the overall approach appears promising. However, I was somewhat puzzled by the precise role of the conditions in Entner et al.’s rule within this framework. Without the faithfulness assumption, those conditions do not directly inform the causal structure. My understanding is that they might be relevant to the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper largely defers discussion of this point. It seems the method isn't critically dependent on those specific conditions, and I wonder if the observed robustness in the experiments is related to this. A clarification on this connection would be appreciated.

Several minor points could enhance the clarity of the paper:

1. In the first paragraph of Section 3, the phrasing “…express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence…” feels slightly inaccurate. Unfaithfulness involves *allowing* additional independencies, not necessarily *violating* existing ones. The framework seems more accurately described as accommodating “near-violations” of faithfulness by constraining the magnitude of dependencies.

2. In the experimental section, including the performance of Entner et al.’s original method—reporting “don't know” when their stringent thresholds aren’t met—would provide a useful baseline for comparison.

3. In the second paragraph on page 8, the statement that "Y = 1 indicates the patient was not hospitalized” contradicts the subsequent claim that a negative ACE suggests a desirable vaccine. This seems reversed; Y=1 should ideally indicate hospitalization for the negative ACE to imply vaccine efficacy.

4. There is a citation inconsistency: “Table 1” appears on page 8, but is referenced as “Table 5” in the text.

Despite these minor concerns, this is an interesting and thoughtful paper, although its technical details require careful study. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Estimating causal effects with unobserved confounders is a fundamental challenge in observational studies.
*   **Novel approach:** The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption and the use of bounds, rather than point estimates, offer a potentially more robust solution.
*   **Sound methodology:** The combination of techniques from causal inference, linear programming, and Bayesian inference appears well-motivated.
*   **Empirical validation:** The experiments, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the method in both synthetic and real-world settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Technical complexity:** The paper is dense and requires significant effort to fully understand.
*   **Role of Entner et al.'s rule unclear:** The connection between the conditions in Entner et al.’s rule and the proposed method is not fully explained.
*   **Limited scope of experiments:** The experimental validation is relatively limited and could benefit from more extensive testing.
