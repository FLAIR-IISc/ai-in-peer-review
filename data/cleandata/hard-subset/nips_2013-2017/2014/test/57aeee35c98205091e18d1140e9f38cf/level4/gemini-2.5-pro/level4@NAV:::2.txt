This paper presents a novel method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between two variables, X and Y. The approach is founded on three key principles: the inverse application of Entner's Rule 1, a relaxation of the faithfulness assumption to permit a degree of path cancellation, and the introduction of novel assumptions expressed as inequality constraints. Based on these principles, the authors formulate a computational framework using linear programming to derive the ACE bounds. Although the proposed algorithm has not been optimized for performance, the initial results demonstrate remarkably tight and precise bounds when compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.

### Quality
The authors set out to develop a novel method for deriving tighter ACE bounds than current approaches, and by combining several plausible principles, they have successfully achieved this goal. This makes the paper's contribution compelling.

However, the criteria for defining the bounds lack sufficient explanation. Specifically, the justification for applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not adequately discussed. This inverse application appears to necessitate the explicit assumption of the graphical structure depicted in Figure 2, a requirement that should be clearly stated and rigorously defended.

Furthermore, the paper does not provide general criteria for selecting the relaxation parameters. Given their significant impact on the final bounds, a principled methodology for setting these parameters requires further investigation and should be incorporated into the work.

### Clarity
The theoretical exposition is largely clear, notwithstanding the crucial omissions noted in the Quality section.

In contrast, the presentation of the experimental results is lacking in detail. As noted earlier, the algorithm's implementation is not fully described, and the parameters presented in the results table (e.g., Table 1) are not adequately defined.

The manuscript also contains several typographical errors, such as incorrect table references:
- Page 6 refers to "Table 1" for the full algorithm, but no such table exists.
- Page 8 refers to "Table 5" for a summary of results, which appears to be a reference to Table 1.

### Originality
As the authors acknowledge, the problem of bounding ACE is well-established in the literature. Consequently, the problem setting itself is not novel.

The novelty lies in the synthesis of the three proposed principles into a unified framework. However, as previously mentioned, the theoretical soundness of this combination is not yet sufficiently justified in the paper.

### Significance
The proposed method demonstrates significant potential by producing tighter ACE bounds. However, its performance has only been validated on a limited set of experiments. Consequently, the generalizability of the approach, both theoretically and across a wider range of empirical settings, has not been rigorously established.

This work tackles the important problem of quantifying the bounds on causal strength. To realize its potential impact, the validity of the core ideas must be substantiated with stronger theoretical arguments and more comprehensive experimental validation.
