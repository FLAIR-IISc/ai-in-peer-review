### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, designed to compute bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) while accounting for potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The method introduces a set of relaxation parameters to constrain unfaithful interactions. It then employs a linear programming framework to solve for the upper and lower bounds on the ACE for a specific causal link, adjusting for an appropriate set of covariates subject to these constraints.

The problem of inference under potential faithfulness violations is receiving significant attention, making this paper both timely and relevant. I find the approach and the core ideas compelling; it is a principled attempt to explicitly quantify the impact of unfaithful interaction patterns by leveraging well-established mathematical optimization tools.

However, the practical utility of the method is not yet established. The output—a distribution over bounds that are a function of 'unfaithful relaxations'—is conceptually difficult to interpret. It is unclear how to assess the likelihood of these unfaithful instances, and consequently, the relevance or realism of the resulting bounds. Furthermore, many relaxation settings lead to bounds so wide as to be uninformative. The method provides no guidance on a preferred point estimate within the derived interval, though end-users are likely to seek one.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The 'bias' metric used is inappropriate for comparing WPP to its competitors, as they measure fundamentally different quantities. Consequently, it is impossible to determine whether the WPP's output is a valuable addition for practitioners or merely a complex distraction. The performance on the influenza dataset is also concerning: the resulting ACE interval of (-0.23, 0.64) is too wide to permit any meaningful conclusions, although this could arguably be the most honest assessment possible given the data and assumptions.

### **Evaluation**

**Quality:** The proposed approach appears technically sound, with many details provided in the supplementary material (which I have not checked in detail). The core ideas are well-introduced, though the broader context is sometimes lost. A key limitation is that crucial aspects, such as the selection of relaxation parameters, are deferred to future work. This makes it difficult to fully assess the method's potential practical issues. The experimental evaluation lacks balance and robustness.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with the exception of the conclusion (see detailed comments). However, the algorithmic steps and the numerous, subtly different parameters are difficult to follow. This is partly due to a lack of detailed explanation for certain components of the method. While I believe I grasp the overall procedure, I am not confident I could implement the algorithm solely from the main paper's description (the supplement may be sufficient).

**Originality:** The method appears to be a novel combination of existing techniques. It is closely related to, yet distinct from, other recent work on this problem. As such, it has the potential to stimulate a fruitful cross-fertilization of ideas in this area.

**Significance:** The paper's primary significance lies in the ideas and direction it introduces, demonstrating how optimization techniques can be integrated to address this challenging problem. In its current form, the method seems unlikely to be a complete solution for practitioners, but some of the concepts employed in the WPP algorithm could prove influential in future work.

### **Summary of Review**

The paper addresses a relevant and highly topical problem with a technically interesting method that will likely find an audience. While the current application seems limited, the approach is likely extensible to more general cases. The paper is reasonably well-written, but the experimental evaluation is a major weakness, and the practical relevance and proper interpretation of the output bounds remain unclear.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   **p. 1, l. 5: 'Witness Protection Program'** – An excellent and memorable name for an algorithm.
*   **p. 1, l. 47:** The word 'unstable' is not precise here. These assumptions are stable but may simply not hold true in a given scenario.
*   **p. 2, l. 74:** What is meant by a 'cartoon representation'? Please clarify.
*   **p. 2, l. 93-94:** This framing also implicitly assumes causal sufficiency or knowledge that there is no unobserved confounder between W and X. This should be stated.
*   **p. 3, l. 127:** It would be helpful to explicitly state that the witness variable W is not needed in the final computation of the ACE itself, but only serves as a tool to identify the admissible adjustment set Z.
*   **p. 3, l. 143:** Typo: 'a useful'.
*   **p. 3, l. 144:** The phrase 'gives greater control over violations of faithfulness' is too vague. It is not clear what specific control is being provided to the user.
*   **p. 4, l. 164:** 'might' -> 'can'.
*   **p. 4, l. 164:** '(conditional)' -> This seems incorrect, as W is always dependent on U given X in this model. Perhaps this should be 'unconditional' or removed.
*   **p. 4, l. 172-180:** This section represents a very abrupt transition in the level of abstraction, making it difficult to follow for readers less familiar with the subject. Please provide more intuition or explanation for what these parameters represent.
*   **p. 4, l. 183:** Please link these parameters back to their original introduction in equation (1).
*   **p. 4, l. 185-189:** The constraint parameters need better interpretation; they could easily be misread as pertaining to weak interactions rather than unfaithful cancellations.
*   **p. 5, l. 221:** Please briefly describe the relationship between the search part of the algorithm and the bounds computation part. Also, please explain how the identification of admissible sets Z for a given W depends on the relaxation parameters theta.
*   **p. 5, l. 239:** The sentence 'Background ... used here' is unclear. How is this background knowledge used, and why is it important for the paper?
*   **p. 5, l. 259:** To be clear, do you mean a maximum of 10 free parameters to describe the entire joint distribution over Z, not a set Z composed of 10 variables? Please clarify.
*   **p. 6, l. 304:** The claim 'We know of no previous analytical bounds' may be too strong. The work seems closely related to recent papers like 'Estimating Causal Effects by Bounding Confounding' (Geiger et al., UAI 2014).
*   **p. 6, l. 305:** As a last-minute reviewer, I have not checked the supplementary material in detail.
*   **p. 6, l. 319-332:** I am skeptical of the practical efficacy of the back-substitution process for refining bounds. It seems rare that one could exploit such constraint violations in practice. Can you provide some indication in Section 5 of how effective this step is?
*   **p. 7, l. 350:** 'more than one bound' – Is this because different witnesses can yield different admissible sets Z, each resulting in a different bound? Please clarify.
*   **p. 7, l. 357:** The statement 'comparison is not straightforward' is an understatement. The subsequent example of a trivial optimal case demonstrates that comparing methods based on the chosen bias metric is meaningless.
*   **p. 7, l. 360:** 'only about 30% of the interval (-1,1)' – An interval covering (0, 0.6) is still extremely wide, spanning everything from a strong positive causal link to no causal link at all.
*   **p. 7, l. 369:** The use of '5000 points' is noted. However, typical medical trials often have far less data (e.g., 500 records). I am concerned about the impact of smaller sample sizes on the width of the bounds.
*   **p. 8, Table 1:** It is impossible to gauge any meaningful information from Table 1 regarding the benefit of using WPP over a standard point estimate that assumes faithfulness.
*   **p. 8, l. 391:** Stating that 'WPP is quite stable' seems to be a euphemism for the fact that the bounds it derives are so loose that they are insensitive to whether the underlying problem is identifiable or not.
*   **p. 8, l. 406-408:** The statement 'This does not mean ... within the bounds' is a crucial point for interpretation. Many casual readers will misinterpret the bounds as a confidence interval around a best-guess value. This highlights the difficulty of using WPP results in practice: what do these bounds actually mean?
*   **p. 8, l. 421+:** The conclusion uses emotive language (e.g., 'risky Bayesian approaches,' 'WPP bounds keep inference more honest') that suggests a biased perspective and potentially misrepresents competing methods. This should be toned down. The claim that WPP is a 'compromise' is also inaccurate; it focuses on an additional, orthogonal aspect of the inference problem.
*   **p. 8, l. 421+:** The phrase 'and purely theory-driven analyses that refuse to look at competing models' is unduly dismissive, and it is unclear what specific work is being referenced here.
*   **p. 9, Ref 9:** Typo in 'influenza'.
