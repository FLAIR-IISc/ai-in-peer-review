This paper proposes a new method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of X on Y. The approach rests on three elements: using Entner’s Rule 1 in the reverse direction, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow limited path cancellation, and introducing inequality-based assumptions. Under these principles, the ACE bounds are computed via linear programming. Although the algorithm is not yet optimized, the reported bounds appear narrow and competitive with the state of the art.

Quality
- The goal of obtaining tighter ACE bounds than existing approaches is valuable. The chosen ingredients are feasible to combine and, in the reported cases, do yield tighter bounds. In this respect, the paper is interesting.
- However, some criteria that define the bounds are insufficiently explained. In particular, the justification for using Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction is unclear. To employ it this way, the paper effectively assumes the causal structure depicted in Fig. 2. This structural assumption should be stated explicitly, its plausibility discussed, and its implications for identifiability and robustness analyzed.
- In addition, there is no general guidance for choosing the relaxation parameters governing faithfulness violations/path cancellation. Because these parameters can substantially affect the bounds, the paper should provide principled selection criteria (e.g., sensitivity analyses, domain-informed priors, or data-driven calibration) and characterize how the bounds vary with these choices.

Clarity
- The theoretical exposition is largely clear, aside from the essential discussion above.
- The experimental section is under-specified. The algorithmic details needed to reproduce the results are incomplete, and the definitions of the parameters listed in Table 1 (possibly referred to as Table 5 in the text) are unclear. Please define every parameter, specify how each is set in the experiments, and detail the linear program’s constraints.
- There are also apparent editorial errors:
  - “The full algorithm is shown in Table 1.” (p. 6) — the algorithm table is missing.
  - “Results are summarized in Table 5.” (p. 8) — Table 5 is not present (this likely refers to Table 1).
  These should be corrected so that all references resolve.

Originality
- The problem has been studied previously, as acknowledged, so the scope is not highly original.
- The specific combination of (reverse) Entner’s Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness with path cancellation, and inequality-based assumptions within a single linear-program framework carries some originality. Still, the paper needs a clearer argument for why these components can be combined without inconsistency, under what structural assumptions, and how each contributes to tightening the bounds.

Significance
- Achieving tighter ACE bounds is important, and the reported improvements are promising.
- Nonetheless, the empirical validation is limited. The paper would benefit from broader experiments (synthetic studies varying DAG structures, confounding strength, and sample sizes; and analyses on multiple real datasets) and a more systematic comparison to baselines. An ablation study isolating the impact of each assumption set would be particularly informative.
- More generally, the theoretical generality of the approach is not fully assessed. The paper should formalize the conditions under which the linear program yields sound bounds given the relaxed assumptions and characterize failure modes when the inverse use of Entner’s Rule 1 or the path-cancellation assumptions are violated.

Conclusion
This work addresses an important problem and likely advances the state of the art in ACE bounding, but it lacks crucial justification for its key assumptions and sufficient experimental and presentation clarity. I encourage the authors to: (1) explicitly state and analyze the structural assumptions enabling the inverse application of Entner’s Rule 1; (2) provide principled methods for selecting relaxation parameters and conduct sensitivity analyses; (3) expand and clarify the empirical evaluation; and (4) fix the editorial issues.
