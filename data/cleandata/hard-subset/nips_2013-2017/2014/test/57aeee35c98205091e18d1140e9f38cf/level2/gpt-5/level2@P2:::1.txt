Summary
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data by exploiting conditional independencies while relaxing the faithfulness assumption. It inverts Entner et al.’s “Rule 1” (which uses conditional independences to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment) to instead generate candidate “witness” variables that act like weakened instruments, and introduces tunable parameters governing bounded violations of faithfulness (allowing path cancellations and limited dependence). Bounds on the ACE are computed via a linear programming formulation building on Balke–Pearl, Dawid, and Ramsahai, and the method provides a posterior distribution over these bounds using a constrained-Dirichlet Bayesian treatment. To address computational cost, the paper derives new algebraic (symbolic) inequalities and a back-substitution/message-passing style algorithm that yields fast, though conservative, bounds. Synthetic experiments and an influenza vaccination application illustrate that WPP can yield substantially tighter bounds than standard IV while being more robust than naive back-door adjustments based solely on faithfulness.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to (i) standard IV bounds for binary models (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai), (ii) causal discovery under faithfulness/strong faithfulness (Spirtes et al.; Meek), and (iii) admissible-set discovery (Entner et al.). It connects to algebraic/graphical constraint methods in marginalized DAGs (Evans; Richardson & Robins), and to partial-identification perspectives (Manski). The novelty lies in systematically relaxing faithfulness in a controlled way to trade point identification for bounds, together with a Bayesian posterior over bounds and computational shortcuts.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A principled bridge between faithfulness-based point identification and IV-based bounding, via explicit, interpretable relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) that map approximate independencies into bounded violations.
- Soundness: The LP-based construction and the conditioning on Z per witness are grounded in established nonparametric IV machinery; the resulting bounds reduce to known cases (standard IV, chain model) under special parameter settings.
- Practical Bayesian workflow: Constrained-Dirichlet priors over observed contingency tables and posterior sampling over bounds acknowledge finite-sample uncertainty and model falsification.
- Computational advances: New symbolic inequalities and the back-substitution scheme provide substantial speedups, with a clear account of the conservativeness introduced.
- Empirical illustration: Synthetic studies show robustness relative to naive estimators; the influenza example demonstrates tighter bounds than standard IV, reflecting information in observed independencies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Choice of relaxation parameters: The core trade-off hinges on w, x, y, β, β̄, yet the paper offers limited, concrete guidance for selecting them from data or domain knowledge. Without principled calibration (e.g., mapping strength-of-independence tests to parameter magnitudes), practitioners may either obtain overly wide or overconfident bounds.
- Reliance on approximate independencies: The search often proposes witnesses even in scenarios with no true admissible set. While WPP is designed to absorb small violations, the risk of systematically misleading “witnesses” needs more diagnostic tooling (e.g., posterior falsification rates, sensitivity curves).
- Bayesian computation details: Rejection sampling for constrained Dirichlet can be inefficient; the “falsification test” (95% rejection) is ad hoc. The switch to using posterior expected contingency tables for a final tight bound, and the fallback when expectations are infeasible, complicate the inferential story and may affect coverage.
- Evaluation metrics: The main metric (distance to the true ACE, zero if interval covers) does not report interval widths prominently; hence comparisons with point estimators are hard to interpret without detailed width/coverage trade-offs. More systematic width–coverage curves and sensitivity to parameter choices would strengthen claims.
- Scope and scalability: The approach assumes small Z (≤10); guidance for higher-dimensional W (beyond brute-force or discrete PCA) and larger problems is limited. The impact of the back-substitution relaxation on bound tightness is mostly deferred to the supplement.

Quality
Technically careful and connects correctly to prior theory. The bounding construction appears valid under the stated relaxations, with reductions to known cases providing checks. However, the practical calibration of relaxation parameters and the Bayesian computational choices need clearer justification and diagnostics to ensure the promised robustness in realistic settings.

Clarity
The paper is readable but dense. The motivation for inverting Rule 1 is well conveyed. Notation is heavy; more intuition for the relaxation parameters and an explicit mapping from empirical independence assessments to parameter choices would help. The algorithmic pipeline is clear, but the switch between sampling-based bounds and bounds at posterior expectations should be clarified.

Originality
High. Relating approximate faithfulness to bounded path cancellations and using this to derive IV-like bounds conditional on witnesses is novel. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution procedure are also original and potentially useful beyond this setting.

Significance
Promising practical impact: many observational studies rely on faithfulness and/or ad hoc adjustment; this work offers a principled alternative that acknowledges uncertainty and provides partial identification. Adoption will hinge on providing better guidance and tools for parameter selection and diagnostics.

Constructive suggestions
- Provide a principled calibration scheme for w, x, y, β, β̄, e.g., linking Bayes factors or p-values from conditional independence tests to credible ranges for these parameters, or using prior elicitation from domain experts with sensitivity plots.
- Report coverage, average width, and width–coverage trade-offs across parameter grids; include ablation comparing full LP vs back-substitution on both tightness and runtime.
- Strengthen falsification diagnostics: prior-to-posterior rejection rates, posterior predictive checks for inequalities, and automated flags when multiple witnesses yield contradictory bounds.
- Consider more efficient sampling over constrained simplices (e.g., hit-and-run, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo with inequality constraints) to avoid high rejection rates.
- Clarify the decision to compute “final tight bounds” using posterior expectations and its implications; alternatively, aggregate bounds across posterior samples (as initially proposed) and report corresponding uncertainty summaries.
- Expand real-data validation and domain-expert elicitation of relaxation parameters; compare to recent bounding confounding methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated compromise between point identification and pure IV bounding.
- Solid theoretical grounding, with reductions to known cases and new symbolic constraints.
- Practical Bayesian workflow and software availability.
- Empirical results indicate robustness and useful tightening of bounds.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient guidance on selecting relaxation parameters; results may be sensitive.
- Evaluation under-emphasizes interval width and coverage; limited real-data validation.
- Some ad hoc choices in Bayesian computation and witness selection under approximate independencies.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The conceptual contribution and methodological innovation are strong and relevant to NeurIPS. With improved parameter calibration, diagnostics, and evaluation of width–coverage trade-offs, this work could have significant impact on practice.
