Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of estimating the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data when confounding may be present and the faithfulness assumption may be only approximately valid. Building on Entner et al. (AISTATS 2013), the authors “invert” Rule 1: rather than using a witness W and admissible set Z to perform back-door adjustment under faithfulness, they treat W as an instrument conditional on Z and derive bounds on ACE that are robust to bounded violations of faithfulness. These violations are parameterized by relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that constrain local deviations from conditional independencies and from instrumental-variable assumptions. Bounds are computed via a linear programming formulation that generalizes Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012) and reduces to Balke–Pearl bounds in a special case. The paper further proposes (i) a Bayesian procedure that places constrained Dirichlet priors on observed contingency tables and returns a posterior over ACE bounds via rejection sampling, and (ii) algebraic, symbolic inequalities and a back-substitution/message-passing-like algorithm that expedites feasibility checks and approximate bound computation. Synthetic experiments compare against naive back-door estimators and Entner et al.’s faithfulness-based point estimator; a flu vaccination dataset is revisited, yielding narrower bounds than standard IV.

This work bridges causal discovery (PC/faithfulness) with partial identification and IV bounds (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Manski). It also relates to recent work on bounding confounding (e.g., Geiger et al., UAI 2014) and graphical inequality constraints (Evans 2014).

Strengths
- Novel compromise between strict faithfulness (point identification) and unconstrained IV (often wide bounds), via interpretable relaxation parameters. The approach interpolates known regimes (chain model and standard IV).
- Formalization as linear programs with clear special cases, plus symbolic inequalities that provide intuition and speedups.
- Bayesian treatment that acknowledges sampling uncertainty and avoids overconfident point estimates under near-unfaithfulness.
- Careful experimental setup that includes scenarios with and without valid witnesses, and a real-world application with publicly discussed limitations of standard IV.
- Practical contribution: code and a workflow (search for witnesses/admissible sets; compute posterior over bounds; falsify if constraints frequently violated).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Choice of relaxation parameters: The paper defers principled selection of (w, y, x, β, β̄) to the supplement and future work. Because these parameters control the tightness and validity of bounds, more guidance is needed. Suggestions:
  - Provide monotonicity guarantees and calibration plots showing how width and coverage change as each parameter varies.
  - Offer elicitation templates (e.g., “at most 10% path cancellation on W→Y after conditioning on X,Z”) and default ranges justified by domain knowledge or sensitivity analysis.
  - Explore data-driven checks using testable implications (inequalities) to rule out overly optimistic parameter settings.
- Bayesian posterior over bounds: Rejection sampling from constrained Dirichlet can have high rejection rates; the 95% rejection “falsification” threshold is ad hoc. Consider hit-and-run or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo inside polytopes, and report effective sample sizes and acceptance rates. Clarify that the posterior is over bounds, not over ACE, and discuss how users should summarize or combine multiple bound posteriors across (W,Z) pairs.
- Witness detection under approximate independencies: In “no-witness” scenarios, the procedure still proposes witnesses in >90% of cases (due to relaxed testing). This tests robustness but may also yield anti-conservative narrowing. Report the empirical false-positive rate for witness detection, and study how it affects bound coverage. Tighten the Bayes model selection step (vary BDeu ESS, Bayes factors) and report sensitivity.
- Evaluation metrics: In addition to distance-to-interval metrics, report empirical coverage probabilities of the true ACE and average interval widths across settings and parameter choices. Include runtime comparisons for full LP vs. back-substitution, and quantify how much width is sacrificed by the analytical relaxations.
- Scope limitations: Methods assume binary variables. Discuss extensions to multi-valued/categorical or continuous variables (e.g., via discretization or alternative parameterizations) and whether algebraic bounds generalize.
- Theoretical properties: The paper hints at nesting properties (bounds widen as parameters relax). Formal statements and proofs (or pointers) would strengthen the contribution. Also, clarify and prove the claim that bounds are at least as conservative as the back-door adjusted estimator of Entner et al. under the proposed relaxations.

Quality
- Generally sound: constraints reduce to known results in special cases; the LP approach is standard for partial identification. The symbolic inequalities and back-substitution are reasonable relaxations, though they are not tight. More formal analysis of coverage/validity under mis-specified witnesses would improve confidence.
- Empirics support the stability claim, but additional diagnostics (coverage, width, runtime) would make the case stronger.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but largely clear. The roles of η, δ, ζ, κ, ω and their “starred” latent versions could be summarized in a concise table. Providing a step-by-step toy example (with numbers) showing how constraints and bounds are computed would aid readability. A pseudocode block for the back-substitution algorithm would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The inversion of Entner’s Rule 1 to generate instrument-like witnesses under bounded faithfulness violations, together with a posterior over bounds and symbolic relaxations, appears novel and timely.

Significance
- If adopted, this could materially improve practice in observational studies by offering honest yet informative bounds that reflect both structure learned from data and controlled deviations from faithfulness. The sensitivity framework and computational tools are likely to be useful beyond the specific setting.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide theoretical guarantees of monotonicity/nesting of bounds in each relaxation parameter?
- How sensitive are results to the witness search prior (BDeu ESS) and to model selection thresholds?
- What is the empirical coverage of the posterior-mean bounds across simulations? How often does the true ACE lie outside the WPP intervals as parameters vary?
- Can the back-substitution method be used alone in large problems with acceptable loss vs. full LP?

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, original methodological contribution tying together causal discovery, partial identification, and Bayesian uncertainty quantification.
- Practical algorithms and code; plausible impact on applied causal inference.
- Careful connection to prior literature; special-case reductions are correct and informative.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited guidance and validation for choosing relaxation parameters; risk of user-induced overconfidence.
- Incomplete evaluation of coverage/width/runtime trade-offs; reliance on ad hoc falsification threshold in Bayesian step.
- Binary-variable restriction and lack of generalization discussion in the main text.

Overall evaluation
A strong and thought-provoking contribution to causal inference under realistic violations of faithfulness. With additional guidance on parameter elicitation, stronger diagnostics for the Bayesian component, and expanded empirical evaluation (coverage and runtime), this would be a very solid NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
