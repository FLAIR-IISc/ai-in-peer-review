

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel approach to causal inference that addresses the challenge of estimating average causal effects (ACE) in observational studies with unobserved confounding. The authors relax the faithfulness assumption commonly used in causal discovery, allowing for "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but don't rule out confounding paths. The core contribution is a method to generate posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE using linear programming and Bayesian inference, which the authors argue should complement existing tools in observational studies.

The paper builds on Entner et al.'s Rule 1 for identifying admissible sets, but reinterprets it to use witness variables as instrumental variables rather than for back-door adjustment. The authors introduce a parameterization of the joint distribution that allows for bounded violations of faithfulness (via parameters w, y, x, β, β̄), then derive bounds on ACE using linear programming. They also develop a Bayesian framework for uncertainty quantification and an efficient back-substitution algorithm for computing bounds.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to causal inference by providing a principled way to handle violations of faithfulness, which is a significant limitation in current causal discovery methods. The relaxation of faithfulness is well-motivated and the approach provides a meaningful middle ground between point estimates (relying on faithfulness) and worst-case bounds (using instrumental variables). The Bayesian framework for uncertainty quantification is a valuable addition, and the back-substitution algorithm offers a computationally efficient alternative to full linear programming. The experimental results on synthetic data and the influenza vaccination study demonstrate that WPP produces reasonable bounds that are more informative than standard instrumental variable approaches in cases where faithfulness is violated.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks sufficient discussion of how to choose the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) in practice. The authors acknowledge this in the supplemental material but provide no concrete guidance for practitioners, which significantly limits the method's applicability. Without clear guidelines for parameter selection, the method's utility is questionable.

2. The computational complexity of the approach is not adequately addressed. While the back-substitution algorithm is introduced as efficient, the paper doesn't provide concrete timing comparisons with other methods or discuss scalability to larger problems. This is particularly concerning given the authors' own admission that the linear programming approach "can be computationally expensive."

3. The real-world example with influenza data is underdeveloped. The authors report a posterior interval of [-0.10, 0.17] but don't clearly explain why this is more meaningful than the standard IV bounds [−0.23, 0.64] they report. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their method for researchers.

4. The paper's connection to prior work could be strengthened. While the authors cite relevant causal inference literature, they don't sufficiently contrast their approach with recent alternatives like the methods of Geiger et al. [8] or the information-theoretic approach of [8]. This weakens the claim of originality.

5. The experimental evaluation is limited to small-dimensional problems (with |W| = 8), which doesn't demonstrate the method's utility for more complex real-world scenarios where causal inference is most needed.

**Quality:** The theoretical framework is sound, and the authors provide a clear derivation of their bounds. However, the lack of guidance on parameter selection and limited empirical validation reduce the paper's quality as a practical contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and generally clear, though it assumes significant prior knowledge of causal inference concepts. The algorithm descriptions are precise, but the practical implementation details would benefit from more concrete examples.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by relaxing faithfulness in a principled way and providing bounds rather than point estimates. However, the core idea of using conditional independence constraints to identify instrumental variables has been explored in prior work, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from these existing methods.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the method could be valuable for practitioners who want to be more cautious about faithfulness assumptions. However, the lack of clear parameter selection guidance and limited empirical validation prevent this from being a transformative contribution to the field.

The paper would be strengthened by providing more concrete guidance for practitioners on choosing relaxation parameters, demonstrating scalability to larger problems, and more thoroughly comparing with recent alternatives. The authors should also expand the real-world example to better illustrate the practical utility of their method.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to causal inference by providing a principled way to handle violations of faithfulness. However, the lack of practical guidance for parameter selection and limited empirical validation prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. With more thorough treatment of the implementation details and empirical validation, this could be a significant contribution. As it stands, it's a good but not outstanding paper that would be better suited for a more specialized statistics or causal inference venue.
