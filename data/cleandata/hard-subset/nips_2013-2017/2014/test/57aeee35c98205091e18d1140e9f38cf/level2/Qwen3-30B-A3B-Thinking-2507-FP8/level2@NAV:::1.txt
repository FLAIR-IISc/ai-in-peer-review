

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel approach for causal inference that relaxes the faithfulness assumption to provide bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) rather than point estimates. The authors claim this method should be adopted in regular practice to complement existing tools in observational studies. The core contribution is a framework that exploits conditional independencies to identify "witness" variables and admissible sets, then uses linear programming and Bayesian inference to generate posterior distributions over bounds on ACE.

The paper is well-organized and provides a clear theoretical foundation, building on Entner et al.'s work while introducing meaningful relaxations of faithfulness. The Bayesian approach to handle uncertainty in bounds is particularly appealing, as it avoids the pitfalls of purely point-estimation methods. The authors demonstrate the method on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study, showing how WPP can produce tighter bounds than standard instrumental variable approaches by leveraging conditional independencies.

However, the paper has several significant limitations. The experimental evaluation is limited to only one real-world dataset, and the synthetic experiments don't convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of WPP over existing methods. While the authors claim their bounds are "more conservative" than point estimates, they don't provide sufficient evidence that these bounds are actually tighter or more useful in practice. The paper also lacks clear guidance on choosing the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), which is crucial for practitioners.

The theoretical contribution is novel and addresses an important gap in causal inference methodology, but the paper overstates its practical significance. The claim that this approach "should be used in regular practice" requires more substantial empirical validation across diverse datasets. The back-substitution algorithm is promising for computational efficiency, but the paper doesn't adequately justify why this approach is preferable to standard linear programming methods.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution with potential value for causal inference practitioners. With more comprehensive experimental validation, clearer parameter selection guidance, and stronger evidence of practical utility, this could be a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution but lacks sufficient evidence to support its broader practical claims.
