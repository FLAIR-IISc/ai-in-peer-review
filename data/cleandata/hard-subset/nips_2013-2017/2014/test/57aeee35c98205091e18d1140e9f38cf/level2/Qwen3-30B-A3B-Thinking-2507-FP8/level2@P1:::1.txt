

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies when confounding variables are present. The authors address a fundamental challenge in causal inference: estimating causal effects without knowing all confounders. They relax the faithfulness assumption commonly used in causal discovery to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that still imply conditional independencies but don't rule out confounding paths.

The paper's main contribution is a framework that exploits conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph, generating posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE through a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference. The authors build on Entner et al.'s work but provide a different interpretation of their search procedure to generate candidate instrumental variables, then modify the linear programming approach of Dawid and Ramsahai to generate bounds on the ACE.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong background section that effectively contextualizes the problem within causal inference literature. The mathematical development is rigorous, and the authors provide both theoretical bounds and a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm for practical implementation. The experimental section includes synthetic data studies and a real-world application to influenza vaccination data, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in both controlled settings and practical applications.

One of the paper's key strengths is its thoughtful approach to handling uncertainty. The Bayesian framework for modeling the posterior distribution over bounds provides a more honest representation of the uncertainty inherent in observational studies compared to point estimates based on faithfulness assumptions. The authors also provide useful insights into how different relaxation parameters affect the bounds, which could be valuable for practitioners.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by bridging the gap between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and purely bound-based approaches. As the authors note, their method provides a practical compromise that could become a standard tool in observational studies. The computational efficiency of the back-substitution algorithm is particularly noteworthy, making the method feasible for real-world applications.

However, the paper has a few limitations that could be addressed in future work. The experimental section could be strengthened with more detailed analysis of when the method works best and under what conditions it might fail. The paper mentions "Supplemental Material" multiple times but doesn't provide sufficient details about what's included, which makes it difficult to fully assess some aspects of the method. Additionally, while the paper discusses the choice of relaxation parameters, it doesn't provide clear guidance on how practitioners should select these parameters in practice.

The paper is well-positioned within the causal inference literature, building on but extending key works by Entner et al., Dawid, and Ramsahai. The authors carefully situate their work relative to existing literature and provide appropriate citations. The paper's focus on providing bounds rather than point estimates aligns well with the NIPS community's interest in principled approaches to uncertainty.

The significance of this work is substantial. Observational studies are ubiquitous in fields like healthcare, economics, and social sciences, where randomized controlled trials are often impractical or unethical. By providing a method that can give meaningful bounds on causal effects without requiring complete knowledge of confounders, the authors address a real and important problem. The method's ability to handle cases where faithfulness assumptions might fail is particularly valuable.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though some sections might be challenging for readers without a strong background in causal inference. The authors could consider adding a brief glossary of key terms or a more detailed explanation of the causal graph notation for broader accessibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution to causal inference that addresses a fundamental problem with a technically sound and practically useful approach. The paper is well-written, well-justified, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly regarding parameter selection guidance and experimental detail, these do not significantly detract from the paper's strengths.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written with clear organization (Clarity), provides a meaningful extension of existing work (Originality), and addresses a significant practical problem with potential for broad impact (Significance). I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the experimental detail and parameter selection guidance.
