- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for partial identification of the ACE by relaxing faithfulness and exploiting conditional independencies.
- Inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to generate candidate “witness” variables that act as weakened instruments under bounded faithfulness violations.
- Introduces tunable relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) to map approximate independencies into explicit bounded violations (path cancellations and limited dependence).
- Computes ACE bounds via a linear programming formulation building on Balke–Pearl, Dawid, and Ramsahai.
- Provides a constrained-Dirichlet Bayesian treatment to produce a posterior distribution over the computed bounds, acknowledging finite-sample uncertainty and model falsification.
- Derives new algebraic (symbolic) inequalities and a back-substitution/message-passing algorithm that yields much faster but conservative bounds.
- Synthetic experiments and an influenza vaccination case show WPP can give substantially tighter bounds than standard IV and be more robust than naive faithfulness-based back-door adjustments.
- Situates the work relative to standard IV literature, causal discovery under faithfulness, admissible-set discovery, algebraic/graphical constraint methods, and partial-identification perspectives.
- Novelty lies in systematically relaxing faithfulness to trade point identification for bounds plus a Bayesian posterior over bounds and computational shortcuts.
- Conceptual strength: principled bridge between faithfulness-based point ID and IV bounding with interpretable relaxation parameters.
- Soundness: LP construction and conditioning on Z per witness are grounded in established nonparametric IV theory and reduce to known cases in special settings.
- Practical workflow strength: constrained-Dirichlet priors and posterior sampling over bounds incorporate sampling uncertainty and model checks.
- Computational advances: symbolic inequalities and back-substitution provide substantial speedups and a clear account of conservativeness.
- Empirical illustration strength: demonstrates robustness and tighter bounds in realistic examples.
- Major weakness: limited guidance for choosing/calibrating relaxation parameters, risking overly wide or overconfident bounds.
- Risk from reliance on approximate independencies: potential for misleading witnesses and need for better diagnostics (e.g., falsification rates, sensitivity curves).
- Bayesian computation concerns: rejection sampling on constrained Dirichlet is inefficient; the 95% falsification test and use of posterior expected contingency tables are ad hoc and complicate inference/coverage.
- Evaluation gaps: primary metric masks interval widths; lacks systematic width–coverage trade-offs and sensitivity analyses across parameter grids.
- Scalability and scope issues: assumes small Z (≤10) with limited guidance for higher-dimensional settings; impact of back-substitution relaxation on tightness deferred.
- Quality: technically careful and connected to prior theory, but practical calibration and Bayesian choices need clearer justification and diagnostics.
- Clarity: readable but dense; notation heavy and mapping from empirical independence to parameter choices needs more intuition and clarity about sampling vs expectation-based bounds.
- Originality: high — novel linking of approximate faithfulness to bounded path cancellations and new algebraic/back-substitution methods.
- Significance: promising practical impact if better tools for parameter selection and diagnostics are provided.
- Constructive suggestions: provide principled calibration schemes, report coverage/width trade-offs, strengthen falsification diagnostics, adopt more efficient constrained sampling, clarify final-bound decision, expand real-data validation and elicitation.
- Pros for acceptance: novel compromise between point ID and IV bounds, solid theoretical grounding, practical Bayesian workflow, and empirical evidence of usefulness.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient guidance on relaxation parameter selection, under-emphasis on interval width and coverage, ad hoc Bayesian/witness selection choices.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept — strong conceptual and methodological contribution but needs improved calibration, diagnostics, and evaluation to ensure practical impact.
