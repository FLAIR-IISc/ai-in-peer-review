This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel methodology for estimating bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data. The core idea is to bridge the gap between methods that rely on the strong, and potentially fragile, faithfulness assumption for point identification (e.g., Entner et al., 2013) and instrumental variable (IV) methods that produce wide bounds by making fewer assumptions. The proposed approach re-purposes the witness/admissible set search procedure of Entner et al. not for point estimation via back-door adjustment, but to motivate a bounding procedure. It relaxes the conditional independence assumptions used to find a witness, parameterizing the potential violations of faithfulness. These relaxations are incorporated as constraints in a linear programming framework (building on Dawid, 2003; Ramsahai, 2012) to derive bounds on the ACE.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper’s main contribution is a clever and significant synthesis of ideas from causal discovery and causal effect bounding. By providing a principled way to relax the faithfulness assumption, it offers a more robust tool for practitioners who suspect unmeasured confounding but have observational data suggesting approximate conditional independencies. This addresses a critical and practical problem in causal inference.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation as a constrained linear program is elegant, and the extension to a Bayesian framework for handling sampling uncertainty is appropriate. The inclusion of an efficient "back-substitution" algorithm based on derived algebraic bounds is a major strength, addressing the potential computational cost of the full numerical procedure.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-motivated. The background section clearly explains the limitations of existing methods, and the methodology is laid out logically. The experiments, though brief, convincingly demonstrate the method's stability compared to naive and faithfulness-based estimators.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Guidance on Relaxation Parameters:** The primary weakness is the limited discussion on how to choose the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). The authors acknowledge this and defer the discussion to supplemental material, but these parameters are crucial to the method's application. Some guidance or sensitivity analysis within the main paper would significantly strengthen it.
*   **Scalability of Witness Search:** While the back-substitution algorithm improves the speed of the bounding step, the initial search for a (witness, admissible set) pair remains a combinatorial problem. The paper suggests brute-force for small covariate sets, which may not be practical for many real-world datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and important contribution to the field of causal inference. The strengths, particularly the originality of the core idea and its technical execution, clearly outweigh the weaknesses. The work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
