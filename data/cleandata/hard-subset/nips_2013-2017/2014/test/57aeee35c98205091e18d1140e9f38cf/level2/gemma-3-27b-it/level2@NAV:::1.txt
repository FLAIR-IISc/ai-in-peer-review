## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies, addressing the common challenge of unobserved confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, interpreting violations as bounded deviations from conditional independence, and utilizing a linear programming approach combined with Bayesian inference to derive bounds on the ACE. The authors frame this as a “Witness Protection Program,” providing a degree of robustness against faithfulness violations by leveraging Entner et al.’s work on instrumental variables and admissible sets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying a critical problem in causal inference. The proposal to move beyond strict faithfulness assumptions is valuable, as this assumption is often fragile in practice. The combination of linear programming and Bayesian methods appears promising for generating more realistic and informative bounds compared to relying solely on point estimates. The inclusion of an analytical approximation and a fast algorithm attempt to address the computational complexity inherent in the approach, which is a definite strength. The experimental evaluation with both synthetic and real data (influenza vaccination) provides some evidence of the method's performance, although the limited scope of the real-world example and relatively basic synthetic setups leave room for more extensive validation.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the motivation is clear, the presentation is dense and requires significant background knowledge in causal inference, potentially hindering accessibility. The notation is heavy, and the jump between the conceptual explanation and the technical details of the linear programming formulation feels abrupt. The discussion of the parameterization (Section 3) feels somewhat disconnected and hard to follow. The sensitivity analysis of the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is limited. While the authors acknowledge computational costs, more detailed benchmarking and scalability analysis are needed.  The comparison to existing methods is also somewhat superficial. The claim of providing a "standard item in the toolbox" feels premature given the complexity and lack of comprehensive evaluation. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in causal inference.
*   Novel approach combining linear programming and Bayesian methods.
*   Addresses limitations of strict faithfulness assumptions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Dense presentation and heavy notation.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Insufficient discussion of parameter sensitivity and computational scalability.
*   Overstated claims of practical impact.

**Overall:**

The paper presents a technically interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. However, the density of the presentation, limited experimental evaluation, and overstated claims currently prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend **rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit**. The authors should focus on improving clarity, providing more comprehensive experimental results, and thoroughly analyzing the computational implications of their approach. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method and a more nuanced assessment of its practical impact would also be beneficial.
