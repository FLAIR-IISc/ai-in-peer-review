- Proposes Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound ACE by inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1, treating a witness W as a candidate IV conditional on Z.
- Relaxes faithfulness with interpretable local parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that bound path cancellations and deviations from independence.
- Constructs relaxed polytopes and uses linear programming (building on Dawid and Ramsahai) to compute ACE bounds.
- Introduces a Bayesian approach using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to produce a posterior over bounds and a pragmatic falsification test.
- Derives algebraic inequalities and a back-substitution algorithm to obtain fast, conservative box constraints that reduce LP usage.
- Experiments compare WPP to naive adjustment, no adjustment, and a faithfulness-based point estimator, plus an influenza encouragement design application.
- Shows WPP can yield tighter bounds than classic IV under modest relaxations and may select different witness/admissible sets.
- Unifies ideas from causal discovery, IV/partial identification, bounded-faithfulness robustness, and graphical inequality constraints.
- Conceptual contribution: offers a principled compromise between point-identification via faithfulness and worst-case IV bounds.
- Methodological rigor: formal LP formulation with explicit, interpretable relaxation parameters consistent with prior work.
- Computational innovation: symbolic inequalities and back-substitution provide scalable, conservative approximations.
- Bayesian uncertainty treatment avoids overconfidence from plug-in estimates and enables falsification checks.
- Practical relevance: addresses finite-sample fragility of faithfulness and demonstrates real-world utility on a flu study.
- Major weakness: results critically depend on user-chosen relaxation parameters, with limited principled guidance.
- Scope limitation: analysis restricted to binary variables; extensibility to multi-valued or continuous cases not shown.
- Search and multiplicity concerns: witness/admissible search can propose spurious witnesses and lacks control for multiple comparisons or aggregation across candidates.
- Calibration and coverage not systematically reported; empirical frequency with which true ACE falls within bounds is missing.
- Falsification rule is ad hoc (95% rejection) and its operating characteristics (Type I/II rates) are unanalyzed.
- Back-substitution can produce infeasible expected tables relative to the full LP; quantitative impact on bound width is unclear.
- Quality assessment: technically coherent and correct conservative algebraic bounds but needs deeper assessment of parameter selection and search multiplicity.
- Clarity: exposition is clear but dense; more numeric intuition for relaxation parameters would help.
- Originality: inversion of Rule 1, bounded-faithfulness parameterization, and back-substitution inequalities are novel and useful.
- Significance: important practical contribution to partial identification under bounded-faithfulness, though immediate impact limited by binary scope and parameter-selection gaps.
- Essential improvements requested: principled guidance for choosing relaxation parameters, empirical coverage studies, and analysis/controls for witness-search multiplicity.
- Nice-to-have suggestions: extensions to multi-valued/continuous variables, runtime/scalability benchmarks, comparisons to more baselines, and diagnostic plots for parameter effects.
- Pros for acceptance: novel, well-motivated framework with solid methodology and useful computational advances addressing an important problem.
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on user-chosen relaxations without guidance, limited empirical calibration and scope, and heuristic elements needing justification.
- Overall: technically substantial and thoughtful work that would be highly impactful with better parameter guidance, calibration, and handling of search multiplicity.
