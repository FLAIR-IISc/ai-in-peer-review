This paper introduces a novel methodology to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies, addressing the fundamental challenge of unobserved confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, allowing for “path cancellations” that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. The authors propose a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) which inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to identify instrumental variables, uses linear programming to generate bounds on the ACE, and provides relaxations for computational efficiency. The work is motivated by the desire to provide more robust bounds than those relying on strict faithfulness assumptions, and is well-connected to existing literature on instrumental variables, causal discovery, and causal inference.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption and the “Witness Protection Program” framework offer a potentially valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. It provides a more pragmatic approach than strictly requiring faithfulness, particularly in observational settings.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology builds upon established techniques (linear programming, instrumental variables) in a rigorous manner. The mathematical formulations appear well-defined, and the use of bounds is a sensible way to address identifiability issues.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions itself within the broader landscape of causal inference. It effectively references relevant work by Pearl, Spirtes et al., Entner et al., Dawid, Ramsahai, and others, demonstrating an understanding of the state-of-the-art.
* **Computational Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the computational challenges of the linear programming approach and propose relaxations to improve scalability, as well as a back-substitution algorithm.
* **Experiments:**  The paper includes both synthetic and real-world experiments, providing empirical support for the proposed methodology. The comparison with naive estimators (NE1, NE2) and the faithfulness-based approach demonstrates the potential benefits of the WPP.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Readability:** The paper is dense and technically challenging. While the authors attempt to provide background information, readers unfamiliar with causal inference may struggle to follow the reasoning. Section 3, in particular, is quite complex and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples. The notation is also quite heavy.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The approach relies on parameters *w, y, x, β, β̄* which control the degree of allowed violations of faithfulness. The paper acknowledges the need to choose these parameters, but the discussion of how to do so is limited, and the sensitivity of the results to these choices isn’t thoroughly investigated. The rationale for the default choices is also weak.
* **Computational Complexity:** Despite the proposed relaxations, the computational cost remains a concern, especially for high-dimensional datasets. The scaling properties of the back-substitution algorithm are not clearly demonstrated.  The reliance on packages like Polymake or SCDD might be a barrier for some researchers.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are a good starting point, they are limited in scope. The synthetic data generation process isn’t fully described, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results. The real-world example with influenza data is compelling, but lacks a more detailed comparison with other established methods in that domain.
* **Bayesian Implementation:** The description of the Bayesian learning approach is somewhat superficial.  Details regarding the Dirichlet priors and the rejection sampling procedure could be more thoroughly explained. The choice of effective sample size of 10 for the BDeu prior seems arbitrary.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to address a fundamental problem in causal inference.
* The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption is a significant contribution, offering a more pragmatic framework for observational studies.
* The computational considerations and proposed relaxations demonstrate a practical awareness of the challenges involved.
* The empirical results, while limited, suggest the potential benefits of the WPP.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper's clarity and readability could be significantly improved.
* The sensitivity of the methodology to the choice of relaxation parameters requires further investigation.
* The computational complexity remains a potential limitation.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and rigorous.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The novelty of the approach and the technical soundness of the methodology are strong points. However, the paper suffers from issues with clarity, parameter sensitivity, and empirical evaluation. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity and readability of the paper, conducting a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the relaxation parameters, and expanding the empirical evaluation. A more detailed discussion of the Bayesian implementation and the computational complexity would also be beneficial. If these revisions are addressed, the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the NIPS community.
