Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a partial-identification framework that bounds the average causal effect (ACE) under observational confounding by leveraging conditional independencies while relaxing faithfulness. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: treat a (witness, admissible-set) pair (W, Z) as inducing IV-like constraints conditional on Z, then introduce bounded violations of faithfulness via parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) to limit path cancellations. Bounds are obtained via a linear program over latent-response parameters (building on Balke & Pearl, Dawid, Ramsahai), with a Bayesian posterior over bounds using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling. To improve scalability and interpretability, the authors derive algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a back-substitution/message-passing-like procedure. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu vaccination encouragement dataset show that WPP can yield tighter, more robust intervals than standard IV bounds and naive back-door estimators when faithfulness is only approximate.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound and carefully tied to existing IV and partial-identification polytopes. The relaxation parameters provide a transparent way to interpolate between strict faithfulness and unconstrained confounding. However, key proofs and constraint derivations are deferred to the supplement, and the falsification/test procedure (95% rejection threshold) and CI decision rule (BDeu) are somewhat ad hoc. Bayesian sampling under constraints may be computationally heavy; sensitivity and coverage properties are not fully characterized.

Clarity: The paper is dense and notation-heavy; core ideas are understandable but many crucial details (polytopes, algebraic bounds) are relegated to the supplement. More guidance on interpreting and choosing relaxation parameters would help practitioners.

Originality: High. Inverting Rule 1 to generate IV-like bounds under controlled faithfulness violations and providing algebraic/back-substitution bounds appears novel and valuable.

Significance: Potentially impactful as a practical complement to IV and faithfulness-based point identification, especially in near-unfaithful regimes common in practice. Current empirical evaluation is promising but limited.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Provide principled strategies for selecting (w, x, y, β, β̄) (elicitation, empirical Bayes, calibration for coverage).
- Assess finite-sample properties (coverage, length) and sensitivity to CI testing errors.
- Compare against alternative partial-identification/sensitivity approaches (e.g., monotone IV/LATE, marginal sensitivity models).
- Extend beyond binary variables and report scalability/ablation of LP vs. back-substitution.
- Expand real-data evaluations and release reproducible code (link provided).

Arguments for acceptance: novel compromise between faithfulness and IV; clear theoretical grounding in polyhedral constraints; practical Bayesian posterior over bounds; promising empirical robustness.

Arguments against: parameter tuning lacks principled guidance; heavy reliance on supplement; limited real-data validation; computational concerns.

Overall: Weak accept for originality and potential, contingent on strengthening clarity and evaluation.
