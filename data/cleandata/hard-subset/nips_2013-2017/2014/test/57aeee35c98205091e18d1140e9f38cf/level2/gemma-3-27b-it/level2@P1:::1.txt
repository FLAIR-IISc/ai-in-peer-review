This paper introduces a novel approach to causal inference in observational studies, aiming to bound the average causal effect (ACE) when faced with potential confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, allowing for “path cancellations” that permit conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths, and then utilize linear programming and Bayesian inference to derive bounds on the ACE. The method, dubbed the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), leverages Entner et al.’s work on identifying admissible sets and modifies Dawid and Ramsahai’s linear programming framework. The authors also propose a fast, approximate algorithm and explore analytical bounds to improve computational efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental challenge in causal inference. The problem of confounding is clearly articulated, and the limitations of relying solely on the faithfulness assumption are convincingly argued. The proposed approach feels like a natural extension of existing work (Entner et al., Dawid, Ramsahai) and attempts to bridge the gap between strict faithfulness assumptions and potentially overly strong instrumental variable requirements. The introduction of the “witness” concept, building on Entner’s Rule 1, provides a helpful intuition for identifying potential instrumental variables. The inclusion of a Bayesian learning component and the exploration of algebraic bounds demonstrate a thorough attempt to address both statistical rigor and computational scalability. The experiments, while limited in scope, are well-conducted and provide preliminary evidence of the method's utility.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the presentation is dense and can be difficult to follow. While the authors acknowledge assuming familiarity with causal inference concepts, the level of detail and notation can be overwhelming for readers not deeply immersed in the field. More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples would significantly improve clarity. For instance, the connection between the “witness protection” metaphor and the technical details of the algorithm isn’t immediately apparent.

Secondly, the computational complexity remains a significant concern. Despite the proposed relaxations and approximations, the linear programming approach and the Bayesian sampling procedure could be prohibitive for large-scale datasets. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a sufficiently detailed analysis of the computational cost or practical scalability. The discussion of the back-substitution algorithm is a positive step, but its performance compared to the full linear programming approach needs further quantification.

Thirdly, the sensitivity analysis and choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) are not fully explored. The authors mention that any choice of these parameters is guaranteed to provide conservative bounds, but they don’t provide clear guidance on how to select appropriate values based on prior knowledge or data characteristics.  The impact of these parameters on the width of the bounds and the resulting usefulness of the inference is critical and warrants further investigation.  The empirical section mentions setting these parameters to 0.2, 0.9, and 1.1, but justification for these specific values is lacking.

Fourthly, the evaluation is limited. The synthetic experiments are useful for demonstrating the method’s behavior under controlled conditions, but they may not fully capture the complexities of real-world datasets. The application to the influenza data is a good starting point, but the analysis is somewhat superficial. A more rigorous comparison with existing causal inference methods (e.g., propensity score matching, inverse probability weighting) would strengthen the evaluation.

Finally, the discussion of the “falsification test” seems somewhat ad hoc. Using a 95% rejection threshold feels arbitrary, and its impact on the reliability of the results isn’t fully investigated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to causal inference that addresses a significant limitation of standard methods.
*   Solid theoretical foundation, building on existing work in the field.
*   Potential for providing more informative bounds on the ACE in the presence of confounding.
*   Well-designed experiments demonstrate the feasibility of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and difficult to follow presentation.
*   Significant computational complexity.
*   Limited exploration of sensitivity to relaxation parameters.
*   Limited evaluation and comparison with existing methods.
*   Ad-hoc falsification test.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The proposed method is innovative and has the potential to improve our ability to estimate causal effects in observational studies. However, the paper’s weaknesses, particularly its lack of clarity, computational concerns, and limited evaluation, prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the concerns raised in this review, particularly regarding clarity, computational scalability, and a more thorough empirical evaluation. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, a guide for selecting relaxation parameters, and a comparison with existing methods would significantly strengthen the paper.
