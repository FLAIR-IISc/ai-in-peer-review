- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound the average causal effect (ACE) under potential faithfulness violations using conditional independencies.  
- Uses linear programming and Bayesian inference to produce bounds and incorporates relaxation parameters to model “weak” paths.  
- Builds on Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2014), extending point-estimate approaches to provide interval bounds.  
- Conceptually interesting and principled: quantifies impact of unfaithfulness and allows prior knowledge via relaxation parameters.  
- Main weakness: conceptual opacity — the method’s interpretability heavily depends on user-chosen relaxation parameters (w,y,x,β,β̄) with little guidance.  
- The approach does not yield a preferred point estimate and risks users misinterpreting intervals as confidence intervals.  
- Bounds can be overly conservative and uninformative in practice (example: influenza ACE in [-0.23, 0.64]).  
- Experimental evaluation is unbalanced: synthetic studies lack clear comparative benchmarks and use an ill-defined “bias” metric.  
- Comparison to NE1/NE2 (naive backdoor) is inappropriate given WPP targets scenarios where backdoor fails; stronger baselines for unmeasured confounding are needed.  
- Technical derivations are largely sound and supplementary material is comprehensive.  
- Presentation issues: roles of witness W vs. admissible set Z are muddled, search step and relation to relaxation parameters unclear, and prose contains ambiguities/typos.  
- Must address related work by Geiger, Janzing, and Schölkopf (UAI 2014) when claiming novelty in analytical bounds.  
- The back-substitution refinement step’s effectiveness is unproven and needs empirical support.  
- Paper should clarify why multiple bounds arise (different witnesses/admissible sets) and assess sensitivity to sample size (experiments used only 5000 points).  
- Acceptance arguments: novel combination of techniques, principled treatment of faithfulness violations, distinct contribution with potential future impact.  
- Rejection arguments: lack of guidance on parameter selection, overly conservative bounds, weak experiments, and unclear presentation — recommendation to reject pending major revision.
