This paper proposes “Witness Protection Program” (WPP), a framework for bounding the ACE of X on Y with binary variables when the causal structure among covariates is unknown. The method inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a witness/admissible set (W,Z) for back-door adjustment under faithfulness, W is treated as an instrumental-like variable and Ramsahai/Dawid-style linear programming is used to compute bounds, with relaxations that allow bounded deviations from faithfulness via parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄). The authors also present algebraic back-substitution bounds to speed computation, and a Bayesian treatment that yields posteriors over bounds. The work connects to classical IV bounds (Balke–Pearl), influence-diagram formulations (Dawid), and recent IV geometry (Ramsahai), while leveraging Entner et al. for covariate search.

Quality: The high-level idea is sensible and potentially useful, and the LP/Bayesian machinery seems consistent with prior constrained approaches. However, I have not checked all technical details. A major conceptual gap is the role of Entner’s conditions once faithfulness is relaxed: the Rule 1 premises no longer imply the underlying structure, so it is unclear why they should justify constraints like (3)–(6) or guide witness selection. The paper hints that Rule 1 informs relaxation magnitudes, but explicitly leaves this unresolved. As written, it appears the proposed machinery would run with any candidate (W,Z); please clarify why Entner’s conditions matter for validity or efficiency, and how they affect both the method and experiments.

Clarity: The paper is ambitious but hard to follow in places (e.g., deferred explanations of how relaxation parameters should be chosen). The phrase “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing—unfaithfulness means extra independences; here you bound dependence magnitudes (i.e., “almost violations” of independence). Please rephrase.

Originality: Novel combination of (i) Rule-1-driven search with (ii) IV-style bounding plus (iii) Bayesian posteriors over bounds and (iv) algebraic back-substitution. This synthesis is new and interesting.

Significance: If clarified and strengthened, WPP could be a practical addition to the toolkit for observational studies.

Specific suggestions and minor issues:
- Evaluate Entner et al.’s method using their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as an uninformative bound, for a fair comparison.
- Clarify p.8 interpretation: with X=1 (flu shot) and Y=1 meaning not hospitalized in text, why does negative ACE indicate a desirable vaccine? Align definitions.
- Fix citation/table error: “Table 1” referred to as “Table 5” on p.8.
- Provide principled guidance for choosing (w, x, y, β, β̄) and sensitivity analyses; report how often witness selection materially tightens bounds vs a naive IV or no-witness baseline.

Pros:
- Timely problem; principled bounding under partial structure.
- Useful computational advances (algebraic bounds, back-substitution).
- Bayesian uncertainty quantification over bounds.

Cons:
- Unclear justification for using Entner’s conditions without faithfulness.
- Parameter-choice guidance and experimental baselines need strengthening.
- Presentation/notation issues impede readability.

Overall: Interesting and promising, but key methodological connections and clarity need work before acceptance.
