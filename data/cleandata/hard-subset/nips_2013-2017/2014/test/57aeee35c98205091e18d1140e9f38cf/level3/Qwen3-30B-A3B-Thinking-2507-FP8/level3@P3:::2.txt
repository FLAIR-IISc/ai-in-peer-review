

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces a novel approach for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) by inverting Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to create a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) that relaxes the faithfulness assumption through path-cancellation constraints. The authors leverage conditional independencies in observational data to generate bounds on ACE via linear programming, with the key innovation being the inversion of Entner's Rule 1 to use witnesses as instrumental variables rather than for back-door adjustment. This builds on prior work in instrumental variables (IV) bounding (e.g., Manski [11]) and causal discovery (Spirtes et al. [22]), but extends it through a relaxation framework that allows for "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a unified framework that combines three key ideas: inverse application of Entner's Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness assumptions, and LP-based bounding. The experimental results demonstrate that WPP can generate tighter bounds than existing approaches (NE1, NE2, and the point estimator from Entner et al.), particularly in scenarios where back-door adjustment fails. The Bayesian implementation for handling uncertainty in the bounds is well-motivated, and the back-substitution algorithm provides a computationally efficient alternative to full LP optimization. The paper also acknowledges limitations of faithfulness assumptions in practical settings, which is an important contribution to causal inference practice.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weaknesses are significant and undermine its scientific contribution. First, the validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not clearly justified. The authors assume a specific graph structure (Fig. 2) without theoretical justification or discussion of when this inverse application is valid, which is essential for the method's credibility. Second, the paper provides no generic criteria for selecting relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), which critically affect the resulting bounds. The authors acknowledge this in the supplemental material but do not provide a clear, general approach in the main paper. Third, the theoretical exposition misses the essential discussion of inverse-rule validity and parameter choices, leaving key assumptions unaddressed. Fourth, experimental details are poorly described: algorithm parameters are unclear, table references are inconsistent (e.g., "Table 1" vs "Table 5"), and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Finally, the paper claims "promising tighter ACE bounds" but provides limited experiments that fail to sufficiently validate the method's robustness across diverse scenarios.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound in principle, but the lack of justification for critical assumptions (inverse rule application, parameter choices) significantly weakens the quality of the theoretical contribution. The experimental validation is limited and does not adequately assess the method's robustness across different parameter settings or data-generating processes.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from multiple clarity issues: typos, inconsistent table references, and insufficient detail about algorithm implementation and parameter selection. The relationship between the theoretical framework and practical implementation is not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to understand how to apply the method.

**Originality:** The paper's novelty lies in the unified combination of three existing ideas (inverse Entner's Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness, LP bounding), but this combination is not sufficiently validated. The paper does not clearly distinguish its contribution from prior work on IV bounds (Ramsahai [16]) or causal discovery (Entner et al. [6]), making the novelty claim somewhat overstated.

**Significance:** While the potential for tighter ACE bounds is promising, the paper's significance is limited by the insufficient theoretical and empirical validation. The claim that the method "should be used in regular practice" is not supported by comprehensive experiments that demonstrate robustness across diverse scenarios. The limited experimental validation (only synthetic data and one real-world example) leaves open questions about the method's general applicability.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a useful framework for bounding ACE that combines multiple causal inference techniques.
- Demonstrates tighter bounds than existing methods in the experiments.
- The Bayesian approach for handling uncertainty in bounds is well-motivated.
- The back-substitution algorithm offers computational efficiency.

*Con:*
- Critical assumptions (inverse rule application, parameter selection) lack theoretical justification.
- Experimental validation is insufficient to support the method's general applicability.
- Algorithm details and parameter definitions are poorly described.
- Theoretical exposition misses essential discussion of key limitations.

This paper presents an interesting but incomplete approach to causal effect bounding. While the core idea has merit, the lack of justification for critical assumptions and the limited validation prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field. The authors should address the gaps in theoretical justification, provide clear criteria for parameter selection, and conduct more comprehensive experiments to validate their method before it can be recommended for regular practice. As it stands, the paper makes a promising but unvalidated contribution that would benefit significantly from additional theoretical and empirical work.
