Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework (Witness Protection Program, WPP) for partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies with possible unmeasured confounding. The key idea is to invert Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes’ Rule 1 for discovering admissible adjustment sets: instead of using a “witness” W and a set Z to obtain a back-door adjustment and point identification under faithfulness, WPP treats W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z and derives bounds on the ACE. To guard against spurious independencies due to path cancellations (i.e., violations of strict faithfulness), the method introduces bounded relaxations via parameters w, x, y, β, β̄ that limit the magnitude of direct W→Y effects, W–U associations, and discrepancies between observed and latent conditional probabilities. These relaxations are incorporated into a linear programming (LP) formulation that builds on the IV-bounds and inequality machinery of Balke and Pearl [1], Dawid [5], and Ramsahai [16]. The paper also develops conservative algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a fast back-substitution procedure to avoid repeated LP solves, and proposes a Bayesian approach using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling to produce a posterior over bounds.

Empirically, the paper reports synthetic experiments contrasting WPP to naive back-door adjustments (NE1/NE2) and to a faithfulness-based point estimator [6], as well as a re-analysis of the influenza encouragement design where standard IV bounds are wide; WPP finds a different witness/admissible set and yields narrower bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge between faithfulness-based point identification and IV-based partial identification, with explicit, tunable relaxations controlling deviations from faithfulness.
- Sound use of the inequality/LP framework from IV literature; the relaxation strategy appears to yield conservative bounds when parameters are chosen appropriately.
- Posterior over bounds is a thoughtful way to reflect sampling uncertainty without committing to a specific latent model.
- Algebraic bounds and back-substitution provide useful intuition and practical speedups.
- Empirical results suggest WPP can produce substantially tighter bounds than standard IV in at least one real dataset, and shows robustness in selected synthetic settings.
- Topic is squarely in scope for NIPS and timely given increasing interest in causality under weak assumptions.

Weaknesses
- The validity of inverting Entner’s Rule 1 is not carefully justified. Treating W as an instrument given Z requires explicit structural assumptions (e.g., no unblocked W→Y or W↔U→Y paths given Z), which the relaxations only partially address. A precise statement of the causal model class under which the LP bounds are guaranteed to be valid and conservative is missing.
- No principled procedure is provided for selecting the relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄), though they critically determine tightness and validity. The paper defers to the supplement and background knowledge, but reviewers and practitioners need a calibration recipe (e.g., mapping from empirical residual dependence to bounds, priors over relaxations with sensitivity analysis).
- Several exposition issues impede reproducibility: unclear or missing definitions in referenced tables; inconsistent table numbering and typos; insufficient detail on the search procedure for (W, Z), the constrained-Dirichlet setup, and the falsification test (e.g., the 95% rejection heuristic).
- Evaluation is limited. Simulations are modest in scope, binary-only, and do not fully characterize coverage, width, and sensitivity to mis-specified witnesses or to relaxation choices. The real-data analysis is intriguing but thin (e.g., why GRP fails as witness; robustness to alternative Z; comparison to standard IV with monotonicity).
- The Bayesian use of the same data to (i) select (W, Z) via BDeu and (ii) estimate bounds may overfit. No sample-splitting or correction is discussed.
- Originality is in the combination (inverse Rule 1 + bounded relaxations + IV inequalities + posterior over bounds) and in the algebraic bounds; however, each component builds on prior work, and the paper falls short of rigorously validating the unified method’s guarantees and practical calibration.

Detailed evaluation
Quality: The LP-based bounding under inequality relaxations is plausible and builds on strong precedents. However, the paper needs a clear theorem establishing that, under stated relaxations, the feasible set contains the true model and the resulting ACE bounds are valid (conservative). The Bayesian constrained-Dirichlet approach is reasonable for small Z, but the rejection-based falsification and the choice of priors require more justification and diagnostics. The back-substitution bounds are conservative by construction but sometimes disagree with the full LP; guidance on when to trust which is needed.

Clarity: The high-level story is understandable, but missing definitions, inconsistent table references, and typos hinder readability. The inverse use of Rule 1 and its assumptions should be stated explicitly and early. Algorithmic details (search over Z, computational complexity, convergence of back-substitution) should be clarified. Notation (ζ, η, δ, κ, ω) needs a concise glossary.

Originality: Moderate. The relaxation parameters and algebraic bounds are novel contributions; the overall framing is a useful synthesis of known tools. The paper would benefit from a sharper comparison to related contemporaneous work on bounding confounding and partial identification (e.g., [8], and IV variants with monotonicity/LATE), to position its novelty.

Significance: Potentially impactful if the method can be reliably calibrated and scaled. Practitioners need principled guidance on selecting relaxations from data and domain knowledge, and evidence that coverage is controlled while achieving nontrivial tightening across diverse settings. Current experiments are suggestive but insufficient to establish generality.

Suggestions for improvement
- State a formal model class for which the inverted Rule 1 yields valid constraints; provide a soundness result showing the bounds are conservative under Assumptions (3–6).
- Provide a principled calibration scheme for (w, x, y, β, β̄), e.g., map empirical conditional dependence measures (partial correlations or conditional mutual information with uncertainty) to relaxation ranges; place priors over relaxations and perform sensitivity analysis; or use sample splitting to select and then bound.
- Expand experiments: report coverage, average width, and error; include sensitivity to parameter choices; ablate the components (inverse Rule 1 vs. standard IV; relaxations on/off; algebraic vs. LP); add more real datasets and compare to standard IV with additional assumptions (e.g., monotonicity).
- Clarify the search procedure for (W, Z), control for multiplicity, and consider sample splitting or cross-fitting.
- Fix table/figure references; define all symbols in the main text; give pseudocode and complexity for back-substitution; release code sufficient to reproduce results.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem: partial identification under weak assumptions, bridging two influential strands (faithfulness and IV inequalities).
- Offers practically useful tools (posterior over bounds, algebraic constraints, fast back-substitution) and shows promising tightening of bounds.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the inverse use of Rule 1 and for the overall validity/conservativeness under the proposed relaxations.
- Lack of principled parameter selection and limited empirical validation undermine practical reliability.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues (missing definitions, typos, table errors).

Overall assessment
Interesting and promising synthesis with useful ideas, but the paper in its current form lacks essential theoretical justification, calibration guidance, and comprehensive evaluation. I recommend rejection at NIPS this year, with encouragement to revise addressing the above concerns.
