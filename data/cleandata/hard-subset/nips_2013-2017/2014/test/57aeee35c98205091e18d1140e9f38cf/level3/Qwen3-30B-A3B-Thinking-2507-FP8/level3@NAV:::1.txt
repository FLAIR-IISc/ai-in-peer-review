

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel approach for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) when the causal structure is not fully known. The authors modify Entner et al.'s rule to identify "witness" variables that can serve as instrumental variables, while relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow bounded violations of local independence. The method uses linear programming to generate ACE bounds, with additional relaxations for computational efficiency.

The main contribution is a sensible framework for causal inference that complements existing methods. The paper builds well on foundational work by Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2012), extending their methods to handle partial knowledge of causal structure. The experimental results on synthetic and influenza vaccination data show that WPP provides meaningful bounds where traditional point estimators may fail.

However, the paper has several significant concerns. The most critical issue is the unclear role of Entner et al.'s rule conditions without the faithfulness assumption. The authors claim these conditions inform relaxation parameters, but this connection remains unresolved. The paper does not convincingly explain why Entner et al.'s specific conditions are necessary for the method, raising doubts about their fundamental importance.

Minor issues include odd phrasing ("express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is conceptually inaccurate), a citation/table error (Table 1 referred to as Table 5), and unclear interpretation of the influenza vaccine example (why a negative ACE indicates a desirable vaccine).

The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its approach, but the theoretical justification for key methodological choices needs strengthening. The computational efficiency claims are supported by experiments, but the connection between the method and Entner et al.'s work requires clarification. While the approach is potentially useful for observational studies, the significance is somewhat limited by the unresolved theoretical questions.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with promising methodology, but it would benefit from a clearer theoretical foundation and more precise explanations of the key methodological choices. The current presentation makes it difficult to assess the full value of the proposed framework. I recommend rejection pending substantial revision addressing the concerns about the role of Entner et al.'s conditions and the theoretical connections.
