This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, where variables are confounded and the causal structure is not fully known. The authors introduce a method that exploits observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph, relaxing the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations." The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The main idea of the paper is sensible and potentially useful, as it provides a compromise between point estimators given by the faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. However, the reviewer has not checked all technical details, and some aspects of the paper require clarification.

One major concern is the unclear role of Entner et al.'s rule conditions without the faithfulness assumption. The conditions do not obviously imply causal structure when faithfulness is dropped, and it is unclear why these conditions matter for the method and experiments. The paper explicitly leaves this connection unresolved, and the reviewer suspects that Entner et al.'s conditions might inform the choice of relaxation parameters. However, this connection is not explored in the paper.

The reviewer also has some minor comments. Firstly, the wording "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is odd, as unfaithfulness means extra independences, and the framework seems to allow "almost violations" by bounding dependency magnitude. Secondly, it would be useful to report Entner et al.'s method performance using their stringent thresholds, treating "don't know" as uninformative bounds, for comparison. Thirdly, clarification is needed on the interpretation in the second paragraph on page 8, where a negative ACE indicates a desirable vaccine. Finally, there appears to be a citation/table error, where "Table 1" on page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the reviewer finds the paper to be technically sound, with well-supported claims and a clear explanation of the methodology. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear, although some aspects of the paper are hard to follow. The reviewer appreciates the provision of a Bayesian learning framework, which provides a more robust approach to estimating the average causal effect.

The originality of the paper is high, as it provides a novel approach to estimating causal effects in observational studies. The paper builds on existing work, such as Entner et al.'s rule and Ramsahai's method, but provides a new interpretation and application of these methods. The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference and provides a useful tool for researchers in this area.

In conclusion, the reviewer finds the paper to be interesting and potentially useful, but with some aspects that require clarification. The paper is technically sound, well-organized, and original, but some minor comments and major concerns need to be addressed.
