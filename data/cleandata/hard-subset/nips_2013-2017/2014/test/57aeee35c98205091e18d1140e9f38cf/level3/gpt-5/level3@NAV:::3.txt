Summary: The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework to compute bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) under possible violations of faithfulness. It inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1 to treat witnesses as quasi-instruments, introduces relaxation parameters that quantify “weak unfaithfulness,” and derives ACE bounds via linear programming, with a Bayesian procedure yielding a posterior over bounds. A back-substitution scheme provides faster, algebraic approximations. The work sits between Balke–Pearl/Dawid–Ramsahai bounds for IV models and faithfulness-based point identification, and relates to partial identification and recent bounding approaches (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf, UAI 2014).

Strengths (originality/significance): The problem is timely and important; the combination of witness search, relaxations, and LP is original and technically sound. The supplement contains detailed derivations, and the direction—quantifying weak unfaithfulness with familiar tools—is promising.

Weaknesses (quality/clarity): Conceptual interpretability is weak: a posterior over bounds is not a posterior over the ACE, and the distribution depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances. Many relaxations yield very wide bounds, often uninformative; the method provides no preferred point estimate, inviting misinterpretation of intervals as estimates. Experimental evaluation is unbalanced and undermines claims of practical usefulness: (i) the “bias” metric is not comparable across point and interval methods; use coverage, expected width, calibration, and decision risk instead; (ii) results rely on n=5000 and may not generalize to typical sample sizes; (iii) on the influenza data, the reported intervals (e.g., ACE in [-0.10, 0.17], and standard IV [-0.23, 0.64]) are too broad to guide practice. The role of the witness W (used to identify Z but not needed for ACE computation) is unclear; how the search step interacts with the bounds and how admissible sets depend on relaxation parameters θ needs explication. Choosing θ is deferred to future work, making applied use difficult; more intuition linking θ to “weak interactions” is needed. The algorithmic description (multiple subtly different parameters) is hard to follow; reproducibility from the paper alone is doubtful. Claims about novelty of analytical bounds should be carefully contrasted with prior work (e.g., Geiger et al., and standard IV algebraic bounds). The impact of the back-substitution refinement is asserted but not substantiated; provide ablations.

Requested clarifications and edits: clarify “Background … used here,” “max. 10 free parameters,” and fix typos; explain why multiple bounds arise (different admissible sets/witnesses); clearly state that bounds are not confidence intervals; tone down emotive language about Bayesian and theory-driven approaches.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap: principled relaxation of faithfulness with computable bounds
- Original combination and solid technical core; useful ideas for future work

Cons for acceptance:
- Weak and potentially misleading empirical evaluation; limited practical guidance
- Ambiguity in interpretation, parameter selection, and reproducibility
- Bounds often too wide to be actionable; unclear advantage over existing methods

Overall: Interesting and promising direction, but current version falls short on clarity, evaluation, and practical guidance. I lean toward rejection, with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revision.
