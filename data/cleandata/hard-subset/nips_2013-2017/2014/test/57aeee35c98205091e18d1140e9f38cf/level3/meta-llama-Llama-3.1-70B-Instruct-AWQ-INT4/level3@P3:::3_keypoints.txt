- Proposes Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel method that relaxes faithfulness and uses linear programming to bound the average causal effect (ACE).
- WPP identifies "weak" paths via covariates in a causal graph to construct bounds on ACE.
- Well-motivated problem statement with clear description of algorithm and theoretical properties.
- Uses familiar mathematical tools (linear programming) to quantify weak unfaithfulness in a principled way.
- Thorough analysis of WPP’s properties and limitations.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive and compares WPP against other approaches.
- Many relaxations yield very wide bounds, which can be uninformative in practice.
- Conceptual interpretability is limited because bounds depend on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances.
- Method does not provide a preferred point estimate, complicating interpretation in some contexts.
- Experimental evaluation has weaknesses: the "bias" metric is ill-defined and potentially misleading.
- Results on the influenza dataset are uninformative and raise concerns about practical performance.
- Language and exposition are generally good but contain unclear or ambiguous passages needing revision.
- Originality: novel combination of existing techniques and a new application of linear programming to ACE bounding.
- Significance: promising direction for future causal inference research but requires further empirical validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, solid literature grounding, and thorough theoretical analysis.
- Arguments against acceptance: interpretability issues, wide bounds, lack of point estimate, limited/weak experiments, and unclear language.
- Recommended improvements: enhance interpretability, develop point-estimate selection methods, broaden empirical evaluation, clarify language, and compare more thoroughly with Bayesian/theory-driven methods.
