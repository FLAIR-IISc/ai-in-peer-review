## Review: Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program

This paper tackles the challenging problem of bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies with potentially unmeasured confounding. It builds on Entner et al.’s work, leveraging conditional independence constraints, and incorporates a Ramsahai (2012)-style linear programming approach, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for “path cancellations.” The core idea – to use Entner’s Rule 1 to identify potential instrumental variables for bounding, rather than point estimation – is sensible and potentially valuable for practical causal inference. The paper is well-motivated, framing the problem clearly and referencing relevant prior work (Pearl, Spirtes et al., Dawid, Ramsahai). 

The strengths of the paper lie in its attempt to address a realistic scenario in causal inference – the relaxation of faithfulness – and its combination of existing techniques into a novel framework. The introduction of the “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) is a catchy and conceptually useful metaphor. The experiments, while relatively small-scale, demonstrate the method’s stability and offer a comparison against naive estimators. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in key methodological connections. My major concern revolves around the role of Entner et al.’s rule *without* the faithfulness assumption. The paper states the conditions don't “obviously imply causal structure,” but doesn’t fully articulate *why* they’re still relevant or how they inform the choice of relaxation parameters. The paper explicitly avoids resolving this connection, which feels like a missed opportunity. It’s not clear if the method critically *depends* on these conditions, or if they are merely suggestive. Clarification on this point, and potentially experiments demonstrating the sensitivity of the results to these conditions, would be crucial.

Several minor issues further detract from the paper’s clarity. The phrasing “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is awkward – unfaithfulness implies *extra* independences, not just bounded violations. The paper should consider reporting Entner et al.'s method performance using their original stringent thresholds for comparison. A clarification is needed regarding the interpretation on p.8 (vaccine example). Finally, there’s a citation/table error: “Table 1” is incorrectly referenced as “Table 5.”

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses an important problem, proposes a novel (and potentially useful) approach, and demonstrates stability in simulations.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of clarity regarding the core methodological connections, specifically the role of Entner et al.’s conditions without faithfulness. Details are hard to follow, and the paper feels incomplete.



Overall, this is an interesting paper with a promising idea, but requires substantial clarification and refinement before it is ready for publication. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised, as the direction of the work is potentially very valuable.
