Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a method to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y using observational data. The approach combines: (i) an inverse use of Entner et al.’s Rule 1—treating a “witness” W and set Z that satisfy Rule 1 as a generator of instrumental-variable-like constraints rather than as a cue for back-door adjustment; (ii) a relaxation of faithfulness formulated as bounded “path-cancellation” (parameters w, x, y) and bounded dependence between W and latent U (β, β̄); and (iii) a linear-programming (LP) formulation that yields inequality constraints and bounds on the ACE. The authors also derive algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a computationally efficient “back-substitution” algorithm, and propose a Bayesian scheme to produce posterior distributions over ACE bounds.

The work is positioned between point-identification approaches based on faithfulness (e.g., PC/Entner Rule 1) and worst-case nonparametric bounds (e.g., Balke–Pearl IV bounds; Dawid; Ramsahai). It builds directly on (a) Balke–Pearl/Dawid/Ramsahai LP for binary IV bounds and observable constraints, (b) Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes’ Rule 1 for admissible set discovery, and (c) the faithfulness literature (Spirtes–Glymour–Scheines; Meek; Robins et al.). The contribution is a unified framework that uses Rule 1 to suggest quasi-IVs under controlled relaxations of faithfulness, then propagates these relaxations through LP constraints to bound the ACE; it adds a Bayesian posterior over bounds and an efficient approximate solver.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling problem motivation: when faithfulness-based point estimates are fragile and IV bounds are wide, a principled middle ground is valuable.
- Conceptual novelty in the unification: inverse application of Rule 1 + relaxed faithfulness via bounded path-cancellation + LP bounds is an interesting synthesis.
- Technical soundness of the LP-based derivation and the algebraic inequalities; connection to known IV polytopes is appropriate.
- Practical promise: reported bounds are often substantially tighter than standard IV bounds, and appear accurate even though the implementation is not optimized.
- The back-substitution procedure is a useful engineering contribution that can reduce computational cost while preserving much of the tightness.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The validity of applying Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not theoretically justified. Under what graphical conditions does a (W,Z) that satisfies Rule 1 imply valid quasi-IV-type constraints after relaxing faithfulness? Without a formal statement (and counterexamples), it is unclear when WPP’s constraints are guaranteed to be conservative (i.e., to cover the true ACE).
- No generic, data-driven criteria are provided for selecting relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄), though they strongly drive the bounds. The supplement reportedly discusses choices, but the main paper lacks guidance, calibration, or sensitivity protocols.
- Experimental section is limited and under-described: algorithmic details, priors, search over Z, and falsification rates are not reproducible as written; several parameter symbols used in tables are undefined or only implicit; important implementation choices (e.g., thresholds, proposal/acceptance rates for constrained Dirichlet) are missing.
- The Bayesian posterior over bounds is attractive, but the inferential meaning is subtle; the paper should clarify what it means to average over bounds and how to summarize or compare intervals fairly across methods.

Clarity
- The theoretical exposition is mostly comprehensive, but it omits the essential discussion of inverse-Rule validity and principled parameter selection.
- Typos and incorrect/missing table references (e.g., “Table 1”/“Table 5”), and unclear parameter definitions in the tables (ζ, η, δ, κ, ω) hinder readability.
- Some figure references and assumptions tied to figures (e.g., the structure in Fig. 2) are insufficiently spelled out in text.

Originality
- The core ingredients are known (Rule 1, LP bounds, faithfulness relaxations). The novelty lies in their integration. This is meaningful but incremental unless the inverse-Rule step is theoretically validated.

Significance
- Potentially significant: the method could become a practical default to complement faithfulness-based discovery and IV analysis by delivering tighter, more realistic bounds.
- However, generality and robustness remain unassessed: limited experiments (one real dataset), no comparison to alternative bounding frameworks (e.g., Geiger et al. 2014; VanderWeele–Shpitser criteria; identifiable cases via do-calculus), and no systematic sensitivity analysis across parameter settings.

Constructive feedback and suggestions
- State and justify the inverse-Rule assumption: explicitly assume and discuss the causal structure depicted in Fig. 2 (or the relevant ADMG/PAG), including conditions under which W behaves as a valid “witness-IV” after conditioning on Z with bounded violations. Provide a theorem establishing that the proposed constraints are conservative for ACE under those assumptions, or give counterexamples delineating the safe operating region.
- Provide principled guidance for choosing (w, x, y, β, β̄):
  - Map statistical evidence of (approximate) independence to relaxation magnitudes (e.g., via posterior predictive checks, equivalence tests, or calibrated BDeu thresholds).
  - Offer default priors, sensitivity grids, and decision rules that control coverage or expected width.
  - Report how bounds vary with these parameters on synthetic and real data.
- Expand experiments:
  - Add baselines: Balke–Pearl IV bounds, Ramsahai bounds under partial relaxations, Entner Rule 1 point estimates, back-door when identifiable, and recent bounding-by-confounding approaches.
  - Include ablations (no inverse-Rule, no β relaxations, no algebraic bounds) and stress tests (cases where Rule 1 fails, near-unfaithful distributions).
  - Report computational costs and acceptance rates for the constrained Dirichlet sampler and for LP vs. back-substitution.
- Clarify algorithmic details:
  - Specify the search over Z, model selection criteria for independence/approximate independence, how multiple (W,Z) candidates are aggregated, and the falsification procedure.
  - Define all parameters used in the tables and align notation across main text and supplement.
- Fix typos and references; ensure all tables/figures exist and are correctly numbered; include full parameter definitions in tables.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common practical need: credible ACE bounds when neither pure faithfulness nor pure IV assumptions are satisfactory.
- Unified, technically coherent framework with promising empirical tightness.
- Back-substitution algorithm provides a useful tool for scaling LP-based bounds.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing theoretical justification for the central “inverse Rule 1” step.
- No principled methodology for choosing relaxation parameters; results may be sensitive.
- Limited, under-documented experiments and clarity issues (typos, missing definitions).

Overall evaluation
A promising and potentially impactful direction that could become a valuable tool in observational causal analysis. At present, however, I recommend revision before acceptance: the paper should (i) explicitly assume and justify the structural conditions underpinning the inverse use of Rule 1 (e.g., the Fig. 2 structure), (ii) provide principled guidance and sensitivity analysis for relaxation parameter choices, and (iii) substantially clarify and expand the experimental and algorithmic descriptions. With these improvements, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
