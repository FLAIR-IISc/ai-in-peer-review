Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method to bound the average causal effect (ACE) between binary X and Y when covariates are available but their causal structure is only partially known. The method inverts Entner et al.’s Rule 1: rather than using a (W, Z) pair to justify back-door adjustment under faithfulness, it treats W as a (conditional) instrument given Z, then relaxes the faithfulness implication via bounded “approximate independence” constraints. These relaxations are encoded by parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) that control the allowed magnitude of postulated violations (e.g., small direct effects W→Y or small dependence between W and unobserved U). Bounds are then computed with a Ramsahai/Dawid-style linear programming approach; a Bayesian layer returns posterior distributions over bounds; and algebraic “back-substitution” inequalities provide faster, albeit looser, bounds. Experiments compare WPP to naive adjusted/unadjusted estimators, to Entner et al.’s method, and apply WPP to a flu vaccination dataset previously analyzed via IV bounds (Balke–Pearl).

This work connects strands from instrumental-variable bounds (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai), faithfulness-based covariate selection (Entner et al.), and recent discussions on bounding under relaxed assumptions. Similar in spirit to “partial identification” and sensitivity analyses (e.g., Manski), it seeks a compromise between point identification from faithfulness and worst-case IV bounds.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and difficult problem: credible causal inference with incomplete causal structure and potential unfaithfulness.
- Sensible idea: treat discovered (approximate) conditional independencies as soft information that shrinks IV bounds instead of declaring point-identification.
- Methodologically interesting: a clear parametric relaxation (w, x, y, β, β̄) with interpretable roles; integration of LP-based bounds with a Bayesian posterior over bounds; algebraic approximations for scalability.
- Empirical results suggest robustness in synthetic settings and produce informative intervals on the flu data that are tighter than standard IV bounds.
- Code availability and practical guidance are appreciated.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
Major issue: role of Entner et al.’s rule without faithfulness
- The paper uses Rule 1 to nominate (W, Z) but explicitly drops faithfulness (or treats it as only approximately true). However, without faithfulness the conditions W ⊥ Y | X, Z and W ⊥̸ Y | Z do not in general imply the structural separations that justify an IV interpretation. The paper states these conditions motivate bounds via relaxation parameters but leaves the connection unresolved.
- Please clarify:
  - What, precisely, do the Rule 1 premises guarantee absent faithfulness? Do they imply any nonparametric structural constraints, or are they only a heuristic to choose (W, Z)?
  - In what sense are the relaxations (w, x, y, β, β̄) “tethered” to the observed (approximate) independencies—e.g., do small p-values or large BDeu score gaps imply smaller relaxation parameters? If not, why do the Rule 1 conditions matter?
  - To what extent does WPP’s machinery actually require Entner’s conditions? Would the same bounds be obtained by searching arbitrary (W, Z) pairs and optimizing over relaxations? An ablation showing WPP with/without Rule 1 would help.

Choosing relaxation parameters
- The paper defers the selection/calibration of (w, x, y, β, β̄) to the supplement and future work. Since the informativeness of bounds hinges on these, please provide concrete guidance:
  - A mapping from model selection evidence (e.g., Bayes factors or BDeu score differences) to default ranges for (w, x, y).
  - Sensitivity analyses demonstrating how conclusions vary with these choices in both synthetic and real data.
  - Any prior elicitation scheme that preserves coverage properties.

Interpretation of “violations of faithfulness”
- The phrasing “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is odd. Unfaithfulness typically introduces additional independencies (via path cancellations) relative to the graph, not “almost independence.” The framework instead seems to encode bounded dependence where faithfulness would set certain effects to exactly zero. Please rephrase and clarify the conceptual mapping.

Use of approximate independencies in experiments
- In settings where no exact witness exists, the paper still proceeds with “approximate” witnesses (relaxed thresholds/BDeu). Please quantify the risk of spurious witnesses and the impact on coverage. Provide results using Entner et al.’s original stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” as the uninformative interval [−1, 1], for a fair baseline comparison of informativeness vs. validity.

Clarity and reproducibility
- The LP/Bayesian pipeline is complex. A small worked example mapping observed tables to κ, ω, constraints, and final ACE bounds would improve clarity.
- The back-substitution derivation is insightful; please summarize key inequalities in the main text and state how much widening they introduce vs. the exact LP across representative scenarios.

Specific technical questions
- What guarantees can be stated about coverage: if the data-generating process (DGP) satisfies the relaxation constraints, do the LP bounds always contain the true ACE? Under what sampling error and posterior rejection thresholds?
- How sensitive is the posterior over bounds to the constrained Dirichlet hyperparameters? Are there pathological cases where rejection sampling frequently fails (beyond the 95% cutoff heuristic)?
- Computational complexity: give empirical runtime scaling in |Z| and sample size, for both LP and back-substitution.

Minor comments
- Please clarify the sign convention on p.8: with X=1 indicating flu shot and Y=1 indicating hospitalization, a negative ACE is desirable (reduces hospitalization). If the text elsewhere states “Y=1 = not hospitalized,” that would invert the interpretation; please fix any inconsistency.
- Report Entner et al.’s method using their original stringent thresholds, and treat “don’t know” as [−1, 1], to benchmark WPP’s informativeness against a conservative faithfulness-based baseline.
- Wording: consider revising “violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” as discussed above.
- Typo: the table referenced as “Table 5” on p.8 appears to be “Table 1.”

Evaluation
- Quality: The main idea is sound and potentially impactful; the technical development is substantial, though I did not check all algebraic details. The principal weakness is the unclear, arguably non-derivative role of Entner’s conditions once faithfulness is relaxed.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but some sections are hard to follow due to heavy notation and deferred details; stronger exposition tying Rule 1 to relaxation choices is needed.
- Originality: A novel and promising combination of IV bounds, soft constraints informed by discovered independencies, and Bayesian posteriors over bounds.
- Significance: If clarified and strengthened, this could become a useful tool for observational studies, offering a pragmatic middle ground between point-ID and worst-case bounds.

Pros
- Pragmatic compromise between faithfulness-based point-ID and IV bounding
- Interpretable relaxations; algebraic speedups; Bayesian uncertainty over bounds
- Encouraging empirical behavior and real-data application

Cons
- Insufficient justification for the use of Entner’s rules absent faithfulness
- Lack of principled guidance for selecting relaxation parameters
- Some clarity and reproducibility issues

Overall: Interesting and potentially useful, but key methodological connections (especially the role of Entner et al.’s conditions) need clarification and stronger empirical validation.
