This paper presents a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) between two binary variables from observational data, particularly when the causal structure among covariates is unknown. The central idea is to provide a compromise between methods that make strong, untestable assumptions (like faithfulness) to yield a point estimate, and methods that make very weak assumptions but can result in wide, uninformative bounds. The proposed approach, named the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), builds upon the work of Entner et al. (2013), who introduced a rule for identifying admissible sets for back-door adjustment under the faithfulness assumption. Instead of using Entner's rule to find an admissible set `Z` and a point estimate, this paper re-purposes the "witness" variable `W` as a candidate instrumental variable. It then employs a linear programming framework, in the style of Ramsahai (2012) and Dawid (2003), to derive bounds on the ACE. The key contribution is that it relaxes the strict faithfulness assumption by allowing for bounded violations of the conditional independencies that would hold in a simpler causal structure (e.g., an instrumental variable model). These relaxations are controlled by a set of user-defined parameters, and inference is performed within a Bayesian framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper tackles a significant and highly relevant problem in causal inference: how to proceed when one has observational data with many covariates but lacks confidence in both the causal graph and the faithfulness assumption.
- The core idea of combining a data-driven heuristic for identifying promising causal structures (via Entner et al.'s rule) with a robust bounding methodology that allows for violations of ideal assumptions is both original and sensible. It offers a practical path forward for applied researchers.
- The proposed WPP framework is a complete methodology, including the bounding formulation, a Bayesian procedure for handling sampling uncertainty, and an efficient algebraic approximation (the "back-substitution" algorithm) for scalability.
- The work is well-situated within the relevant literature, clearly building on and extending previous results in causal discovery and bounds on causal effects.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
The main idea is appealing, but the manuscript's clarity on a key methodological justification is lacking, which makes it difficult to assess the soundness of the approach.

**Major Concern:**
My primary concern is the unclear role of Entner et al.'s conditions when the faithfulness assumption is dropped. Entner's Rule 1—finding a `W` and `Z` such that `W` is dependent on `Y` given `Z` but independent of `Y` given `Z` and `X`—is justified under faithfulness as a sound method for identifying an admissible set `Z`. Without faithfulness, it is not obvious what this pattern of conditional (in)dependencies implies about the underlying causal structure. The paper uses this rule as a heuristic to propose `(W, Z)` pairs for its bounding machinery, but the connection is not justified.

Why should a `(W, Z)` pair satisfying this rule be a good candidate for the relaxed instrumental variable model? The proposed bounding machinery seems generic enough to be applied to *any* choice of `(W, Z)`. What is special about the pairs selected by this specific rule? One might suspect that the degree to which Entner's conditions hold in the data could inform the choice of the relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`), but the paper explicitly defers this crucial point to future work. Without this link, the use of Entner's rule feels ad-hoc. The paper would be much stronger if it could provide a theoretical argument for why satisfying this rule (even approximately) makes it more plausible that the true causal structure can be well-approximated by the relaxed IV model with small relaxation parameters.

**Minor Comments:**
1.  The phrasing on page 3, "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence," is slightly confusing. Faithfulness violations typically refer to the presence of conditional independencies that are not entailed by the graph structure (e.g., due to canceling paths). The framework presented here seems to do the opposite: it models situations where a conditional independence expected from a simpler graph (e.g., `W ⊥ Y | {X, Z}`) does not quite hold, by placing a bound on the magnitude of the dependence. This seems more like a relaxation of the Causal Markov condition for a simpler model rather than a direct modeling of unfaithfulness. Clarifying this terminology would be helpful.

2.  The experimental comparison could be strengthened. The paper compares WPP against two naive estimators. It would be very informative to also compare it against the original method of Entner et al. (2013). One way to do this would be to run their method with their suggested stringent statistical thresholds. In cases where their method fails to find a witness and returns "don't know," this outcome could be treated as the uninformative bound `[-1, 1]` for comparison purposes. This would provide a more direct benchmark against the state-of-the-art method that WPP aims to relax.

3.  A small clarification in the real-data experiment (second paragraph, page 8) would be useful. The paper states that `X=1` is receiving a flu shot and `Y=1` is being hospitalized, and correctly concludes that "a negative ACE therefore suggests a desirable vaccine." For the sake of absolute clarity for all readers, it might be worth explicitly stating that a negative ACE means `P(hospitalized | do(shot)) < P(hospitalized | do(no shot))`.

4.  There appears to be a typo on page 8. The text refers to "Table 5," but the relevant parameterization seems to be in Table 1 on page 3.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a potentially significant contribution. The approach of building a robust bounding method on top of a data-driven discovery heuristic is promising. However, the manuscript needs to provide a much clearer justification for its foundational step: why Entner et al.'s rule is the right heuristic to use when faithfulness is not assumed. Addressing this and the minor points above would substantially improve the quality and impact of the work.
