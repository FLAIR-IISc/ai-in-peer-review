- Proposes WPP: invert Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes Rule 1 to treat a “witness” W (given Z) as a candidate instrument and produce LP-based bounds on the ACE with bounded relaxations (w,x,y,β,β̄), algebraic bounds, back-substitution, and a Bayesian posterior via constrained Dirichlet priors.

- Conceptual strength: bridges faithfulness-based point identification and IV-based partial identification with explicit, tunable relaxations.

- Methodological strength: leverages established IV inequality/LP machinery to produce conservative bounds when relaxations are appropriately set.

- Statistical strength: provides a posterior over bounds to reflect sampling uncertainty without committing to a single latent model.

- Computational strength: algebraic bounds and back-substitution give intuition and practical speedups over repeated LP solves.

- Empirical strength: shows tighter bounds than standard IV on at least one real dataset and robustness in selected synthetic settings.

- Scope/timeliness: topic fits NIPS and is relevant to causality under weak assumptions.

- Major weakness: no rigorous justification or formal model class stated for inverting Rule 1; missing theorem that bounds are conservative under the proposed relaxations.

- Major weakness: no principled procedure to select or calibrate relaxation parameters (w,x,y,β,β̄), which critically affect validity and tightness.

- Weakness: exposition and reproducibility issues—missing definitions, inconsistent table numbering, typos, and insufficient details on search for (W,Z), constrained-Dirichlet setup, and falsification test.

- Weakness: limited evaluation—binary-only simulations, modest scope, lacking coverage/width/sensitivity analyses, and thin real-data analysis without robustness checks or comparisons (e.g., IV with monotonicity).

- Weakness: possible overfitting by using the same data to select (W,Z) via BDeu and to estimate bounds; no sample-splitting or correction discussed.

- Originality: moderate—combination of existing components is useful, with novel relaxation parameters and algebraic bounds, but the unified method lacks rigorous validation against prior work.

- Quality assessment: LP-based approach plausible but needs a soundness theorem; Bayesian/rejection approaches need more justification and diagnostics; back-substitution can disagree with full LP without guidance on trust.

- Clarity assessment: high-level story clear but many missing definitions, unclear assumptions (inverse Rule 1), and absent algorithmic/complexity details; notation needs a glossary.

- Significance assessment: potentially impactful if calibration and scaling issues are resolved and coverage is demonstrated across settings.

- Suggested improvements: formalize the causal model and soundness theorem; provide principled calibration or priors for relaxations and sensitivity analysis; expand experiments (coverage, width, sensitivity, ablations, more datasets); clarify (W,Z) search, control multiplicity, and consider sample-splitting; fix exposition and release reproducible code.

- Arguments for acceptance: tackles important partial-identification problem, synthesizes useful tools, and provides practical algorithms with promising empirical tightening.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient theoretical justification, no principled parameter selection, limited empirical validation, and clarity/reproducibility problems.

- Overall recommendation: interesting and promising but currently lacks essential theory, calibration guidance, and comprehensive evaluation—recommend revise and resubmit rather than acceptance.
