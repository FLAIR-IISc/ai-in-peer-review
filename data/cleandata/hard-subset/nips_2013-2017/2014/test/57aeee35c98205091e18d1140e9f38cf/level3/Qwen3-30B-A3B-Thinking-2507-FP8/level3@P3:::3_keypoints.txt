- Addresses an important problem: handling potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference.  
- Conceptually interesting and principled: relaxes faithfulness via parameters and uses linear programming to quantify weak unfaithfulness.  
- Technically sound with detailed derivations in the supplement and an original combination of existing techniques.  
- Provides a posterior distribution over ACE bounds using Bayesian inference.  

- Interpretability is weak: the posterior over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances, making practical meaning unclear.  
- Many relaxations yield very wide, uninformative bounds (e.g., ACE in (-0.23, 0.64) for influenza data).  
- Experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced; the "bias" metric is meaningless because it measures different quantities across methods.  
- Paper fails to provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates.  
- Algorithmic description is hard to follow with subtly different parameters and insufficient reproducibility details.  
- Choice of relaxation parameters is deferred, offering no practical guidance for application.  
- Role of the witness W is unclear (used to identify Z but not needed for ACE computation).  
- Relationship between relaxation parameters and weak interactions is inadequately explained.  
- Missing citations to related work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014) when claiming novelty of analytical bounds.  
- Back-substitution refinement step lacks empirical evidence of benefit.  
- Sample-size sensitivity not addressed; results on large samples (5000) may not generalize to typical smaller datasets.  
- Reported summaries (e.g., Table 1) do not show clear benefit of WPP over standard faithfulness-based point estimates.  
- Paper has ambiguous sentences, typos, and occasionally emotive/dismissive language about Bayesian approaches that should be toned down.  
- Overall: promising theoretical direction but lacks practical utility, clarity, and stronger empirical support; requires substantial revision before publication.
