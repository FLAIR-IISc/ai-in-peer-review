This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework to bound the average causal effect (ACE) from observational binary data when faithfulness may be violated. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1: instead of using a witness W and admissible covariates Z to obtain a back-door adjustment point estimate, the method treats W as an “almost-instrument” and computes bounds on ACE conditional on Z by relaxing faithfulness through bounded deviations in local conditional independences. These relaxations are encoded by parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that limit path cancellations and W–U dependence. The bounds are obtained by a linear programming approach combining Balke–Pearl IV-style polytopes with Dawid/Ramsahai projections, and a Bayesian procedure returns a posterior distribution over the bounds. To scale up, the paper introduces analytical (symbolic) inequalities and a back-substitution refinement that approximates the LP bounds at lower cost. Experiments on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination dataset illustrate the method.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on faithfulness-based discovery (PC), admissible set selection (Entner et al.), IV bounds (Balke–Pearl), LP formulations for partial identification (Dawid; Ramsahai), and inequality constraints in latent-variable models (Evans; Richardson & Robins). It is directionally aligned with Manski’s partial identification and recent attempts to bound confounding (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf, UAI 2014). The algebraic bounds and the relaxation of faithfulness via explicit parameters are presented as novel.

Quality: The technical construction is careful and mostly sound, with clear links to known polytopes and dual representations; the supplement appears to carry substantial derivations. However, several aspects undermine the empirical claims:
- The posterior over bounds is not a posterior over ACE, and the paper risks misinterpretation of intervals as confidence/credible intervals. The likelihood of unfaithful instances is not quantified, so the Bayesian averaging over constraints is hard to interpret.
- Many relaxation settings yield very wide bounds (often only marginally sharper than [−1, 1] or standard IV bounds), limiting practical usefulness.
- The evaluation metric (“error” as distance to the true ACE for point estimates, and zero if the interval contains the truth) is not commensurate across methods; it favors wide intervals and obscures trade-offs between information content and coverage. Standard interval scores, average width at fixed coverage, and containment frequency would be more informative.
- The influenza case returns [−0.23, 0.64] under standard IV and [−0.10, 0.17] under WPP with chosen relaxations—both largely uninformative for decision-making. No sensitivity to smaller sample sizes or to different θ choices is shown.
- The benefit of the back-substitution step over the full LP is claimed but only anecdotally supported; its empirical impact on tightness and runtime should be quantified.

Clarity: The paper is dense and difficult to follow. Notation (ζ, κ, ω, η) and multiple relaxations (w, y, x, β, β̄) are introduced quickly and used in subtly different places. The role of W is conceptually confusing: W is used to “witness” Z and then reinterpreted as a near-IV; make explicit that W is not part of the final adjustment set for ACE and what assumptions justify its use in bounding. Important details of the search step and its dependence on θ are left implicit. Several sentences are ambiguous (“Background knowledge… used here”; “max. 10 free parameters”) and typos remain. Reproducibility from the text alone is doubtful.

Originality: The combination of (i) inverting Rule 1 to propose near-instruments, (ii) parameterized relaxations of faithfulness, and (iii) LP-based and symbolic bounds is novel and well-differentiated from purely faithfulness-based or purely IV-based methods. Claims of novelty for analytical bounds should cite closely related work (e.g., Geiger–Janzing–Schölkopf, UAI 2014) and clarify what is genuinely new.

Significance: The contribution is primarily conceptual: it opens a path to principled partial identification under “weak unfaithfulness.” As a ready-to-use tool, however, its current form is limited by the dependence on user-chosen relaxation parameters, wide intervals, and evaluation that does not convincingly demonstrate practical gains over standard baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem: robust causal inference under potential unfaithfulness.
- Technically sound framework building on established partial identification machinery.
- Novel perspective of turning witnesses into near-instruments; insightful algebraic inequalities.
- Potentially impactful direction that could influence how observational studies report uncertainty.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited practical informativeness: intervals are often wide; influenza case not compelling.
- Posterior-over-bounds is hard to interpret and may be misread as posterior over ACE.
- Experimental design and metrics are unbalanced; comparisons to faithfulness-based point estimates and IV baselines are not on equal footing.
- Choice and interpretation of relaxation parameters are deferred; no concrete guidance.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; the algorithmic pipeline and notation are difficult to follow.
- Insufficient evidence on the gain from back-substitution versus full LP.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide operational guidance for selecting θ = (w, y, x, β, β̄): elicitation from domain knowledge, default priors, and sensitivity analyses; show how bounds trade off width vs. plausibility.
- Use proper evaluation metrics: average interval width at fixed empirical coverage; interval scoring rules; calibration diagnostics; small-sample studies (e.g., n = 500, 1000).
- Report the frequency with which WPP improves over standard IV bounds and over faithfulness-based point estimates, and by how much.
- Quantify the impact of back-substitution on tightness and runtime (ablation study).
- Clarify the role of W vs. Z; detail the search step and its dependence on θ; unify notation and provide a worked example.
- Tone down language dismissive of Bayesian or theory-driven approaches and ensure balanced citations (include Geiger–Janzing–Schölkopf).
- Improve writing: fix ambiguities/typos; add intuition for relaxation parameters as bounds on specific path strengths.

Overall assessment: A thoughtful and technically solid partial-identification approach that advances the discussion of faithfulness violations. However, the empirical evidence and practical guidance are not yet sufficient for NIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to strengthen the exposition, parameter guidance, and evaluation.
