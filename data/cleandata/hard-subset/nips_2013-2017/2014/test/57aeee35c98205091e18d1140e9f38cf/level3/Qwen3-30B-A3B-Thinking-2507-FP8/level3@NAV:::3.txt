

This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel method for computing bounds on average causal effects (ACE) under potential violations of the faithfulness assumption in causal inference. The approach leverages conditional independence constraints identified through a modified version of Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to generate instrumental variables and employs linear programming with relaxation parameters to produce conservative bounds on ACE. The method represents a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools.

The paper's main strengths lie in its originality and technical soundness. WPP combines existing techniques in a novel way, providing a valuable conceptual framework for handling weak unfaithfulness. The mathematical derivations are detailed and correct, as evidenced in the supplement. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the causal inference literature by addressing a timely problem that has been largely overlooked in previous work.

However, several significant weaknesses undermine the paper's impact. The conceptual interpretability is weak: the distribution over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances, and the paper fails to provide clear practical guidance on what these bounds mean in real-world contexts (they are not confidence intervals). The experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced, with the "bias" metric being meaningless as it measures different quantities across methods. The influenza dataset results (-0.23, 0.64) are uninformative and raise concerns about practical performance. Many relaxation parameters produce very wide, uninformative bounds, and the method lacks a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates.

The algorithm description is difficult to follow due to numerous subtly different parameters and insufficient detail for reproducibility. Key elements like the role of the witness W and the relationship between relaxation parameters and weak interactions remain unclear. The paper also fails to adequately cite closely related work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014) when claiming novelty.

While the paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas rather than immediate practitioner readiness, the current evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate practical utility. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail to assess the method's benefits over standard approaches, and the sample-size sensitivity (5000 points) is questionable for typical observational studies.

Overall, this is an interesting and technically sound contribution to causal inference methodology, but the current presentation and evaluation do not sufficiently demonstrate its practical value or provide the clarity needed for adoption in real-world applications.
