**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, called the Witness Protection Program (WPP). The WPP algorithm uses a linear programming formulation to obtain bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y, under possible violations of faithfulness. The approach relaxes the faithfulness assumption by introducing relaxation parameters that allow for weak interactions between variables. The WPP algorithm uses a Bayesian learning approach to obtain a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools.
* The WPP algorithm is an original combination of existing techniques and is sufficiently distinct from recent related work.
* The paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas, which could lead to new approaches in causal inference.

Weaknesses:

* Conceptual interpretability is weak, as the distribution over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances.
* Many relaxations produce very wide, potentially uninformative bounds.
* The method does not provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates.
* Experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced, undermining claims about practical usefulness.
* The "bias" metric used for comparisons is meaningless because it measures different quantities across methods.
* Influenza dataset results are uninformative and raise concerns about practical performance.

**Originality**

The WPP algorithm is an original combination of existing techniques, including linear programming and Bayesian learning. The paper's approach to relaxing the faithfulness assumption is novel and could lead to new approaches in causal inference.

**Significance**

The paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas, which could lead to new approaches in causal inference. The WPP algorithm has the potential to provide more robust estimates of causal effects in observational studies, where faithfulness assumptions may not hold.

**Clarity and Organization**

The paper's technical approach is sound and detailed in the supplement, but overall context and some derivations are unclear. The algorithmic description and many subtly different parameters are hard to follow and may not be reproducible from the paper alone. The paper could benefit from clearer interpretation and guidance on what the derived bounds mean in practice.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

* Clarify the role of the witness W (used to identify Z but not needed for ACE computation).
* Explain and give intuition for the relaxation parameters and how they relate to weak interactions.
* Describe how the search step relates to bounds computation and how admissible sets Z depend on relaxation parameters θ.
* Clarify ambiguous sentences (e.g., "Background ... used here," "max. 10 free parameters") and fix typos.
* Cite closely related work (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf UAI2014) when claiming novelty of analytical bounds.
* Question effectiveness of the back-substitution refinement step; provide empirical evidence of its impact.
* Explain why multiple bounds arise (different admissible sets for different witnesses).
* Address sample-size sensitivity (results reported for 5000 points may not generalize to typical smaller datasets).
* Table 1 and other reported summaries do not allow gauging the benefit of WPP over standard faithfulness-based point estimates.
* Tone down emotive or dismissive language about Bayesian and theory-driven approaches; avoid biased evaluations.

**Conclusion**

The WPP algorithm is a novel approach to estimating causal effects in observational studies, but it requires further clarification and improvement. The paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas, which could lead to new approaches in causal inference. However, the experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced, and the paper could benefit from clearer interpretation and guidance on what the derived bounds mean in practice.
