This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y in the presence of unobserved confounding. The approach builds upon work by Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2012), relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for varying degrees of “path cancellations” and utilizing a linear programming approach with Bayesian inference to obtain posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE. The core idea – to invert the usage of Entner’s Rule 1 to suggest instrumental variables and to bound violations of faithfulness – is sensible and potentially valuable for observational studies where complete causal knowledge is unavailable.

The paper is situated within a large body of work on causal inference. It directly references key papers by Pearl (2009), Spirtes et al. (2000), and others, demonstrating an understanding of the foundational concepts. The authors correctly identify the challenge of confounding and the limitations of standard back-door adjustment when all confounders are not observed. Related work in instrumental variable methods (e.g., Balke et al., 1997; Ramsahai, 2012) is also acknowledged. The motivation for relaxing the faithfulness assumption is well-stated, acknowledging that it can be overly restrictive in practice (Meek, 1995).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) offers a fresh perspective on bounding causal effects in observational settings. The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption and the combined use of linear programming and Bayesian inference are potentially significant contributions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – estimating causal effects with imperfect knowledge of the causal graph – is highly relevant to many real-world applications.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper attempts a comprehensive treatment, including a discussion of algebraic bounds, computational considerations, and an experimental evaluation.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and the rationale for their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity:** The central methodological connection—the role of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 conditions *without* the faithfulness assumption—remains unclear. The conditions, W ⊥ Y | Z and W ⊥ Y | Z ∪ {X}, do not readily suggest a causal structure when faithfulness is dropped. It’s not obvious how these conditions, in the absence of faithfulness, inform the bounding procedure or the selection of relaxation parameters. The paper states this connection is unresolved.
*   **Dependence on Entner et al.'s Rule?** A key question is whether the machinery developed relies crucially on Entner et al.’s conditions. The authors need to clarify if the method would function (and how) without these conditions, or if they are merely used to guide the search for suitable relaxation parameters.
*   **Odd Wording:** The phrase “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” (p. 8) is conceptually awkward. Unfaithfulness implies *extra* conditional independences, not a weakening of existing dependencies. The proposed framework seems to allow for “almost violations” by bounding the magnitude of dependency.
*   **Incomplete Comparison:** While the authors compare their method to naive estimators, a more thorough comparison to Entner et al.’s original method—using their stringent thresholds for Rule 1, and treating “don’t know” cases as providing uninformative bounds—would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Minor Errors:** There are minor inconsistencies in the text (e.g., the reference to “Table 5” when it should be “Table 1” on p. 8) and a potentially confusing statement regarding the interpretation of a negative ACE in the influenza vaccination example (p. 8). If X=1 (flu shot) and Y=1 (hospitalized), a negative ACE means the flu shot *reduces* the probability of hospitalization, hence is desirable, but this should be clarified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem in causal inference.
*   The proposed method offers a novel approach that potentially overcomes limitations of existing techniques.
*   The experimental evaluation provides some evidence of the method's effectiveness, although more extensive testing would be beneficial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The core methodological connection between Entner et al.'s conditions and the overall framework is not sufficiently clear.
*   The lack of a direct comparison to Entner et al.’s original method with stringent thresholds hinders a full assessment of the contribution.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting paper with the potential to contribute to the field of causal inference. However, the current presentation lacks clarity, particularly regarding the role of Entner et al.’s conditions. I recommend acceptance with *major revisions*. The authors should prioritize clarifying the connection between their method and the work of Entner et al., providing a more comprehensive comparison to existing techniques, and addressing the minor errors and points of ambiguity identified in this review. A more rigorous explanation of how the relaxation of faithfulness is implemented and the implications of this relaxation would also be beneficial.
