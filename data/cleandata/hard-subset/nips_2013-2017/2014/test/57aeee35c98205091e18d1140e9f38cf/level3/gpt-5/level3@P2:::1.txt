Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y in observational studies when covariate causal structure is uncertain. The key idea is to invert Entner-Hoyer-Spirtes Rule 1 (originally used under faithfulness to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment) by treating a “witness” W as an approximate instrument conditional on Z, and then to relax faithfulness via bounded deviations from conditional independences. These relaxations are encoded as local inequalities (on η, δ, and the dependence of U on W via β, β̄), leading to linear-program-based bounds on ACE following the general approach of Dawid and Ramsahai and the Balke-Pearl IV polytope. The paper further develops a constrained-Dirichlet Bayesian treatment to obtain a posterior over bounds, supplies faster symbolic “algebraic bounds” and a back-substitution (message-passing–like) algorithm to reduce computation, and illustrates behavior on synthetic data and an influenza-vaccine dataset. The work is positioned as a compromise between point-identification under faithfulness and worst-case IV bounds.

Relation to prior work:
- Uses Entner et al. (AISTATS’13) Rule 1 but reinterprets the witness as a conditional instrument and relaxes faithfulness.
- Builds on the IV bounds literature (Balke-Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai; Richardson & Robins) and connects to Manski’s partial identification perspective.
- Provides computationally tractable approximations related to polyhedral characterizations of binary IV models (e.g., Richardson 2012).
- Positions itself alongside recent attempts to bound confounding and use inequality constraints in graphical models.

Quality
- The high-level construction is sensible: turning approximate conditional independences into bounded violations, and propagating these through LP to get ACE bounds, is a coherent strategy with clear roots in the IV/inequality-constraints literature. The algebraic relaxation and back-substitution algorithm are practical contributions.
- However, the technical rationale for why Rule 1 should guide the choice of witness/admissible set once faithfulness is explicitly relaxed is not fully justified. As written, the premises of Rule 1 do not imply the structural conclusions they rely on without (approximate) faithfulness, yet the paper uses Rule 1 as a selection heuristic. The paper hints that “small” violations (w, x, y, β, β̄) could be informed by the observed near-independences but leaves this unresolved. This is the main conceptual gap.
- The Bayesian posterior-over-bounds treatment is reasonable, but details of priors, constraint handling, rejection sampling efficiency, and how contradictory bound posteriors are summarized could be clearer. The falsification criterion (>95% rejections) is pragmatic but ad hoc; some sensitivity analysis is shown but not deeply discussed.
- Experiments are informative, but a crucial baseline is missing: Entner et al.’s method run with their stringent thresholds, counting “don’t know” as yielding trivial bounds, to show the trade-off WPP achieves.

Clarity
- The paper is dense and sometimes difficult to follow. The causal interpretation of each relaxation parameter (w, x, y, β, β̄) should be stated early and precisely, and the mapping from “approximate independences” to these parameters needs an explicit discussion (even if only as a principled heuristic).
- Notation (ζ, κ, ω; starred vs unstarred) is heavy; a concise legend and a guiding example would help. Algorithm 1’s “falsification” and model-selection step need more transparency (e.g., thresholds, priors, how many candidate pairs explored).
- Minor inconsistencies/typos (see below) impede readability.

Originality
- Reinterpreting Rule 1 to produce relaxed IV-style bounds and developing posterior-over-bounds with constrained Dirichlet priors, plus novel algebraic relaxations and a back-substitution solver, is a novel and interesting combination. The idea of “protecting” witnesses by bounding violations is appealing and, to my knowledge, new in this explicit form.

Significance
- Many observational studies face precisely this dilemma: weak CI evidence, uncertain causal graphs, and no gold-standard IV. A principled way to turn approximate CIs into partial identification with tunable robustness would be valuable. If the link between CI strength and relaxation parameters can be tightened, this could be broadly useful.

Major questions/concerns for the authors
1) Why do the Entner et al. Rule 1 conditions matter once faithfulness is relaxed? Please make explicit which parts of Rule 1 you retain and which you abandon, and provide a formal or at least principled argument that a (W, Z) pair satisfying Rule 1 in the data is informative for bounding under your relaxations, rather than being just a generic conditional-IV search. If the value of Rule 1 is primarily heuristic, say so and justify empirically.

2) Mapping CI evidence to relaxation parameters: The framework seems to need a way to set (w, x, y, β, β̄) based on observed near-independences or model scores. Can you propose and evaluate at least a simple scheme (e.g., calibrating y to posterior probabilities of small mutual information, or β/β̄ to measures of dependence between W and U proxies)? Even limited guidance would strengthen the methodology.

3) Experiments: Please add a baseline showing Entner et al.’s original procedure with their stringent thresholds, treating “no admissible set” as an uninformative interval, to quantify the robustness/coverage trade-off. Also report the frequency of model falsification and the distribution of interval widths, to contextualize error rates.

4) Interpretation in vaccine example: There appears to be confusion about coding. In the text, Y=1 is “hospitalized,” and a negative ACE means the vaccine lowers hospitalization, which is desirable. Please double-check and clarify the paragraph on p.8 to avoid contradictory statements (the current wording in the submission notes X=1 (shot) and Y=1 (not hospitalized) at one point).

5) Provide more intuition for the algebraic bounds. Even if not tight, short derivations or a worked numerical example would help readers understand how each relaxation tightens/loosens bounds relative to standard IV.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical gap between faithfulness-based point ID and worst-case IV bounds.
- Clear partial-identification stance with Bayesian posterior over bounds.
- Practical computational contributions (symbolic bounds, back-substitution).
- Sensible synthetic design where naive adjustment is guaranteed to be wrong; real-data illustration.

Cons
- Key conceptual link (Rule 1 under relaxed faithfulness) is under-justified.
- Choice of relaxation parameters left largely unspecified; limited guidance for practitioners.
- Presentation is dense; some notational and editorial issues.
- Missing baseline and deeper sensitivity analyses limit empirical persuasiveness.

Minor comments
- Wording: “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing. Unfaithfulness introduces extra independences; you are bounding deviations from the CIs implied by Rule 1. Consider rephrasing to “bound the magnitudes of local dependences that would be zero under faithfulness.”
- Please include Entner et al.’s method with their thresholds as a baseline, counting “don’t know” as trivial bounds.
- Clarify coding and interpretation in the influenza example (see above).
- Fix citation/table mismatch: the text refers to “Table 1” and “Table 5” for the same object; please standardize.

Recommendation
Borderline. I lean weak accept if the authors clarify the methodological role of Rule 1 under relaxation and add the requested baseline/sensitivity analysis; otherwise, I would lean weak reject on clarity and justification grounds.

Reviewer confidence: Medium. I have not checked all polyhedral details but am familiar with the IV/partial ID literature.
