This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm to compute Average Causal Effect (ACE) bounds, addressing potential violations of the faithfulness assumption through relaxation parameters and linear programming. While the problem is timely and the attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using established mathematical tools is principled, the paper suffers from several weaknesses in clarity, practical applicability, and experimental validation.

The core idea – inverting Entner’s Rule 1 to use witnesses as instrumental variables and relaxing faithfulness to allow bounded path cancellations – is interesting. However, the conceptual interpretability is weak. The distribution over bounds hinges on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances, making it difficult to understand the practical meaning of the resulting intervals. Many of the relaxations employed produce excessively wide bounds, rendering them potentially uninformative. Crucially, the method doesn’t offer a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of the bounds as confidence intervals, which is explicitly discouraged by the authors but a likely pitfall for users.

The experimental evaluation is a significant shortcoming. It's unbalanced and lacks compelling evidence of practical benefit. The comparison uses a “bias” metric that's ill-defined as it measures different quantities across methods, making any comparative assessment meaningless. The influenza dataset results (ACE in (-0.23, 0.64)) are particularly uninformative, raising serious concerns about the method’s practical performance in realistic scenarios.

Technically, the approach appears sound, and the supplemental material provides detailed derivations. However, the overall context and certain derivations remain unclear. The paper defers choosing relaxation parameters to future work, severely limiting practical application and assessment. The algorithmic description is complex, with numerous subtly different parameters, making the paper difficult to follow and likely irreproducible without significant effort. While the combination of existing techniques is original and distinct from recent related work, such as Geiger, Janzing, and Schölkopf (UAI2014) on causal bounds, the paper fails to adequately acknowledge and cite this closely related work when claiming novelty in analytical bounds.

The effectiveness of the back-substitution refinement step is questionable, and the paper lacks empirical evidence demonstrating its impact. The explanation for why multiple bounds arise – different admissible sets for different witnesses – is insufficient. The sensitivity of the method to sample size is a critical concern; results reported for 5000 points may not generalize to smaller, more typical datasets. Table 1, along with other reported summaries, doesn’t allow for a proper gauging of WPP’s benefit over standard faithfulness-based point estimates.

The paper’s tone occasionally veers into emotive or dismissive language regarding Bayesian and theory-driven approaches, creating an unnecessary bias in the evaluation. The authors should adopt a more neutral stance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of existing techniques.
*   Principled attempt to address faithfulness violations.
*   Potential for future development and refinement.
*   Direction and ideas have merit.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak conceptual interpretability.
*   Overly wide and often uninformative bounds.
*   Poor and unbalanced experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of clear guidance on interpreting the bounds.
*   Unclear explanation of key components (relaxation parameters, witness role).
*   Reproducibility concerns.
*   Deferral of critical parameter selection.



**Specific Points for Revision:**

*   **Clarify the role of the witness W:** Explain its purpose in identifying Z but acknowledge its non-necessity for ACE computation.
*   **Explain relaxation parameters:** Provide intuition for the parameters and their relationship to weak interactions.
*   **Describe the search step:** Elaborate on how it relates to bound computation and the dependence of admissible sets Z on relaxation parameters θ.
*   **Address ambiguous sentences:** Revise sentences like “Background … used here” and “max. 10 free parameters.” Correct typos.
*   **Cite related work:** Explicitly cite Geiger, Janzing, and Schölkopf (UAI2014) and other relevant publications.
*   **Evaluate back-substitution:** Provide empirical evidence of its impact and justify its inclusion.
*   **Explain multiple bounds:** Clearly explain why different witnesses yield different bounds.
*   **Address sample size sensitivity:** Discuss the implications of the 5000-point simulation and potential limitations for smaller datasets.
*   **Provide meaningful comparison:** Revise the evaluation to allow for a proper assessment of WPP’s benefits over standard estimators.
*   **Neutralize tone:** Avoid biased language and dismissive evaluations of alternative approaches.
*   **Guidance on Interpretation:** Provide clearer interpretation and guidance on what the derived bounds mean in practice, emphasizing they are *not* confidence intervals.



In conclusion, while the WPP algorithm presents an interesting theoretical contribution, significant revisions are needed to address the issues of clarity, experimental validation, and practical applicability before it can be considered a substantial advance in the field. The paper's significance currently lies more in its direction and ideas than in its immediate readiness for practitioners.
