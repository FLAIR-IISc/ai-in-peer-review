This paper presents a novel method for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) for binary variables when the full covariate causal structure is unknown. The approach bridges two distinct lines of work: it uses the witness-based search procedure of Entner et al. (2013), which relies on conditional independencies, to motivate a set of relaxed constraints within a linear programming framework for causal bounds, in the style of Ramsahai (2012). Instead of using a witness to identify an admissible set for point estimation under the faithfulness assumption, the authors use it to inform a more robust bounding procedure that allows for violations of faithfulness.

The main strength of the paper is its originality and potential significance. The idea of softening a strong assumption like faithfulness, while still leveraging the statistical patterns it would otherwise identify to tighten causal bounds, is an important and practical contribution. It offers a sensible middle ground between point estimators that rely on strong, often untestable assumptions and standard instrumental variable bounds that can be too wide to be useful. This could be a valuable tool for practitioners conducting observational studies.

However, the paper suffers from a major weakness in its methodological clarity. The central justification for using Entner et al.'s rules is not well-established once the faithfulness assumption is dropped. These rules derive their causal interpretation directly from faithfulness; without it, it is not obvious what causal information a witness/admissible set pair provides. The authors suggest the rules could inform the choice of relaxation parameters but explicitly leave this connection for future work. This is a critical gap. The authors need to clearly articulate why satisfying these conditions, even approximately, justifies the subsequent bounding procedure.

Minor points:
1.  The wording "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is imprecise. Unfaithfulness implies the existence of extra conditional independencies, whereas the framework seems to model near-independencies by bounding the magnitude of a dependency.
2.  The experiments would be more convincing if they included a direct comparison with Entner et al.'s original method, treating its "don't know" output as uninformative [-1, 1] bounds.
3.  On p.8, the interpretation that a negative ACE indicates a desirable vaccine needs clarification in the context of the variables defined (Y=1 for hospitalization).
4.  There is a typo on p.8, referring to "Table 1" as "Table 5".

In summary, the paper introduces a promising idea but needs to significantly clarify the connection between its search procedure and its bounding machinery to be considered a complete contribution.
