Summary
This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a method to bound the average causal effect (ACE) under possible violations of faithfulness. The key idea is to invert Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes Rule 1: treat a “witness” W as an instrument conditional on an admissible set Z, but allow bounded violations of the faithfulness implications using relaxation parameters θ = (w, x, y, β, β̄). These relaxations translate into linear inequalities that define a polytope over causal response variables; ACE bounds are obtained by linear programming. The authors also derive looser algebraic bounds enabling a fast “back-substitution” refinement. A Bayesian layer places Dirichlet priors on P(Y,X,W|Z) and P(Z) and returns a posterior distribution over ACE bounds. Experiments include synthetic studies and an influenza vaccination dataset.

Relative to prior work, WPP synthesizes instrumental-variable bounds (Balke & Pearl 97; Dawid 02; Ramsahai 12), faithfulness-based covariate selection (Entner et al., AISTATS’13), and polyhedral/inequality approaches, and offers a quantitative way to model “weak unfaithfulness.” Closely related and missing from the main text when claiming novelty of analytical bounds is Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf (UAI’14), who also study bounding with controlled violations of standard assumptions.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LP formulation and the mapping from relaxation parameters to linear constraints are sound; the supplement appears to contain the essential derivations. The algebraic/back-substitution approximation is reasonable but not proven tight; its empirical benefit is asserted but not convincingly quantified.
- Conceptual interpretability: The output is a posterior over bounds, which depends on (i) the independence tests/selectors that identify (W,Z), (ii) the chosen θ, and (iii) the Dirichlet priors. Without guidance on θ, the posterior over bounds is difficult to interpret, and there is a risk that readers misread an interval as a point estimate.
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic experiments are unbalanced (n=5000 throughout; atypically large), compare to limited baselines, and use an error metric that conflates incomparable quantities (point estimates vs intervals). The claimed “bias” comparisons are not meaningful because interval estimators should be judged by coverage and width (or decision loss), not distance to a point. The influenza example yields the IV interval (−0.23, 0.64), which is indeed wide and uninformative; WPP reports a narrower (−0.10, 0.17) under chosen θ but does not establish that the assumed relaxations are appropriate or robust. Overall, the experiments do not substantiate practical reliability.

Clarity
The paper is ambitious but hard to follow in places: the search step for (W,Z), the precise role of W as “witness” (used to identify Z; not needed to compute ACE once Z is fixed), the dependence of admissible sets on θ, and the aggregation across multiple (W,Z) pairs are insufficiently explained. The algorithmic description and the interplay among subtly different parameters (ζ, κ, ω, η, δ) will be difficult to reproduce from the main text. Several sentences are ambiguous (“Background … used here”; “max. 10 free parameters”), and there are typos and a broken Polymake URL. The tone occasionally uses emotive language about Bayesian and theory-driven approaches that should be toned down.

Originality
The problem is timely and interesting. The combination—quantified relaxations of faithfulness, LP-based ACE bounds, and a Bayesian posterior over bounds—is original and distinct from standard IV bounds and from Entner et al.’s point-identification under faithfulness. The analytical/back-substitution angle is a useful addition. Please cite and contrast with Geiger–Janzing–Schölkopf (UAI’14) and related inequality-based or information-theoretic constraint methods.

Significance
The contribution is significant mainly for direction-setting: it offers a principled way to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar tools. However, the practical readiness is limited: bounds can be very wide for many relaxations, θ selection is deferred, and the evaluation leaves open whether WPP provides clear gains over standard faithfulness-based or IV analyses in realistic sample sizes.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Interpretation and guidance:
  - Clearly explain that posterior samples are over bounds, not over ACE; provide guidance to avoid misinterpretation. Consider decision-theoretic summaries (e.g., minimax-regret, midpoint with caveats) if a single actionable estimate is needed.
  - Explain why multiple bounds arise (different admissible sets from different witnesses) and propose aggregation strategies: intersection for conservatism, union for coverage, or model averaging weighted by posterior evidence for each (W,Z).
- Relaxation parameters:
  - Provide intuition for w, x, y, β, β̄ as bounds on specific local violations (e.g., path cancellations, strength of W–U association). Map them to interpretable scales (risk differences or odds ratios). Include sensitivity plots and default priors/heuristics; consider placing priors on θ and marginalizing.
- Search and dependence on θ:
  - Make explicit how the search for (W,Z) interacts with θ and whether the search is re-run as θ changes. Clarify the falsification step and decision thresholds (BDeu settings), and report how often models are rejected.
- Back-substitution:
  - Quantify its impact: report runtime and bound-width differences vs the full LP, and show whether it changes accept/reject decisions. The current evidence is anecdotal.
- Evaluation:
  - Replace the “bias” metric with interval-specific criteria: coverage of the true ACE, mean width, and decision loss under a simple utility. Report performance across smaller n (e.g., 300–1000), where approximate independencies are less reliable.
  - Add baselines: Entner et al.’s point estimator; standard IV with and without monotonicity/LATE; methods that bound under partial confounding (e.g., Geiger et al., UAI’14).
  - For the influenza data, justify θ, assess instrument strength, perform sensitivity analysis over θ, and report how the interval changes. Make clear that the narrower WPP interval reflects additional assumptions beyond IV.
- Reproducibility:
  - Provide a complete algorithmic specification and hyperparameter settings; clarify all symbols; fix typos; ensure code and data scripts reproduce the reported numbers.
- W’s role:
  - State explicitly: W is used to witness Z via Rule 1 tests; once Z is accepted under θ, ACE bounds depend on (X,Y,Z) and the relaxations, not on W itself.
- Related work and tone:
  - Cite and discuss Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf (UAI’14) and other inequality-based approaches; moderate language regarding Bayesian and theory-driven methods.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely challenge: robustness to weak unfaithfulness.
- Sound LP formulation; thoughtful use of relaxations; novel algebraic/back-substitution approximation.
- Clear distinction between identification and bounding; pragmatic Bayesian layer.

Cons
- Conceptual interpretability of a posterior over bounds is weak without θ guidance.
- Many relaxations yield wide, practically uninformative intervals; no preferred point summary.
- Experimental design and metrics do not convincingly demonstrate practical value; strong sensitivity to sample size likely.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; some derivations and algorithmic details are hard to follow.

Overall
A principled and original direction with solid technical components, but the paper is not yet practitioner-ready. With clearer exposition, stronger and fairer empirical evaluation (including smaller samples and better interval metrics), guidance on choosing θ, and improved reproducibility, this could become a valuable contribution. I recommend revision along the lines above.
