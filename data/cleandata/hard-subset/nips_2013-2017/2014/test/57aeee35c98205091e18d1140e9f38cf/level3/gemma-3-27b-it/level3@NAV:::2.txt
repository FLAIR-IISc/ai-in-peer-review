## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes a novel approach to bounding Average Causal Effects (ACE) in observational studies by combining inverse Entner’s Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness (path-cancellation), and linear programming (LP). The core idea is to reinterpret Entner’s Rule to identify instrumental variables and use LP to establish bounds on the ACE, accommodating potential violations of faithfulness through adjustable relaxation parameters. The authors aim to provide a more robust and practical method for causal inference, complementing existing techniques. 

The approach builds upon established work in causal inference, including the do-calculus [Pearl, 2014], instrumental variables [Manski, 2007], and prior bounding techniques [Dawid et al., 1990; Ramsahai, 2009]. It extends the work of Entner et al. [2013] by moving beyond point estimates and incorporating a framework for handling uncertainty due to potential unmeasured confounders. The use of a Bayesian approach to incorporate uncertainty within the LP framework is a potentially valuable addition, moving beyond purely frequentist methods.

The paper’s strength lies in the attempt to unify several concepts into a coherent framework for ACE bounding. The theoretical exposition is largely comprehensive and the potential for tighter bounds, even without algorithmic optimization, is promising. The introduction of relaxation parameters offers a degree of flexibility in dealing with violations of the faithfulness assumption.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in reverse is not adequately explained, and the authors rely on the assumption of a specific graph structure (Fig. 2) without sufficient discussion. Critically, there are no clear guidelines provided for selecting the relaxation parameters, which demonstrably affect the resulting bounds. The lack of theoretical justification or empirical guidance for parameter selection is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, stating it requires future work, its absence hinders immediate applicability. 

Furthermore, the experimental implementation is poorly described. Parameter definitions in referenced tables are unclear or missing, making reproducibility difficult. The text contains typos and incorrect table references, impacting clarity. While the method shows promise, the experiments are limited and lack sufficient validation of its generality and robustness. The reported results, while encouraging, do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods across a wider range of scenarios.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Potential for tighter ACE bounds, novel combination of existing ideas, and a potentially more robust approach to causal inference than relying solely on faithfulness.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of clear justification for key assumptions, absence of guidance for parameter selection, poorly described experimental setup, limited validation, and insufficient clarity due to typos and reference errors.



**Overall Assessment:** The paper presents an interesting idea with potential, but its current form is not ready for publication in a highly selective venue like NIPS. The theoretical shortcomings and practical limitations regarding parameter selection and reproducibility need to be addressed. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing these concerns.
