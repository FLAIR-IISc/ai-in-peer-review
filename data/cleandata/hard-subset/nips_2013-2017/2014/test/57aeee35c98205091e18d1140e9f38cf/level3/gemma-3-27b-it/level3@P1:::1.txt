This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y in observational studies, addressing the challenge of unmeasured confounding. The approach leverages observational conditional independencies, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for varying degrees of “path cancellations” and employing a linear programming approach combined with Bayesian inference.  The work builds upon previous research in causal inference, particularly the do-calculus of Pearl [14], causal discovery algorithms like the PC algorithm [22], and the work of Entner et al. [6] and Ramsahai [16] on bounding causal effects.  The core idea is to invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1, using potential instrumental variables to bound the ACE, rather than identifying back-door adjustment sets.

The paper’s contribution lies in providing a framework that aims to offer greater control over violations of the faithfulness assumption, which is known to be problematic in practice.  By introducing constraints on causal paths and utilizing linear programming and Bayesian techniques, the authors attempt to provide more robust bounds on the ACE than those relying solely on faithfulness. The method is presented with both theoretical analysis and experimental evaluation, demonstrating its application to both synthetic and real-world data (influenza vaccination).

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Estimating causal effects in observational studies remains a crucial challenge, and this paper tackles the issue of unmeasured confounding with a potentially valuable approach.
*   **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The combination of relaxing the faithfulness assumption, inverting Entner et al.’s rule, employing linear programming, and incorporating Bayesian inference is innovative.
*   **Practical Focus:** The paper’s emphasis on bounding the ACE rather than seeking point estimates is a strength, acknowledging the inherent uncertainty in observational studies.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of experiments with both synthetic and real data provides some empirical support for the proposed methodology.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and motivates the need for a more robust method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity Regarding Entner et al.’s Rule:** The central motivation—inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1—remains unclear.  Without the faithfulness assumption, the conditions of Rule 1 (W ⊥ Y |Z and W ⊥ Y |Z ∪ {X}) do not immediately suggest a clear causal interpretation or justification for their use in bounding the ACE. The paper states this connection is not further resolved, which is a significant oversight.  It’s crucial to understand *why* these conditions, when faithfulness doesn’t hold, are informative.
*   **Dependence on Entner et al.’s Rule Questionable:** A primary concern is whether the machinery relies critically on the conditions derived from Entner et al.  The authors should clarify to what extent the proposed method would function – or differ – if these conditions were not employed. This requires a careful dissection of the methodology.
*   **Interpretation of “Almost Violations”:** The phrasing “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is somewhat misleading.  Unfaithfulness implies *extra* conditional independencies beyond those explained by the causal structure.  The proposed framework appears to allow for “almost violations” by bounding the *magnitude* of dependencies, which is a different concept.
*   **Comparison with Entner et al.’s Original Method:** The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison with Entner et al.’s original method, specifically using their stringent thresholds for accepting the conditions of Rule 1. Treating cases where Rule 1 doesn't hold as providing uninformative bounds (e.g., a very wide interval) would offer a useful baseline.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the paper’s readability.  The interpretation of the ACE in the influenza vaccination example is unclear (p. 8), and there is a citation/table error (“Table 1” incorrectly referred to as “Table 5”).

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically complex methodology. While the overall approach appears sensible, the lack of clarity regarding the role of Entner et al.’s rule and the questionable dependence on its conditions raise concerns about the technical soundness. The experimental evaluation provides some support, but further analysis is needed to assess the robustness of the method.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the technical details are challenging to follow. The lack of a clear explanation of the connection between Entner et al.’s Rule 1 and the proposed methodology significantly hinders understanding.  More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The combination of techniques presented in this paper is novel.  However, the originality is somewhat diminished by the lack of clarity surrounding the core motivation and the dependence on existing work (Entner et al., Ramsahai).

**Significance:** If the technical issues can be addressed and the methodology is shown to be robust, this work could have a significant impact on the field of causal inference. Providing more reliable bounds on the ACE in observational studies would be a valuable contribution. However, the significance is currently limited by the unanswered questions about the core method.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel approach to a difficult problem in causal inference.
*   Combines existing techniques in a creative way.
*   Provides some empirical evidence of its effectiveness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Key methodological connections (role of Entner et al.’s rule) are unclear.
*   Dependence on Entner et al.’s conditions is questionable.
*   Lack of a thorough comparison with existing methods.
*   Clarity issues hinder understanding of the technical details.



**Recommendation:**  I recommend **rejection** with the possibility of resubmission after significant revisions. The paper presents an interesting idea, but the lack of clarity surrounding the core motivation and the questionable dependence on Entner et al.’s rule are significant concerns. Addressing these issues, providing a more thorough comparison with existing methods, and improving the clarity of the presentation are essential for the paper to be considered for publication.
