This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, which is a fundamental problem in causal inference. The authors introduce a new methodology that exploits observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. This approach relaxes the widely used faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths.

The main idea of the paper is to invert the usage of Entner's Rule 1, which is a method for identifying instrumental variables in causal graphs. The authors propose to use this rule to generate candidate instrumental variables, which are then used to bound the average causal effect (ACE) using a linear programming formulation. The approach also introduces relaxations of the faithfulness assumption, which allows for more flexibility in the estimation of the ACE.

The paper presents a comprehensive theoretical exposition of the proposed method, including the derivation of the linear programming formulation and the relaxations of the faithfulness assumption. The authors also provide a Bayesian learning framework for estimating the bounds on the ACE, which takes into account the uncertainty in the estimation.

The experimental results presented in the paper are promising, showing that the proposed method can provide tighter bounds on the ACE compared to existing approaches. However, the experiments are limited, and the generality and robustness of the method are not fully assessed.
