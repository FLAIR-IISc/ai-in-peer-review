This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE) of a binary X on a binary Y in observational settings with potential confounding and limited causal knowledge. The key idea is to invert Entner et al.’s “Rule 1”: rather than using conditional independences to justify back-door adjustment (point identification under faithfulness), the paper uses the same witness–admissible-set pairs (W,Z) to construct IV-like bounds on ACE, conditional on Z, while explicitly relaxing faithfulness. Faithfulness violations are encoded as bounded deviations from local independences via parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) that cap how strongly W can directly affect Y, how much W relates to unmeasured confounding, and how far observed conditionals can deviate from their latent counterparts. Bounds are obtained by modifying Dawid/Ramsahai/Balke-Pearl linear-programming polytopes; a Bayesian scheme puts Dirichlet priors on observable contingency tables (constrained by the inequalities) and returns a posterior distribution over bounds. To reduce computational cost, the authors derive algebraic inequalities and a back-substitution “message-passing”-style algorithm that yields conservative box constraints and fast approximate bounds. Experiments on synthetic data compare WPP to naïve back-door estimators and a faithfulness-based estimator; a flu-vaccine dataset illustrates narrower bounds than the classical binary-IV bounds.

Relation to prior work: The approach sits between faithfulness-based discovery (PC, Entner-Hoyer-Spirtes, chain/Y-structures) and partial identification with instruments (Balke-Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai/Lauritzen), plus Manski’s decision-theoretic perspective on bounds. The novelty is to treat (near-)independences as evidence for weakening rather than eliminating certain causal pathways, producing calibrated relaxations that interpolate between point estimates (faithfulness) and wide IV bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual synthesis: The inversion of Entner’s Rule 1 to generate candidate conditional-IV pairs and then relax them is elegant and practically appealing when exact faithfulness is dubious.
- Sound partial-identification machinery: The LP formulation, link to polytopes, and Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty over observables are appropriate and conservative.
- Practicality: The algebraic bounds and back-substitution algorithm offer a scalable approximation with clear monotonicity in the relaxation parameters.
- Empirical illustration: Synthetic studies probe cases with and without valid witnesses; the vaccine case shows tighter bounds than standard IV without assuming exact faithfulness.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality (technical soundness)
- Role of Entner’s conditions without faithfulness: Once faithfulness is relaxed, Rule 1’s premises no longer imply the causal separation that justifies using W as an instrument given Z. The paper treats (W,Z) from Rule 1 as a proposal mechanism but does not make explicit why those pairs are preferable—e.g., why they should induce smaller relaxation parameters or tighter/valid bounds than alternative choices. Please clarify the formal relationship: under what quantitative “approximate faithfulness” conditions do Rule-1 pairs guarantee nonvacuous or tighter bounds?
- Calibration of relaxation parameters: The method’s informativeness hinges on w, x, y, β, β̄. The paper leaves their choice largely to the user and a Supplement. A principled mapping from observed (near) independences—e.g., via odds-ratio/partial association bounds, posterior predictive checks, or sensitivity curves—to these parameters would increase credibility and reproducibility. At a minimum, report systematic sensitivity analyses (coverage vs. width) and guidance on defaults tied to effect sizes of tested independences.
- Model falsification: The 95% rejection threshold in the constrained-Dirichlet sampler is pragmatic but ad hoc. How sensitive are results to this threshold? Can you report the frequency of false acceptances/rejections under known generative models? Also, when the back-substitution expected table falls outside the feasible region, the fallback to approximate bounds should be quantified (how often, how much wider).

Clarity
- The phrase “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is confusing. Unfaithfulness implies extra independences; this framework bounds dependence magnitudes to allow near-independence, not “violations” of independence. Rephrase to “encode approximate independences by bounding the magnitude of direct and confounding pathways.”
- The connection from the statistical test for W ⊥ Y | Z ∪ {X} to specific numeric bounds on y, x, β is not spelled out. A short subsection giving an illustrative mapping (e.g., from Bayes factor or posterior of mutual information to parameter ranges) would help.
- Minor: Page/figure/table cross-references need checking (“Table 1” on p.8 appears as “Table 5” elsewhere).

Originality
- The overall combination—using witness search to seed conditional-IV bounds with parameterized relaxations, plus a Bayesian posterior over bounds and a back-substitution approximation—appears novel and useful. Please clarify how your algebraic bounds extend Ramsahai’s results beyond fixing w, and what is genuinely new vs. prior derivations.

Significance
- If the parameter calibration and witness-selection rationale are tightened, WPP could be a valuable default sensitivity/bounding tool in observational studies, offering a principled middle ground between faithfulness-based point estimates and very wide IV bounds. As is, the dependence on user-chosen relaxations and the unclear necessity of Entner’s conditions limit reproducibility and adoption.

Empirical evaluation
- Please add a comparison to Entner et al.’s method using their recommended stringent thresholds, counting “don’t know” as the uninformative [−1,1] interval. This would fairly contrast your conservative intervals against their high-specificity point estimates.
- Include ablations: (i) random or heuristic (W,Z) selection without Rule 1; (ii) different mappings from near-independence strength to (w,x,y,β); (iii) computational/runtime trade-offs for LP vs. back-substitution.
- Report coverage (proportion of times the true ACE lies within WPP bounds) and average width, in addition to your “error” metric. These are standard for partial identification.

Flu example
- Coding clarification: Earlier you state Y=1 indicates hospitalization; then you write “X=1 (flu shot) and Y=1 (not hospitalized)”. Please reconcile. If ACE = P(Y=1|do X=1) − P(Y=1|do X=0) and Y=1 means hospitalized, a negative ACE indeed indicates a beneficial vaccine (reduced hospitalization).
- Explain the exclusion of GRP from admissible sets and the rationale for preferring DM with {AGE, SEX} under your relaxation; provide sensitivity of the bounds to including/excluding GRP and to parameter choices.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, common problem with a principled, conservative approach.
- Bridges causal discovery and partial identification in a novel way.
- Provides both exact LP-based and fast approximate algorithms, with code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently justified reliance on Entner’s conditions after relaxing faithfulness; unclear whether they are essential or merely heuristic.
- Lack of principled calibration for relaxation parameters and limited sensitivity/coverage reporting.
- Some clarity and referencing issues that impede reproducibility.

Overall evaluation: Interesting and potentially impactful contribution to causal inference under weak assumptions. With clearer theoretical justification for witness selection under approximate faithfulness, a principled parameter calibration strategy, and more thorough empirical validation (coverage/width and baseline comparisons), this paper would be significantly strengthened.
