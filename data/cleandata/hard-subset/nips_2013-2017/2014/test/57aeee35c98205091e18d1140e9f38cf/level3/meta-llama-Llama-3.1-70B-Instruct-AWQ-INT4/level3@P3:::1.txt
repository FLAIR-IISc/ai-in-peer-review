This paper introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in the presence of confounding variables when the causal structure is not fully known. The authors modify the results of Entner et al. to generate candidate instrumental variables and use a linear programming approach to obtain bounds on the average causal effect (ACE). The method, called the Witness Protection Program (WPP), is a compromise between point estimators based on faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables.

The main idea of the paper is sensible and potentially useful, but the reviewer has not checked all technical details. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference, and the approach is well-motivated. However, the reviewer has some concerns regarding the role of Entner et al.'s rule conditions without the faithfulness assumption. The conditions do not obviously imply causal structure when faithfulness is dropped, and it is unclear how they inform the choice of relaxation parameters.

The reviewer suspects that Entner et al.'s conditions might inform the choice of relaxation parameters, but the paper explicitly leaves this connection unresolved. The reviewer also doubts that the proposed machinery depends crucially on Entner et al.'s conditions and asks for clarification on why those conditions matter for the method and experiments.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear. However, the reviewer has some minor comments. The wording "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is odd, as unfaithfulness means extra independences. The framework seems to allow "almost violations" by bounding dependency magnitude. The reviewer suggests reporting Entner et al.'s method performance using their stringent thresholds, treating "don't know" as uninformative bounds, for comparison.

The reviewer also requests clarification on the interpretation in the second paragraph of page 8. With X=1 (flu shot) and Y=1 (not hospitalized), why does a negative ACE indicate a desirable vaccine? Additionally, there is a citation/table error: "Table 1" on page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

Overall, the paper is interesting, but the details are hard to follow, and key methodological connections need clarification. The reviewer believes that the paper has the potential to contribute to the field of causal inference, but it requires further refinement and clarification.
