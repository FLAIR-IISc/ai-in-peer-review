- Proposes a novel algorithm (Witness Protection Program, WPP) for estimating causal effects in observational studies.  
- WPP uses linear programming to compute bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) under possible faithfulness violations.  
- The approach is an original combination of existing techniques and is distinct from recent related work.  
- Provides a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools.  
- Uses relaxation parameters and linear programming in a technically sound way to compute ACE bounds.  
- Conceptual interpretability is weak: the distribution over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances.  
- Many relaxations yield very wide, potentially uninformative bounds that may be useless in practice.  
- The method does not provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates.  
- Experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced, undermining practical claims.  
- The "bias" metric for comparisons is inappropriate because it measures different quantities across methods.  
- Results on the influenza dataset are uninformative and raise concerns about practical performance.  
- Clarify interpretation of bounds (they are not confidence intervals) and provide practical guidance for users.  
- Explain the role of the witness W, give intuition for relaxation parameters, and relate them to weak interactions.  
- Describe how the search step relates to bounds computation and how admissible sets Z depend on relaxation parameters θ.  
- Fix unclear sentences and typos, and cite closely related work when claiming novelty of analytical bounds.  
- Question the effectiveness of the back-substitution refinement step and provide empirical evidence of its impact.  
- Overall: valuable ideas but requires significant improvements in clarity, interpretability, and experiments to be a strong contribution.
