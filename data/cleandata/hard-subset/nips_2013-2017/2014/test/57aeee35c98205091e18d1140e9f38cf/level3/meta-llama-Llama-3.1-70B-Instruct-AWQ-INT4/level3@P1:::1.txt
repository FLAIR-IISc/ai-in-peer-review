This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, leveraging Entner et al.'s rule and a Ramsahai-style method to bound the average causal effect (ACE) for binary variables. The main idea is sensible and potentially useful, but several concerns and clarifications are needed to fully appreciate the method's strengths and limitations.

The paper's core contribution is the development of a Witness Protection Program (WPP) that provides a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE by exploiting observational conditional independencies. The WPP framework is built upon the relaxation of the faithfulness assumption, allowing for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the ACE via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

One of the major concerns is the unclear role of Entner et al.'s rule conditions without the faithfulness assumption. The conditions do not obviously imply causal structure when faithfulness is dropped, and it is unclear how they inform the choice of relaxation parameters. The paper explicitly leaves this connection unresolved, which raises questions about the necessity of Entner et al.'s conditions for the method and experiments. Clarification is needed on why these conditions matter for the proposed machinery.

Additionally, the wording "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is odd, as unfaithfulness means extra independences. The framework seems to allow "almost violations" by bounding dependency magnitude, which may be misleading. Furthermore, it would be helpful to report Entner et al.'s method performance using their stringent thresholds, treating "don't know" as uninformative bounds, for comparison.

The paper also requires clarification on the interpretation of the results in the second paragraph on page 8. With X=1 (flu shot) and Y=1 (not hospitalized), a negative ACE indicates a desirable vaccine, but this seems counterintuitive. Moreover, there appears to be a citation/table error, as "Table 1" on page 8 is referred to as "Table 5" in the text.

In terms of the paper's strengths, the WPP framework provides a novel compromise between point estimators given by the faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. The approach is potentially useful for observational studies, and the R code is available for implementation. However, the details are hard to follow, and key methodological connections need clarification.

Overall, the paper is interesting, but further clarification and resolution of the mentioned concerns are necessary to fully appreciate the method's strengths and limitations. The paper's quality is good, but the presentation could be improved for better understanding.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies.
* The WPP framework provides a compromise between point estimators and bounds based on instrumental variables.
* The approach is potentially useful for observational studies, and the R code is available for implementation.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The unclear role of Entner et al.'s rule conditions without the faithfulness assumption.
* The lack of clarification on the connection between Entner et al.'s conditions and the choice of relaxation parameters.
* The odd wording and potential misinterpretation of "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence."
* The need for clarification on the interpretation of the results in the second paragraph on page 8.
* The citation/table error.

Recommendation:

The paper is interesting, but further clarification and resolution of the mentioned concerns are necessary to fully appreciate the method's strengths and limitations. The authors should address the major concerns, provide clarification on the methodological connections, and improve the presentation for better understanding. With revisions, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference.
