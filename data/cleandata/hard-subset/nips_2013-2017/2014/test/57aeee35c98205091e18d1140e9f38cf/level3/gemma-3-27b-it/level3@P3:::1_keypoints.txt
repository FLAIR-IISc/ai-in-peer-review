- Proposes a novel method to bound average causal effect (ACE) under unobserved confounding by inverting Entner et al.’s rule and using a Ramsahai (2012)-style approach with relaxed faithfulness.
- Core idea of identifying instrumental variables via inverted Entner et al. rule and allowing bounded violations of local independence is sensible and potentially useful.
- Paper extends existing NIPS literature on causal inference and builds on prior work (Pearl, Spirtes, Dawid, Ramsahai).
- Presentation is difficult to follow and key methodological connections are not clearly articulated.
- Major concern: unclear why Entner et al.’s Rule 1 conditions remain relevant or informative when faithfulness is relaxed and path cancellations are allowed.
- The paper fails to justify theoretically how Entner et al.’s conditions interact with the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄).
- Experiments do not adequately address the core question of why Entner et al.’s conditions matter under relaxed faithfulness.
- Wording issue: “violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is awkward and potentially misleading.
- Suggest reporting Entner et al.’s original method as a baseline (treating “don’t know” as uninformative/infinite bounds).
- Interpretation error/confusion: unclear why a negative ACE (given X=1 flu shot, Y=1 not hospitalized) is labeled a desirable vaccine.
- Minor error: wrong table citation (Table 1 referred to as Table 5).
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, practical significance, and potential theoretical soundness by combining existing techniques.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of clarity, insufficient justification for retaining Entner conditions, incomplete explanation of parameter interplay, and weak experimental validation.
- Overall: interesting and potentially significant contribution but currently unclear and incomplete; recommend rejection with encouragement to revise addressing theoretical justification, clarity, and experiments.
