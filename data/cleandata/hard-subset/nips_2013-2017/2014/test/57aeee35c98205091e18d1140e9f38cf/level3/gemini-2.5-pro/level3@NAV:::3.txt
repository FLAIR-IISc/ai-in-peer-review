This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), an algorithm to compute bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) while relaxing the standard faithfulness assumption. The core idea is to adapt the witness-based search for admissible sets from Entner et al. (2013) and, instead of finding a single point estimate, use linear programming with relaxation parameters to generate bounds that account for potential weak unfaithfulness. The work is a novel combination of existing search procedures and bounding techniques (e.g., Dawid, 2003; Ramsahai, 2012) applied to a timely and important problem in causal inference.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its principled attempt to quantify the impact of near-violations of faithfulness, a critical and often overlooked issue in observational studies. The approach of using familiar mathematical tools like linear programming to formalize these violations is sound and the technical details appear solid. The significance of this work is in the direction it proposes—moving beyond brittle point estimates towards more robust interval-based reasoning—rather than its immediate readiness for practitioners.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its practical application and evaluation. A central issue is that the method for choosing the relaxation parameters, which are critical to the entire procedure, is deferred to future work. This makes the current method difficult to assess or apply. The conceptual interpretation of the output—a distribution over bounds—is also weak, and the authors do not provide clear guidance on how to use these intervals, which are not standard confidence intervals.

The experimental evaluation is another major concern. The reported bounds are often extremely wide and potentially uninformative, as evidenced by the influenza dataset result (ACE in [-0.23, 0.64]). The “bias” metric used for comparison is ill-defined for interval estimates, making the quantitative comparison against point estimators meaningless. The evaluation fails to demonstrate a clear benefit of WPP over standard faithfulness-based estimates and does not address sensitivity to sample size. Furthermore, the paper’s clarity could be improved; the algorithmic description is difficult to follow, several parameters are introduced without sufficient intuition, and key related work on analytical bounds (e.g., Geiger et al., UAI 2014) is not cited.

In summary, while the paper introduces an interesting and original idea for a crucial problem, its practical utility is undermined by a lack of guidance on parameter selection, weak conceptual interpretation, and a poor experimental evaluation. The work has potential but requires substantial revision to clarify its contributions and demonstrate its practical value.
