- Introduces a novel method to bound the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data by inverting Entner et al.'s Rule 1.  
- Reinterprets covariate selection to create an instrumental-variable-like setup rather than an admissible set for point estimation.  
- Integrates a relaxation of the faithfulness assumption via "path-cancellation" encoded as inequality constraints.  
- Formulates the bounding problem as a linear program (LP), building on Dawid and Ramsahai’s LP frameworks.  
- Novel contribution: data-driven structuring of LP constraints based on observed conditional independencies.  
- Strength: empirical results show narrower and more accurate bounds than existing approaches.  
- Major theoretical weakness: no clear justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction or explicit causal-structure assumptions.  
- Key methodological gap: no generic criteria or guidance for selecting relaxation parameters, making the choice ad-hoc.  
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: poorly described experiments, opaque algorithm details, numerous typos, and incorrect table references.  
- Originality: a novel combination of existing ideas rather than a wholly new paradigm.  
- Potential significance is high (tighter, robust ACE bounds), but limited experiments and insufficient validation leave generality and robustness unproven.  
- Overall conclusion: promising approach hindered by an unjustified core assumption, lack of parameter-selection guidance, and weak empirical clarity.
