This paper proposes a novel approach for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) when faced with unobserved confounding, building upon Entner et al.'s work and employing a Ramsahai (2012)-style method with relaxed faithfulness assumptions. The core idea – inverting Entner et al.'s rule to identify instrumental variables and incorporating bounded violations of local independence – is sensible and potentially useful for practical causal inference. The paper attempts to provide a more robust framework than relying solely on the faithfulness assumption. It's positioned well within the NIPS literature on causal inference, extending existing techniques for dealing with unidentifiable effects and building upon prior work on instrumental variables and causal discovery (e.g., Pearl, Spirtes et al., Dawid, Ramsahai).

However, despite the promising premise, several aspects of the paper require significant clarification. While I haven’t exhaustively verified all technical details, the overall presentation is difficult to follow and key methodological connections are not clearly articulated.

My major concern revolves around the role of Entner et al.’s rule’s conditions when the faithfulness assumption is relaxed. The paper argues for allowing “path cancellations,” but it's unclear *why* the conditions of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 remain relevant or informative when faithfulness no longer guarantees a meaningful causal structure. The authors state that the conditions don’t *rule out* confounding paths, but this doesn't explain why they should still be used to identify potential instrumental variables or guide the search for admissible sets. It feels as though the conditions are being retained for historical reasons, rather than due to a strong theoretical justification. I suspect these conditions might be used to inform the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄), but the paper explicitly states this connection remains unresolved. A critical clarification is needed: why do these conditions matter for the method, and how do they interact with the relaxation of faithfulness? The experiments do not seem to adequately address this question.

Several minor points detract from the paper's clarity. First, the phrasing “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is somewhat awkward. Unfaithfulness implies *extra* independences, not simply bounded dependencies. The framework, as presented, appears to allow for "almost violations" by bounding the *magnitude* of dependency, which is subtly different. Second, for comparison, it would be beneficial to report Entner et al.’s method’s performance using their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” responses as uninformative bounds (e.g., an infinite bound). This would establish a clear baseline. Third, the interpretation on p.8, second paragraph, is unclear. Given X=1 (flu shot) and Y=1 (not hospitalized), why does a *negative* ACE indicate a *desirable* vaccine? This seems counterintuitive and requires further explanation. Finally, there’s a citation/table error: “Table 1” on p.8 is incorrectly referred to as “Table 5” in the text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method offers a potentially valuable approach to bounding causal effects under weaker assumptions than traditional methods.
*   **Potential Significance:** The ability to provide bounds in the presence of unobserved confounding is practically important.
*   **Soundness (Potential):** The combination of existing techniques (Entner et al., Ramsahai) is theoretically grounded.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to follow, and key methodological connections are not clearly explained.
*   **Justification:** The continued relevance of Entner et al.’s conditions under relaxed faithfulness is not adequately justified.
*   **Completeness:** The paper feels incomplete without a clearer explanation of the interplay between the relaxation parameters and the Entner et al. conditions.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments don't strongly address the core methodological concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting paper with a potentially significant contribution to the field of causal inference. However, the details are hard to follow and the key methodological connections need substantial clarification. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit, addressing the concerns outlined above. Specifically, the authors need to provide a compelling justification for retaining the Entner et al. conditions when the faithfulness assumption is relaxed and clarify how these conditions inform the choice of relaxation parameters. Improved clarity and a more thorough experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.
