This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, which is an important and timely problem. The authors introduce the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, which uses a linear programming approach to compute bounds on the average causal effect (ACE) under possible violations of faithfulness. The WPP algorithm is an original combination of existing techniques and is sufficiently distinct from recent related work.

The paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas rather than immediate practitioner readiness. The authors provide a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The use of relaxation parameters and linear programming to compute ACE bounds is a sound technical approach.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper. The conceptual interpretability of the method is weak, as the distribution over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances. Many relaxations produce very wide, potentially uninformative bounds, which may not be useful in practice. The method does not provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates.

The experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced, undermining claims about practical usefulness. The "bias" metric used for comparisons is meaningless because it measures different quantities across methods. The influenza dataset results are uninformative and raise concerns about practical performance.

Additionally, the paper has several areas for improvement. The authors should provide clearer interpretation and guidance on what the derived bounds mean in practice (not confidence intervals). They should clarify the role of the witness W and explain and give intuition for the relaxation parameters and how they relate to weak interactions. The authors should also describe how the search step relates to bounds computation and how admissible sets Z depend on relaxation parameters θ.

Furthermore, the paper has some unclear sentences and typos that need to be fixed. The authors should cite closely related work when claiming novelty of analytical bounds. They should also question the effectiveness of the back-substitution refinement step and provide empirical evidence of its impact.

Overall, while the paper has some valuable ideas and contributions, it needs significant improvement in terms of clarity, interpretability, and experimental evaluation to be considered a strong scientific contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies.
* The WPP algorithm is an original combination of existing techniques and is sufficiently distinct from recent related work.
* The authors provide a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools.
