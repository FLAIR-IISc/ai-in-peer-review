This paper presents a novel method, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), for estimating bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data, particularly in the presence of unmeasured confounding. The core idea is to leverage conditional independence tests, as used in methods like that of Entner et al. (2013), not to make a hard decision about an admissible set for back-door adjustment, but rather to suggest a "weak" instrumental variable structure. The paper relaxes the faithfulness assumption, which is critical for Entner et al.'s method, by parameterizing potential violations as bounded dependencies. These bounds are then incorporated into a linear programming framework, extending the work of Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012), to derive a posterior distribution over the bounds on the ACE. The authors also contribute an efficient algebraic approximation to the full linear programming solution, making the method more scalable.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and difficult problem in causal inference: how to proceed when one is unwilling to fully commit to the faithfulness assumption. The proposed approach offers a principled compromise between making strong, potentially incorrect assumptions to get a point estimate (as in standard constraint-based methods) and making very weak assumptions that lead to uninformatively wide bounds. This "middle ground" is a valuable contribution and is highly relevant to practitioners of observational causal inference. The combination of Entner et al.'s search procedure with a relaxed, IV-style bounding framework is novel and clever.

2.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a complete pipeline from data to a posterior over bounds, including a Bayesian framework for handling sampling uncertainty and a model falsification step. The development of symbolic algebraic bounds and the "back-substitution algorithm" is a significant practical contribution, offering a scalable alternative to the computationally intensive linear programming approach.

3.  **Potential Impact:** If the methodological justification can be strengthened, this approach could become a valuable tool in the standard causal inference toolbox. It operationalizes the common intuition that statistical independencies which "almost" hold should provide some, albeit uncertain, information about causal effects.

### Weaknesses & Questions for the Authors

My main concern revolves around the justification for using Entner et al.'s Rule 1 as the starting point for the analysis once the faithfulness assumption is dropped.

1.  **Central Justification:** The power of Rule 1 in the original work by Entner et al. comes directly from the faithfulness assumption. If `W ⊥ Y | Z, X` and `W not ⊥ Y | Z`, faithfulness allows one to infer a specific graphical structure where Z is an admissible set. However, this paper explicitly relaxes faithfulness. It is therefore unclear what causal structure is implied by the (approximate) satisfaction of these two conditions. Why should finding a (W, Z) pair that satisfies these conditions suggest a structure that is a "small" perturbation of a standard IV model? Without faithfulness, these are just statistical properties of the distribution, and their connection to the underlying causal graph is not obvious. The paper seems to jump from observing these statistical patterns to imposing the structural constraints in equations (3)-(6) without a clear justification for this leap. Could the authors elaborate on the theoretical argument for why these specific conditions are the right ones to search for when initiating the WPP analysis?

2.  **Choice of Relaxation Parameters:** The paper leaves the choice of relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`) to the user, suggesting this can be guided by background knowledge. This is a weakness. It seems the degree to which the conditions of Rule 1 are met in the data (e.g., the strength of the `W - Y` dependence given `Z`, versus given `Z, X`) should directly inform the choice of these parameters. A more integrated method would use the data to help set these parameters, rather than treating them as arbitrary knobs. The paper defers this to future work, but it feels central to the method's coherence. Does the machinery proposed depend crucially on Rule 1, or could any variable W be chosen as a candidate instrument, with the relaxation parameters set based on some other criteria?

### Minor Comments & Suggestions

1.  **Clarity on "Violations of Faithfulness":** On page 3, the authors state their goal is to "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence." This phrasing is slightly confusing. A violation of faithfulness is typically the presence of a conditional independence that is not entailed by the graph structure (e.g., due to parameter cancellations). The framework presented here seems to be about relaxing the *implications* of faithfulness by allowing for small dependencies where faithfulness would imply a strict independence (e.g., `W` and `Y` given `X, Z`). This is more about robustness to "near-unfaithful" distributions than modeling faithfulness violations directly. Clarifying this distinction would improve the paper's precision.

2.  **Experimental Comparison:** In the experiments, the faithfulness-based method of Entner et al. is evaluated using a relaxed criterion for accepting independencies. For a fairer comparison, it would be informative to also report the performance of their method using their original, more stringent thresholds. In cases where their method returns "I don't know" (because no witness/admissible set is found), this could be treated as the uninformative interval [-1, 1] for the purposes of error calculation. This would better reflect how that method would be used in practice.

3.  **Interpretation of Influenza Study:** In the second paragraph of Section 5, the authors state "A negative ACE therefore suggests a desirable vaccine." The text defines `X=1` as receiving a flu shot and `Y=1` as being hospitalized. Therefore, `ACE = P(Y=1|do(X=1)) - P(Y=1|do(X=0))` represents the change in hospitalization probability due to the vaccine. A negative ACE indeed means the vaccine reduces hospitalization risk. While correct, this could be stated more explicitly for clarity, as the reader has to connect several pieces of information to verify the claim.

### Summary

Overall, this is an interesting and ambitious paper with a potentially significant contribution. The core idea of using statistical patterns not for hard decisions but as a guide for a relaxed bounding procedure is highly appealing. However, the paper's primary weakness is the lack of a clear theoretical justification for why the specific conditions of Entner et al.'s Rule 1 should motivate the proposed IV-like bounding structure in the absence of faithfulness. If the authors can strengthen this connection and provide more guidance on setting the relaxation parameters, the paper would be a very strong contribution.
