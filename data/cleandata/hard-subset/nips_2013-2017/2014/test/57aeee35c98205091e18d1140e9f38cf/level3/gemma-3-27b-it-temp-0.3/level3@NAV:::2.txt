## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces a novel approach to bounding Average Causal Effects (ACE) in observational studies, building upon and extending the work of Entner et al. by inverting their Rule 1, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for “path cancellations,” and formulating the problem as a Linear Program (LP). The core idea – using conditional independencies to suggest weak paths and derive bounds – is a valuable contribution to the ongoing challenge of causal inference with limited data and assumptions. The method is presented with a Bayesian learning component and attempts to address computational complexity through relaxations and a back-substitution algorithm. 

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on causal inference, instrumental variables, and faithfulness. It correctly identifies the limitations of relying solely on faithfulness and the need for methods that are robust to violations. The combination of Entner’s Rule, relaxed faithfulness, and LP formulation is a potentially interesting direction, and the initial results suggest tighter bounds compared to existing approaches, even without extensive optimization. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in reverse is not sufficiently clear. The authors mention the Fig. 2 structure but don’t explicitly state the assumptions underpinning this inversion, leaving a crucial theoretical gap. While the relaxation of faithfulness is motivated, the criteria for selecting the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) are absent, and their impact on the resulting bounds is not thoroughly discussed. The theoretical exposition, while largely comprehensive, is incomplete without addressing these points.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is weak. Algorithm details are poorly described, and parameter definitions in the referenced tables are unclear or missing, hindering reproducibility. The use of “Table 1” and “Table 5” where other tables exist creates confusion. While the synthetic experiments demonstrate stability, the real-world application lacks sufficient detail and justification for the chosen parameters. The claim of significance is undermined by the limited scope of the experiments and the lack of robust validation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of existing ideas with potential for tighter ACE bounds.
*   Addresses a critical problem in causal inference – dealing with unmeasured confounding.
*   Clear presentation of the core methodology and its theoretical foundations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clear justification for the inverse application of Entner’s Rule 1.
*   Absence of guidance on selecting relaxation parameters.
*   Poorly described experimental setup and limited validation.
*   Numerous typos and referencing errors.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising approach, but its current form is not ready for publication at a competitive conference like NIPS. The theoretical gaps and experimental shortcomings need to be addressed. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined above. A stronger theoretical justification, a more detailed experimental evaluation, and improved clarity are essential for this work to reach its full potential.
