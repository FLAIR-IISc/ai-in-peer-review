This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, leveraging observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors introduce a new ACE bounding method using inverse Entner's Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness, inequality constraints, and LP formulation. The obtained bounds are narrow and appear more accurate than existing approaches, despite the algorithm not being optimized.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. A new methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y.
2. A relaxation of the faithfulness assumption, allowing for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths.
3. A linear programming approach to obtain bounds on the ACE, using a parameterization of the joint distribution of {W,X,Y,U}.

The paper's strengths include:

1. The proposed method provides a novel compromise between point estimators given by faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables.
2. The approach is able to handle situations where the faithfulness assumption is violated, providing a more robust estimate of the ACE.
3. The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed method to existing approaches and demonstrating its effectiveness in various scenarios.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

1. The validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not clearly justified, and the authors should explicitly assume and discuss the Fig.2 structure.
2. No generic criteria are given for selecting relaxation parameters, which strongly affect the resulting bounds.
3. The theoretical exposition is mostly comprehensive but misses the essential discussion on the inverse-rule validity and parameter choices.
4. Experimental implementation and algorithm details are poorly described, and parameter definitions in the referenced tables are unclear or missing.
5. Typos and incorrect/missing table references reduce clarity.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is limited by prior work on the problem, although the unified combination of three ideas has some novelty that is not fully validated. The significance of the paper lies in its promising tighter ACE bounds, but limited experiments and insufficient theoretical/empirical validation leave generality and robustness unassessed.

Overall, I would recommend this paper for publication, but with revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Clarify the validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction and discuss the Fig.2 structure.
2. Provide generic criteria for selecting relaxation parameters.
3. Include a more comprehensive discussion on the inverse-rule validity and parameter choices.
4. Improve the experimental implementation and algorithm details, and clarify parameter definitions in the referenced tables.
5. Correct typos and missing table references.

With these revisions, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of causal inference.
