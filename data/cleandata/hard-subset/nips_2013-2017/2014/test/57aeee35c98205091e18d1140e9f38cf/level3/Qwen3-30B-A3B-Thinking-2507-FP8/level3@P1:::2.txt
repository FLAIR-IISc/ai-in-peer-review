

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel approach to estimating average causal effects (ACE) in observational studies when unobserved confounding might be present. The authors address a fundamental challenge in causal inference: estimating ACE when variables are confounded, which is difficult because we have no direct evidence that all confounders have been adjusted for. The paper relaxes the faithfulness assumption (Spirtes et al., 2000) to allow for "path cancellations" that create conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. Instead of using faithfulness to identify causal structures, they invert Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to use "witnesses" as instrumental variables for bounding ACE, using linear programming with constraints that allow for varying degrees of faithfulness violation.

The main contribution is a method to generate posterior distributions over bounds on ACE via a linear programming approach. The authors modify the linear programming framework of Dawid (1979) and Ramsahai (2011) by introducing constraints on causal paths motivated by relaxing Entner et al.'s conditions. They also develop a Bayesian approach to handle uncertainty in the bounds and propose a back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency.

The paper's strengths include a clear motivation for the problem, a theoretically grounded approach that provides more honest inference than point estimates, and promising empirical results showing tighter bounds than standard instrumental variable methods. The Bayesian treatment of uncertainty in the bounds is particularly noteworthy, as it avoids the sensitivity issues of prior methods that put direct priors on unidentifiable parameters. The experimental section demonstrates the method on synthetic data and a real-world influenza vaccination study, showing that WPP can provide meaningful bounds even when no valid witness exists under the strict faithfulness assumption.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. The most critical issue is the lack of clear justification for applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction. The authors claim to invert Rule 1, but they don't explicitly state or discuss the structural assumptions required for this inversion (e.g., the "Fig. 2" structure mentioned in the key points). Without this justification, the theoretical foundation of the method is shaky. The paper also lacks generic criteria for selecting relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄), which strongly affect the resulting bounds but are arbitrarily set to default values in experiments. This makes the results highly dependent on these choices without clear guidance.

The theoretical exposition is mostly comprehensive but misses essential discussions on the inverse-rule validity and parameter selection. Experimental implementation details are poorly described, with unclear parameter definitions in referenced tables (e.g., "Table 1" and "Table 5" are referenced but not properly explained). The paper contains several typos and incorrect table references that reduce clarity, and the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Regarding originality, while the paper combines three ideas (inverse Rule 1, relaxed faithfulness, and LP formulation), the novelty is limited by prior work. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related approaches like Entner et al. (2013), Ramsahai (2011), and Geiger et al. (2014), which also explore causal bounds with constraints. The significance of the work is promising but remains unproven due to limited experiments and insufficient theoretical/empirical validation of the method's robustness and generalizability.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting approach to causal inference with potentially useful bounds, but its theoretical foundation is incomplete, implementation details are insufficient, and the method's practical utility is not adequately demonstrated. The authors should strengthen their justification for the inverse Rule 1 application, provide clear guidelines for parameter selection, and better distinguish their work from prior literature. With these improvements, the method could make a valuable contribution to causal inference in observational studies, but as currently presented, the paper's weaknesses outweigh its strengths. The paper should be rejected due to insufficient theoretical grounding and lack of clear validation.
