- Proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) to bound ACE from binary observational data by treating a witness W as a near-instrument and relaxing faithfulness.
- Key mechanism: invert Entner et al.’s Rule 1, compute conditional ACE bounds by allowing bounded deviations in local conditional independences.
- Relaxations are parameterized by (w, y, x, β, β̄) that limit path cancellations and W–U dependence.
- Bounds computed via linear programming combining Balke–Pearl IV polytopes with Dawid/Ramsahai-style projections; a Bayesian procedure yields a posterior over bounds.
- Scalability addressed with analytical symbolic inequalities and a back-substitution approximation to the LP.
- Experiments include synthetic data and an influenza vaccination dataset to illustrate the method.
- Builds on PC/faithfulness discovery, Entner et al.’s admissible set selection, IV bounds, LP partial-identification work, and inequality constraints literature.
- Novelty claimed for inverting Rule 1, parameterized faithfulness relaxations, and combining LP-based and symbolic bounds.
- Technical construction is careful and mostly sound with substantial derivations in the supplement.
- Major concern: the posterior over bounds is not a posterior over ACE and risks being misinterpreted as credible/confidence intervals.
- Many relaxation settings produce very wide bounds, often only slightly better than trivial [−1,1] or standard IV bounds.
- Evaluation metric is flawed—favors wide intervals and obscures trade-offs; recommends interval scores, width at fixed coverage, and containment frequency instead.
- Influenza case results ([−0.23,0.64] IV; [−0.10,0.17] WPP) are largely uninformative for decision-making and lack sensitivity analyses.
- Benefit of back-substitution over full LP is asserted but not empirically quantified in tightness/runtime (ablation needed).
- Clarity issues: dense presentation, rapid introduction of notation (ζ, κ, ω, η) and parameters, and confusing role of W versus Z; search step and dependence on θ left implicit.
- Reproducibility doubtful due to ambiguous sentences, typos, and missing operational details.
- Originality: useful and well-differentiated combination of ideas, but should cite closely related work (e.g., Geiger–Janzing–Schölkopf) and clarify what is genuinely new.
- Significance: conceptually important for partial identification under weak unfaithfulness, but limited as a ready-to-use tool because of user-chosen relaxations and wide intervals.
- Pros: addresses an important problem, technically sound framework, novel near-instrument perspective, and potentially impactful direction.
- Cons: limited practical informativeness, posterior-over-bounds hard to interpret, imbalanced experimental design and metrics, unclear guidance for relaxation parameters, clarity/reproducibility problems, and insufficient evidence for back-substitution gains.
- Suggestions: provide guidance for choosing θ (elicitation, defaults, sensitivity), use proper interval metrics and small-sample studies, report frequency/amount of improvement over IV and faithfulness-based estimates, quantify back-substitution impact, clarify W vs. Z and search step, improve citations and writing.
- Overall recommendation: thoughtful and technically solid but empirically and expositively underdeveloped—recommend rejection now with encouragement to strengthen exposition, parameter guidance, and evaluation.
