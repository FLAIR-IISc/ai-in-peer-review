This paper introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in the presence of confounding variables, exploiting observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors relax the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The paper builds upon previous work by Entner et al. and Ramsahai, and provides a new methodology to bound the average causal effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y. The authors modify the results of Entner et al. to provide a different interpretation of their search procedure, which is used to generate candidate instrumental variables. The linear programming approach of Dawid and Ramsahai is then modified to generate bounds on the ACE by introducing constraints on some causal paths, motivated as relaxations of Entner et al.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of estimating causal effects in the presence of confounding variables is an important one. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their methodology, including the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm. The use of linear programming and Bayesian inference to generate bounds on the ACE is a novel and interesting approach.

However, there are several limitations to the paper. Firstly, the conceptual interpretability of the results is weak, as the distribution over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances. Additionally, many relaxations produce very wide, potentially uninformative bounds. The method does not provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates.

The experimental evaluation is also poor and unbalanced, undermining claims about practical usefulness. The "bias" metric used for comparisons is meaningless because it measures different quantities across methods. The influenza dataset results are uninformative and raise concerns about practical performance.

Furthermore, the technical approach is sound and detailed in the supplement, but overall context and some derivations are unclear. Choosing relaxation parameters is deferred to future work, making practical application and assessment difficult. The algorithmic description and many subtly different parameters are hard to follow and may not be reproducible from the paper alone.

Despite these limitations, the paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas rather than immediate practitioner readiness. The authors provide a novel combination of existing techniques, and their approach is sufficiently distinct from recent related work.

To improve the paper, the authors should provide clearer interpretation and guidance on what the derived bounds mean in practice (not confidence intervals). They should also clarify the role of the witness W (used to identify Z but not needed for ACE computation), explain and give intuition for the relaxation parameters and how they relate to weak interactions, and describe how the search step relates to bounds computation and how admissible sets Z depend on relaxation parameters θ.

Additionally, the authors should clarify ambiguous sentences (e.g., "Background ... used here," "max. 10 free parameters") and fix typos. They should also cite closely related work (e.g., Geiger, Janzing, Schölkopf UAI2014) when claiming novelty of analytical bounds, and question the effectiveness of the back-substitution refinement step, providing empirical evidence of its impact.

Finally, the authors should explain why multiple bounds arise (different admissible sets for different witnesses), address sample-size sensitivity (results reported for 5000 points may not generalize to typical smaller datasets), and provide a more detailed comparison of WPP to standard faithfulness-based point estimates.
