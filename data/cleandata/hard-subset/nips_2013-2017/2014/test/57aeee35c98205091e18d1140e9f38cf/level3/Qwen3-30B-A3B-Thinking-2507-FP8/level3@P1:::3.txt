

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel framework for bounding average causal effects (ACE) under potential violations of the faithfulness assumption. The approach relaxes the faithfulness condition to allow "weak" path cancellations, using a linear programming formulation with relaxation parameters to generate posterior distributions over ACE bounds. It leverages Entner et al.'s Rule 1 to identify witness-admissible set pairs (W,Z), then computes bounds via Bayesian inference over these pairs. The method's computational efficiency is enhanced through symbolic algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm.

**Originality** is a clear strength. WPP creatively combines causal discovery (Entner et al.), instrumental variable bounds (Dawid, Ramsahai), and Bayesian inference to address a fundamental limitation in causal inference—faithfulness violations. The relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) provide a principled way to quantify weak unfaithfulness, distinct from recent work like Geiger et al. (UAI 2014) which focuses on exact constraints. The paper's analytical bounds as functions of relaxation parameters also advance the field beyond prior numerical approaches.

**Quality** is mixed. The technical approach is sound, with rigorous LP formulations and Bayesian posterior sampling. However, the method's practical utility is undermined by two critical issues. First, the reported bounds (e.g., influenza ACE [−0.10, 0.17] vs. standard IV [−0.23, 0.64]) are often too wide to be informative, especially without clear interpretation guidelines. The paper fails to clarify whether these bounds should be treated as confidence intervals or conservative estimates, risking misinterpretation as point estimates. Second, the experimental evaluation is unbalanced: synthetic results show WPP stability, but the "hard" case (where no witness exists) is poorly addressed, and the influenza application provides no evidence that WPP outperforms standard IV bounds. The "bias" metric used for comparison is invalid—measuring Euclidean distance for point estimates (NE1, NE2) versus interval coverage for WPP conflates fundamentally different error types.

**Clarity** suffers from significant ambiguities. The role of the witness W is unclear: it identifies admissible sets Z but is not used in ACE computation, creating confusion. Parameter selection (e.g., w = x = y = 0.2) is deferred to future work, making practical application opaque. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) lacks detail on how the search procedure for (W,Z) pairs interacts with relaxation parameters θ. Key sentences like "Background knowledge, after a user is suggested a witness..." are unexplained, and typos (e.g., "Naı̈ve Estimator") undermine readability. The supplement is essential but not sufficient to reproduce the method from the paper alone.

**Significance** is directionally important but currently limited. While WPP offers a principled compromise between faithfulness-based point estimates and IV bounds, its immediate impact is constrained by the lack of practical guidance. The paper correctly notes that "the bounds are considerably stricter than standard IV" in the influenza case, but fails to demonstrate *why* this matters for practitioners. The claim that WPP "should be used in regular practice" is overstated given the uninformative bounds and unresolved parameter selection. The back-substitution refinement's empirical impact is also unquantified—without evidence that it significantly tightens bounds or speeds computation, its value is questionable.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: The problem addressed is timely and central to causal inference; the relaxation framework is conceptually novel and technically rigorous; the Bayesian approach avoids sensitivity to hyperparameters common in latent variable models.

**Con-acceptance arguments**: The method's practical utility is questionable due to overly wide bounds and lack of point estimate guidance; experimental evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods; key implementation details (parameter selection, interpretation) are omitted; the "bias" metric misrepresents comparative results.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by formalizing weak unfaithfulness via relaxation parameters, but its significance is currently limited by the disconnect between the method's promise and its practical implementation. To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) provide concrete guidance on interpreting bounds (e.g., as conservative intervals); (2) demonstrate with synthetic data how WPP bounds narrow as relaxation parameters approach faithfulness; (3) clarify the witness's role and parameter selection; and (4) compare WPP to state-of-the-art bounds (e.g., Geiger et al.) using a consistent error metric. Without these, the method remains an interesting theoretical extension rather than a practical tool. While the technical approach is sound, the current presentation and evaluation do not sufficiently justify its adoption in standard causal practice.
