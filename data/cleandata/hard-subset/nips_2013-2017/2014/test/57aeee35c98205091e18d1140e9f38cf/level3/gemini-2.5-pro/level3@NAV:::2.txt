This paper introduces a novel method for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data. The core idea is to reinterpret the covariate selection criteria from Entner et al. Specifically, it proposes inverting Entner’s Rule 1, not to identify an admissible set for point estimation, but to motivate an instrumental variable-like setup. This approach is combined with a relaxation of the faithfulness assumption, parameterized as "path-cancellation" through a set of inequality constraints, and is ultimately formulated as a linear programming (LP) problem to derive the bounds. This work builds directly upon the LP bounding frameworks of Dawid and Ramsahai but contributes by integrating a data-driven method for structuring the constraints based on observed conditional independencies.

The primary strength of this work lies in its promising results. The bounds obtained appear to be narrower and more accurate than those from existing approaches, suggesting a potentially powerful tool for causal inference. However, the paper has significant weaknesses in both theoretical soundness and clarity. A critical theoretical gap is the lack of clear justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction; the authors should explicitly state and defend the underlying causal structure this implies. Furthermore, no generic criteria are provided for selecting the relaxation parameters, which fundamentally determine the resulting bounds, leaving a crucial aspect of the method ad-hoc. The clarity is hampered by a poorly described experimental section and opaque algorithm details, making reproducibility difficult. Numerous typos and incorrect table references further detract from the paper's quality.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of existing ideas rather than a completely new paradigm. While prior work on ACE bounding is extensive, the unified approach of using an inverted rule, relaxed faithfulness, and an LP formulation is a new contribution. The potential significance is high, as tighter, more robust ACE bounds are of great interest. However, this potential is not yet fully realized. The limited experiments and insufficient validation leave the generality and robustness of the proposed "Witness Protection Program" unassessed. In summary, while the paper is promising for its novel combination of techniques and tight preliminary bounds, its acceptance is hindered by an unjustified core assumption, a lack of guidance on parameter selection, and poor clarity in its empirical validation.
