

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces a novel approach called the Witness Protection Program (WPP) for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) when the causal graph is partially unknown, relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for bounded violations. The authors build on Entner et al.'s (2013) rule for identifying admissible sets (for back-door adjustment) and Ramsahai's (2012) linear programming approach for bounding ACE. The key insight is to reinterpret Entner's "witness" variables as potential instrumental variables and introduce relaxation parameters to account for "bounded violations" of faithfulness, generating more robust bounds on ACE.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a practical challenge in causal inference: how to handle uncertainty about the causal structure when faithfulness may not hold. This is particularly relevant in observational studies where unmeasured confounding is a concern. The authors demonstrate how to convert Entner's Rule 1 into a bounding procedure rather than a point estimation method, which is a meaningful conceptual shift. The Bayesian framework for uncertainty quantification over the bounds and the "back-substitution algorithm" for efficient computation are also noteworthy technical contributions.

**Quality:** The paper presents a well-motivated problem and a novel methodological approach. The theoretical foundations appear sound, though the reviewer has not verified all technical details. The method is well-positioned between point estimation under faithfulness assumptions and purely instrumental variable approaches. However, the connection between Entner's rule conditions and the relaxation parameters remains unclear, which is a significant concern. The paper explicitly states that the relationship between Entner's conditions and the relaxation parameters is unresolved, which is a major gap in the theoretical foundation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, though some sections are challenging to follow. The key concern is the unclear role of Entner et al.'s rule conditions without faithfulness. The phrase "express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence" is misleading since unfaithfulness implies *additional* conditional independences, not violations of independence. The paper would benefit from more precise language throughout. The reference to "Table 1" as "Table 5" in the text is a distracting error. The interpretation of negative ACE in the influenza example (where X=1 is flu shot and Y=1 is not hospitalized) is confusing without clarification.

**Originality:** The paper introduces a meaningful synthesis of existing methods (Entner et al. and Ramsahai) to create a new approach for causal effect bounding under uncertainty. The "Witness Protection Program" concept is novel, and the relaxation of faithfulness via bounded violations is a useful contribution. While the core methodology builds on prior work, the specific combination of techniques for generating robust bounds represents a clear advancement.

**Significance:** The method addresses a real and important problem in causal inference practice. The ability to generate bounds that are more robust to faithfulness violations than traditional methods could be valuable in observational studies. The application to influenza vaccination data demonstrates practical relevance. However, the paper does not clearly establish how much better WPP performs compared to existing methods, especially given the unresolved connection to Entner's conditions.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a practical problem in causal inference (robustness to faithfulness violations)
- Novel reinterpretation of Entner's rule for bounding rather than point estimation
- Bayesian framework for uncertainty quantification over bounds
- Efficient back-substitution algorithm for computational scalability

**Weaknesses:**
- Unclear theoretical connection between Entner's rule conditions and the relaxation parameters
- Misleading phrasing about "violations of faithfulness"
- Critical gap in explaining why Entner's conditions matter for the method
- Unclear interpretation of negative ACE in the influenza example
- Technical errors (e.g., table reference)
- Limited comparison with Entner's method using stringent thresholds

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting and potentially useful approach to causal inference under uncertainty. However, the unresolved theoretical connection between Entner's rule conditions and the relaxation parameters is a major concern. Without clarifying why these specific conditions matter for the method (rather than just being a convenient search procedure), the contribution's foundation is questionable. The minor issues with clarity and interpretation are also significant.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the authors must address the major theoretical concern before the paper can be considered a strong contribution. The paper has potential but requires substantial clarification of the methodological foundation, particularly regarding the role of Entner's conditions without faithfulness. If these concerns are addressed, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference.
