This paper proposes a novel methodology for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) of a variable X on a variable Y in observational studies, addressing the fundamental challenge of unobserved confounding. The authors’ approach inverts Entner’s Rule 1, relaxes the faithfulness assumption to allow for “path cancellations” representing potential confounding, and employs a linear programming (LP) formulation with inequality constraints to derive these bounds. The work builds upon prior research in causal inference, instrumental variables, and graphical models, aiming to provide more robust and informative bounds than existing methods.

The core idea of inverting Entner’s Rule 1 to identify potential instrumental variables for bounding the ACE is interesting and represents a shift in perspective. The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption, acknowledging that conditional independencies may not definitively rule out confounding, is a pragmatic step towards addressing real-world observational data challenges. The authors appropriately frame their work within the context of Pearl’s do-calculus and acknowledge related work, including Dawid & Ramsahai (1999) and Entner et al. (2013). The combination of these elements – inverse rule application, relaxed faithfulness, and LP formulation – constitutes the paper’s primary contribution.

The paper's strengths lie in its attempt to bridge theoretical rigor with practical applicability. The potential for tighter ACE bounds, as demonstrated in the synthetic and real-data experiments, is promising. The proposed “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) framework offers a systematic approach for handling violations of faithfulness and potentially improving causal inference in observational settings. The inclusion of a Bayesian learning component, while computationally intensive, acknowledges the uncertainty inherent in observational data analysis.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A crucial concern is the lack of explicit justification for applying Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction. The authors allude to a specific causal structure (akin to Figure 2) but do not formally establish the conditions under which this inversion is valid. This assumption needs to be clearly stated and discussed, as it fundamentally underpins the methodology. Without it, the rationale for treating ‘witnesses’ as instrumental variables is unclear.

The selection of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is another significant weakness. The authors acknowledge their importance but offer no generic criteria for choosing these values. The impact of these parameters on the resulting bounds is not adequately explored, and the choice seems somewhat arbitrary. While acknowledging the trade-off between bound width and assumption strength (Manski, 2007), a more systematic approach to parameter selection or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.

The theoretical exposition, while generally comprehensive, feels incomplete. Specifically, the discussion surrounding the validity of the inverse rule and the parameter selection process is underdeveloped. The experiments are also hampered by poor implementation descriptions. Definitions of parameters within referenced tables (e.g., Table 3) are either unclear or missing entirely, hindering reproducibility and detailed evaluation. Numerous typos and incorrect/missing table references further detract from the clarity of the presentation.

The originality of the work is somewhat limited. The individual components—Entner’s Rule, relaxed faithfulness, and LP formulations—have been explored in prior work. However, the unified combination of these ideas and the specific application to ACE bounding represent a novel contribution, although its practical advantage over existing methods isn’t definitively established.

Regarding significance, the prospect of tighter ACE bounds is valuable, but the limited scope of the experiments and insufficient validation raise concerns about the generality and robustness of the approach. The real-data application, while illustrative, lacks a thorough comparison to alternative methods. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including a wider range of datasets and comparative benchmarks, is needed to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed methodology.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of existing techniques for ACE bounding.
*   Potential for tighter, more informative bounds than existing methods.
*   Pragmatic relaxation of the faithfulness assumption.
*   Systematic WPP framework for handling observational data challenges.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for inverting Entner’s Rule 1.
*   Absence of generic criteria for relaxation parameter selection.
*   Incomplete theoretical exposition and poorly described experiments.
*   Limited empirical validation and robustness assessment.
*   Numerous clarity issues due to typos and table reference errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. However, the methodological gaps, incomplete exposition, and limited empirical validation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** but encourage the authors to address the aforementioned weaknesses and resubmit a revised version. Specifically, the authors should rigorously justify the inversion of Entner’s Rule 1, provide guidance on parameter selection, improve the clarity of the presentation, and conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation.
