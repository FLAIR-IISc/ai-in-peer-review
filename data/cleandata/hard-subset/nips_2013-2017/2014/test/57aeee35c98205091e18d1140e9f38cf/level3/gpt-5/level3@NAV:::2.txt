This paper proposes a method to bound the ACE using (i) an inverse application of Entner’s Rule 1 to generate witness–admissible-set pairs, (ii) a relaxed faithfulness assumption modeled as bounded “path-cancellation,” (iii) inequality constraints linking observed and latent-conditioned parameters, and (iv) a linear-program (LP) formulation with a Bayesian posterior over bounds. It also provides analytic (symbolic) inequalities and a fast back-substitution algorithm as a relaxation to the full LP. The work relates to classical IV bounds (Balke & Pearl), LP-based partial identification (Dawid; Ramsahai), inequality approaches (Richardson & Robins; Evans), and recent “bounding confounding” methods (Geiger et al.), building on Entner et al.’s admissible-set discovery.

Quality: The technical development is generally careful and the empirical bounds are often narrow and seemingly more accurate than standard IV bounds, even with an unoptimized algorithm. However, the central move—using Entner’s Rule 1 in the inverse direction—is insufficiently justified. The method implicitly assumes a structural template (your Fig. 2) in which W has no direct effect on Y given Z and controlled violations are bounded; this needs to be stated and defended. The relaxation parameters (w, x, y, β, β̄) crucially determine bound width, yet no generic selection/diagnostic criteria are provided; sensitivity analysis is limited.

Clarity: The exposition of the LP and symbolic bounds is mostly clear, but the experimental and algorithmic details are under-specified. Parameter definitions in the tables are unclear or missing; there are typos and incorrect/missing table references (e.g., “Table 1/5”), which materially reduce readability and reproducibility. Please fully specify constraints, priors, proposal/rejection thresholds, and the back-substitution steps, and fix table/figure cross-references.

Originality: Prior work has addressed each ingredient separately. The unified combination—inverse witness use + bounded faithfulness + LP/Bayesian posterior—is modestly novel, but its validity hinges on the inverse-rule assumption and principled parameter calibration, both currently underdeveloped.

Significance: If validated, the approach offers practically tighter ACE bounds than standard IV, an important contribution for observational studies. As-is, limited experiments (synthetic plus one real dataset) and lack of principled parameter selection prevent assessing robustness and generality.

Pros for acceptance:
- Promising, often tighter ACE bounds; useful symbolic approximations and a fast solver.
- Unifies several strands (faithfulness, IV bounds, inequality constraints) in a pragmatic framework.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing justification of inverse Entner’s Rule 1; structural assumptions not explicit.
- No principled procedure for relaxation-parameter choice; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Incomplete experimental/algorithmic details; clarity issues (typos, table refs).
- Novelty incremental relative to established partial identification literature.

Overall: borderline reject; with clearer assumptions, parameter-selection guidance, fixed presentation, and stronger empirical validation, this could become impactful.
