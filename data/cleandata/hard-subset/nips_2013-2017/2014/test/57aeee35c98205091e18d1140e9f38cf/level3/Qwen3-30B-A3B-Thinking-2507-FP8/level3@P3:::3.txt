

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a method for computing bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE) when the faithfulness assumption might be violated. The authors propose relaxing the faithfulness condition through relaxation parameters and linear programming to quantify weak unfaithfulness. The core idea is to invert Entner et al.'s Rule 1, using pairs (W,Z) where W is a witness and Z is an admissible set to generate bounds on the ACE. The method provides a posterior distribution over these bounds using Bayesian inference.

## Strengths

The paper addresses a timely and important problem in causal inference: how to handle potential violations of the faithfulness assumption. The approach is conceptually interesting and represents a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools. The technical approach is sound, with detailed derivations provided in the supplement. The method is an original combination of existing techniques that appears sufficiently distinct from recent related work. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the direction of causal inference research, particularly in providing a framework for handling unfaithfulness.

## Weaknesses

The paper has several significant weaknesses that undermine its claims about practical usefulness. The conceptual interpretability of the results is weak: the distribution over bounds depends on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances, making it difficult to interpret the practical meaning of the bounds. Many relaxations produce very wide, potentially uninformative bounds (e.g., the influenza dataset results yield ACE in (-0.23, 0.64), which is uninformative and raises concerns about practical performance).

The experimental evaluation is poor and unbalanced. The "bias" metric used for comparisons is meaningless because it measures different quantities across methods. The paper claims to provide a "posterior distribution over bounds," but fails to provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of intervals as point estimates. The algorithmic description is hard to follow due to subtly different parameters, and the paper does not provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The choice of relaxation parameters is deferred to future work, making practical application and assessment difficult. The paper does not provide clear guidance on what the derived bounds mean in practice (not confidence intervals) or explain the role of the witness W (used to identify Z but not needed for ACE computation). The relationship between relaxation parameters and weak interactions is not adequately explained.

The paper should cite closely related work (e.g., Geiger et al. UAI 2014) when claiming novelty of analytical bounds. The back-substitution refinement step lacks empirical evidence of its impact. The sample-size sensitivity is not adequately addressed (results reported for 5000 points may not generalize to typical smaller datasets). Table 1 and other reported summaries do not allow gauging the benefit of WPP over standard faithfulness-based point estimates.

The paper contains ambiguous sentences (e.g., "Background ... used here," "max. 10 free parameters") and typos that need fixing. The tone occasionally contains emotive or dismissive language about Bayesian approaches, which should be toned down.

## Overall Assessment

The paper's significance lies in its direction and ideas rather than immediate practitioner readiness. While the technical approach is sound, the lack of clear interpretation guidance, poor experimental evaluation, and unaddressed practical concerns significantly weaken its contribution. The method does not provide clear value over standard approaches in the cases where it produces informative bounds.

The paper would be strengthened by clearer explanations of the relaxation parameters' relationship to weak interactions, more informative experimental results, proper citation of related work, and better guidance on interpreting the bounds. The authors should also address the sample-size sensitivity and provide more concrete examples of when WPP provides meaningful improvements over existing methods.

While the paper makes a promising theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference, it falls short in demonstrating practical utility and clarity needed for a NIPS paper. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these concerns before it can be considered for publication at NIPS.
