## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) in observational studies with limited knowledge of the underlying causal structure, building upon Entner et al.’s work and Ramsahai’s linear programming approach. The core idea – to relax the faithfulness assumption and interpret violations as bounded deviations from local independence – is sensible and potentially valuable for practical causal inference. The proposed “Witness Protection Program” aims to provide more robust bounds than methods relying heavily on faithfulness. The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the limitations of existing approaches and positioning itself as a complementary tool.

The strength of the paper lies in its attempt to move beyond strict faithfulness assumptions, a known fragility in causal discovery. The combination of Entner’s rule with Ramsahai’s bounding technique is a novel approach, and the introduction of relaxation parameters offers a degree of control over the sensitivity to unfaithfulness. The experiments, while limited in scope, demonstrate the method’s stability and provide a comparison to naive estimators. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several key areas. My major concern revolves around the role of Entner et al.’s rule *without* the faithfulness assumption. The paper states the conditions don’t obviously imply causal structure when faithfulness is dropped, yet the method heavily relies on these conditions to identify potential instrumental variables. The connection between these conditions and the choice of relaxation parameters is left unresolved, weakening the justification for the proposed framework. It’s unclear why those conditions matter for the method, and whether the machinery fundamentally depends on them. 

Several minor issues further detract from the paper’s clarity. The phrasing “express violations of faithfulness as bounded violations of local independence” is awkward; unfaithfulness *creates* extra independences, not violations of them. The comparison to Entner et al.’s method would be strengthened by reporting their performance using their stringent thresholds, treating “don’t know” results as uninformative bounds. The interpretation on p.8 regarding a negative ACE indicating a desirable vaccine is counterintuitive and requires clarification. Finally, there’s a citation error – Table 1 is referenced as Table 5.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in causal inference.
*   Presents a novel approach to bounding ACEs under uncertainty about causal structure.
*   Offers a potential improvement over methods relying solely on faithfulness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Key methodological connections (Entner’s rule without faithfulness) are unclear.
*   Lack of clarity in several sections hinders understanding.
*   Limited scope of experiments.



Overall, this is an interesting paper with a promising idea, but the details are hard to follow and the core methodological connections require significant clarification before it can be considered a strong contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns raised.
