## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP) algorithm, a novel approach to bounding Average Causal Effects (ACE) under potential violations of the faithfulness assumption. The core idea is to relax faithfulness by introducing parameters quantifying potential “path cancellations” and then employing linear programming to derive bounds on the ACE. This is a timely and interesting problem, as faithfulness is a strong assumption often challenged in practice, and a principled attempt to quantify weak unfaithfulness using familiar mathematical tools is valuable. The method builds upon existing work in instrumental variables, causal discovery, and linear programming, presenting a unique combination of these techniques.

The paper’s strength lies in its technical ambition and detailed formulation, particularly evident in the supplementary material. The attempt to provide algebraic bounds and a back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency is commendable. However, the conceptual interpretability of the method is weak. The resulting distribution over bounds relies on unquantified likelihoods of unfaithful instances, making it difficult to understand what the bounds truly represent. Many of the relaxations introduced lead to very wide, and potentially uninformative, bounds, raising concerns about practical applicability. Crucially, the method does not provide a preferred point estimate, risking misinterpretation of the derived intervals as confidence intervals, which they are not.

The experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The synthetic experiments are poorly described and unbalanced, and the influenza dataset results (ACE in (-0.23, 0.64)) are uninformative. The use of a “bias” metric for comparison is also problematic, as it measures different quantities across methods. Furthermore, the choice of relaxation parameters is deferred to future work, hindering practical application and assessment. The algorithmic description is complex, with numerous subtly different parameters, making it difficult to follow and potentially non-reproducible. 

While the paper demonstrates originality in combining existing techniques, its significance currently lies more in the direction and ideas presented than in immediate practitioner readiness. The authors need to provide clearer interpretation and guidance on what the derived bounds *mean* in practice, beyond simply stating they are not confidence intervals. Clarification is also needed regarding the role of the “witness” variable W, and a more intuitive explanation of the relaxation parameters and their relationship to weak interactions is essential. Addressing the points raised regarding reproducibility, clarifying ambiguous sentences, and citing closely related work (e.g., Geiger et al., 2014) would significantly strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of existing techniques for a challenging problem.
*   Detailed technical formulation and exploration of algebraic bounds.
*   Potential for future development and application.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak conceptual interpretability and potentially uninformative bounds.
*   Poor experimental evaluation and unbalanced comparisons.
*   Lack of guidance on parameter selection and practical application.
*   Complex and potentially non-reproducible algorithmic description.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject with encouragement to revise and address the significant weaknesses outlined above. The core idea is promising, but substantial work is needed to improve clarity, interpretability, and practical relevance.
