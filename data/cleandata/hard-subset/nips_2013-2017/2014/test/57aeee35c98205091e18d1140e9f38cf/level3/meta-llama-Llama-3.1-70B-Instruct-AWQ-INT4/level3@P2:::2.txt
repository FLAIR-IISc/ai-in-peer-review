This paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies, leveraging observational conditional independencies to identify "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors relax the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The paper builds upon previous work on instrumental variables and faithfulness assumptions, but the authors' contribution lies in their modification of Entner et al.'s results to generate candidate instrumental variables and their introduction of a linear programming formulation to obtain bounds on the average causal effect. The authors also provide a Bayesian learning approach to handle uncertainty in the estimation of the bounds.

The paper's strengths include its ability to provide narrow and accurate bounds on the average causal effect, despite the algorithm not being optimized. The authors' use of a linear programming formulation and Bayesian inference is a novel combination of techniques that shows promise in addressing the problem of estimating causal effects in observational studies.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement in the paper. Firstly, the validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not clearly justified, and the authors should explicitly assume and discuss the Fig. 2 structure. Additionally, no generic criteria are given for selecting relaxation parameters, which strongly affect the resulting bounds. The theoretical exposition is mostly comprehensive but misses the essential discussion on the inverse-rule validity and parameter choices.

Furthermore, the experimental implementation and algorithm details are poorly described, and parameter definitions in the referenced tables are unclear or missing. Typos and incorrect/missing table references reduce clarity. The originality of the paper is limited by prior work on the problem, though the unified combination of three ideas has some novelty that is not fully validated.

In terms of significance, the paper's contribution is promising, but the limited experiments and insufficient theoretical/empirical validation leave the generality and robustness of the approach unassessed. The authors' claim that their approach should be used in regular practice to complement other default tools in observational studies is not fully supported by the current results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies.
* The authors' use of a linear programming formulation and Bayesian inference is a novel combination of techniques that shows promise in addressing the problem.
* The paper's results show that the approach can provide narrow and accurate bounds on the average causal effect.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The validity of applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction is not clearly justified.
* No generic criteria are given for selecting relaxation parameters, which strongly affect the resulting bounds.
* The experimental implementation and algorithm details are poorly described.
* The originality of the paper is limited by prior work on the problem.
* The limited experiments and insufficient theoretical/empirical validation leave the generality and robustness of the approach unassessed.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a clear justification for applying Entner's Rule 1 in the inverse direction and discuss the Fig. 2 structure.
* Develop generic criteria for selecting relaxation parameters and discuss their impact on the resulting bounds.
* Improve the experimental implementation and algorithm details, including clear parameter definitions and table references.
* Conduct additional experiments to assess the generality and robustness of the approach.
* Provide a more comprehensive discussion of the theoretical and empirical validation of the approach.
