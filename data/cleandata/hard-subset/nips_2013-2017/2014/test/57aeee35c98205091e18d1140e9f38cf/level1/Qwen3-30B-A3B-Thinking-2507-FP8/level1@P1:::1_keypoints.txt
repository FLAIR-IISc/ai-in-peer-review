- Introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel method that relaxes faithfulness to allow path cancellations and yields bounds on causal effects rather than point estimates.  
- Builds on Entner et al.'s conditional-independence Rule 1 and integrates ideas from Spirtes et al. and Pearl's do-calculus.  
- Uses linear programming plus a Bayesian inference step to generate posterior distributions over bounds on the average causal effect (ACE).  
- Demonstrates robustness in synthetic experiments and a real-world influenza vaccine study, producing meaningful bounds when faithfulness fails.  
- Connects to Ramsahai's LP-based bounding approach but extends it with more flexible handling of faithfulness violations.  
- Technically sound with solid theoretical grounding and careful construction from prior causal-inference literature.  
- Theoretical analysis is insufficiently developed on bound tightness and how relaxation parameters trade off with bound width.  
- Lacks comprehensive analysis of statistical properties of the bounds under different sampling regimes.  
- Writing and structure are clear overall; background and formulation are precise, though some methodological sections need more explanation.  
- The Bayesian learning procedure and algorithm would benefit from more explanation and extra pseudocode/comments for accessibility.  
- Back-substitution algorithm is a practical, computationally efficient alternative to full LP.  
- Originality is high: the "witness protection" concept and focus on bounds are novel and well-differentiated from prior work.  
- Practical significance is strong for fields like medicine and social sciences where faithfulness is problematic.  
- Weaknesses include limited guidance on choosing relaxation parameters and incomplete sensitivity/parameter analyses.  
- Recommendation: accept — significant contribution with solid experiments and theory, despite areas needing stronger analysis and clarification.
