This paper introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in the presence of confounding variables, which is a fundamental problem in causal inference. The authors propose a methodology that exploits observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph, relaxing the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas and their relation to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The methodology is well-explained, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a compromise between point estimators given by the faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. The authors' approach is more robust than traditional methods that rely on the faithfulness assumption, and it provides a more honest inference by focusing on bounds rather than point estimates.

The paper also presents an extensive set of experiments, including synthetic studies and a real-world study on the effect of influenza vaccination on hospitalization. The results show that the proposed method is stable and provides more accurate estimates than traditional methods.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors acknowledge that the choice of relaxation parameters is crucial and that background knowledge is necessary to make informed decisions. They also mention that the approach can be computationally expensive, especially for large datasets.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach that combines elements of instrumental variable analysis and Bayesian inference. The authors' use of a linear programming approach to obtain bounds on the average causal effect is also a new contribution.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to provide a more robust and honest inference in the presence of confounding variables. The authors' approach has the potential to become a standard item in the toolbox of anyone who needs to perform an observational study.

In conclusion, this paper presents a well-written and well-structured contribution to the field of causal inference. The authors' approach is novel, robust, and honest, and it has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

Arguments con acceptance:

Rating: 8/10
