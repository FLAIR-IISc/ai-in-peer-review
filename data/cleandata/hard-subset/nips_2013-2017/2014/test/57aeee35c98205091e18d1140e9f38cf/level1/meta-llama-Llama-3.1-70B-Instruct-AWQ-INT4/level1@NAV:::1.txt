This paper introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in the presence of confounding variables, which is a fundamental problem in causal inference. The authors propose a "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) that exploits observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The approach relaxes the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the theoretical and empirical results. The WPP algorithm is outlined in a step-by-step manner, making it easy to follow. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the advantages of their approach over existing methods.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposal of a novel approach for estimating causal effects in the presence of confounding variables.
* The relaxation of the faithfulness condition, which allows for more flexibility in the estimation of causal effects.
* The use of a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference to obtain bounds on the average causal effect.
* The provision of a thorough discussion of the related work and the advantages of the proposed approach.
* The inclusion of empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The complexity of the proposed approach, which may make it difficult to understand and implement for non-experts.
* The reliance on the assumption of a modest dimensionality of the covariate set Z, which may not always hold in practice.
* The use of a uniform prior on the hypothesis of independence, which may not be the most informative prior in all cases.
* The lack of a clear discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach and its scalability to large datasets.
