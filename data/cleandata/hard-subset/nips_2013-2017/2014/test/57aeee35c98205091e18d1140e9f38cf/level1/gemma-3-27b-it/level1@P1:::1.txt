This paper introduces a novel method, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), to bound the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies when faced with potential confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, allowing for “path cancellations” that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. The method leverages Entner et al.’s work on admissible sets and instrumental variables, formulating the problem as a linear program (LP) to find bounds on the ACE. To address computational challenges, the authors propose further relaxations and a fast, message-passing-like algorithm. They demonstrate the approach on synthetic and real (influenza vaccination) data.

The work builds upon a substantial body of literature in causal inference, particularly the work of Pearl [14], Spirtes et al. [22], and Dawid [5]. The authors correctly position their contribution relative to these foundational works, acknowledging the limitations of faithfulness [13, 20] and seeking a more robust approach. The connection to instrumental variable (IV) methods [1, 11] is clearly established, and the paper explicitly attempts to bridge the gap between point estimation (e.g., [6]) and bounding techniques. The use of linear programming, inspired by Ramsahai [16], is also well-motivated.  More recent work on non-parametric identification utilizing constraints, such as Geiger et al. [7], is mentioned but could be explored more thoroughly.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption and the formulation of the problem as a bounded optimization problem, offering a nuanced approach to causal inference in observational studies, represents a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The method is grounded in causal graphical models and leverages established concepts like admissible sets and instrumental variables.
*   **Practical considerations:**  The authors address the computational complexity of the LP formulation by proposing relaxations and a faster algorithm, demonstrating an awareness of practical implementation challenges.
*   **Comprehensive experiments:** The evaluation includes both synthetic data (allowing for ground truth comparison) and a real-world example (influenza vaccination), enhancing the credibility of the findings.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and technical details, with references to related work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational complexity:** Despite the proposed relaxations, the method still appears computationally demanding, particularly for high-dimensional datasets. The performance scaling with the number of covariates remains a concern. The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion on scalability limitations would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter sensitivity:** The performance of the WPP seems sensitive to the choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄). While the authors briefly discuss this, a more systematic sensitivity analysis is needed to understand how the bounds vary with different parameter settings and to provide guidance on parameter selection.
*   **Interpretation of bounds:** The paper focuses on obtaining bounds, but provides limited discussion on how to interpret these bounds in practical decision-making.  What does it mean to have a wide bound? How can stakeholders use this information?
*   **Bayesian implementation details:**  The Bayesian learning section, while intriguing, could benefit from more detailed explanation of the Dirichlet priors and the rejection sampling process. The impact of the effective sample size (10) on the results should be investigated.
*  **Lack of comparison to alternative bounding methods:** The paper compares against naieve estimators and Entner's approach, but doesn’t compare to other recent bounding techniques (e.g. those from [8]) which may offer alternative tradeoffs between assumptions and computational complexity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to causal inference in observational studies.
*   The method addresses a fundamental challenge in causal inference—dealing with unobserved confounding—by relaxing the faithfulness assumption.
*   The experiments demonstrate the potential of the WPP to provide more informative bounds on the ACE compared to traditional methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the method remains a concern, potentially limiting its applicability to large-scale datasets.
*   The sensitivity of the results to the choice of relaxation parameters needs further investigation.
*   The lack of detailed discussion on the interpretation of the bounds and their practical implications.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of causal inference. The proposed WPP offers a valuable tool for bounding the ACE in observational studies, particularly when the faithfulness assumption is questionable. While the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity are legitimate concerns, the authors acknowledge these limitations and propose mitigation strategies.  The paper is well-written, theoretically grounded, and supported by experimental results. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion on the computational complexity of the method, perform a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the relaxation parameters, and elaborate on the interpretation of the bounds in practical applications. Additionally, a comparison to other bounding techniques would strengthen the paper.
