### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data. The work elegantly bridges two major areas in causal inference: constraint-based causal discovery and partial identification via instrumental variables (IVs).

The core idea is to start with a search procedure for an "admissible set" `Z` for backdoor adjustment, as proposed by Entner et al. (2013). This procedure identifies a "witness" variable `W` that becomes conditionally independent of the outcome `Y` after conditioning on the cause `X` and `Z`. Rather than taking this conditional independence at face value (as a strict faithfulness assumption would imply), the authors propose to "protect the witness" by treating this as evidence for an *approximate* IV structure. `W` is treated as a candidate instrument, and the method allows for bounded violations of the assumptions that define a perfect instrument. These violations are controlled by a set of user-specified relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`) that quantify the maximum allowable direct effect of `W` on `Y`, dependencies between `W` and the unobserved confounder `U`, and other deviations.

Building on the linear programming (LP) framework of Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012), the paper derives bounds on the ACE under these relaxed assumptions. The methodology is embedded in a Bayesian framework to propagate sampling uncertainty into the final bounds. A key practical contribution is a fast, approximate "back-substitution" algorithm that avoids solving the full LP at each step of a Monte Carlo procedure, making the Bayesian approach computationally feasible. The method is demonstrated on synthetic data, where it proves more robust than methods relying on strict faithfulness, and on a real-world influenza vaccination dataset, where it provides informative bounds.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution is the conceptual link it forges between constraint-based discovery and partial identification. This is a highly original and significant idea. The faithfulness assumption, while powerful, is known to be problematic in practice, especially with finite data where statistical independencies are never exact. This work provides a principled and flexible framework for sensitivity analysis around this assumption. Instead of a binary choice between trusting an identified adjustment set or discarding it, WPP offers a continuum of intermediate options, controlled by explicit parameters. This could have a substantial impact on how observational studies are analyzed, making causal discovery tools more robust and trustworthy.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed methodology is technically sound and well-developed. The extension of the LP bounding machinery to incorporate the new relaxation parameters is a non-trivial contribution. The Bayesian approach for handling uncertainty is well-justified and appropriate for this problem, moving beyond simple plug-in estimates. The development of the algebraic bounds and the fast back-substitution algorithm is a crucial addition that demonstrates a thoughtful approach to making the method practical and scalable.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and motivated. The "Witness Protection Program" metaphor is effective and aids intuition. The background section clearly explains the concepts of admissible sets, witnesses, and instrumental variables, making the paper accessible. The progression from Entner et al.'s rule to the relaxed IV formulation is logical and easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions**

1.  **Guidance on Choosing Relaxation Parameters:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of clear guidance on setting the relaxation parameters (`w, y, x, β, β̄`). The authors acknowledge this, stating they "will not discuss in detail how to choose" them. While background knowledge is invoked, this is too vague for a practitioner to apply the method confidently. The utility of the entire framework hinges on making a sensible choice for these parameters. For the paper to be more impactful, it should provide some intuition or heuristics. For instance, could the strength of the statistical evidence for the conditional independence (e.g., a Bayes factor or p-value) be used to calibrate the relaxation? A sensitivity analysis in the experiments showing how the bounds change as a function of these parameters would have been highly illustrative and would have strengthened the paper considerably.

2.  **The Search and Relaxation Procedure:** The current approach is two-staged: first, a (W, Z) pair is selected based on a test for approximate independence, and second, the relaxation is applied. This seems suboptimal. One could imagine a more integrated procedure where the model selection step itself accounts for the possibility of these bounded violations. For instance, instead of selecting the "best" witness, perhaps multiple plausible witnesses could be combined or marginalized over.

3.  **Falsification Test:** The proposed "falsification test" (rejecting the model if >95% of posterior samples are rejected) is a pragmatic but ad-hoc solution. It would be beneficial to discuss its statistical properties. Is it possible for a model to be a poor fit yet still pass this test if the prior and likelihood are sufficiently diffuse? A more formal model comparison or goodness-of-fit test would make the framework more rigorous.

4.  **Scalability of the Search:** While the back-substitution algorithm addresses the scalability of the bounding step, the initial search for a witness `W` and an admissible set `Z` remains combinatorial in the number of covariates. The paper notes this and suggests discrete PCA, but the severity of this limitation for moderately large covariate sets should be discussed more explicitly.

### **Overall Evaluation**

This is an excellent paper that addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference with a novel and powerful idea. The synthesis of causal discovery and partial identification is a significant contribution to the field. The technical development is solid, and the writing is clear. While the critical issue of selecting the relaxation parameters needs to be addressed more thoroughly in future work, the current paper lays a strong and compelling foundation for a new class of robust causal inference methods. The weaknesses identified are primarily avenues for future research rather than fatal flaws. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it a clear fit for NIPS.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly original synthesis of two major fields in causality.
*   Addresses the important practical problem of violations of the faithfulness assumption.
*   Technically sound methodology, including a clever computational speed-up.
*   High potential for impact on applied observational research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Insufficient guidance on choosing the crucial relaxation parameters, limiting immediate practical applicability.
*   The search and falsification steps are somewhat ad-hoc.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
