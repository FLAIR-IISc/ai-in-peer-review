This paper tackles causal effect estimation under unobserved confounding by proposing the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework that converts conditional independence evidence into partial identification of the average causal effect (ACE). The core idea is to invert Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes’s “Rule 1”: instead of using a witness W and admissible set Z to assert point identification via back-door adjustment under faithfulness, WPP treats W as a candidate instrument conditional on Z and derives bounds on the ACE. Crucially, the method relaxes faithfulness by bounding violations through parameters controlling path cancellations and residual dependencies (w, x, y, β, β̄). Bounds are computed via a linear programming formulation extending Dawid and Ramsahai’s work on IV models, with a Bayesian posterior over bounds using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling. The paper also introduces algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a back-substitution procedure that approximates the LP solution more efficiently. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu vaccination study suggest WPP yields informative intervals and improves stability relative to naive back-door estimators and can be tighter than classical Balke–Pearl IV bounds when conditional independencies are informative.

Relation to prior work is strong and appropriate: the paper builds on Pearl’s do-calculus/back-door criterion, faithfulness and PC (Spirtes et al.), Entner et al.’s witness/admissible set search, Manski’s partial identification, and IV bounding (Balke–Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai), while providing novel relaxations and computational machinery.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and carefully positioned. The LP formulation and algebraic relaxations are plausible, and the Bayesian treatment acknowledges sampling uncertainty. However, coverage guarantees for the posterior over bounds are not fully quantified, and the falsification heuristic (95% rejection in constrained Dirichlet sampling) seems ad hoc. Guidance on selecting relaxation parameters is deferred to the supplement, limiting practical reproducibility.

Clarity: The paper is dense but largely well-organized. Some notation (e.g., ζ, κ, ω) could be introduced earlier and streamlined; the algorithmic steps and complexity could be clarified; minor typos remain. More explicit pseudo-code for the back-substitution algorithm would help.

Originality: High. Inverting Rule 1, parameterizing bounded faithfulness violations, and deriving algebraic bounds/back-substitution are novel contributions.

Significance: High. Bridging faithfulness-based discovery with IV bounds advances robust causal inference in observational studies and should influence practice.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled relaxation of faithfulness yielding usable ACE bounds.
- Solid integration of LP, Bayesian inference, and symbolic bounds.
- Empirical evidence on synthetic and real data; improved stability.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited guidance and sensitivity analysis for choosing relaxation parameters.
- Heuristic model falsification and unclear frequentist coverage of bounds.
- Scalability and complexity analysis could be expanded; clarity can improve.
