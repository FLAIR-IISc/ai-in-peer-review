

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel approach for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies when confounding variables may be present. The authors relax the faithfulness assumption commonly used in causal discovery (Spirtes et al.) to allow for "varying degrees of path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. The key contribution is a method that provides posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE using a linear programming approach combined with Bayesian inference, rather than point estimates. This work builds upon Entner et al.'s instrumental variable approach and Dawid/Ramsahai's linear programming bounds, but extends them by introducing a flexible relaxation framework.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a clear parameterization of the joint distribution and derive bounds using linear programming, with careful attention to the trade-offs between assumptions and bound tightness. The Bayesian framework for handling uncertainty is well-motivated and properly implemented (using constrained Dirichlet priors), avoiding the sensitivity issues of directly placing priors on unidentifiable latent variables. The experiments on synthetic data and real influenza vaccination data demonstrate the method's robustness compared to baseline approaches, with clear metrics for comparison. The authors are honest about the conservative nature of their bounds, acknowledging that they are wider than point estimates but more reliable when faithfulness assumptions might be violated. The supplementary material provides additional theoretical details and algorithmic implementations, enhancing the paper's completeness.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear progression from background to methodology to experiments. The authors effectively explain the core concepts (witness variables, admissible sets) and how they differ from previous work. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, with sufficient explanation for the target audience. The algorithm descriptions (particularly Algorithm 1) are clear and precise. The paper could be slightly improved by providing a brief glossary of key causal inference terms for readers less familiar with the field, but this is a minor point given the conference's expected audience. The experiments section clearly describes the synthetic setup and real-world application, with appropriate evaluation metrics.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution to causal inference by introducing a novel relaxation framework for the faithfulness assumption. The key insight—treating witness variables as protected against faithfulness violations rather than discarding them—is both conceptually fresh and practically useful. The authors clearly differentiate their work from Entner et al. (who provide point estimates) and standard IV approaches (which provide bounds but don't account for partial faithfulness violations). The development of symbolic algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm for computational efficiency represent significant technical innovations that build upon Ramsahai's work but extend it in novel ways. The paper demonstrates sufficient novelty compared to recent NIPS papers on causal inference (e.g., recent works by Entner, Richardson, and Geiger).

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental challenge in causal inference: how to estimate causal effects when faithfulness assumptions might be violated. The proposed method provides a practical compromise between point estimation (which can be unreliable when faithfulness is violated) and purely conservative bounds (which can be too wide to be useful). The authors convincingly argue that this approach should become a standard tool in observational studies. The real-world application to influenza vaccination data demonstrates practical utility, showing how WPP can provide more informative bounds than standard IV approaches by leveraging conditional independence constraints. The method's ability to handle approximate independence (rather than strict conditional independence) makes it particularly relevant for real-world data where faithfulness is often violated in practice.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel relaxation of faithfulness assumption that better reflects real-world data
- Combines theoretical rigor with practical computational improvements
- Bayesian framework properly handles uncertainty in bounds
- Demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data
- Clear differentiation from related work

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational cost of the Bayesian approach may limit scalability to very large datasets (though the back-substitution algorithm helps mitigate this)
- The choice of relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) is somewhat ad hoc; while the authors acknowledge this in the supplementary material, more guidance on selecting these parameters would strengthen the method
- The paper doesn't fully address how the method performs when multiple witness-admissible set pairs exist, beyond selecting the highest-scoring pair

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**
Pro: This paper makes a significant contribution to causal inference by providing a practical, theoretically grounded method for bounding causal effects that accounts for potential violations of faithfulness assumptions. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful, with clear advantages over existing approaches in scenarios where faithfulness might be violated. The writing is clear, the experiments are thorough, and the approach has strong potential for adoption in observational studies.

Con: The method's computational complexity might limit its applicability to very large datasets, and the choice of relaxation parameters remains somewhat arbitrary. However, these limitations are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the approach.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to causal inference. The methodology is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a significant practical challenge in observational studies. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and demonstrates both theoretical and empirical significance. I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The work advances the state of the art in causal inference by providing a more robust approach that better reflects the realities of observational data analysis.
