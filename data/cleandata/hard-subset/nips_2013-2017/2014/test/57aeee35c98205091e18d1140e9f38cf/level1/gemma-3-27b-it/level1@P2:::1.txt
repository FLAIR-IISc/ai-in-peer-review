This paper introduces a novel approach to bounding causal effects in observational studies, relaxing the faithfulness assumption and leveraging conditional independencies to identify potential instrumental variables. The core idea is the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), which aims to provide more robust bounds than relying solely on faithfulness, particularly when dealing with potential violations of this assumption. The method combines ideas from Entner et al., Dawid, and Ramsahai, and proposes both a linear programming formulation and algorithmic relaxations for computational efficiency.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental challenge in causal inference – dealing with unobserved confounders. The authors correctly identify the limitations of the faithfulness assumption and propose a sensible approach to account for potential violations.  The connection to instrumental variable (IV) theory is clearly articulated, and the paper builds upon established work in this area (Pearl, Spirtes et al., Dawid, Ramsahai). The introduction of the WPP framework, and the associated terminology, is a clear and memorable contribution.  The exploration of both a full linear programming solution *and* efficient relaxations demonstrates a pragmatic awareness of the computational challenges. The inclusion of both synthetic and real data experiments, including a well-known example from the literature, strengthens the evaluation. 

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact. Firstly, the paper suffers from significant density and a lack of clarity in presentation. The notation is heavy and often lacks sufficient explanation, making it difficult to follow the derivations. For example, the introduction of η parameters in Section 3 is not immediately clear without careful re-reading and reference back to the provided equation (2). The many cross-references to the Supplementary Material indicate that essential details are hidden away, hindering a complete understanding based on the paper alone. 

Secondly, while the authors acknowledge the computational complexity, the description of the algorithmic relaxations, especially the "back-substitution algorithm," lacks sufficient detail to assess its effectiveness. The claims of efficiency need more concrete justification, such as a comparative timing analysis against the full linear programming approach on various problem sizes.  The claim that the back-substitution method does not *increase* the width of the bounds, while desirable, requires more rigorous proof or empirical validation.

Thirdly, the experimental results, while present, are not sufficiently compelling. The comparison against naive estimators (NE1, NE2) and the faithfulness-based estimator of Entner et al. is useful, but the conditions under which each method performs best are not fully explored.  The discussion of the influenza vaccination example lacks depth.  The authors state WPP "could not validate GRP as a witness", but do not explain *why* (e.g., which conditions of Rule 1 failed) or what specific insights were gained by using DM, AGE, and SEX instead. The absence of a more detailed analysis of the real-world data limits the practical impact of the study.

Finally, while the paper explicitly states its contribution is a "bound" on the ACE, the discussion of how to *interpret* these bounds is relatively limited.  How should practitioners use a wide interval? What is a "useful" bound?  Addressing these questions would increase the practical value of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to causal inference that addresses the limitations of the faithfulness assumption.
*   Well-motivated and builds upon a strong foundation of existing work in causal inference and IV theory.
*   Combines a theoretical framework (linear programming) with practical algorithmic considerations (relaxations).
*   Includes both synthetic and real data experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Significant lack of clarity and density in presentation, making the paper difficult to follow.
*   Insufficient detail regarding the algorithmic relaxations and their computational efficiency.
*   Limited depth of the experimental results and analysis.
*   Lack of guidance on how to interpret and use the resulting bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. While the core idea is novel and potentially impactful, the significant clarity issues and limitations in the experimental evaluation prevent a stronger recommendation. The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of the relevant literature and proposes a sensible approach, but it needs substantial revisions to improve its readability, rigor, and practical relevance. I believe with significant revisions addressing the weaknesses, this could become a strong contribution. The approach is interesting and addresses an important problem. However, the current state of the manuscript does not fully convince me of its technical soundness and practical value.
