

# Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies when confounding variables may not be fully observed. The authors address a fundamental challenge in causal inference: estimating the average causal effect (ACE) when variables are confounded, which is difficult because one lacks direct evidence that all confounders have been adjusted for. The work builds on and extends Entner et al. (2013), who used conditional independence constraints to obtain point estimates of the ACE, and Dawid and Ramsahai, who developed linear programming approaches for instrumental variable bounds. The key innovation is relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that create conditional independencies without ruling out confounding causal paths. The approach provides posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE through a linear programming framework, rather than point estimates, which the authors argue should complement existing tools in observational studies.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it reinterprets Entner's Rule 1 to generate candidate instrumental variables rather than admissible sets, enabling a bounding approach. Second, it introduces a linear programming formulation with constraints on causal paths that relax the faithfulness assumption. Third, it develops a Bayesian inference framework for uncertainty quantification, and fourth, it provides a computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm for deriving symbolic bounds. The authors validate their approach through synthetic experiments and a real-world influenza vaccination study.

**Quality Assessment:** The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The authors clearly derive the linear programming formulation, provide a Bayesian framework for uncertainty, and develop an efficient back-substitution algorithm. The theoretical derivations are rigorous, and the experimental results support the claims. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need to choose relaxation parameters and the computational expense of the full linear programming approach. The Bayesian approach using constrained Dirichlet priors is well-motivated and avoids the sensitivity issues of directly placing priors on unidentifiable latent variables. The supplementary material provides additional details that strengthen the technical presentation.

**Clarity Assessment:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The background section clearly explains the problem and relates to prior work on instrumental variables and causal discovery. The methodology is presented step-by-step with clear mathematical notation. The experimental section adequately describes the synthetic studies and real-world application. Some technical details (e.g., the exact implementation of the Bayesian framework) are relegated to the supplementary material, but the main paper provides sufficient information for experts to understand and reproduce the core contributions. The paper would benefit from slightly more detail on how to choose the relaxation parameters, though the authors acknowledge this as an area for future work.

**Originality Assessment:** The work makes a clear contribution to the field by relaxing the faithfulness assumption in a meaningful way. While prior work has considered bounds for instrumental variables (e.g., Ramsahai, 2013), the authors introduce a novel framework that provides a compromise between faithfulness-based point estimators and IV-based bounds. The symbolic bounds derived in Section 4 are particularly novel, as the authors note they provide algebraic relationships between the free parameters and the resulting bounds, unlike prior work that typically provides numerical bounds for fixed parameter values. The paper clearly differentiates itself from Entner et al. (2013), which focuses on point estimates, and from standard IV approaches, which assume faithfulness. The related work section adequately cites relevant NIPS papers, including Entner et al. (2013) and Ramsahai (2013).

**Significance Assessment:** The results address a fundamental problem in causal inference with practical implications for observational studies. The authors demonstrate through experiments that WPP provides more robust estimates than naive approaches when faithfulness assumptions are violated. In the influenza vaccination case study, WPP returns meaningful bounds that differ from standard IV results, suggesting the approach can provide more informative causal inferences when faithfulness is questionable. The approach could become a standard tool in observational studies, as claimed by the authors. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a principled method for quantifying uncertainty in causal effect estimation when full confounder adjustment is not possible.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical framework that builds on and extends existing causal inference methods
- Novel relaxation of faithfulness assumption with practical implications
- Well-motivated Bayesian approach for uncertainty quantification
- Efficient back-substitution algorithm for practical implementation
- Comprehensive experimental validation with both synthetic and real data

**Weaknesses:**
- The choice of relaxation parameters is not fully addressed, though the authors acknowledge this as future work
- The computational complexity of the full linear programming approach may limit applicability to very large datasets
- The real-world application is limited to a single case study, though the synthetic experiments are thorough

**Conclusion:** This paper presents a technically sound, novel approach to causal inference that addresses a significant limitation of current methods. The relaxation of faithfulness assumptions to provide robust bounds on causal effects is both theoretically well-founded and practically useful. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While the choice of relaxation parameters requires further development, the core methodology is strong and has clear potential for impact in observational studies. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. I recommend acceptance.
