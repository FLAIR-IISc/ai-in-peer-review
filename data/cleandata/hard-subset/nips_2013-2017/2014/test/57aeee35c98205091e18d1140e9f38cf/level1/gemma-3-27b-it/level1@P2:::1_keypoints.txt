- Introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP) to bound causal effects by relaxing the faithfulness assumption and using conditional independencies to identify potential instruments.  
- Proposes a linear programming formulation and algorithmic relaxations (including a "back-substitution" algorithm) for computational efficiency.  
- Well-motivated contribution that builds on and connects to existing IV and causal inference literature (Pearl, Entner et al., Dawid, Ramsahai).  
- Memorable terminology and a sensible approach to handle unobserved confounding when faithfulness may be violated.  
- Includes both synthetic and real-data experiments, strengthening empirical evaluation.  
- Paper is dense with heavy notation; key parameters (e.g., η) and derivations lack clear explanation, hurting readability.  
- Relies heavily on Supplementary Material, leaving essential details hidden and making the main paper hard to follow independently.  
- Description of algorithmic relaxations, especially the back-substitution method, is insufficiently detailed to assess correctness or efficiency.  
- Claims about computational gains and that back-substitution does not increase bound width lack rigorous proof or comparative timing evidence.  
- Experimental comparisons (NE1, NE2, Entner et al.) are useful but do not fully explore conditions where each method excels or fails.  
- The influenza vaccination case study is under-analyzed; authors do not explain why GRP failed as a witness or the specific insights from chosen witnesses.  
- Discussion on interpreting the resulting ACE bounds is limited—practitioners need guidance on what constitutes a "useful" or actionable bound.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept—novel and promising but requires substantial revisions to clarity, algorithmic detail, experimental depth, and practical guidance.
