This paper proposes a novel approach, the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), to estimate causal effects in observational studies by relaxing the faithfulness assumption and providing bounds on the Average Causal Effect (ACE). The core idea is to reinterpret Entner et al.’s work on instrumental variables and utilize linear programming to handle violations of faithfulness, arising from potential unmeasured confounders. The method aims to provide more robust estimates than traditional approaches, particularly when faithfulness is questionable.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in causal inference – dealing with unobserved confounding. The problem is clearly stated, and the connection to existing work (Pearl’s do-calculus, instrumental variables, Entner et al., faithfulness) is appropriately established. The authors correctly identify the limitations of relying solely on faithfulness and the potential instability of point estimates derived from it. The proposed methodology, while complex, appears logically sound, building upon existing linear programming techniques for bounding causal effects. The introduction of relaxation parameters to account for faithfulness violations is a key contribution. The presentation of algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm show a strong theoretical understanding and a desire to improve computational efficiency. The experiments, with both synthetic and real data (influenza vaccination), provide initial evidence of the method’s potential.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, clarity is a significant issue. The notation is dense, and the numerous equations, particularly in Section 3.1 and 4, are difficult to follow without repeated reading. While the authors acknowledge the complexity, a more pedagogical approach, perhaps with simpler illustrative examples alongside the general formulations, would greatly improve comprehension. The connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation (Algorithm 1) is somewhat unclear. It’s not immediately obvious how the relaxation parameters are chosen in practice, and the “search procedure” mentioned in the algorithm is left undefined, raising questions about the reproducibility of the results.

Secondly, the empirical evaluation is limited. The synthetic experiments, while helpful, are not extensive enough to fully assess the performance of WPP under various scenarios (e.g., varying degrees of faithfulness violation, different network structures). The real-world application, while relevant, feels somewhat under-developed. The choice of the influenza dataset and the justification for excluding GRP from the admissible set require more thorough explanation. Further, simply stating that WPP "could not validate GRP as a witness" feels incomplete; a more detailed comparison with the traditional IV approach using GRP would strengthen the analysis. The results in Table 3, while demonstrating stability, are difficult to interpret without a clearer understanding of the error metrics.

Thirdly, the paper lacks a robust comparison to alternative methods for handling unobserved confounding, such as sensitivity analysis (e.g., Rosenbaum’s bounding approach) or methods based on latent variable models. While the authors mention the limitations of these approaches, a direct comparison would provide valuable context and highlight the unique strengths of WPP. Additionally, the discussion of the computational complexity is somewhat superficial. While the authors mention relaxations and the back-substitution algorithm, a more detailed analysis of the scaling behavior of the method would be beneficial. The claim of an “order of magnitude” improvement needs to be supported by concrete benchmarks.

Finally, the paper contains some minor issues with referencing. Several citations are missing necessary detail (e.g., year of publication).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to addressing unobserved confounding by relaxing faithfulness.
*   Solid theoretical foundation, building upon established causal inference techniques.
*   Potential for providing more robust bounds on causal effects compared to traditional methods.
*   Initial experimental results suggest promise, although further evaluation is needed.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Low clarity and difficult-to-follow notation.
*   Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison to alternative methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Some minor issues with referencing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The proposed methodology is innovative and addresses a significant practical challenge. However, the lack of clarity, limited empirical validation, and insufficient comparison to existing methods prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I recommend a **weak reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and address the concerns raised above. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, expanding the empirical evaluation, providing a more detailed comparison to alternative methods, and providing a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity. If these issues are addressed, the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS.
