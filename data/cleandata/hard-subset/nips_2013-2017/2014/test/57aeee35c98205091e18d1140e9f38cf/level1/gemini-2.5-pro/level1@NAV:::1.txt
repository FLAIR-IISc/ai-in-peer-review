This paper introduces the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a novel framework for bounding the Average Causal Effect (ACE) from observational data in the presence of unmeasured confounding. The central idea is to relax the strong faithfulness assumption, which is often used in causal discovery algorithms like that of Spirtes et al. WPP builds upon the "witness" concept from Entner et al. (2013), which uses conditional independencies to identify admissible sets for back-door adjustment. Instead of using a witness for point identification, this work re-purposes it to suggest an instrumental variable-like structure. It then extends the linear programming approaches of Dawid (2003) and Ramsahai (2012) to derive bounds on the ACE while allowing for controlled violations of faithfulness, quantified by a set of user-defined relaxation parameters.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its originality and significance. It thoughtfully bridges the gap between data-driven causal discovery and robust effect estimation. By using observed conditional independencies to motivate a bounding strategy rather than assuming they imply exact causal structure, the method offers a principled compromise between the often brittle point estimates derived from faithfulness and the potentially uninformative bounds from standard IV models that require a pre-specified instrument. The technical quality is high; the methodology is a sound synthesis of existing ideas, and the inclusion of a Bayesian framework to handle statistical uncertainty is well-justified. The proposed "back-substitution algorithm" is a valuable contribution that improves the method’s computational feasibility. The paper is also very clearly written and well-motivated.

The main weakness concerns the practical application of the method. The framework relies on several relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that directly control the trade-off between assumption strength and bound width. The paper provides little guidance on how to set these parameters in a principled manner, which could leave the results open to subjectivity. While acknowledged as future work, this is a critical aspect for practitioners. A second concern is the scalability of the search for a witness and an admissible set, which is combinatorial in nature and was tested on a relatively small number of covariates.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   A novel and significant contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference.
*   The proposed framework is technically sound, well-motivated, and provides a valuable compromise between existing approaches.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and includes strong experimental validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Lack of clear guidance on setting crucial relaxation parameters limits practical objectivity.
*   Scalability of the search procedure to high-dimensional settings is not fully demonstrated.

Overall, the strengths of this work, particularly its novelty and significance, outweigh its weaknesses. This paper presents an important new tool for observational causal inference and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
