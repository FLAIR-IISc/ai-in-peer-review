Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Witness Protection Program (WPP), a framework for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) of X on Y in observational studies with binary variables. The key idea is to invert the use of Entner et al.’s Rule 1 [6]: instead of using (W,Z) pairs that satisfy conditional independence tests to justify a back-door adjustment (point identification), the paper treats W as a “witness” akin to an instrumental variable conditional on Z, and uses linear programming to produce bounds on the ACE under relaxations of faithfulness. These relaxations are encoded as bounded deviations from conditional independences (parameters w, x, y, β, β̄), allowing small direct effects or residual confounding while avoiding vacuous bounds. The approach generalizes IV bounds (Balke–Pearl [1], Dawid [5], Ramsahai [16]) and connects to Pearl’s do-calculus framework [14] and causal discovery under (approximate) faithfulness [22]. The authors further provide algebraic (symbolic) bounds and a back-substitution algorithm that approximates the LP solution with substantial speedups, and a Bayesian procedure that yields a posterior distribution over bounds using constrained Dirichlet priors and rejection sampling. Empirical studies on synthetic data and an influenza vaccination dataset [12, 9, 18] illustrate that WPP can yield informative bounds that are often narrower than standard IV bounds and more robust than faithfulness-based point estimates when independence tests are only approximately true.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is careful in mapping relaxations to constraints on the latent model (η, δ) and observables (ζ), and in showing how IV bounds are recovered as special cases (e.g., w=0, β=β̄=1; y=x=0 recovers W→X→Y). The LP construction follows established practice [5,16], and the algebraic relaxations are conservative by design. The Bayesian treatment acknowledges estimation uncertainty and provides a pragmatic falsification check.
- Evidence: Synthetic experiments are thoughtfully constructed to stress-test naive adjustment (by ensuring collider bias) and to test both solvable and unsolvable cases for Rule 1. The influenza example compares against standard IV bounds and shows practically tighter intervals.
- Caveats: The method’s validity hinges on user-chosen relaxation parameters; while any choice yields conservative bounds relative to back-door estimators, the informativeness of bounds is sensitive to these settings. Guidance is deferred to the supplement; more principled elicitation/calibration would strengthen the case. The falsification rule (reject if >95% of posterior samples fail constraints) is ad hoc; more formal posterior predictive checks or Bayes factors would be preferable. Computationally, exhaustive searches over Z and constrained rejection sampling may not scale; although the back-substitution algorithm helps, empirical scaling analyses are limited.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a solid background section bridging causal discovery, IVs, and partial identification. Notation is heavy (η, δ, ζ, κ, ω), and some definitions appear only once; keeping a running table of symbols would help. The search procedure for Z is left open; the paper uses BDeu model comparison, but more details on thresholds and the effect of approximate independence would aid reproducibility. The algorithmic outline is helpful, and the motivating examples (noncompliance, influenza) ground the ideas.

Originality
- The inversion of Rule 1 to construct bounding problems, and the explicit parameterized relaxations of faithfulness that interpolate between IV bounds and back-door adjustment, are novel and insightful. Combining LP-based bounds with a posterior over bounds is also a useful contribution for uncertainty quantification. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution “message-passing” approximation provide new computational tools beyond prior IV bounding work.

Significance
- Addressing partial identification under plausible, tunable relaxations is highly relevant to observational studies where faithfulness is fragile and full identification rare. The framework offers a principled alternative to both naive adjustment and pure IV worst-case bounds, with potential to influence practice in ML/causal inference. Broader impact depends on guidance for setting relaxation parameters, scaling to higher-dimensional covariates and non-binary variables, and richer comparisons to other sensitivity/partial identification methods (e.g., Rosenbaum-type sensitivity analyses, marginal sensitivity models, [8], [23]).

Pros
- Conceptually elegant bridge between causal discovery (faithfulness) and IV bounding.
- Clear generalization: recovers known models as special cases; parameters have intuitive interpretations.
- Provides both exact (LP) and fast approximate (algebraic/back-substitution) computations.
- Bayesian uncertainty over bounds acknowledges sampling variability and model falsification.
- Empirical results demonstrate robustness to approximate independencies and improved informativeness over standard IV in a real dataset.

Cons
- Dependence on user-specified relaxation parameters without strong, principled elicitation; risk of post hoc tuning.
- Scalability concerns: exhaustive Z search and constrained rejection sampling may be costly; limited runtime/complexity analysis.
- Binary-variable restriction; extensions to multi-valued/continuous variables are not developed.
- Falsification and model-checking procedures are heuristic; stronger diagnostics desirable.
- Experimental comparisons omit several alternative partial identification/sensitivity approaches; synthetic designs may favor WPP.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide practical guidelines for eliciting/calibrating w, x, y, β, β̄ (e.g., prior predictive calibration, domain-expert elicitation linked to causal path magnitudes), and report sensitivity curves routinely.
- Include scaling experiments (vary |W|, |Z|) and comparisons of LP vs back-substitution w.r.t. tightness/runtime.
- Discuss extensions to non-binary variables (e.g., monotone transformations, discretization strategies, or multi-category LP formulations).
- Replace ad hoc falsification with posterior predictive checks and/or Bayes factors for inequality-constrained models.
- Broaden baselines (e.g., Rosenbaum bounds, marginal sensitivity models, [8], [23]) to situate WPP more fully in the partial identification literature.
- In the influenza case, justify the witness choice with domain knowledge and assess robustness to alternative witnesses/admissible sets.

Recommendation
Overall, the paper is technically sound, original, and addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and practically useful framework. Despite open issues around parameter selection, scalability, and broader empirical comparisons, the contribution is significant. I lean toward acceptance.
