This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding average causal effects (ACE) in observational studies, addressing the challenge of unmeasured confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, allowing for “path cancellations” that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. The approach utilizes a linear programming framework and Bayesian inference to obtain posterior distributions over bounds on the ACE. The authors claim their “Witness Protection Program” should complement existing tools in observational studies.

The paper builds upon existing work in causal inference, particularly the do-calculus of Pearl [14], instrumental variable techniques [1, 5, 16], and the work of Entner et al. [6] on identifying admissible sets. It addresses a critical limitation of standard causal inference methods – the sensitivity to the faithfulness assumption – by allowing for violations of this assumption while still providing bounds on the causal effect. The connection to instrumental variable analysis is well-established and utilized for bounding, and the introduction of Bayesian learning for parameter estimation is a reasonable approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a significant problem:** Unmeasured confounding is a fundamental issue in observational studies, and the paper directly tackles this challenge.
*   **Novel approach:** Relaxing the faithfulness assumption and using a linear programming/Bayesian framework offers a new perspective on bounding causal effects.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The methodology is grounded in causal graphical models and leverages existing theoretical results from the field.
*   **Practical considerations:** The authors acknowledge the computational complexity of the approach and propose relaxations to make it more feasible. The back-substitution algorithm is a significant contribution towards computational scalability.
*   **Comprehensive background:** The paper provides a thorough review of relevant concepts, including instrumental variables, witnesses, and admissibility.
*   **Experimental validation:** The synthetic and real-world experiments demonstrate the potential of the method and compare it to existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The methodology is complex and requires a significant amount of background knowledge to fully understand. The notation and mathematical formulations are dense.
*   **Computational cost:** Despite the proposed relaxations, the computational cost of the method remains a concern, particularly for high-dimensional datasets. The discussion of scalability relies heavily on future work and analytical approximations.
*   **Parameter sensitivity:** The method relies on setting parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) that control the degree of faithfulness violation. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of how to choose these parameters in practice and their impact on the resulting bounds. The discussion in Section 5 acknowledges this and proposes a basic approach.
*   **Interpretation of bounds:** While the paper emphasizes the importance of providing bounds rather than point estimates, the interpretation of these bounds is not always clear. How should practitioners use a wide interval?
*   **Limited scope of experiments:** The experiments are relatively limited in scope. The real-world experiment uses a single dataset and focuses on a specific application. More diverse datasets and scenarios would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Dependence on Rule 1:** The method is still fundamentally reliant on Entner’s Rule 1, and its sensitivity to that rule’s assumptions is not fully explored. The authors admit that the algorithm may report solutions even when none theoretically exist.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to addressing a major challenge in causal inference.
*   The method offers a potentially valuable complement to existing tools for observational studies.
*   The computational relaxations and the back-substitution algorithm demonstrate a commitment to practical applicability.
*   The experimental results provide initial evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the methodology may limit its widespread adoption.
*   The computational cost remains a significant concern.
*   The lack of guidance on parameter selection and the interpretation of bounds may hinder practical application.
*   The limited scope of the experiments raises questions about the generalizability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of causal inference. The proposed methodology offers a novel and potentially valuable approach to bounding causal effects in the presence of unmeasured confounding. However, the complexity of the method, its computational cost, and the lack of clear guidance on parameter selection are significant limitations. The paper is well-written and provides a thorough background, but greater clarity and more extensive experimentation would strengthen the work.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses related to computational cost, parameter selection, and the interpretation of bounds. They should also expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets and scenarios. Addressing these points would increase the impact and practicality of the proposed method.
