## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces a novel method for bounding average causal effects (ACE) in observational studies, addressing the challenge of unmeasured confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, allowing for “path cancellations” that suggest potential confounding without completely ruling them out. By inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1, the authors propose identifying “witness” variables that, along with admissible sets, provide instrumental variable-like bounds on the ACE using a linear programming approach, further enhanced with Bayesian inference and computational relaxations for scalability. 

The work builds upon a rich body of literature in causal inference, notably Pearl’s do-calculus [14], instrumental variable theory [1, 5, 16], and causal discovery algorithms like PC [22]. It directly addresses limitations of relying on strict faithfulness [22] and the instability of point estimates from Entner et al. [6], offering a framework for explicitly quantifying uncertainty due to potential confounding. The proposed “Witness Protection Program” (WPP) aims to provide more robust bounds when faithfulness is questionable.

**Strengths:** The paper tackles a crucial problem in causal inference – dealing with unmeasured confounding – with a mathematically grounded and innovative approach. The relaxation of the faithfulness assumption is a significant contribution, and the combination of linear programming, Bayesian methods, and algorithmic optimizations demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of practical implementation. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments (using the influenza vaccination dataset [12]) provides some empirical validation. The authors are thorough in referencing related work and acknowledging limitations.

**Weaknesses:** While technically sound, the paper's clarity suffers in places. The mathematical notation is dense, and the connections between the different steps (relaxations, back-substitution, Bayesian inference) aren’t always clearly explained. The computational complexity, even with the introduced relaxations, remains a concern, and the scalability is only briefly addressed. The experimental results, while present, lack detail regarding sensitivity analysis of the relaxation parameters and a more rigorous comparison with existing methods beyond the naively estimated ACE. The discussion of the theoretical implications of the relaxed faithfulness assumption could be further developed. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The approach offers a potentially valuable contribution to the toolkit for observational causal inference. It is technically novel, well-motivated, and addresses a significant practical challenge.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The paper’s complexity and lack of extensive empirical validation might limit its immediate impact. The presentation could be improved for better accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should prioritize improving clarity, providing more detailed experimental analysis (sensitivity analysis, comparison to alternative bounding methods), and expanding the discussion of the theoretical implications of their work. The originality and potential significance of the contribution outweigh the current weaknesses.
