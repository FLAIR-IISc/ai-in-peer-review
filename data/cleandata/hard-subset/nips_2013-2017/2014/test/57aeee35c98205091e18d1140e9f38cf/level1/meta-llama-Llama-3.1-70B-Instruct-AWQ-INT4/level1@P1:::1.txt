This paper introduces a novel approach for estimating causal effects in observational studies by exploiting observational conditional independencies to suggest "weak" paths in an unknown causal graph. The authors relax the faithfulness condition of Spirtes et al. to allow for varying degrees of "path cancellations" that imply conditional independencies but do not rule out the existence of confounding causal paths. The outcome is a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect via a linear programming approach and Bayesian inference.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology and its relationship to previous work. The use of a witness protection program to estimate causal effects is a clever idea, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness through a series of experiments.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a compromise between point estimators given by the faithfulness assumptions and bounds based on instrumental variables. The authors show that their approach can provide more accurate estimates than traditional methods, especially in cases where the faithfulness assumption is violated.

The paper also provides a good discussion of the limitations of the faithfulness assumption and the need for more robust methods.
