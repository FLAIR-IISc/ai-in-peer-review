## Review of "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"

This paper introduces a novel methodology for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) in observational studies, addressing the challenge of unmeasured confounding. The core idea is to relax the faithfulness assumption, allowing for “path cancellations” that imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding paths. By inverting Entner et al.’s Rule 1, the authors propose using conditional independence tests to identify potential “witnesses” and admissible sets for bounding the ACE via linear programming and Bayesian inference. This work builds upon established concepts in causal inference like do-calculus (Pearl, 2014), instrumental variables, and graphical models (Spirtes et al., 1993). It also connects to recent work on relaxing faithfulness (e.g., Geiger et al., 2011) and bounding causal effects (e.g., Ramsahai, 2016).

The paper’s strengths lie in its attempt to provide a more robust approach to causal inference in the face of potential violations of faithfulness, a common concern in practice. The combination of linear programming, Bayesian inference, and a novel interpretation of existing work is promising. The inclusion of both analytical approximations and a fast algorithm to address computational challenges is also commendable. The experiments, while limited, demonstrate the method’s stability and provide a comparison to naive estimators. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, hindering clarity. While the authors acknowledge the computational expense, the scalability of the proposed method remains a significant concern, particularly for high-dimensional datasets. The experimental evaluation is limited to small synthetic datasets and a single real-world example, making it difficult to assess the method’s generalizability. The discussion of the relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) lacks concrete guidance on how to choose them in practice. Furthermore, the connection between the “Witness Protection Program” metaphor and the technical details feels somewhat tenuous. The paper also relies heavily on prior work without fully explaining the nuances of those methods for readers unfamiliar with the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem (confounding in observational studies).
*   Presents a novel approach that combines established techniques in a creative way.
*   Offers a potential improvement over existing methods by relaxing the faithfulness assumption.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Scalability concerns.
*   Lack of clear guidance on parameter selection.
*   Dense notation and potentially low clarity.



**Overall:** I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the ideas presented are interesting and potentially valuable, the limited evaluation and clarity issues prevent a stronger recommendation. Addressing the scalability concerns and providing more comprehensive experimental results would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should also consider simplifying the notation and providing more detailed explanations of the underlying concepts.
