

**Peer Review: "Causal Inference through a Witness Protection Program"**

This paper introduces the "Witness Protection Program" (WPP), a novel framework for estimating causal effects in observational studies by providing bounds on average causal effects (ACE) while relaxing the faithfulness assumption. The authors build upon Entner et al.'s work on conditional independence constraints for identifying admissible sets, but instead of providing point estimates, they develop a method to generate bounds on ACE using a linear programming approach with Bayesian inference. The key innovation is allowing "path cancellations" that can imply conditional independencies without ruling out confounding causal paths, thus addressing a critical limitation of faithfulness-based methods.

The paper makes important connections to several strands of prior work: Spirtes et al.'s faithfulness assumption, Entner et al.'s rule-based approach for identifying admissible sets, and the instrumental variable (IV) literature (e.g., Balke and Pearl, Manski). The authors correctly position their work as bridging the gap between faithfulness-based point estimates and IV-based bounds, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Quality:** The theoretical development is rigorous and well-justified. The authors carefully formalize the relaxation of faithfulness, provide a clear parameterization of the joint distribution, and develop both numerical and algebraic approaches for obtaining bounds. The Bayesian framework for handling uncertainty in the parameters is well-motivated and addresses a key limitation of previous point estimate approaches. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. The synthetic experiments only consider a specific graph structure with 8 covariates, and the real-world influenza example is presented as a single case study. More comprehensive experiments comparing against a wider range of causal inference methods (e.g., other bounding approaches, machine learning-based causal estimators) would strengthen the claims. The computational complexity analysis is also insufficient, particularly for larger datasets where the linear programming approach may become prohibitive.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. Key concepts (witnesses, admissible sets, faithfulness relaxation) are clearly explained with helpful figures and examples. The algorithm outline (Algorithm 1) is straightforward. However, some technical details in Section 3 (particularly regarding the dual polytope constraints) would benefit from more intuitive explanation for readers without extensive background in linear programming. The supplementary material appears to contain important details that aren't fully elaborated in the main text.

**Originality:** The core contribution is novel: a framework for obtaining bounds on ACE that explicitly accounts for potential violations of faithfulness through the "witness" concept. While bounded causal inference has been studied (e.g., Ramsahai, Manski), the specific approach of using witnesses to identify and relax faithfulness assumptions is new. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior work, particularly from Entner et al.'s point estimate approach. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution algorithm provide a computationally efficient alternative to full linear programming, which is an important practical contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. Causal inference in observational studies is a fundamental problem with wide applications. Current practice often relies on fragile point estimates based on faithfulness assumptions, which can produce misleading results when assumptions are violated. The WPP approach provides a more robust alternative that acknowledges uncertainty about causal structure. The real-world application to influenza vaccination demonstrates the method's practical relevance, though the limited empirical evaluation weakens this claim.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**  
- Novel framework addressing a significant limitation in current causal inference practice  
- Rigorous theoretical development with clear connections to prior work  
- Practical implementation with real-world application  
- Bayesian approach provides principled handling of uncertainty  
- Computationally efficient back-substitution algorithm  

**Arguments against Acceptance:**  
- Limited experimental evaluation (only one real-world example)  
- Insufficient computational complexity analysis  
- Some technical details not fully explained  
- Relaxation parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) lack systematic exploration or justification  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution to causal inference by providing a more robust method for estimating causal effects in observational studies. The WPP framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and addresses a genuine limitation in current practice. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons and sensitivity analyses, the methodological contribution is significant. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited experimental evaluation and clarify some technical details. The work should be of interest to both the causal inference and machine learning communities at NIPS.
