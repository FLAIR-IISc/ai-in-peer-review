- Introduces a novel method for estimating causal effects under confounding by exploiting observational conditional independencies to suggest “weak” paths and relax faithfulness.  
- Produces a posterior distribution over bounds on the average causal effect using linear programming coupled with Bayesian inference.  
- Well-written paper with clear summary and connection to prior work.  
- Methodology is clearly explained with a thorough discussion of strengths and weaknesses.  
- Provides a compromise between point estimators under faithfulness and bounds from instrumental variable (IV) methods.  
- More robust than traditional faithfulness-reliant methods and favors honest inference via bounds rather than point estimates.  
- Demonstrates extensive experiments, including synthetic studies and a real-world influenza vaccination–hospitalization study.  
- Empirical results show stability and more accurate estimates compared to traditional methods.  
- Choice of relaxation parameters is crucial and requires domain background knowledge.  
- Approach can be computationally expensive and may not scale well to large datasets.  
- Assumes modest problem dimensionality, which limits applicability in high-dimensional settings.  
- Originality: novel combination of IV analysis, Bayesian inference, and linear programming for causal bounds.  
- Significance: offers a robust, honest tool for observational studies with potential widespread adoption.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—address parameter guidance, computational complexity, and scalability; overall rating 8/10.
