- Introduces a novel method to bound average causal effects (ACE) in observational studies by relaxing the faithfulness assumption to allow “path cancellations.”
- Uses a linear programming framework combined with Bayesian inference to produce posterior distributions over ACE bounds.
- Builds on Pearl’s do-calculus, instrumental variable techniques, and Entner et al.’s admissible set work.
- Addresses the important problem of unmeasured confounding in observational causal inference.
- Proposes a new perspective by allowing faithfulness violations and leveraging LP/Bayesian machinery.
- Provides theoretical grounding in causal graphical models and connects to existing instrumental variable analyses.
- Offers practical considerations and computational relaxations, notably a back-substitution algorithm for scalability.
- Supplies a comprehensive background review on relevant concepts (witnesses, admissibility, IVs).
- Presents synthetic and one real-world experiment demonstrating the method’s potential.
- Methodology is complex with dense notation and heavy background required to understand it fully.
- Computational cost remains a concern, especially for high-dimensional data, with scalability left to future work.
- Relies on tunable parameters (w, y, x, β, β̄) with insufficient guidance on selection and impact.
- Interpretation of possibly wide bounds is unclear, leaving practitioners uncertain how to use results.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope (single real dataset) and lacks diverse scenarios.
- Method depends on Entner’s Rule 1 and may report solutions even when none theoretically exist.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, complementary to existing tools, with practical algorithmic work and initial empirical support.
- Arguments against acceptance: complexity and computational burden, lack of parameter-selection guidance, ambiguous bounds interpretation, and limited experiments.
- Overall: significant and well-written contribution but needs clearer parameter guidance, better scalability, and broader empirical validation.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address computational cost, parameter selection, bounds interpretation, and expanded experimental evaluation.
