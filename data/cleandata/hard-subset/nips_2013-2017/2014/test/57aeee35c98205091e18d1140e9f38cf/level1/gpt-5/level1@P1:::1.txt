Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new framework, the Witness Protection Program (WPP), for bounding the average causal effect (ACE) from observational data when confounding may be present and faithfulness may fail approximately. The key idea is to invert the use of Entner–Hoyer–Spirtes Rule 1: instead of using a “witness” W and an admissible set Z to justify back-door adjustment (hence a point estimate under faithfulness), WPP treats W as an “almost-instrument” and derives bounds on ACE using linear programming under explicitly parameterized relaxations of faithfulness. The relaxations allow bounded violations of (i) exclusion (W→Y given X,Z), (ii) independence between W and unobserved confounders of X and Y, and (iii) stability of P(X|W) and P(Y|X,W) across levels of W, controlled by parameters w, x, y, β, β̄. These induce constraints on partially latent parameterizations that extend Balke–Pearl and Dawid/Ramsahai IV polytopes. The authors further provide (i) a Bayesian implementation using constrained Dirichlet priors for P(Y,X,W|Z), with posterior inference over upper and lower ACE bounds via rejection sampling, and (ii) analytical algebraic inequalities plus a “back-substitution” message-passing-like procedure that accelerates feasibility checks and bound computation. Experiments on synthetic data and a flu-vaccine encouragement example show that WPP intervals can be substantially tighter than standard IV bounds and more robust than naively adjusted or faithfulness-based point estimators.

This work builds on and connects several strands: instrumental-variable bounds for binary models (Balke & Pearl; Dawid; Ramsahai & Lauritzen), constraint-based discovery and faithfulness (Spirtes, Glymour, Scheines; Entner et al.), and recent inequality-based approaches (Evans; Richardson & Robins). The inversion of Rule 1 to generate candidate near-IVs and the explicit, tunable relaxations appear novel.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A principled bridge between faithfulness-driven point identification and IV-based partial identification, with explicit knobs (w, x, y, β, β̄) to encode “how much” unfaithfulness is tolerable.
- Methodological depth: Solid use of convex-analytic machinery from IV polytopes, extended to conditional settings with relaxations. The algebraic bounds and back-substitution heuristic offer useful insight and computational savings.
- Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty: Constrained Dirichlet priors over observable margins, yielding posterior distributions over bounds rather than fragile priors on latent causal parameters.
- Practicality: An end-to-end procedure (Algorithm 1) that searches witnesses/admissible sets, tests model feasibility, and aggregates posterior bounds. Code availability is a plus.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic studies where naive back-door adjustment is provably wrong, and a real encouragement design with tighter WPP bounds than standard IV.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Assumption selection: The paper defers principled choice of relaxation parameters. Since these critically determine bound width and potential bias, more guidance or data-driven calibration (e.g., sensitivity curves, priors over w,x,y,β,β̄, or elicitation protocols) is needed beyond brief remarks.
- Bayesian implementation: Rejection sampling from constrained Dirichlets can be inefficient; acceptance-rate diagnostics and comparison against more scalable samplers (hit-and-run, projected HMC) would strengthen the claim of practicality.
- Experiments: Comparisons focus on NE1/NE2 and the Entner Rule 1 estimator. Missing are baselines such as (i) standard IV bounds conditional on Z where applicable, (ii) Manski-type worst-case bounds without witness information, and (iii) sensitivity analyses in the spirit of Rosenbaum for unmeasured confounding. Reporting coverage vs. width trade-offs, and how often WPP falsifies candidate witnesses, would also help.
- Robustness when Rule 1 fails: In “no valid witness” cases, the method still often proposes witnesses under approximate independencies. While empirically robust, there is a risk of anti-conservatism; more diagnostics (posterior probability of constraint satisfaction, multiplicity correction over searched pairs) would be reassuring.

Clarity
- The exposition is dense; several definitions and indices (e.g., table/parameter notation, mapping between ζ, κ, η, ω) would benefit from a compact glossary. Some equations are referenced but not shown fully in the main text; include at least a representative subset with intuition.
- The description of how κ and ω are linked to observables via β,β̄ bounds is central; a small worked example (one z-cell) would substantially improve readability.
- Minor typos and glitches (e.g., Polymake URL misspelling, “parameterizatios”) should be fixed.

Originality
- Using faithfulness-derived witnesses as approximate IVs with tunable relaxations is, to my knowledge, new, as are some of the algebraic bounds and the back-substitution algorithm. The combination of linear programming, Bayesian posterior over bounds, and constraint-based discovery is also novel.

Significance
- The problem is important. Many observational studies sit between the ideal of correct back-door adjustment and the pessimism of worst-case IV bounds. A calibrated middle ground is valuable. However, current scope is restricted to binary variables and modest Z; discussing extensions (multinomial, continuous via discretization or parametric links) would broaden impact.
- The real-data example is compelling, but further case studies and a more thorough sensitivity analysis would better demonstrate practical adoption.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a principled strategy to set or learn (w,x,y,β,β̄): e.g., weakly informative priors with posterior-averaged bounds, or reporting bounds as functions of these parameters (sensitivity plots).
- Quantify computational complexity and acceptance rates for the Bayesian step; compare numeric LP vs. back-substitution in time and bound tightness on benchmarks.
- Add baselines (standard IV conditional on Z; Manski bounds) and report width/coverage jointly.
- Discuss multiple-testing corrections or Bayesian model averaging across (W,Z) pairs to avoid cherry-picking.
- Consider diagnostics that flag when the posterior mass concentrates near constraint boundaries, indicating potential model misspecification.

Pros
- Timely and conceptually elegant compromise between competing causal paradigms.
- Sound convex-analytic foundation; useful new inequalities and fast approximate solver.
- Bayesian treatment of sampling uncertainty over bounds.
- Promising empirical behavior.

Cons
- Choice of relaxation parameters left largely to the user; limited guidance.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader; diagnostics limited.
- Restricted to binary variables; clarity could be improved.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a novel and potentially influential perspective on causal inference under imperfect assumptions, with solid methodology and reasonable empirical support. With improved guidance for choosing relaxations and clearer exposition, it would make a strong contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
