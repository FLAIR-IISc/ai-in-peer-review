Summary
The paper tackles a central challenge in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss, lapses, motor noise) from behavioral data. The authors (i) introduce a flexible, one-dimensional observer model that parameterizes stimulus-to-measurement mapping, likelihood (via max-entropy two-Gaussian mixtures), priors (Gaussian lattice mixtures), loss (inverted Gaussian mixtures), lapses, and motor noise; (ii) derive an a priori identifiability measure based on the expected log-likelihood of data generated from a reference observer θ* under a candidate observer θ, leading to a sum of KL divergences between response distributions; and (iii) propose to sample an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(E[log p(D|θ)]) via MCMC to map identifiability landscapes. They apply the framework to two case studies: interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen-like) and speed perception (Stocker & Simoncelli-like). For timing, they find substantial degeneracy between sensory and motor noise and poor recovery of priors unless motor noise is known; encouraging MAP-like behavior worsens prior recovery due to low identifiability of loss width. For speed, they find that slow-speed priors and noise parameters are relatively well recoverable, even when decoupling internal from external noise.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-motivated by classic ill-posedness of inverse BDT modeling and connects to prior critiques and identifiability work (e.g., Myung, Pitt, Navarro). The approach relates to asymptotic model comparison via expected log-score/cross-entropy, and to optimal design/MI/Fisher information ideas, though the latter are not fully developed. The observer class synthesizes elements widely used in perceptual modeling (log/Weber-Fechner mappings, Gaussian mixtures for likelihoods/priors, lapse models, motor noise) and packages them for identifiability analysis. The case studies build on well-known timing and speed literature.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a priori identifiability as expected log-likelihood under the true model, yielding a tractable KL-based objective that scales with trials and experimental stimulus frequencies.
- A flexible yet computationally efficient observer class with analytic structure (Gaussian-mixture-based expected loss), enabling broad coverage of priors/likelihoods/losses and shared parameters across conditions.
- Concrete, insightful case studies: diagnosis of parameter degeneracies in timing, and robustness in speed perception. The design-level implications (e.g., independent motor noise measurement helps; imposing narrow losses can hurt) are non-trivial and useful.
- Emphasis on experimental design: comparing designs by expected identifiability and pinpointing fragile parameters.

Weaknesses
- The identifiability analysis is entirely within-class (the true observer lies within the chosen parametric family). Robustness to model misspecification (e.g., mismatched likelihood family, non-Gaussian tails, non-difference losses) is not systematically assessed.
- The “approximate expected posterior” is not a true posterior; its properties (e.g., dependence on implicit parameter volume, sensitivity to reparameterization) and MCMC convergence diagnostics are not detailed. No comparison to local criteria (Hessian/Fisher information, profile likelihoods) or to Bayesian optimal design baselines.
- Some key modeling choices can bias identifiability: two-component mixtures may be restrictive; the prior lattice size M and grid spacing a can affect recoverability; the mapping f(s) and its scaling interact with σℓ identifiability.
- Claims of “theoretical validation” for slow-speed priors are a bit strong given simulation-only evaluation and problem conversion from 2AFC to estimation; details of that mapping are deferred.
- Important practical details are missing: computational cost, number of MCMC samples, chains, diagnostics, annealing/temperatures, and integration methods for KL terms.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the expected log-likelihood derivation giving sums of KL divergences is correct and appropriate for a priori identifiability. The observer model is carefully constructed and internally consistent. However, the empirical section would benefit from more rigorous diagnostics (e.g., posterior surface geometry, convergence checks, ablations on mixture components M, and demonstration of numerical stability). Some conclusions (e.g., speed prior “validation”) should be tempered to “within-model robustness.”

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful modular breakdown (sensation, perception, decision, response). Notation is heavy and sometimes overloaded (e.g., s0 is both sensory scale and prior scale elsewhere; A,B chosen to map to [-1,1] could be clarified). Some references lack years/details. More explicit algorithmic details (pseudo-code, computational complexity) and a brief schematic of the MCMC workflow would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The observer class and the use of expected log-likelihood/KL to map identifiability landscapes are sensible and useful, though conceptually related to established ideas (model distinguishability, cross-entropy, optimal design). The contribution here is a cohesive framework tailored to Bayesian perception models and its concrete application to experimental design choices.

Significance
The framework can inform experimental design and guard against over-interpretation of fitted Bayesian models—issues of broad interest in cognitive/computational neuroscience and ML. If adopted, it could raise the standard for reporting identifiability and for planning informative experiments.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide computational details: number of trials per design in simulations, MCMC settings (chains, steps, acceptance, ESS), and runtime. Release code.
- Compare to local identifiability metrics: Hessian/Fisher information at θ*, profile likelihoods, or sloppiness analysis; do they agree with your global findings?
- Sensitivity analyses: vary M (prior lattice size), allow >2 mixture components, or different f(s) families; test robustness to misspecification (e.g., true loss not a difference function; multimodal posteriors).
- Stimulus optimization: given Eq. 12, can you optimize Pi (and stimulus set) to maximize expected KL (or reduce posterior entropy), turning this into a principled design tool?
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and check that conclusions hold in a discrete-choice variant.
- Consider including parameter priors in E(θ|θ*) and discuss reparameterization effects.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-served problem with clear practical impact.
- Solid theoretical grounding and insightful case-study findings.
- Provides a general, reusable framework for a priori identifiability and design comparisons.

Cons against acceptance
- Evaluation is simulation-only and within-model; limited robustness analyses and methodological baselines.
- Missing implementation details and diagnostics; uncertain computational scalability.
- Some modeling choices and claims could be better justified or tempered.

Overall recommendation
A strong and timely contribution that will benefit the community. With added robustness checks, implementation details, and modest tempering of claims, it would be a compelling NeurIPS paper.
