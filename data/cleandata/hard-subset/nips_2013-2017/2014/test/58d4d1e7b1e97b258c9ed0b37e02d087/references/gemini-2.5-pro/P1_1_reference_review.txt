### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in the field of computational cognitive science: the non-identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that observed behavior can often be explained by multiple combinations of internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function), which undermines the effort to recover these representations from data. To tackle this, they propose a novel framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis.

The core of their contribution is twofold:
1.  They develop a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians and flexible mappings to represent a wide range of sensory noise characteristics, priors, and loss functions. The careful construction ensures that the observer's response distribution can be computed efficiently.
2.  They introduce a method to quantify the identifiability of model parameters without running costly simulations of experiments. This is achieved by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model given a "ground truth" reference model. They show this quantity is directly related to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from a resulting "approximate expected posterior density" via MCMC, they can visualize the "identifiability landscape" for the model parameters.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies. First, in a time interval estimation task, they show that recovering the observer's prior is surprisingly difficult and highly dependent on experimental design. Their analysis yields non-obvious predictions, such as that forcing a MAP-like loss function can worsen identifiability, and that independently measuring motor noise is crucial. Second, they re-examine the classic "slow-speed prior" in vision, showing that, in contrast to the timing task, the key parameters (the prior and noise characteristics) are robustly identifiable within their framework. This provides valuable theoretical support for previous empirical findings.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a critical, well-known, and often-ignored problem in cognitive modeling. The idea of creating a systematic, quantitative framework to assess model identifiability *before* conducting an experiment is highly original and of immense practical value. It moves the field from ad-hoc assumptions and post-hoc justifications to a principled, a priori design methodology. This could potentially save countless hours of experimental work and lead to more robust scientific conclusions.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and elegant. The construction of the general observer model is thoughtful, balancing flexibility (e.g., non-Gaussian noise/loss, non-linear sensory mappings) with computational tractability. The methodological leap of linking the expected log-likelihood to the KL divergence is clever, providing an efficient way to explore the identifiability landscape without brute-force simulation. The application to the case studies is convincing and demonstrates the power of the approach.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction. The four stages of the model are explained logically and systematically. The derivation of the identifiability metric is concise, and its application in the results section is easy to follow. The conclusions drawn from the case studies are concrete and insightful.

4.  **Potential Impact:** The work is likely to be highly influential for researchers using Bayesian models to study perception and cognition. It provides a practical tool for designing better experiments. The finding that certain experimental manipulations can have counter-intuitive effects on parameter recovery (e.g., the MAP loss function example) is a crucial insight that could prevent researchers from drawing erroneous conclusions.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The analysis is conditioned on a chosen "reference observer" θ*. The conclusions about which experimental design is best might depend on the specific parameter values chosen for this reference. While the authors mention that the framework allows testing robustness by modifying these assumptions, the paper would be stronger if it included a brief sensitivity analysis. For example, in the interval timing case, would the MTR design still be superior if the true sensory noise (σ) in the reference observer was significantly larger or smaller? A short demonstration of this would bolster the generality of the conclusions.

2.  **Scope of the Model:** The framework is currently limited to 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this is a large and important class of experiments, many classic paradigms in psychophysics rely on discrete choices (e.g., 2-Alternative Forced Choice, 2AFC). The authors mention extending the model to these paradigms as future work, but the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the specific challenges this would entail. For instance, how would the KL-divergence metric, which operates on probability densities, be adapted for discrete response probabilities?

3.  **Model Class Assumption:** The analysis tests for identifiability *within the proposed class of models*. It implicitly assumes that this flexible model class is a good approximation of the true generative process in the human observer. While this is a necessary and reasonable assumption for any modeling endeavor, it is a limitation worth acknowledging explicitly. The framework can identify degeneracies within its own parameter space, but it cannot rule out the possibility that an entirely different model structure could also explain the data.

4.  **Clarity of Terminology:** The term "approximate expected posterior density" (Eq. 13) is slightly unwieldy and potentially confusing, as it is not a posterior in the standard sense (i.e., P(θ|Data)). It is a measure of model distinguishability from θ*, cleverly formulated to be sampleable like a posterior. Perhaps reframing it as an "identifiability distribution" or explicitly clarifying this distinction in the text could prevent misinterpretation.

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the methodology of computational modeling in neuroscience and psychology. It offers a novel, principled, and practical solution to the persistent problem of model non-identifiability. The framework is technically strong, the presentation is clear, and the case studies provide compelling demonstrations of its utility. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the scope of the current work and potential avenues for future extensions rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it an ideal candidate for acceptance at NIPS. It provides a tool and a way of thinking that other researchers are very likely to use and build upon.
