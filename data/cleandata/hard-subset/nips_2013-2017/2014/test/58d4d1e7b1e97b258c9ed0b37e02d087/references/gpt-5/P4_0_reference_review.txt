Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the long-standing non-identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception: distinct combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss can yield indistinguishable behavior. The authors propose an a priori framework to assess practical identifiability for estimation tasks before collecting data. They introduce a flexible parametric observer family encompassing: (i) a monotonic stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) with Weber–Fechner/Stevens-like behavior; (ii) measurement noise modeled as a unimodal mixture of Gaussians (matching first four moments); (iii) a Gaussian-mixture basis prior in internal space; (iv) a smooth, unimodal loss modeled as a mixture of inverted Gaussians; plus lapse and motor noise. The key methodological contribution is to quantify expected model support for a candidate parameter vector θ when data are generated from a reference observer θ*, via the expected log-likelihood, which reduces to a trial-weighted sum of KL divergences between predictive response densities. They sample an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp〈log Pr(D|θ)〉 using MCMC to map identifiability and compare experimental designs.

Two case studies illustrate the approach: (1) interval timing with short/long stimulus ranges (as in Jazayeri & Shadlen 2010) where the goal is to reconstruct priors; and (2) speed perception with contrast-dependent noise and a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli 2006). The analysis reveals that in interval timing, priors are poorly recovered under a baseline design due to degeneracy between sensory and motor noise; narrowing the loss (MAP-like feedback) further worsens identifiability of the prior; fixing motor noise in a separate measurement substantially improves recovery. In speed perception, both the prior exponent and noise parameters are more reliably identifiable, even when allowing the observer’s internal likelihood to decouple from the generative noise.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the identifiability problem in Bayesian observer models and its practical consequences for inferring internal representations.
- A flexible observer model that yields fast computation (analytic mixture-of-Gaussians structure for expected loss), enabling exploration of high-dimensional parameter spaces.
- Derivation of an expected log-likelihood criterion reducible to KL divergences, linking to established model comparison/objective design ideas and avoiding dataset resampling.
- Concrete, well-motivated case studies demonstrating non-trivial design insights (e.g., MAP-like losses can hinder prior recovery; motor-noise calibration is crucial).
- Potentially general tool for pre-experimental design assessment in a widely used modeling paradigm.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (technical soundness)
- The expected-support criterion corresponds to maximizing expected log-likelihood (equivalently minimizing expected KL). This is sensible, but the paper would benefit from situating it explicitly within the literature on Bayesian optimal experimental design/expected utility (e.g., Lindley 1956; Chaloner & Verdinelli 1995; MacKay 1992; Paninski 2005), and clarifying that this is an a priori, average-case metric rather than a full posterior over θ given finite data.
- The identifiability analysis assumes the inference model class matches the data-generating class (aside from parameter values). Robustness to model mismatch is not examined (e.g., true loss not representable by the chosen mixture, f(s) not in Eq. 2, or non-unimodal priors). This may overstate identifiability in practice. A sensitivity analysis to structured misspecifications would strengthen claims.
- The assumption that the loss depends only on t̂ − t (translation-invariant in internal space) is restrictive; some tasks have state-dependent losses or asymmetric penalties. The supplementary mentions generalization; a brief main-text discussion of implications for identifiability would be useful.
- The lapse model draws from the prior rather than a uniform or broad distribution. This choice can materially affect identifiability of the prior and should be stress-tested (e.g., compare prior-weighted vs uniform lapses).
- In the timing study, the “w1” parameter mentioned as motor noise appears to be a notational error for ρ1. Please correct.
- For the speed study, mapping a 2AFC paradigm to an equivalent estimation task is asserted but not detailed. The equivalence may not hold for identifiability. Provide the exact transformation or validate conclusions on simulated 2AFC data.

Clarity
- The overall structure is clear. However, several key implementation details necessary for reproducibility are deferred to the supplement: choice of M (beyond M=15), grid/range in internal space, constraints for the max-entropy mixtures, MCMC settings (sampler, iterations, diagnostics), computational cost, and Ntr per condition in the speed study. Summarize the most important choices in the main text.
- Some figures are critical to interpret results (e.g., posterior marginals showing degeneracies) but are only referenced. Ensure figures highlight parameter correlations that drive non-identifiability (e.g., σ vs ρ1).
- Provide quantitative summaries for identifiability beyond qualitative statements: credible intervals or entropy of E(θ|θ*), condition numbers of local Hessians, or KL separations across designs.

Originality
- The combination of a unified, fast observer model with an explicit expected-KL identifiability landscape for experimental design appears novel in this domain. Prior distinguishability work (Navarro et al., 2004) is acknowledged; further connecting to the broader optimal design literature would sharpen the contribution.

Significance
- The framework directly addresses a key barrier to interpreting Bayesian observer fits and offers practical guidance for experimenters (e.g., value of measuring motor noise; potential pitfalls of MAP-like feedback). If adopted, it can improve reliability of inferences about priors/likelihoods across sensory domains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Relate the expected-KL objective to information-theoretic optimal design (expected information gain) and discuss design optimization (e.g., over stimulus sets or feedback policies) rather than comparison of a few hand-crafted designs.
- Provide a local identifiability analysis (e.g., Fisher information/Hessian at θ*) to complement global MCMC exploration; this yields interpretable parameter correlations and can guide design.
- Add robustness checks: alternative lapse models; true loss/likelihood outside the chosen mixture families; different f(s) forms; non-Gaussian motor noise.
- Clarify the equivalence between 2AFC and estimation for identifiability or include a 2AFC variant of the model.
- Fix notational inconsistencies and minor typos; define all parameters on first use; report Ntr for speed experiments; include code or explicit pseudocode.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, general problem with a method that is principled, efficient, and practically useful.
- Produces actionable, non-obvious design insights in realistic case studies.
- Provides a versatile observer model that many researchers can adopt.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited robustness analysis to model misspecification; conclusions are within-class.
- Some methodological links (to optimal design literature) and implementation details are underdeveloped in the main text.
- The 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and lapse modeling choices may affect generality and should be substantiated.

Overall, this is a solid contribution on identifiability and experimental design for Bayesian perception models, with room for clarifications and robustness checks that would increase impact and reliability.
