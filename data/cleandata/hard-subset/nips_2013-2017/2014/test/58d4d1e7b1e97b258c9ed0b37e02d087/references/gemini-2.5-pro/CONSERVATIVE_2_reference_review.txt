### Summary

This paper introduces a computational framework for assessing, *a priori*, the identifiability of parameters in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly identify a critical and long-standing issue in computational modeling: different combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can lead to identical observable behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover these components from experimental data. To address this, they first propose a flexible, yet computationally tractable, class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. This model parameterizes the sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss function using mixtures of Gaussians. Second, they propose a method to map the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood for a candidate model, which they show is a function of the KL-divergence from a ground-truth "reference" model. Using MCMC to sample from an approximate posterior based on this measure, they can visualize which parameters are well-constrained by a given experimental design. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one on temporal interval estimation, which highlights potential degeneracies, and another on speed perception, which suggests that the inference of a slow-speed prior is robust.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound in its derivations. The formulation of the observer model is comprehensive, and the derivation that links the expected log-likelihood to the KL-divergence (Eq. 12) is correct and provides a clear computational benefit over brute-force simulations. The use of MCMC to explore the resulting "expected posterior" is a reasonable approach.

However, the main weakness of the paper lies in the strong assumptions underpinning the framework and the limited scope of its validation. The entire analysis is contingent upon a new, highly specific, and complex parametric model class (Eq. 10). While described as "flexible," this model introduces a large number of its own parameters and structural assumptions (e.g., parameterizing all components as mixtures of Gaussians based on their first four moments). The central claim is that this framework can test identifiability, but it primarily tests for identifiability *within its own proposed model class*.

This leads to several concerns:
1.  **Model-Dependence:** The conclusions drawn are entirely conditional on the validity of this specific modeling architecture. For example, the finding in the time-perception case study that a narrow loss function worsens prior recovery is an interesting prediction. However, it is a prediction about the behavior of their model, not necessarily a general truth about experimental design. It is unclear how robust this conclusion would be under a different, equally plausible observer model.
2.  **The "Reference Observer":** The entire method relies on the user specifying a "reference observer" `θ*`. The paper suggests this can be based on "pilot experiments, the literature, or educated guesses." This is a critical step, but its sensitivity is never analyzed. If the initial guess for `θ*` is poor, the resulting identifiability analysis could be misleading. An analysis of the framework's robustness to misspecification of the reference observer is essential but absent.
3.  **Confounding Model Complexity with True Non-identifiability:** The framework uses a very high-dimensional model to diagnose non-identifiability. There is a risk that some of the identified degeneracies (e.g., between sensory noise σ and motor noise ρ1 in the time perception example) are artifacts of the model's specific parameterization rather than fundamental trade-offs in the cognitive process itself. The authors themselves note this degeneracy is "not surprising" because both terms are approximately Gaussian in the internal space chosen, which points to a model-induced issue.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the model is described in sufficient detail (with further details in the supplement), and the case studies effectively illustrate the proposed methodology. Figure 1 provides a helpful schematic of the model pipeline.

### Originality and Significance

The problem of non-identifiability is not new, and general statistical methods for assessing it exist (e.g., based on the Fisher information matrix, or simulation-based methods as in Navarro et al., 2004, cited as [23]). The paper's primary claim to originality lies in providing an "efficient method that exploits the common structure shared by many Bayesian models of sensory estimation."

The significance of this contribution is questionable. Rather than providing a general tool, the authors have developed a specialized tool for a bespoke model class. While the goal of aiding *a priori* experimental design is laudable, the framework's practical utility seems limited by its own complexity and specificity. It is not clear that this approach is fundamentally superior to more established (though perhaps computationally intensive) methods for assessing parameter confounding in hierarchical models. The paper does not provide any comparison against such existing methods, making it difficult to judge the advance.

The framework seems most useful for researchers who are already committed to using the authors' specific observer model. For the broader NIPS community working on cognitive modeling, its applicability is less certain. The core idea of using KL-divergence as an efficient proxy for distinguishability is elegant, but its value is diminished by its tight coupling to a single, unvalidated model architecture.

### Pros and Cons

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and fundamental problem in computational cognitive science.
*   The goal of creating tools for *a priori* analysis of experimental designs is important.
*   The analytical shortcut using KL-divergence is computationally efficient and technically sound.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The proposed solution is tied to a new, complex, and highly specific model class whose generality and validity are not established.
*   The conclusions of the framework are highly dependent on the choice of a "reference model," and the sensitivity to this choice is not explored.
*   The paper fails to compare its approach to existing, more general statistical methods for identifiability analysis, making its novelty and significance difficult to assess.
*   The validation is limited to two case studies, which is insufficient to establish the general utility and robustness of the framework.

### Conclusion

This paper presents an interesting and technically competent attempt to tackle the important problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian cognitive science. However, the proposed framework is built upon a foundation of strong, specific, and largely unjustified modeling assumptions. By creating a bespoke tool for a bespoke model, the authors have limited the work's potential impact and generality. Without a more thorough validation, a sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of the reference model, and a comparison to established methods for identifiability analysis, the paper feels more like a proof-of-concept for a particular piece of software than a fundamental contribution to the field. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
