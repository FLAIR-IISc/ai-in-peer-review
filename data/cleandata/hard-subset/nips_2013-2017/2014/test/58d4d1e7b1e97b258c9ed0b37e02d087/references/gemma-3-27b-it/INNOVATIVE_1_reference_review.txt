This paper tackles a crucial, and often overlooked, issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: identifiability. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inverting behavioral data to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) and propose a framework for *a priori* analysis of identifiability. The core idea – systematically exploring the parameter space to assess how easily different models can explain the same behavior – is excellent and represents a significant step forward. This isn't simply about technical details; it's about fundamentally questioning the validity of conclusions drawn from Bayesian model fitting. The framework itself, built around a flexible observer model, is well-motivated and appears thoughtfully constructed. The application to time and speed perception is a smart choice, connecting the framework to existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the paper’s strongest asset. The problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, while acknowledged, hasn’t been systematically addressed with a framework like this. The *a priori* approach is a particularly clever innovation, shifting the focus from post-hoc validation to design-stage analysis.
*   **Significance:** If implemented and widely adopted, this framework could substantially improve the rigor of Bayesian modeling in perception. It forces researchers to explicitly consider model degeneracy *before* investing time and resources in experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the underlying issues and proposed solutions. The mathematical notation is standard and the model’s architecture is well-illustrated.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the expected support (Eq. 12) and the methodology for exploring the parameter space appear solid. While a full verification of all mathematical details isn't possible within a review, the logic seems consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the computational demands of exploring the model space. While they offer strategies for efficiency, the scaling of the method with increasing model complexity isn't fully clear. A more detailed discussion of computational limitations would be helpful.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The framework, while flexible, still relies on a specific class of observer models. The generalizability of the findings to other model structures isn't explicitly addressed. The choice of Gaussian mixtures, while convenient, might limit the representation of certain prior or likelihood shapes.
*   **Validation:** The application to time and speed perception is illustrative but doesn’t fully *validate* the framework's practical utility. A more extensive set of case studies, potentially including tasks where identifiability is known to be particularly challenging, would strengthen the argument.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate how to use the framework to compare experimental designs, they do not perform a thorough sensitivity analysis to show how robust their conclusions are to slight changes in the assumptions about the 'true' observer (θ\*).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and important framework for addressing a critical issue in Bayesian modeling.
*   The theoretical foundation is sound, and the methodology appears well-developed.
*   The application to established perceptual tasks provides a compelling demonstration of the framework’s potential.
*   The potential impact on the field is significant – it could lead to more robust and reliable inferences about internal representations.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The computational cost could be prohibitive for complex models.
*   The reliance on specific model assumptions limits generalizability.
*   The validation is somewhat limited and a stronger case could be made for the practical usefulness of the framework.
*   The sensitivity analysis is missing, and a more in-depth investigation of the robustness of the framework would be useful.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a genuinely novel contribution. While some limitations exist, the potential benefits of the framework outweigh the drawbacks. I lean strongly towards **acceptance**. The emphasis on *a priori* identifiability analysis is a crucial step towards more rigorous Bayesian modeling in perception. The minor flaws (computational cost, model assumptions, validation) are not fatal, and could be addressed in future work. I believe this paper has the potential to stimulate significant research in this area and improve the quality of Bayesian models used to understand human perception. I would recommend the authors address the computational cost and sensitivity issues in the camera-ready version, if possible.
