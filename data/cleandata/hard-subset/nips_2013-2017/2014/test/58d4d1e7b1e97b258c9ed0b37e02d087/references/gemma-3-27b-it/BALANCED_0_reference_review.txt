This paper tackles the crucial, yet often overlooked, problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inverting behavioral data to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions), and propose a framework to systematically analyze this issue *a priori*. This is a significant contribution, as it shifts the focus from post-hoc justifications of model choices to a more rigorous, predictive analysis of experimental design. The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulates the problem, and presents a technically sound approach. The extension of previous work to a flexible observer model is valuable, and the application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples demonstrating the utility of the framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of non-identifiability is fundamental to the validity of interpreting Bayesian models as process models. This paper directly addresses this challenge.
* **Systematic Framework:** The proposed method provides a systematic way to assess identifiability before running an experiment, allowing for informed experimental design choices.
* **General Observer Model:** The presented observer model is flexible and incorporates many relevant features (sensory mapping, prior specification, loss function, lapses, noise) into a single parametric framework. This enables comprehensive analysis.
* **Analytical Tractability:** Despite the model’s complexity, the authors manage to derive analytical solutions, enabling efficient exploration of the parameter space.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The application to time and speed perception tasks provides concrete demonstrations of the framework’s utility and generates actionable insights. The results regarding the impact of loss function assumptions in time perception are particularly interesting.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The figures are helpful in understanding the model and the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors claim efficiency, exploring the full parameter space, even with analytical solutions, remains computationally intensive, particularly for high-dimensional parameter spaces. The practical limitations of MCMC sampling aren't fully discussed, and the sensitivity of results to MCMC settings could be explored.
* **Model Assumptions:** The framework relies on specific assumptions about the observer model (e.g., Gaussian distributions, specific functional forms for sensory mapping). While these assumptions are reasonable, the sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to these choices isn't thoroughly investigated. It's unclear how robust the conclusions are to deviations from these assumptions.
* **Focus on Parameter Identifiability:** The paper largely focuses on the identifiability of individual parameters.  However, the problem of non-identifiability can also manifest as correlations *between* parameters, even if each parameter individually is identifiable. Exploring these correlations could be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Applications:** The application examples are well-chosen but relatively narrow. Expanding the framework’s application to other psychophysical paradigms (e.g., categorization, perceptual grouping) would further strengthen the paper.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the paper mentions existing techniques for addressing non-identifiability (e.g., Bayesian transfer), it doesn't provide a direct comparison of their effectiveness with the proposed framework. A more thorough comparison would position the contribution more clearly.
* **Discussion of Practical Implications:** The paper touches on experimental design implications, but a more detailed discussion of *how* researchers can use the framework in practice (e.g., specific guidelines for choosing priors, designing experiments) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in computational perception.
* The proposed framework offers a novel and systematic approach to analyzing identifiability.
* The technical details are sound and the results are well-supported.
* The illustrative examples demonstrate the utility of the framework.
* The work has the potential to significantly improve the rigor and interpretability of Bayesian modeling in perception.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the framework could limit its practical applicability.
* The reliance on specific model assumptions raises concerns about robustness.
* The focus on parameter identifiability may not fully capture the complexities of non-identifiability.
* The limited scope of applications and lack of comparison to existing methods weaken the impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The framework presented is a valuable contribution to the field, and the authors have demonstrated its utility with compelling examples. While there are some weaknesses, they are not insurmountable. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Suggested revisions:**

*   **Computational Considerations:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with the framework and potential strategies for addressing them.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to variations in model assumptions (e.g., different distributions, functional forms).
*   **Parameter Correlations:** Explore correlations between parameters and their impact on identifiability.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** Include a more thorough comparison of the proposed framework to existing techniques for addressing non-identifiability.
*   **Practical Guidelines:** Provide more specific guidelines for how researchers can use the framework to inform experimental design.
*   **Expand Applications:** Consider applying the framework to a wider range of psychophysical paradigms.
