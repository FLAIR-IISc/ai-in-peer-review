- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models and proposes an a priori identifiability framework for 1D estimation tasks.  
- Constructs a flexible observer model: parametric sensory mapping, measurement noise & loss as max-entropy two-Gaussian mixtures, Gaussian-mixture prior basis, lapses and response noise.  
- Defines expected support ⟨log p(D|θ)⟩, yielding a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions; maps identifiability landscapes by MCMC sampling of E(θ|θ*).  
- Demonstrates the framework on two case studies (interval timing, visual speed) to compare experimental designs and diagnose parameter degeneracies.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian observer and identifiability literature; novelty lies in applying expected-KL mapping to Bayesian perceptual model identifiability with a unified parameterization.  
- The derivation relating expected log-likelihood to weighted KLs is correct and provides a principled dataset-averaged support metric.  
- The observer parameterization (mixture-of-Gaussians, prior lattice) is coherent and computationally efficient for exploring large parameter spaces.  
- Interval timing case shows σ (sensory) vs motor noise degeneracy and that narrow loss can worsen prior recovery; independent motor measures help identification.  
- Speed perception case shows robust recovery of slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters under the model.  
- Analysis is entirely simulation-based; no empirical validation on human data to confirm predicted identifiability patterns.  
- Results depend on specific parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures, lattice size M, form of f(s)); robustness to model misspecification is not assessed.  
- Key computational details (runtimes, MCMC priors/proposals, convergence diagnostics) are not reported in the main text.  
- Design component is comparative rather than an optimization — no formal optimal-design or information-theoretic algorithm provided.  
- Extensions to discrete-choice paradigms are mentioned but deferred, limiting current scope to 1D estimation.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-structured, but crucial implementation and numerical-method details are relegated to the supplement.  
- Recommend including MCMC convergence diagnostics, priors/proposals, and wall-clock runtimes to improve reproducibility.  
- Suggest sensitivity analyses: number of mixture components, prior lattice resolution M, and robustness to misspecified likelihoods or f(s).  
- Ask for details on how presp integrals are computed, approximations used, and numerical error quantification.  
- Propose complementing E(θ|θ*) mapping with local curvature/Fisher information or posterior-volume measures to quantify identifiability.  
- Recommend exploring automatic design optimization (e.g., maximizing expected KL or mutual information) beyond pairwise comparisons.  
- Request explicit description of the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping used in the speed task and clearer discussion of lapse/sensory/motor trade-offs.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept — technically sound and useful but needs empirical/robustness evidence and clearer implementation details to be a strong accept.  
- Strengths: principled expected-log-likelihood/KL objective, flexible efficient observer model, actionable design insights, good literature coverage.  
- Weaknesses: no real-data validation, heavy reliance on chosen parametric classes, missing computational/ablation studies, limited scope to 1D tasks.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem with a practical tool and yields non-trivial design insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical tests, somewhat ad hoc methodological choices with untested robustness, and limited current scope.
