

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical methodological issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) when attempting to recover internal representations from behavioral data. The authors present a general framework for systematically assessing identifiability *a priori* before conducting experiments. They develop a flexible class of Bayesian observer models that can represent a wide range of perceptual tasks, particularly one-dimensional estimation tasks, and propose a method to quantify identifiability by computing the expected log likelihood of alternative models given data generated by a reference model.

The paper builds on previous work on model identifiability in psychology [22, 23] and Bayesian observer models in perception [7, 9, 12, 14, 24], but provides a novel, systematic framework specifically designed for the perceptual domain. The authors demonstrate their approach with two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception, showing how experimental design choices affect the reliability of parameter recovery.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Significance**: The paper tackles a fundamental and underappreciated issue in the field—model non-identifiability—which has been acknowledged as a concern [17, 18, 19] but has lacked a systematic method for analysis. The authors provide a rigorous framework to address this problem.

2. **Novel Methodology**: The proposed approach to quantify identifiability through expected log likelihood (Equation 12) is elegant and computationally efficient, avoiding the intractable need to simulate thousands of datasets as suggested in previous work [23].

3. **Practical Utility**: The framework provides concrete guidance for experimental design. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can identify suboptimal design choices (e.g., the counterintuitive finding that a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery) and suggest effective alternatives (e.g., measuring motor noise parameters separately).

4. **Clear Technical Development**: The authors provide a thorough mathematical foundation for their framework, with careful attention to the structure of Bayesian models in perception. The model class they define is both general and computationally tractable.

5. **Comprehensive Case Studies**: The two case studies demonstrate the framework's utility in different perceptual domains and provide meaningful insights that could inform future experimental work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: While the framework is designed for one-dimensional estimation tasks, the authors acknowledge that extensions to other domains (e.g., discrete choice paradigms like 2AFC, multidimensional variables) would be needed. The paper's impact is somewhat limited by this scope.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The framework requires sampling from the approximate posterior density (Equation 13) via MCMC, which may become computationally expensive for more complex models or larger parameter spaces. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of computational scaling.

3. **Assumptions in Case Studies**: The case studies rely on specific assumptions about the reference observer (e.g., the prior form in the speed perception study). While the authors acknowledge this, the robustness of their conclusions to alternative assumptions could be further explored.

4. **Comparison to Existing Approaches**: The paper states that "there has been no systematic analysis" of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, but it would be helpful to more explicitly contrast their approach with previous ad-hoc methods used in the field.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, with well-supported theoretical claims and careful implementation of the framework. The authors are appropriately cautious about the limitations of their approach and acknowledge where their framework might not apply. The case studies provide solid empirical validation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the case studies are effectively presented. The figures (referenced in the paper) would likely help readers understand the key insights.

**Originality**: This is a significant contribution to the field. While model identifiability has been discussed before [22, 23], the authors present a novel, systematic framework specifically designed for Bayesian models of perception. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by providing a method that can be applied *a priori* to experimental design.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental issue that affects the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian models in perception. By providing a framework to assess identifiability before conducting experiments, the paper has the potential to improve the quality of research in this area. The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can guide better experimental design.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Addresses a critical methodological gap in the field
- Provides a novel, systematic framework with clear practical applications
- Demonstrates utility through well-executed case studies
- Well-written and technically sound
- Has the potential to significantly improve experimental design in Bayesian perception modeling

**Against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope to one-dimensional estimation tasks
- Computational complexity may limit applicability to more complex models
- Some assumptions in the case studies could be challenged

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of computational neuroscience and psychophysics. The framework addresses a fundamental methodological issue that has been largely overlooked in Bayesian modeling of perception. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides practical guidance for experimental design. While the scope is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, this is a significant step forward that could be extended to other domains.

The paper meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS submissions. It is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents a novel approach to a well-known problem (Originality), and addresses a significant methodological issue with practical implications (Significance).

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The framework has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers using Bayesian models of perception, and the case studies provide compelling evidence of its utility.

## Specific Suggestions

1. The authors could briefly address how their framework might be extended to handle discrete choice paradigms like 2AFC, which are common in perception studies.

2. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability would strengthen the paper.

3. Including a brief comparison to the most common ad-hoc methods used for addressing identifiability in the field would help contextualize the novelty of their approach.

This paper represents an important methodological advance for the field, and I believe it would be valuable to the NIPS community.
