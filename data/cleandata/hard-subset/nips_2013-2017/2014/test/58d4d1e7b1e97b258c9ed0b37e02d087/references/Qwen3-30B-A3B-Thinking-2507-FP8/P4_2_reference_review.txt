

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors correctly identify that while Bayesian models have been widely used to explain perceptual behavior, the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations is ill-posed due to degeneracies in the model parameters. Previous work has attempted to address this through ad hoc constraints (e.g., theoretical assumptions, experimental design choices), but without systematic analysis of their effectiveness. The paper presents a framework for a priori identifiability testing of Bayesian observer models, which allows researchers to evaluate how well model parameters can be recovered before conducting experiments.

The authors introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model class that encompasses a wide range of sensory estimation tasks, with parametric representations for sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages. The key contribution is their method for quantifying identifiability through expected log likelihood under a reference model, which provides a principled way to assess the quality of experimental designs. They validate their framework through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. In the first case, they demonstrate that experimental designs can significantly impact the reliability of inferred priors, with counterintuitive findings (e.g., imposing a narrow loss function may worsen inference). In the second case, they show that the inference of slow-speed priors is generally robust to model degeneracies.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with clear mathematical derivations. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their identifiability measure using KL divergence and log likelihood, which is well-supported by empirical validation in the case studies. The framework is complete, with detailed implementation instructions and a clear demonstration of its utility. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the parametric assumptions in their model class and the focus on one-dimensional estimation tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to validation. The mathematical formulations are well-motivated and explained, and the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's practical utility. The figures (particularly Figures 2 and 3) are informative and clearly present the key results. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the analyses.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature: there is no systematic framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. While general model identifiability techniques exist (e.g., [23]), this work specifically tailors them to Bayesian models of perception, which is a novel application. The framework's ability to evaluate experimental designs before conducting experiments represents a substantial advance over previous approaches that relied on post-hoc analysis of model fits.

**Significance:** This work has high significance for the field of perception research, as Bayesian models of perception are widely used to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The framework directly addresses a fundamental limitation of these models—non-identifiability—which has been noted in previous literature ([17, 18, 19]) but not systematically addressed. By providing a tool to evaluate experimental designs, the paper has the potential to improve the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian models of perception across many studies. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can yield counterintuitive insights (e.g., the negative impact of narrow loss functions) that would be difficult to discover through ad hoc approaches.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a fundamental and previously unaddressed issue in Bayesian models of perception
- Provides a theoretically rigorous and practically implementable framework
- Demonstrates clear utility through well-chosen case studies
- Offers concrete guidance for experimental design
- Carefully evaluates both strengths and limitations of the approach

**Weaknesses:**
- The framework is currently limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, though the authors acknowledge potential extensions
- The parametric assumptions in the sensory mapping and other components may limit applicability to some models
- More diverse case studies would strengthen the argument for general utility

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature with a technically sound solution
- The framework has clear practical utility for improving experimental design in perception research
- The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's value
- The work represents a significant methodological advance that will benefit the field

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The scope is limited to a specific class of Bayesian models (one-dimensional estimation)
- The framework requires significant computational resources for more complex models

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of computational perception. It provides a much-needed methodological advance that addresses a fundamental limitation of Bayesian models of perception. The framework is well-justified, technically sound, and demonstrated through relevant case studies. The paper is clearly written and provides valuable guidance for researchers using Bayesian models of perception. Given the importance of the problem addressed and the quality of the solution, I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
