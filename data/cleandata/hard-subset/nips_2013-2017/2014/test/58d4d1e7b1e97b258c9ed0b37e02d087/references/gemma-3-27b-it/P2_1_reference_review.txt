## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical observed behavior, potentially invalidating inferences about internal representations. They propose a novel framework for *a priori* analysis of identifiability, combined with a flexible Bayesian observer model, and illustrate its use with two case studies – time perception and speed perception. The core idea is to quantify identifiability by measuring the expected support for alternative models given data generated by a reference model, approximating this through KL-divergence.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a long line of work applying Bayesian decision theory to perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4]). It acknowledges the growing trend of interpreting Bayesian models as process models, attempting to recover internal representations ([5, 6, 7]).  However, it directly tackles the issue of identifiability highlighted by previous works ([17, 18, 19]), moving beyond simply acknowledging the problem to offering a systematic analytical approach. It relates to work on model selection and complexity ([28]) by leveraging the concept of likelihood and KL divergence to assess the distinguishability of models. It also connects to broader concerns about the validity of inferring cognitive mechanisms from behavioral data ([22]).

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is a fundamental limitation of applying Bayesian models to understand internal representations, and this paper tackles it head-on.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a structured way to *a priori* evaluate the identifiability of Bayesian models, which is a significant contribution. This allows researchers to anticipate potential problems *before* running experiments.
* **Flexible Model:** The continuous class of Bayesian observer models is well-motivated and covers a wide range of scenarios. The parametrization (Eq. 2, 4, 5, 6) appears to offer a good balance between flexibility and computational tractability.
* **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The mathematical derivations, particularly the connection between identifiability and KL divergence (Eq. 12), are clearly presented.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The case studies on time and speed perception demonstrate the practical applicability of the framework, providing valuable insights into experimental design. The speed perception example provides a nice validation against existing literature.
* **Potential for Impact:**  This work could significantly improve the rigor of Bayesian modeling in perception by encouraging more careful consideration of identifiability issues.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors highlight efficiency, exploring the identifiability landscape (sampling from Eq. 13) could be computationally expensive for complex models or high-dimensional parameter spaces. This isn't a fundamental flaw, but a practical consideration.
* **Assumptions:** The framework relies on several assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, specific loss function forms) which, while reasonable, may not hold in all cases. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions should be further investigated.
* **Reference Observer Dependence:** The results are strongly tied to the choice of the reference observer (θ*).  A more thorough exploration of how different choices of θ* affect the identifiability analysis would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The analysis in the time perception example showed a counterintuitive result (MAP loss worsening inference). This needs further investigation and ideally, a wider range of experimental scenarios should be explored.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the MCMC sampling procedure (e.g., the proposal distribution, burn-in period, convergence criteria). Providing this information would enhance reproducibility.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical analysis is well-supported, and the case studies provide empirical validation. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential challenges.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, some sections (e.g., the mathematical derivations) could benefit from further clarification. The notation is consistent, and the figures are helpful.
* **Originality:** The framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis is novel and represents a significant advance over previous approaches. The combination of a flexible Bayesian observer model with KL divergence-based identifiability assessment is also original.
* **Significance:**  The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of Bayesian modeling in perception.  The framework could be used by other researchers to design more robust experiments and draw more reliable inferences about internal representations.



**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a crucial and often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling.
* Offers a novel and practical framework for assessing identifiability.
* Demonstrates the applicability of the framework with compelling case studies.
* Potential for significant impact on the field.

**Con:**

* Computational cost may be a limitation.
* Relies on several assumptions that may not always hold.
* Limited scope of experimental exploration.
* Lack of detailed implementation information.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong paper** that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The framework proposed is innovative, and the analysis is rigorous. While there are some weaknesses, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns regarding computational cost, sensitivity to assumptions, and providing more details about the MCMC sampling procedure.
