

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical methodological gap in Bayesian models of perception: the issue of model non-identifiability. The authors correctly identify that the inverse mapping from behavioral data to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is often ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these elements can produce identical behavior. While previous work has used various constraints to address this degeneracy, the authors rightly note that there has been no systematic analysis of these approaches' effectiveness. The paper proposes a novel framework to explore identifiability a priori, which could significantly improve experimental design in the field.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel and Important Contribution:** The paper tackles a fundamental issue that has been overlooked in the Bayesian models of perception literature. The identification of model non-identifiability as a central concern is well-justified, and the authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their framework.

2. **Rigorous Methodology:** The development of a flexible Bayesian observer model class (with the four-stage construction) that maintains computational tractability is impressive. The derivation of the expected log likelihood as a proxy for identifiability (Eq. 12-13) is theoretically sound and elegantly connects to established statistical concepts.

3. **Practical Case Studies:** The two case studies (temporal context in interval timing and slow-speed prior in speed perception) effectively demonstrate the framework's utility. The counterintuitive finding that a MAP-like loss function can worsen prior reconstruction (Figure 2d) is particularly valuable and challenges common assumptions in the field.

4. **Clear Organization and Presentation:** The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions, logical progression, and effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts. The mathematical derivations are thorough and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Practical Implementation:** While the framework is theoretically compelling, the paper doesn't provide sufficient guidance for practical implementation. The authors mention "MCMC sampling" but don't discuss computational requirements, software tools, or how this would integrate with existing Bayesian model fitting pipelines in psychophysics.

2. **Assumption Sensitivity:** The framework's results depend heavily on assumptions about the reference model θ*. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the identifiability analysis is to these assumptions. For instance, how would the results change if the reference model had different sensory noise parameters?

3. **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** The two case studies, while well-executed, cover only two specific domains. The paper claims to address "a large class of psychophysical tasks," but the framework's applicability to more complex tasks (e.g., cue integration, multiple conditions with varying constraints) isn't demonstrated. The discussion of extensions in Section 5 remains speculative.

4. **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The paper doesn't compare the proposed framework to existing methods for addressing identifiability (e.g., [23]), nor does it discuss how it might complement them. This weakens the argument for the framework's necessity.

**Quality Assessment:**
The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. The case studies provide convincing empirical demonstrations of the framework's utility. However, the theoretical nature of the framework (not tested on real data) limits its immediate impact. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could have been more explicit about how their framework would need to be validated with empirical data.

**Clarity Assessment:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The four-stage model construction is logically presented, and the case studies are effectively explained. Some technical details (e.g., the parametric form of the sensory map) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with psychophysics, but these are well-justified in the supplementary material.

**Originality Assessment:**
The core contribution—systematic identifiability analysis for Bayesian models of perception—is novel and fills a significant gap in the literature. While related to general model identifiability literature (e.g., [23]), the paper's focus on Bayesian models of perception and the specific application to psychophysical tasks represents a meaningful advance.

**Significance Assessment:**
The paper addresses a critical issue that affects the validity of many conclusions in the field. If adopted, the framework could substantially improve experimental design and interpretation of Bayesian models in perception research. The case studies provide concrete evidence of its potential impact, particularly in highlighting non-intuitive design considerations.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a significant methodological contribution. While the framework is theoretically compelling, its practical adoption would benefit from more concrete implementation details and broader validation across diverse experimental paradigms. The current version is well-executed but would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations noted above.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include a practical implementation guide or pseudocode for the framework, addressing computational considerations.
2. Add a sensitivity analysis exploring how results change with different assumptions about the reference model θ*.
3. Expand the case studies to include at least one more domain (e.g., visual cue integration) to demonstrate broader applicability.
4. Explicitly compare the proposed framework to existing approaches for addressing identifiability.
5. Discuss how the framework might be integrated into existing Bayesian model fitting workflows in psychophysics.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, addressing a fundamental methodological challenge. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS, providing researchers with a powerful new tool for designing and interpreting Bayesian models of perception. As it stands, it's a strong paper that would benefit from additional practical details and broader validation.
