The paper proposes a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue given the increasing reliance on such models to infer internal representations from behavior. The core idea is to provide tools for *a priori* assessment of identifiability, allowing researchers to design experiments that yield more reliable parameter recovery. The authors introduce a flexible observer model and a method for exploring the parameter space to determine how easily different parameter combinations can produce similar behavioral predictions. The application to time and speed perception tasks demonstrates the framework’s utility.

However, despite the importance of the problem and the apparent novelty of the approach, I find several aspects of the paper concerning, ultimately leading me to recommend rejection. My concerns stem from a combination of methodological weaknesses, insufficient clarity, and a lack of compelling demonstration of practical benefit.

Firstly, the choice of a mixture of two Gaussians for both the likelihood and the loss function feels arbitrary and limiting, even if justified as a computationally tractable simplification. The authors acknowledge the possibility of extending this to more components but don’t adequately discuss the impact of this choice on the results. The emphasis on maximum entropy feels similarly ad-hoc. While a reasonable starting point, it isn't convincingly argued why this *should* lead to more representative models. Moreover, the analytical tractability gained by these choices appears to be prioritized over a more realistic or flexible modeling approach.

Secondly, the application to time perception (Section 4.1) reveals a surprising result: a design encouraging a narrow loss function *worsens* inference. This is presented without sufficient exploration of *why* this occurs within the model itself. The authors attribute this to non-identifiability of sensory and motor noise, but the discussion is cursory. The analysis of the posterior density (Figure 2c) feels underexplored – a simple statement that both parameters are poorly identifiable is insufficient. A more in-depth investigation of the correlation structure within the posterior would be highly valuable. The claim that “the degeneracy is mostly removed” with independent motor parameter measurement is also overstated; Figure 2d still suggests considerable uncertainty, even in that scenario.

Thirdly, the computational details surrounding the MCMC sampling (Eq. 13) are insufficient. The choice of prior for the MCMC sampler, convergence criteria, and burn-in period are not described, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the sampled posterior densities. The claim of efficiently exploring the parameter space needs to be substantiated with more details about the computational cost.

Fourthly, the presentation suffers from inconsistencies and a lack of precision. For example, the meaning of “identifiability” itself shifts throughout the paper. Initially defined as the ability to reliably recover parameters, it later becomes linked to the peakedness of the approximate posterior density (Eq. 13). This ambiguity hinders clear understanding. The notation is also sometimes inconsistent.

Fifthly, the application to speed perception (Section 4.2) feels more like a validation exercise than a genuine demonstration of the framework’s power. The results largely confirm existing findings without offering substantial new insights. The statement that inference is “relatively reliable” is vague and not fully supported by the presented figures. The claim that the framework can "correctly infer back the absence of a prior" when using the flat prior model feels trivial, as it should naturally converge to that solution.

Arguments *for* acceptance: The framework addresses a critically important issue in Bayesian modeling of perception. The general approach of *a priori* identifiability analysis is valuable. The model, while somewhat restrictive, provides a starting point for more sophisticated analyses.

Arguments *against* acceptance: Methodological weaknesses and arbitrary modeling choices. Insufficient clarity in presentation and computational details. Limited demonstration of practical benefit beyond confirming existing findings. Lack of in-depth analysis of the results, particularly in the time perception case study. Overstated claims about the robustness and effectiveness of the framework.

In conclusion, while the paper tackles a relevant problem, the methodological limitations, lack of clarity, and insufficient empirical support prevent me from recommending acceptance. The work, as presented, feels incomplete and requires significant refinement before it could be considered a valuable contribution to NIPS. The paper needs a more rigorous methodological justification for its choices, a more thorough analysis of the results, and a more honest assessment of the framework’s limitations.
