This paper tackles a fundamental and often glossed-over issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: identifiability. The authors correctly point out the degeneracy inherent in inverting behavioral data to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions), and propose a framework for *a priori* analysis of this identifiability. This is a bold and valuable contribution. The core idea – systematically exploring the parameter space to assess how much different model configurations can produce indistinguishable behavior – is insightful. The proposed model class, while complex, appears thoughtfully constructed to balance flexibility and tractability. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples and demonstrates the potential utility of the framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The explicit focus on *a priori* identifiability analysis in the context of Bayesian perceptual models is a significant novelty. Most work focuses on fitting models *after* the data are collected, implicitly assuming identifiability. This paper challenges that assumption head-on.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical justification for the degeneracy and the proposed method for exploring identifiability (using KL divergence and MCMC) seem well-founded.
*   **Generality:** The model class is designed to be broadly applicable across different sensory modalities and tasks.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The demonstrated ability to compare experimental designs is highly valuable. Identifying designs that yield more identifiable parameters is a major step forward. The case studies highlight non-obvious dependencies, such as the detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function.
*   **Well-Written:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized, despite the technical complexity.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors emphasize efficiency, the MCMC-based exploration of identifiability is still computationally demanding. Scaling this to more complex models or high-dimensional parameter spaces remains a concern.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The framework relies on a specific parametric form for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. While the authors argue for the generality of this form, it’s still a limitation. The impact of deviating from these assumptions is not fully explored.
*   **Simplified Tasks:** The case studies, while illustrative, are relatively simple. The identifiability landscape might be much more complex in real-world perceptual tasks with more intricate stimuli and responses.
*   **Limited Validation:** The analyses are largely theoretical, based on the assumption of a perfectly rational "reference observer". Empirical validation, comparing the framework’s predictions to actual human performance data, is lacking. A comparison with independent estimation of some key parameters (e.g., motor noise) would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **KL Divergence as Metric:** While justifiable, using KL divergence to quantify similarity of behavioral predictions isn’t necessarily the only or best approach. A more perceptually relevant metric could be considered.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   This paper presents a genuinely novel and important perspective on a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   The framework has the potential to significantly improve experimental design and the interpretation of model fitting results.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the proposed method is well-motivated.
*   Even with current limitations, the insights gained from this work are valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost could limit the practical applicability of the framework.
*   The reliance on specific model assumptions might restrict its generality.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the conclusions.
*   The paper doesn’t fully explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of identifiability metric.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important and often-overlooked problem. While there are some limitations, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I lean towards acceptance. The framework is a valuable contribution that will likely stimulate further research in this area. The authors have provided a compelling theoretical justification and demonstrated the framework's utility with insightful case studies. While empirical validation is desirable, I believe the current work is significant enough to warrant acceptance, even in its current form. I strongly encourage the authors to address the computational cost and model assumptions in future work, and to prioritize empirical validation.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
