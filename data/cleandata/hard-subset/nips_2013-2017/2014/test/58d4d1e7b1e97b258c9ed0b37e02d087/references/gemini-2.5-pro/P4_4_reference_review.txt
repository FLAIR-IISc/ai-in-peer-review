### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a computational framework for assessing, *a priori*, the identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors first highlight a critical, known issue in this field: different combinations of internal representations—specifically priors, likelihoods, and loss functions—can produce identical observable behavior, a problem of model non-identifiability. This degeneracy poses a significant challenge to studies aiming to recover these internal representations from behavioral data. While experimental design choices can help constrain the problem, there has been no systematic way to evaluate their effectiveness before collecting data.

To address this, the paper proposes a two-part solution. First, it develops a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model parameterizes all key components of the decision process: the sensory mapping from physical to internal space, the sensory noise (likelihood), the prior belief, the loss function, decisional lapses, and motor response noise. Crucially, components like the prior and loss function are represented as mixtures of Gaussians, which allows for approximating a wide range of shapes while retaining analytical tractability for key computations.

Second, the paper introduces a method to quantify identifiability without running simulations. It defines the expected support for a candidate model θ, given data generated by a "ground truth" reference model θ*, in terms of the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions predicted by the two models. This metric is used to construct an "approximate expected posterior" over the parameter space. By sampling from this distribution (e.g., via MCMC), one can visualize the "identifiability landscape," where narrow marginals indicate well-identified parameters and wide or multimodal marginals reveal degeneracies.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies from the literature: (1) inferring priors in a time interval estimation task, and (2) reconstructing the "slow-speed prior" in visual motion perception. For the timing task, the analysis reveals significant degeneracies, particularly between sensory and motor noise, and suggests that independently measuring motor noise is a highly effective way to improve the recovery of the prior. Conversely, it shows that encouraging a MAP-like loss function might unexpectedly worsen identifiability. For the speed perception task, the framework suggests that the inference of the slow-speed prior is largely robust, providing theoretical support for the original findings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically strong. The proposed observer model is comprehensive and thoughtfully parameterized. The use of mixtures of Gaussians to represent priors, likelihoods, and loss functions is a powerful choice that balances flexibility with computational efficiency, enabling the analytical calculation of the expected loss. The core methodological proposal—using the KL divergence between predicted response distributions to define an expected posterior—is a principled and clever way to formalize the notion of a priori identifiability. The derivation (Eq. 12) is a standard result from information theory, applied appropriately here. The claims are well-supported by the two case studies, which provide concrete and compelling demonstrations of the framework's utility in evaluating and comparing different experimental designs. The authors are careful to state their assumptions, such as the need to define a "reference observer."

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation, succinctly explaining the fundamental problem of non-identifiability with a simple mathematical argument. The structure of the model is broken down logically into four stages (sensation, perception, decision-making, response), which aids comprehension. The figures are informative and effectively communicate the model's structure (Fig 1) and the results of the identifiability analyses (Figs 2 & 3). The core concepts are explained with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the approach.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While the problem of model non-identifiability is well-known in cognitive science and has been discussed theoretically in the context of Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2006), this paper appears to be the first to propose a general, computational framework to systematically and quantitatively analyze it *before* an experiment is run. This shifts the consideration of identifiability from a post-hoc concern to a key component of principled experimental design. The synthesis of a flexible parametric observer model with an efficient KL-based analysis method represents a significant and novel methodological contribution to the field.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high. Bayesian observer models are a cornerstone of modern computational neuroscience and psychology, but their interpretation is often clouded by the identifiability problem. This paper provides a practical tool that could be widely adopted by researchers to design more informative experiments and to assess the robustness of their modeling claims. The insights derived from the case studies are valuable in their own right; for instance, the analysis of the timing task highlights non-obvious trade-offs in experimental design and provides specific, actionable recommendations (e.g., independently measuring motor noise). By promoting more rigorous modeling practices, this work has the potential to substantially advance our ability to link behavioral data to neural representations of uncertainty.

A minor weakness is that the framework, in its current form, is tailored to 1D continuous estimation tasks. While the authors mention potential extensions, its applicability to other common paradigms like discrete-choice (e.g., 2AFC) or multi-dimensional tasks remains to be demonstrated. Furthermore, the analysis depends on a sensible choice for the reference observer θ*, and the conclusions could be sensitive to this choice. However, this is an inherent feature of any a priori analysis, and the framework itself allows for testing robustness to this assumption.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a fundamental problem:** The paper tackles the critical issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling, which currently limits the interpretability of many studies.
2.  **Novel and practical methodology:** It proposes a novel, principled, and computationally efficient framework for a priori identifiability analysis, representing a significant methodological advance.
3.  **High potential impact:** The tool can directly guide better experimental design in computational neuroscience and psychology, improving the reliability of scientific inferences about internal representations.
4.  **Excellent execution:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and convincingly demonstrates its utility with well-chosen case studies that yield non-trivial insights.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited scope:** The current implementation is restricted to 1D estimation tasks, though the principles may be generalizable.
2.  **Dependence on assumptions:** The analysis requires specifying a "reference observer," which introduces an element of subjectivity, although this is arguably unavoidable for any a priori power analysis.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that is likely to have a lasting impact on its field.
