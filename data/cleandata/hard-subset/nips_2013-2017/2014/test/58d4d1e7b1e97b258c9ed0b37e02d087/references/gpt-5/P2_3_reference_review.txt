Summary
The paper addresses a core challenge in Bayesian models of perception: non-identifiability of internal components (priors, likelihoods, loss) when inferring them from behavioral data. It proposes an a priori identifiability framework tailored to 1D estimation tasks. The authors build a flexible observer model with: (i) a parametric sensory mapping to an internal space where noise is approximately translationally invariant, (ii) measurement noise and loss modeled as maximum-entropy mixtures of two Gaussians (controlling variance, skew, kurtosis), (iii) a Gaussian-mixture lattice prior basis, (iv) lapses and response noise. They define an expected support metric for an alternative parameter vector θ relative to a reference observer θ* as the expected log-likelihood over datasets generated by θ*, leading to a tractable expression in terms of a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimulus conditions. They then sample from E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) via MCMC to map the identifiability landscape. Two case studies (interval timing, visual speed) illustrate how the framework can compare experimental designs and diagnose degeneracies.

Relation to prior work
This builds on the Bayesian observer modeling literature (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli on speed priors; Jazayeri & Shadlen on timing; Maloney & Mamassian on transfer), and on earlier work on model identifiability/distinguishability in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung). The expected log-likelihood/KL connection is standard, but using it for a priori identifiability mapping in Bayesian perception with a unified, efficient observer parameterization is, to my knowledge, new. The paper also connects to “cognitive tomography” and parameter recovery analyses, but focuses specifically on design choices affecting identifiability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation ⟨log p(D|θ)⟩ = const − Ntr Σi Pi KL(presp(·|si;θ*) || presp(·|si;θ)) is correct and provides a principled, dataset-averaged proxy for model support. The observer model is coherent, leveraging mixture-of-Gaussians forms to keep computations fast and flexible.
- Support for claims: The two case studies are convincing as demonstrations. In interval timing, the framework diagnoses a σ (sensory) vs motor noise degeneracy, shows that imposing a narrow loss worsens prior recovery due to σℓ non-identifiability, and that independent motor measurements help. In speed perception, recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters is robust, even when decoupling internal/external noise.
- Limitations: The analysis is entirely simulation-based; there is no empirical validation that identifiability predictions match real parameter recovery on human data. Conclusions depend on the chosen parametric class (two-Gaussian mixtures, fixed prior lattice size M, form of f(s)); robustness to model misspecification is not assessed. Computational details (runtimes, MCMC settings, convergence checks) are not reported in the main text. The design component is comparative rather than optimizing (no algorithm for optimal design). Extensions to discrete-choice tasks are deferred.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. The modeling assumptions and notations are consistent, and the high-level idea is easy to follow. However:
- Some crucial implementation details are relegated to the supplement (maximum-entropy construction of mixture parameters, analytical expressions for expected loss, numerical methods for presp integration). A brief summary in the main text would help reproducibility.
- MCMC details (priors, proposal, diagnostics) and computational efficiency (wall-clock times) should be reported.
- The mapping from 2AFC to estimation for the speed case is only cited; a succinct description would help.

Originality
Moderately high. The observer parameterization is a useful unification of several modeling choices in the literature; the expected-KL objective is standard, but its use to map identifiability landscapes of Bayesian perceptual models and to compare designs is novel in this domain. The finding that loss width can harm identifiability of priors in timing (within this model) is a non-obvious, actionable insight.

Significance
Identifiability is a central issue in Bayesian cognition and psychophysics. A practical, a priori tool to expose degeneracies and to inform design choices is valuable to many researchers. The scope is currently limited to 1D estimation, but the approach could transfer to multi-cue and discrete-choice tasks. The work is likely to influence how Bayesian observer studies are designed and interpreted.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of an a priori identifiability objective based on expected log-likelihood/KL.
- Flexible yet efficient observer model enabling exploration of large parameter spaces.
- Actionable insights for design (e.g., measuring motor noise; risks of narrow loss) and validation of known results (robust recovery of slow-speed prior).
- Good coverage of relevant literature and principled discussion of degeneracies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- No empirical validation on real data to test whether predicted identifiability patterns match practical parameter recovery.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures, prior lattice size), with limited sensitivity analyses.
- Lack of computational detail (runtime, MCMC diagnostics) and ablations (e.g., number of mixture components, M).
- Design component is comparative; no formal optimization or information-theoretic design is attempted.
- Extension to discrete-choice paradigms (very common in this field) is only promised.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide runtime and MCMC convergence diagnostics; include priors over parameters and proposal settings.
- Add sensitivity analyses: number of mixture components; prior lattice resolution M; robustness to model misspecification (e.g., true observer has non-unimodal likelihood or different f(s)).
- Report how presp integrals are computed and any approximations used; quantify numerical error.
- Can you quantify identifiability via local curvature (Fisher information) or posterior volume to complement the E(θ|θ*) samples?
- Consider an automatic design optimization (e.g., maximizing expected KL or mutual information) to move beyond pairwise design comparisons.
- For the speed task, provide the exact mapping from 2AFC to estimation assumptions.
- Discuss identifiability trade-offs between lapse, sensory, and motor noise more explicitly.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a technically sound, useful framework with clear relevance to a recurring problem in Bayesian perceptual modeling, and yields non-trivial design insights. The main drawbacks are the lack of empirical validation and limited reporting of computational details/sensitivity, which reduce immediate reproducibility and generality. With stronger empirical or robustness evidence and clearer implementation details, this would be a solid accept.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-served problem (a priori identifiability) with a principled method.
- Offers a practical tool researchers can use to plan experiments.
- Provides new insights into design choices in interval timing and validates speed prior inference.

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical tests to confirm that predicted identifiability aligns with real-data recovery.
- Methodological choices are somewhat ad hoc and their robustness is not fully explored.
- Limited scope (1D estimation), with key extensions left for future work.
