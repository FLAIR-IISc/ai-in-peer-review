Summary
The paper proposes an a priori framework to assess identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks. The authors build a flexible parametric class of observers comprising: (i) a stimulus-to-internal-space mapping f(s) with parameters allowing both Weber–Fechner and Stevens-like effects; (ii) a sensory measurement model (mixture of two Gaussians in internal space with controlled mean/variance/skew/kurtosis); (iii) a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians; (iv) a loss function expressed as an inverted mixture of Gaussians; (v) lapses and additive motor/report noise. Using this structure, they compute response distributions presp(r|s;θ). To quantify identifiability a priori, they derive the expected log-likelihood of model θ under datasets generated by θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response densities at each stimulus (Eq. 12). They then define an approximate “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(〈log L〉) and sample it via MCMC to visualize identifiability and compare experimental designs.

Two case studies illustrate the approach: (1) interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.), highlighting degeneracy between sensory/motor noise and loss width that hampers prior recovery unless motor noise is independently measured; (2) speed perception (Stocker & Simoncelli), where the slow-speed prior exponent and contrast-dependent noise are recovered relatively reliably even if internal likelihood parameters are decoupled from the generative noise.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well situated within Bayesian perception and decision theory (e.g., Geisler; Knill & Richards; Trommershäuser et al.) and speaks directly to critiques about non-identifiability and “Bayesian just-so stories” (Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis; Navarro, Pitt & Myung). The degeneracy of the inverse mapping (priors × likelihood × loss) is well known; the novelty here is an implementable, task-generic pipeline for probing which parameters are recoverable under specific experimental designs. The approach is close in spirit to model recovery/identifiability analyses common in cognitive modeling, but offers a tailored analytical reduction (Eq. 12) and a compact observer class that enables efficient exploration.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem: Identifiability is central to interpreting inferred priors/likelihoods/loss from behavior; the framework could be widely useful for design planning in psychophysics and computational cognitive neuroscience.
- Technically sound core: The expected log-likelihood reduction to a sum of KLs is correct under standard assumptions and gives a principled, quantitative identifiability criterion. Using E(θ|θ*) for sampling is practical.
- Flexible observer class: The internal-space mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians parameterizations, and analytical expected loss yield tractable yet expressive models that cover many tasks.
- Insightful case studies: The interval timing analysis helps explain typical degeneracies (sensory vs motor noise; loss width) and provides actionable design advice (measure motor noise; be cautious about MAP-like feedback). The speed perception results provide theoretical support for robustness of slow-speed prior inference.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on parametric choices: Identifiability conclusions may hinge on modeling choices (two-component Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss, lattice prior with M=15, lapse drawn from prior, additive report noise). Sensitivity analyses beyond the two case studies would strengthen claims of generality (e.g., single Gaussian likelihood/loss; different lapse models such as uniform responses; alternative priors).
- “Expected posterior” is not a true posterior: E(θ|θ*) lacks priors and is scaled by Ntr; it is fine for ranking/design comparison, but readers may misinterpret it as calibrated uncertainty. Some validation comparing E(θ|θ*) predictions to actual parameter recovery from simulated datasets (even a limited set) would be valuable.
- Limited exploration of design optimization: The framework ranks predefined designs but stops short of optimizing stimuli s_i and frequencies P_i. Casting this as maximizing expected information (e.g., minimizing expected KL risk or maximizing Fisher information) would increase impact.
- Potential identifiability of the internal mapping f(s): The parameters s0 and d may be entangled with prior and likelihood parameters; the paper does not explicitly examine identifiability of f(s) itself.
- Minor clarity/consistency issues: A variable mismatch appears in Sec. 4.1 (“w1” vs ρ1). The 2AFC-to-estimation reduction is referenced but not specified. MCMC details (priors/bounds, diagnostics, runtimes) and the max-entropy construction for mixtures are deferred to the supplement; brief main-text summaries would aid readability.

Quality
Overall technically sound with correct derivations and careful reasoning. The two applications are thoughtfully constructed, although additional validation (simulated model recovery) would increase confidence in the practical utility of the E(θ|θ*) heuristic.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with a coherent narrative from motivation to method to applications. Some notation and methodological choices could be clarified in the main text (as noted above). References are incomplete in formatting.

Originality
Moderately novel. Identifiability analysis per se is not new, but the combination of: (i) a versatile observer class with analytic components; (ii) an a priori, KL-based identifiability objective; and (iii) explicit comparison of experimental designs within canonical perceptual tasks, is a useful contribution.

Significance
High practical relevance for researchers inferring internal representations from behavior. The framework can inform better experimental design and interpretation, and could be extended to cue integration and discrete-choice paradigms.

Suggestions/questions
- Provide limited model-recovery experiments to corroborate that high E(θ|θ*) indeed predicts successful recovery in full likelihood fits.
- Explore alternative lapse models and report sensitivity.
- Analyze identifiability of f(s) parameters explicitly.
- Sketch how to turn the KL-based objective into a design optimization criterion over s_i and P_i.
- Release code to facilitate adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and understudied methodological gap.
- Principled, efficient, and broadly applicable framework.
- Concrete, insightful case studies with actionable guidance.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on specific parameterizations; limited sensitivity analyses.
- No empirical model-recovery validation of the expected-posterior heuristic.
- Design optimization not fully developed.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
