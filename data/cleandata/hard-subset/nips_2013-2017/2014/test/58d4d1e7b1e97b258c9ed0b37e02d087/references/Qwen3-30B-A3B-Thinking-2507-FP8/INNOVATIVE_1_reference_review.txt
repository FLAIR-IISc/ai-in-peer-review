

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a fundamental and long-recognized problem in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors correctly note that multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, making it impossible to uniquely recover internal representations from data alone. While this issue has been acknowledged in the literature (e.g., [17, 18, 19]), the paper makes a significant contribution by presenting the first systematic framework for analyzing identifiability a priori before conducting experiments.

The authors introduce a general class of Bayesian observer models that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks, along with an efficient method to explore identifiability within this framework. Their key insight is to use the expected log-likelihood under the true model as a proxy for identifiability, which they derive using KL divergence. This approach allows them to compute the "identifiability landscape" for different experimental designs without needing to generate and analyze numerous datasets.

The paper's strength lies in its novel conceptual contribution rather than methodological refinements. The framework provides a principled way to evaluate experimental designs before running experiments, addressing a critical gap in the field. The authors demonstrate the utility of their approach through two well-chosen case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. These case studies yield non-intuitive insights (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery in interval timing tasks) that would be difficult to discover without their framework.

The paper makes clear contributions that distinguish it from previous work. While Bayesian observer models have been used extensively in perception research (e.g., [7, 9, 12]), and the identifiability problem has been acknowledged ([19]), no systematic framework for analyzing this problem a priori has existed. The authors build on previous work but provide a novel approach to a fundamental issue.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear mathematical derivations. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility, and the results are well-explained. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the specific parameter choices in their case studies) and suggest clear directions for future work.

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a critical methodological issue that could invalidate many existing studies in Bayesian models of perception. The framework could fundamentally change how experiments are designed in this field, leading to more reliable inferences about internal representations. The authors' finding that standard experimental designs may be insufficient for reliable inference (e.g., in interval timing) is particularly important and has practical implications for experimental psychologists.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to solution to validation. The mathematical formulations are clear, and the case studies provide concrete demonstrations of the framework's utility. The paper would benefit from slightly more discussion about the boundary cases where the framework might not apply, but this is a minor limitation.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel contribution I value highly. It doesn't just propose a better algorithm or a slight improvement to existing methods—it introduces a new conceptual framework for addressing a fundamental problem in the field. While the specific implementation of the framework might be refined in future work, the core idea is significant and has the potential to change how Bayesian models of perception are developed and evaluated.

The paper's main weakness is that it focuses narrowly on perception, which might limit its appeal to the broader NeurIPS audience. However, the framework could easily be extended to other domains (as the authors note in their conclusion), and the core idea of identifying and addressing non-identifiability is broadly applicable across machine learning and cognitive science.

In conclusion, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field with its novel framework for identifiability analysis. It directly addresses a critical methodological issue that has been acknowledged but not systematically solved. The framework is general, theoretically sound, and practically useful, as demonstrated by the case studies. The paper provides a significant new direction for research in Bayesian models of perception and would be valuable to both experimental psychologists and computational modelers.

I recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a genuinely novel conceptual framework that addresses a fundamental problem in the field, with significant potential to change how experiments are designed and interpreted in Bayesian models of perception. The contribution is substantial and represents a meaningful advance beyond incremental improvements.
