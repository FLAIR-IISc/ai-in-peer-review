Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks. The core idea is to (i) instantiate a flexible, computationally tractable class of observer models covering sensation, prior, likelihood, loss, lapse, and response noise; (ii) quantify practical identifiability by the expected log likelihood of a candidate parameterization θ when data are generated from a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions; and (iii) explore the resulting “expected posterior” landscape E(θ|θ*) via MCMC to diagnose degeneracies and guide experimental design. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (demonstrating nontrivial degeneracies between sensory and motor noise, and the negative impact of narrow loss/MAP-like feedback on prior recovery) and speed perception (showing that recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent and noise properties is robust under realistic designs).

This work builds on a large literature using Bayesian Decision Theory to model perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli 2006; Jazayeri & Shadlen 2010; Acerbi et al. 2012; Houlsby et al. 2013), and on general ideas about model identifiability and model comparison (Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004; Spiegelhalter et al. 2002). The use of expected KL to formalize expected evidence under a known data-generating model is consistent with standard information-theoretic approaches and model discrimination. The observer parameterization (mixtures in measurement and loss spaces; lattice-mixture prior) aims to unify and extend prior psychophysical models while maintaining computational efficiency.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The identifiability metric: The derivation that the expected log likelihood over datasets equals a constant minus Ntr times a weighted sum of KL divergences between presp(r|si; θ*) and presp(r|si; θ) is correct and appropriate for quantifying practical non-identifiability under a known generative model. Using E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩) as a surrogate “expected posterior” is reasonable, although it is a likelihood-based diagnostic rather than a true posterior over datasets; clarifying terminology would help.
- Observer model: The sensation stage uses a monotone mapping f(s) with parameters encompassing Weber–Fechner and Stevens’ laws; measurement and loss are modeled as (unimodal) mixtures of Gaussians constrained by low-order moments via a maximum-entropy rationale; the prior is a Gaussian lattice mixture; decision minimizes expected loss; response adds amplitude-dependent motor noise and lapses. These choices are defensible and connect to prior models, and the claim of analytic expected loss for mixture components is plausible (products/integrals of Gaussians lead to closed-form expressions), which supports efficiency.
- Computation: Presp(r|s; θ) requires integrating over latent variables; the paper suggests fast numerical solutions but does not provide complexity, runtime, or accuracy details for KL evaluations and MCMC over a high-dimensional θ (including M mixture weights). Reporting these would strengthen claims of efficiency.
- Case studies: The interval timing analysis convincingly identifies a sensory–motor noise degeneracy and poor identifiability of the loss width σℓ, with concrete design implications (e.g., measuring motor noise improves recovery of priors; MAP-like feedback can be detrimental). The speed perception analysis shows robust recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters across contrast conditions, even when decoupling internal and external likelihoods; this provides theoretical support for prior empirical inferences.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. Assumptions are listed explicitly for each case study. Some technical elements critical to reproducibility are deferred to the Supplement (e.g., how unimodality is enforced for mixtures, the maximum-entropy selection, and numerical evaluation of KL). A brief in-text summary or algorithmic sketch would improve readability. Notation is mostly consistent (p for generative, q for internal), though constraints on mixture weights (normalization, positivity) should be stated explicitly. References are incomplete in formatting.

Originality
- Conceptually, the contribution is to systematize a priori identifiability analysis for Bayesian observer models via expected KL, within a unified parametric observer family and with clear guidance for experimental design. While related to prior work on distinguishability and parameter recovery, the integration and application to concrete perceptual paradigms appear novel and useful.
- Methodologically, the specific parameterization (loss and likelihood as constrained mixtures; lattice-mixture prior) and the use of expected evidence to sample identifiability landscapes are original in this context.

Significance
The framework addresses a recognized concern—non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and losses in Bayesian perception—and provides actionable guidance for experimenters (e.g., which parameters to constrain or measure separately, how multiple conditions help, when MAP-like feedback harms inference). The case studies target prominent paradigms (interval timing; speed perception), increasing relevance. If code is released, this could become a practical tool for power/identifiability analysis in perceptual research.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of a priori practical identifiability using expected KL under known generative assumptions.
- Flexible yet tractable observer class enabling analytic components and efficient numerical evaluation.
- Concrete, nontrivial design insights in interval timing; theoretical support for robustness in speed perception.
- Appropriate positioning within literature; explicit recognition of degeneracy sources and design levers.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Validation against finite-sample recovery: No demonstration that regions of high E(θ|θ*) correspond to successful parameter recovery by standard inference on simulated datasets. Including a parameter recovery study (e.g., MLE/MCMC fits to simulated datasets across Ntr) would validate the surrogate metric.
- Computational details: Provide complexity, runtime benchmarks, and numerical accuracy for presp and KL computations, and MCMC diagnostics over the high-dimensional θ (including handling of mixture label symmetry and constraints on weights).
- Maximum-entropy and unimodality: The construction of mixture parameters from moments and the enforcement of unimodality are central; include a concise description and failure modes (e.g., moment combinations that are infeasible).
- Prior over θ and “expected posterior” terminology: Clarify that E(θ|θ*) is proportional to the expected likelihood (not a posterior unless a prior over θ is introduced). State any priors used during MCMC and parameter bounds/constraints.
- 2AFC-to-estimation mapping: The equivalence used in the speed study is referenced but not detailed; briefly outline or validate that this transformation does not alter identifiability conclusions.
- Scope and robustness: Discuss sensitivity to the choice of M for the lattice prior, to misspecification of f(s), and to alternative loss families. Consider connections to optimal experimental design (e.g., Fisher information, D-optimality) to suggest principled design optimization beyond comparative ranking.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, recognized problem with a principled, general, and practical framework.
- Technically sound core derivation; well-motivated model class with analytic components.
- Provides actionable insights for experimental design and theoretical validation for influential paradigms.
- Likely to be used and extended by practitioners in computational neuroscience/psychophysics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation that the proposed identifiability metric predicts actual parameter recovery with finite datasets.
- Insufficient computational details and benchmarks to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Heavy reliance on Supplement for key technical constructions; some notation/constraint details unclear.
- The 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence is assumed rather than demonstrated within the paper.

Overall, the paper presents a solid and useful contribution; addressing the validation and clarity points above would significantly strengthen it.
