### **Summary**

This paper addresses the critical and well-known problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that identical observed behavior can be produced by multiple different combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, and loss functions). This degeneracy fundamentally challenges the strong interpretation of Bayesian models as process models and undermines attempts to recover an observer's "true" internal representations from data. To tackle this, the paper proposes a formal framework to assess model identifiability *a priori*, before an experiment is conducted. The framework consists of two main components: 1) A highly flexible, yet computationally efficient, class of Bayesian observer models for 1D continuous estimation tasks. This model uses parametric forms and mixture-of-Gaussians to represent a wide range of sensory mappings, likelihoods, priors, and loss functions. 2) A method to quantify identifiability by computing the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, given data generated by a "ground truth" reference model. This expectation is shown to be directly related to the KL-divergence between the response distributions of the two models, providing an efficient way to map the "identifiability landscape" via MCMC sampling. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies from the literature: interval timing and visual speed perception. They show that recovering the prior in the timing task is prone to significant degeneracies (e.g., between sensory and motor noise), and use their framework to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs. In contrast, they find that recovering the "slow-speed prior" in the speed perception task is much more robust.

The work builds on a rich literature critiquing or highlighting the flexibility of Bayesian models (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) and the specific issue of degeneracy (Mamassian & Landy, 2001). It also extends previous flexible modeling work (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014). The primary contribution is not the modeling components themselves, but their synthesis into a principled, systematic framework for prospectively evaluating experimental designs against the challenge of non-identifiability, a problem often acknowledged but rarely addressed with such a formal tool.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-thought-out piece of work.
*   **Strengths:** The mathematical formulation, from the flexible model parameterization to the use of KL-divergence to approximate the expected posterior, is well-justified and clever. The use of mixture-of-Gaussians is a pragmatic choice that balances flexibility with computational tractability (allowing for analytical expressions for the expected loss). The case studies are well-executed and demonstrate the framework's power, yielding both intuitive results (e.g., measuring motor noise independently greatly improves prior recovery) and non-obvious ones (e.g., a narrower, MAP-like loss function can worsen identifiability under their model assumptions). This demonstrates the value of such a formal analysis over simple intuition.
*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis relies on the specification of a "reference observer" `θ*`. The conclusions drawn about an experimental design are therefore conditional on the assumptions made about this observer. The paper is honest about this, stating `θ*` is based on "pilot experiments, the literature, or educated guesses," but it would be valuable to include a discussion on the robustness of the conclusions to variations in `θ*`. For example, how much would the ranking of experimental designs in the timing task change if the true sensory noise was non-Gaussian?

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation is laid out compellingly in the introduction. The four-stage model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) provides a clear conceptual structure. The description of the method for mapping identifiability is concise and understandable. The results sections clearly connect the framework's output to concrete, practical questions about experimental design and the reliability of scientific claims.
*   **Weaknesses:** The conversion of the 2AFC task from the speed perception literature [9] into an "equivalent estimation task" is a critical step that is mentioned only briefly. While this is a common theoretical move, the paper would be stronger if it provided more detail or justification for how this equivalence is established and what assumptions it entails, as it directly impacts the setup of the second case study.

**Originality:** The work is highly original in its approach and synthesis.
*   **Strengths:** While general methods for assessing model identifiability exist (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), this paper presents the first framework, to my knowledge, that is specifically tailored to the structure of Bayesian observer models in perception. This specialization makes it far more efficient and directly applicable for researchers in this field. The idea of using such a framework to *prospectively* compare and optimize experimental designs is a significant and novel contribution. It shifts the discussion from post-hoc criticism of Bayesian models to pre-hoc, constructive design.
*   **Weaknesses:** The individual modeling components (e.g., mixture models for priors, parametric sensory maps) have appeared in previous work, including by the same authors. However, their integration into a general-purpose identifiability analysis tool is what constitutes the main novelty here.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial.
*   **Strengths:** This paper provides a much-needed tool for a community that increasingly relies on fitting complex Bayesian models to behavioral data. If adopted, this framework could lead to more robust and rigorous experimental designs, strengthening the conclusions drawn about internal representations. It provides a concrete way to address the persistent and valid criticisms of the "Bayesian Brain" program regarding model mimicry and non-identifiability. The finding that certain experimental choices can have counter-intuitive negative effects on identifiability is an important lesson for practitioners.
*   **Weaknesses:** The framework's current implementation is limited to 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a large class of psychophysical paradigms, its applicability to other common designs, such as discrete-choice (2AFC) or multi-dimensional integration tasks, is an open question. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it does limit the immediate scope of the contribution.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles the fundamental issue of non-identifiability, a major conceptual weakness in the application of Bayesian models to cognition.
2.  **Novel and Practical Tool:** It provides a novel, principled, and practical framework for researchers to assess the robustness of their experimental designs *before* collecting data.
3.  **High Technical Quality:** The methodology is sound, well-explained, and cleverly implemented for computational efficiency.
4.  **Insightful Demonstrations:** The case studies are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the framework's value, providing new insights into classic findings in perception research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope:** The framework is currently restricted to 1D estimation tasks, though this is a common paradigm.
2.  **Dependence on Assumptions:** The utility of the analysis is conditional on the choice of the "reference observer," and the paper could discuss the robustness of its findings to these assumptions more thoroughly.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the fields of computational neuroscience and cognitive science. It addresses a deep-rooted methodological problem with a novel, rigorous, and practical solution. The strengths in significance, originality, and quality far outweigh the current limitations in scope. This work has the potential to improve the standards of modeling and experimental design in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
