- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models: multiple priors/likelihoods/losses can yield identical observed behavior, impeding recovery of internal representations.
- Proposes a novel a priori framework for testing identifiability plus a flexible Bayesian observer model applicable across psychophysical tasks.
- Uses KL divergence for quantifying model support and provides a rigorous theoretical derivation of the identifiability problem.
- Demonstrates framework utility with case studies in time and speed perception that reveal experimental design issues and yield testable predictions.
- Builds on Bayesian Decision Theory and prior work on degeneracy/identifiability, extending methods with a systematic a priori evaluation approach.
- Model design choices (log-transformed stimulus space, mixture of Gaussians, parsimonious parameters) are well-motivated and practical.
- Framework is a practical tool for researchers to evaluate experiments and interpret Bayesian model results before data collection.
- Writing is clear and the paper is logically organized despite topic complexity.
- Computational cost may be high; exploring parameter space could require substantial computing resources and limit accessibility.
- Key model assumptions (Gaussian noise, unimodal likelihoods, specific loss forms) may affect identifiability; sensitivity to violations is underexplored.
- Focus is primarily on parameter identifiability and does not fully address structural identifiability of model forms (e.g., prior families).
- Case studies are relatively simplified; identifiability in more complex, ecologically valid tasks remains to be investigated.
- Limited exploration of how different prior assumptions influence identifiability and sensitivity to the choice of reference observer θ*.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical methodological issue, is theoretically sound, practically useful, well-implemented, and demonstrated convincingly.
- Arguments against acceptance: potential computational barriers, dependence on specific assumptions, and narrow focus on parameter (not structural) identifiability.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with strengths outweighing weaknesses; recommendation to accept with minor revisions.
- Requested revisions: detail computational cost, expand discussion of model assumptions and impacts, include structural identifiability discussion, and examine sensitivity to reference observer θ*.
