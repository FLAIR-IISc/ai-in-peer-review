Summary
This paper proposes an a priori framework to assess identifiability in Bayesian observer models for 1D estimation tasks. The authors introduce a flexible parametric family: (i) a monotonic sensory map f(s) to an internal space; (ii) a measurement distribution modeled as a two-Gaussian mixture with moment constraints; (iii) a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians; (iv) a loss function modeled as an inverted two-Gaussian mixture; plus lapses and motor/report noise. They derive an expected log-evidence criterion under a reference observer θ∗: the expected log-likelihood over datasets of size Ntr reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response densities under θ∗ and θ, which they exponentiate to obtain an “approximate posterior” E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp(〈log Pr〉) for MCMC exploration of identifiability. Case studies: interval timing (two range conditions); and speed perception (high/low contrast). They show non-identifiability and design impacts (e.g., motor noise confounds) in the timing task, and relatively robust recovery of a slow-speed prior in the speed task.

Strengths
- Timely focus on identifiability in Bayesian models of perception; acknowledges inverse-problem degeneracies.
- Unified, fast-to-evaluate observer model spanning many variants used in the literature; analytical tractability via mixtures is attractive.
- Expected log-likelihood/KL formulation yields a clean, data-size-dependent identifiability landscape, suitable for a priori design comparisons.
- Concrete case studies with plausible reference observers and design variants; some non-obvious conclusions (e.g., narrow loss can hurt identifiability; measuring motor noise helps).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness and completeness
- The “maximum entropy” construction for the two-Gaussian mixtures (likelihood and loss) under moment constraints is under-specified. The max-entropy distribution with fixed moments is not generally a Gaussian mixture; maximizing entropy within a mixture family needs a precise, well-posed optimization and proof of unimodality. Please detail the optimization/constraints and uniqueness; otherwise the construction looks heuristic.
- Prior representation (Gaussian lattice with M=15) lacks discussion of basis sufficiency/approximation error and regularization. Are wm ≥ 0 and sum to 1 (Dirichlet prior)? How are edge effects handled when the true prior has heavier tails than the lattice range?
- The mapping f(s): A and B are chosen so that f(si) spans [−1,1], which depends on the experimental stimulus set. This implies the internal scale adapts to the experiment, which is behaviorally debatable and may bias identifiability outcomes. Either justify this assumption or treat scaling as identifiable parameters with appropriate priors.
- The “approximate posterior” E(θ|θ∗) is simply exp of expected log-likelihood; it is not a posterior (no parameter prior, no complexity penalty). It is equivalent to a cross-entropy objective at sample size Ntr. Please clarify terminology and discuss the relationship to established measures: expected information gain, Fisher information, or Bayesian experimental design (Lindley/MacKay), and motivate why this criterion (asymmetric KL on response densities) is preferable for design ranking.
- MCMC over a very high-dimensional θ (including 15 prior weights + many nuisance parameters) without details on constraints, proposal, convergence diagnostics (ESS, R-hat), or runtime undermines credibility. Provide MCMC settings, priors, constraints (e.g., log/softmax reparameterizations), and convergence evidence.
- Lapse model: sampling from the prior (transformed) is non-standard and potentially confounds lapse rate with prior shape. Sensitivity to alternative lapse models (e.g., uniform or broad Gaussian in response space) should be assessed.
- Decision/response separation: you exclude report noise from the BDT stage; in practice, observers may compensate for motor noise. This choice can create identifiability artifacts (e.g., σ vs ρ1 confounds), and should be justified or tested for robustness.
- In the speed study, converting 2AFC to estimation is asserted but not derived. Provide the mapping/equivalence and validation.

Novelty and relation to prior work
- The use of expected log-likelihood or KL for model discriminability/design has a substantial literature (e.g., Myung/Pitt/Navarro on model distinguishability; Fisher information; sloppiness/sensitivity; Bayesian design via expected information gain). The paper should more carefully position its contribution beyond these, and cite relevant work (including EIG-based design for cognitive/perceptual experiments).
- The observer parametrization (mixture bases, lapse, Gaussian motor noise) is a pragmatic synthesis of familiar components; novelty lies mainly in assembling them for an identifiability workflow. Make this explicit.

Empirical evaluation
- Conclusions depend on a single θ∗ per task; robust design recommendations should marginalize over plausible θ∗ (or report sensitivity analyses across multiple θ∗). Otherwise the “ranking” may not generalize.
- No quantitative design optimization is attempted (e.g., optimizing Pi over stimuli to maximize average KL or EIG). This seems like a natural next step given Eq. 12.
- Figures are critical but not available here; the text mentions “w1” as motor noise, but earlier motor parameters are ρ0, ρ1. This inconsistency suggests notation/typo issues and should be fixed.
- Reporting the magnitude of biases/uncertainties (e.g., credible intervals on kprior, σ̃) and the effective dimensionality (DIC/WAIC/free energy) would better support claims of “reliable” recovery.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation inconsistencies: pmeas vs qmeas; s vs t (task vs internal) not always clear; loss L(s,ŝ) vs L(t̂−t); w1 vs ρ1; pprior vs qprior. Define and stick to one convention.
- Several derivations, including Eq. 12, the analytical expected loss as mixtures, and the max-entropy constructions, are deferred to the supplement; at least sketches or key steps should be in the main text.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (many missing years/volumes/pages; inconsistent formatting). This does not meet NeurIPS standards.

Pros
- Addresses an important and under-discussed issue (identifiability) in a popular modeling paradigm.
- Provides a reusable modeling and computation framework with clear levers for experimental design.
- Case studies are relevant and yield actionable insights (e.g., measuring motor noise; counterintuitive loss effects).

Cons
- Heavy reliance on heuristic constructions and a non-posterior “approximate posterior,” with insufficient theoretical grounding and missing details.
- High-dimensional inference with MCMC lacks essential methodological and convergence details.
- Sensitivity to θ∗ not explored; no principled design optimization; limited scope (1D estimation only).
- Numerous notation/formatting/reference issues and minor inconsistencies.

Questions for authors
1) How exactly are the mixture parameters chosen under the “maximum entropy + unimodality” criterion? Please formalize and provide proofs/algorithms.  
2) How do you enforce constraints on wm and other bounded parameters in MCMC? What priors do you use?  
3) How sensitive are your design rankings to the choice of θ∗? Can you average over a prior on θ∗?  
4) Can you relate Eq. 12 to expected information gain and discuss why your KL direction is most appropriate?  
5) How do results change under alternative lapse models and when incorporating motor noise into decision-making?

Minor issues
- Fix notation (w1→ρ1, p vs q, s vs t), and typos (“consider-able”, inconsistent hyphenation, spacing).  
- Add units for parameters (e.g., s0 in deg/s, ρ0).  
- Provide code and supplement for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The topic is important and the direction promising, but the current manuscript has too many methodological gaps, unclear or heuristic derivations, and presentation/notation issues for NeurIPS standards. Addressing the above concerns—especially formalizing the mixture constructions, clarifying the statistical criterion and MCMC, and strengthening empirical validation and design optimization—would significantly improve the contribution.
