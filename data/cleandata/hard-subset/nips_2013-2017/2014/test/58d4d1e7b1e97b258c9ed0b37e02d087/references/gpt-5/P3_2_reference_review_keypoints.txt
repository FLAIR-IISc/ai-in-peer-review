- Paper addresses non-identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian perceptual models and proposes tools to analyze it a priori.
- Introduces a flexible, computationally efficient 1D Bayesian observer family with parametrized sensory mapping, noise, prior, loss, lapses, and response noise.
- Proposes an expected log-likelihood / dataset-averaged criterion that reduces to a sum of stimulus-wise KL divergences for identifiability analysis.
- Defines an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩) and samples it with MCMC to visualize identifiability landscapes and compare designs.
- Case study 1 (interval timing): finds strong degeneracies, especially between sensory and motor noise and poorly identifiable loss width; measuring motor noise helps.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters are robustly recoverable, even when separating internal and external noise.
- Builds on known degeneracy literature but contributes a unified parametric observer class and systematic KL-based design comparisons.
- Strength: timely, important problem with clear implications for interpreting Bayesian-perception results.
- Strength: coherent, tractable modeling framework using moment-constrained mixtures, Gaussian prior lattice, and monotone sensory maps.
- Strength: analytic criterion avoids dataset Monte Carlo and maintains experimental interpretability (depends on trials and stimulus distribution).
- Strength: provides practical design insights and transparently articulates parameter degeneracies and levers to resolve them.
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation linking expected-evidence landscapes to finite-sample parameter recovery (MLE/Bayesian fits).
- Weakness: identifiability conclusions may depend on specific parametric choices; limited sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: missing computational details (runtimes, MCMC diagnostics, scaling with parameter dimension) affecting reproducibility.
- Weakness: 2AFC case treated via an “equivalent estimation” mapping, which may not capture discrete-choice identifiability differences.
- Weakness: no principled design optimization (e.g., stimulus distribution or reward selection) demonstrated.
- Quality: theoretical derivation is sound and observer class is thoughtfully engineered, but needs empirical validation and robustness checks.
- Clarity: generally well-written but some notational overload and deferred technical details; key summaries in main text would help.
- Originality: moderately high—standard statistical tools applied novelly within a general observer class for design comparisons.
- Significance: high for Bayesian perception and computational cognitive science; potential to guide experiments if validated and toolified.
- Suggestion: validate proxy via finite-sample fits and report coverage for key parameters.
- Suggestion: perform local Hessian/Fisher analyses to diagnose sloppiness and principal degeneracy directions.
- Suggestion: run sensitivity analyses over prior basis size, mixture count, sensory map family, and model mismatch.
- Suggestion: provide computational transparency (runtimes, MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics) and release code.
- Suggestion: demonstrate principled design optimization for stimuli or loss to maximize identifiability.
- Suggestion: include a proper 2AFC discrete-choice variant or ablation to validate the estimation surrogate.
- Minor: clarify mapping between qmeas(x|t) and pmeas(x|s), discuss lapse identifiability, fix typos and supplement references.
- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on stronger validation and computational detail.
- Reviewer confidence: high in methodology/statistics; medium in domain-specific experimental nuances.
