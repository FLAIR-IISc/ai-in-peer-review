This paper addresses the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing area of research aiming to link behavioral data to underlying cognitive mechanisms. The authors provide a framework for *a priori* analysis of identifiability, allowing researchers to assess whether experimental designs can reliably recover model parameters. They introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model and a method for exploring the identifiability landscape, demonstrating its application to time and speed perception tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the problem of degeneracy in Bayesian models and the need for systematic identifiability analysis. The model presented is a substantial contribution, unifying several existing approaches into a single parametric framework that balances flexibility and computational efficiency. The method for mapping identifiability, based on KL divergence and MCMC sampling, appears sound and provides a practical tool for researchers. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used to evaluate and improve experimental design. The theoretical validation through reconstruction of known priors in the speed perception task further strengthens the paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-uniqueness of solutions when inverting behavioral data.
* **Comprehensive Model:** The proposed observer model is impressively comprehensive, covering a wide range of tasks and incorporating key elements like sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages.
* **Practical Framework:** The developed framework offers a concrete methodology for *a priori* identifiability analysis, providing actionable insights for experimental design.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with helpful figures and equations. The mathematical derivations appear correct and are referenced to supplementary material.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating the framework’s applicability with established phenomena in time and speed perception adds credibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors emphasize computational efficiency, exploring the identifiability landscape with MCMC can still be computationally demanding, particularly for complex models and large parameter spaces. This limitation could hinder broader adoption.
* **Model Assumptions:** The framework relies on specific assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific functional forms for priors and loss functions). While these are reasonable starting points, they may not hold for all perceptual tasks or observers.  The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored.
* **Limited Scope of Results:** The impact of varying specific experimental parameters (e.g., number of trials, stimulus distribution) is demonstrated, but a more systematic exploration of the design space could be beneficial. The conclusion that a narrower loss function is detrimental is surprising and requires further justification.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Techniques:** The paper mentions existing techniques for addressing model non-identifiability (e.g., [23]) but does not provide a detailed comparison of their strengths and weaknesses relative to the proposed framework.
* **Interpretation of KL Divergence:** The use of KL divergence to assess identifiability relies on the assumption that the reference model (θ*) is “true”. However, the "truth" is often unknown, and a more robust measure of model similarity might be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant methodological challenge in the field of Bayesian modeling of perception.
* The proposed framework and observer model are novel and represent a substantial contribution.
* The application to concrete examples demonstrates the practical utility of the framework.
* The paper is well-written, technically sound, and clearly presents its findings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost of the method may limit its practical applicability.
* The results are sensitive to model assumptions, and the framework may not be suitable for all perceptual tasks.
* The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to existing techniques for addressing model non-identifiability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The framework presented offers a practical and theoretically sound approach to addressing the important issue of identifiability. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, such as further discussion of computational limitations and a comparison to other identifiability analysis methods. Specifically, discussing the computational complexity more explicitly, and adding a section that contrasts this approach with other model comparison techniques, would strengthen the paper considerably.
