This paper addresses the fundamental and often-overlooked problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The core issue is that behaviorally equivalent outcomes can arise from different underlying combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, making the "inversion" of behavioral data to recover these internal representations an ill-posed problem. The authors propose a novel and ambitious computational framework to systematically analyze the identifiability of a given Bayesian observer model *a priori*, before an experiment is run. The framework is composed of two key elements: first, a flexible and computationally tractable class of observer models capable of representing a wide range of internal representations (using mixture models for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions); and second, a principled method for exploring the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood, which is shown to be proportional to the KL divergence between the response distributions of a hypothesized "true" observer and a candidate observer. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two compelling case studies from the perception literature: temporal interval estimation and visual speed perception.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and conceptual boldness. The problem of model non-identifiability is a specter that haunts much of the work in computational cognitive science, yet it is often addressed with ad-hoc assumptions rather than systematic analysis. This paper tackles the problem head-on, proposing not just a solution for a specific model but a general-purpose meta-scientific tool. This represents a significant conceptual leap, encouraging a move from purely post-hoc model fitting to a more rigorous practice of a priori experimental design analysis. This is precisely the kind of work that can shift the direction of a field.

The framework itself is impressively general. The authors' choice to model priors, likelihoods, and loss functions with flexible mixture models allows the framework to escape the overly restrictive assumptions (e.g., Gaussianity, quadratic loss) that are common in the literature. This generality is crucial for the framework's credibility and potential for broad application.

The case studies effectively demonstrate the power and utility of the proposed approach. They are not merely toy examples but are grounded in well-known experimental paradigms and scientific questions. The results are insightful and, importantly, often non-obvious. For example, the finding that a task design encouraging a MAP-like strategy could paradoxically *worsen* the ability to recover the observer's prior is a powerful illustration of how the framework can reveal counter-intuitive trade-offs in experimental design. Similarly, the theoretical validation of the robustness of inferences in the speed perception domain adds significant confidence to prior empirical work.

**Weaknesses:**

While the approach is highly innovative, it is not without limitations. The entire analysis is conditional on a pre-specified "reference observer," which stands in for the true, unknown cognitive process. The conclusions about a given experimental design are therefore only as good as the assumptions about this reference model. While this is an inherent feature of any a priori analysis, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the robustness of its findings to variations in this reference observer.

The observer model, while flexible, still relies on specific parametric forms (e.g., the two-component Gaussian mixtures, the specific sensory mapping function). It is possible that these choices could introduce their own biases into the identifiability analysis.

Finally, the work is purely theoretical, providing an analysis of idealized experiments rather than applying the framework to real-world data. While this is perfectly aligned with the goal of a priori analysis, the paper's impact could be strengthened by even a small-scale demonstration on real data or a more extensive set of model and parameter recovery simulations to bolster confidence in the method's practical application.

**Recommendation:**

Despite these minor limitations, this paper is an exceptional contribution. The weaknesses are largely unavoidable consequences of tackling such an ambitious and foundational problem. They are points for future development rather than fatal flaws. The paper's core idea is bold, highly novel, and of great significance. It provides a principled, computational approach to a problem that is critical to the validity of a huge body of research in cognitive science and neuroscience. This is not an incremental advance but a new direction. It is exactly the type of thought-provoking, high-impact work that NIPS should champion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, foundational problem of non-identifiability in a principled and novel way.
*   Proposes a general and powerful framework for a priori analysis of experimental designs, a significant methodological advance.
*   Provides non-obvious, actionable insights that could directly improve research practices in the field.
*   The work is conceptually bold and has the potential for very high impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on a posited "ground truth" reference observer, and robustness to this choice is not fully explored.
*   The work is purely theoretical and lacks application to real experimental data (though this is consistent with its stated goal).

I strongly recommend acceptance.
