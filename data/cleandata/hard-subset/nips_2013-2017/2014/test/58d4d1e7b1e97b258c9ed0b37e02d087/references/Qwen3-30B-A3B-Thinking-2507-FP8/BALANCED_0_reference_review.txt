

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability problem, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors propose a systematic framework for analyzing identifiability a priori, which is particularly valuable given that many studies in the field assume faithful recovery of internal representations from behavioral data without addressing this critical issue.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it develops a general class of Bayesian observer models that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks, with efficient computational properties. Second, it introduces a method to explore the identifiability landscape of these models using KL divergence and MCMC sampling. The authors demonstrate their framework through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception, showing how it can inform experimental design and help identify critical parameters affecting identifiability.

**Strengths:**
1. **Significance and Problem Identification:** The paper correctly identifies a serious methodological issue in the field that has been largely overlooked. The non-identifiability problem undermines claims about recovering internal representations from behavioral data, making this a timely contribution. The authors effectively connect this to broader issues in cognitive science, such as the interpretation of Bayesian models as process models rather than just benchmarks.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical framework is well-developed, with clear derivations (particularly Eq. 12) and appropriate use of KL divergence to measure model distinguishability. The model class they propose is both general and computationally tractable, which is crucial for exploring the complex model space.

3. **Practical Utility:** The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can inform experimental design. For instance, the temporal interval estimation study reveals counterintuitive findings (e.g., that a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery), demonstrating the framework's potential to guide empirical work.

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making complex concepts accessible. The figures effectively illustrate key results, and the supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the paper presents a theoretically sound framework, it lacks empirical validation showing how the framework would improve real-world experiments. The case studies are simulations based on assumed reference observers rather than testing the framework on actual behavioral data.

2. **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** Only two case studies are presented, which may not fully demonstrate the framework's versatility. Additional examples across different perceptual domains would strengthen the argument for the framework's broad applicability.

3. **Sensitivity to Assumptions:** The results in both case studies depend on the assumed reference observer (θ*). The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the identifiability conclusions are to these assumptions, which is crucial for real-world application.

4. **Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient guidance on how to implement the framework in practice. While the supplementary material helps, a more detailed discussion of computational requirements and potential pitfalls would be valuable for potential users.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic approach to address a critical methodological issue in Bayesian models of perception. The framework is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and has clear potential to improve experimental design in the field. However, the purely theoretical nature of the analysis and limited empirical validation represent significant limitations.

The paper is technically strong, well-written, and addresses an important problem, but the lack of empirical validation reduces its impact. I believe the paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could demonstrate the framework's utility with actual behavioral data, showing how it would change experimental design or interpretation of results.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper is strong in its core contribution but would benefit from:
1. Adding a brief empirical validation section showing the framework applied to a small set of real behavioral data
2. Expanding the sensitivity analysis of the reference observer assumptions
3. Providing more concrete guidance on implementing the framework in practice

The paper represents a meaningful step toward more rigorous application of Bayesian models in perception research, and its contribution is well-aligned with NIPS's focus on methodological advances in computational approaches to perception and cognition.
