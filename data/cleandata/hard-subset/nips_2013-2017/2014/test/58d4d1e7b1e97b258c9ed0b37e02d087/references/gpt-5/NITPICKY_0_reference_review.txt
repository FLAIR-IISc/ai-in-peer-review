Summary
The paper tackles a critical and under-discussed issue in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions when inferring internal representations from behavioral data. It proposes (i) a flexible 1D observer model class that parameterizes sensation (stimulus-to-measurement mapping with stimulus-dependent noise), prior (mixture of Gaussians on a lattice), decision (loss as negative mixture of Gaussians), lapses, and response noise; and (ii) an a priori identifiability metric based on the expected log-likelihood of datasets generated by a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli. The authors define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(〈log Pr(D|θ)〉) and sample it by MCMC to visualize identifiability. Two case studies (interval timing and speed perception) illustrate how the framework can rank experimental designs and diagnose parameter confounds.

Strengths
- Important problem: identifiability of Bayesian observer components is central to the field and often glossed over.
- Sound use of expected log-likelihood and its reduction to KL sums (Eq. 12); this is a principled route grounded in information theory and model comparison.
- Unifying treatment of several factors (noise, priors, loss, lapses, motor noise) and cross-condition parameter sharing; can be useful for design comparisons.
- Case studies yield actionable design insights (e.g., motor-noise calibration helps; narrow loss may worsen prior recovery under the proposed model).
- Potentially impactful for planning psychophysics experiments and diagnosing degeneracies before data collection.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Over-parameterization and ad hoc parameterizations:
  - Measurement and loss modeled as “maximum-entropy” mixtures of two Gaussians with matched first four moments. The “max-entropy under moment and unimodality constraints” is not standard and is not derived; max-entropy with higher moments generally yields exponential-of-polynomials and can be ill-posed. The mixture choice appears heuristic.
  - Loss as a negative mixture of Gaussians is unusual. Losses should be nonnegative; flipping sign and speaking of “mode at zero” is unconventional and can lead to confusion. The implications of arbitrary scaling/shifting of loss (which leaves argmin invariant) are not handled cleanly.
  - Prior as a dense Gaussian lattice with M weights introduces many free parameters (M=15) without clear priors/constraints in inference; normalization/positivity handling is unspecified.
- The “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) is not a posterior and may be misleadingly named. It lacks a principled prior on θ and ignores the entropy term; while useful as a scoring function, it is not a posterior distribution. The dependence on Ntr scales the surface arbitrarily.
- The sensation mapping f(s):
  - A and B are chosen to map the experimental stimulus set to [−1,1], so they depend on the chosen design. This makes cross-design comparisons confounded by reparameterization of the internal space, and could entangle identifiability conclusions with choice of stimulus set.
  - s0 and d are simultaneously inferred while A and B are re-fitted to the stimulus set, complicating interpretability and possibly inducing degeneracies.
- Decision stage: While the expected loss reduces to Gaussian-mixture integrals, t*(x) still requires nontrivial optimization; no details on numerical robustness, initialization, or differentiability are provided. Claims of efficiency cite previous work but do not quantify complexity in this setting (with many mixture components).
- Lapse modeling: drawing lapses from the prior rather than a broad response distribution can substantially affect identifiability. No sensitivity analysis is provided, and this choice is nonstandard in parts of the literature.
- Case studies are purely simulated with a single “reference observer” per task. Conclusions are strongly model-dependent (e.g., the detrimental effect of narrow loss). No model misspecification analyses (beyond the “UNC” variant) or robustness checks to alternative loss/noise families are shown in the main text.
- The translation from 2AFC to estimation in the speed task is only mentioned; details matter for identifiability but are relegated to Supplement. This translation is nontrivial and can alter the mapping from internal to observed variables.
- Reproducibility: no code, algorithmic details (MCMC kernels, constraints for mixture weights, parameter priors, diagnostics), or computational budgets are provided.
- Clarity/formatting:
  - Numerous missing bibliographic details (years, volumes) and typos. For instance, “w1” appears where ρ1 is intended (Fig. 2c text); “considerable amount of reporting noise, with ρ0 = 0.3 deg/s, ρ1 = 0.21” is inconsistent with previous notation; footnote marker “1” appears mid-paragraph; several figure/table references (“Figure 1 a & b”, “Figure 2”, “Figure 3”) are present but figures are absent here.
  - Several assumptions buried in text (e.g., prior range “50% wider than true stimulus range”) can substantially affect inference but are not justified.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Provide a rigorous derivation/justification for the “maximum-entropy mixture” constructions for measurement and loss. If heuristic, state so and provide empirical validation that results are not sensitive to these choices.
- Rename E(θ|θ*) to avoid confusion with a Bayesian posterior; clarify its role as an expected evidence surface. Consider alternative identifiability diagnostics (Fisher Information, local curvature of the KL surface, profile likelihoods, or mutual information between data and parameters).
- Explicitly state constraints and parameterizations used in MCMC (e.g., softmax for wm, bounds for σ, γ, κ, σ`, etc.), proposal distributions, convergence diagnostics, and runtimes.
- Analyze sensitivity to lapse model (e.g., uniform over response range vs. prior) and to the response-noise model.
- Justify or remove the rescaling of internal space via A and B per design, or demonstrate that conclusions are invariant to this choice.
- Provide details of the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and verify identifiability conclusions under multiple plausible mappings.
- Include ablations demonstrating that the main qualitative findings (e.g., motor-noise calibration helps; narrow loss hurts) hold across alternative parametric families (single-Gaussian likelihood/loss, different prior bases).
- Consider simpler, more interpretable parameterizations when possible (e.g., parametric priors for timing, single-Gaussian likelihoods with Weber scaling) to reduce over-parameterization.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a reasonably general modeling framework.
- Clear information-theoretic basis for the identifiability metric (Eq. 12).
- Design-oriented insights are valuable for the community.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on ad hoc parameterizations (esp. “max-entropy” mixtures) with insufficient justification.
- Over-parameterization and lack of reproducible methodological detail.
- Potential confounds in mapping/normalization that may affect identifiability conclusions.
- Clarity/formatting issues and missing references/figures undermine evaluation.

Recommendation
Reject. The topic and high-level approach are promising, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological and presentation issues. With stronger theoretical grounding of the mixture constructions, clearer and reproducible methodology, robustness analyses, and improved clarity/formatting, this work could be impactful.
