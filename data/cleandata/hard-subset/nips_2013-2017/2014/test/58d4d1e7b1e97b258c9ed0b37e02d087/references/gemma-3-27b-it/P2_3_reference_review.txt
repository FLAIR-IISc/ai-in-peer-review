## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical, yet often overlooked, issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical observed behavior, leading to ambiguity in interpreting recovered internal representations. They propose a framework for *a priori* analysis of identifiability, allowing researchers to evaluate experimental designs before data collection and potentially improve their ability to reliably estimate parameters of interest. The framework involves a novel class of observer models, a method for exploring the identifiability landscape, and applications to time and speed perception.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of Bayesian modeling in perception – the inherent degeneracy of solutions. Recognizing and addressing this issue is crucial for the validity of inferences about underlying cognitive processes.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a systematic way to analyze identifiability, moving beyond ad-hoc constraints and offering a more principled approach to experimental design.
* **Comprehensive Model:** The Bayesian observer model presented is flexible and allows for a wide range of representations, balancing computational efficiency with expressive power. The use of analytical expressions wherever possible is a significant advantage.
* **Illustrative Applications:** The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used, and demonstrates interesting insights (e.g., the detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function in the time perception task).
* **Clear Motivation & Presentation:** The paper is well-motivated and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the results.  The mathematical notation is generally clear and the organization is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors emphasize computational efficiency, exploring the identifiability landscape via MCMC remains computationally intensive, particularly for higher-dimensional parameter spaces.  The scalability of the method to more complex models and tasks could be a limitation.
* **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The framework relies on several simplifying assumptions, such as unimodal likelihoods, specific parametric forms for priors and loss functions, and independent noise in the response stage. While these simplifications are necessary for tractability, they limit the generality of the framework. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions isn’t fully explored.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The analysis is largely theoretical, based on simulations using an idealized observer model. It would be strengthened by demonstrating the framework’s utility in analyzing *real* psychophysical datasets and comparing the results to existing inference methods.
* **KL-Divergence as a Metric:** Using KL-divergence to quantify “support” has limitations. It’s asymmetrical and can be sensitive to small differences in distributions, potentially leading to overly pessimistic conclusions about identifiability.
* **The ‘True’ Observer:** The framework relies on the concept of a ‘true’ observer (θ*) against which other models are compared. In reality, the true underlying mechanism is unknown, and this choice can influence the results.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and demonstrates a thorough understanding of Bayesian modeling and identifiability issues. The analysis is well-reasoned and the claims are generally supported. While the framework has limitations, it represents a significant step forward in addressing a challenging problem.  (Score: 4/5)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, some sections (particularly the mathematical derivations) could benefit from further clarification. (Score: 4/5)
* **Originality:** The approach of systematically analyzing identifiability *a priori* is novel and distinguishes this work from previous efforts. While the individual components of the framework (Bayesian models, MCMC) are not new, their combination is innovative. (Score: 4/5)
* **Significance:**  This paper has the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. By providing a tool to assess identifiability, it can help researchers design more rigorous experiments and draw more reliable conclusions about underlying cognitive mechanisms. (Score: 4/5)

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical, often-overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Proposes a novel and systematic framework for analyzing identifiability.
* Demonstrates the framework's utility through illustrative applications.
* Is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on simplifying assumptions that limit the generality of the framework.
* Lacks empirical validation with real psychophysical datasets.
* Computational complexity could be a barrier for some researchers.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This is a high-quality paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. While the framework has limitations, the potential benefits for improving experimental design and strengthening inferences in Bayesian modeling of perception are significant. I would suggest that the authors address the reviewers’ concerns regarding the computational complexity, the sensitivity to assumptions, and the need for empirical validation in a revised version of the paper. Addressing these points would further strengthen the contribution and increase its impact.
