- Paper identifies non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavior and proposes assessing identifiability a priori.  
- Proposes expected log likelihood (Eq. 12) as a rigorous proxy for identifiability, grounded in model comparison and information theory.  
- Implements a flexible four-stage Bayesian observer model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response).  
- Demonstrates framework on two case studies (temporal interval estimation and speed perception).  
- Shows framework can guide experimental design by revealing which constraints aid recovery (e.g., known motor parameters help prior recovery; narrow loss assumptions can hurt).  
- Strength: Clearly highlights a fundamental and underappreciated problem in Bayesian perception models.  
- Strength: Theoretical derivation is mathematically sound and well-founded.  
- Strength: Offers practical, actionable guidance for experimental design.  
- Strength: Case studies are well-chosen and align with empirical observations.  
- Strength: Balances theory and implementation at an appropriate technical depth.  
- Weakness: Observer model is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks and lacks discussion of extensions to multidimensional or cue-integration scenarios.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to existing identifiability methods in psychology (e.g., [23]), weakening claims of novelty.  
- Weakness: Does not address computational burden of MCMC sampling for larger models or designs.  
- Weakness: Results depend heavily on reference model assumptions (θ*) with no systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Lacks empirical validation using actual human data to confirm theoretical predictions.  
- Overall: Strong, technically sound contribution with clear implications for experimental design, though scope and some practical issues remain.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Suggestion: Better articulate differences from and improvements over prior identifiability work (e.g., [23]).  
- Suggestion: Discuss computational complexity and strategies for scaling to larger model spaces.  
- Suggestion: Outline potential extensions to multidimensional stimuli and cue integration as future work.  
- Suggestion: Provide sensitivity analyses for assumptions about the reference model (θ*) in supplementary material.
