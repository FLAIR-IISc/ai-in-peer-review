This paper presents a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue given the increasing use of these models to infer internal representations from behavior. The authors introduce a novel, flexible observer model and a method for exploring identifiability a priori, aiming to improve experimental design. While the motivation is strong and the framework conceptually sound, the execution and presentation suffer from numerous minor flaws that, cumulatively, detract from the paper’s impact.

The core idea – systematically investigating the degeneracy of solutions in Bayesian models – is valuable. The authors correctly identify the non-uniqueness of mapping behavior to priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. The proposed framework, while complex, offers a potentially useful tool for researchers. However, the presentation is overly dense and difficult to follow. Equations are introduced without sufficient contextual explanation, and the logical flow within sections (particularly Section 2) is often unclear. The extensive use of equations, while demonstrating technical rigor, hinders readability and obscures the key concepts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability in Bayesian models is genuinely crucial and often overlooked.
*   **Novel Framework:** The presented framework, integrating a flexible observer model with a method for identifiability analysis, is a potentially significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The analysis builds on a solid theoretical foundation, referencing relevant literature on Bayesian inference and model identifiability.
*   **Illustrative Examples:** The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is densely written, with an overwhelming number of equations and a lack of intuitive explanations. The connections between steps in the model are often unclear.
*   **Excessive Detail:** The level of detail is often excessive and detracts from the main points. For example, the exhaustive description of the parametric form of the sensory map (Eq. 2) feels unnecessarily detailed for a NIPS paper focused on identifiability.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from several notational inconsistencies (e.g., the use of both *t* and *s* to represent stimulus-related variables).
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the examples are illustrative, they rely on simulated data and existing literature. A more robust validation would involve applying the framework to a new dataset or a more complex psychophysical task.
*   **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The authors mention the need for efficient computation, but do not provide a thorough discussion of the computational cost of their method, which appears substantial given the MCMC sampling.
*   **Minor Mathematical Errors/Omissions:** Several equations lack sufficient explanation, and there are a few instances where the derivation is unclear or potentially flawed (e.g., the derivation of Eq. 12). The statement regarding the constant term in Eq. 12 is also vague.
*   **Figure Quality:** Figure 1 is overly busy and difficult to interpret. The labels are small and the overall layout is cluttered. Figure 2 is better, but could benefit from clearer labeling and more informative captions.
*   **Unjustified Assumptions:** Some assumptions (e.g., the unimodality of the sensory distribution) are stated without sufficient justification.

**Specific Concerns & Typos:**

*   In the introduction, the phrase “even before considering other confounding issues, such as latent states” seems abrupt and could benefit from further elaboration.
*   Equation 2: The meaning of φi(s) in the introduction is not consistent with its use in the paper.
*   Equation 4: The explanation of the mixing weights ‘wm’ is vague.
*   Section 4.1: The description of the experimental setup and the rationale behind the different experimental designs are not fully clear. The discussion of the impact of a narrow loss function seems counterintuitive and needs further justification.
*   Section 4.2: The statement about the robustness of the results is somewhat overstated, given the reported underestimation of certain parameters.
*   Multiple instances of overly verbose sentences and redundant phrasing throughout the text.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The excessive detail and lack of clarity make the paper difficult to understand, even for experts in the field.
*   The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work.
*   The limited empirical validation weakens the claims of the paper.
*   The computational cost of the method is not adequately addressed.

**For:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to a growing body of research.
*   The proposed framework is novel and potentially useful.
*   The theoretical grounding is sound.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a valuable idea, the numerous flaws in clarity, presentation, and execution outweigh the strengths. The paper would benefit significantly from a major revision focusing on simplification, improved explanations, and a more thorough validation of the framework. A more focused presentation of the core concepts, coupled with a clearer discussion of the computational challenges, would substantially improve the paper’s impact. The current manuscript feels more like a technical report than a polished, insightful contribution to NIPS. The attention to detail, while commendable in principle, has unfortunately resulted in a paper that is unnecessarily difficult to navigate and appreciate.
