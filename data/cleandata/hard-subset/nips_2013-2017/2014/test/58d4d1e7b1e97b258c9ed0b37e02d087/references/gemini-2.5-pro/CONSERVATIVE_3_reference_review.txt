**Summary**

This paper addresses the critical problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception, a widely used framework in computational neuroscience and cognitive science. The authors correctly point out that different combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can lead to identical observable behavior, making the "inverse problem" of recovering these representations from data ill-posed. To tackle this, they propose a novel computational framework designed to test model identifiability *a priori*, before data collection. The framework is composed of two main parts: (1) a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models intended to capture a wide range of perceptual estimation tasks, and (2) a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, which is shown to be proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the candidate and a ground-truth "reference" model. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two simulated case studies: an interval timing task and a speed perception task, arguing that their method can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs and validate previous findings.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Model identifiability is a pervasive and often-overlooked issue in the modeling of cognitive processes. The attempt to provide a systematic, quantitative tool for researchers to proactively address this is a laudable and important goal. The motivation is clear and well-grounded in the literature.

2.  **Focus on Experimental Design:** The paper's positioning of the framework as a tool for improving experimental design is a significant strength. The idea of running *in silico* experiments to identify potential parameter degeneracies before committing resources to data collection could be of great practical value to the field.

3.  **Comprehensive Observer Model:** The authors have constructed a detailed and ambitious observer model that aims to incorporate many relevant factors, from sensory encoding (e.g., Weber-Fechner vs. Stevens's law) and non-Gaussian noise to flexible priors and loss functions, lapses, and motor noise. The mathematical effort to maintain computational tractability within such a complex model is noteworthy.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Over-reliance on a Specific, Unvalidated Model Class:** The entire framework is built upon a specific set of parametric assumptions for the prior, likelihood, and loss function (all modeled as mixtures of Gaussians). While flexible, this is a strong constraint. The conclusions drawn from the framework are therefore conditional on the true observer falling within this specific functional class. The paper provides no evidence that this is the case, nor does it explore the framework's robustness to violations of these assumptions. For instance, the conclusion that a narrow (MAP-like) loss function worsens prior recovery in the timing task (Fig 2b) might be an artifact of interactions within their chosen parameterization, rather than a general principle. The work would be much stronger if it could demonstrate that its conclusions are not overly sensitive to these specific functional forms.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper dismisses generic techniques for identifiability analysis as "intractable" without sufficient justification. The field of system identification has a long history of methods for assessing parameter identifiability, such as analyzing the Fisher Information Matrix or performing posterior uncertainty quantification on simulated datasets. While the authors' model is complex, a comparison against a more standard approach—even on a simplified version of their model—is necessary to validate their proposed KL-divergence-based metric. As it stands, the paper proposes a new and unproven methodology for assessing identifiability without benchmarking it against existing standards.

3.  **Absence of Validation with Real Data:** The analysis is entirely theoretical, relying on simulations from a chosen "reference observer." This is a significant limitation. The framework makes predictions about which parameters should be difficult to recover (e.g., the trade-off between sensory and motor noise in Fig 2c). A crucial validation step would be to fit the model to actual experimental data from a relevant study and show that the posterior distributions for the recovered parameters exhibit the exact degeneracies and uncertainties predicted by the *a priori* analysis. Without this link back to empirical reality, the framework remains a speculative tool whose practical utility is unconfirmed. The conclusions are about an idealized observer, not necessarily about a real human subject.

4.  **Limited Novelty from a Machine Learning Perspective:** While the problem is important for cognitive science, the core technical contribution from a machine learning standpoint appears limited. The method essentially uses the KL divergence between output distributions as a measure of model distinguishability, which is a well-established concept. The main novelty lies in the construction of the specific, complex observer model for psychophysics. Therefore, the paper may be better suited for a specialized computational neuroscience or psychology venue rather than a general machine learning conference like NIPS.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents an interesting and ambitious framework for addressing the important problem of model non-identifiability in a specific domain of cognitive modeling. The idea of providing a tool for *a priori* analysis of experimental designs is compelling.

However, the work in its current form is premature. It introduces a new, highly-parameterized modeling approach and a new method for analyzing it, but validates neither against established methods nor against real-world data. The conclusions are entirely contingent on strong parametric assumptions that may not hold in practice. Before this framework can be endorsed as a reliable tool for the community, it requires much more rigorous testing. Specifically, the authors would need to (1) demonstrate the robustness of their findings to different modeling assumptions, (2) benchmark their identifiability analysis against standard statistical techniques, and (3) validate the predictions of their *a priori* analysis with fits to real experimental data.

Without this further evidence, the paper reads as a position paper presenting a promising but ultimately unproven idea. While the direction is valuable, the lack of rigorous validation means the work does not yet meet the high standards of quality and completeness expected for NIPS.
