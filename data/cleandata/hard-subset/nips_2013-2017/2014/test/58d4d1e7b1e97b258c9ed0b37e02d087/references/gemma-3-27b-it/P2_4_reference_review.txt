## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical issue in the growing field of Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavioral predictions within the Bayesian framework. They propose a novel framework, based on a flexible class of observer models, to *a priori* investigate identifiability and to improve experimental design by comparing identifiability across different setups. They apply their framework to two established psychophysical tasks – time perception and speed perception – showcasing its potential to identify problematic experimental designs and validate existing inferences.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a fundamental issue:** The problem of non-identifiability is a significant limitation of Bayesian modeling, and this paper provides a rigorous approach to address it.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework offers a systematic and *a priori* method for analyzing identifiability, a significant improvement over purely post-hoc evaluations.
* **Flexible Model Class:** The generalized observer model, parameterized by θ, is a strong point, capable of representing a wide range of perceptual phenomena while maintaining computational efficiency. The use of maximum entropy principles for defining the likelihood and loss functions is a sensible and well-justified approach.
* **Practical Application & Insights:** Applying the framework to time and speed perception tasks provides concrete examples and generates interesting insights, such as the potential detriment of tightly constrained loss functions in the time perception task. The validation of prior work on speed perception is also a positive outcome.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although dense with mathematical detail (see weaknesses). The figures are helpful in illustrating the model and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Density:** The paper is *very* mathematically heavy. While the detailed derivations are available in the supplementary material, the main text could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts, especially for readers less familiar with the underlying statistical machinery. This density could limit accessibility.
* **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The framework relies on several assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, specific functional forms for priors and loss functions).  The robustness of the conclusions to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly explored. While the authors acknowledge these, a deeper discussion about their potential impact would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the authors emphasize computational efficiency, exploring the full parameter space for identifiability is still likely to be computationally expensive, potentially limiting the applicability of the framework to more complex models. The MCMC sampling step is not described in great detail.
* **Limited Scope of Results:** The case studies, while illustrative, are somewhat limited.  Expanding the applications to a wider range of perceptual tasks would strengthen the generalizability of the framework.  The speed perception study largely *validates* existing work, which is positive, but doesn’t dramatically demonstrate the framework’s ability to uncover unexpected problems.
* **Connection to Neural Implementation:** While the motivation stems from linking behavioral data to neural representations, the paper remains at a purely behavioral level. Discussing potential implications for neural modeling (e.g., what neural constraints could help resolve identifiability issues) would be valuable.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the derivations appear rigorous. The analysis is complete within the scope defined by the authors. They are generally careful in their evaluations, acknowledging limitations and assumptions.  **Score: 8/10**

**Clarity:** The paper suffers somewhat from mathematical density. While logically structured, the heavy reliance on equations and notation can be challenging for a broad audience. **Score: 6/10**

**Originality:** The core idea of systematically investigating identifiability *a priori* is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The flexible observer model also contributes to the originality. **Score: 9/10**

**Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. By providing a tool to assess identifiability, it can help researchers design better experiments and draw more reliable conclusions from their data.  **Score: 8/10**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental, and increasingly problematic, issue in Bayesian modeling.
* Presents a novel and well-developed framework for analyzing identifiability.
* Demonstrates practical application with interesting insights into experimental design.
* Potentially significant impact on the field by promoting more rigorous model fitting and interpretation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* High mathematical density may limit accessibility.
*  Assumptions and simplifications could affect the generalizability of the results.
* Limited scope of the case studies.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the mathematical complexity and the limited scope of the examples are weaknesses, the novelty and significance of the proposed framework outweigh these concerns. The authors have identified a critical issue and developed a promising approach to address it. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the clarity issues (e.g., providing more intuitive explanations of the math) and expanding the discussion of the limitations and assumptions of their framework.  Adding a brief discussion of potential implications for neural modeling would also be a valuable addition.
