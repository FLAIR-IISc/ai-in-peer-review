

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a fundamental yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability problem. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral data, making it impossible to reliably recover internal representations from experimental data without careful experimental design. They propose a systematic framework for testing identifiability a priori, which allows researchers to evaluate whether their experimental design can recover the parameters of interest before conducting experiments.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a general framework for analyzing identifiability in Bayesian observer models, addressing a gap in the literature that has been noted but not systematically solved (citing [19]). Second, they develop a flexible yet computationally efficient observer model class that can represent a wide range of sensory estimation tasks. Third, they introduce a novel method to compute expected support for different models using KL divergence, which enables efficient exploration of the identifiability landscape. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their framework through two compelling case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception.

The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty. While the non-identifiability problem has been recognized in the literature (e.g., [19]), this is the first systematic framework to address it. The authors' approach is bold in its ambition to provide a general solution to a problem that has undermined the validity of many Bayesian models in perception. Their framework shifts the focus from post-hoc analysis to a priori experimental design, which has significant practical implications for the field.

The paper excels in its theoretical development. The derivation of the expected log likelihood (Eq. 12) is elegant and provides a clear, computationally tractable measure of identifiability. The case studies are well-chosen and demonstrate the framework's utility in revealing counterintuitive insights (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function can actually worsen prior recovery in temporal interval estimation). The authors also provide valuable guidance on experimental design that goes beyond the typical "more trials = better" heuristic.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to validation. The technical details are presented with sufficient rigor for replication, and the supplementary material provides additional depth for interested readers. The authors are appropriately cautious about the scope of their framework, acknowledging that it applies to a specific class of Bayesian models and suggesting natural extensions.

While the paper is strong, there are minor limitations. The framework assumes a specific parametric form for the observer model, which might not capture all possible perceptual mechanisms. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and note that their framework could be extended to other model classes. The case studies are limited to two specific tasks, though the framework's generality suggests it would apply to many other psychophysical paradigms.

This paper is highly significant for the field of computational neuroscience and cognitive science. By providing a systematic way to test identifiability a priori, it addresses a critical methodological issue that has plagued Bayesian models of perception for years. The framework could prevent many of the questionable conclusions drawn from Bayesian models due to non-identifiability. As the authors note, their work has the potential to improve experimental design and interpretation across a wide range of perception studies.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' interest in novel theoretical frameworks that have practical implications. It represents a significant conceptual advance rather than an incremental improvement, which fits with the conference's preference for bold, novel approaches. While the paper is technically sophisticated, the authors have made it accessible to a broad audience of computational neuroscientists and cognitive scientists.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel framework that addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian models of perception. The conceptual contribution is significant, the theoretical development is sound, and the practical implications are clear. The framework is generalizable to many experimental paradigms and has the potential to improve experimental design across the field.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**:
- Addresses a fundamental problem (non-identifiability) that has been recognized but not systematically solved
- Provides a novel, general framework for testing identifiability a priori
- Theoretical development is elegant and rigorous
- Demonstrated utility through two compelling case studies
- Offers practical guidance for experimental design
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Weaknesses**:
- Framework is limited to a specific class of Bayesian observer models
- Case studies are limited to two specific tasks (though the framework is generalizable)
- Requires a reference observer model for analysis (though this is a common limitation in identifiability analysis)

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by providing a necessary tool for validating Bayesian models of perception. It represents the kind of bold, conceptual advance that NIPS should prioritize, and it has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian models in perception research.
