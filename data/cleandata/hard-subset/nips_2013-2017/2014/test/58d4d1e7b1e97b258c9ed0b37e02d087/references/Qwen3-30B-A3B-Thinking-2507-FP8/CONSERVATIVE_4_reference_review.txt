

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a well-known challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to degeneracies in the inverse problem. The authors propose a framework to systematically investigate identifiability a priori for Bayesian observer models, with applications to temporal and speed perception tasks.

The paper builds on established work in Bayesian perception (e.g., [1,2,3,4,5,6,7,9,12,14,24]) and makes a case for systematic identifiability analysis before experiments. The core contribution is a computational framework using KL divergence to quantify how well parameters can be distinguished given a specific experimental design. The authors develop a flexible Bayesian observer model class that can approximate many existing perceptual models, and demonstrate its use in two case studies.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper clearly identifies a genuine methodological challenge in the field - the non-identifiability of Bayesian model components.
2. The framework is well-structured and provides a systematic approach to experimental design.
3. The case studies (temporal context and speed perception) provide concrete examples of how the framework can inform experimental design decisions.
4. The mathematical derivations are generally sound and the implementation details are well-specified.
5. The paper acknowledges limitations and provides a clear path for future work.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of novelty:** The core technique (using KL divergence to assess model distinguishability) is a standard approach in model comparison and identifiability analysis, as evidenced by prior work [23, 28]. The authors fail to clearly distinguish their contribution from existing statistical methods applied to this specific problem. The "novel" framework appears to be a domain-specific application of established statistical techniques rather than a methodological advancement.

2. **Insufficient validation:** The paper demonstrates the framework's utility through two case studies, but these are limited to specific experimental paradigms. The authors do not compare their approach against existing methods for identifiability analysis in psychology or neuroscience. Without rigorous comparison to established techniques, it's unclear whether this framework offers significant advantages over current practice.

3. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims to "fill a gap" in the literature, but previous work has already addressed similar issues. For example, [12] discusses degeneracy in Bayesian models of perception and proposes solutions, while [23] provides a general framework for model distinguishability. The current paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how its approach is superior to these existing methods.

4. **Limited scope:** The framework is restricted to a specific class of Bayesian models (one-dimensional stimulus estimation with Gaussian mixtures), which excludes many important perceptual tasks. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately justify why their approach is sufficiently general for broader applications.

5. **Lack of empirical testing:** The framework is presented as a theoretical tool for experimental design, but the paper doesn't include any empirical validation where the framework was actually used to improve real experimental outcomes. The case studies are simulations based on assumed reference models, not demonstrations of the framework's utility in actual research.

**Critical Assessment:**

As a reviewer who values methodological rigor and established approaches, I find this paper to be an incremental contribution rather than a significant advancement. The core methodological approach is not novel, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that it outperforms existing methods for addressing identifiability issues in Bayesian perception models.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. A more detailed comparison to existing identifiability methods in psychology/neuroscience
2. Empirical validation showing how the framework actually improves experimental design in real research
3. A clearer articulation of what makes their approach distinct from standard statistical techniques

The paper's claim that "there has been no systematic analysis—neither empirical nor theoretical—of their effectiveness" is inaccurate given the references provided (e.g., [23]). The authors have not sufficiently established the novelty of their contribution.

**Recommendation:**
Reject

While the paper identifies a genuine problem in the field and presents a well-organized framework, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or practical significance to warrant publication at NIPS. The approach appears to be an application of standard statistical methods to a specific domain, without demonstrating clear advantages over existing approaches. The case studies are limited in scope and lack empirical validation. Given the high bar for novelty and significance at NIPS, I believe this paper does not meet the conference's standards for methodological advancement.
