This paper addresses the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing concern as these models become increasingly sophisticated and used to infer internal representations. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in mapping observed behavior to priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. They present a framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis, along with a novel class of observer models designed for efficient exploration of the parameter space. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used to evaluate and improve experimental designs.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of Bayesian modeling – the non-uniqueness of solutions. Addressing this is crucial for the field's progress, as it undermines the interpretability of inferred internal representations.
* **Comprehensive Framework:** The proposed framework appears well-structured and capable of systematically analyzing identifiability across a large class of models. The development of a flexible, parameterized observer model is a significant step.
* **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical treatment of the identifiability problem, leveraging KL-divergence and MCMC sampling for analysis. The derivations are generally clear and well-motivated.
* **Practical Application:** The case studies in time and speed perception demonstrate the practical utility of the framework. The insights gained – e.g., the detrimental effect of a narrowly defined loss function in time perception – are valuable for experimental design.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately situates its contribution within the broader literature on Bayesian modeling, identifiability, and psychophysics, referencing relevant work effectively.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex arguments.  Figure 1 is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors emphasize efficiency, exploring the parameter space, even with their framework, seems computationally intensive. The scalability to more complex models or tasks isn't thoroughly addressed.  A discussion of computational limits, and potential acceleration techniques (beyond MCMC), would be beneficial.
* **Model Assumptions:** The observer model, while flexible, still relies on several assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific functional forms for priors and loss functions). The sensitivity of the identifiability results to these assumptions is not extensively explored. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Simplified Tasks:** The chosen case studies, while illustrative, are relatively simple. It's unclear how well the framework would generalize to more ecologically valid, complex perceptual tasks involving multiple cues, decision stages, or sources of uncertainty.
* **Interpretation of KL-Divergence:** Using the expected KL-divergence as a proxy for identifiability is reasonable, but it’s an indirect measure. It doesn’t directly quantify the precision with which parameters can be recovered.  Exploring alternative identifiability criteria might be fruitful.
* **Limited Exploration of Prior Distributions:** The prior parametrization, while comprehensive, might still not encompass all plausible prior structures. The impact of different prior families on identifiability isn't fully investigated.
* **Lack of Neural Grounding:** Although the motivation centers on linking behavior to neural representations, the framework lacks direct connections to neurophysiological data or plausible neural implementations of the model components.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental and increasingly important issue in Bayesian modeling.
* Presents a novel and potentially impactful framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
* Demonstrates practical utility through concrete case studies.
* Rigorous mathematical treatment and clear presentation.
* Builds upon and extends existing work in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Computational cost and scalability concerns.
* Reliance on simplifying assumptions that may limit generalizability.
* Limited exploration of the framework's performance on more complex tasks.
* Indirect measure of identifiability.
* Lack of direct neural grounding.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. While the computational cost and reliance on assumptions are legitimate concerns, the framework presented is a significant step towards addressing the critical issue of identifiability. The clear mathematical formulation, practical applications, and connection to existing work make this a worthwhile contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  **Discuss computational limitations:** Elaborate on the computational challenges associated with scaling the framework to more complex models and tasks, and mention potential mitigation strategies.
2.  **Conduct a sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the impact of key model assumptions (e.g., noise distributions, prior functional forms) on the identifiability results.
3.  **Acknowledge limitations for complex tasks:** Discuss the potential challenges of applying the framework to more ecologically valid, complex perceptual tasks.
4.  **Consider alternative identifiability metrics:** Explore alternative measures of identifiability beyond the KL-divergence.
5.  **Briefly discuss potential neural implementations:** A short discussion of how the model components might be instantiated in neural circuits would strengthen the connection to the broader neuroscience literature.
