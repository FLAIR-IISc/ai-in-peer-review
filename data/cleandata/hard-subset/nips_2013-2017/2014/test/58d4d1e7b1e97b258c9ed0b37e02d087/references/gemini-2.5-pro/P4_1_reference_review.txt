### Summary

This paper introduces a computational framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors highlight a well-known but critical issue in this field: multiple combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss function can produce identical observable behavior, a degeneracy that undermines the goal of recovering an observer's internal representations from data. To address this, the paper proposes two main contributions. First, it develops a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians to approximate arbitrary priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, and includes a parametric mapping from stimulus space to an internal measurement space. This construction allows for fast, often analytical, computation of the observer's predicted behavior. Second, the paper proposes a method to assess model identifiability by sampling from an "approximate expected posterior density." This density is defined in terms of the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, averaged over all possible datasets generated by a known "reference" observer model. This quantity can be efficiently computed via the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the reference and candidate models.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies from the literature. In a time interval estimation task, they show that different experimental designs have a significant and sometimes non-intuitive impact on the ability to recover the observer's prior. For instance, they find that having observers perform under two different stimulus distributions is less effective at removing parameter degeneracies than independently measuring motor noise. In a second case study on speed perception, they re-examine the classic finding of a "slow-speed prior" and find that, within their framework, both the prior and the sensory noise characteristics can be recovered with high fidelity, providing theoretical support for the original findings.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed approach to a priori identifiability testing is principled and sound. The formulation of the expected log-likelihood in terms of KL divergence (Eq. 12) is a correct and computationally efficient way to bypass the need for expensive data simulation. The construction of the generic observer model is also a strong point. By using mixtures of Gaussians and a flexible sensory mapping function, the model can approximate a wide range of observer characteristics found in the literature while retaining computational tractability. The analytical derivation of the expected loss as a mixture of Gaussians is a key feature enabling the efficient exploration of the parameter space.

The claims are well-supported by the results of the two case studies. The analyses provide concrete examples of how the framework can be used to compare experimental designs and probe for potential parameter degeneracies. The authors are careful to state their assumptions about the "reference observer" in each case and to discuss the sources of the observed non-identifiability (e.g., the trade-off between sensory and motor noise in the timing task).

### Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, explaining the non-identifiability problem with a concise mathematical argument. The four stages of the observer model (Sensation, Perception, Decision-making, Response) are presented logically, and Figure 1 provides a helpful schematic. The method for mapping identifiability is also explained well, though the transition from the expected log-likelihood (Eq. 12) to the "approximate expected posterior density" (Eq. 13) could be slightly more explicit about its heuristic nature (i.e., it's a proxy for a true posterior, not a formally derived one). The case studies are presented clearly and the figures effectively visualize the results of the identifiability analysis. The work is sufficiently detailed to allow an expert to understand the core methods.

### Originality and Significance

The paper's primary contribution is a novel and systematic framework for addressing a fundamental problem in computational cognitive science. While the problem of non-identifiability is not new (as acknowledged by the authors, citing [17-19]), and general methods for model comparison exist (e.g., [23]), this paper provides the first tool, to my knowledge, that is specifically tailored to the structure of Bayesian observer models and is designed for *a priori* experimental design. This is a significant advance. Researchers often rely on intuition or post-hoc analyses to deal with identifiability; this framework allows them to quantitatively assess whether a planned experiment is even capable of isolating the model components of interest before collecting data. This could save considerable resources and lead to more robust and reliable scientific conclusions.

The work is clearly differentiated from previous contributions. It builds upon prior modeling work (e.g., [12, 14]) but generalizes it into a broader framework and applies it to the novel problem of a priori design evaluation. The significance is high, as the ideas and methods are likely to be used by other researchers in the field of computational modeling of perception and decision-making.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for:**

1.  **High Significance:** The paper tackles a critical and pervasive problem in the field of Bayesian cognitive modeling. The proposed framework is a practical and powerful tool for improving experimental design.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-founded, combining a flexible parametric model with a principled, efficient technique for assessing identifiability.
3.  **Novelty:** It provides a novel, systematic approach for *a priori* analysis of model identifiability, moving beyond post-hoc critiques of the Bayesian modeling enterprise.
4.  **Clarity and Impact:** The paper is well-written, and the case studies compellingly demonstrate the utility of the framework, yielding non-trivial insights that could guide future research.

**Arguments against:**

1.  **Limited Scope:** The current framework is restricted to one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a substantial portion of psychophysics research, it excludes common paradigms like discrete-choice (e.g., 2AFC) and multi-dimensional tasks, which the authors acknowledge. The conversion of the 2AFC speed perception study into an estimation task is a simplification.
2.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The conclusions are conditional on the choice of the "reference observer" θ*. The paper does not explore the robustness of its findings to different assumptions about this reference model, which could be a limitation.
3.  **Model Complexity:** The proposed observer model is highly parameterized, which might raise concerns about its own identifiability and ease of use for other researchers. Some choices (e.g., M=15 basis functions for the prior) are not fully justified.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, sound, and highly significant contribution to the field of computational modeling. The limitations in scope are acknowledged and represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current framework.
