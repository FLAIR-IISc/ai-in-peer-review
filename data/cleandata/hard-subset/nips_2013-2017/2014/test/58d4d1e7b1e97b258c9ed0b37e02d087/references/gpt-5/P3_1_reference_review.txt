Summary
The paper tackles a central and underappreciated problem in Bayesian modeling of perception: a priori practical identifiability of model components (prior, likelihood, loss) when fitting observer models to behavioral data. The authors (i) formalize a flexible 1D observer class spanning sensation, prior, decision under a parametric loss, lapses, and motor reporting noise; (ii) derive an efficient way to compute the expected support for an alternative parameter vector θ when data are generated from a reference θ*, showing it reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions; and (iii) explore identifiability by sampling an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(〈log L〉), thereby mapping degeneracies and comparing experimental designs. Two case studies are given: interval timing (recovering priors across conditions) and speed perception (recovering a heavy-tailed slow-speed prior), yielding actionable design insights (e.g., measuring motor noise greatly improves identifiability; encouraging MAP-like loss can harm prior recovery; speed prior parameters are relatively robust).

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-positioned within Bayesian observer modeling (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Maloney & Mamassian; Houlsby et al.; Acerbi et al.). The identifiability problem is connected to classic “model mimicry” concerns (Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and to critiques of unconstrained Bayesian reverse engineering (Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love). The expected KL formulation aligns with long-standing ideas in (Bayesian) model distinguishability and design (e.g., Lindley; MacKay; Chaloner & Verdinelli; Paninski), though this literature is not fully integrated in the discussion. The use of mixtures and moment constraints follows pragmatic psychophysics practice, and the emphasis on a priori design evaluation is timely.

Strengths
- Important problem: Identifiability issues in Bayesian perception are real and consequential. Providing an explicit tool to assess them before data collection is valuable.
- Methodological clarity: The reduction of expected log evidence to a sum of KL divergences across stimuli is elegant and computationally convenient; the “expected posterior” perspective is intuitive and tunable by Ntr.
- Flexible, efficient observer class: The mixture-of-Gaussians parameterizations enable fast computation of posteriors and expected loss, making exploration of high-dimensional θ feasible.
- Practical design insights: The interval timing case shows nontrivial design trade-offs (e.g., narrow loss worsens prior recovery; independent motor-noise calibration helps). The speed case gives theoretical validation of prior inference robustness under moderate misspecification.
- Relevance to NeurIPS: Bridges cognitive modeling, information-theoretic evaluation, and experimental design.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality/technical
- Novelty of the core criterion: Using expected KL (or expected log likelihood) for model distinguishability is standard. The paper’s contribution is primarily tailoring this to a specific observer class and using MCMC to explore identifiability. Please better situate this within Bayesian optimal experimental design (e.g., Lindley 1956; Chaloner & Verdinelli 1995; Paninski 2005; Hennig & Schuler 2012; Ryan et al. 2016) and clarify what is new beyond applying expected KL to this family.
- Maximum-entropy claims: The paper states that the two-Gaussian mixtures “locally maximize entropy” under moment constraints and unimodality. The maximum-entropy distribution with fixed moments is generally in an exponential-polynomial family, not a Gaussian mixture. The construction needs more justification, references, and clarity on what is actually optimized and how unimodality is enforced.
- Model misspecification: Identifiability claims depend on the parametric family. Please report robustness when the true measurement or loss deviate from mixtures (e.g., skewed heavy tails, nonstationary noise, or asymmetric/plateaued losses) and when the prior has structure not well captured by the fixed lattice M=15.
- Gauge symmetry and invariances: The paper highlights the prior–likelihood–loss compensatory degeneracy analytically but does not exploit formal invariance analysis to identify and “fix a gauge.” A local sensitivity/Hessian or profile-likelihood analysis around θ* would complement the global KL results and isolate near-symmetries (e.g., prior width vs. sensory/motor noise).
- 2AFC vs estimation: The speed example converts 2AFC to estimation. This equivalence holds only under specific assumptions (monotonic decision rules, symmetric noise). It would strengthen the paper to include an explicit 2AFC variant within the same framework, as promised in the conclusions, or at least a sensitivity analysis showing that conclusions transfer.

Clarity
- Provide implementation details: MCMC proposal schemes, priors on θ, convergence diagnostics, runtime, and numerical integration specifics for KL computations.
- Notational consistency: There are typos and symbol inconsistencies (e.g., “w1” vs ρ1). Ensure figures referenced in text directly show the degeneracies claimed.
- Scaling of internal space: f(s) is rescaled to [−1,1]; discuss implications for comparability of σ and σℓ across designs/conditions.

Originality
- The observer parameterization and the end-to-end pipeline are useful, but the central expected-KL-based criterion is known. Highlight distinctive aspects (e.g., joint treatment of prior/likelihood/loss/lapses/motor noise in one tractable class; linking design levers to identifiability in specific tasks).

Significance
- The design insights (e.g., independent motor calibration; costs of MAP-like feedback) are actionable and likely to influence future experiments. Making code and templates public would increase impact.

Additional concrete suggestions
- Incorporate formal design optimization (choose stimuli, conditions, feedback) by maximizing expected KL or mutual information under constraints.
- Report parameter-wise identifiability metrics (e.g., local curvature or profile likelihoods) alongside global posterior samples.
- Examine confounds between lapse rate and heavy-tailed priors.
- Explore multi-condition “transfer” constraints (shared likelihood or loss) more systematically.
- Consider hierarchical extensions across observers.

Pros
- Addresses a crucial methodological gap with a practical, computationally grounded framework.
- Provides nontrivial, experiment-relevant insights and validation in two canonical domains.
- Generally clear exposition and sensible assumptions.

Cons
- Core identifiability metric is not novel; missing connections to OED literature.
- Strong reliance on specific parametric choices (mixture forms, maximum-entropy claims) with limited robustness checks.
- 2AFC-to-estimation mapping not fully justified.
- Some clarity and notation issues; heavy reliance on supplement.

Recommendation: Weak accept. With stronger positioning relative to Bayesian experimental design, clearer justification of the distributional choices, and more robustness analyses, this work can be a valuable reference for designing and critiquing Bayesian perceptual experiments.
