

# Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical yet overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability problem. The authors demonstrate that the inverse mapping from observed behavior to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these elements can produce identical behavioral outputs. They propose a systematic framework to analyze identifiability a priori, before conducting experiments, by quantifying the expected support for one model over another when data is generated by a reference model. The framework leverages KL divergence to measure identifiability and uses MCMC sampling to explore the parameter space. The authors apply their method to two well-known perceptual tasks (temporal interval estimation and speed perception), demonstrating how the framework can inform experimental design choices and identify problematic parameter confounds.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and addresses a fundamental methodological issue in the field. The authors provide rigorous mathematical derivations for their framework, including the key equation (12) that quantifies expected model support using KL divergence. Their observer model is well-justified, building on previous work [9, 12, 14] while extending it to a more general class of perceptual tasks. The case studies are well-designed and demonstrate the framework's utility in practical scenarios. The authors carefully analyze the limitations of their approach, acknowledging that their framework applies to a specific class of models and that identifiability depends on the reference model. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for replication.

However, the paper could have strengthened its theoretical foundation by explicitly comparing their approach to existing identifiability analysis methods [22, 23]. While they mention these works, a more detailed discussion of how their method differs and improves upon them would have added depth.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors systematically build their framework from basic principles, with each section logically following the previous one. The four-stage construction of their observer model (sensation, perceptual, decision-making, response) is particularly well-explained, with clear equations and intuitive descriptions. The case studies are presented with sufficient detail, and the figures effectively illustrate key findings (e.g., Figure 2 showing the impact of different experimental designs on prior recovery). The paper would benefit from a more explicit statement of the framework's limitations upfront, but overall it's highly readable and accessible to experts in the field.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by focusing specifically on the identifiability problem in Bayesian models of perception - a problem that has been largely ignored despite its fundamental importance. While general identifiability analysis exists in statistics [23], the authors adapt and extend these ideas to the specific context of perceptual models, which have unique constraints and parameter spaces. The framework is novel in its application to experimental design in perception research, and the case studies provide concrete evidence of how the framework can guide researchers in practice.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on Bayesian models of perception [1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 9, 12, 24] by addressing a methodological gap that has been acknowledged in the literature [17, 18, 19] but not systematically solved. The authors effectively cite relevant prior work while making it clear how their contribution advances the field.

## Significance

This paper addresses a critical methodological issue that impacts the validity of numerous studies in the field of perceptual modeling. The authors demonstrate that common experimental designs may lead to unreliable recovery of internal representations, which has implications for interpreting many existing studies. Their framework provides a practical tool for researchers to evaluate and improve experimental designs before collecting data, potentially saving significant time and resources. The case studies show how the framework can yield non-intuitive insights (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function may worsen identifiability in interval timing tasks), which could inform future research.

The significance is further enhanced by the authors' clear demonstration of how their framework can be applied to real experimental problems in perception. The paper's approach could be extended to other domains where Bayesian models are used, making it broadly applicable within cognitive science and neuroscience.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a fundamental methodological gap in Bayesian models of perception
- Provides a theoretically sound, computationally efficient framework for a priori identifiability analysis
- Demonstrates practical utility through two well-chosen case studies
- Offers concrete guidance for experimental design that can improve data quality
- Well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- The framework assumes knowledge of a reference model, which may not always be available in practice
- The case studies are limited to two specific tasks; more diverse examples would strengthen the argument
- The paper doesn't fully explore how their framework compares to alternative approaches for handling identifiability

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the methodological toolkit of researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. It addresses a critical problem that has been largely ignored in the literature, and provides a practical framework that can be immediately applied to improve experimental design. While the scope is somewhat limited to specific types of perceptual tasks, the core ideas are broadly applicable and the framework could be extended to other domains.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of considerable importance to the field. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility and yield non-intuitive insights that could inform future research. The authors have carefully considered the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and the paper would be valuable to both experimentalists and modelers in perception research.

I recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. This paper is a solid contribution that will have a meaningful impact on how researchers design experiments and interpret results in Bayesian models of perception. The framework fills an important gap in the literature and provides a valuable tool for the field.
