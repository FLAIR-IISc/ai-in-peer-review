- Addresses the important and often-overlooked problem of parameter non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models.  
- Proposes a theoretical framework to test identifiability a priori by analyzing sensitivity of model predictions to parameter variations.  
- Uses MCMC sampling of the approximate posterior to practically explore identifiability when analytical integrals are intractable.  
- Demonstrates the approach on time and speed perception tasks with illustrative examples.  
- Shows that different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions.  
- Highlights that loss function shape can critically affect parameter recovery.  
- Framework is relatively general for one-dimensional stimulus-estimation psychophysical tasks.  
- Builds appropriately on prior Bayesian-perception and identifiability literature and situates contribution within existing debates.  
- Mathematical development is rigorous, with derivations in supplementary material.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized; helpful figures (e.g., model stages).  
- Main weakness: observer model is very complex with many parameters, raising computational feasibility and identifiability concerns.  
- Strong assumptions (parametric forms for sensory map, prior, loss) may limit generality; sensitivity to these assumptions not fully explored.  
- Limited scope of empirical examples; would benefit from application to more diverse or complex tasks.  
- Need for more detailed discussion of computational resources and implementation choices for practitioners.  
- Focus on ideal observers leaves uncertain how results generalize to suboptimal/realistic observers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem, offers a novel a priori identifiability method, is technically sound and useful.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical applicability limited by model complexity, strong assumptions, limited examples, and computational demands.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions to address computational details and sensitivity analyses.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
