- Proposes a framework to assess parameter identifiability in Bayesian perception models a priori, showing multiple priors/likelihoods/losses can yield identical behavior.

- Builds on prior work (Maloney & Mamassian; Stocker & Simoncelli), introduces a flexible Bayesian observer model, and demonstrates utility on time-interval estimation and speed perception.

- Addresses a significant, under-analyzed problem that undermines recovery of internal representations from behavioral data.

- Theoretically sound: uses KL divergence and a rigorous derivation of expected log likelihood as an identifiability metric.

- Practical utility: case studies show how experimental constraints (e.g., measuring motor parameters) affect recoverability and guide experimental design.

- Model flexibility: observer model is general enough to cover many perceptual tasks while remaining computationally tractable.

- Clear, well-organized presentation that makes the framework accessible to researchers.

- Limited validation: evaluated on estimation tasks only, with unclear applicability to common choice-based paradigms like 2AFC.

- Computational cost concerns: requires MCMC from approximate posteriors, risking prohibitive expense for complex models or large parameter spaces.

- Sensitivity to the assumed reference observer (θ*) is noted but not systematically explored, limiting robustness of conclusions.

- No direct comparison to alternative identifiability methods, weakening claims of novelty/advantage.

- Limited practical implementation guidance (e.g., how many trials are needed for reliable assessments).

- Overall assessment: valuable, well-motivated contribution that can improve reliability of Bayesian modeling despite computational and validation limitations.

- Recommendation: accept at NIPS.

- Suggestion: extend the framework to choice-based paradigms (e.g., 2AFC).

- Suggestion: include a brief comparison to alternative identifiability methods and emphasize advantages.

- Suggestion: discuss computational requirements and possible approximations for scalability.

- Suggestion: address partially identifiable parameters and how the framework would handle them.

- Suggestion: in case studies, more explicitly relate findings to existing literature (e.g., narrow loss functions worsening prior recovery).
