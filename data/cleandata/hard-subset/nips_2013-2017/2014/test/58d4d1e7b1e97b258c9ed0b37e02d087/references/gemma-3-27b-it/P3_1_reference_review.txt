## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical issue in the burgeoning field of Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors argue, correctly, that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions, rendering the recovery of “true” internal representations from observed data problematic. They present a novel framework for *a priori* assessment of identifiability in a broad class of Bayesian observer models, centered around a flexible parametrization and efficient exploration of the parameter space using KL divergence. They demonstrate the utility of their framework through case studies in time and speed perception, revealing how experimental design choices can significantly impact the reliability of parameter recovery.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of work in Bayesian modeling of perception (Knill & Richards, 1996; Trommershäuser et al., 2003; Körding & Wolpert, 2004) and the challenges of model inversion (e.g., Landy & Maloney, 2005).  It directly addresses critiques of Bayesian modeling related to identifiability (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) and extends previous attempts to mitigate degeneracy through constraints (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Acerbi et al., 2013). The novelty lies in providing a systematic, quantitative framework for evaluating identifiability *before* running experiments, allowing for informed experimental design, a gap explicitly acknowledged by the authors. It draws inspiration from identifiability analysis in other fields (e.g., Navarro et al., 2008) but tailors it to the specifics of psychophysical modeling.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of model identifiability is fundamental to the validity of inferences drawn from Bayesian models of perception.  Addressing this problem is a significant contribution.
* **General Framework:** The authors present a surprisingly general and flexible observer model, capable of encompassing a wide range of psychophysical tasks and prior assumptions. The clever use of parametric forms (Eqs. 2, 4, 6) balances expressive power with computational tractability.
* **Rigorous Methodology:** The use of KL divergence as a measure of model distinguishability and the MCMC sampling approach provide a principled way to explore the identifiability landscape.
* **Practical Applications:** The case studies are well-chosen and illustrate the practical utility of the framework. The identification of experimental design flaws (e.g., the detrimental effect of enforcing a narrow loss function in the time estimation task) is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though dense.  The inclusion of a detailed Supplementary Material is crucial for understanding the technical details.
* **Open-Source Potential:** The framework could be readily implemented as a tool for researchers in the field, facilitating more rigorous model building and experimental design.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors emphasize efficiency, the MCMC sampling required for identifiability analysis can still be computationally expensive, particularly for high-dimensional parameter spaces.  A discussion of scalability and potential optimization strategies would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions of the Observer Model:** The framework’s utility is tied to the validity of the chosen observer model. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different model assumptions (e.g., the Gaussian mixture form of the likelihood) would strengthen the paper.
* **Simplified Response Stage:** The response stage (Eqs. 8-9) seems somewhat simplistic.  Assuming independent motor noise may not always be realistic, and could introduce another source of identifiability issues.
* **Limited Exploration of Parameter Space:** The paper focuses on specific parameter ranges motivated by previous work. A more comprehensive exploration of the entire parameter space could reveal unexpected identifiability challenges.
* **Lack of Neural Link:** While the motivation stems from linking behavior to neural representations, the paper remains purely behavioral.  A discussion of how the framework could be extended to incorporate neural data would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial and often overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Presents a novel and rigorously developed framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
* Demonstrates practical utility through compelling case studies.
* Has the potential to significantly improve the quality and interpretability of psychophysical research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost of the analysis could limit its widespread adoption.
* The framework's utility is contingent on the validity of the chosen observer model.
* Relatively narrow focus on estimation tasks; applicability to other paradigms (e.g., decision-making) is not fully explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The authors have identified a critical issue and developed a compelling framework for addressing it. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses. The framework is theoretically sound, practically relevant, and has the potential to advance the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding computational cost and the sensitivity of the results to model assumptions.
