### Summary

This paper presents a computational framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis of Bayesian models of perception. The authors tackle a well-known, fundamental problem: behavior alone is often insufficient to uniquely determine the internal components of a Bayesian model (the prior, likelihood, and loss function), as different combinations of these components can produce identical behavioral predictions. This non-identifiability poses a significant challenge to studies aiming to recover an observer's internal representations.

The core contributions are twofold. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians and other functional forms to represent a wide variety of priors, likelihoods (with varying moments), and loss functions, as well as stimulus-dependent sensory noise and motor noise. This construction is designed to be both expressive and computationally tractable. Second, they propose an efficient method to assess identifiability. Instead of running computationally expensive simulations, they derive an analytical expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model given data generated by a "true" reference model. This metric simplifies to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" based on this KL divergence, they can map out the "identifiability landscape" for a given experimental design, revealing which parameters can be reliably recovered and which are degenerate.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies. In a time perception task, they show how different experimental designs (e.g., using one vs. two stimulus conditions, assuming a MAP-like loss function, or independently measuring motor noise) dramatically affect the ability to recover the observer's prior. In a speed perception task, they provide theoretical validation for the classic finding of a "slow-speed prior," showing that the key model components in this context are indeed identifiable.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical and pervasive problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. While the non-identifiability issue is known [17, 19], this work is, to my knowledge, the first to propose a systematic, generic, and practical framework to analyze it *before* an experiment is run. This shifts the problem from a post-hoc critique to a tool for principled experimental design, which is a major conceptual and practical advance. The idea of using the expected KL divergence as an efficient proxy for identifiability is elegant and novel.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-executed. The observer model is thoughtfully constructed, balancing flexibility (using mixtures of Gaussians as a basis set for distributions) with computational efficiency (leading to an analytical expression for the expected loss). The derivation of the identifiability metric (Eq. 12) is clever, correctly identifying the KL divergence as the key quantity and avoiding the intractability of direct simulation. The use of MCMC to explore the resulting identifiability landscape is appropriate.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for the work is laid out plainly, the four stages of the model are described logically, and the method for assessing identifiability is well explained. The case studies are well-chosen to illustrate the power of the framework, with one demonstrating how to compare experimental designs and another validating a well-known result from the literature. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

4.  **Practical Impact:** This work has the potential for significant impact on how psychophysical and behavioral experiments are designed. Researchers can use this framework to test whether their planned experiment is capable of disentangling the parameters they care about, potentially saving considerable time and resources. The non-obvious finding that encouraging a MAP-like loss function could *worsen* the recovery of the prior is a perfect example of the kind of valuable, counter-intuitive insight this approach can provide.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scope of the Model:** The framework is currently limited to 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a substantial portion of the literature, many classic paradigms use discrete choices, such as two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but it would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion of the challenges or potential avenues for extending the framework to such paradigms.

2.  **Reliance on the Reference Observer:** The analysis is contingent on the choice of the reference observer, θ*. The conclusions about which experimental design is "best" are therefore conditional on the assumptions made about this ground-truth observer. While this is inherent to any *a priori* analysis, the paper would benefit from a short discussion on the robustness of its conclusions. For example, how sensitive is the ranking of experimental designs in the time perception case study to different (but plausible) assumptions about the reference observer's noise parameters or lapse rate?

3.  **Clarity of Terminology:** The term "approximate expected posterior density" for E(θ|θ*) in Eq. 13, while technically motivated, could be slightly confusing as it is not a posterior in the standard sense (i.e., conditioned on observed data). Describing it as an "identifiability landscape" or an "expected evidence landscape" might be more intuitive for the reader, though this is a minor point.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, well-recognized problem in Bayesian modeling of cognition.
*   The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and provides a practical tool for improving experimental design.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and supported by compelling case studies that demonstrate its value.
*   The work has high potential for impact in computational neuroscience and cognitive science, fields with strong representation at NeurIPS.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The framework's applicability is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks, excluding other common experimental paradigms.
*   The analysis depends on assumptions about a "ground truth" reference observer, and the sensitivity to these assumptions is not explored.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, elegant, and highly significant contribution. It provides a principled and practical solution to the long-standing problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models. The strengths—in terms of significance, technical quality, and clarity—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to the current scope of the model. This work will likely become an important tool for researchers designing experiments to probe the internal representations underlying perception and decision-making. I strongly recommend acceptance.
