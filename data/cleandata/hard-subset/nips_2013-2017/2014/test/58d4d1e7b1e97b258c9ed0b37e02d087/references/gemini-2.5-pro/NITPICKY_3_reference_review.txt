### Summary
This paper introduces a computational framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis on Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors identify a core problem in the field: many combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavior, making it difficult to reliably recover these representations from experimental data. To address this, they first construct a flexible, parametric class of observer models capable of representing a wide range of internal functions using mixtures of Gaussians. Second, they propose a method to map the "identifiability landscape" by quantifying the similarity between the predicted response distributions of a reference model and other models in the parameter space, using the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence as a distance metric. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one on inferring priors in a time interval estimation task, and another re-examining the inference of a "slow-speed prior" in visual perception. The results suggest that the framework can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs and to assess the robustness of previous findings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically ambitious and addresses a fundamentally important problem. The core idea of creating a systematic framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis is a significant contribution. The mathematical formulation, culminating in Eq. 12, appears sound and provides a principled way to quantify the expected difference between models. The case studies are well-chosen and yield non-trivial, interesting conclusions (e.g., that a MAP-like loss function might *worsen* prior recovery).

However, the work is undermined by a concerning number of under-justified methodological choices and a lack of critical technical detail, which compromises its claim of being a rigorous framework.

1.  **Observer Model Specification:** The model components are justified by "computational convenience" and "maximum-entropy approach," but these justifications feel superficial. For instance, why are the likelihood (Eq. 3) and loss function (Eq. 6) specifically parametrized by their first four moments? What is the justification for this choice over, say, two or six moments? The procedure for fixing the remaining degree of freedom by "locally maximiz[ing] the differential entropy" is a critical detail that is relegated to the supplement, making it impossible to evaluate from the main text. The choice of M=15 for the prior basis is stated as a computational constraint, but without any analysis of its sufficiency for approximating the priors in question.

2.  **The "Approximate Expected Posterior":** The definition in Eq. 13 is the linchpin of the analysis, but it is presented without sufficient statistical grounding. Calling it an "approximate expected posterior density" is misleading. It is not a posterior in any formal sense; it is a heuristic measure constructed from the exponentiated expected log-likelihood. While this may be a useful tool for visualizing the identifiability landscape, the paper should be more precise about its statistical status and limitations.

3.  **Missing MCMC Details:** The paper states that it samples from Eq. 13 via MCMC, but provides zero details about this procedure. What sampler was used? How was convergence assessed? Given the high dimensionality and potential multimodality of the parameter space (Eq. 10 lists 18+ parameters), MCMC is highly non-trivial. Without these details, the results in Figures 2 and 3 are not reproducible and their reliability cannot be verified.

4.  **Case Study Simplifications:** The conversion of the 2AFC task from [9] into an "equivalent estimation task" for the second case study is a major simplification that is mentioned only in passing. The equivalence between these paradigms is not straightforward and is a subject of research in its own right. This assumption could significantly impact the conclusions drawn about the identifiability of the slow-speed prior.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and the motivation is clearly articulated. However, several aspects detract from its clarity.
- The footnote on page 4 is stylistically poor and contains what appears to be a typo ("motor noise, w1," presumably meaning ρ1). Such footnotes disrupt the reading flow and should be integrated into the main text.
- The parameter vector in Eq. 10 is simply listed. It would be more informative to state the total dimensionality and group the parameters by the model component they belong to.
- The derivation from Eq. 11 to Eq. 12 is a key result and should at least be sketched in the main text rather than being entirely offloaded to the supplementary material.
- Formatting is inconsistent, for example "Figure 1 a & b" vs. "Figure 2 a".

**Originality:**
The core contribution—a framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis in this specific domain—is novel and valuable. While individual components (mixture models, KL divergence, MCMC) are standard tools, their synthesis into a coherent framework for improving experimental design in psychophysics is a new and welcome idea. The work is well-situated within the relevant literature (e.g., [17, 19, 23]).

**Significance:**
The problem addressed is of high significance to researchers using Bayesian models in cognitive science, psychology, and computational neuroscience. If robust and reliable, this framework could become a standard tool for designing more informative experiments and for testing the validity of conclusions drawn from model fitting. The case studies demonstrate this potential by providing concrete, actionable insights (e.g., the value of independently measuring motor noise). However, the aforementioned weaknesses in quality and clarity currently limit its immediate impact and utility.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro:**
- Addresses a fundamental and pervasive problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
- Proposes a novel and principled framework for *a priori* analysis, which could improve experimental design.
- The flexible observer model is a useful contribution in itself.
- The case studies provide non-obvious insights that challenge common assumptions and validate previous findings.

**Con:**
- Key methodological choices in the model's construction are poorly justified.
- The central analytical tool (Eq. 13) is presented as a "posterior" but is a heuristic that lacks rigorous statistical interpretation.
- The complete absence of MCMC implementation details makes the core results unverifiable and not reproducible.
- A critical assumption in the second case study (converting 2AFC to estimation) is not justified.
- The paper suffers from several minor but telling issues of clarity, formatting, and polish that, taken together, suggest a lack of meticulousness.

### Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem with a novel and promising approach, the execution and presentation are not yet up to the standards of a NIPS publication. The accumulation of under-justified methodological choices, glossed-over assumptions, and a critical lack of technical detail regarding the MCMC sampling prevents a confident assessment of the results. The work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete, rigorous framework. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors have a strong foundation for an excellent paper, but it requires substantial revision to address the issues of rigor, reproducibility, and clarity raised above before it is ready for publication.
