This paper addresses the critical and well-known problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, which fundamentally undermines the goal of recovering an observer's internal representations from behavioral data. To address this, they propose a computational framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis. The core of the framework is a flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models and a method to quantify the confusability between a "ground truth" reference model and other models in the class. This is achieved by calculating the expected log-likelihood over hypothetical datasets, which is shown to be proportional to the KL-divergence between the models' response distributions. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility through two case studies: one in time perception, where they show that recovering priors is difficult without strong constraints, and another in speed perception, where they find the inference of a slow-speed prior to be more robust.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The issue of model non-identifiability is a serious methodological challenge in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. Many studies fit complex Bayesian models without rigorously assessing whether the parameters of interest can be uniquely recovered. This paper tackles this important problem head-on, providing a principled approach to think about and quantify identifiability before an experiment is even run.
2.  **Potential for Practical Utility:** If robust, the proposed framework could be a valuable tool for experimenters. The ability to compare different experimental designs (e.g., number of trials, stimulus distributions, feedback schemes) in terms of their power to identify specific model components (like the prior) could save significant resources and lead to more conclusive scientific findings.
3.  **Technically Sound Formulation:** The proposed observer model is commendably flexible, encompassing stimulus-dependent noise, non-Gaussian likelihoods and loss functions, and motor variability, which are all common features of psychophysical tasks. The parameterization via mixtures of Gaussians is a standard and sensible choice for maintaining computational tractability. The use of expected log-likelihood, and its connection to KL-divergence, is a principled way to define the identifiability landscape.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on an Assumed Ground Truth:** The primary weakness of the proposed framework is its reliance on a pre-specified "reference observer" θ*. The entire analysis is a simulation that evaluates how well one could recover the parameters of θ* if it were the true generative model. However, the true parameters are precisely what the experiment aims to discover. This introduces a degree of circularity. The framework's conclusions are only as good as the assumptions made about the reference observer. If the true observer's model structure is different from what is assumed (e.g., the true loss function is not well-approximated by an inverted mixture of two Gaussians), the analysis may be misleading. The paper does not sufficiently grapple with this conceptual limitation.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Methods:** The problem of parameter identifiability is not new, and other methods exist, ranging from Fisher Information analysis to brute-force simulation-and-refitting approaches. The authors mention generic techniques but do not position their work relative to them. They claim their method is "efficient" but provide no quantitative comparison against any alternative method for identifiability analysis. For a methodological contribution, it is crucial to demonstrate superiority or novelty over existing approaches, rather than simply presenting the new method in isolation.
3.  **Absence of Empirical Validation:** The framework is an unproven tool until it is validated against real-world data. The case studies are purely theoretical simulations. A much stronger demonstration would involve taking an existing, published dataset, performing a standard model fit to find a best-fitting θ*, and then running the *a priori* analysis to see if the predicted degeneracies from the framework (i.e., the shape of the approximate posterior E(θ|θ*)) match the actual posterior distributions or confidence intervals obtained from fitting the real data. Without such validation, the framework remains a speculative exercise.
4.  **Model Complexity and Scope:** The proposed observer model is highly complex, with over 20 parameters. While flexible, this complexity may itself be a source of identifiability issues, and it raises questions about the robustness of the MCMC sampling used to explore the high-dimensional parameter space. Furthermore, the framework is currently limited to one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This excludes a large class of psychophysical paradigms, most notably discrete-choice tasks like 2-alternative forced choice (2AFC), where many influential findings have been reported.

### Summary of Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated idea. The goal of providing tools for *a priori* identifiability analysis is laudable and important for the field. However, the work feels premature for publication at NIPS in its current form. The reliance on an assumed ground truth observer model is a significant conceptual issue that needs more discussion. More critically, as a paper proposing a new methodology, it fails to benchmark its approach against any established alternatives and lacks any validation on real experimental data. The conclusions drawn from the case studies are intriguing, but they are ultimately derived from a purely theoretical framework whose practical utility has not been demonstrated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and often overlooked methodological problem in a principled manner.
*   The framework is flexible and could serve as a valuable tool for designing more robust experiments in computational cognitive science.
*   The case studies provide non-trivial and potentially useful insights.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The entire analysis is conditional on strong, untestable assumptions about the "true" reference observer model.
*   The proposed method is not compared against or validated with respect to existing, established methods for identifiability analysis.
*   The framework's practical value is not demonstrated, as it is never applied to or tested against real experimental data.
*   The work is presented as a complete framework, but its scope is limited and its complexity raises concerns that are not fully addressed.

Overall, while the direction is promising, the contribution lacks the rigorous validation and comparison to prior work expected of a methodological paper at NIPS.
