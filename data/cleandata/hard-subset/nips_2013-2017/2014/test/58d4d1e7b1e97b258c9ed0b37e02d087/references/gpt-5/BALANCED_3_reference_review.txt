Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses identifiability in Bayesian models of perception: given behavioral data, to what extent can we uniquely recover the observer’s internal components (prior, likelihood, loss)? The authors (i) define a flexible 1D estimation observer model: a stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) that generalizes Weber–Fechner/Stevens; sensory noise and internal likelihoods parameterized as two-Gaussian mixtures with moment constraints; priors represented as mixtures on a Gaussian lattice; loss functions as inverted Gaussian mixtures; lapses and motor noise; (ii) derive an a priori identifiability metric based on the expected log-likelihood of data generated by a reference observer θ*, showing it equals −Ntr Σi Pi DKL(presp(·|si;θ*) || presp(·|si;θ)) + const; and (iii) sample from the induced “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) to map parameter degeneracies and compare experimental designs. Two case studies follow: interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010) and visual speed perception with slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006).

The identifiability problem and invariances in Bayesian decision models are well known (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Acerbi et al.; Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis). General techniques for model distinguishability and expected evidence (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung; information-theoretic design) are relevant antecedents. The paper’s contribution is to instantiate a practical, unified framework tailored to Bayesian observer models, with computational tricks that enable broad exploration and design comparison.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem: systematic, a priori identifiability analysis for Bayesian perception models, with direct implications for experimental design.
- Coherent, flexible observer model spanning priors, likelihoods, losses, lapses, and motor noise, with efficient computations enabled by Gaussian-mixture algebra.
- Clear derivation linking expected log-likelihood to averaged KL divergences across stimuli; sensible and interpretable utility.
- Case studies are informative: interval timing reveals nontrivial degeneracies (sensory vs motor noise; loss width) and actionable design advice (measure motor noise; beware narrow loss); speed perception supports robustness of slow-speed prior inference under noise manipulations.
- Emphasis on comparing designs under realistic constraints (shared parameters across conditions).

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: the expected-evidence/KL formulation and identifiability via a reference model follow established distinguishability and optimal design literatures. The paper could better position itself relative to Bayesian optimal experimental design (e.g., Lindley; Chaloner & Verdinelli; MacKay; Cavagnaro et al.; adaptive psychophysical methods like PSI/QUEST).
- Heavy modeling assumptions: 1D estimation tasks, mixture-of-Gaussian parameterizations (including loss), and a fixed lattice prior basis. It is unclear how robust conclusions are to misspecification (e.g., non-unimodal losses, heavier-tailed likelihoods, non-mixture priors).
- Computational aspects are underspecified in the main text: how are KLs between continuous response densities computed, what quadrature/resolution is used, how are MCMC samplers configured and diagnosed, and what are runtime/complexity characteristics as dimensionality grows?
- Overparameterization: θ is high-dimensional (including 15 prior weights). Although the method is for a priori mapping, results may be sensitive to implicit priors/constraints on weights (positivity, normalization, smoothness). Clarify constraints and regularization.
- The 2AFC-to-estimation mapping in the speed case is mentioned but not detailed. Identifiability conclusions may depend on this equivalence; more justification would strengthen the claim.
- Design optimization is not attempted. The framework could, in principle, optimize stimulus distributions Pi or condition structure to maximize expected separation; here, designs are compared qualitatively a posteriori.

Quality
- The derivation of the expected log-likelihood as a sum of KL divergences is sound and useful.
- The invariance argument in Eq. 1 is correct and nicely motivates the need for constraints.
- The case studies’ conclusions are plausible and align with intuitions (e.g., need to measure motor noise) while providing less obvious insights (narrow loss can harm identifiability).
- However, important methodological details are deferred to the supplement. Reproducibility would benefit from code release and explicit numerical schemes.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but dense. The main text would benefit from: (i) a concise algorithmic summary of the identifiability pipeline; (ii) explicit constraints on mixture weights; (iii) a short paragraph on numerical evaluation of KLs and presp; (iv) a schematic of the MCMC setup and convergence checks.

Originality
- The unified, efficient instantiation for Bayesian observer models and the practical design comparisons are the main novel aspects. The identifiability metric itself is standard.

Significance
- For the computational perception community, a principled tool to stress-test identifiability and guide design is valuable. Impact will hinge on robustness beyond 1D tasks and availability of an implementation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the approach within Bayesian optimal experimental design and adaptive stimulus selection literatures; discuss when your expected KL objective matches mutual information or Bayes risk, and potential to optimize stimuli Pi or condition allocation.
- Provide implementation details, computational costs, and release code. Include convergence diagnostics and sensitivity analyses (e.g., number of basis components M, grid resolution).
- Explore model misspecification: generate data from observers with non-Gaussian-tailed likelihoods, non-mixture priors, or non-unimodal losses, and assess robustness.
- In the interval timing study, quantify how much fixing motor parameters improves identifiability across a range of plausible values; consider additional constraints (e.g., feedback manipulations to identify σ`).
- In the speed study, justify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and check that identifiability conclusions carry over to discrete-choice models. Alternatively, include a discrete-choice variant of your framework (as noted in Conclusions).
- Consider hierarchical or random-effects extensions to account for inter-subject variability when using θ* as a reference.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-served need (a priori identifiability and design guidance).
- Thoughtful, interpretable analyses with concrete, sometimes counterintuitive design insights.
- Framework likely to be useful to practitioners.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty in the core identifiability metric; positioning relative to existing design theory is thin.
- Strong modeling assumptions; robustness and generality (beyond 1D estimation) not yet demonstrated.
- Missing methodological details and code limit reproducibility and adoption.

Recommendation
Weak reject. I find the problem important and the framework promising, but the current version falls short on novelty positioning, methodological transparency, and scope. Addressing the suggested points—especially clarifying computational details, connecting to the experimental design literature, demonstrating robustness to misspecification, and providing code—could raise this to a strong submission.
