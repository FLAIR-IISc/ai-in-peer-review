- Summary: Proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian perception models using KL divergence, demonstrated on time-interval and speed perception tasks.  
- Strength: Addresses the important, often-overlooked identifiability problem in Bayesian behavioral modeling.  
- Strength: Provides a flexible, comprehensive observer model capable of representing many psychophysical tasks.  
- Strength: Emphasizes a priori analysis to improve experimental design before data collection.  
- Strength: Clearly motivated and well-connected to existing literature on Bayesian perception and identifiability.  
- Strength: Demonstrates utility via case studies that reveal practical pitfalls in experimental design.  
- Strength: Technically rigorous; KL-divergence formulation is appropriate and well-justified.  
- Weakness: Potentially high computational cost and unclear scaling of MCMC sampling with model complexity.  
- Weakness: Relies on simplifying assumptions (functional forms for priors, likelihoods, loss) without thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Limited and insufficiently justified exploration of parameter ranges in case studies.  
- Weakness: Lacks explicit comparison to other general identifiability techniques and their relative advantages.  
- Weakness: Focuses on ideal Bayesian observers, with limited treatment of sub-optimal human decision noise beyond lapse rates.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated, and useful contribution with some limitations around computation and assumptions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Tackles a significant problem, offers a novel, well-developed framework, and provides insightful demonstrations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Concerns over computational scalability, reliance on assumptions, and limited comparisons to existing methods.  
- Qualitative evaluation: Quality—strong; Clarity—good though some derivations need clarification; Originality—high; Significance—high.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address computational complexity, sensitivity to assumptions, and comparison to other techniques.
