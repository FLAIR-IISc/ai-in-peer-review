## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and timely contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. It tackles a critical, often overlooked issue – the identifiability of parameters in these models – and proposes a systematic framework for addressing it *a priori*. The framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and the application to concrete examples (time and speed perception) is insightful. While the mathematical formalism is dense, the authors do a commendable job of explaining it, and the potential impact on experimental design is significant. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions (detailed below).

**1. Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the fundamental problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception: multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can generate the same observed behavior. This degeneracy has long been acknowledged (references [17, 18, 19] are relevant), but a systematic method for analyzing it *before* running an experiment has been lacking. The authors address this gap by presenting a framework for exploring the identifiability landscape, leveraging the common structure of many perceptual models. They introduce a flexible parameterized class of observer models and a method based on Kullback-Leibler divergence to quantify the degree of degeneracy.  This builds upon earlier work attempting to constrain identifiability through experimental design or auxiliary measurements [5, 12, 19], but provides a much more general and proactive approach. The work also acknowledges connections to model identifiability issues in other areas of psychology [22, 23], but smartly adapts those techniques to the specifics of Bayesian perceptual modeling.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is crucial for the validity of inferences drawn from Bayesian models. This work directly addresses a fundamental limitation.
* **Systematic Framework:** The proposed framework provides a structured, quantitative approach to assess identifiability *a priori*. This is a significant advance over ad-hoc attempts to constrain model parameters.
* **Flexible Model Class:** The introduced Bayesian observer model is impressively flexible, encompassing a wide range of perceptual tasks and parameterizations. The use of a single parameterization across different aspects (sensation, perception, decision-making) is a key strength.
* **Clear Methodology:** The use of KL-divergence and MCMC sampling to explore the identifiability landscape is well-justified and provides a practical means for assessing model degeneracy.  The derivation of the key equation (12) is clearly explained in the supplementary material.
* **Illustrative Examples:** Applying the framework to time and speed perception demonstrates its utility and provides concrete insights into how experimental design can be optimized. The counterintuitive finding regarding the MAP loss function in the time perception example is particularly noteworthy.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., focusing on one-dimensional stimuli) and discusses potential extensions.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Complexity:** The paper is mathematically dense, and while the authors attempt to explain the formalism, readers unfamiliar with Bayesian statistics and information theory may struggle to follow all the details. This isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, but it could limit the broader impact of the work.
* **Computational Cost:** Exploring the identifiability landscape using MCMC can be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional parameter spaces. This limitation should be more explicitly addressed. While they aim for efficiency, the practical scaling of the method needs further consideration.
* **Reliance on Reference Observer:** The framework relies on defining a “reference observer” (θ*).  The choice of this observer is somewhat arbitrary and could influence the results.  While the authors acknowledge this, more discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different choices of θ* would be beneficial.
* **Simplifying Assumptions:** The model incorporates several simplifying assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific functional forms for priors and loss functions).  While justified, it's important to acknowledge that these assumptions may limit the generalizability of the framework.



**4. Specific Comments & Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Clarity:** Consider adding a more intuitive explanation of the KL-divergence in the main text. Perhaps a simplified example illustrating its interpretation would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:** Explicitly discuss the computational demands of the MCMC sampling and potential strategies for mitigating these costs (e.g., dimensionality reduction, approximate inference).
* **Reference Observer Sensitivity:** Perform a sensitivity analysis to assess how the results change with different choices for the reference observer (θ*).  Demonstrating the robustness of the conclusions is crucial.
* **Model Assumptions:** Briefly discuss the impact of the simplifying assumptions on the generalizability of the framework.
* **Future Work:** Expand the discussion of future work to include applications to more complex perceptual phenomena (e.g., multisensory integration, decision-making under uncertainty).



**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a critical and often overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling.
* Proposes a systematic and quantitative framework for assessing identifiability.
* Provides valuable insights into experimental design.
* Technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis.
* Demonstrates practical utility through concrete examples.

**Con:**

* Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
* Computational cost could be a barrier for some applications.
* Reliance on a reference observer introduces a degree of arbitrariness.

**Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It represents a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to influence how Bayesian models of perception are designed, analyzed, and interpreted.**  I strongly recommend acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
