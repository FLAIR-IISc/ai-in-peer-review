Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework to assess, a priori, the identifiability of parameters in Bayesian observer models of perception. The core idea is to define a flexible, computationally efficient class of observer models for 1D estimation tasks, where the sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss are parameterized in an internal space using mixtures of Gaussians (with moments matched by a maximum-entropy construction). Given a “reference” observer θ*, the authors derive an expression for the expected log marginal likelihood of any candidate model θ over all datasets of fixed size Ntr generated by θ*, obtaining a sum of Kullback–Leibler divergences between predicted response distributions across stimuli. They use this to define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(E[log p(D|θ)]), sample it by MCMC, and map practical non-identifiability. Two case studies illustrate the approach: interval timing (where recovering priors is fragile unless motor parameters are known, and narrowing the loss function can worsen identifiability) and speed perception (where recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters is relatively robust).

This work is well positioned within debates about Bayesian models of cognition/perception (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Stocker & Simoncelli; Acerbi et al.) and critiques about non-identifiability and “just-so stories” (Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love). It connects to general distinguishability/identifiability analyses (Navarro, Pitt & Myung) but tailors the analysis to a class of Bayesian observer models with closed-form expected loss, enabling efficient computation.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem: identifiability of Bayesian observer components (prior/likelihood/loss) is a central issue in perception research.
- Clear formal criterion: the expected log-evidence reduces to weighted sums of KL divergences, avoiding brute-force averaging over simulated datasets.
- Flexible model class: a unified parametrization for priors, likelihoods, and loss (mixtures in internal space) supports fast evaluation and broad coverage of models common in psychophysics.
- Practical insights: the case studies yield actionable design guidance (e.g., measure motor noise; beware narrow loss functions when inferring priors).
- Transparent assumptions: separation of sensation, decision, and response stages; explicit handling of lapses; sharing of parameters across conditions.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures; log-like sensory mapping f(s); unimodality constraints). It is unclear how sensitive the conclusions are to these choices (e.g., M components, non-unimodal priors).
- Limited validation of the identifiability metric: E(θ|θ*) is motivated, but the paper lacks a systematic comparison to ground-truth empirical identifiability (e.g., simulating datasets and running full inference to show correspondence with posterior concentration/bias).
- Some key constructions are deferred to the Supplementary (max-entropy mixture with unimodality, expected loss derivation, Eq. 12 derivation), reducing reproducibility from the main text.
- The 2AFC-to-estimation mapping in the speed case is nontrivial; the paper asserts equivalence but does not quantify potential distortions in identifiability.
- Computational aspects (MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, runtime scaling with parameter dimension and number of stimuli) are not detailed.

Assessment by review criteria
Quality: The central derivation (expected log-evidence as KL sum) is sound and standard. The modeling pipeline is technically careful, and the case studies demonstrate the method. However, the empirical validation is limited to the proposed metric itself; there is no demonstration that E(θ|θ*) accurately predicts recoverability under actual inference from synthetic datasets. Some choices (e.g., lapse drawn from prior; excluding motor noise from decision stage) are reasonable but could be discussed for their impact on identifiability.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The modular structure of the model and the identifiability criterion are clear. Nonetheless, several crucial technical details are relegated to the Supplementary, and references are inconsistently formatted. A brief sketch of the max-entropy mixture construction and the derivation of Eq. 12 in the main text would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality: The identifiability framework—using the expected log-evidence expressed as KL divergences to sample an “expected posterior” over models—is a novel and useful contribution in this domain. The unifying mixture-based parametrization that enables closed-form expected loss builds on prior work but is combined here in a new way for design-level identifiability analysis.

Significance: High. The paper addresses a widely recognized bottleneck in interpreting Bayesian observer fits, offering a tool that can influence experimental design and claims about internal representations. The case studies hit prominent topics (interval timing, slow-speed prior). With stronger validation and broader scope, this framework could become a standard pre-registration step for perceptual modeling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Empirical validation: For selected scenarios, simulate multiple datasets from θ*, perform full Bayesian or ML fitting, and quantify how posterior concentration/bias aligns with E(θ|θ*). Report correlations between predicted and realized reconstruction errors.
- Robustness analyses: Vary number of mixture components; allow non-unimodal/peaked priors; test misspecified forms (e.g., true loss not representable by mixture) to characterize failure modes.
- Design optimization: Show how to optimize stimulus frequencies Pi (or conditions) to maximize identifiability (e.g., via gradients of the KL sum), turning the framework into an active design tool.
- 2AFC mapping: Provide a derivation and limits of the equivalence, or include an explicit discrete-choice variant of the framework.
- Computation: Document MCMC settings, convergence checks, runtime, and scaling. Provide code to encourage adoption.
- Clarify the max-entropy constructions and unimodality enforcement in the main text, and tidy references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-served problem with a principled, computationally efficient method.
- Offers concrete experimental design insights for widely studied perceptual tasks.
- Likely to be used by practitioners; aligns with ongoing conversations at NeurIPS about model identifiability and interpretability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Validation is mostly internal; lacks demonstrations that the proposed metric predicts actual inferential performance across datasets and fitting methods.
- Dependence on specific parametric choices may limit generality; sensitivity analyses are thin.
- Some claims (e.g., effect of narrow loss) may be task- and parametrization-dependent; generality is not established.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The contribution is conceptually strong and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit from additional validation and robustness analyses. If space allows to address these concerns (or via supplemental material and code), this work merits inclusion.
