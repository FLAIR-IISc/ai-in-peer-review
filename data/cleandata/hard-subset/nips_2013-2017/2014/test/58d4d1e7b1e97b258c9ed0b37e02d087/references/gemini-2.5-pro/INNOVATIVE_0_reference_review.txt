**Summary:**
This paper addresses the fundamental problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. It is a well-known issue that different combinations of internal representations—priors, likelihoods, and loss functions—can produce identical observable behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover these components from experimental data. The authors' central contribution is a novel computational framework to analyze this problem *a priori*, before an experiment is run. The framework consists of two main parts: (1) a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of capturing a wide range of behaviors in 1D sensory estimation tasks; and (2) an efficient method based on Kullback-Leibler divergence to quantify the expected similarity between different observer models for a given experimental design. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior," the framework allows researchers to visualize the "identifiability landscape" and assess which model parameters can be reliably recovered. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies from time and speed perception, showing how it can be used to compare and improve experimental designs, and to provide theoretical validation for existing empirical findings.

**Strengths:**
*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a foundational, critical, and often-underappreciated problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. While the issue of non-identifiability is known, approaches to address it have been largely ad-hoc. This work introduces a principled, systematic, and general framework for analyzing it. This is not an incremental advance but a proposal for a new meta-level tool that could change how researchers design and interpret experiments involving Bayesian observer models. The ambition to formalize the process of experimental design itself is bold and highly commendable.
*   **Novel Conceptual Approach:** The core idea of mapping an "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected posterior support for alternative models is both elegant and powerful. It provides a formal, quantitative language to express intuitions about good experimental design and can reveal non-obvious trade-offs and parameter degeneracies.
*   **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The case studies are not mere toy examples; they are grounded in well-known perceptual domains and provide genuinely insightful results. For instance, the analysis of the time perception task yields the non-intuitive conclusion that enforcing a MAP-like loss function could actually *hinder* the recovery of the observer's prior, due to a degeneracy with other model parameters. This highlights the framework's power to go beyond simple intuition. Conversely, the validation of the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference in the speed perception task adds significant theoretical weight to a classic finding in the field.
*   **Well-Constructed General Model:** The authors have clearly put significant thought into creating a "meta-model" of the Bayesian observer that is flexible enough to be useful. The use of mixture models for the prior, likelihood, and loss function, combined with a parametric sensory mapping, provides a rich representational basis while maintaining computational tractability—a key requirement for the proposed MCMC-based exploration.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Dependence on the Chosen Model Class:** The framework's conclusions are necessarily conditioned on the specific, albeit flexible, class of observer models proposed. The reliance on Gaussian mixtures and the particular functional form for the sensory mapping (Eq. 2) are pragmatic choices, but it remains an open question how sensitive the resulting identifiability analyses are to these assumptions. A brief discussion of this potential limitation would strengthen the paper.
*   **Scalability and Scope:** The framework is currently presented for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a large swath of psychophysics, its applicability to other common paradigms, such as discrete-choice tasks (e.g., 2AFC) or multi-dimensional stimuli, is not yet established. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but the current scope should be clearly stated as a limitation.
*   **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper describes the method as "efficient," but lacks detail on the computational cost. For this tool to be adopted by experimentalists, it would be useful to have a sense of the computational resources required to run such an analysis (e.g., typical runtime for the case studies presented).

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a highly novel and significant idea. It proposes a new tool and a new way of thinking about the relationship between theoretical modeling and experimental design in a field where this link is often weak.
2.  The approach is bold and ambitious. It moves beyond fitting models to existing data and towards a principled method for designing experiments that can actually distinguish between models. This is a major conceptual contribution.
3.  The framework is technically sound and its utility is convincingly demonstrated through well-chosen, non-trivial case studies that provide valuable insights for practicing researchers.
4.  The potential impact is very high. If adopted, this framework could lead to more robust and reliable findings in the study of perception, saving significant experimental resources by heading off poorly-posed research questions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The work is arguably more of a methodological and conceptual advance than a direct data-driven discovery.
2.  The generality of the framework is limited by the specific parametric choices of the observer model and its current focus on 1D estimation tasks.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance are overwhelmingly stronger. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the scope of the current work than to fatal flaws in the core idea. This paper is a perfect example of a high-risk, high-reward submission that offers a significant new direction. It prioritizes a powerful new concept over exhaustive validation, which is a trade-off I find highly valuable. This is exactly the kind of thought-provoking and potentially field-shaping work that NIPS should champion.
