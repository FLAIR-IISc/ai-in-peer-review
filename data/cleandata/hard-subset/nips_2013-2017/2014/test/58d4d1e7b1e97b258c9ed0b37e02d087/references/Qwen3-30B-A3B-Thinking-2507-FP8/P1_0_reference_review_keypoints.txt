- Paper addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models and proposes a framework to assess identifiability a priori.  
- Proposes a flexible class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks and quantifies identifiability via expected log likelihood.  
- Builds on prior identifiability and Bayesian observer work but offers a novel, systematic, and computationally efficient approach.  
- Demonstrates framework with two case studies (temporal interval estimation, speed perception) showing how design choices affect parameter recovery.  
- Theoretical significance: tackles a fundamental, underappreciated methodological issue with a rigorous framework.  
- Novel methodology: expected log likelihood metric avoids the need to simulate thousands of datasets.  
- Practical utility: provides concrete guidance for experimental design and reveals counterintuitive effects (e.g., narrow loss can worsen prior recovery).  
- Clear technical development: mathematically thorough and defines a general, tractable model class.  
- Comprehensive case studies: provide meaningful, domain-specific insights and validation.  
- Limited scope: currently applies mainly to one-dimensional estimation tasks, restricting broader impact.  
- Computational complexity: relies on MCMC sampling of the approximate posterior and lacks analysis of scaling to larger/complex models.  
- Case-study assumptions: conclusions depend on specific reference observer choices; robustness to alternative assumptions is not fully explored.  
- Comparison to prior work: claim of novelty would be strengthened by explicitly contrasting with common ad-hoc methods in the field.  
- Quality and clarity: paper is technically sound, well-written, and precise in mathematical presentation.  
- Originality and significance: presents a novel, potentially widely useful framework for experimental design in perception modeling.  
- For acceptance: fills a methodological gap, is novel and practical, validated by solid case studies, and is well-executed.  
- Against acceptance: limited to 1D tasks, potential computational limitations, and some vulnerable case-study assumptions.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends acceptance, viewing it as an important methodological advance.  
- Specific suggestions: discuss extensions to discrete choice paradigms (e.g., 2AFC), provide more analysis of computational scalability, and compare to common ad-hoc identifiability methods.
