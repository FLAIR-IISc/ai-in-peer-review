- Proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of parameters in Bayesian observer models using a flexible mixture-based parametrization for 1D estimation tasks.  
- Core derivation: expected log marginal likelihood across datasets reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions.  
- Defines an "approximate expected posterior" E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(E[log p(D|θ)]) and samples it via MCMC to map non-identifiability.  
- Demonstrates method on two case studies (interval timing and speed perception) with concrete design insights (e.g., measure motor noise; narrow loss can worsen identifiability).  

Strengths:
- Tackles a timely, important problem in perceptual Bayesian modeling and identifiability.  
- Provides a clear formal criterion avoiding brute-force dataset averaging.  
- Uses a flexible, unified mixture-based parametrization for priors/likelihoods/loss facilitating fast evaluation.  
- Yields practical, actionable experimental design guidance.  
- Makes modeling assumptions explicit (sensation/decision/response separation, lapse handling).  

Weaknesses:
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures, log-like mapping, unimodality) with unclear sensitivity to those choices.  
- Limited empirical validation: no systematic comparison between E(θ|θ*) and actual posterior recovery from simulated datasets.  
- Key technical constructions and derivations are relegated to the Supplementary, reducing reproducibility in the main text.  
- The asserted equivalence between 2AFC and estimation in the speed case is not fully quantified for identifiability distortions.  
- Computational details (MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, runtime scaling) are insufficiently reported.  

Assessment — Quality:
- Central derivation is sound and pipeline is careful, but empirical validation of the identifiability metric against real inference is lacking.  

Assessment — Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, but important technical details and some inconsistent references should be brought into the main text.  

Assessment — Originality:
- Novel and useful combination of expected log-evidence as KL sums and mixture parametrization for design-level identifiability analysis.  

Assessment — Significance:
- High potential impact for experimental design and interpretation of perceptual models, especially if validated and extended.  

Suggestions for improvement:
- Empirically validate E(θ|θ*) by simulating datasets, performing full inference, and correlating predicted vs. realized recovery errors.  
- Conduct robustness analyses over mixture component counts, non-unimodal priors, and misspecified loss forms.  
- Extend the framework to optimize stimulus distributions Pi for maximal identifiability (design optimization).  
- Provide a derivation and limits for the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping or include a discrete-choice variant.  
- Report MCMC settings, convergence checks, runtime scaling, and release code; clarify max-entropy and unimodality constructions in the main text.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, under-served problem with a principled and computationally efficient method and offers concrete experimental guidance.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Validation is mainly internal; lack of demonstrations that the metric predicts actual inferential performance and dependence on specific parametrizations limit generality.  

Overall recommendation:
- Borderline accept — conceptually strong and potentially impactful but needs additional empirical validation and robustness analyses to strengthen the contribution.
