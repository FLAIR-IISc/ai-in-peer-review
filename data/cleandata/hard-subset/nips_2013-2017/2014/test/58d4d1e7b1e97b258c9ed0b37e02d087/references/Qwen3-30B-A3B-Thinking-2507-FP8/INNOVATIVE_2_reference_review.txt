

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical but overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the fundamental problem of model non-identifiability. The authors demonstrate that the inverse mapping from observed behavior to elements of the decision process (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavioral outputs. This degeneracy undermines the validity of inferences about internal representations that are central to the field. The paper's main contribution is a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis, allowing researchers to evaluate whether their experimental design can reliably recover model parameters before collecting data.

The authors introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks involving one-dimensional stimulus estimation. This model is designed to be computationally efficient while maintaining sufficient flexibility to capture diverse perceptual phenomena. The core innovation lies in their method for quantifying identifiability through the expected log-likelihood of a model given data generated by a reference model. This approach avoids the intractable sampling required by previous identifiability methods and provides a practical way to explore the identifiability landscape.

The paper demonstrates the framework on two compelling case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. In the time perception case, the authors reveal non-intuitive results about experimental design - for instance, imposing a narrow loss function (closer to MAP) actually worsens prior recovery, and measuring motor parameters significantly improves identifiability. In speed perception, the framework validates previous findings about slow-speed priors while revealing minor biases in noise parameter recovery. These results show how the framework can guide experimental design and interpretation in real research contexts.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While identifiability issues have been recognized in psychology (e.g., [22, 23]), no systematic framework existed for Bayesian models of perception. The authors fill this gap with a general, computationally feasible approach that can be applied to a wide range of tasks. This represents a significant conceptual advance that could reshape how Bayesian models are developed and validated in perception research.

The significance of this work is high. Many papers in the field make strong claims about internal representations based on Bayesian model fits, but without addressing identifiability concerns. This framework provides a principled way to assess whether such claims are justified. The authors show how their approach can prevent researchers from drawing invalid conclusions about mental representations, potentially resolving debates about loss functions in interval timing [24] and prior expectations in speed perception [9].

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The model formulation is clear, the mathematical derivations are rigorous, and the case studies are well-executed. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the framework currently focuses on continuous estimation tasks) and provide concrete suggestions for future extensions.

Minor weaknesses include the limited scope of case studies (only two domains) and the complexity of the framework, which might present a barrier to adoption. However, these are reasonable limitations for a foundational contribution, and the authors provide clear guidance on implementation.

This paper is a significant contribution to the field that directly addresses a fundamental problem with substantial implications for how we interpret Bayesian models of perception. The framework is novel, well-motivated, and offers a practical solution to a pervasive issue. While the methodological complexity is non-trivial, the potential impact on experimental design and interpretation makes it highly valuable.

The paper aligns with NIPS' focus on theoretical advances with practical implications. It builds on but significantly extends previous work on Bayesian models of perception [12, 14, 19] and connects to broader identifiability concerns in psychological modeling [22, 23]. The authors clearly distinguish their contribution from prior work, providing a well-reasoned justification for their approach.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a bold, novel approach to a critical problem in the field, with significant potential to improve the reliability of inferences about perceptual processing. While the framework is complex, its utility in preventing invalid conclusions from Bayesian model fits makes it a valuable addition to the field. The authors have provided a solid foundation for a new line of research in perceptual modeling, and I expect this paper to become a reference point for future work in the area.

The work exemplifies the type of conceptual advance that NIPS seeks - addressing a fundamental issue with a novel framework that has broad applicability. It's exactly the kind of paper that, while not addressing a specific application, provides a crucial tool for the entire field to build upon.
