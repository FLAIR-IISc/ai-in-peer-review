### Summary

This paper addresses the critical issue of model non-identifiability within the popular framework of Bayesian observer models in cognitive science and neuroscience. The authors correctly point out that behavior alone is often insufficient to uniquely determine the internal components of a Bayesian model (the prior, likelihood, and loss function), as different combinations of these elements can produce identical predictions. This "inverse problem" threatens the validity of studies that claim to recover an observer's internal representations. To address this, the paper proposes a novel computational framework to assess, *a priori*, the practical identifiability of a model's parameters for a given experimental design.

The core of their contribution is twofold: 1) They develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models that can represent a wide range of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions using mixtures of Gaussians. This class covers many standard 1D estimation tasks in psychophysics. 2) They introduce a method to map the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, assuming data is generated by a known "reference" model. This quantity, related to the KL-divergence between the models' response distributions, allows them to use MCMC to sample from an approximate expected posterior, thereby visualizing which parameters are well-constrained and which are degenerate.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies from the literature: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task. For the timing task, they show that recovering the observer's prior is surprisingly difficult and that certain experimental design choices (e.g., encouraging a MAP-like loss function) can unexpectedly worsen identifiability, while others (e.g., independently measuring motor noise) can resolve it. For the speed perception task, they show that the classic inference of a "slow-speed prior" is, in contrast, theoretically robust and identifiable within their framework.

### Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental, pervasive, and often-underappreciated problem in computational modeling of cognition. The entire research program of using Bayesian models to "read out" the brain's internal representations rests on the assumption of identifiability. This work provides the first systematic, practical tool for researchers to prospectively evaluate whether their experimental designs are sufficient to break the inherent degeneracies. This is a significant meta-scientific contribution that could improve the rigor and validity of future research in the field.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** While the general problem of model identifiability is not new, the authors' approach is highly original for this domain. The combination of a flexible yet tractable parametric model class with a principled, KL-divergence-based metric for exploring the identifiability landscape *before* data collection is a novel and powerful idea. It moves beyond post-hoc model comparison to a tool for proactive experimental design.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-executed.
    *   The model class is clever, using mixtures of Gaussians and a maximum-entropy principle to flexibly approximate a wide variety of functional forms for priors, likelihoods, and losses, while crucially maintaining computational tractability (e.g., yielding an analytical expression for the expected loss).
    *   The derivation of the expected log-likelihood in terms of KL-divergence (Eq. 12) is correct and provides a solid theoretical foundation for their method.
    *   The two case studies are well-chosen, directly relevant to prominent findings in the literature, and effectively illustrate the power of the framework to yield both cautionary and confirmatory insights.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction. The four-stage model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) is explained logically and systematically. The results are presented compellingly, showing how the framework can be used to compare concrete experimental designs and assess the robustness of previous scientific claims.

### Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer (`θ*`):** The entire analysis is conditioned on the choice of a "ground truth" reference observer, `θ*`. The conclusions about which experimental design is better are therefore contingent on this choice being accurate. The paper could be strengthened by briefly discussing the sensitivity of the results to this choice. For instance, in the timing example, how would the ranking of experimental designs change if the true reference observer had a non-Gaussian sensory noise profile or a different "true" loss function? A short discussion on this limitation would be valuable.

2.  **Assumptions of the Model Class:** While the proposed model class is flexible, it still imposes strong parametric assumptions (e.g., mixtures of two Gaussians, the specific form of the sensory mapping in Eq. 2). The conclusions are valid *within* this model class. It would be beneficial to acknowledge that if the true human observer's internal representations lie outside this class, the identifiability analysis might not hold.

3.  **Scope of Application:** The framework is currently demonstrated for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of psychophysical research, many influential studies use discrete choice paradigms (e.g., 2-Alternative Forced Choice, 2AFC). The authors briefly mention this as a future direction, but it's a key limitation. The conversion of the 2AFC speed perception study into an "equivalent estimation task" is asserted rather than justified, which slightly weakens that case study.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that addresses a critical methodological problem with a novel, principled, and practical solution. It has the potential to have a significant positive impact on the standards of computational modeling in perception and cognitive science. The technical approach is sound, the presentation is clear, and the case studies convincingly demonstrate the framework's value. The weaknesses are primarily related to the inherent limitations of any modeling framework and do not detract significantly from the core contribution. This work is a perfect fit for NIPS, sitting at the intersection of machine learning theory (identifiability, model comparison) and its application to understanding intelligence (Bayesian models of the brain). I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, critical problem of non-identifiability in a popular class of models.
*   Provides a novel and practical framework for *a priori* analysis of experimental designs.
*   Methodologically sound, elegant, and well-executed.
*   Clearly written with compelling case studies that yield non-obvious insights.
*   High potential for impact on future research practices in computational cognitive science and neuroscience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The framework's conclusions are conditional on the choice of a "reference observer" and the assumptions of the model class.
*   The current scope is limited to 1D estimation tasks.

The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution.
