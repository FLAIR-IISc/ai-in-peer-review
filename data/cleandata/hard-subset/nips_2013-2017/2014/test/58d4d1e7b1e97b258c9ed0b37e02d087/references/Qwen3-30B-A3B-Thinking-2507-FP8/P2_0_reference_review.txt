

# Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: model non-identifiability. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs, making it impossible to reliably recover internal representations from data. They present a systematic framework for assessing identifiability *a priori* before conducting experiments, using the expected log likelihood as a measure of model distinguishability. The framework is demonstrated through two case studies: interval timing and speed perception. This work builds on previous identifiability literature (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004) but specifically targets Bayesian models of perception, which have been widely used in the field but lack systematic identifiability analysis.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors derive the key equation (12) showing how expected log likelihood relates to KL divergence, providing a theoretically grounded approach to identifiability analysis. Their parametrization of the Bayesian observer model (equations 2-8) is both flexible and computationally efficient, enabling exploration of the model space. The case studies (Section 4) provide strong empirical validation of the framework, with clear demonstrations of how different experimental designs affect identifiability. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., the specific parametric form they assume) and validate their approach with a "flat prior" sanity check.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by applying identifiability analysis to a specific class of models (Bayesian observer models in perception) that have been widely used but lack systematic identifiability assessment. While identifiability analysis has been discussed in other fields (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), the authors provide a tailored approach for perception models that accounts for their unique structure. The framework's focus on *a priori* analysis before conducting experiments is novel and addresses a practical gap in experimental design.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is compelling, the technical contributions are clearly presented, and the case studies effectively illustrate the framework's utility. Figures 2 and 3 provide clear visualizations of the identifiability results. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors make excellent use of concrete examples to explain complex theoretical concepts.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental methodological issue that affects the reliability of many Bayesian models in perception research. The framework provides researchers with a tool to design better experiments that can reliably recover internal representations, potentially preventing misinterpretations of behavioral data. The case studies reveal counterintuitive findings (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function may worsen prior recovery), which could significantly impact experimental design in the field. This work has the potential to improve the rigor of Bayesian modeling in perception research.

## Weaknesses

**Scope limitations:** While the framework is well-suited for the specific class of models they consider (one-dimensional stimulus estimation with certain parametric assumptions), it may not generalize to more complex experimental paradigms (e.g., multi-dimensional stimuli, discrete choice tasks). The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 5, but it's worth noting that many perception tasks involve more complex structures.

**Assumptions in the reference observer:** The analysis relies on a specific reference observer model (θ*), which could influence the identifiability results. While the authors note this is a theoretical analysis, the framework's performance might vary depending on the true underlying model. The paper doesn't explore how robust the framework is to misspecification of the reference model.

**Computational complexity:** While the authors claim their approach is "efficient," the MCMC sampling from the approximate posterior density (Equation 13) could become computationally intensive for high-dimensional parameter spaces. The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how the computational cost scales with model complexity.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the methodology of Bayesian modeling in perception. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical framework is sound, and the empirical validation through two case studies is compelling. The paper addresses a critical gap in the field—systematic identifiability analysis for Bayesian perception models—that has been overlooked despite the widespread use of these models.

The paper's significance is particularly noteworthy. Many studies in perception have claimed to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data, but without addressing the fundamental identifiability issues. This framework provides researchers with a tool to ensure their experimental designs can actually support the conclusions they draw. The case study on interval timing revealing that imposing a narrow loss function may worsen prior recovery is a particularly valuable insight that could prevent methodological errors in future work.

The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a novel contribution to the field. While it has some limitations in scope, these are acknowledged by the authors and are reasonable for a first step in this direction.

## Recommendation

This paper should be accepted. It makes a substantive contribution to the methodology of Bayesian modeling in perception, addressing a critical issue that has been largely ignored in the literature. The framework is well-developed, the case studies provide strong empirical validation, and the paper is clearly written. The work has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of Bayesian models in perception research, making it highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental methodological issue in Bayesian perception modeling
- Provides a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear empirical validation
- Practical implications for experimental design
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Con Rejection:**
- Scope limitations for more complex experimental paradigms (acknowledged by authors)
- Some computational complexity concerns (not severe for the intended use cases)
- Relies on specific assumptions about the reference observer (acknowledged)

The strengths of this paper far outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a valuable contribution to the field that should be widely adopted by researchers in computational perception.
