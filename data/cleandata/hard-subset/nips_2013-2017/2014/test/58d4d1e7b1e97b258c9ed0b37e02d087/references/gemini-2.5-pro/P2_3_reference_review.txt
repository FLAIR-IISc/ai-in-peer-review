### Summary

This paper introduces a computational framework to assess, *a priori*, the identifiability of parameters in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly identify a critical weakness in the field: the same observed behavior can often be explained by multiple combinations of internal components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions), making it difficult to uniquely recover these representations from data. To address this, they propose a two-part solution. First, they develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models capable of representing a wide range of assumptions about sensory mapping, noise, priors, and loss functions, primarily using mixtures of Gaussians. Second, they introduce a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, given data generated by a known "reference" model. This expectation elegantly simplifies to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" based on this metric, the framework can map out the "identifiability landscape," revealing which parameters are well-constrained by a given experimental design and which are degenerate. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one in time perception, showing how different experimental designs impact the ability to recover an observer's prior, and one in speed perception, providing theoretical validation for the robustness of inferring a "slow-speed prior."

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The core theoretical contribution—linking the expected log-likelihood to the KL divergence between response distributions (Eq. 12)—is sound and provides a principled, efficient way to quantify model distinguishability without resorting to repeated, costly data simulations. The construction of the generic observer model is thoughtful and pragmatic; the use of Gaussian mixtures to parameterize the prior, likelihood, and loss function is a unifying and computationally tractable approach that covers a vast model space. The case studies are well-executed and provide compelling demonstrations. The analysis goes beyond simple confirmation, yielding non-obvious insights, such as the finding that a narrow (MAP-like) loss function could *worsen* the recovery of the prior in the time perception task due to other model degeneracies. This demonstrates the power of performing this kind of *a priori* analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, methodically explaining the fundamental non-identifiability problem. The four stages of the observer model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) are presented in a logical sequence, making a complex model understandable. The figures are illustrative, particularly Figure 2, which effectively visualizes the differences in recovered priors under various experimental designs and pinpoints the underlying parameter degeneracies. The authors do a good job of connecting their abstract framework to concrete experimental questions.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the problem of non-identifiability in cognitive modeling is not new, a systematic, quantitative framework to prospectively evaluate it for Bayesian observer models has been lacking. Previous work has often pointed out the problem or used ad-hoc methods to constrain it (e.g., Bayesian transfer). This paper provides the first general-purpose tool to diagnose and potentially fix identifiability issues at the experimental design stage. The approach of building a flexible "meta-model" and then exploring its identifiability landscape is a novel and powerful paradigm for the field. The work significantly advances the methodology for connecting Bayesian theory to experimental practice.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. The conclusions drawn from fitting Bayesian models to behavioral data are central to many modern theories in cognitive science and neuroscience. This paper provides a crucial "sanity check" tool for researchers in this area. By allowing experimenters to test whether their proposed experiment can actually disentangle the parameters they care about, this framework could prevent wasted effort and lead to more robust and reliable scientific conclusions. For example, the finding that independently measuring motor noise greatly improves prior recovery in the time perception task offers concrete, actionable advice for experimental design. This work has the potential to become a standard tool for designing psychophysical experiments and for evaluating the claims of existing studies. It directly addresses a core challenge to the interpretation of Bayesian models as process models of cognition, as highlighted by critiques from Jones & Love (2011) and Bowers & Davis (2012).

### Detailed Comments

While the paper is excellent, there are a few points that could be strengthened:

1.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently demonstrated for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. The authors suggest it can be extended to discrete-choice paradigms like 2-alternative forced choice (2AFC), which are very common in psychophysics. However, this extension is non-trivial. The mapping from a continuous posterior to a discrete choice probability introduces its own set of assumptions and potential degeneracies, which are not explored here. A more detailed discussion of the challenges and potential solutions for such an extension would strengthen the paper's claims of broad applicability.

2.  **The "Reference Observer":** The entire analysis hinges on the choice of a "reference observer" θ*. The conclusions are therefore conditional on the assumptions made about this ground truth. While the paper does explore the effect of changing some parameters of θ* (e.g., the loss function width in the MAP condition), it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the robustness of the framework's conclusions to misspecification of this reference observer. How much do the identifiability landscapes change if the true observer is slightly different from the one assumed by the experimenter?

3.  **Computational Details:** While the core ideas are clear, some implementation details are important for reproducibility. The MCMC sampling of the "approximate expected posterior density" (Eq. 13) is central to the method. Providing more detail in the main text or supplement about the sampler used, convergence diagnostics, and the computational cost would be valuable for readers who wish to adopt this framework.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Addresses a fundamental, critical, and often-overlooked problem in a widely used class of models in computational cognitive science and neuroscience.
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and computationally efficient framework to diagnose identifiability issues *before* running an experiment.
*   The methodology is elegant, and the two case studies provide compelling, non-obvious insights that offer concrete guidance for experimental design.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
*   The work has high potential impact and could become a standard methodological tool for researchers in the field.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   The current application is limited to 1D estimation tasks, and the claimed extension to other paradigms (like 2AFC) is not demonstrated.
*   The analysis is conditional on the choice of a "reference observer," and the sensitivity to this choice is not fully explored.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend acceptance. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the scope of the current demonstration rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. This paper makes a significant and original methodological contribution to a key area of research represented at NIPS. It is the kind of work that can improve the rigor and reliability of an entire subfield.
