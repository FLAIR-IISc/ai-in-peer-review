**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper addresses the critical problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. It is well-established that behaviorally-equivalent outcomes can be produced by different combinations of an observer's internal prior, likelihood, and loss function. This degeneracy complicates the scientific goal of inverting these models to reliably recover an observer's internal representations from behavioral data. The authors propose a formal, computational framework to assess the *a priori* practical identifiability of model parameters for a given experimental design, before data is collected.

The framework consists of two main components. First, the authors develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model decomposes the process into four stages: sensation (stimulus-to-measurement mapping and noise), perception (Bayesian inference with an internal prior and likelihood), decision-making (minimizing an expected loss function), and response (motor noise). Key model components like the prior, likelihood, and loss function are flexibly parameterized as mixtures of Gaussians, which critically allows for fast, analytical computation of the expected loss.

Second, the paper introduces a method to quantify identifiability. Assuming a "ground truth" reference observer model (θ*), the framework assesses how distinguishable another model (θ) would be given a finite number of experimental trials. The authors show that the expected log-likelihood of a model θ, averaged over datasets generated by θ*, is proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. This allows them to define an "approximate expected posterior density" over the parameter space, which can be explored via MCMC sampling. The marginal distributions of this posterior for each parameter serve as a measure of its identifiability: well-identified parameters will have sharp, unimodal distributions centered on the true value, while non-identifiable parameters will have broad or multimodal distributions.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies. In a time interval estimation task, the analysis reveals significant non-identifiability between sensory and motor noise parameters, which compromises the recovery of the observer's prior. The framework is used to compare several experimental designs, concluding that independently measuring motor noise is the most effective way to break this degeneracy. In a second study on speed perception, the framework is used to re-examine the classic finding of a "slow-speed prior". Here, the analysis shows that both the prior and noise characteristics are robustly identifiable, providing novel theoretical support for the original conclusions.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is theoretically sound, and the derivation linking the expected log-likelihood to the KL divergence (Eq. 12) provides a principled and efficient foundation for the analysis. The parameterization of the observer model components using mixtures of Gaussians is a key strength, affording great flexibility while maintaining computational tractability—a non-trivial achievement essential for the proposed MCMC-based exploration. The assumptions are clearly stated and are standard within the field of perceptual modeling. The claims are well-supported by the results of the two case studies, which convincingly demonstrate the framework's diagnostic power. The work represents a complete and polished contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for addressing non-identifiability is presented compellingly in the introduction. The structure of the general observer model is broken down into logical stages, and the mathematical formulation is clear. The method for assessing identifiability is also developed step-by-step, from the high-level intuition to the final implementation. The figures effectively visualize the results, for example by showing the recovered priors and the posterior distributions over key parameters.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is its originality. While the problem of model non-identifiability is known in psychology and statistics, this paper is, to my knowledge, the first to propose a systematic, efficient, and domain-specific framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis for Bayesian models of perception. Previous approaches have typically been post-hoc or have relied on generic methods that are less suited to the specific structure of these models. The synthesis of a flexible observer model with an efficient method for mapping the "identifiability landscape" is a novel and powerful idea. Using this framework to systematically compare the power of different experimental designs is a significant conceptual advance for the field.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is potentially very high. It provides a practical methodology that could, and should, be used by researchers in computational neuroscience and cognitive science to design more powerful and rigorous experiments. By allowing researchers to anticipate and mitigate parameter degeneracies before running costly experiments, this framework can lead to more reliable and replicable scientific conclusions about internal representations. The specific findings from the case studies are also significant: the cautionary results for interval timing highlight the challenges in that domain, while the validation of the speed perception findings strengthens confidence in a landmark study. This work has the potential to raise the standard of methodological rigor for computational modeling in the behavioral sciences. A potential limitation to its immediate impact is that the framework is currently restricted to 1D estimation tasks, though the authors note that it is extensible.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and critical methodological problem in a popular and influential area of research (Bayesian cognitive modeling).
*   Proposes a novel, theoretically sound, and computationally efficient framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
*   The flexible and tractable observer model is a valuable contribution in its own right.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and persuasive.
*   The case studies provide excellent demonstrations of the framework's utility and deliver non-trivial scientific insights.
*   The work has high potential significance for improving the rigor and reliability of experimental design in computational neuroscience and psychology.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The current implementation is limited in scope to one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks.
*   The complexity of the framework might present a barrier to entry for widespread adoption by experimentalists without strong computational backgrounds (though this is a challenge for the field, not a flaw of the paper).

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its limitations. It is a rigorous, original, and highly significant contribution that provides both a theoretical framework and a practical tool to address a core problem in its field.
