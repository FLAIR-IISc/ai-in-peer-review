

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a fundamental but often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: model non-identifiability. The authors correctly identify that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral responses, making it impossible to reliably recover internal representations from data. While this problem has been acknowledged in the literature (e.g., [19, 22]), the authors present the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis in Bayesian perceptual models.

The paper's core contribution is a general framework for assessing identifiability before conducting experiments. The authors introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model that can represent a wide range of sensory estimation tasks while maintaining computational efficiency. They then develop a method to efficiently explore the identifiability landscape using KL divergence between response probability densities. This approach allows researchers to evaluate how different experimental designs affect the ability to recover specific model components, such as priors or noise characteristics.

The paper demonstrates the framework through two compelling case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. In the first case study, the authors show that standard experimental designs may fail to recover priors due to parameter degeneracies, and they identify that measuring motor noise parameters significantly improves identifiability. Surprisingly, they find that experimental manipulations designed to make the loss function more "Bayesian" (closer to MAP) can actually worsen recovery of priors. In the second case study, they demonstrate that the inference of slow-speed priors in speed perception is generally robust, providing theoretical validation for prior findings.

The paper makes several strong contributions to the field. First, it tackles a critical problem that has been recognized but not systematically addressed in Bayesian perception modeling. Second, it provides a practical, computational framework that researchers can immediately apply to their own experimental designs. Third, the case studies yield non-obvious insights that could directly impact experimental design in perception research. The framework's ability to rank experimental designs by their ability to resolve parameter degeneracies is particularly valuable.

I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in the field. While previous work has acknowledged identifiability issues (e.g., [12, 19]), the authors provide the first systematic methodology to address them. The framework is general enough to apply to many perceptual tasks while being specific enough to yield concrete recommendations for experimental design. The paper's theoretical foundation is sound, and the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical derivations. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the framework's current restriction to one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks, and suggest potential extensions (e.g., to discrete-choice paradigms).

Weaknesses are minor. The framework is currently limited to specific perceptual tasks, though the authors note how it could be extended. The case studies, while compelling, could be strengthened by additional examples from different domains. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's substantial contribution.

This paper stands out for its bold approach to addressing a fundamental problem in the field. It's not merely a methodological refinement but offers a new perspective on experimental design in Bayesian perception modeling. The authors don't just identify a problem—they provide a practical solution that could change how researchers approach Bayesian modeling of perception.

The significance of this work is substantial. It directly addresses a critical vulnerability in Bayesian models of perception that has been acknowledged but not systematically resolved. By providing a framework to assess identifiability a priori, the paper enables researchers to design better experiments and interpret their results more accurately. This has the potential to improve the validity of many conclusions drawn from Bayesian models in perception research.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception with a novel, practical framework. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility through compelling case studies that yield non-obvious insights about experimental design. The paper is theoretically sound, well-written, and offers immediate value to researchers in the field.

The paper's novelty and significance outweigh its minor limitations. It represents a substantial advance in the methodology for Bayesian models of perception, providing a tool that researchers can use to improve their experimental designs and strengthen their conclusions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
