Summary
The paper proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in psychophysics. The core idea is to define a broad, parametric class of observer models (mapping stimulus to internal measurement via a monotonic transform; likelihoods and loss functions as mixtures of Gaussians; priors as a Gaussian lattice mixture) and to quantify identifiability by the expected log-likelihood of a candidate parameter vector θ when data are generated by a reference θ*. This reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response densities across stimuli (Eq. 12). The authors then define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) and sample it via MCMC to explore degeneracies and rank experimental designs. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (showing poor identifiability of priors under standard designs; improvement when motor noise is independently measured; and degradation under narrow loss/MAP) and speed perception (recovering a slow-speed prior and noise parameters reasonably well).

Relation to prior work
The identifiability issue in Bayesian observer models is well-known (e.g., prior–likelihood–loss tradeoffs; cited [19]). Using expected log-likelihood/cross-entropy as a measure is standard, and the connection to KL divergence is classical. The work is related to broader literatures on model distinguishability (Navarro, Pitt, Myung), Bayesian experimental design and expected information gain (e.g., Lindley 1956; adaptive design optimization in cognitive modeling by Myung/Pitt/Navarro; psychophysics design by Kujala & Wichmann; neuroscience design by Lewi et al.). The paper does not engage deeply with these established methodologies, nor does it compare to Fisher Information approaches or BOED-based design optimization.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical problem: recovering priors/likelihoods/losses from behavior is notoriously ill-posed; pre-experiment identifiability analysis is valuable.
- Provides a unifying parametric observer family with computational conveniences (mixture-of-Gaussians structure) and a closed-form expected loss, enabling exploration of large parameter spaces.
- The KL-based expected support is principled and interpretable; dependence on Ntr and stimulus frequencies is explicit.
- Case studies illustrate nontrivial findings (e.g., narrow loss can harm identifiability; measuring motor noise helps), and a scenario (speed perception) where recovery is relatively robust.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. The identifiability metric is essentially expected cross-entropy; similar criteria are standard in model comparison and experimental design. The paper does not compare against established design criteria (expected information gain, Fisher Information/Z-optimality) or adaptive design methods.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions: two-component Gaussian mixtures for both likelihood and loss; lattice-Gaussian prior parameterization; a fixed monotonic transform f(s) with particular form. Conclusions about identifiability could hinge on these modeling choices; no systematic sensitivity analysis is provided (e.g., different M, alternative bases, different f(s)).
- The “approximate expected posterior” is not a posterior over θ for finite datasets but a functional of θ under the assumed θ*. This assesses average-case distinguishability but not variability across datasets. There is no systematic finite-sample study (e.g., distributions of MLE/MAP estimates over repeated datasets) or coverage/power analysis.
- Computational details are underspecified. The parameter space is high-dimensional (including ≥15 prior weights plus multiple nuisance parameters). It is unclear how presp(r|s; θ) and the KL integrals are computed efficiently given nonlinearity (f−1) and lapses, what samplers are used, convergence diagnostics, and runtimes. Reproducibility may be challenging without more detail and code.
- The mapping of 2AFC speed tasks to an estimation framework is asserted rather than rigorously justified or validated on real data. The second case study remains entirely simulated, and the claimed robustness would be more convincing with empirical corroboration.
- Some clarity issues: minor notation inconsistencies (e.g., “w1” seemingly meaning ρ1); “maximum-entropy” construction for mixtures under moment and unimodality constraints is nonstandard and deferred to the supplement.

Quality
Technically mostly sound in high-level derivations (Eq. 12), but critical computational steps and robustness analyses are missing. Claims about design ranking are supported only under specific parametric assumptions and expectation over datasets; no baseline comparisons to EIG/Fisher-based design. No real-data validation.

Clarity
Generally readable, but dense. Important algorithmic details (integration, MCMC, constraints on weights, priors over parameters) are deferred. Some notation issues and typos.

Originality
Moderate. The parametric observer class is a pragmatic synthesis. The identifiability criterion is standard. The framing as an a priori mapping is useful but not conceptually new relative to existing BOED/ADO and model distinguishability literatures.

Significance
Potentially useful to researchers designing psychophysical experiments with Bayesian observer models, but practical impact is limited without stronger validation, comparisons to established design methods, and real-data demonstrations.

Questions/clarifications
- How are the prior weights constrained (nonnegativity/sum-to-one), and what priors/regularization are used on θ during MCMC?
- How are KL integrals computed numerically, and what is the computational cost per θ evaluation? Any gradient use?
- Sensitivity to M (number of Gaussian basis functions) and to the choice of f(s)?
- How variable are identifiability conclusions across datasets (beyond expectation)? Any empirical distribution over parameter recovery errors?
- Can the authors compare their design rankings to EIG/Fisher-based rankings?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide comparisons to Fisher Information and expected information gain design criteria; consider BOED/ADO baselines.
- Include finite-sample parameter recovery studies (simulate many datasets) to complement expected log-likelihood analysis.
- Add sensitivity analyses over basis size, mixture complexity, and alternative transformations.
- Validate on at least one real dataset, showing that a design predicted to be superior yields better recovery in practice.
- Clarify algorithmic details and release code.

Pros
- Addresses an important methodological issue.
- Principled, interpretable identifiability metric.
- Useful illustrative case studies and some non-obvious insights.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established design/identifiability methods.
- Strong parametric assumptions; limited robustness analysis.
- No empirical validation; missing comparisons to baselines.
- Sparse computational details; reproducibility concerns.

Overall evaluation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the framework is promising, but the paper relies on strong modeling assumptions, lacks comparison to established experimental design methods, and presents only simulated validation under expected-case analysis. Strengthening empirical and methodological validation would significantly improve the contribution.
