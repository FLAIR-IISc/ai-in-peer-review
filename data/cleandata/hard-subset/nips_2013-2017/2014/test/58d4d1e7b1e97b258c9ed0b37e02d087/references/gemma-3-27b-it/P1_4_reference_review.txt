## Peer Review for “A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception”

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions within a Bayesian framework. They present a novel framework to *a priori* assess the identifiability of Bayesian observer models, allowing researchers to systematically investigate and potentially mitigate this issue through experimental design. The framework centers around a flexible, parameterized observer model and utilizes KL divergence to quantify the potential for alternative models to explain observed data. They demonstrate its application to two established psychophysical paradigms: time perception and speed perception, offering concrete insights into experimental design choices.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a long line of work applying Bayesian decision theory (BDT) to perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3]). It acknowledges the success of these models in explaining behavioral data, but critically examines the limitations arising from the inherent ill-posedness of inferring internal representations from behavior [17, 18, 19].  The work differentiates itself from purely theoretical discussions by offering a practical, computational framework for *testing* identifiability before data collection.  It draws connections to broader issues of model non-identifiability in psychology [22] and leverages existing techniques like KL divergence [28] in a novel application tailored to Bayesian observer models. The analysis and method presented can be seen as a generalization and systematization of earlier attempts to address this issue through constraints on model shape [12, 19].

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial, often-ignored issue in the field of Bayesian cognitive modeling. Addressing model identifiability is essential for drawing meaningful conclusions about underlying cognitive processes.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a systematic and quantitative approach to assessing identifiability, going beyond qualitative arguments.
* **Flexible Model:** The developed observer model is remarkably flexible, encompassing a wide range of perceptual tasks and prior/likelihood shapes with a relatively compact parameterization. This makes the framework broadly applicable.
* **Practical Application:**  The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used to evaluate and improve experimental designs.  The results offer actionable insights, such as highlighting the potential pitfalls of imposing narrow loss functions.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized.  The mathematical derivations are well-motivated, and the use of a figure to illustrate the observer model is helpful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors emphasize the computational efficiency of their approach, which is critical for exploring the high-dimensional parameter space.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The framework relies on several assumptions, such as the parametric forms of the sensory mapping (Eq. 2) and loss functions (Eq. 6). While these are motivated by existing literature, their influence on the identifiability analysis should be further discussed. The choice of Gaussian mixtures for likelihoods and priors is also a potential limitation.
* **KL Divergence as a Proxy:**  Using KL divergence as a proxy for posterior probability is a reasonable simplification, but it’s important to acknowledge the limitations. It does not fully capture the complexities of Bayesian inference with finite data.
* **Reference Observer Dependence:** The results are inherently tied to the chosen reference observer (θ*).  The sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to different choices of θ* should be explored more thoroughly.  
* **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** While the case studies are illustrative, they are relatively constrained.  Expanding the application to other perceptual domains (e.g., visual illusions, multisensory integration) would strengthen the paper.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper would benefit from providing more details on the MCMC sampling procedure used to approximate the posterior density (Eq. 13). Details about convergence diagnostics and sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in the results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
* The proposed framework is novel, well-motivated, and potentially impactful.
* The case studies provide concrete examples and actionable insights.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The framework relies on simplifying assumptions that may limit its generality.
* The use of KL divergence as a proxy for posterior probability is a simplification.
* The limited scope of the case studies restricts the generalizability of the findings.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian cognitive modeling. While the framework relies on certain assumptions and the case studies could be expanded, the authors have identified a critical issue and proposed a practical, well-motivated solution. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of different choices for the reference observer (θ*) on the identifiability results.
* **Assumption Discussion:**  Expand the discussion of the assumptions underlying the framework, particularly regarding the parametric forms of the sensory mapping and loss functions.
* **Implementation Details:** Provide more details about the MCMC sampling procedure used to approximate the posterior density.
* **Wider Application:** Briefly discuss the potential application of the framework to other perceptual domains.
