- Paper proposes a unified, flexible observer model for 1D estimation with parametric sensory mapping, mixture-form likelihoods/priors/losses, lapses, and motor noise.
- Derives an efficient a priori identifiability analysis by showing expected log-likelihood equals −Ntr Σ P_i KL between response distributions (plus constant).
- Introduces sampling from an “approximate expected posterior” to map practical non-identifiability and compare experimental designs.
- Two case studies (interval timing, speed perception) demonstrate identifiable confounds (sensory vs motor noise; weak loss-width identifiability) and provide experimental guidance.
- Core derivation and expected-support formulation are correct and appropriate for a priori identifiability.
- Observer model engineering (mixture choices) makes expected loss tractable and exploration feasible.
- Case studies are well-designed and yield interpretable, nontrivial conclusions relevant to practitioners.
- Framework relies on strong parametric assumptions (two-Gaussian mixtures, fixed M=15 prior basis, specific f(s) mapping).
- “Local maximum entropy” construction for mixture parameters is under-specified in the main text.
- Analysis centers on practical identifiability relative to a single θ*; sensitivity to θ* is discussed qualitatively but not systematically quantified.
- Mapping of 2AFC to estimation is assumed but not detailed or validated.
- MCMC diagnostics, computational costs, and sensitivity to choices (M, mixture count, stimulus discretization, Ntr) are not reported.
- No explicit connection to Fisher Information/local identifiability or to optimal design (expected information gain) is provided.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, but key implementation details are deferred to the supplement and references have incomplete bibliographic entries.
- Suggested clarity fixes: sketch derivation of Eq.12, clarify local maximum-entropy criterion, report MCMC settings/convergence/runtimes, and add ablations on M and component counts.
- Novelty lies in combining known ingredients into a concrete, tractable parameterization for mapping identifiability in realistic perceptual tasks.
- Practical significance: framework can guide experiment design and interpretation in Bayesian perception, with immediate applicability to timing and speed tasks.
- Scientific significance: offers a quantitative method to reason about degeneracy and compare designs; extension to adaptive design and other paradigms is feasible.
- Key weaknesses: parametric rigidity, limited robustness analyses to model mis-specification, and missing formal local identifiability measures.
- Recommended improvements: test robustness to mis-specification, compute Fisher Information/Hessian at θ*, link KL expected support to Bayesian design, detail 2AFC conversion, and validate at least one real dataset prediction.
- Reporting needs: include code, full supplement derivations, MCMC diagnostics, and additional sensitivity analyses.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a principled, efficient method, actionable insights, and solid derivations.
- Cons for acceptance: heavy parametric assumptions, missing methodological details and diagnostics, limited formal ties to optimal design, and scope limited to 1D estimation.
- Recommendation: Lean accept.
