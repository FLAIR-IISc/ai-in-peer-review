Summary
The paper tackles a key problem in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions when inferring them from behavior. The authors build a broad, parametric observer model for 1D estimation tasks: (i) a monotonic stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s), (ii) a measurement model as a two-Gaussian mixture with moments matched via a maximum-entropy heuristic, (iii) a prior represented as a mixture of Gaussians on a lattice, (iv) a loss function represented as a mixture of inverted Gaussians (again moment-matched), with occasional lapses and motor/report noise. They propose an a priori identifiability metric: the expected log-likelihood of model θ given data from θ*, which reduces to a stimulus-averaged sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions. They define E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(expected log-likelihood) and sample it by MCMC to explore identifiability as a function of experimental design. Two case studies are presented: (1) interval timing (priors are hard to recover unless motor parameters are known; narrowing the loss can worsen identifiability), and (2) speed perception (the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters are recovered more reliably).

Relation to prior work
The modeling style builds on established Bayesian observer paradigms in perception [e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert; Houlsby et al.]. The identifiability focus relates to broader model-recovery and identifiability literature in cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung), as well as to optimal experimental design and identifiability in dynamical/biophysical models (e.g., Fisher information, D-/KL-optimal designs, structural/practical identifiability). The KL-based expected log-likelihood identity is standard, and the use of it as a proxy for model evidence is common.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and often underappreciated issue (identifiability) in Bayesian perception models with an explicit, quantitative framework.
- Provides a unified parametric observer family with analytic structure enabling efficient computation of response distributions and expected losses.
- The KL-based criterion is principled and naturally connects dataset size, stimulus distribution, and discriminability.
- Case studies surface nontrivial design insights (e.g., the detrimental effect of narrower loss on prior recovery in timing; the need to fix/report noise parameters).
- The framework can, in principle, guide experimental design by ranking candidate designs before data collection.

Weaknesses
- Validation is limited. The evaluation is confined to simulated case studies within the same parametric family; there is no model recovery against alternative observer classes, nor comparisons to established identifiability diagnostics (Fisher information, profile likelihoods, posterior contraction across simulated datasets, or structural identifiability analyses). The proposed E(θ|θ*) is not calibrated against actual posterior model recovery from finite datasets.
- Novelty is moderate. The central identifiability criterion (expected log-likelihood equals a sum of KL divergences) is standard. The main contribution is packaging this with a particular observer parametrization.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions (two-Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss, lattice-mixture priors, the log/power mapping f). It is unclear how sensitive conclusions are to misspecification (e.g., non-unimodal posteriors, heavier-tailed measurement noise, different loss classes). Identifiability results may not generalize beyond this family.
- Experimental design angle stops at qualitative ranking. Given Eq. 12, one could optimize stimulus distributions (and feedback structures) to maximize expected KL (or information) subject to constraints; no such algorithmic design or quantitative optimization is presented.
- Clarity and completeness depend heavily on the Supplement. Key choices (maximum-entropy construction, unimodality constraints, MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, computational cost) are not fully justified in the main text.
- The speed example translates a classic 2AFC setting into an estimation framework; potential mismatches between task structures and response distributions are not discussed, and no check on robustness to that mismatch is shown.
- Mixture models introduce label-switching and local-mode issues; the paper does not discuss identifiability within the mixture parameterization itself.

Quality
Technically competent but lacking rigorous validation against competing methods and across model classes. The KL derivation is fine; the core modeling choices are plausible but require stronger justification and sensitivity analyses. The empirical claims are based on simulations within the proposed class.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but dense. Many essential details are deferred to the Supplement. Better exposition of assumptions, algorithmic steps, and diagnostics would help. References appear incomplete in the provided text; ensure accurate citations.

Originality
Moderate. The combination and instantiation are useful for this domain, but the identifiability criterion and much of the modeling scaffold are known. The paper would benefit from clearer differentiation relative to prior identifiability/model-recovery and experimental design literature.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners designing Bayesian perception experiments. However, without stronger validation, broader generality, and design optimization, the immediate impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Calibrate E(θ|θ*) by full model-recovery experiments: simulate many finite datasets, infer θ with standard methods, and report recovery rates/coverage as a function of Ntr; compare to Fisher information and profile likelihood diagnostics.
- Provide sensitivity analyses to misspecification: vary number of mixture components, prior basis size M, non-Gaussian/heavy-tailed likelihoods, alternative loss families; report how conclusions change.
- Introduce principled experimental design: choose stimuli and conditions to maximize expected KL/information for target parameters under constraints; compare to baselines.
- Include runtime, scalability, and MCMC convergence diagnostics; release code.
- Discuss mixture identifiability (label switching) and constraints used to mitigate it.
- Justify the maximum-entropy constructions and show that they do not unduly restrict realism.
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and test robustness to task mismatch.

Pros
- Important problem; principled KL-based framework
- Flexible analytic observer family
- Useful qualitative insights for two canonical tasks

Cons
- Limited novelty and validation; no comparison to established identifiability/OED methods
- Strong parametric assumptions; unclear robustness
- No algorithmic design optimization; heavy reliance on Supplement

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With stronger validation against baselines, principled design optimization, and robustness analyses, this could become a valuable contribution. As it stands, the methodological soundness and demonstrated generality fall short of NeurIPS standards.
