

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper proposes a systematic framework for assessing identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception research, addressing a critical gap in the literature regarding the non-uniqueness of inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations. The authors develop a flexible class of observer models and introduce a method to explore identifiability a priori through expected log-likelihood analysis. The framework is demonstrated through two case studies in temporal and speed perception.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine and significant problem in Bayesian modeling of perception - the non-identifiability of model components due to degeneracy in the inverse mapping. The authors provide a theoretically sound approach to address this issue, with clear mathematical formulation and two well-chosen case studies. The framework could substantially improve experimental design in perception research, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The paper also thoughtfully discusses limitations and potential extensions of the approach.

## Weaknesses

Despite its promising contribution, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation issues that undermine its quality and credibility:

1. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains inconsistent mathematical notation throughout. For example, equation (2) has inconsistent spacing: "s0 d √ e t−B A − 1" should be "s_0 \sqrt[d]{e^{(t-B)/A} - 1}". In equation (3), the variance terms are written as "σ21" instead of "σ_1^2", which is confusing. The paper also uses inconsistent spacing around operators (e.g., "argmin ŝ ∫" instead of "argmin_{\hat{s}} \int").

2. **Technical inaccuracies**: The paper claims to use "symmetric KL-divergence" in section 4.1, but KL divergence is inherently asymmetric. This fundamental error casts doubt on the analytical rigor of the paper. Additionally, the paper states "the degeneracy is not surprising, as both sensory and motor noise... are approximately Gaussian" but provides no mathematical justification for this claim.

3. **Missing details**: The paper repeatedly references "Supplementary Material" without providing it, making it impossible to verify key claims. For instance, the paper states "the remaining degrees of freedom are fixed by picking a distribution that satisfies unimodality and locally maximizes the differential entropy" but doesn't specify how this is achieved. The derivation of the analytical expression for expected loss (mentioned in section 2.3) is also missing.

4. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper claims "a single inverted Gaussian already allows to express a large variety of losses" but provides no evidence for this assertion. Similarly, the claim that "the model can also cover angular variables such as orientation (for small errors)" is made without mathematical justification.

5. **Methodological issues**: The paper uses the expected log-likelihood as a proxy for identifiability (equation 13), but this is not a standard metric for identifiability analysis. The paper cites [23] as using a similar approach, but the cited work uses posterior probabilities rather than log likelihoods.

6. **Formatting issues in references**: The reference list contains incomplete entries (e.g., "Vision Res, , , " with missing volume and page numbers), suggesting poor attention to detail.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in Bayesian modeling of perception, the numerous technical inconsistencies, mathematical errors, and lack of detail significantly undermine its quality. The fundamental error in using "symmetric KL-divergence" is particularly concerning, as it suggests a lack of understanding of basic information theory. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues, including providing the missing derivations and correcting the mathematical notation.

The framework itself has potential value, but the current presentation fails to demonstrate its rigor. The case studies are well-chosen but their interpretation is compromised by the methodological issues. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about the framework's utility.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper's technical shortcomings and presentation errors are too significant to overlook. While the problem addressed is important and the framework conceptually promising, the current version fails to demonstrate the necessary rigor expected for NIPS. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to fix the mathematical errors, provide missing derivations, and improve the presentation before being reconsidered for publication.
