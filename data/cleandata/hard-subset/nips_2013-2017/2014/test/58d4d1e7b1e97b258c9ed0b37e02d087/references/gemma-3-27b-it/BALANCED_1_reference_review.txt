This paper addresses a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: identifiability. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inverting behavioral data to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) within a Bayesian framework. They propose a framework to systematically analyze a priori identifiability for a specific class of observer models, aiming to improve experimental design and ultimately increase confidence in recovered internal representations. The core contribution lies in providing a methodology to assess which experimental manipulations are most likely to resolve the degeneracy, rather than simply assuming constraints.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the problem and its importance. The proposed observer model is a sensible generalization of previous work, offering a good balance between flexibility and computational tractability. The mathematical formulation, while dense, appears sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail (with references to supplementary material) to allow for replication. The application of the framework to two well-established psychophysical tasks – time perception and speed perception – strengthens the contribution by demonstrating its practical utility and yielding interesting, if sometimes counterintuitive, results. For example, the finding that imposing a narrow loss function may hinder prior recovery in the time perception task is noteworthy.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of their chosen observer model class, the scope is still relatively restrictive. The assumed form of the sensory mapping (Eq. 2) and the use of Gaussian mixtures for likelihoods and loss functions may not be appropriate for all perceptual tasks. It would be valuable to discuss the potential impact of these assumptions on the generality of the findings. Furthermore, the model relies on the somewhat strong assumption of unimodal sensory distributions. 

Second, the connection between the theoretical framework and concrete experimental design recommendations could be strengthened. While the authors compare different setups (BSL, SRT, etc.), the interpretation of the results is sometimes vague. For instance, what specific changes to the experimental protocol would be necessary to implement the “MTR” condition (independent measurement of motor noise)? Providing more concrete guidance on how to translate the identifiability analysis into actionable experimental choices would enhance the paper's impact. The discussion of the MAP condition also feels somewhat disconnected. Why would imposing a narrower loss function *worsen* inference? This needs a deeper explanation.

Third, the treatment of lapses (λ) feels slightly tacked on. The authors acknowledge its presence but don’t deeply explore its impact on identifiability, or how it interacts with other parameters. Given the sensitivity of Bayesian models to prior specification, the potential influence of lapse rate on the recovered parameters warrants more investigation.

Finally, the presentation could benefit from improved clarity. The mathematical notation is occasionally cumbersome, and the flow of the text sometimes feels disjointed. While the figures are helpful, they could be more visually appealing and better integrated into the narrative.  For example, Figure 2 presents a lot of information, making it difficult to quickly grasp the key findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Addresses a fundamental problem:** The paper tackles a crucial issue in Bayesian modeling of perception – the lack of identifiability – with a novel and systematic approach.
*   **Well-defined framework:** The authors provide a mathematically sound and computationally tractable framework for analyzing identifiability.
*   **Demonstrated utility:** The application to time and speed perception tasks demonstrates the practical value of the framework.
*   **Potential for impact:** The findings could significantly improve the design of psychophysical experiments and increase confidence in recovered internal representations.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Limited generality:** The observer model class is relatively restrictive and may not apply to all perceptual tasks.
*   **Weak connection to experimental design:** The translation of the identifiability analysis into concrete experimental recommendations could be strengthened.
*   **Clarity and presentation:** The paper could benefit from improved clarity and a more cohesive presentation.
*   **Depth of analysis:** Some aspects, such as the role of lapses, are not explored in sufficient detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, despite its weaknesses. The problem addressed is important, the proposed framework is novel and well-developed, and the results are intriguing. However, the limitations regarding generality, experimental guidance, and clarity prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance.  

**Recommendation:**  Reject with encouragement to revise. 

**Suggestions for Revision:**

1.  **Expand discussion of model limitations:** Explicitly address the potential impact of the chosen model assumptions (sensory mapping, Gaussian mixtures) on the generality of the findings.
2.  **Strengthen connection to experimental design:** Provide more concrete and actionable recommendations for translating the identifiability analysis into experimental choices.
3.  **Investigate the role of lapses:** Explore the impact of lapse rate on identifiability and its interaction with other parameters.
4.  **Improve clarity and presentation:** Streamline the mathematical notation, improve the flow of the text, and enhance the visual appeal of the figures.
5.  **Provide a more detailed explanation of the findings:** Specifically, elaborate on why imposing a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery in the time perception task.
