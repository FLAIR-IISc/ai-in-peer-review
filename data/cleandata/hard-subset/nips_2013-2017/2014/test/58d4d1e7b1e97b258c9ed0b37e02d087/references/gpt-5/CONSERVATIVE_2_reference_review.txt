Summary
The paper addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models for psychophysics. It proposes (i) a parametric, computationally efficient observer class for 1D estimation tasks (sensation via a monotone transform and a moment-matched two-Gaussian mixture; prior as a Gaussian lattice mixture; loss as a mixture of inverted Gaussians; lapses and response noise), and (ii) an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log likelihood of data generated by a reference observer θ* under a candidate θ. The expectation yields a sum of KL divergences between predicted response densities, and this defines an “approximate expected posterior” over θ used to explore identifiability via MCMC. Two case studies (interval timing and speed perception) illustrate design comparisons and point to parameter degeneracies (e.g., sensory vs motor noise in timing), and to relatively robust recovery of a slow-speed prior in the speed task.

Relation to prior work
The degeneracy of priors/likelihoods/losses is well known in Bayesian perception; the paper usefully re-emphasizes it. The identifiability objective—expected log-likelihood difference—is standard information-theoretic ground (e.g., Lindley’s expected information gain; KL-based design; Fisher-information/D-optimal design). The cognitive modeling literature on model identifiability and parameter recovery (e.g., Myung, Pitt, Navarro) is cited, but there is little engagement with the extensive Bayesian experimental design and frequentist optimal design literature (Lindley; MacKay; Chaloner & Verdinelli; Atkinson & Donev), profile likelihood and structural identifiability (Bellman & Åström), or “sloppiness” analyses (Transtrum et al.). The mixture parameterizations and “maximum-entropy” constraints are nonstandard choices that are not clearly tied to prior psychophysics practice.

Strengths
- Important problem: identifiability of Bayesian observer components is a real concern in the field.
- Clear formalization: expected log-evidence under θ* reduces to a KL sum, giving a sensible, data-size-dependent identifiability objective.
- A unified observer class with analytic computations (mixtures leading to fast expected loss evaluation) is practically useful.
- Case studies highlight plausible degeneracies (sensory vs motor noise) and provide actionable design advice (measure motor noise separately).
- The speed-perception example supports prior empirical findings, lending face validity.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Methodological novelty is limited: the core identifiability criterion (expected log-likelihood / KL) and the idea of pre-experimental design evaluation are well established; there is no comparison to standard tools (Fisher information, expected information gain, D-/A-optimal design), nor to profile likelihood or structural identifiability methods.
- Validation is weak: results are entirely synthetic and within-class; there is no parameter-recovery on simulated datasets (to check that the “expected posterior” predicts actual posteriors), no runtime/convergence diagnostics, and no comparisons against established baselines for identifiability assessment.
- Modeling choices seem ad hoc and under-justified: the “local maximum-entropy” selection of two-Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss functions is nonstandard; uniqueness, stability, and sensitivity are not demonstrated. The restriction to t̂−t losses and 1D tasks limits claims of generality.
- The mapping from 2AFC to an equivalent estimation paradigm is asserted rather than derived; conclusions about robustness in the speed task depend on this nontrivial conversion.
- Over-optimism risk: the reference and inference models share the same parametric family, potentially inflating identifiability; limited exploration of model mismatch (e.g., different number of mixture components, non-log transformations).
- Design analysis is qualitative: no quantitative optimal design procedure or sensitivity analysis (e.g., gradients of the expected log-likelihood), and little insight into sample complexity beyond heuristic trends.
- Reproducibility gaps: MCMC details, priors over parameters, convergence criteria, and code availability are not described. Some reference entries appear incomplete.

Quality
Technically competent in deriving the expected log-likelihood objective; the observer implementation appears efficient. However, claims about broad utility and design guidance are under-evidenced given the absence of comparisons to established design/identifiability methods and lack of dataset-based validation. The “maximum-entropy mixture” constructions need more rigorous justification.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and structured. Key derivations and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement; several choices (e.g., fixing A,B to map to [−1,1], selection of mixture parameters under moment constraints) should be explained more clearly. The case studies are easy to follow, but more quantitative summaries and diagnostics would help.

Originality
The main ingredients (KL-based expected evidence, pre-experimental identifiability assessment) are not novel. The specific observer parameterization and the use of mixtures throughout may be a practical contribution, but again incremental.

Significance
The topic is significant; a robust, validated framework could impact experimental design in psychophysics and beyond. In its current form, the contribution feels preliminary: useful as a cautionary illustration, but not yet a standard one would rely on without stronger validation and benchmarking.

Suggestions for improvement
- Benchmark against Fisher information-based local identifiability and Bayesian design criteria (expected information gain), including quantitative design optimization and sample-complexity curves.
- Validate the “expected posterior” by repeated parameter-recovery fits on simulated datasets; report calibration of credible intervals and coverage.
- Provide MCMC specifics, priors, computational cost, convergence checks, and code.
- Analyze sensitivity to model class choices: number of mixture components, M in the prior lattice, alternative f(s), non-Gaussian motor noise, non-translation-invariant losses.
- Derive or cite a rigorous mapping from 2AFC to estimation for the speed task.
- Compare to profile likelihood and structural identifiability; discuss when the KL-based criterion agrees/disagrees.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-discussed issue (identifiability) with practical implications.
- Provides a unified, efficient observer family and a clear identifiability objective.
- Case studies surface nontrivial design insights.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established experimental design/identifiability frameworks.
- Insufficient validation and lack of comparisons to standard baselines.
- Heavy reliance on within-class synthetic tests; unclear robustness under model mismatch.
- Missing algorithmic and reproducibility details; some modeling assumptions are ad hoc.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper raises valuable points and has potential, but needs stronger methodological grounding, benchmarking against established identifiability/design methods, and rigorous validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
