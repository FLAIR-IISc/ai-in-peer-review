Summary
The paper proposes a framework to assess a priori practical identifiability of parameters in Bayesian observer models for 1D estimation tasks. The authors define a flexible parametric observer class covering sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages. Key modeling choices include (i) a monotone sensory mapping f(s) to an internal measurement space where likelihoods are translation-invariant; (ii) a maximum-entropy-inspired parameterization of both the measurement distribution and the internal likelihood as two-Gaussian mixtures matched to the first four moments; (iii) a Gaussian-mixture parameterization of the prior on a fixed lattice; (iv) a loss function expressed as a mixture of inverted Gaussians; and (v) lapse and reporting noise models. For identifiability, they derive the expected log-likelihood of a model θ when data are generated by θ*, yielding a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli. They then define E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩) and sample with MCMC to map identifiability. Two case studies—interval timing and speed perception—illustrate how the approach can rank experimental designs and diagnose parameter degeneracies; e.g., in timing, priors are poorly recovered in a baseline design but are improved if motor noise is known; in speed, the slow-speed prior exponent can be recovered robustly.

Strengths
- Addresses a real, under-discussed problem: practical non-identifiability in Bayesian observer modeling, with a principled KL-based criterion.
- Provides a relatively general observer class unified under a single parameterization and intended for efficient computation.
- Clear conceptual link between expected log-likelihood under θ* and KL-divergence, enabling a tractable identifiability proxy that scales with trial count.
- Demonstrates use for design comparison (e.g., effect of learning conditions, motor noise measurement) and highlights non-obvious findings (e.g., narrow loss can hurt prior recovery).
- Sensible separation of “true” generative p and observer-internal q elements, with options to couple/decouple them.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/methodological
- Maximum-entropy claims are underspecified. Within the two-Gaussian mixture family, the paper states parameters are chosen to “locally maximize differential entropy” under unimodality while matching moments. This requires precise constraints: not all (σ, γ, κ) tuples are feasible for a unimodal two-Gaussian mixture. The domain of admissible skewness/kurtosis and the optimization/verification of unimodality are not specified (reproducibility risk). Please formalize feasibility conditions and the optimization procedure, or provide proofs/algorithms ensuring unimodality and convergence.
- Loss parameterization. The inverted Gaussian-mixture loss is convenient, but the paper assumes a unique minimum at zero. Mixtures can be non-convex and multimodal; conditions on mixture parameters to ensure a single global minimum at zero are not given. This directly affects existence/uniqueness of the Bayes estimator t*(x). Provide sufficient conditions (e.g., constraints on component means/weights/variances) and how they’re enforced during sampling.
- Mapping f(s). A and B are chosen “without loss of generality” to map the experimental stimulus set into [-1,1], while s0 and d are inferred. Since A and B depend on s0, d and the discrete stimulus set, clarify how A, B are recomputed during inference and how this affects identifiability and comparability across θ. Is the Jacobian handled consistently in all transformations?
- Lapse model. Lapses draw responses from the (task-space) prior. This can confound prior inference and identifiability (lapses inflate mass where prior is large). Alternative lapse models (e.g., uniform over response range, broad Gaussian) should be analyzed; sensitivity to lapse assumptions should be reported.
- “Approximate expected posterior.” Equation (13) is an exact expected log-likelihood exponentiated (up to an additive constant and an omitted parameter prior), not a posterior. The naming E(θ|θ*) is potentially misleading; please clarify terminology and whether a prior over θ is used in MCMC.
- Practical MCMC details and scalability. The parameter space is high-dimensional (including 15 prior weights, multiple moment parameters, mapping, lapse, and motor/reporting noise). No chain lengths, diagnostics, runtime, proposal details, or failure cases are reported. Without this, claims of efficiency are unconvincing. Please report convergence diagnostics, runtimes, and ablations (e.g., effect of M).
- Design analysis. The KL-based criterion naturally scales with Ntr, but no exploration of identifiability vs Ntr, number of stimulus levels Nexp, or stimulus placement is shown. This would strengthen the design utility.
- 2AFC-to-estimation conversion (speed case) is asserted but not specified. Since conclusions rest on this mapping, the derivation and assumptions should be in the main text or succinctly summarized.

Theoretical clarity
- Degeneracy argument (Eq. 1). The scaling by φi(s) with ∏ φi(s) = c is fine, but it implicitly allows the loss to depend on s beyond t̂ − t; later, the model restricts L to depend only on t̂ − t. Please reconcile these assumptions and clarify the scope of the degeneracy relative to your modeling restrictions.
- Structural vs practical identifiability. The paper frames practical identifiability via KL, but some discussion contrasting with local/structural identifiability (e.g., Fisher information, profile likelihoods) would be appropriate; related work in model distinguishability/design (Navarro et al. 2004) could be connected more concretely.

Presentation/formatting
- Heavy reliance on Supplementary for essential algorithms/derivations; the main text currently lacks enough detail for reproduction.
- Figures are referenced but not available; conclusions in Results are hard to verify. Please ensure key plots (e.g., identifiability posteriors along critical parameters, Ntr dependence) are in the main paper.
- Multiple formatting/typo issues: an in-text footnote marker “1” appears mid-paragraph in Sec. 4.1; broken words (“consider-”); inconsistent notation (p vs q for priors/likelihoods), occasional missing bars/superscripts; inconsistent spacing. References are incomplete (many missing years, volumes, pages) and should be standardized to NeurIPS format.

Originality and significance
- The idea of using expected log-likelihood/KL to assess model distinguishability is known; the contribution here is the specific observer class and a practical pipeline to probe identifiability/design for Bayesian perception models. This is valuable but incremental; significance hinges on rigor and reproducibility of the modeling/algorithmic pieces and the depth of the design insights.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely problem; practical tool that many computational perception labs could use.
- Sensible, unified observer parameterization; separation of experimental vs internal elements.
- KL-based identifiability criterion is principled and efficient to evaluate.
- Case studies provide actionable design insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological underspecification (maximum-entropy mixture construction, loss convexity/uniqueness conditions, MCMC details).
- Sensitivity to modeling choices (lapse, mapping normalization) not adequately explored.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of scalability and design variables (Ntr, Nexp).
- Heavy dependence on Supplementary; missing key details/figures in main paper.
- Numerous formatting and reference issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper tackles an important topic and proposes a promising framework, but key technical and reproducibility gaps, plus several avoidable presentation issues, undermine confidence. I would be inclined to accept if the authors add rigorous specifications (and proofs or algorithms) for the mixture/entropy and loss constraints, provide full inference details and diagnostics, analyze sensitivity to lapse and mapping choices, and include core figures and design trade-offs in the main text, while fixing formatting/references.
