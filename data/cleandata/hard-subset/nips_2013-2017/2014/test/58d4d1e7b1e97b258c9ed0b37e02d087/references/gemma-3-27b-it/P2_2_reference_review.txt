## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors argue that while Bayesian models are increasingly used to explain human behavior, the inverse problem of recovering internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from data is fundamentally ill-posed. They present a novel framework for *a priori* analyzing the identifiability of a flexible class of Bayesian observer models, and demonstrate its application to time and speed perception tasks. The framework centers around a parameterized observer model and utilizes KL divergence to quantify the potential for different parameter settings to generate similar behavioral predictions. Crucially, the work aims to provide researchers with a tool to proactively assess experimental designs and improve their ability to uniquely recover internal representations.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a fundamental problem:** The issue of non-identifiability is a long-standing concern in the field, and this paper provides a systematic and potentially powerful approach to address it.
* **Well-motivated and clearly explained:** The authors clearly articulate the problem and its implications, building upon existing literature. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the paper is generally well-written.
* **Generality of the model:** The proposed observer model is impressively general, encompassing a wide range of perceptual tasks and internal representations. The choice of parametric forms appears reasonable and allows for efficient computation.
* **Practical framework:** The framework provides a tangible method for researchers to evaluate experimental designs *before* data collection, potentially saving time and resources.  The use of KL divergence as a measure of identifiability is a sensible choice.
* **Demonstrated application:** The application to time and speed perception tasks provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used and yields interesting insights – e.g., the potential pitfalls of imposing narrow loss functions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational complexity:** While the authors emphasize computational efficiency, the MCMC sampling required to explore the identifiability landscape could still be computationally intensive for complex models or high-dimensional parameter spaces.  This isn’t explicitly addressed, and could limit the practical applicability.
* **Assumptions within the model:** While the model is general, it still relies on several assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, unimodal likelihoods). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions isn’t thoroughly investigated.  A more detailed discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited validation:** The applications are largely theoretical, demonstrating the *potential* of the framework rather than a rigorous validation against real-world data. While the authors acknowledge this, further exploration of the framework’s performance with simulated or real datasets would be beneficial.
* **KL divergence as the sole metric:** The authors rely heavily on KL divergence. While appropriate, other metrics capturing different aspects of model similarity might provide a more nuanced understanding of identifiability.
* **Connection to existing identifiability techniques:** While the authors acknowledge related work in model identifiability, the connection to and differentiation from established techniques (e.g., profile likelihood analysis, Fisher information matrix) could be more explicitly discussed.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are generally well-supported. The model building appears careful. However, the lack of extensive validation with real or simulated data slightly detracts from the overall quality. (Score: 8/10)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is used consistently, and the explanations are accessible. (Score: 9/10)
* **Originality:** The framework itself is a novel contribution, and the application to the problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception is original. (Score: 8/10)
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. Providing researchers with a tool to assess identifiability *a priori* could substantially improve the quality and interpretability of Bayesian modeling studies in perception. (Score: 8/10)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a crucial and often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling.
* The proposed framework is novel, general, and potentially very useful to the community.
* The applications demonstrate the framework’s potential and offer insightful observations.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The framework’s computational demands could limit its practical applicability.
* The lack of extensive validation with real-world data weakens the claims.
* The reliance on specific model assumptions could affect the generality of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The authors have identified a critical problem and proposed a well-motivated and potentially powerful solution. While some limitations remain, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding computational complexity, model assumptions, and validation.  I would encourage the authors to explore opportunities for making the framework more accessible and efficient, and to demonstrate its applicability with more extensive data analysis.
