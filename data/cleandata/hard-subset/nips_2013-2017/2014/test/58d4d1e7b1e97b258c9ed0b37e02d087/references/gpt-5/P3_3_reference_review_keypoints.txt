- Paper addresses non-identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian perceptual models and proposes a flexible 1D observer family with analytic tractability.
- Introduces an a priori identifiability analysis based on expected log marginal likelihood reducing to a KL-weighted sum over stimuli.
- Uses MCMC sampling of an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) to explore identifiability landscapes and compare experimental designs.
- Case studies: interval timing/reproduction and visual speed perception (slow-speed prior), showing priors are hard to recover in timing without independent motor-noise constraints and that loss narrowing can worsen prior recovery.
- Finds slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters in speed perception are more robustly identifiable than timing priors.
- Builds on established model-degeneracy and design literature; contribution is tailoring the KL/formalism to a Gaussian-mixture observer family for fast evaluation.
- Strength: tackles a timely, impactful problem for interpreting Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) fits.
- Strength: proposes a computationally convenient observer family with Gaussian-mixture closure and analytic expected loss.
- Strength: principled identifiability metric grounded in expected log evidence/KL.
- Strength: provides practical design insights (motor-sensory-lapse confounds, effects of MAP-like feedback) and frames analysis a priori.
- Weakness: identifiability metric and expected-evidence sampling are not novel; novelty rests on the specific parametrization and application.
- Weakness: unconventional “maximum-entropy” derivation of two-component Gaussian mixtures under moment constraints lacks clear justification and uniqueness proof.
- Weakness: very expressive model class may itself cause non-identifiability; sensitivity to model mis-specification is not analyzed.
- Weakness: computational/implementation details (timings, MCMC diagnostics, discretization, robustness to number of components) are missing.
- Weakness: second case study maps 2AFC to estimation without a discrete-choice implementation; identifiability may differ for 2AFC tasks.
- Weakness: lapse and response noise models (lapses drawn from prior, additive motor noise) may be unrealistic and affect identifiability.
- Quality: derivations are sound but key components (maxent mixture, posterior stability, KL accuracy) need clearer exposition and validation.
- Clarity: paper is readable but relies heavily on supplement; needs precise algorithm descriptions, parameter constraints, and computational settings.
- Originality: moderate—methodological idea is established; novelty lies in observer family enabling analytic loss and application to psychophysics.
- Significance: high for psychophysics/BDT practitioners; impact for NeurIPS would be stronger with links to optimal design and higher-dimensional/discrete tasks.
- Recommendation suggestions: compare to Fisher/mutual-information/ADO methods, validate expected posterior with simulated datasets, analyze mis-specification sensitivity, test alternative lapse models, add a discrete-choice example, and report performance and release code.
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, principled framework, useful case-study findings, practical utility for practitioners.
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty vs existing literature, under-justified technical assumptions, sparse empirical validation and efficiency details, no discrete-choice demo.
- Overall: lean weak accept conditional on stronger links to design literature, justification of modeling assumptions, and additional validation/sensitivity analyses.
