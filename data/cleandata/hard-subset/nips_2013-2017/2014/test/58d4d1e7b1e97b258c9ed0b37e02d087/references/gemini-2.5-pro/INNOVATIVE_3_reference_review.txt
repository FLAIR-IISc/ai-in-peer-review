### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a fundamental and pervasive problem in the field of computational cognitive science: the non-identifiability of Bayesian observer models. The authors correctly point out that behavior alone is often insufficient to uniquely determine an observer's internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function), as different combinations of these components can produce identical behavioral predictions. This "inverse problem" threatens the validity of many studies that aim to recover such representations from data.

The core contribution is a novel, general-purpose framework to assess the practical identifiability of a given Bayesian model *a priori*, before an experiment is run. The framework consists of two main components:
1.  A highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for continuous estimation tasks, capable of capturing a wide range of sensory mappings, noise characteristics, priors, and loss functions using mixtures of Gaussians.
2.  An efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, given data generated by a hypothetical "reference" observer. This quantity, related to the KL-divergence between the models' response distributions, allows the use of MCMC to explore which regions of the parameter space are behaviorally indistinguishable from the reference model.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies based on influential work in time and speed perception. They show how their method can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs in resolving parameter degeneracies and can provide theoretical validation for the robustness of inferences made in previous studies.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations connecting the expected log-likelihood to the KL-divergence are solid and provide a clever, computationally tractable alternative to brute-force simulation. The implementation of the flexible observer model is well-conceived, balancing expressiveness with the need for efficient computation. The case studies are well-executed and provide clear, insightful results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation for the problem, systematically builds up the components of the framework, and then lucidly applies it to the case studies. The logic is easy to follow, and the figures are effective at communicating the main results (e.g., visualizing the recovered priors and the posterior densities over key parameters).

**Originality and Significance:** This is where the paper truly excels. The problem of non-identifiability is a well-known "elephant in the room" for Bayesian cognitive modeling. While often acknowledged, proposed solutions are typically ad-hoc, task-specific, or limited to pointing out the problem without offering a constructive path forward. This paper presents a bold and highly original solution: a general, quantitative tool that allows researchers to proactively address the issue at the experimental design stage.

This is not an incremental advance. It is a significant conceptual and methodological contribution that could change how researchers in this field design and interpret their experiments. The ability to rank potential experimental designs based on their power to identify parameters of interest, or to identify fatal degeneracies before investing in data collection, is of immense practical and scientific value. This work provides a much-needed tool for improving the rigor and reproducibility of a whole class of scientific studies.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

While the strengths are profound, there are a few limitations worth noting. My persona, however, views these as minor points in the face of the paper's significant conceptual leap.

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The framework's conclusions are necessarily conditional on the chosen parameters of the "reference observer" (`θ*`). The analysis provides insights for a hypothetical, typical subject, but it's unclear how robust these insights are to variations in this reference. A brief discussion or analysis of the sensitivity of the conclusions (e.g., the ranking of experimental designs) to small changes in `θ*` would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Parametric Assumptions:** The framework's flexibility relies on specific parametric forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians for all distributions). While these are quite expressive, they are still assumptions. This is a reasonable trade-off for computational feasibility, but it is a limitation. This is not a critical flaw, as the core idea is independent of the specific basis functions used and could be extended in future work.

3.  **Focus on Estimation Tasks:** The current model is tailored to continuous estimation tasks. A large portion of psychophysics research uses discrete-choice paradigms (e.g., 2AFC). The authors acknowledge this and suggest it as a future direction, but it does limit the immediate applicability of the presented tool.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces a genuinely new idea and a powerful tool to address a fundamental, long-standing problem in its field. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-impact work that NIPS should champion.
*   **Broad Significance:** The framework is not tied to a single perceptual modality or task but provides a meta-level contribution that could be used by a wide community of researchers in cognitive science, computational neuroscience, and psychology.
*   **Practical Utility:** The case studies are excellent, demonstrating how the framework can generate non-obvious and actionable insights for improving experimental design. This moves the discussion of non-identifiability from a purely theoretical concern to a practical engineering problem that can be solved.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is entirely theoretical, and its conclusions depend on assumptions about a "true" observer model that may not perfectly map onto real, noisy human subjects.
*   The specific parametric forms used, while flexible, are a potential limitation.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance far outweigh the arguments against. The weaknesses are acceptable limitations for a paper introducing such a novel and ambitious framework. The contribution lies in the conceptual approach and the demonstration of its potential. This work has the capacity to significantly elevate the standards of modeling in its domain, and I expect it to be highly influential.
