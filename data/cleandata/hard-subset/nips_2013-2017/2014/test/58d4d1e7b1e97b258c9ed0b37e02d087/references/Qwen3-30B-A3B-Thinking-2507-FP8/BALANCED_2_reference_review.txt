

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that produce identical behavioral data. The authors propose a systematic framework to analyze identifiability a priori, before conducting experiments, which is a significant gap in the literature. They introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model class that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks, and develop a method using KL divergence to quantify identifiability across different experimental designs. The framework is validated through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception.

**Strengths**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational neuroscience and psychophysics. The problem of model non-identifiability is well-motivated and has been acknowledged in prior work (e.g., [17, 18, 19]), but this is the first systematic framework for addressing it. The authors' approach is both theoretically sound and practically useful, as demonstrated by their two case studies. The model class they propose is sufficiently general to cover many common psychophysical tasks while remaining computationally tractable, which is essential for exploring the identifiability landscape. The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can guide experimental design—revealing that standard approaches (e.g., assuming a narrow loss function) can actually be detrimental to parameter recovery, and that measuring motor parameters significantly improves identifiability. The paper's clarity is commendable, with well-organized sections and figures that effectively illustrate key concepts and results. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, which is important for a technical contribution.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper makes a strong contribution to the specific domain of Bayesian models of perception, its broader relevance to NIPS is somewhat limited. The field of computational neuroscience has its own conferences (e.g., CogSci, Neural Computation), and while Bayesian modeling is relevant to machine learning, the paper's focus on psychophysical tasks may not resonate with the majority of NIPS readers. The authors could strengthen the connection to machine learning by discussing how their framework could be applied to more general Bayesian models in ML, such as those used in reinforcement learning or probabilistic programming.

The technical details of the model (particularly the parametrization of the sensory stage) may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with psychophysics. While the paper is clear within its domain, a brief discussion of how this model relates to more standard Bayesian models in ML would help bridge this gap. Additionally, the paper doesn't sufficiently engage with related work on model identifiability in machine learning (e.g., work on identifiability in neural networks or latent variable models). The authors reference [23] but don't clearly connect their approach to broader ML literature on identifiability.

The case studies, while well-executed, are limited in scope. The paper focuses on two specific tasks (temporal estimation and speed perception), but doesn't demonstrate how the framework would generalize to other experimental paradigms (e.g., multi-cue integration or discrete choice tasks). The authors mention these extensions in the conclusion, but the paper would be stronger with at least one more case study or a more thorough discussion of the framework's generalizability.

**Recommendation**

The paper presents a technically sound and practically useful contribution to the specific domain of Bayesian models of perception. While the problem it addresses is important within its field, the paper's relevance to the broader NIPS community is somewhat limited by its narrow focus on psychophysical tasks. The framework is well-developed, and the case studies provide convincing evidence of its utility. However, the paper would benefit from:

1. A stronger connection to machine learning literature on model identifiability and Bayesian inference
2. More explicit discussion of how this framework could be applied to more general Bayesian models beyond psychophysics
3. One additional case study or more thorough discussion of generalizability to other experimental paradigms

Given the high quality of the technical contribution within its domain, and the clear potential for the framework to improve experimental design in computational neuroscience, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should strengthen the connection to broader ML research and provide more discussion of the framework's potential applications beyond psychophysics.

**Summary of Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant, well-motivated problem in Bayesian perceptual modeling
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis
- Demonstrates practical utility through two well-chosen case studies
- Technically sound and well-executed
- Clear, well-organized presentation

**Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Narrow focus on psychophysics may limit relevance to NIPS audience
- Insufficient connection to broader ML literature on model identifiability
- Limited scope of case studies without clear discussion of generalizability

With minor revisions to strengthen the connection to broader ML research and provide more discussion of the framework's general applicability, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
