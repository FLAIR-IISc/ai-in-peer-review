### Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical and well-known issue of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions. The authors propose a general framework to assess *a priori* the identifiability of model parameters for a given experimental design. Their approach involves two main components: 1) a flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of representing a wide range of sensory mappings, internal representations (priors, likelihoods), and loss functions using mixtures of Gaussians; and 2) a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, which simplifies to the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of the candidate and a "ground truth" reference observer. The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two case studies: recovering priors in a time interval estimation task and examining the inference of a slow-speed prior in visual perception. The authors use their framework to compare different experimental designs and identify potential parameter degeneracies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The problem of non-identifiability is a major obstacle to interpreting the results of fitting Bayesian models to behavioral data. Providing a systematic, *a priori* framework to analyze and mitigate this issue is a significant and valuable contribution to computational cognitive science and neuroscience, fields well-represented at NIPS.

2.  **Principled and General Approach:** The proposed framework is principled, grounding the analysis in the expected log-likelihood. The construction of a general observer model using flexible parametric forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians) is a strong point, allowing the framework to be applied to a wide variety of one-dimensional estimation tasks.

3.  **Practical Utility for Experimental Design:** The paper successfully demonstrates how the framework can be used as a tool to improve experimental design. The analysis in Section 4.1, which compares different experimental setups (BSL, SRT, MAP, MTR) and reveals non-intuitive results (e.g., a MAP-like loss function worsening prior recovery), is a compelling proof of concept. This meta-scientific approach has the potential to save experimental effort and lead to more robust scientific conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, technical ambiguities, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality and call into question the robustness of its conclusions.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision in Key Formulations:**
    *   The initial explanation of the degeneracy is imprecise. In the paragraph following Eq. 1, the loss function is written as `L(ŝ, s)` while in Eq. 1 it is `L(s, ŝ)`. While potentially a trivial notational switch, such inconsistencies in a core motivating section are concerning.
    *   The term `E(θ|θ*)` in Eq. 13 is referred to as an "approximate expected posterior density." This is misleading terminology. This quantity is not a posterior over θ; it is an unnormalized surface derived from the expected log-likelihood, which serves as a proxy for the posterior evidence. Calling it a posterior could cause confusion.
    *   The paper refers to Figure 1 throughout Section 2, but the figure itself is not provided in the manuscript. This is a major omission that significantly hampers understanding of the model's structure.

2.  **Strong and Under-justified Modeling Assumptions:**
    *   While the use of Gaussian mixtures for the prior, likelihood, and loss function is computationally convenient, it is a very strong constraint. The justification via a "maximum-entropy approach" feels somewhat post-hoc, as the primary motivation is clearly computational tractability. It is unclear how conclusions would change if the true internal representations were not well-approximated by these mixtures (e.g., a sharply bounded uniform prior).
    *   The conversion of the 2AFC paradigm from Stocker & Simoncelli [9] into an "equivalent estimation task" is a critical step in the second case study, yet it is mentioned only in passing. This is a non-trivial conversion that involves its own set of assumptions about decision rules and loss functions. Without a detailed explanation and justification for this step, the validity of the entire analysis in Section 4.2 is questionable.
    *   The entire framework hinges on the choice of a "reference observer" θ*, which is determined from "pilot experiments, the literature, or educated guesses." The analysis does not explore the sensitivity of its conclusions to this choice. For instance, would the MTR design still be superior in Section 4.1 if the reference observer had a slightly different motor noise profile? This lack of robustness analysis is a significant weakness.

3.  **Minor Errors and Typos:**
    *   Footnote 1 on page 4 mentions a non-identifiability between sensory noise, `σ`, and motor noise, `w1`. The symbol `w1` is not defined anywhere in the paper; it is presumably a typo for the motor noise parameter `ρ1`. This error makes the footnote, which explains a key degeneracy, difficult to parse.
    *   The citations are not formatted consistently or cleanly within the reference list.
    *   The phrase "in practice, for σ` & 1" in Section 2.3 is ambiguous; it likely means `σ` >> 1, but the notation is non-standard.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem for any researcher fitting complex models to data.
*   The core idea of using a flexible computational framework to proactively test for identifiability is novel and highly significant.
*   The work provides a potentially powerful tool for designing more informative psychophysical experiments.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript is replete with minor errors, inconsistencies, and imprecise statements that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
*   A key methodological step in the second case study (conversion from 2AFC to estimation) is completely unexplained.
*   The analysis relies on strong parametric assumptions and a single "reference observer" without assessing the robustness of the conclusions to these choices.
*   The absence of Figure 1 is a critical flaw that hinders comprehension.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a promising and important research direction, the execution in its current form does not meet the high standards expected at NIPS. The accumulation of numerous minor flaws, combined with more significant methodological omissions (the 2AFC conversion, lack of robustness analysis), severely diminishes confidence in the presented results. The work has the potential to be a strong contribution, but it requires a thorough revision to address the issues of clarity, precision, and methodological justification. As it stands, the paper feels more like a preliminary report than a complete piece of work. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
