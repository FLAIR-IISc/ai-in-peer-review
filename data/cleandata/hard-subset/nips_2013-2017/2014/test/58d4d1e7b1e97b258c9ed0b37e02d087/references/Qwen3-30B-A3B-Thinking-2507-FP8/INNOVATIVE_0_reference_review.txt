

# Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

This paper addresses a fundamental and long-recognized challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions). The authors present a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis, which is a significant conceptual contribution to the field. Previous work has acknowledged this identifiability issue (e.g., [19, 23]), but none has provided a general, computationally efficient method for systematically exploring it before conducting experiments. The paper's framework enables researchers to evaluate whether their experimental designs will permit reliable recovery of internal representations—a critical question for the field.

The authors develop a flexible Bayesian observer model that encompasses a wide range of perceptual tasks (Section 2), with a key innovation being the use of maximum-entropy approaches to parametrize sensory, perceptual, and decision components. This parametrization allows for efficient computation while maintaining flexibility. The core contribution (Section 3) is the novel method for quantifying identifiability through expected log likelihood comparisons, which avoids the computational intractability of previous approaches. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility in two case studies (Sections 4.1-4.2), showing how it can identify problematic experimental designs and reveal non-intuitive relationships between parameters.

The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty: it shifts the field from post-hoc model validation to proactive identifiability analysis. This is a bold, paradigm-shifting approach that could fundamentally change how experimental designs are conceived in perceptual psychology. The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility—particularly the counterintuitive finding that a "MAP-like" loss function (which might seem ideal) actually worsens prior recovery in interval timing tasks.

The paper also makes a significant contribution to methodological rigor in the field. By providing a systematic way to evaluate whether model components can be reliably recovered from data, it addresses a critical weakness in many existing Bayesian models of perception. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the framework applies to one-dimensional stimulus estimation), but this is reasonable given the paper's scope and the complexity of the problem.

Minor weaknesses include limited exploration of the framework's applicability to more complex perceptual tasks (e.g., multi-dimensional stimuli) and some technical details that could be expanded (e.g., the MCMC implementation details). However, these are minor compared to the paper's substantial contribution.

The paper's significance is high. It provides a tool that could prevent years of wasted research on experiments where model components cannot be identified. The field has long struggled with the "black box" nature of Bayesian models of perception, and this work offers a way to make these models more transparent and interpretable. The case studies demonstrate clear practical value, showing how the framework can guide experimental design to yield more reliable results.

This work stands out from previous NIPS papers on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., [6, 12, 24]) by addressing a fundamental methodological issue rather than proposing new models or applications. While previous work has focused on applying Bayesian models, this paper provides a framework for evaluating whether those models are even identifiable in the first place—a critical but previously overlooked step.

The authors have clearly situated their work within the literature, acknowledging prior work on identifiability (e.g., [19, 23]) while highlighting how their approach differs. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail for replication.

In summary, this paper presents a genuinely novel framework that addresses a critical gap in the field of Bayesian models of perception. The conceptual contribution is substantial, and the framework's potential to improve experimental design across the field is significant. While the case studies could be expanded, the paper's core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
1. Addresses a fundamental, long-recognized problem in the field
2. Provides a systematic, computationally efficient method for a priori identifiability analysis
3. Has clear practical implications for experimental design
4. Demonstrates the framework's utility through compelling case studies
5. Offers a bold shift from post-hoc validation to proactive design

Con-acceptance arguments:
1. Limited to one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks
2. Some technical details could be expanded
3. Case studies are limited to two specific paradigms

The novelty and significance of this work far outweigh these minor limitations. This paper should be accepted at NIPS as it presents a significant conceptual advance that will benefit the entire field of computational perception research.
