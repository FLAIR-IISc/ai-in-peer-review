This paper addresses the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The core idea is to provide a framework for *a priori* analysis of model identifiability, enabling researchers to design experiments that better constrain parameter recovery in Bayesian observer models. The authors introduce a flexible, parameterized observer model encompassing various perceptual tasks and propose a method to efficiently explore the identifiability landscape through MCMC sampling of the approximate posterior density. They demonstrate the framework's utility with case studies in time and speed perception, highlighting potential issues with existing experimental designs and offering recommendations for improvement.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the challenges posed by non-identifiability in Bayesian modeling and the need for a systematic approach to address them. The proposed framework is a valuable contribution, moving beyond ad-hoc constraints to offer a principled way to assess and improve experimental design. The mathematical formulation appears sound, leveraging existing results on KL divergence and maximum entropy principles to create a computationally tractable method. The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can be applied, revealing potentially counterintuitive insights (e.g., the detrimental effect of a narrow loss function in time interval estimation). 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the model presented is “flexible,” its reliance on Gaussian mixtures throughout (for likelihoods, priors, and losses) might limit its applicability to scenarios where these assumptions are demonstrably violated. The authors acknowledge this implicitly but don’t fully explore the consequences. The choice of specific parametric forms (e.g., Eq. 2 for the sensory mapping) feels somewhat arbitrary; a more thorough justification or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The discussion of computational complexity is limited.  The authors state the framework allows for “fast” computation, but do not quantify this or provide details on the computational resources needed for realistic applications. This is particularly important given the MCMC sampling involved.  The reliance on the “reference observer” (θ*) is a potential limitation.  The results are dependent on the choice of this observer, and a more thorough exploration of sensitivity to this parameterization would strengthen the conclusions. In the time perception example, the decision to focus on the *short* condition for illustrating the reconstructed priors is not well justified. The discussion section is a bit brief and could benefit from a more nuanced consideration of the limitations of the framework and potential avenues for future research (e.g., extensions to more complex models or non-parametric approaches). Finally, while the authors reference related work on model identifiability in psychology [22, 23], the connections could be made more explicit.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework provides a novel and systematic approach to address a critical issue – identifiability – in Bayesian models of perception.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-justified.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework has the potential to significantly improve experimental design in perceptual research, leading to more reliable inferences about underlying cognitive processes.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Generalizability:** The reliance on Gaussian mixtures may restrict the framework's applicability.
*   **Computational Considerations:** Lack of discussion on computational complexity.
*   **Sensitivity to Assumptions:** Results depend on the choice of the “reference observer” and other model parameters.
*   **Limited Scope of Discussion:** The discussion section could be more nuanced and comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed framework outweigh them. The framework offers a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding computational complexity, sensitivity analysis, and the limitations of the Gaussian mixture assumption. I also suggest a slightly expanded discussion section.
