This paper attempts to provide a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue given the increasing reliance on these models to infer internal representations. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inverting Bayesian models – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior. They propose a method to *a priori* assess identifiability based on exploring the parameter space and comparing the resulting behavioral predictions. The approach involves defining a flexible class of observer models and using KL divergence to quantify the similarity of model predictions. They demonstrate the framework with two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a genuine and important problem in the field. The authors are clearly knowledgeable about Bayesian modeling and psychophysics. The proposed framework has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers attempting to infer internal representations from behavior. However, despite the potential, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing in its current form, primarily due to a lack of extensive validation and a reliance on idealized assumptions. 

My main concern lies with the methodological soundness. The simplification of the problem, while necessary for tractability, feels *too* strong. The chosen model, while flexible, still relies on numerous parametric assumptions (e.g., the specific functional form of the sensory map, the Gaussian mixture for likelihoods and loss functions, the form of the prior). The impact of these specific choices on identifiability isn't fully explored. The authors state they aim for a "general" framework, but the results are heavily tied to the specifics of *their* chosen model. A more robust analysis would involve sensitivity testing with variations on these key assumptions. Furthermore, while the use of KL divergence as a metric for comparing model predictions is reasonable, its interpretation is somewhat unclear, particularly in relation to the practical implications for experimental design. What constitutes a ‘sufficiently large’ KL divergence to warrant a change in experimental setup? This requires further discussion. 

The application to time perception is interesting, but the conclusions – particularly the finding that encouraging a narrow loss function *worsens* reconstruction – are surprising and require more detailed justification. The explanation involving the non-identifiability of sensory and motor noise is plausible but feels somewhat post-hoc. The application to speed perception, while confirming existing findings, doesn't showcase the framework’s ability to reveal novel insights. It largely serves as a validation exercise rather than a demonstration of its power.

Clarity is generally good, but the notation is dense and could be simplified. The description of the mathematical derivations is concise, potentially making it difficult for readers less familiar with the technical details to follow.  

Originality is moderate. While the specific combination of techniques—the parameterized model class, the KL divergence-based identifiability analysis, and the application to psychophysical tasks—is novel, the underlying concepts (model identifiability, Bayesian modeling, KL divergence) are well-established. 

Significance is the most debatable aspect. If the framework can be rigorously validated and extended, it could be highly significant, providing a principled way to design experiments that maximize the recoverability of internal representations. However, in its current form, the contribution feels incremental. The framework's utility is limited by the strong assumptions and the lack of practical guidance on how to apply it in real-world experimental settings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Proposes a novel framework for assessing identifiability.
* Demonstrates the framework with relevant case studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Relies on strong parametric assumptions that limit generalizability.
* Lacks extensive validation and sensitivity analysis.
* Conclusions are sometimes surprising and require more justification.
* Practical implications for experimental design are unclear.
* Significance is currently limited by the framework’s constraints.



I recommend **rejection** at this time. The paper presents an interesting idea, but it needs substantial further development and validation before it can be considered a significant contribution to NIPS. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Perform a more thorough sensitivity analysis, exploring the impact of different model assumptions on the identifiability results.
2.  Provide clearer guidance on how to interpret the KL divergence metric and use it to inform experimental design.
3.  Investigate the framework’s performance with more complex psychophysical tasks and real-world datasets.
4.  Address the surprising finding regarding the effect of a narrow loss function in the time perception example.
5.  Consider exploring alternative identifiability measures beyond KL divergence.
