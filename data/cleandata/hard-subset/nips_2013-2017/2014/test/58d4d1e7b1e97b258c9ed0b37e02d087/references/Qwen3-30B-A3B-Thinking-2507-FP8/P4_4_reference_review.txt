

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical methodological issue in Bayesian models of perception: the problem of model non-identifiability, where multiple combinations of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavioral responses. The authors present a framework for systematically analyzing identifiability a priori, enabling researchers to evaluate and improve experimental designs before data collection.

The paper builds on well-established work in Bayesian perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10, 12, 14, 15, 24]) that has successfully used Bayesian models to explain behavioral data, but acknowledges that the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations is often ill-posed [19]. Previous work has addressed this degeneracy through various constraints (theoretical assumptions, experimental design, or additional measurements [12, 19]), but the authors correctly identify the absence of a systematic framework for evaluating these approaches.

The main contribution is a general framework for assessing identifiability of Bayesian observer models. The authors introduce a flexible class of observer models parametrized by θ, which encompasses key elements of perceptual tasks (sensation, perception, decision-making, response stages). Crucially, they develop a method to quantify identifiability by measuring the expected log-likelihood of alternative models given data generated by a reference model (Eq. 12-13). This approach efficiently explores the identifiability landscape through MCMC sampling from the approximate posterior density.

The framework is validated through two case studies: (1) time interval estimation and (2) speed perception. In the time estimation task, the authors demonstrate how different experimental designs (e.g., single vs. dual conditions, loss function constraints) affect the identifiability of priors, revealing non-intuitive findings (e.g., imposing a MAP-like loss function worsens identifiability). In the speed perception task, they show that the inference of slow-speed priors is generally robust to degeneracies.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper clearly identifies and addresses a significant methodological gap in Bayesian models of perception, which has been previously noted but not systematically analyzed [17, 18, 19].
2. The framework is theoretically sound, with clear derivations of the expected log-likelihood as a function of KL divergence (Eq. 12).
3. The case studies provide concrete, actionable insights for experimental design in psychophysics.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
5. The authors carefully consider limitations (e.g., the theoretical nature of the analysis, assumptions about the sensory map).

**Weaknesses:**
1. The framework is limited to one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks, though the authors acknowledge this and suggest extensions.
2. The case studies focus on two specific tasks; while these are well-chosen, broader validation across different perceptual domains would strengthen the contribution.
3. The computational complexity of the framework for very large parameter spaces isn't fully addressed, though the authors claim efficiency for their purposes.
4. The paper doesn't explicitly discuss how the framework might integrate with existing model comparison techniques (e.g., Bayesian model selection).

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by providing the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis in Bayesian models of perception. While related work exists on model distinguishability [23], this paper specifically targets the unique challenges of Bayesian models of perception, making it distinct from previous contributions at NIPS and elsewhere.

**Significance:** The problem of model non-identifiability is critical for interpreting Bayesian models of perception, as it directly impacts the validity of conclusions about internal representations. The proposed framework has clear practical value for experimental design in psychophysics, potentially improving the reliability of inferences about mental representations. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can reveal non-intuitive relationships between experimental design and identifiability, which could inform future research.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** The paper addresses a significant methodological gap with a theoretically sound framework that has practical implications for experimental design. The case studies provide concrete evidence of the framework's utility, and the approach is general enough to be applicable to other tasks within the domain.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** The framework's current scope is limited to specific types of perceptual tasks, and the theoretical nature of the analysis means empirical validation in real experimental contexts would be needed to fully establish its utility.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic approach to a critical methodological problem. The framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrated through relevant case studies. While the scope is currently limited to specific perceptual tasks, the authors clearly articulate potential extensions. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance, and represents a meaningful advancement in the methodological toolkit for researchers studying Bayesian models of perception.
