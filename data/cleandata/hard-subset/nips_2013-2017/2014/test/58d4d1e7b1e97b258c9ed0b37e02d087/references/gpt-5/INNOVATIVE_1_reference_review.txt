Summary
This paper proposes a principled, simulation-based framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks. The authors build a flexible, fast-to-evaluate class of 1D observer models parameterizing: (i) a stimulus-to-measurement mapping (monotonic f(s) with Weber/Stevens-like flexibility), (ii) measurement noise as a two-Gaussian mixture matched to first four moments, (iii) priors as a lattice mixture of Gaussians in internal space, (iv) loss as an “inverted” two-Gaussian mixture (again moment-matched), (v) lapses and motor noise. They then derive an expected marginal log-likelihood of a candidate model θ under datasets generated by a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli (Eq. 12). Exponentiating this gives an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩), from which they sample via MCMC to map identifiability and compare experimental designs.

Two case studies illustrate the framework. In interval timing, they show that priors are hard to recover under a baseline design; identifiability is degraded by confounds between sensory and motor noise and by weak identifiability of loss width (σℓ), with a MAP-like loss further worsening prior recovery; constraining motor noise via independent measurement improves identifiability. In speed perception, they show that a heavy-tailed slow-speed prior and contrast-dependent noise can be reliably recovered, even when decoupling internal and external noise assumptions.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Bayesian observer modeling (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert), identifiability concerns in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung), and discussions of degeneracy between priors/likelihoods/losses. The specific contribution is to (a) introduce a unified parametric observer family that is broad yet computationally efficient, and (b) connect ex-ante identifiability to an expected KL landscape over response distributions, enabling design comparison. It would benefit from a more explicit connection to Bayesian optimal experimental design (e.g., Lindley; Chaloner & Verdinelli) and local/Fisher-information-based design for psychophysics.

Strengths
- Novel and useful framing: Turning identifiability into an expected KL-based landscape over observer parameters is simple, elegant, and actionable for design.
- Flexible yet efficient observer family that unifies priors, likelihoods, loss, lapses, and motor noise, enabling broad “what if?” analyses.
- Clear demonstration that common design choices can hurt or help parameter recovery (e.g., MAP-like loss harming prior recovery; measuring motor noise helps), producing nontrivial, actionable guidance.
- Addresses a timely pain point in the field: degeneracy between priors, likelihoods, and loss in BDT-based analyses.

Weaknesses
- Validation is purely simulated. A small real-data demonstration (e.g., reanalysis that predicts observed design effects or recovers known manipulations) would substantively strengthen the claims.
- Scope limited to 1D magnitudes; the paper promises extensions but does not explore multidimensional or discrete-choice (2AFC) tasks. The mapping of 2AFC to estimation may distort identifiability; a native discrete-choice variant would be valuable.
- Parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures; moment matching; “local maximum-entropy” approximations) are pragmatic but under-justified. True MaxEnt with moment constraints does not yield Gaussian mixtures; a brief justification or sensitivity analysis would help.
- The “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) lacks explicit priors over θ and collapses design considerations to KL alone. Connections to Fisher information (local identifiability), and to optimizing stimulus sets Pi for identifiability, would enhance theoretical depth.
- Some key practical details are deferred to the supplement: MCMC setup, computational cost, convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to M and grid spacing a, and robustness to misspecification (e.g., true observer outside the model class).
- References appear incomplete (missing years/venues in places).

Questions and suggestions
- Provide an ablation/sensitivity analysis: number of prior components M; alternative f(s); mixture vs single-Gaussian losses; and robustness to model mismatch (true observer outside the class).
- Report computational budgets (per-likelihood evaluation, per-MCMC run), convergence diagnostics, and code availability to support reproducibility and adoption.
- Clarify the “maximum-entropy” rationale for the mixture choices and the unimodality constraint.
- Consider local analyses (Fisher information or Hessian of Eq. 12 at θ*) to complement global MCMC exploration and to suggest algorithmic design optimization (stimulus sets, condition counts, reward structures).
- For the interval timing task, suggest targeted manipulations that could identify σℓ (e.g., controlled asymmetric loss, risk-sensitivity probes) and disentangle sensory vs motor noise (e.g., delayed/averaged responses, separate motor calibration).
- For speed perception, discuss the impact of mapping 2AFC to estimation on identifiability; adding a native 2AFC variant (even preliminary) would broaden scope.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and practically valuable framework for a pervasive problem.
- Clear, nontrivial, and actionable design insights.
- Methodologically bold in unifying a broad observer class with an efficient identifiability metric.

Cons against acceptance
- No real-data validation; limited task scope (1D estimation).
- Some modeling choices and the MaxEnt claims are under-justified; computational details are sparse.
- Missing connections to Bayesian experimental design literature.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling and broadly useful approach to a central issue in Bayesian perception modeling, with nontrivial insights for experimental design. While empirical validation and broader scope would strengthen the work, the conceptual novelty and potential impact justify inclusion.

Confidence
High for conceptual assessment; medium for empirical claims (simulation-only).
