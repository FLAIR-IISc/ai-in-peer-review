- Paper tackles non-identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models, showing multiple component combinations can produce identical behavior.
- Introduces the first systematic, a priori framework for testing identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions.
- Develops a flexible Bayesian observer model class and a method to explore identifiability landscapes.
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies: time-interval estimation and speed perception.
- Clear problem formulation with a compelling mathematical demonstration (Eq. 1) of degeneracy.
- Theoretical approach is sound: uses expected log-likelihood and KL divergence to quantify identifiability (Eq. 12).
- Framework has immediate practical utility for experimental design, identifying problematic designs and suggesting improvements.
- Proposed observer model balances flexibility and computational tractability for many sensory estimation tasks.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with effective figures and notation.
- Model class is limited: assumes unidimensional stimuli and specific parametric forms, with no demonstrated extension to multidimensional or multi-cue tasks.
- Computational cost of exploring the identifiability landscape is not discussed and may be prohibitive for high-dimensional spaces.
- Case studies are limited in scope and assumptions (e.g., natural prior vs. learned experimental prior) may not reflect typical experiments.
- Lacks clear practical guidance for implementation (e.g., choosing reference observer θ*, defining sufficient identifiability).
- Insufficient comparison to existing identifiability methods in the literature.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and practically useful contribution that could prevent wasted experiments, though scope and implementation details need expansion.
- Recommendation: Accept, with minor revisions to address computational requirements, model limitations/extensions, comparisons to existing methods, and concrete implementation guidance.
