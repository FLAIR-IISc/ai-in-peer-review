### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the critical issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors note that different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, making it difficult to reliably recover an observer's internal representations from data. This "inverse problem" undermines a core goal of the Bayesian cognitive modeling enterprise. The central contribution of this work is a computational framework to systematically assess, *a priori*, the identifiability of a given Bayesian model for a specific experimental design.

The framework consists of two main components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional perceptual estimation tasks. This model is structured in four stages (sensation, perception, decision, response) and uses mixtures of Gaussians to flexibly represent the observer's prior, internal likelihood, and loss function. This parameterization allows for the approximation of a vast range of observer characteristics within a single, computationally tractable structure.

Second, the paper proposes a method to quantify identifiability by mapping out the "identifiability landscape." This is achieved by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model under the assumption that data is generated by a "true" reference model. The authors show this quantity is proportional to the negative Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior density" derived from this measure, one can visualize regions of the parameter space that are indistinguishable from the reference model, thereby revealing potential degeneracies.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies based on prior work. In a time interval estimation task (related to Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010), the analysis reveals significant non-identifiability, particularly a degeneracy between sensory and motor noise. It suggests that independently measuring motor noise is critical for reliably recovering the observer's prior. In a second study on speed perception (related to Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), the framework validates the original findings, showing that the inference of a slow-speed prior and noise characteristics is robust.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is theoretically sound, grounding the identifiability analysis in the principled information-theoretic measure of KL-divergence. The derivation of the expected log-likelihood (Eq. 12) is a key result that makes the analysis computationally feasible. The four-stage observer model is comprehensive and thoughtfully constructed; the use of mixture models parameterized by their moments is a clever approach that balances flexibility with tractability, as it permits an analytical expression for the expected loss. The claims are well-supported by the two case studies, which provide concrete and compelling demonstrations of the framework's power to yield actionable insights for experimental design.

A potential weakness is that the analysis is conditional on the choice of a "reference observer" (`θ*`). While necessary for the framework, the conclusions drawn about a given experimental design may depend on the specific assumptions made about this reference. A more extensive discussion on the robustness of the findings to variations in `θ*` would strengthen the work. Furthermore, the framework in its current form is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, and its extension to other common paradigms like 2AFC or multi-cue integration is mentioned but not demonstrated.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and clear. The motivation for the work is compellingly laid out in the introduction. Each stage of the observer model is described systematically and is complemented by a helpful diagram (Figure 1). The logic behind the identifiability analysis is explained intuitively before the formal equations are introduced. The results of the case studies are presented effectively through informative figures that clearly visualize the recovered priors and the posterior distributions over key parameters, making the conclusions easy to grasp. The manuscript provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the methodology and its implications.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. While the problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models is known in the field (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2001), this paper appears to be the first to propose a systematic, computational framework to analyze it prospectively. Previous approaches have been more qualitative or task-specific. The synthesis of a highly flexible observer model with an information-theoretic measure of distinguishability provides a novel and powerful tool for the community. The specific findings from the case studies—such as the counter-intuitive result that a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery in the timing task—demonstrate that the framework can produce novel insights beyond confirming existing intuitions.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is potentially very high. It provides a practical methodology for improving the rigor of experimental design in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. By allowing researchers to test whether their planned experiment can actually disentangle the model components they are interested in, this framework could help prevent the collection of uninformative data and strengthen the conclusions drawn from Bayesian modeling. It directly addresses the "just-so stories" critique often leveled at Bayesian cognitive models by providing a tool to assess whether a model's explanatory power is genuine or an artifact of model degeneracy. The framework is general enough to be widely adopted and is likely to inspire follow-up work extending it to other experimental paradigms and modeling domains.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles the critical and pervasive issue of non-identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling, offering a principled and practical solution.
2.  **Novel and Sound Framework:** The proposed combination of a flexible observer model and a KL-divergence-based analysis is a highly novel and technically sound contribution.
3.  **High Potential Impact:** The work provides a tool that can directly improve the quality and rigor of experimental design and model-based inference in the field, making it highly significant for practitioners.
4.  **Clear and Insightful Demonstrations:** The case studies compellingly demonstrate the framework's utility, providing valuable insights into the specific domains of time and speed perception.
5.  **Excellent Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and organized, making a complex topic accessible.

### Arguments Against Acceptance
1.  **Limited Scope:** The framework is currently demonstrated only for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks, which limits its immediate applicability.
2.  **Dependence on Assumptions:** The conclusions rely on the choice of a reference observer, and the robustness of these conclusions to different assumptions could be further explored.
3.  **Practical Implementation Concerns:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or provide details about the practical implementation (e.g., MCMC sampling), which may be a barrier to adoption.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is an excellent and important contribution.
