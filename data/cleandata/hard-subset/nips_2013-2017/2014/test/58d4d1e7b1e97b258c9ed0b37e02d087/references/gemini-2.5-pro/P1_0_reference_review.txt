### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a framework to systematically test the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors tackle the well-known problem that behavior alone is insufficient to uniquely determine the internal components of a Bayesian observer (prior, likelihood, and loss function), as different combinations of these components can lead to identical behavior. This non-identifiability issue threatens the validity of studies that aim to "invert" behavioral data to recover an observer's internal representations.

To address this, the authors propose a two-part solution. First, they develop a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians to represent the sensory likelihood, the prior, and the loss function, allowing for a wide range of shapes (e.g., skewed, heavy-tailed) beyond simple Gaussian assumptions. Second, they propose a method to map the "identifiability landscape" for a given experimental design. Instead of relying on computationally expensive data simulations, they derive an analytical expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, given a ground-truth "reference" observer. This quantity is shown to be proportional to the negative Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from the resulting "approximate expected posterior" via MCMC, they can visualize which parameters of the reference model can be reliably recovered and which are confounded with others.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies. The first examines a time interval estimation task (inspired by Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010), showing that recovering the observer's prior is difficult and highly dependent on the experimental design. For instance, they find that independently measuring motor noise significantly improves recovery, while, perhaps counter-intuitively, encouraging a MAP-like loss function worsens it. The second case study revisits the inference of a "slow-speed prior" in vision (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), finding that the key parameters of the prior and sensory noise are robustly identifiable within their framework, thus providing theoretical support for the original findings.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles a critical and widely acknowledged issue in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The non-identifiability of Bayesian models is a major hurdle for the "Bayesian brain" hypothesis, and this work provides a constructive, principled tool for addressing these concerns, moving beyond general critiques (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011) towards practical solutions.

2.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The core contribution—a method to assess identifiability *before* data collection—is highly original and significant. The use of the expected log-likelihood, and its connection to KL divergence, is an elegant and computationally efficient way to circumvent the need for massive simulations. This provides a formal basis for comparing the power of different experimental designs.

3.  **Flexible and Powerful Observer Model:** The proposed observer model is a major strength. By using mixtures of Gaussians for all key components (likelihood, prior, loss), it can capture a much richer set of representations than typical models that assume Gaussianity or other simple parametric forms. The fact that this complex model remains computationally tractable is a significant technical achievement.

4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The case studies are not mere toy examples; they engage with influential findings in the literature. The results are insightful and practically relevant. The analysis of the time perception task provides a clear recipe for how an experimenter could use this framework to optimize their design (e.g., highlighting the importance of measuring motor noise). The validation of the speed perception findings adds theoretical weight to an important empirical result.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The entire analysis is conditional on a chosen "reference observer" `θ*`. While this is inherent to the *a priori* approach, the conclusions drawn about an experimental design's quality might be sensitive to this choice. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the robustness of its findings to variations in the reference `θ*`. For example, how would the ranking of experimental designs in the time perception task change if the true observer had a different, non-quadratic loss function?

2.  **Conversion from 2AFC to Estimation:** In the speed perception case study, the authors state they convert the original 2AFC paradigm into an "equivalent estimation task." This is a non-trivial step that is not detailed in the paper. The validity of the conclusions for this case study rests on this conversion being appropriate. Given the prevalence of 2AFC tasks, more detail on how the framework handles (or approximates) such discrete-choice paradigms is warranted.

3.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The parameter space of the model is large (Eq. 10 lists ~20+ parameters). While the KL-divergence calculation is efficient for any two models, exploring this high-dimensional space via MCMC to map the full posterior could still be computationally demanding. Some discussion of the computational cost and potential scalability issues would make the paper more complete.

### **Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation connecting expected log-likelihood to KL divergence is correct, and the construction of the flexible observer model is well-motivated and carefully described. The claims are well-supported by the results from the case studies. This is a complete and polished piece of work.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, the model is built up in logical steps, and the figures effectively illustrate the results. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to understand their methods.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While model identifiability is a known problem, the development of a general, *a priori* framework to formally analyze it for this class of models is a novel and important contribution. It synthesizes ideas from Bayesian modeling, information theory, and experimental design in a new way.

*   **Significance:** The significance is high. This framework has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers designing psychophysics experiments within the Bayesian framework. It provides a way to make experimental design more rigorous and to assess the credibility of claims about recovered internal representations. This work will be of great interest to researchers in computational neuroscience, cognitive science, and machine learning who build models of behavior.

### **Final Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong methodological contribution to a significant problem. It is well-executed, clearly presented, and the results are both insightful and practically useful. The framework proposed could have a substantial impact on how experiments are designed and how Bayesian models of perception are evaluated in the future. The minor weaknesses are points for clarification or future work rather than fundamental flaws.
