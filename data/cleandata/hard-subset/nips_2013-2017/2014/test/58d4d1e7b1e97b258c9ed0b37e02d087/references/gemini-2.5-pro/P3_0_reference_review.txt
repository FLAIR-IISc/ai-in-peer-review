This paper presents a computational framework to assess, a priori, the identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. It addresses the well-known degeneracy problem where different combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavior, which poses a fundamental challenge to studies aiming to recover these representations from data. The authors' proposed solution is twofold: first, they develop a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of capturing a wide range of behaviors. Second, they introduce an efficient method based on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between response distributions to quantify the expected statistical evidence for one model versus another, given a "ground truth" reference model. This allows them to map out the "identifiability landscape" and evaluate how different experimental designs can help resolve model degeneracies before any data is collected. The framework is demonstrated on two case studies from the literature: interval timing and visual speed perception.

This work relates to a long-standing critique of Bayesian cognitive modeling (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) and specifically to the known non-identifiability issue in Bayesian Decision Theory (Mamassian & Landy, 2002). While general methods for model identifiability analysis exist (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), the novelty here lies in a framework tailored to the specific structure of Bayesian observer models, making the analysis computationally tractable and directly applicable to experimental design.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a critical and foundational problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. Many studies fit Bayesian models to behavioral data and draw strong conclusions about the nature of internal representations. This work provides a principled and practical tool to assess the validity of such inferences, potentially improving the rigor and design of future experiments. The ability to rank experimental designs based on their power to identify parameters of interest is a significant contribution.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The approach is novel and technically sound. The construction of a general observer model using mixtures of Gaussians for the prior, likelihood, and loss function is a clever way to achieve flexibility while maintaining computational tractability (e.g., enabling an analytical expression for the expected loss). The core methodological idea of using the expected log-likelihood, which elegantly simplifies to a KL divergence term, is an efficient and well-justified proxy for exploring the identifiability landscape without resorting to expensive simulations of full datasets.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is motivated clearly in the introduction, the four-stage observer model is explained logically, and the method for mapping identifiability is well-described. The case studies are well-chosen and effectively illustrate the power of the framework, yielding non-trivial and insightful conclusions. For example, the finding that a MAP-like loss function could *worsen* prior recovery in the timing task is a compelling, counter-intuitive result that highlights the value of such an a priori analysis.

4.  **Impact:** The framework has the potential to be widely used by researchers in the field. It provides a concrete recipe for moving beyond hand-waving arguments about potential degeneracies to a quantitative assessment. The authors' analysis of the speed perception literature, which provides theoretical validation for the recoverability of the slow-speed prior, is a good example of how this tool can be used to strengthen existing claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer (`θ*`):** The entire analysis is conditioned on a chosen "reference" or "ground truth" observer `θ*`. The conclusions about which experimental design is best are therefore dependent on the assumptions made about this observer. While the authors acknowledge that `θ*` can be based on pilot data or literature, it would strengthen the paper to include a discussion or sensitivity analysis showing how the conclusions (e.g., the ranking of experimental designs) might change if `θ*` were different. For instance, is the MTR design in the timing task robustly superior across a range of plausible reference observers?

2.  **Model Class Limitations:** The framework tests identifiability *within* its proposed (albeit flexible) model class. It cannot speak to the possibility that the true observer lies outside this class entirely. A brief discussion of this inherent limitation would be appropriate. Furthermore, the choice of a two-component mixture for likelihoods and loss functions is pragmatic, but the sensitivity of the results to the number of components could be mentioned.

3.  **Conversion from 2AFC to Estimation:** The analysis of the speed perception study by Stocker & Simoncelli (2006) requires converting a 2AFC task into an equivalent estimation task. This is a non-trivial step that is mentioned only in passing. The validity of this conversion is crucial for the conclusions drawn. The authors should provide more details, either in the main text or the supplement, on how this conversion was performed and justify why the identifiability properties are expected to be preserved.

4.  **Scope:** The current framework is limited to one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of psychophysics, extending it to discrete-choice paradigms (like 2AFC) and multidimensional stimuli would greatly increase its impact. While mentioned as future work, this is a notable current limitation.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental, important problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian modeling.
    *   Provides a novel, principled, and computationally efficient framework for a priori analysis.
    *   Demonstrates practical utility through insightful case studies with non-obvious results.
    *   High potential impact on improving experimental design and rigor in the field.

*   **Cons:**
    *   Conclusions are contingent on the choice of a "ground truth" reference observer.
    *   Key procedural detail (2AFC-to-estimation conversion) is underspecified.
    *   The framework's scope is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to scope and areas for future work. It is technically solid, clearly communicated, and addresses a topic of great importance to the NIPS community interested in computational models of cognition and brain function. I strongly recommend acceptance.
