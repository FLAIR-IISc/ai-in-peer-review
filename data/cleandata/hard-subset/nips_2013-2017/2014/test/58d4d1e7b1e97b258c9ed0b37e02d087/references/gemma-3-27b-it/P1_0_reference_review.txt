## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical, yet often overlooked, issue in the application of Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions within the Bayesian framework. They present a novel framework for *a priori* assessing the identifiability of these models, providing a valuable tool for experimental design and interpretation of results. The framework centers on a parameterized class of Bayesian observer models and a method for efficiently exploring the parameter space to determine the robustness of parameter recovery.  The authors illustrate their approach with applications to time and speed perception, highlighting potential pitfalls and design improvements.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a long history of applying Bayesian decision theory to perceptual phenomena (e.g., [1, 2, 3]). It acknowledges the growing trend of inferring internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavior [5, 6, 7, 8] and directly addresses a core concern raised in previous work regarding the ill-posed nature of this inverse problem [17, 18, 19].  The contribution lies in providing a systematic, quantifiable method for analyzing this non-identifiability, moving beyond anecdotal warnings and offering a practical solution for researchers.  It draws on techniques from model selection and identifiability analysis, familiar to statistics and systems identification communities [22, 23], but adapts them specifically to the context of psychophysical modeling.  

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a fundamental problem:** The issue of parameter non-identifiability is a major limitation in the field, and the authors directly tackle this challenge.
* **Novel Framework:**  The proposed framework offers a concrete method for *a priori* evaluating identifiability, allowing researchers to design more informative experiments.
* **Comprehensive Model:** The parameterized observer model is flexible enough to encompass a wide range of tasks and assumptions, offering broad applicability.  The use of maximum entropy principles provides a principled approach to model specification.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The equations are presented clearly, and the figures (though not provided in the submission) are referenced appropriately and described in the text.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The applications to time and speed perception provide concrete examples of how the framework can be used and demonstrate its potential utility.  The analysis of the impact of experimental design choices (e.g., loss function shape, motor noise measurement) is particularly insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The authors emphasize the efficiency of their method, which is crucial for exploring complex model spaces.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions in Model Specification:** The model, despite its generality, relies on several assumptions (e.g., specific functional forms for the prior, likelihood, and loss function, the use of Gaussian mixtures).  The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored. While justified, these assumptions limit the scope of application and warrant careful consideration.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the authors claim computational efficiency, the MCMC sampling for approximating the posterior density (Eq. 13) could still be computationally expensive for high-dimensional parameter spaces. A more detailed discussion of computational demands and scalability would be beneficial.
* **KL Divergence as a Metric:** Using KL divergence to quantify support is a reasonable choice, but it’s an asymmetric measure. The authors acknowledge this, but a more nuanced discussion of its implications for interpreting the results might be helpful.
* **Limited Exploration of Parameter Space:** While the MCMC approach is efficient, it may not fully explore the entire parameter space, potentially missing regions of low but non-negligible support.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The framework’s effectiveness is demonstrated through simulation, but a validation using real behavioral data would significantly strengthen the claims.  Applying the framework to analyze existing datasets or designing new experiments specifically to test its predictions would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper identifies and addresses a critical issue that has been largely ignored in the field of Bayesian modeling of perception.
* The proposed framework offers a practical and systematic approach to improve experimental design and interpretation of results.
* The work is technically sound and well-motivated, building upon existing literature and offering a novel contribution.
* The illustrative examples demonstrate the framework's potential utility and provide valuable insights.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on several simplifying assumptions may limit the generality of the framework.
* The computational cost of the MCMC sampling could be a barrier to applying the framework to complex models.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims about the framework's practical effectiveness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. While the framework relies on some assumptions and further empirical validation is needed, its potential impact on experimental design and interpretation of results is significant. The authors have presented a compelling case for the importance of considering identifiability issues in Bayesian modeling of perception.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Discuss sensitivity to assumptions:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of how the results might change if different functional forms are used for the prior, likelihood, and loss function.
2.  **Address computational complexity:**  Provide a more concrete assessment of the computational demands of the MCMC sampling, including typical runtimes and scaling with the number of parameters.
3.  **Consider alternative metrics:**  Briefly discuss alternative metrics for quantifying support beyond KL divergence.
4. **Elaborate on empirical validation plan:** Outline plans for applying the framework to analyze real behavioral data or design experiments to test its predictions.
