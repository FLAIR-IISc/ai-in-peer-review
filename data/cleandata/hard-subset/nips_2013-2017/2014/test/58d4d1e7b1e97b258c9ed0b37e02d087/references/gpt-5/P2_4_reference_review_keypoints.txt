- Addresses non-identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian perceptual models and formalizes how degeneracy arises in decision-theoretic settings.
- Proposes a flexible, computationally tractable 1D observer class using mixture-based parameterizations for sensory mapping, likelihood, priors, loss, lapses, and reporting noise.
- Introduces an a priori identifiability metric based on expected log marginal likelihood under a reference observer, reducible to weighted sums of KL divergences and sampleable via MCMC.
- Demonstrates the framework on interval timing (shows prior is poorly identifiable without constrained motor noise; narrower loss can worsen inference).
- Demonstrates robust recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters in a speed perception case study, with some parameter biases noted.
- Builds on ideal observer/BDT and prior inference literature, connecting to model identifiability and KL-based model discrimination in experimental design.
- Theoretical derivation of the expected log-likelihood as weighted KL sums is correct and appropriate for a priori discrimination.
- Mixture parameterizations, monotone sensory mappings, and analytic expected-loss for Gaussian mixtures are sensible and well-motivated.
- Evidence is simulation-based, appropriate for an a priori framework, but empirical validation is limited to the authors’ model class.
- Key caveats: conclusions depend on the reference observer and parametric family; robustness checks and MCMC diagnostics are insufficiently reported.
- Conversion of 2AFC to an equivalent estimation task is plausible but not validated; a native 2AFC treatment is deferred.
- Clarity is good overall, but Eq.12 intuition and connections to cross-entropy/KL design need a short explanatory paragraph.
- Recommend adding pseudocode/algorithm box for the identifiability-mapping procedure and summarizing MCMC settings and runtime.
- Important technical supplement steps (maximum-entropy mixtures, analytic expected loss) should include brief sketches in the main text.
- Originality: novel, practical combination of flexible observer class and expected-evidence identifiability metric targeted at Bayesian perception.
- Practical significance: framework can guide experimenters on recoverable parameters and design modifications (e.g., fixing motor noise).
- Scientific impact: supports robustness of slow-speed prior inference and flags weak identifiability of loss width in timing tasks.
- Broader applicability: potentially extensible to multi-cue and discrete-choice tasks, but extensions remain future work.
- Strengths: clear degeneracy formulation, efficient observer model with analytic parts, actionable design insights, and well-positioned literature discussion.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: test robustness to model misspecification (non-Gaussian/noisy/posterior shapes), report computational/MCMC details, and quantify sensitivity to Ntr, Nexp, and mixture complexity M.
- Suggest stronger linkage to KL-optimal and Bayesian experimental design, possibly moving toward stimulus optimization for identifiability.
- Recommend validating or justifying the 2AFC mapping or adding a discrete-choice experiment to confirm transferability.
- Code/data sharing requested to increase impact and adoption.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a practical, technically sound framework yielding non-obvious, useful insights.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation under misspecification, missing computational details, and incomplete connection to broader optimal-design literature.
- Overall recommendation: positive leaning conditional on added robustness analyses, computational details, and clearer ties to experimental design literature.
