### Summary

This paper addresses the critical issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that multiple combinations of internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function) can produce identical observable behavior, which undermines the goal of recovering these representations from experimental data. To tackle this, they propose a novel framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis, allowing researchers to evaluate and improve experimental designs before data collection. The framework consists of two core components: 1) a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models that can capture a wide range of perceptual and motor characteristics using a unified parameterization (e.g., representing priors as mixtures of Gaussians); and 2) a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a test model under a ground-truth "reference" model. This expectation cleverly simplifies to the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the models' response distributions, allowing for efficient MCMC-based exploration of the "identifiability landscape." The authors demonstrate the framework's utility in two well-chosen case studies: recovering priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing the well-known "slow-speed prior" in visual perception. Their results provide concrete, and sometimes non-intuitive, insights into how experimental design choices can impact the reliability of scientific conclusions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is methodologically sound, building upon established principles of model comparison and information theory. The derivation connecting the expected log-likelihood to the KL divergence is elegant and provides a computationally tractable solution to a complex problem. The parametric observer model is thoughtfully constructed, balancing flexibility (using mixtures of Gaussians to approximate arbitrary functions) with computational efficiency (allowing for analytical or fast numerical solutions). The claims are well-supported by the simulation results in the two case studies, which convincingly demonstrate the framework's practical value.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction. The four stages of the observer model are explained systematically and logically. The identifiability analysis method is described with sufficient detail for the reader to grasp the core concepts. The case studies are well-contextualized and the results are interpreted clearly, highlighting the practical implications for experimental design.

**Originality:** While the problem of non-identifiability in cognitive modeling is not new (e.g., Anderson, 1978; Navarro et al., 2006), this paper offers a highly original and domain-specific solution. Previous work has often relied on post-hoc parameter recovery simulations or more generic identifiability analyses. The primary novelty lies in creating a unified, computationally efficient framework tailored specifically for Bayesian observer models, with the express purpose of prospective (a priori) experimental design. This shifts the paradigm from analyzing the limitations of existing data to proactively designing experiments that can overcome those limitations.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. The "Bayesian Brain" is a dominant paradigm in computational neuroscience and cognitive science, but it faces persistent criticism regarding model falsifiability and identifiability (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011). This paper provides a powerful and practical tool to directly address these criticisms. Researchers can now quantitatively assess whether a planned experiment is capable of disentangling, for example, the effects of a prior from those of a loss function. The insights from the case studies are themselves significant: the demonstration that a MAP-like loss function can paradoxically worsen the ability to recover a prior is a crucial, non-obvious finding. Similarly, providing theoretical validation for the recoverability of the slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006) strengthens confidence in that foundational result. This work has the potential to substantially improve the methodological rigor of research in this field.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a fundamental problem:** The paper tackles the critical issue of non-identifiability that challenges a major research program in cognitive science.
2.  **Novel and practical solution:** It provides a new, computationally efficient framework that researchers can actually use to design better experiments.
3.  **High quality and clarity:** The work is technically sound, rigorously executed, and presented with exceptional clarity.
4.  **Impactful results:** The case studies provide valuable insights and demonstrate the framework's power, with findings that are both useful and, in some cases, surprising.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Complexity of the model:** The proposed observer model is comprehensive but also has a large number of parameters. This might create a barrier to adoption and raises questions about the sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to the specific parameterization choices (e.g., the number of mixture components).
2.  **Dependence on the "reference observer":** The entire analysis is conditioned on a chosen reference model (`theta*`). The robustness of the conclusions to variations in this reference model is not explored.
3.  **Limited scope of application (in its current form):** The framework is presented for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While extensions are mentioned, its direct applicability to other common paradigms, like discrete-choice tasks (e.g., 2AFC), is not fully developed.

### Constructive Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is an excellent paper. The arguments against are minor and can be addressed to further strengthen the manuscript.

1.  **Discussing Model Specification Choices:** The authors could add a brief discussion, perhaps in the supplement, on the sensitivity of the analysis to the "hyperparameters" of the model construction, such as the number of mixture components (`M`) for the prior. How does one determine if `M=15` is sufficient? Providing some heuristics or a sensitivity analysis would increase user confidence.

2.  **Robustness to the Reference Observer:** The conclusions are derived with respect to a single `theta*`. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the importance of this choice. A simple but effective addition would be to show that the main conclusions (e.g., the degeneracy between sensory and motor noise in the time perception task) hold even if the parameters of `theta*` are perturbed within a reasonable range. This would demonstrate that the identified degeneracies are features of the model and task structure, not quirks of a specific parameter choice.

3.  **Clarifying the 2AFC to Estimation Mapping:** The conversion of the 2AFC paradigm from the speed perception study into an equivalent estimation task is a key step that is currently glossed over. Since 2AFC and other discrete-choice paradigms are extremely common, expanding on this mapping and discussing how it might influence the identifiability analysis would significantly broaden the paper's impact. A more detailed explanation in the supplement would be very helpful for practitioners.

4.  **Practical Guidance:** To facilitate adoption, the authors might consider adding a short "recipe" or checklist in the supplement that walks a researcher through the steps of applying the framework to a new experimental design problem. This would lower the barrier to entry and maximize the work's practical impact.

Despite these minor suggestions, the paper represents a superb contribution that is timely, important, and rigorously executed. It should be of great interest to the NIPS community, particularly those in cognitive science, computational neuroscience, and Bayesian modeling.
