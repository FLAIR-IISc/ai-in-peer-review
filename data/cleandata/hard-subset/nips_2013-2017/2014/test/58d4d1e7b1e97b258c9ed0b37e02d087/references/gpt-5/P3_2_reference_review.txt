Summary
The paper tackles a central problem in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data. The authors (i) introduce a flexible, computationally efficient class of 1D Bayesian observer models with parametrized stages (sensory mapping, measurement noise, prior, loss, lapses, response noise), designed to cover many estimation tasks; and (ii) propose an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log-likelihood of datasets generated by a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions per stimulus. They define an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩) and sample it with MCMC to visualize identifiability landscapes and compare experimental designs. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (where they find strong degeneracies, especially between sensory and motor noise and poorly identifiable loss width; independent motor estimates greatly help) and speed perception (where the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters are recovered robustly, even when decoupling internal from external noise).

Relation to prior work
The degeneracy of Bayesian decision-maker models (reweighting prior/likelihood/loss) is well-known (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Stocker & Simoncelli), as are general identifiability concerns in cognitive modeling (Myung, Pitt, Navarro). The paper’s contributions are: a unified parametric observer family (mixtures constrained by moments, monotone sensory map) that allows analytic/fast expected loss and likelihood computations; and a principled, dataset-averaged identifiability criterion (expected log-likelihood/KL) to compare designs. The KL-based expected evidence is standard statistically, but its systematic deployment to map identifiability for Bayesian observers, and the concrete design comparisons in two canonical domains, appear novel and useful.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem with clear implications for interpreting many Bayesian-perception results.
- Coherent modeling framework: flexible yet computationally tractable construction (moment-constrained mixtures for measurement and loss; Gaussian lattice prior; monotone sensory mapping f(s)) that unifies a large literature.
- Clean theoretical criterion: expected log-likelihood reduces to a sum of stimulus-wise KL divergences, avoiding Monte Carlo over datasets while preserving direct experimental interpretability (dependence on Ntr, Pi).
- Practical design insights: nontrivial predictions (e.g., narrower loss can worsen identifiability in timing; measuring motor noise can dramatically help), and confirmation that slow-speed priors are identifiable in estimation-like settings.
- Transparent articulation of degeneracies (sensory vs motor noise; loss width) and levers (task conditions, constraints, auxiliary measurements).

Weaknesses
- Validation gap: the link between the expected-evidence landscape and actual parameter recovery under finite-sample inference (MLE/Bayesian posterior) is asserted but not empirically verified. Simulations comparing E(θ|θ*) predictions to fits on many synthetic datasets would strengthen claims.
- Parametric dependence: identifiability conclusions may hinge on specific modeling choices (two-component mixtures, Gaussian prior lattice with M=15, sensory map class in Eq. 2, unimodality constraints, lapse model). Sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Computational details missing: no runtimes/complexity, MCMC diagnostics, or discussion of scaling with parameter dimension (θ includes many wm). Without these, practical applicability and reproducibility are unclear.
- 2AFC equivalence: the speed case interprets a 2AFC paradigm via an “equivalent estimation” mapping, but identifiability in discrete-choice can differ substantially; a direct 2AFC variant would be more convincing.
- Design optimization: while the method can rank a few hand-crafted designs, there is no principled optimization (e.g., choose stimuli Pi to maximize expected discrimination, or choose rewards to enhance identifiability), limiting impact for experiment planning.

Quality
The theoretical derivation (Eq. 12) is sound and leverages standard results for expected log-likelihood. The observer class is thoughtfully engineered for tractability, and the case studies are plausible and informative. However, empirical validation against finite-data inference and more thorough robustness checks are needed for a strong methodological paper.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with a clear flow from model to criterion to applications. Some notational overload (p vs q; “measurement likelihood” vs “internal likelihood”) could be streamlined. Important construction details (ensuring unimodality, max-entropy selection of mixture parameters, numerical solution of Eq. 5 and Eq. 9, MCMC setup/diagnostics) are deferred to the supplement; key summaries in the main text would help.

Originality
Moderately high. While the expected log-likelihood/KL perspective is standard, the integration with a general observer class and its use for a priori design comparison in Bayesian perception is novel and likely to influence practice.

Significance
High for the Bayesian perception community and, more broadly, for computational cognitive science. The framework can caution against over-interpreting inferred priors/losses and guide acquisition of auxiliary constraints. With stronger validation and tooling, it could become a go-to method for experimental design.

Suggestions for improvement
- Validate the proxy: run finite-sample fits (e.g., MLE/VI/MCMC) on many synthetic datasets and show that dispersion/bias matches predictions from E(θ|θ*). Report coverage for key parameters.
- Local analysis: compute/visualize the Hessian/Fisher information of ⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩ at θ* to diagnose sloppiness and parameter trade-offs; report principal degeneracy directions.
- Sensitivity: vary M (prior basis size), mixture component count, and the sensory map family; show robustness of conclusions. Explore model mismatch (e.g., true observer with asymmetric noise but inference model symmetric).
- Computational transparency: provide runtimes, dimensionality, MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, and open-source code.
- Design optimization: demonstrate optimizing Pi (stimulus frequencies) or reward/loss parameters to maximize a criterion (e.g., expected KL, D-optimality), yielding concrete, actionable designs.
- 2AFC: include a discrete-choice variant or at least an ablation showing that the estimation surrogate gives similar identifiability predictions to a proper 2AFC likelihood.

Minor comments
- Clarify when qmeas(x|t) equals pmeas(x|s) composed with f, and when they are decoupled.
- Discuss the identifiability role of λ (lapses) more explicitly.
- Typos/formatting: some references incomplete; equation references to supplements could be tightened.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses an important and under-examined problem with a principled framework and provides nontrivial insights in two canonical domains. Strengthen with additional validation and computational details.

Reviewer confidence: High in methodology/statistics; medium in specific perceptual domains’ experimental nuances.
