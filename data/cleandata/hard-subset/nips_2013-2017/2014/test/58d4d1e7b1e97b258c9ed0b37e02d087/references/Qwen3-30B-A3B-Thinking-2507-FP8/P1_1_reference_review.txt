

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical yet overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: model non-identifiability. The authors demonstrate that the inverse mapping from behavioral data to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is inherently degenerate, as multiple parameter combinations can produce identical behavior. They present a general framework to systematically assess identifiability a priori through a novel class of Bayesian observer models and an efficient method for exploring identifiability landscapes. The framework is validated through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. This work builds on foundational Bayesian Decision Theory literature [1-4] and addresses criticisms of Bayesian models of perception [17-19], while providing a systematic approach to what has previously been handled through ad hoc experimental constraints.

## Strengths

**Originality:** The core contribution—systematically analyzing identifiability a priori for Bayesian models of perception—is novel in this context. While identifiability is a general statistical concept [23], its application to Bayesian models of perception in this systematic, computational framework is original. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work [12, 19] by providing a comprehensive method rather than ad hoc constraints.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical development. The authors derive the expected log-likelihood for model comparison (Eq. 12), which is both theoretically grounded and computationally tractable. The case studies provide strong empirical validation, demonstrating how the framework reveals non-intuitive insights about experimental design (e.g., that a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the assumption of Gaussian motor noise.

**Significance:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a major concern in the field, potentially undermining many previous Bayesian modeling studies. This framework directly addresses this issue, offering a practical tool to improve experimental design and interpretation. The case studies demonstrate immediate applicability to key problems in perception research, with findings that could change how researchers approach Bayesian modeling in perception.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are thorough, and the case studies effectively illustrate the framework's utility. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their analyses, including the parametric form of the sensory map (Eq. 2) and the mixture model for likelihoods (Eq. 3). The supplementary material further enhances clarity.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations:** While the framework is general for one-dimensional estimation tasks, it doesn't directly address more complex scenarios like multi-dimensional stimulus spaces (despite mentioning "symmetries" in Section 2). The authors note this limitation in Section 5, but it's worth emphasizing that this restricts applicability to a subset of perception studies.

**Computational Cost:** The framework requires sampling from the approximate posterior density (Eq. 13) via MCMC for each experimental design. For high-dimensional parameter spaces, this could become computationally intensive. While the authors note computational constraints (M=15 for priors), they don't quantify the scalability of their approach.

**Assumption Sensitivity:** The framework's results depend on the assumed reference observer θ*. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't fully explore how sensitive their identifiability conclusions are to deviations from θ* (e.g., what if the reference loss function is slightly different from assumed?). This could affect the robustness of the experimental design recommendations.

## Specific Comments

1. The case study on temporal interval estimation (Section 4.1) provides a compelling demonstration of the framework's utility, particularly the non-intuitive finding that a MAP-like loss function worsens prior recovery. This insight could significantly impact how researchers interpret interval timing experiments.

2. The speed perception case study (Section 4.2) effectively shows that some Bayesian models are more robust to non-identifiability than others, validating previous work [9, 29] through theoretical analysis.

3. The authors could strengthen their claims by briefly discussing how their framework might handle more complex loss functions beyond the two-component Gaussian mixture (Eq. 6), which they mention as a possible extension in the supplementary material.

4. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how their framework relates to recent work on model selection in neuroscience [31-32], which also addresses identifiability issues but from a different perspective.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental but overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling of perception. The framework is novel, well-developed, and has clear practical implications for experimental design. While the scope is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, this is still a substantial class of perception studies. The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility, and the authors are appropriately cautious about its limitations.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's of high technical quality, clearly written, original in its specific application, and highly significant for the field. The weaknesses noted are minor and do not detract from the paper's core contribution.

**Recommendation:** Accept

## Final Thoughts

This work fills an important gap in the literature by providing a systematic method to assess identifiability before conducting experiments. It has the potential to improve the reliability of Bayesian modeling in perception research, which is crucial given the widespread use of these models. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a genuine problem in the field with significant practical implications. It would be valuable to see extensions of this framework to more complex experimental paradigms in future work.

The authors have made a strong case for the importance of their approach, and their framework provides a valuable tool for researchers in the field. This paper represents a meaningful advancement in the methodological toolkit for Bayesian modeling of perception.
