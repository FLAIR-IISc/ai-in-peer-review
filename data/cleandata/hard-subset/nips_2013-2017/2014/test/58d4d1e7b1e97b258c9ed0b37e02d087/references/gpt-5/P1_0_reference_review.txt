Summary
The paper proposes a general, efficient framework for a priori identifiability analysis of Bayesian observer models in psychophysics. The authors (i) define a flexible 1D estimation model class with: a parametric sensory mapping f(s) to measurement space; a maximum-entropy mixture-of-Gaussians likelihood with controlled variance/skew/kurtosis; a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians; a similarly parameterized (inverted) mixture-Gaussian loss; lapses; and motor/report noise; (ii) derive an analytic expression for the expected log evidence of any model θ when data are generated by θ*, which reduces to a sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions across the stimulus set; (iii) use this quantity to construct an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability; and (iv) demonstrate the tool on two case studies: interval timing (showing strong degeneracies between sensory and motor noise, poor identifiability of the loss width, and that fixing motor noise parameters recovers priors), and speed perception (showing robust recoverability of the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters under multiple contrast conditions, even when decoupling internal from objective noise).

Relation to prior work
The paper is situated in the literature on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Knill & Richards; Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert), model inversion to recover priors/likelihoods/loss, and the critique on identifiability and “just-so” stories in BDT (Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis). It connects to distinguishability/identifiability work in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and to psychophysical designs that aim to break degeneracies (Bayesian transfer). Compared to generic identifiability tools (Fisher Information, profile likelihood, or information-theoretic design), the core novelty is a task-specific, computationally tractable observer class with closed-form components enabling systematic, pre-experiment identifiability mapping for realistic psychophysics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The expected log-likelihood derivation leading to a KL-sum is correct and appropriate for expected evidence under θ*. The modeling choices (mixtures for likelihood and loss; lattice mixture for priors; lapse and motor noise) are reasonable and allow both breadth and computational efficiency. Using E(θ|θ*) as a sampling target is pragmatic, though it is not a Bayesian posterior and omits priors on θ; the authors should be explicit about this and the implications.
- Empirical support: The case studies are thoughtful and reveal non-obvious design implications (e.g., MAP-like losses can harm identifiability in interval timing). However, some claims rely on modeling assumptions that deserve sensitivity analyses (the authors partly acknowledge this).
- Completeness/care: Many implementation details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., maximum-entropy construction of mixtures subject to unimodality; MCMC configuration and diagnostics; computational complexity). The paper would benefit from reporting runtime, mixing behavior, and robustness across seeds.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The decomposition into stages and the final observable presp are clear, and the identifiability criterion is intuitive.
- Some constructs need more explicit constraints: mixtures constrained to be unimodal while matching moments; the feasible range of skew/kurt achievable with two-Gaussian mixtures; constraints and parameterization of prior weights wm on the simplex; how A and B are tied to s0, d and the stimulus set across conditions.
- The description of transforming a 2AFC speed task into an estimation framework should be justified more carefully (beyond a brief citation), as some identifiability conclusions could depend on paradigm.

Originality
- The combination of (i) a unified, expressive observer parameterization with (ii) an analytic expected log-likelihood criterion used to (iii) systematically compare experimental designs for identifiability appears novel in this domain. While related to distinguishability metrics and information-theoretic design, the paper makes a valuable, domain-specific contribution tailored to Bayesian-perception models.

Significance
- The framework addresses a central barrier for interpreting fits of Bayesian observer models: non-identifiability among priors, likelihoods, and losses. Providing a practical tool to audit and improve experimental design is important for computational neuroscience and psychophysics, and is likely to be adopted.
- The case studies give concrete guidance (e.g., measure/fix motor noise; be cautious about inferring loss width; robustness of slow-speed prior inference under multi-contrast designs).

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the identifiability problem and an actionable criterion based on expected KL.
- Flexible yet efficient observer model enabling analytic/numerically stable computations.
- Insightful case studies revealing non-trivial design trade-offs.
- Direct relevance to ongoing debates about interpretability of Bayesian model fits.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- E(θ|θ*) is not a true posterior; clarify terminology, state any implicit priors, and discuss how this affects MCMC exploration and conclusions.
- Provide computational details: dimensionality of θ in each study, MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, runtime scaling with Nexp and M.
- Validate against alternative identifiability analyses (e.g., Fisher Information Matrix/profile likelihood/sloppiness) on at least a reduced model to cross-check conclusions.
- The maximum-entropy/unimodality-constrained mixture construction needs more transparency: feasible moment ranges; how the remaining DoF is chosen; numerical stability.
- Expand the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping or, ideally, include a discrete-choice variant (the paper mentions this as future work, but an example would strengthen claims).
- Consider linking to formal Bayesian optimal experimental design: the same KL objective suggests principled stimulus selection (e.g., Chaloner & Verdinelli, 1995; Lindley, 1956; more recent BOED/active learning), which could turn the analysis into prescriptive design.
- Discuss robustness to model mismatch (e.g., non-Gaussian motor noise; heavier-tailed sensory noise; alternative loss families).
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues) and should be corrected.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a practical and theoretically grounded tool.
- Novel combination of modeling and identifiability analysis; actionable insights for experiment design.
- Broadly relevant to NeurIPS audiences in computational neuroscience, cognitive modeling, and experimental design.

Cons for acceptance
- Some technical choices under-specified; empirical evaluation limited to simulations with assumed θ*; no validation against alternative identifiability metrics.
- The 2AFC reinterpretation could be better justified.
- Reproducibility details (code, diagnostics) are missing from the main text.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. This is a valuable, well-motivated contribution that, with clarifications and added methodological details, can have meaningful impact on how Bayesian observer models are designed and interpreted.
