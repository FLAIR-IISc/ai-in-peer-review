This paper presents a computational framework for assessing, a priori, the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. It addresses the well-known but often-overlooked problem that different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover an observer's internal representations from data. The authors propose a two-part solution: first, a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of representing a wide range of sensory mappings, noise characteristics, priors, and loss functions; second, an efficient method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood landscape around a hypothetical "true" observer model. This is cleverly shown to be equivalent to computing the KL-divergence between the response distributions of the true and tested models. The framework's utility is demonstrated through two compelling case studies: one in time perception, used to compare the efficacy of different experimental designs, and one in speed perception, used to validate the robustness of a classic finding about a "slow-speed prior."

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The paper tackles a fundamental, critical issue in cognitive science and computational neuroscience. The "Bayesian brain" hypothesis has moved from a normative framework to a process model, with many studies claiming to have recovered internal representations like priors or likelihoods. However, critiques of this approach (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) have highlighted the serious problem of non-identifiability. This paper provides a concrete, constructive, and quantitative tool to address these criticisms head-on, moving the field towards more rigorous and principled modeling. Its potential impact is high, as it could become a standard tool for designing psychophysical experiments.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** While the problem of non-identifiability is not new, the proposed solution of a systematic, *a priori* framework for testing it is highly novel. Previous work has often dealt with identifiability post-hoc or with simpler models. The authors' contribution is to provide a general-purpose, computational methodology that allows researchers to proactively design experiments that mitigate these issues. The combination of a flexible observer model with an efficient MCMC-based exploration of the KL-divergence landscape is an elegant and powerful approach.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The framework is technically sound and well-conceived. The observer model is thoughtfully constructed, using mixtures of Gaussians to flexibly approximate arbitrary distributions for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, while retaining computational tractability. The derivation of the identifiability metric from the expected log-likelihood (Eq. 11) to the KL-divergence (Eq. 12) is correct and provides a strong theoretical foundation. The application to the case studies is convincing and yields non-trivial insights.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, the model is built up in a logical, step-by-step fashion (sensation, perception, decision, response), and the method for assessing identifiability is explained lucidly. The case studies serve as excellent illustrations of the framework's practical value.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Reliance on the Reference Observer (`θ*`):** The entire analysis is conditioned on the choice of a "reference observer" `θ*`. The conclusions about which experimental design is better are, therefore, dependent on the assumptions made about this ground truth observer. The paper could be strengthened by discussing the robustness of its conclusions to this choice. For instance, in the time perception example, does the surprising result that a MAP-like loss function worsens prior recovery hold if the reference observer's sensory noise (`σ`) or motor noise (`ρ1`) were different? A sensitivity analysis or at least a more thorough discussion of this dependence would be valuable.

2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This is a common paradigm, but a large portion of psychophysics relies on discrete-choice tasks, such as two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors acknowledge this as future work, but the conversion of the Stocker & Simoncelli (2009) 2AFC study into an "equivalent estimation task" is a significant simplification that merits more discussion. The identifiability properties of a model are strongly tied to the task structure. It is not obvious that the robust identifiability found for the estimation task would transfer directly to the original 2AFC experiment, where information is inherently reduced to a binary choice. This point should be highlighted more explicitly as a limitation of the current application.

3.  **Model Complexity and its Own Identifiability:** The proposed observer model is very powerful due to its high number of parameters (Eq. 10). While this provides flexibility, it also raises questions about the model's own internal degeneracies. The results in Figure 2c nicely illustrate a trade-off between sensory noise (`σ`) and motor noise (`ρ1`), a known challenge. Are there other, more complex degeneracies between, for example, the skewness of the likelihood (`γ̃`) and the skewness of the loss function (`γ`_l_)? The paper demonstrates the framework's ability to find these degeneracies, but a deeper discussion of the common trade-offs inherent to this specific flexible model class would be informative.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper makes a significant and novel contribution to the methodology of computational modeling in perception and cognition. It provides a much-needed, principled framework to assess and improve the identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a cornerstone of the field. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and the case studies effectively demonstrate its practical utility for guiding experimental design.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and pervasive methodological problem in Bayesian cognitive science.
*   Proposes a novel, general, and computationally tractable framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
*   The technical approach is elegant and well-founded.
*   The paper is clear, well-structured, and provides insightful, non-trivial results in its case studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is contingent on assumptions about the "true" reference observer, and robustness is not fully explored.
*   The framework's applicability is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks, and its application to a 2AFC study involves a major simplification.

Despite the limitations, the strengths of this paper are overwhelming. It is a mature piece of work that provides a valuable tool and a new standard for rigor in the field. It will likely be highly cited and influential for researchers designing and interpreting experiments through the lens of Bayesian models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
