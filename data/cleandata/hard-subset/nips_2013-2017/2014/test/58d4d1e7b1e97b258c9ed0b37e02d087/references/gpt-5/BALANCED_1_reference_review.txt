Summary
This paper tackles a central challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) when inferring them from behavioral data. The authors (i) formalize a general, 1D, estimation-based observer model that parametrizes sensation (a monotone mapping f with stimulus-dependent noise modeled as a 2-Gaussian mixture by moment constraints), the prior (Gaussian mixture on a lattice), the internal likelihood (possibly distinct from the true measurement), the loss (mixture of inverted Gaussians), lapses, and motor noise; (ii) derive an a priori identifiability measure based on the expected log-likelihood of data generated by a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions under θ* and θ across stimuli; and (iii) sample an “approximate expected posterior” over parameters to visualize identifiability landscapes and compare experimental designs. Two case studies are presented: interval timing and speed perception. The framework diagnoses strong degeneracies in interval timing unless additional constraints (e.g., independent motor noise measurements) are available, and it supports the robustness of slow-speed prior inference in speed perception.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on longstanding debates about the interpretability of Bayesian observer models (e.g., model degeneracies and “just-so stories”), and on the psychological literature on model distinguishability and identifiability (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung). It is also related to Bayesian experimental design and information-theoretic criteria (expected log-likelihood/KL divergences), as well as earlier observer modeling in vision and time perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.). The contribution lies in combining a broadly expressive yet computationally efficient parametric observer class with an a priori identifiability analysis that can guide design choices.

Strengths
- Timely problem: Identifiability of priors/likelihoods/losses is a core issue in interpreting Bayesian perceptual models, and this work provides a concrete, general tool to assess it before data collection.
- Sound criterion: The expected log-likelihood under θ* yielding a sum of KL divergences is principled, interpretable, and scales with trial count; using it to sample an “identifiability posterior” is pragmatic.
- Flexible, efficient observer: The mixture-of-Gaussians parameterization across stages plus a monotone sensory mapping allows analytic or fast numerical computation of response distributions and expected loss.
- Case studies are informative: The interval timing analysis reveals nontrivial design implications (e.g., that narrowing the loss can worsen prior recovery; motor-vs-sensory noise confounding), while the speed example supports the recoverability of the slow-speed prior.
- Practical design insight: The framework can rank alternative designs and identify which parameters must be constrained or validated independently.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Scope and sensitivity: The identifiability conclusions depend on the choice of θ*. Although the authors note this, the paper would benefit from systematic sensitivity analyses (e.g., sweeping key θ* dimensions such as lapse rates, f(s) form, prior smoothness) and reporting how conclusions change.
- Model misspecification: Identifiability is studied within a specific parametric class (e.g., 2-Gaussian mixtures, fixed M=15 for priors, specific f(s)). It is unclear how robust the conclusions are if the true observer lies outside this class; some “out-of-class” simulations would strengthen the claims.
- Comparisons to established design criteria: The method effectively evaluates expected cross-entropy; comparison against Fisher information, profile likelihoods/sloppiness, or mutual information criteria from Bayesian optimal experimental design would contextualize and validate the approach.
- Reproducibility details: The “maximum-entropy under moment constraints with unimodality” mixture construction is central but deferred to the supplement; guarantees of existence/uniqueness and implementation details matter for adoption. Runtime/convergence diagnostics for the MCMC over E(θ|θ*) are not reported.
- 2AFC to estimation reduction: The speed example relies on an equivalence to an estimation task. More detail on this transformation and checks that conclusions are consistent with a direct 2AFC formulation would improve credibility.

Clarity
- The model is heavy with notation and cross-references to the supplement. A concise algorithmic summary (inputs, outputs, computational steps) and clearer guidance for practitioners (e.g., how to pick M, mixture components, priors over θ, convergence checks) would aid adoption.
- Some decisions (e.g., drawing lapse responses from the prior) should be justified empirically, as they can materially affect identifiability.

Originality
- Using KL-based expected log-likelihood for identifiability is not new per se, and parameter-recovery-style simulations are standard. The contribution here is the specific, unified observer parameterization that permits efficient computation and its application to design comparisons in this domain.

Significance
- For the perception/psychophysics community, this is a useful, actionable framework that could prevent overconfident interpretations of inferred priors/likelihoods/losses and improve experiment design. For the broader NeurIPS audience, the methodological novelty is moderate but the work addresses a real, recurring problem in model-based cognitive/neuroscience research.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses over θ* and over model class choices (e.g., M, number of mixture components, alternative f(s)).
- Compare with other identifiability/design criteria (Fisher information, mutual information) and show whether recommended designs differ.
- Formalize design optimization: beyond ranking a few candidate designs, demonstrate optimizing stimulus distributions, number of conditions, or reward structures to maximize information about target parameters.
- Include an explicit 2AFC version or validate the estimation reduction empirically/simulated.
- Release code and document computational costs, MCMC settings, and diagnostics.
- Explore robustness to lapse modeling and motor noise assumptions (e.g., non-Gaussian report noise).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underappreciated issue with concrete tools.
- Provides actionable guidance with compelling, non-obvious findings in interval timing.
- Framework seems general, efficient, and extensible.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on specific parametric assumptions and on θ* without thorough sensitivity analysis.
- Limited comparison to established optimal design/identifiability methodologies.
- Some key implementation details deferred to the supplement; reproducibility and generality not fully demonstrated.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a practical, principled framework likely to impact how Bayesian perceptual models are fit and how experiments are designed. Strengthening empirical validation, sensitivity analyses, and connections to optimal experimental design would make the contribution more convincing and broadly relevant.
