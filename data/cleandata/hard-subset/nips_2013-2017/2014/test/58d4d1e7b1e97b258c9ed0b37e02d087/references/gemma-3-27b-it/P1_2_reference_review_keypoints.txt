- Tackles non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models and presents an a priori identifiability testing framework.  
- Demonstrates that multiple prior/likelihood/loss combinations can produce identical behavior, causing ambiguity in inference.  
- Framework systematically explores parameter space to predict the effectiveness of experimental constraints before data collection.  
- Implements a flexible class of Bayesian observer models applicable to various sensory estimation tasks.  
- Validated with two case studies (time interval estimation, speed perception) showing practical utility and experimental design implications.  
- Builds on Bayesian modeling and identifiability literature, moving from ad-hoc constraints to a predictive, systematic approach.  
- Addresses a core interpretability problem in Bayesian models of perception—high scientific importance.  
- Provides a principled, quantifiable method for assessing identifiability a priori.  
- Uses parametric forms for priors/likelihoods/losses to balance generality and tractability.  
- Case studies highlight the impact of loss-function assumptions and potential pitfalls in inference.  
- Well-written, logically structured, with concise derivations and comprehensive supplementary material.  
- Computationally demanding to explore full parameter space; needs more discussion of scaling and practical limits.  
- Relies on simplifying observer-model assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, specific functional forms) that may limit applicability.  
- Focuses mainly on parameter identifiability, not on identifiability of broader theoretical constructs or qualitative features.  
- Limited number of experimental designs explored; broader design-space analysis could yield more insights.  
- Reliance on KL divergence for model support may be sensitive to distribution choices; alternative metrics should be considered.  
- Quality: strong; technical soundness and well-supported analysis.  
- Clarity: good; readable and well-organized.  
- Originality: high; novel systematic approach to a long-standing problem.  
- Significance: high; likely to impact researchers using Bayesian perceptual models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses critical limitation, novel systematic framework, compelling case studies, clear and technically sound, high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, simplifying model assumptions, narrow focus on parameter identifiability.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address computational scaling, model assumptions, experimental design scope, and KL divergence interpretation.
