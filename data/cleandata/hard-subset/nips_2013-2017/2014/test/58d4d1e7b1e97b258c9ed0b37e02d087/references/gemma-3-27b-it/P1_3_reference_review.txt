## Peer Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a crucial and often overlooked problem in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate, through a theoretical framework, that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can lead to identical behavioral predictions within a Bayesian observer model. They introduce a method for *a priori* investigating identifiability, and showcase its application to time and speed perception tasks. The core idea is to analyze the sensitivity of model predictions to variations in parameter values, effectively mapping out the identifiability landscape. This work provides a valuable tool for researchers aiming to reliably recover internal representations from behavioral data, moving beyond simply fitting models to explicitly assessing the robustness of the recovered parameters.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the established foundation of Bayesian models in perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 2003; Trommershäuser et al., 2005). It acknowledges the existing work on inferring internal representations like priors (e.g., Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Girshick et al., 2001) but addresses the fundamental issue of whether these inferences are uniquely determined. The authors correctly highlight the historical debate surrounding "Bayesian fundamentalism" vs. "enlightenment" (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) and position their work as a means to move beyond merely *postulating* Bayesian computations to rigorously *testing* their validity. They reference relevant work on non-identifiability in psychology (Anderson, 1983) and statistical modeling (Navarro et al., 2004) but importantly adapt these concepts specifically to the context of perceptual inference.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Addressing model identifiability is critical for the field. Many studies claim to have "recovered" neural representations based on Bayesian modeling, and this paper provides a framework to assess the reliability of those claims.
* **General Framework:** The proposed framework is relatively general, applicable to a broad class of psychophysical tasks involving one-dimensional stimulus estimation.
* **Novel Approach:** The method of exploring identifiability through sampling the approximate posterior density (using MCMC) is a clever and practical solution to the difficulty of analytically evaluating the integral in Eq. 12.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples and demonstrates the framework’s potential utility. The example concerning the effect of loss function shape on parameter recovery is particularly insightful.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical development appears rigorous and the derivations are referenced in supplementary material, increasing transparency.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity of the topic, the paper is generally well-written and organized.  The figure illustrating the model stages (Fig. 1) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Complexity:** While the framework is general, the proposed observer model is *very* complex, with a large number of parameters (θ).  This raises concerns about the computational feasibility of applying the framework to many real-world scenarios, and potentially exacerbates the identifiability problem itself.  The authors acknowledge this to some extent, but it remains a significant hurdle.
* **Assumptions:** The model relies on several strong assumptions, such as the specific parametric forms of the sensory map (Eq. 2), prior (Eq. 4), and loss function (Eq. 6). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions isn’t fully explored.  For example, the choice of a two-Gaussian mixture for the likelihood and loss function might not be appropriate for all tasks.
* **Limited Scope of Examples:** The two case studies are well-chosen, but it would be beneficial to see the framework applied to a wider range of tasks, particularly those with more complex stimuli or response options.
* **Practical Implementation:**  While the authors describe the method, a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required and the specific implementation choices would be helpful for other researchers attempting to use the framework.
* **Focus on Ideal Observer:** The analysis focuses on the identifiability of *ideal* observer parameters.  Real observers are undoubtedly suboptimal. The extent to which the framework’s findings generalize to more realistic models with biases or limitations is unclear.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental and often ignored problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Provides a novel and potentially powerful method for *a priori* evaluating the identifiability of model parameters.
* Demonstrates the framework’s utility with concrete examples.
* Technically sound and clearly presented.
* Offers a valuable tool for improving experimental design and interpreting the results of Bayesian modeling studies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the model could limit its practical applicability.
* The reliance on strong assumptions could compromise the generality of the results.
* The scope of the examples is somewhat limited.
* Requires significant computational resources.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong accept** paper. The authors identify a critical problem, propose a novel and theoretically sound solution, and demonstrate its potential utility. While the complexity of the model and the reliance on specific assumptions are valid concerns, they do not fundamentally undermine the significance of the contribution. The framework offers a valuable tool for researchers in the field and has the potential to significantly improve the rigor and reliability of Bayesian modeling studies.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, such as a more detailed discussion of the computational requirements and a sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different assumptions.



**Confidence:** 8/10
