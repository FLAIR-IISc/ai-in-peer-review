

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) when fitting to behavioral data. The authors demonstrate that the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior. While previous work has identified this degeneracy (e.g., [19]), the paper provides the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis, enabling researchers to assess whether their experimental design can reliably recover model parameters before collecting data.

The authors develop a general Bayesian observer model class that covers sensorimotor estimation tasks with one-dimensional stimulus magnitudes (e.g., duration, speed). The model is constructed in four stages: sensation (stimulus to internal measurement), perception (prior and likelihood combination), decision-making (loss function application), and response (motor noise). Crucially, the model uses a flexible parametrization that allows efficient computation of observer behavior. The key contribution is a method to assess identifiability by calculating the expected log likelihood of candidate models given data generated by a reference model, formalized as 〈log Pr(D|θ)〉 = -Ntr Σ Pi · DKL(presp(r|si;θ*)||presp(r|si;θ)) + const. This framework enables systematic exploration of the identifiability landscape for different experimental designs.

The paper demonstrates the framework's utility through two case studies. In temporal interval estimation, they show that the baseline experimental design (testing in both Short and Long conditions) performs poorly at recovering priors due to non-identifiability between sensory noise and motor noise parameters. They further demonstrate that imposing a narrow loss function (MAP strategy) actually worsens reconstruction quality. In speed perception, they find that the inference of slow-speed priors and noise characteristics is relatively robust, providing theoretical validation for prior findings.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a practical problem that undermines the validity of many Bayesian models in perception research. The framework is technically sound, with clear mathematical derivation and empirical validation through two well-chosen case studies. The authors carefully distinguish their approach from previous work on model identifiability (e.g., [23]), demonstrating its specific utility for Bayesian observer models in perception.

Strengths:
- Clearly identifies a genuine problem in the field that has been acknowledged but not systematically addressed
- Develops a general, computationally efficient framework specifically tailored to Bayesian models of perception
- Provides a mathematically rigorous approach to a priori identifiability assessment
- Demonstrates practical utility through two well-executed case studies
- Well-written with clear structure and sufficient technical detail (supplementary material provides additional derivations)

Weaknesses:
- The framework is specific to Bayesian observer models in perception, limiting broader applicability to other model classes
- The case studies, while illustrative, are somewhat narrow in scope (only two specific tasks)
- The paper doesn't fully address how the framework scales to more complex models with many parameters
- The assumption that the reference observer θ* is known might not always be valid in practice
- The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity for larger parameter spaces

Novelty is strong for the specific domain of Bayesian models of perception. While model identifiability is a general statistical problem, the authors develop a tailored approach that addresses the specific structure of Bayesian observer models used in perception research. The paper significantly advances the field by providing a systematic method for experimental design that directly addresses non-identifiability concerns.

Significance is high for the perception research community. Many studies in this field rely on Bayesian modeling to infer internal representations, but the validity of these inferences is undermined by the non-identifiability problem. This framework provides a practical tool to improve experimental design and interpretation of results. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can lead to non-intuitive insights about experimental design (e.g., narrow loss functions may be detrimental to prior recovery).

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it presents a rigorous statistical framework with clear implications for experimental design in a core area of cognitive science. The technical contribution is substantial, and the practical utility for researchers is evident.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant, long-recognized problem in perception research
- Presents a novel, well-founded framework with clear technical contributions
- Demonstrates practical utility through two case studies
- Provides actionable insights for experimental design
- Well-written with strong mathematical foundation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope to Bayesian models of perception (though this is appropriate for the field)
- Case studies could be expanded to more diverse tasks
- Doesn't fully address scalability to very high-dimensional parameter spaces

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that addresses an important practical problem in the field. The framework is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and has clear practical implications for experimental design in perception research. The paper makes a significant contribution to the methodology of Bayesian modeling in perception, and its insights are likely to be widely adopted by researchers in the field.
