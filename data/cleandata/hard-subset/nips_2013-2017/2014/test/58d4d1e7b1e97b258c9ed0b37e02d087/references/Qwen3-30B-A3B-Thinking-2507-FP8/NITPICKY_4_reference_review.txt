

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper proposes a framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, addressing the critical issue that multiple combinations of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors present a general class of observer models and a method to systematically explore identifiability a priori for experimental design optimization.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies a genuine and important problem in the field of Bayesian modeling in perception. The authors make a compelling case for why identifiability is a critical issue that has been overlooked in prior work. The framework they propose is well-motivated, and the two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) demonstrate the practical utility of their approach. The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear sections that build logically toward the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Mathematical Rigor and Justification:** The core claim in the motivation section (equation 1) that "the solution of Eq. 1 is the same for any triplet of prior qprior(s)·φ1(s), likelihood qmeas(x|s)·φ2(s), and loss function L(ŝ, s)·φ3(s), where ∏3 i=1 φi(s) = c" lacks mathematical justification. The authors fail to prove this statement or provide a clear explanation of why this degeneracy occurs. This is a fundamental claim that underpins the entire paper but is presented without proof or sufficient elaboration.

2. **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, in equation (2), the expression "s0 d √ e t−B A − 1" is poorly formatted and contains ambiguous spacing. The transition between task space (s) and internal measurement space (t) is not consistently explained, and the relationship between these variables is sometimes confusingly presented.

3. **Incomplete Technical Details:** The authors claim their model "allows for a fast numerical solution" (section 2.3) but provide no computational complexity analysis or runtime comparisons. The statement "we can compute through Eqs. 3–8" the response probability density (equation 9) is misleading without a closed-form expression or clear computational procedure. The supplementary material is referenced multiple times but isn't provided, making it impossible to assess whether the computational details are sufficient.

4. **Misleading Claims:** The paper claims their model "covers a large representational basis with a single parametrization" (section 2), but the parameter space is actually quite large (equation 10 lists 22 parameters), and the authors don't clearly explain how this is "efficient" compared to existing approaches.

5. **Citation Misuse:** In section 4.1, the authors state "this worked example also shows how our framework can be used to rank experimental designs by the quality of the inferred features of interest (here, the recovered priors), and to identify parameters that may critically affect the inference." They cite [12] for this claim, but [12] is a paper on Bayesian transfer, not on experimental design optimization for identifiability.

6. **Formatting Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous formatting issues, including inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators, inconsistent use of parentheses (e.g., "Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT)" vs. "Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT)"), and inconsistent citation styles in the references.

7. **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper claims to present "a novel class of observer models" but doesn't compare their approach to existing methods for identifiability analysis. The authors cite [23] as a reference for generic techniques of analysis but fail to clearly differentiate their approach from this prior work.

8. **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper states that "the loss width parameter σ` is only weakly identifiable" (section 4.1) but doesn't provide any quantitative evidence for this claim beyond the posterior density plot in Figure 2d, which is not sufficiently detailed to support such a strong statement.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian modeling of perception, the methodological and technical shortcomings significantly undermine the contribution. The core mathematical claim lacks proof, the notation is inconsistent, the computational details are insufficient, and the paper makes unsubstantiated claims that aren't supported by the evidence provided. The paper's theoretical contribution is limited by the lack of rigorous mathematical justification for its central claim.

The paper would benefit from substantial revisions, including: (1) providing a formal proof of the identifiability degeneracy claim; (2) resolving all notation inconsistencies; (3) providing complete computational details for reproducibility; and (4) clearly differentiating the approach from prior work on identifiability.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The paper presents a potentially valuable framework but fails to meet the rigorous standards expected of a NeurIPS paper. The methodological gaps and technical inconsistencies are too significant to overlook, especially given the paper's focus on a precise mathematical problem. While the problem addressed is important, the execution does not sufficiently justify the contribution. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these fundamental issues before considering resubmission.
