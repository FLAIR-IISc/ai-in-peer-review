This paper presents a computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors correctly identify that the inverse problem of inferring internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavior is ill-posed due to inherent degeneracies. Their proposed solution is twofold: first, they introduce a flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks that is computationally efficient. Second, they propose a method to map the identifiability landscape by sampling from an "approximate expected posterior density," a metric based on the expected log-likelihood (or KL-divergence) between a ground-truth reference model and other models in the parameter space. The framework is demonstrated on two case studies from the literature: interval timing and speed perception, yielding insights into which experimental designs are more likely to successfully constrain model parameters.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The issue of model identifiability is fundamental to computational modeling in cognitive science and neuroscience, yet it is often addressed post-hoc or ignored. The paper’s goal of creating a systematic, *a priori* framework to guide experimental design is highly valuable and timely.
2.  **Novel Computational Approach:** The proposed method of using the expected log-likelihood, and its connection to KL-divergence, to map the identifiability landscape is an elegant and efficient alternative to brute-force, simulation-based approaches. This is a solid technical contribution.
3.  **Flexible Observer Model:** The parameterized observer model is comprehensive, encompassing non-linear sensory mappings, non-Gaussian likelihoods and priors, flexible loss functions, and motor noise. This flexibility allows the framework to be applied to a wide range of existing studies.
4.  **Insightful Case Studies:** The applications to interval timing and speed perception are well-chosen. They concretely demonstrate the framework's utility, showing how it can reveal non-obvious parameter degeneracies (e.g., between sensory and motor noise in the timing task) and validate the robustness of previous findings (e.g., the slow-speed prior).

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising direction, the paper is undermined by a significant number of technical ambiguities, methodological gaps, and presentation flaws that detract from its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Technical Rigor and Clarity:**
    *   The central metric in Equation (13), termed the "approximate expected posterior density," is statistically ill-defined. It is not a posterior density in any formal sense, as it is not conditioned on observed data. It is a similarity metric between generative models. This terminological imprecision is misleading and suggests a lack of statistical rigor.
    *   The justification for using two-Gaussian mixtures for the likelihood (Eq. 3) and loss function (Eq. 6) via a "maximum-entropy approach" is vague. The text claims the form is chosen by fixing the first four moments and then finding a local maximum-entropy solution, but the details of this procedure are omitted. This is a critical detail of the model construction that is not adequately explained.
    *   The conversion of the 2AFC task from the speed perception literature [9] into an "equivalent estimation task" is a crucial step for the second case study. However, this conversion is mentioned only in passing with a citation. The validity of this entire case study hinges on the assumptions made during this conversion, which are not stated or justified in the paper. This is a major methodological omission.

2.  **Under-specified Model and Inference:**
    *   The MCMC procedure used to sample from Eq. (13) is not detailed. Given the high dimensionality of the parameter space (θ contains over 20 parameters), this is a non-trivial sampling problem. Issues such as sampler choice, convergence diagnostics, and potential multi-modality of the target distribution are not discussed, making it impossible to assess the reliability of the reported identifiability landscapes.
    *   The choice of M=15 for the number of basis functions in the prior (Eq. 4) is arbitrary. The sensitivity of the results to this key hyperparameter is not analyzed.
    *   The parametric form for the sensory map `f(s)` in Eq. 2 is specific. The paper does not discuss how robust the conclusions might be to this particular choice versus other plausible mappings.

3.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   There are several minor but distracting inconsistencies. For instance, the loss function in Equation (1) is written as `L(s, ŝ)`, but in the subsequent paragraph, it becomes `L(ŝ, s)`.
    *   The footnote on page 4 is incorrectly formatted as an endnote within the main text body, disrupting the flow of reading.
    *   The text frequently switches between stimulus space (`s`) and internal measurement space (`t`) without consistent and clear notation (e.g., `qprior(s)` vs. `qprior(t)`), which can lead to confusion.
    *   The provided reference list is incomplete (e.g., reference [1] is missing), which hinders verification of claims.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   The paper tackles an important and difficult problem in computational modeling.
    *   The core idea of an *a priori* identifiability analysis framework is a significant contribution.
    *   The analytical approach using KL-divergence is clever and computationally appealing.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The work suffers from a lack of technical precision, particularly in its central statistical definitions and methodological justifications.
    *   Crucial methodological details are omitted (e.g., the 2AFC-to-estimation conversion, MCMC implementation), severely limiting reproducibility and trust in the results.
    *   The reliance on specific, unjustified parametric forms without sensitivity analysis questions the generality of the findings.
    *   The accumulation of minor notational and formatting errors suggests a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript.

### Decision

While the paper addresses a significant problem with a promising approach, the execution is not yet at the level required for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The lack of methodological transparency, imprecise statistical language, and numerous minor flaws combine to create a substantial barrier to understanding and trusting the paper's conclusions. The work is interesting, but it feels more like a preliminary report than a complete, rigorously vetted piece of research. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to significantly revise the manuscript to address the concerns about rigor, clarity, and reproducibility before resubmission.
