This paper introduces a formal framework for analyzing the *a priori* practical identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly identify a fundamental and often-overlooked problem in this line of research: the inverse problem of inferring an observer's internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function) from behavioral data is ill-posed. Multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, leading to a severe non-identifiability issue that can undermine the conclusions of many studies. Previous work has acknowledged this problem and used experimental constraints to mitigate it, but a systematic method for evaluating the effectiveness of these constraints before data collection has been lacking.

The core contribution of this work is a two-part framework to fill this gap. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. Key components like the sensory likelihood, the observer's prior, and the loss function are modeled as mixtures of Gaussians, allowing them to capture a wide range of complex, asymmetric, and non-Gaussian forms. Second, they propose a principled metric for identifiability based on the expected log-likelihood, which is shown to be equivalent to the negative KL-divergence between the response distributions of a "true" reference observer and any other model. By sampling from an approximate expected posterior distribution derived from this metric using MCMC, the framework allows one to map out the "identifiability landscape" and assess which parameters can be reliably recovered for a given experimental design. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two compelling case studies from the literature: recovering priors in a time interval estimation task and inferring a "slow-speed prior" in visual perception.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance to computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The conclusions drawn from fitting Bayesian models are only as strong as the models are identifiable. By providing a tool to formally assess identifiability *before* running an experiment, this work has the potential to significantly increase the rigor and reliability of research in this area. It effectively provides a form of "power analysis" for model recovery, which could become an essential step in designing model-based psychophysics experiments.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** While the general problem of model identifiability is not new, this paper is, to my knowledge, the first to propose a systematic, computational framework for tackling it *a priori* within the specific context of Bayesian models of perception. The combination of a highly flexible generative model for behavior with a principled identifiability analysis is novel and powerful.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The framework is technically sound and well-conceived. The decision to model all components as mixtures of Gaussians is a clever choice that balances representational flexibility with the computational tractability required for the MCMC-based analysis. The derivation of the identifiability metric from the expected log-likelihood is principled. The application to the case studies is thorough and yields concrete, non-trivial insights.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, the complex model is described in a logical, step-by-step manner, and the results are presented in an intuitive way. The figures are effective in visualizing the core concepts and findings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The entire analysis is conditioned on the choice of a "true" reference observer, θ*. The conclusions about the relative merit of different experimental designs could be sensitive to this choice. For example, in the time perception study, would the MTR design still be superior if the true observer's lapse rate (λ) were much higher, or their sensory noise (σ) much lower? The authors rightly state that the framework allows for testing such robustness, but the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion or demonstration of the sensitivity of the conclusions to the choice of θ*. This is not a flaw in the framework itself, but a crucial consideration for its practical application that deserves more emphasis.

2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently demonstrated for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of the literature, many foundational studies in perception use discrete choice paradigms like two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors mention extending the model to such paradigms as future work. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion of the specific challenges this would entail. For instance, would the KL-divergence metric seamlessly apply to discrete response distributions? What modifications to the observer model's decision stage would be required?

3.  **Computational Cost:** The proposed analysis involves MCMC sampling in a high-dimensional parameter space, which is likely to be computationally expensive. For the framework to be adopted by the community, it is important to have a sense of its practical feasibility. A brief mention of the computational resources and time required to run the analyses for the case studies would be a helpful addition.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pro:**
*   Addresses the critical and fundamental problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
*   Provides a novel, principled, and practical framework for the *a priori* evaluation and optimization of experimental designs.
*   The flexible observer model is a significant technical contribution in its own right.
*   The case studies are well-executed and provide compelling, non-obvious insights with direct relevance to existing literature.
*   High potential for broad impact on the methodology and rigor of the field.

**Con:**
*   The analysis is conditional on a chosen "true" model, and the sensitivity to this choice is not explored.
*   The current scope is limited to 1D estimation tasks, with challenges for generalization not fully discussed.
*   The practical computational cost of the method is not addressed.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the field. It is technically strong, conceptually important, and clearly communicated. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to the scope of the discussion than to fundamental flaws in the approach. The proposed framework provides a much-needed tool for improving the quality of model-based inference in perception and cognition. I strongly recommend acceptance.
