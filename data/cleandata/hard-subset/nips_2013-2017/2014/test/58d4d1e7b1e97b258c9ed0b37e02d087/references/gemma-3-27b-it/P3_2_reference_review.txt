## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the inherent non-identifiability of parameters given observed behavior. The authors present a novel framework for *a priori* assessing the identifiability of Bayesian observer models, focusing on a flexible class of models encompassing a wide range of sensory estimation tasks. They develop a method based on Kullback-Leibler divergence to quantify the likelihood of recovering true model parameters given alternative, behaviorally-equivalent models. They demonstrate the framework's utility through applications to time and speed perception tasks, revealing insights into experimental design and parameter recovery challenges.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work applying Bayesian models to perception (Knill & Richards, 1996; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2012), and acknowledges the longstanding concerns about parameter identifiability (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).  It directly addresses the degeneracy identified by previous researchers (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2000) where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavior.  However, unlike purely theoretical analyses (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), this work provides a computational framework for proactively investigating identifiability *before* data collection, a significant step forward. The use of KL divergence as a measure of model distinguishability is standard in model selection and related to broader Bayesian model comparison techniques (Spiegelhalter et al., 2002). The work also builds on existing methods for constraining model fitting in perception (e.g., Körding & Wolpert, 2004), but provides a systematic way to evaluate the *effectiveness* of these constraints.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is fundamental to the validity of inferences drawn from Bayesian models of perception.  Addressing this problem is crucial for moving beyond purely descriptive models to truly understanding the underlying cognitive processes.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a valuable tool for researchers to assess the potential for parameter recovery *before* investing time and resources in experiments.  The use of KL divergence and MCMC sampling offers a principled and computationally tractable approach.
* **Generality and Flexibility:** The defined class of observer models is impressively flexible, encompassing a wide range of tasks and accommodating various prior distributions, likelihood functions, and loss functions.
* **Illustrative Applications:** The case studies in time and speed perception demonstrate the practical utility of the framework and yield interesting insights into experimental design.  The findings regarding the impact of loss function constraints are particularly noteworthy.
* **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a detailed mathematical formulation of the framework.  The supplementary material appears to be comprehensive and helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors claim efficiency, exploring the parameter space even with MCMC can be computationally demanding, especially for high-dimensional models. This could limit the applicability of the framework to very complex scenarios. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational limitations and potential optimization strategies.
* **Model Assumptions:** The framework relies on specific assumptions about the model structure (e.g., the parametric form of the sensory map, the Gaussian mixture representations). While these assumptions are motivated and allow for analytical tractability, they may not hold for all perceptual tasks.  A sensitivity analysis to these assumptions would strengthen the findings.
* **Reference Observer Dependence:** The identifiability assessment is relative to a *reference* observer (θ*). The choice of this reference observer can influence the results.  The authors should explicitly address this point and potentially explore the sensitivity of the results to different choices of θ*.
* **Limited Exploration of Task Complexity:** The two case studies are relatively well-understood.  Applying the framework to more complex and less-constrained perceptual tasks (e.g., those involving multiple cues or ambiguous stimuli) would further demonstrate its power and limitations.
* **Link to Neural Data:** While the paper focuses on behavioral data, a discussion of how the identifiability analysis relates to the interpretation of neural data (e.g., linking inferred priors to neural representations) would be valuable, given the goal of linking behavioral to neural representations mentioned in the motivation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental and largely overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for *a priori* identifiability assessment.
* Demonstrates practical utility through illustrative case studies.
* Is well-written, clearly explained, and provides a valuable tool for researchers in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost may limit applicability to complex models.
* Relies on specific model assumptions that may not generalize to all perceptual tasks.
* The reference observer assumption requires careful consideration.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The authors have identified a critical problem and developed a novel and potentially impactful framework for addressing it.  While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors: 

1.  Discuss the computational cost and potential optimization strategies more explicitly.
2.  Conduct a sensitivity analysis to the key model assumptions.
3.  Address the impact of the reference observer choice on the results.
4.  Expand the discussion to include the implications for interpreting neural data.



**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
