- Proposes an a priori framework to assess and improve identifiability of Bayesian observer models in 1D estimation tasks.
- Defines a flexible observer class: parametric sensory mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians measurement/internal likelihoods, Gaussian-mixture priors on a lattice, smooth loss as inverted-Gaussians, plus lapse and motor noise.
- Derives expected log-likelihood = constant minus sum of KL divergences between response distributions; samples an expected posterior to map identifiability landscapes by trials and design.
- Interval-timing case study: strong degeneracies (sensory vs motor noise; weak identifiability of loss width); independent motor-noise calibration improves prior recovery.
- Speed-perception case study: slow-speed prior exponent and contrast-dependent noise differences are reasonably identifiable under realistic designs.
- Technical soundness: observer class and KL-based expected log-likelihood derivation are correct and computationally efficient.
- Evidence/validation: simulation-based analyses are appropriate but lack MCMC convergence diagnostics, computational cost reporting, sensitivity to prior basis size M, and robustness across multiple reference observers θ*.
- “Maximum-entropy” terminology for constructing Gaussian mixtures is imprecise/misleading and needs clarification.
- Limitations acknowledged: dependence on θ*, restriction to 1D tasks, need to adapt 2AFC, narrow loss harms prior identifiability, and independent motor measurements help.
- Clarity: generally readable and well-organized, but inconsistent notation (p vs q) and key implementation/derivation details are deferred to the supplement, risking reproducibility.
- Originality: core identity is standard; novelty lies in packaging a tractable, flexible observer class and MCMC exploration of identifiability for concrete psychophysical designs.
- Significance: addresses a practical problem for experimenters by enabling a priori comparison of designs and highlighting parameters that threaten identifiability.
- Strengths: principled identifiability objective, unified flexible observer class, efficient forward models, and insightful, actionable case-study conclusions.
- Weaknesses: limited connection to optimal experimental design/information-theory, reliance on a single θ* per scenario, underspecified “maximum-entropy” claim, incomplete 2AFC-to-estimation details, missing computational/MCMC reporting, and incomplete references.
- Suggested improvements: relate to expected information gain/Fisher/D-optimality, run sensitivity analyses over θ* and M, clarify mixture-construction method, detail 2AFC assumptions, provide computational benchmarks and convergence diagnostics, and release code; fix references.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills an important practical need with a usable framework, technically sound, and yields actionable experimental guidance.
- Arguments against acceptance: conceptual core is known and novelty is mainly packaging; some methodological/practical claims are underspecified and evaluation lacks robustness checks or real-data validation.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
