

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors note that while Bayesian observer models have been widely used to infer internal representations, the inverse mapping from behavior to model components is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of model elements can produce identical behavior. Previous work has proposed various constraints to address this degeneracy (e.g., theoretical assumptions, experimental design choices), but there has been no systematic framework for evaluating identifiability before conducting experiments. The paper proposes a method to assess identifiability a priori using KL divergence and approximate posterior densities, applied to two case studies in temporal interval estimation and speed perception.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound framework with rigorous mathematical derivations. The authors develop a flexible Bayesian observer model that can represent a wide range of perceptual tasks while maintaining computational efficiency. The identifiability analysis using KL divergence (Eq. 12) is well-justified and provides a practical measure for comparing model fits. The case studies are well-executed, with appropriate experimental designs and clear interpretation of results. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the focus on one-dimensional stimulus estimation) and provides specific suggestions for future extensions. The theoretical analysis is complemented by empirical validation, strengthening the claims about identifiability.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively frames the problem and its significance, while the methodology section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The four-stage observer model (sensation, perceptual, decision-making, response) is logically presented with clear mathematical definitions. The case studies are described with appropriate visualizations (Figures 2-3) that effectively communicate the results. The supplementary material contains necessary technical details without overwhelming the main text. The writing maintains a professional tone throughout, with precise terminology and logical flow.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first systematic framework for analyzing identifiability of Bayesian observer models before conducting experiments. While previous work has identified the identifiability problem [19], this is the first paper to propose a general method for a priori analysis. The authors clearly differentiate their contribution from related work on model distinguishability [23] by focusing specifically on Bayesian models of perception and providing practical tools for experimental design. The framework's application to two well-known perceptual tasks demonstrates its utility beyond theoretical interest, showing how it can identify non-obvious experimental design issues (e.g., the detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function in temporal estimation).

**Significance:** The framework addresses a fundamental problem that undermines the validity of many Bayesian models in perception research. By providing a method to assess identifiability before conducting experiments, the paper has the potential to significantly improve experimental design in the field. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can identify problematic design choices and suggest improvements, with implications for both theoretical understanding and empirical practice. The paper's focus on a priori analysis is particularly valuable as it can prevent wasted resources on experiments that cannot reliably recover the intended model components.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a critical, well-documented problem in Bayesian models of perception
- Provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient framework
- Demonstrates practical utility through two well-chosen case studies
- Clearly articulates how the framework can improve experimental design
- Well-organized and clearly written with appropriate mathematical rigor
- Includes comprehensive supplementary material for reproducibility

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited scope to one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- Computational complexity of the framework for very large parameter spaces is not thoroughly evaluated
- The case studies, while well-executed, could be strengthened with additional examples from different perceptual domains
- The paper does not fully explore how the framework interacts with other sources of model misspecification beyond identifiability

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper solves a well-known, significant problem in the field with a technically sound method
- The framework has clear practical applications for improving experimental design in perception research
- The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility
- The paper is well-written and would be valuable to both theoretical and experimental researchers
- The contribution is novel and addresses a gap that has been recognized but not systematically solved

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The scope of the framework is limited to a specific class of perceptual tasks
- The paper could benefit from more extensive computational analysis of the framework's scalability
- The case studies, while good, don't fully demonstrate the framework's potential across diverse perceptual domains

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing the first systematic framework for analyzing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The technical quality is high, the methodology is sound, and the practical implications are substantial. While the scope is somewhat limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, the framework's design allows for extension to more complex scenarios as outlined in the conclusions. The paper addresses a critical issue that has implications for the validity of many existing studies in the field, making it highly relevant to the NIPS community. The case studies provide convincing evidence of the framework's utility, and the paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. This work would be valuable to both researchers developing Bayesian models and those designing psychophysical experiments.
