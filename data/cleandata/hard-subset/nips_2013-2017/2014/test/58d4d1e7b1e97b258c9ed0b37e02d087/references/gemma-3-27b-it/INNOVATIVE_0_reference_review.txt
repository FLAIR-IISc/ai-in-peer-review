This paper tackles a crucial, often-overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: identifiability. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inverting behavioral data to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) and propose a framework for *a priori* analysis of this problem. This is a significant contribution, moving beyond simply fitting models and attempting to understand the limits of inference. The approach, leveraging a parameterized class of observer models and KL divergence to assess practical non-identifiability, is clever and potentially broadly applicable. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete illustrations, though the level of detail regarding implementation warrants further scrutiny (see below).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The systematic investigation of identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models is a genuinely novel contribution. Most work focuses on *performing* inference, not questioning its validity.
*   **Significance:** Addressing identifiability is vital for interpreting results from Bayesian models of perception. If the underlying representations cannot be reliably recovered, the conclusions drawn from these models are suspect. This framework provides a tool for researchers to assess this risk *before* investing significant effort in data collection and analysis.
*   **Generality:** The proposed observer model, while complex, appears flexible enough to encompass a wide range of psychophysical tasks. The use of parametric forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions allows for efficient exploration of the model space.
*   **Practicality:** The use of KL divergence as a proxy for model comparison is a reasonable and computationally tractable approach. The demonstration of how this framework can inform experimental design is particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim efficiency, the sheer number of parameters in their observer model raises concerns about the feasibility of exploring the identifiability landscape comprehensively, particularly for more complex tasks. The details of the MCMC sampling and how convergence was assessed are not sufficiently clear.
*   **Assumptions:** The choice of parametric forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, while providing flexibility, introduces assumptions that may not hold for all perceptual systems. Sensitivity analysis to these assumptions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Validation:** The two case studies, while illustrative, are somewhat limited. The speed perception example benefits from prior work establishing the slow-speed prior, making the analysis less a true test of the framework and more a validation of existing findings.
*   **Clarity (Minor):** The notation is dense and requires careful parsing. A more visual representation of the model (beyond Figure 1) might improve accessibility. The connection between the theoretical framework and the specific experimental setups (e.g., the motivation for choosing particular parameter values) could be clearer.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and important contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   The framework has the potential to significantly improve the rigor and interpretability of research in this area.
*   The authors demonstrate the feasibility and utility of their approach with two concrete examples.
*   Even with minor flaws, the value of identifying and addressing the issue of identifiability outweighs the weaknesses.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the framework raises concerns about its scalability and practical applicability.
*   The strong assumptions embedded in the observer model could limit its generality.
*   The limited validation with only two case studies leaves open the possibility that the framework may not be robust to variations in task complexity or perceptual systems.
*   The paper feels slightly incomplete. Additional analysis, such as sensitivity to model assumptions, would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong submission with a bold, novel idea. The concerns regarding computational complexity and limited validation are valid, but I believe the significance of the problem addressed and the potential impact of the proposed framework outweigh these weaknesses. The work represents a substantial step forward in our understanding of the limitations of Bayesian modeling of perception. I lean towards acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer's concerns regarding computational details and validation in a future revision. I would be very comfortable seeing this work presented at NIPS.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
