Summary and context
The paper addresses a core challenge in Bayesian models of perception: non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) when inferring them from behavior. The authors (i) propose a flexible, analytically tractable class of 1D Bayesian observer models that unifies sensation (stimulus-to-internal mapping), measurement noise (mixtures of Gaussians), priors (mixture-of-Gaussians on a lattice in internal space), loss (inverted Gaussian mixture), lapses, and response noise; and (ii) introduce an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log marginal likelihood of a candidate model θ under data generated from a reference model θ*, which reduces to a sum of KL divergences over stimuli weighted by trial counts. They sample from an approximate “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) via MCMC to explore identifiability landscapes and compare experimental designs. Two case studies illustrate the approach: interval timing/reproduction and visual speed perception with a slow-speed prior. They show that in interval timing, priors are hard to recover unless motor noise is independently constrained and that narrowing the loss (MAP-like feedback) can worsen prior recovery, while in speed perception, the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters are more robustly identifiable.

Relation to prior work
The model-degeneracy argument builds on established critiques of BDT identifiability (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis). The expected log-likelihood/KL formalism for model distinguishability has strong precedence in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and information-theoretic design. The contribution here is to tailor this framework to Bayesian observer models with a parametric family that affords fast evaluation via Gaussian-mixture algebra, and to use it for comparing psychophysical designs. The case studies connect to seminal work on interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.) and speed priors (Stocker & Simoncelli).

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem with real impact on interpreting BDT fits.
- A flexible yet computationally convenient observer family (Gaussian-mixture closure yields analytic expected loss).
- Clear, principled identifiability metric grounded in expected log evidence (reducing to a KL sum).
- Practically useful insights: e.g., motor-sensory-lapse confounds; design implications for learning priors vs enforcing MAP-like losses; relative robustness in speed perception.
- Framed as a priori analysis to guide design, not only post hoc model recovery.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope/novelty: The identifiability metric (expected log-likelihood/KL) and the idea of sampling over expected evidence for model comparison are not new; the paper’s novelty hinges on the specific observer parametrization and application. Positioning vs prior model recovery/design-of-experiments literature (e.g., Bayesian optimal design, mutual information, ADO) is limited.
- Assumptions about distributions: The “maximum-entropy” derivation of two-component Gaussian mixtures with fixed moments and unimodality is unconventional—maximum entropy under moment constraints typically yields exponential-family forms, not Gaussian mixtures. The details, uniqueness, and numerical stability are relegated to the supplement; stronger justification is needed.
- Overparameterization: The chosen class (mixture likelihoods, mixture priors, mixture losses, lapse drawn from prior, motor noise) is very expressive; identifiability (or lack thereof) may reflect this choice more than a general property of tasks. Sensitivity to model class mis-specification (true observer outside the family) is not analyzed.
- Efficiency and reproducibility: The approach depends on repeated evaluation of presp and KL integrals. Timings, MCMC diagnostics, discretization choices (Nexp, quadrature grids), and robustness to M (number of prior components) are not reported.
- 2AFC vs estimation: The second case study assumes an “equivalent” estimation model for a classic 2AFC paradigm; identifiability may differ materially in discrete-choice tasks. Given the paper’s claim of broader applicability, at least one discrete-choice implementation would strengthen the case.
- Lapse and response models: Lapses drawn from the prior and simple additive motor noise in task space are convenient but potentially unrealistic; different lapse models (uniform, off-task responses) can materially change identifiability.

Quality
Technically sound in deriving the expected evidence as a KL-weighted sum and in constructing an observer class that permits analytic expected loss. However, some key components (mixture-from-moments “maxent,” posterior computation stability, KL estimation accuracy) need clearer exposition and validation. Case-study conclusions are plausible and align with intuitions (e.g., sensory/motor confounds), but more systematic sensitivity analyses would bolster the claims.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful decomposition into stages. Some parts rely heavily on the supplement (mixture parameterization from moments; details of KL computation and MCMC). It would help to:
- Precisely describe the algorithm that maps target moments to mixture parameters and discuss uniqueness.
- Provide computational costs, convergence diagnostics, and numeric integration settings.
- Clarify parameter constraints (e.g., priors over weights wm, nonnegativity, normalization) used in MCMC.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of using expected log-likelihood/KL for identifiability and model distinguishability is well-established. The originality lies in the specific parametric observer family enabling analytic expected loss and its application to psychophysical design comparisons.

Significance
High for the psychophysics/BDT community: a practical tool to diagnose parameter degeneracies and compare designs before data collection. For NeurIPS, impact is credible but would be strengthened by tighter links to existing optimal design methods and by demonstrating applicability beyond 1D estimation (e.g., discrete choice).

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a direct comparison to alternative identifiability/design criteria: Fisher information, mutual information, Bayes risk, and ADO methods (e.g., Cavagnaro et al.).
- Validate the “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) by simulating datasets and comparing the actual posterior to E across a grid (model/parameter recovery).
- Analyze sensitivity to mis-specification: e.g., true loss not in the inverted-Gaussian family; non-log transforms; non-unimodal noise.
- Explore different lapse models and show their effect on identifiability.
- Include at least one discrete-choice (2AFC) implementation to substantiate generality.
- Report computational performance and release code.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with concrete design guidance.
- Solid, principled framework; insightful case-study findings.
- Likely to be used by practitioners modeling perception.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing identifiability/model design literature; missing citations and comparisons.
- Some technical assumptions (mixture-from-moments “maxent”) are under-justified.
- Empirical validation and efficiency details are sparse; no discrete-choice demonstration.

Overall
I lean toward a weak accept if the authors strengthen connections to optimal design/identifiability literature, clarify/justify key modeling assumptions, and add validation/sensitivity analyses. The framework is useful and the case-study insights are valuable to the community.
