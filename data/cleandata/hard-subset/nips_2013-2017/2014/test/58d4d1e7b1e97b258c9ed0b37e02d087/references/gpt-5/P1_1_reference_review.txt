Summary
This paper addresses a central and long-standing challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the practical identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from observed behavior. The authors formalize “a priori identifiability” by deriving the expected log-evidence of a candidate observer θ under data generated by a reference observer θ*, showing it reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli (Eq. 12). They then define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩) and explore its landscape via MCMC to diagnose which parameters (or combinations) can be recovered and how design choices affect identifiability.

To make this feasible, they propose a flexible, computationally tractable family of observer models for 1D estimation tasks: (i) a parametric stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) that yields approximately translationally invariant internal noise; (ii) measurement noise as a constrained mixture of Gaussians matching first four moments; (iii) priors as mixtures of lattice Gaussians; (iv) a loss modeled as a negative mixture of Gaussians parameterized by moments; (v) lapse and reporting (motor) noise. This class supports analytic expressions for expected loss and efficient evaluation of presp(r|s; θ).

They illustrate the framework on two case studies. In interval timing, they find strong confounds between sensory and motor noise that bias prior recovery under a baseline design; driving the loss toward MAP worsens identifiability; fixing motor noise in advance substantially improves prior recovery. In speed perception, their analysis supports robust recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters, even when decoupling internal from generative likelihoods.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to ideal observer modeling (e.g., Geisler; Knill & Richards), Bayesian transfer (Maloney & Mamassian), and recent attempts to infer internal representations (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Houlsby et al.; Acerbi et al.). It is also closely related to model identifiability/distinguishability and informativeness of data (Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and to Bayesian experimental design (e.g., MacKay, Paninski, Chaloner & Verdinelli), although the latter is not explicitly discussed.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The expected log-likelihood derivation (Eq. 12) is sound and a good choice for measuring practical identifiability; the asymmetry appropriately conditions on θ*. The proposed generative model is internally consistent and yields tractable computations.
- Empirical support: The two case studies are illustrative and give actionable design insights. However, the paper relies solely on synthetic a priori analysis; it would benefit from validation that the predicted ranking of designs correlates with actual parameter recovery in finite data fits (e.g., simulate datasets and perform full inference to confirm E(θ|θ*) predictions).
- Modeling choices: Some choices warrant more justification and sensitivity analyses: (a) the “local maximum-entropy” selection within a Gaussian-mixture family for likelihood and loss is unconventional—the true maximum entropy distribution given moments is not a mixture of Gaussians. Please clarify the optimization criterion and show robustness to varying mixture parameterizations (e.g., single vs two components). (b) The prior basis (Eq. 4) fixes component means/variances; discuss how M, lattice spacing, and component variance affect identifiability and whether overcomplete bases or alternative priors (e.g., log-normal mixtures) change conclusions. (c) Lapses sampled from the prior may interact with prior recovery; compare with uniform lapse or contaminant models. (d) Converting 2AFC to estimation is reasonable but not always equivalent; a direct discrete-choice variant would strengthen claims.
- Computation: The MCMC over a high-dimensional θ (including mixture weights) can be challenging; more details on constraints, parameterizations (e.g., softmax for weights), runtime, and convergence diagnostics would increase confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. Derivations are clear at a high level, and the case studies are easy to follow. That said:
- Provide more detail (or a concise main-text summary) of the “local maximum-entropy” procedure and the analytical form of expected loss to support claims of efficiency.
- Specify the numerical methods to compute KL over continuous responses and any discretization settings.
- Some references are incomplete. Adding key related work on Bayesian optimal experimental design and identifiability would contextualize the contribution.

Originality
The key novelty is applying an analytically tractable, KL-based expected evidence to map identifiability over a rich observer-model class, enabling a priori ranking of experimental designs. While the components (KL-based expected log-evidence; Bayesian observer models; mixtures) are known, their integration and practical workflow for psychophysics is novel and useful.

Significance
This is a timely contribution for computational neuroscience/psychophysics: it addresses a major criticism of Bayesian modeling—degenerate parameter recovery—and provides a practical tool to stress-test designs and assumptions. If adopted, it could improve rigor in interpreting inferred priors/likelihoods and guide experimental design. The significance would be amplified by code release and a path to optimize experimental designs (e.g., selecting stimuli to maximize expected KL).

Pros
- Clear, principled identifiability metric with an efficient surrogate posterior.
- Flexible observer model enabling analytic computations.
- Actionable insights in two canonical domains (interval timing, speed perception).
- Addresses a widely recognized problem and connects to experimental design.

Cons
- Validation limited to synthetic analyses; no demonstration that E(θ|θ*) rankings predict finite-data recovery.
- Several modeling choices (max-entropy in mixture family, lapse model, prior basis) need further justification/sensitivity analysis.
- Lack of direct discrete-choice instantiation; 2AFC “conversion” may not always hold.
- Computational and MCMC details are sparse; no runtime, convergence, or ablation on model complexity (e.g., M).

Suggestions for improvement
- Empirically validate the framework: simulate datasets under θ* across designs, fit θ by standard Bayesian inference, and verify that identifiability predictions match actual recovery accuracy and uncertainty.
- Add sensitivity analyses: number of prior components M; single vs two-component likelihood/loss; alternative lapse models; robustness to model misspecification (e.g., skewed likelihood not in the family).
- Discuss/compare with Bayesian optimal experimental design; show how your KL-based metric could drive stimulus selection or condition choice.
- Provide a discrete-choice variant (2AFC) and demonstrate on the original speed task without conversion.
- Expand computational details (parameter constraints, priors, samplers, diagnostics) and, if possible, release code.
- Improve and complete citations (e.g., MacKay 1992; Paninski 2005; Chaloner & Verdinelli 1995; Lewi et al. 2009; van den Berg & Ma on model recovery).

Overall recommendation
This is a solid, well-motivated, and potentially influential paper. With added validation and clarifications, it would make a strong NeurIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the key concerns about validation, modeling choices, and clarity.
