Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a well-known problem in Bayesian observer modeling: non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavioral data. The authors propose an a priori framework to assess practical identifiability for a large class of one-dimensional estimation tasks. They define a flexible parametric observer family (sensory mapping to an internal space; measurement noise as a two-Gaussian mixture with fixed moments; a Gaussian-mixture lattice prior; an inverted-Gaussian-mixture loss; lapse and motor noise), and derive that the expected log marginal likelihood for a candidate parameter vector θ, under data generated by θ*, is proportional to minus a weighted sum of KL divergences between predictive response densities (Eq. 12). They then sample a pseudo-posterior E(θ|θ*) to explore regions of parameter degeneracy. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (where motor noise and sensory noise confound prior recovery unless motor noise is independently constrained; narrowing the loss function degrades prior recovery) and speed perception (where a slow-speed prior exponent is recovered robustly).

The work is positioned against prior concerns about “Bayesian just-so stories,” and cites classic identifiability and cognitive modeling literature (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004). However, the core utility—using expected KL to assess identifiability—closely resembles long-standing Bayesian optimal experimental design (OED) criteria based on expected information gain (e.g., Lindley 1956; Chaloner & Verdinelli 1995; Lewi et al. 2009; Cavagnaro et al. 2010; Atkinson & Donev), which is not discussed.

Strengths
- Timely problem with high practical importance for Bayesian modeling in perception.
- Clean derivation linking expected log evidence to sums of KL divergences across stimuli, providing an interpretable identifiability proxy.
- A computational observer class engineered for analytic or efficient numerical computation; ingredients are sensible and reusable.
- Case studies yield actionable design guidance (e.g., independently measuring motor noise materially improves prior recovery).
- Emphasis on a priori assessment and design comparison is valuable for pre-registration and planning.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Validation is limited and largely circular. All demonstrations use the same parametric family for data generation and for analysis. There is no end-to-end parameter recovery by actually fitting simulated datasets (with likelihood-based or Bayesian inference) to test whether the E-based identifiability predictions match recovery performance, nor any test of robustness to model misspecification (e.g., different loss family, different sensory map). Without such validation, it is hard to judge the practical reliability of the recommendations.

2) Relation to prior OED/identifiability work is thin. The central utility function (expected log likelihood ≈ expected KL) underpins decades of OED. The paper does not connect to that literature nor compare to standard identifiability diagnostics: Fisher Information / local sensitivity, profile likelihoods, posterior curvature, or parameter-recovery simulations. Given the emphasis on “framework,” this lack of baselines undermines claims of novelty and soundness.

3) Heavy reliance on restrictive parametrizations. Key results may be artifacts of choices such as two-Gaussian mixtures for measurement noise and loss, lattice Gaussian priors with fixed variances, and a specific f(s) mapping. There is no sensitivity analysis to mixture order, prior basis (e.g., splines/GPs), or alternative loss families. The surprising “MAP hurts recovery” result, for instance, may not generalize beyond the chosen loss parametrization.

4) Task mapping and external validity. The speed-perception example is adapted from a 2AFC paradigm by positing an “equivalent” estimation task; identifiability properties of 2AFC can differ materially from estimation. Claims of robustness in that domain are therefore tentative.

5) No quantitative decision rule or practical thresholds. The framework yields a landscape E(θ|θ*), but the paper does not specify identifiability criteria, sample complexity curves, or confidence thresholds that practitioners can use. Nor are computational costs reported (in such a high-dimensional θ, MCMC over E could be expensive).

6) Clarity and reproducibility. Many core implementation choices are deferred to the supplement (e.g., “maximum-entropy unimodal” mixture construction, enforcement of unimodality, numerical details for presp integrals, MCMC setup). Code availability is not mentioned.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Validate with parameter-recovery experiments: simulate many datasets across Ntr, perform actual inference (MLE or Bayesian posterior), and compare recovery accuracy to E-based identifiability predictions. Include misspecification tests.
- Compare to Fisher Information and profile-likelihood analyses at θ*; show when local methods agree/disagree with your global E landscape.
- Situate the work within Bayesian OED: expected information gain, mutual information, adaptive stimulus selection. Consider leveraging OED to optimize designs, not only compare hand-crafted ones.
- Test robustness to key modeling choices: number of mixture components, prior basis (splines/GP), alternative loss classes, and different f(s) forms.
- Analyze the 2AFC setting directly rather than relying on an estimation surrogate.
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and release code to enable reproduction.
- Clarify the “maximum-entropy unimodal” construction and uniqueness; explain how unimodality is guaranteed.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-discussed practical issue in Bayesian perceptual modeling.
- Offers a potentially useful computational toolkit and some nontrivial design insights.
- Could stimulate better practices in pre-experimental identifiability assessment.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established expected-KL/OED frameworks; missing connections to that literature.
- Insufficient empirical validation and no baselines against standard identifiability diagnostics.
- Strong dependence on specific parametric assumptions, with little sensitivity analysis.
- External validity of the case studies (especially 2AFC) is unclear.

Recommendation
I lean toward rejection in the current form. With stronger validation (parameter recovery and misspecification), clearer connections to OED/identifiability literature, a direct 2AFC treatment, and robustness analyses, this could become a solid contribution.
