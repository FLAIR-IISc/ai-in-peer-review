- Addresses practical identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian perception by deriving an expected log-evidence (Eq. 12) and defining an approximate expected posterior E(θ|θ*).
- Uses MCMC on the approximate posterior to diagnose which parameters or parameter combinations are recoverable and how design choices affect identifiability.
- Proposes a tractable observer-model family for 1D estimation tasks: parametric stimulus-to-internal mapping, constrained Gaussian-mixture measurement noise, lattice-Gaussian priors, negative Gaussian-mixture losses, and lapse/reporting noise.
- Provides analytic expressions for expected loss and efficient evaluation of p(resp|s; θ) within this model family.
- Interval-timing case study: finds strong confounds between sensory and motor noise biasing prior recovery; driving loss toward MAP worsens identifiability; fixing motor noise improves recovery.
- Speed-perception case study: supports robust recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters, even with decoupled internal and generative likelihoods.
- Connects to prior work in ideal observer modeling, Bayesian transfer, inferential approaches to internal representations, model identifiability, and experimental design.
- Technical correctness: derivation of expected log-likelihood is sound and the generative model is internally consistent and tractable.
- Empirical support limited: analyses are synthetic a priori only and need validation that predicted design rankings match finite-data recovery from full inference.
- Modeling choices need more justification and sensitivity checks: the local maximum-entropy Gaussian-mixture approach, fixed prior basis parameters, lapse sampled from the prior, and 2AFC-to-estimation conversion.
- Computational concerns: high-dimensional MCMC (including mixture weights) requires more details on parameterizations, constraints, runtimes, and convergence diagnostics.
- Clarity is generally good, but the paper needs more detail on the local max-entropy procedure, analytic expected-loss forms, KL computation methods, and incomplete references.
- Originality: novel integration of KL-based expected evidence with a tractable observer-model class to map identifiability and rank experimental designs.
- Significance: timely and useful for psychophysics and computational neuroscience; utility would increase with code release and demonstration of design optimization.
- Pros: principled identifiability metric, flexible analytic observer model, actionable insights in two domains, addresses a key problem in Bayesian modeling.
- Cons: lacks finite-data validation, several modeling assumptions need justification, absence of a discrete-choice instantiation, and sparse computational/MCMC details.
- Suggested improvements: validate predictions with simulated data and full inference, run sensitivity analyses (M, mixture components, lapse models, misspecification), compare to Bayesian optimal design, add a discrete-choice variant, expand computational details and release code, and fix citations.
- Overall recommendation: solid and potentially influential paper; lean toward acceptance provided authors address validation, modeling-choice justification, and clarity concerns.
