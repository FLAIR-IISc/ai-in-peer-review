

# Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral data, making it impossible to reliably recover internal representations from behavioral data without careful experimental design. They propose a novel framework for a priori identifiability analysis that allows researchers to systematically evaluate how well parameters of interest can be recovered before conducting experiments. The framework is implemented through a general class of Bayesian observer models that encompasses many psychophysical tasks, and they demonstrate its utility through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception.

This work builds on the extensive literature on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., [1-6]) and addresses a fundamental limitation that has been noted but not systematically resolved in the field ([17-19]). While previous work has attempted to address model degeneracy through various constraints ([12, 19]), this paper provides the first systematic framework for evaluating identifiability before conducting experiments.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound solution to a well-defined problem. The mathematical derivation of the expected log-likelihood as a measure of identifiability (Equation 12) is rigorous and well-justified. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of implementing this framework through efficient approximations and demonstrate its practical utility through two detailed case studies. The paper thoroughly evaluates both strengths and weaknesses of the approach, acknowledging limitations such as the specific parametric assumptions made in the observer model.

**Originality:** This is a significant contribution to the field. While the problem of model non-identifiability has been recognized in Bayesian perception models ([19, 17]), the authors present the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis. The approach is novel in its use of the expected log-likelihood as a proxy for identifiability, which avoids the computational intractability of traditional approaches. The specific application to psychophysical tasks and the demonstration of how experimental design choices affect identifiability represent a substantial advance over previous work.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a critical methodological issue that affects the validity of many studies in Bayesian perception research. By providing a tool to evaluate experimental designs before data collection, the framework has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of conclusions about internal representations. The case studies demonstrate that the framework can reveal non-intuitive findings (e.g., that forcing a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery), which could guide better experimental design in the field. This work has clear potential to impact how researchers approach Bayesian modeling in perception.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the work is compelling, and the technical content is presented in a logical flow. The case studies are well-explained with helpful figures that effectively communicate the key findings. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Computational complexity:** While the authors claim the framework is "efficient," the computational requirements for exploring the full parameter space may be substantial for more complex models. The paper could provide more guidance on the practical scalability of the approach for models with many parameters.

**Assumptions about the observer model:** The framework relies on the specific parametric form of the observer model presented in Section 2. While this provides computational tractability, it may limit the applicability to certain types of perceptual tasks. The authors acknowledge this in Section 5 but could more clearly delineate the scope of the framework.

**Limited empirical validation:** The paper demonstrates the framework's utility through theoretical analysis of two case studies, but the approach itself has not been validated against real experimental data. While this is understandable for a methodological paper, the authors could more explicitly discuss how the framework would perform with actual behavioral data.

## Evaluation

**Quality:** 9/10. The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical foundations and thorough theoretical analysis. The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility. The only minor limitation is the computational complexity for very large parameter spaces.

**Clarity:** 8.5/10. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with excellent figures that effectively communicate key findings. Some aspects of the computational implementation could be clarified for readers.

**Originality:** 9/10. The framework for a priori identifiability analysis represents a significant methodological advance that is clearly distinct from previous work. The specific application to Bayesian models of perception is novel.

**Significance:** 9/10. The paper addresses a fundamental methodological issue that affects a large body of work in Bayesian perception modeling. The potential impact on experimental design and interpretation of results is substantial, making this highly significant for the field.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant methodological contribution to the field of Bayesian models of perception. The problem addressed is important, the solution is novel and well-executed, and the potential impact on the field is substantial. The paper is well-written and provides a useful tool for researchers to improve the reliability of their conclusions about internal representations.

The minor weaknesses in computational complexity and limited empirical validation do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. The framework presented here has the potential to significantly improve experimental design in Bayesian perception research, making it a valuable addition to the field.

I recommend **acceptance** at NeurIPS. This paper would be of interest to a broad audience in computational neuroscience and cognitive science, and it makes a clear methodological contribution that advances the field's ability to draw reliable conclusions from behavioral data.
