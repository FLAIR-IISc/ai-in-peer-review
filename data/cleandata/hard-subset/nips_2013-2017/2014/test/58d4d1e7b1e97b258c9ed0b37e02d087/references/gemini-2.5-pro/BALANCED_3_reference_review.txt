This paper addresses the critical and pervasive issue of model non-identifiability in the context of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that many combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavior, which poses a fundamental challenge to the program of "inverting" these models to recover the brain's representations. The central contribution is a novel, principled framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of a given Bayesian model within a specific experimental design. This framework consists of two parts: 1) a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of capturing a wide range of perceptual and decision-making phenomena, including stimulus-dependent noise, complex non-Gaussian priors, and asymmetric loss functions; and 2) an efficient method for quantifying identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a model over datasets generated by a known "ground truth" reference model. This quantity is elegantly shown to be proportional to the KL divergence between the response distributions of the two models. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies from the literature: temporal interval estimation and visual speed perception. The analysis yields non-trivial insights, suggesting that recovering priors in the timing task is difficult and highly dependent on experimental design choices (e.g., independently measuring motor noise), while recovering the well-known "slow-speed prior" is theoretically more robust.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a major thorn in the side of computational cognitive science and neuroscience. Many papers fit complex Bayesian models to behavioral data and draw strong conclusions about internal representations, often without formally addressing whether those representations could be traded off against other model components. This paper provides a concrete, usable tool to rigorously probe these issues *before* an experiment is run, potentially saving resources and leading to more robust and credible scientific claims. The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community, where Bayesian models of cognition are a staple.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is novel and technically sound. While the general concept of assessing model distinguishability is not new (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), its application here is tailored specifically to the structure of Bayesian observer models. The formulation of the identifiability problem in terms of the expected log-likelihood (and its connection to KL divergence) is elegant and provides a principled, information-theoretic metric. The construction of the observer model is also a significant contribution; it is remarkably flexible (approximating Weber-Fechner and Stevens' laws, allowing non-Gaussian likelihoods/priors and asymmetric losses) while retaining computational tractability, which is essential for the proposed MCMC-based exploration of the identifiability landscape.

3.  **Clarity and Practical Utility:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and well-structured. The framework is presented as a practical tool that can be used to compare and rank different potential experimental designs. The case studies are excellent demonstrations of this utility. For instance, the analysis of the temporal estimation task provides the non-obvious and valuable insight that a design encouraging a MAP-like (narrow) loss function might actually *worsen* the ability to recover the prior, due to a degeneracy with the loss function's own width parameter. This is exactly the kind of counter-intuitive result that makes such *a priori* analyses indispensable.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Sensitivity to the Reference Observer:** The analysis is conditioned on a "ground truth" reference observer, θ*. The authors suggest that θ* can be chosen based on pilot data, literature, or educated guesses. However, the conclusions about the relative merits of different experimental designs could potentially be sensitive to this choice. A stronger case would be made if the authors could demonstrate that the *ranking* of experimental designs is robust to reasonable variations in the parameters of θ*. For example, in the timing task, would the MTR design still be superior if the true observer had a skewed sensory likelihood? A brief discussion or supplementary analysis of this robustness would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Model Class Complexity:** The parametric observer model is impressively flexible, but also very complex, with over 20 free parameters. It is possible that some of the observed non-identifiability issues (e.g., the sensory vs. motor noise trade-off in Figure 2c) are exacerbated by this specific, high-dimensional parameterization. It would be helpful for the authors to discuss how the choice of the model class itself might interact with the identifiability analysis. Are these degeneracies fundamental to the task, or are they partially an artifact of the chosen model space?

3.  **Justification of Task Conversion:** In the second case study, the authors analyze the speed perception work of Stocker & Simoncelli (2009), which used a 2-alternative forced choice (2AFC) paradigm. The paper states that this is converted into an "equivalent estimation task" without providing details. This is a critical step, as the information available from discrete choice data is fundamentally different from that of continuous estimation data, and their identifiability properties can differ significantly. The authors rightly note that extending the framework to discrete-choice paradigms is an important future direction. However, as it stands, the claims about the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference are predicated on this un-justified conversion. This limitation should be more clearly acknowledged, and ideally, some justification for the "equivalence" should be provided in the main text or supplement.

4.  **Minor Points:**
    *   The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the computational cost of the MCMC analysis, as this is a key factor in its practical usability for experimentalists.
    *   In the text discussing Figure 2c, motor noise is referred to as `w1`, but in the model definition (Eq. 10) it is `ρ1`. This should be made consistent.
    *   The caption for Figure 2c could be more explicit about the key takeaway, which is the strong negative correlation between sensory noise `σ` and motor noise `ρ1`, as this visualizes the core degeneracy.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, important problem of non-identifiability in a popular class of models.
*   Provides a novel, technically sound, and computationally tractable framework for *a priori* analysis.
*   The framework is a practical tool for improving experimental design.
*   The case studies provide compelling and non-obvious scientific insights.

**Con:**
*   The analysis depends on a single reference observer, and robustness to this choice is not explored.
*   The high complexity of the assumed model class might itself contribute to identifiability issues.
*   The conversion from a 2AFC to an estimation task in the second case study is not sufficiently justified.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and highly valuable contribution to the field. It provides both a conceptual advance and a practical tool for improving the rigor of research using Bayesian observer models. The weaknesses are addressable and do not detract from the core importance of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
