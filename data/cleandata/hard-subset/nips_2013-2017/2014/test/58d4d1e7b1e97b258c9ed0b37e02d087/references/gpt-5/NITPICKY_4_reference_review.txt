Summary
The paper proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks. The authors (i) define a flexible parametric class of observers with: a monotone sensory transform f(s), a measurement distribution expressed as a two-Gaussian mixture parameterized by its first four moments, a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians, and a loss function modeled as an inverted Gaussian mixture (also moment-parameterized); (ii) derive an expression for the expected log-likelihood of a model θ under data generated by θ*, showing it equals a constant minus Ntr times a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions; (iii) define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(〈log L〉) and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability; and (iv) apply the method to two case studies (interval timing and visual speed), to rank experimental designs and examine parameter degeneracies. They find that prior inference in interval timing is fragile unless additional constraints (e.g., motor noise known) are enforced, and that inferring slow-speed priors appears more robust.

Relation to prior work
This paper lies at the intersection of Bayesian decision models in psychophysics (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, Jazayeri & Shadlen, Körding & Wolpert), concerns about non-identifiability (Navarro, Pitt & Myung), and recent attempts to reconstruct internal representations (Houlsby et al., Acerbi et al.). The KL-based expected log-likelihood identity is standard; the contribution is to instantiate it for a specific observer class and use it to evaluate design choices.

Strengths
- Addresses a genuine and important problem (practical non-identifiability) that affects inference of priors/likelihoods/loss in perceptual tasks.
- Unifies several common modeling choices into a single, flexible observer class with shared machinery (mixtures, moment parameterizations) intended to facilitate fast evaluation.
- Uses an analytically tractable structure (mixture-of-Gaussians throughout) to argue computational efficiency for expected loss and response prediction.
- Case studies are relevant and produce interpretable insights (e.g., the detrimental effect of narrow loss on prior inference; known motor noise improving identifiability).
- Provides a design-comparison perspective that is valuable for planning experiments.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness
- The “maximum-entropy” construction of the measurement and loss models is underspecified and likely incorrect as stated. The maximum-entropy distribution with fixed moments is not a Gaussian mixture; it’s an exponential family with polynomial sufficient statistics. You instead fix moments and then “pick a distribution” (a mixture) that “locally maximizes entropy” subject to unimodality. Without a precise optimization statement, constraints, and a proof of uniqueness/existence, this is not a well-defined mapping from (σ,γ,κ) to mixture parameters. The results hinge on this mapping.
- The mixture parameter recovery for the measurement model (π, μ1, μ2, σ1, σ2) from (σ, γ, κ) is not described. How are identifiability and unimodality enforced? What if specified moments are infeasible?
- Loss modeling: L(t̂−t) = −∑ πℓ N(·). While additive constants do not affect decisions, negative-valued losses and mixture parameterization need precise justification. The “unique minimum at zero” and unimodality assumptions require explicit conditions on the mixture parameters; otherwise multiple local minima arise.
- The sensory transform f(s): A and B are chosen to map the empirical stimulus set to [−1,1], making f depend on the experimental stimuli. This undermines the interpretation of f as an observer property and may confound across-design comparisons. “Without loss of generality” is not justified.
- The lapse model draws t* from the prior. This couples lapses and prior shape, potentially worsening identifiability of the very quantity of interest. Justification and sensitivity analysis are needed, especially since this choice differs from standard uniform lapses.
- The expected log-likelihood identity (Eq. 12) is standard, but numerical evaluation of the KL between continuous response densities presp(r|si;θ*) and presp(r|si;θ) is nontrivial. The paper gives no details on quadrature/discretization, stability, or error control.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Many essential derivations and algorithms are relegated to Supplementary Material, which is indispensable (mixture parameterization, entropy-based selection, analytical expressions for expected loss/posterior mixtures, and MCMC details). As is, the paper is not reproducible.
- No information on MCMC configuration, proposals, convergence diagnostics, runtime, or numerical tolerances. Given the high-dimensional θ (including M=15 weights), this is critical.
- Notation inconsistencies: motor parameter appears as w1 once (should be ρ1); qprior used for both t and s; Eq. 2 inverse uses “d√” notation (should be exponent 1/d); some variables reused (s0 both in f and elsewhere); footnote “1” is misplaced in Sec. 4.1/4.2; several hyphenation/line-break errors; multiple typos.
- References are incomplete (years/venues/pages missing), which is below NeurIPS standards.

Originality
- The central metric (expected log-likelihood as sum of KLs) and the idea of exploring identifiability across designs are known. The main novelty is the particular observer class and the application. The contribution feels incremental without stronger theoretical guarantees or a more systematic design optimization.

Significance
- The problem is important; however, given the reliance on unspecified procedures (moment-to-mixture mapping; MaxEnt heuristics) and the lack of rigorous validation, it is difficult to trust the conclusions or to reuse the framework. Significance would increase with code release, ablations (e.g., basis size M, choice of lapse prior, loss families), and a more principled experiment-design optimization.

Requests for clarification
1) Provide the exact optimization problem and algorithm that maps (σ,γ,κ) to the two-Gaussian mixture parameters under unimodality and entropy criterion. What are feasibility conditions?
2) Show the closed-form expressions used for posterior mixtures and expected loss; report numerical complexity.
3) Justify choosing A,B to map to [−1,1]. How does this impact cross-experiment comparability?
4) Report MCMC settings, diagnostics, run times, and sensitivity to initializations.
5) Quantify how the lapse-as-prior choice affects prior identifiability vs. a uniform lapse.
6) Provide sensitivity to M (basis size), prior lattice spacing, and boundary truncation.
7) In the speed case, justify the 2AFC→estimation equivalence used and test robustness to reporting noise levels.

Minor comments and typos
- Eq. 2 inverse: f−1(t) = s0[(exp((t−B)/A)−1)]^{1/d}. Current notation is ambiguous.
- Sec. 2.4: “parameteric” → “parametric”.
- Sec. 4.1: “motor noise, w1” → “ρ1”.
- Footnote marker “1” appears mid-text; the footnote content bleeds into Sec. 4.2.
- In Eq. 7, qprior(s*) should be pprior(s*) or clarify notation since qprior was defined over t.
- Many references lack year/venue/page info; please fix to NeurIPS format.

Pros
- Important problem; sensible design-comparison perspective.
- Flexible, practically useful modeling ingredients (if fully specified).
- Case studies produce actionable insights (e.g., measure motor noise; beware narrow loss).

Cons
- Key technical components underspecified; MaxEnt claims questionable.
- Reproducibility and numerical details missing.
- Notation/formatting/reference issues throughout.
- Incremental methodologically; limited theoretical guarantees.

Overall recommendation: Reject (clear)
Confidence: High

Constructive suggestions
- Provide full derivations and code; replace “MaxEnt mixture” with a transparent, identifiable parametric mapping (e.g., explicit two-Gaussian parameterization with constraints) or justify the MaxEnt claim rigorously.
- Add a formal experiment-design optimization (e.g., maximize expected KL or FI-based criteria) to move beyond qualitative comparisons.
- Include comprehensive sensitivity analyses (M, lapse model, loss family, transform f, noise coupling) and numerical benchmarks.
