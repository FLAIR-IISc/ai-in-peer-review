This paper addresses the important, and often overlooked, issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inferring priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavioral data, and propose a framework for *a priori* assessment of identifiability. The motivation is sound, stemming from the increasing reliance on Bayesian models as process models and the desire to recover internal representations. The authors present a general observer model and a method based on KL divergence to assess the practical non-identifiability of competing models. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples.

However, while the motivation and framing are strong, the paper suffers from a lack of extensive empirical validation and relies heavily on theoretical analysis within a specifically defined model. My primary concern is that the framework, while elegant, appears heavily tailored to the specific observer model presented, and its generalizability is not convincingly demonstrated. The authors acknowledge this to some extent, but the scope feels limited. The choice of Gaussian mixtures for likelihoods and priors, while convenient for analytical tractability, feels restrictive and doesn't fully address the potential complexities of real neural representations. The analysis within the time perception example, specifically the finding that a narrow loss function *worsens* reconstruction, is interesting, but feels highly dependent on the specific assumptions baked into the model (e.g., the precise form of the loss function and its relationship to feedback). It's unclear how robust this finding is to changes in these assumptions. Furthermore, the statement that the motor parameters were approximately Gaussian in internal measurement space is an assumption and not a finding from the speed perception analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is crucial for the validity of inferences made from Bayesian models of perception.
*   **Well-Defined Framework:** The proposed method for assessing identifiability using KL divergence is clearly presented and logically sound.
*   **General Observer Model:** The parameterized observer model is reasonably flexible and encompasses a wide range of psychophysical tasks.
*   **Illustrative Examples:** The applications to time and speed perception demonstrate the practical application of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Generalizability:** The framework is tightly coupled to the chosen observer model and its assumptions (Gaussian mixtures, specific parameterizations).
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The analysis relies primarily on theoretical calculations. Demonstrating the effectiveness of the framework on *real* data (not just simulated data derived from the model) would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Strong Model Assumptions:** The choice of Gaussian mixtures and specific functional forms for priors and loss functions may not be appropriate for all perceptual tasks.
*   **Sensitivity to Assumptions:** The results appear sensitive to specific parameter settings and assumptions within the model (e.g., the loss function in the time perception example).
*   **Limited Discussion of Alternatives:** While the authors acknowledge previous work on model identifiability, they don’t thoroughly discuss how their approach compares to, or improves upon, existing techniques in other fields.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in the field of Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   The framework provides a novel and potentially valuable tool for *a priori* experimental design.
*   The work is technically sound and well-presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited generalizability of the framework and lack of empirical validation raise concerns about its practical utility.
*   The strong model assumptions may restrict the applicability of the results.
*   The paper feels more like a methodological contribution than a substantive scientific advance.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution, but ultimately falls short of the bar for acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical framework is well-developed, and the identified problem is important. However, the lack of extensive empirical validation and the limitations in generalizability are significant concerns. I would encourage the authors to address these issues by: (1) applying the framework to a wider range of perceptual tasks and datasets; (2) exploring the robustness of the results to changes in model assumptions; and (3) comparing the proposed method to alternative approaches for model identifiability. As it stands, the paper presents a promising framework but requires further development and validation before it can be considered a major advance in the field. A rejection with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the stated concerns seems appropriate.
