This paper presents a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a critical issue in interpreting inferences drawn from fitting such models to behavioral data. The core problem—that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavior—is clearly articulated and motivated. The authors propose a method for *a priori* investigation of identifiability based on exploring the parameter space of a flexible Bayesian observer model. They demonstrate the utility of their framework through case studies in time and speed perception, providing insights into experimental design and parameter recovery.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of Bayesian inference and psychophysical modeling. The mathematical framework is well-developed, and the derivation of the key equation (Eq. 12) linking identifiability to KL divergence is clear. The model presented (Section 2) is a significant strength, offering a good balance between flexibility and computational tractability. The application to both time and speed perception provides concrete examples of the framework’s utility and validates its core principles. The computational aspects appear well-handled, although details of the MCMC sampling are relegated to the supplementary material. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential caveats, enhancing the credibility of their work. However, the reliance on specific parameterizations (e.g., the form of the sensory map in Eq. 2) could be a limitation, and sensitivity to those choices is not thoroughly investigated.  The authors correctly identify that their method provides *a priori* identifiability, but do not elaborate on how to quantify the impact of data/noise levels in a downstream analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly presented, and the technical details are explained with sufficient detail for a reader familiar with Bayesian modeling. The figures are helpful in illustrating the model and results. However, the paper is dense, and the level of mathematical detail may be challenging for some readers. The notation, while consistent, can be overwhelming.  A more streamlined presentation of the model equations (e.g., using clearer variable names and grouping related equations) would improve readability. The jump from general framework description to specific experimental setups is somewhat abrupt.  A more explicit roadmap outlining the subsequent steps after introducing the framework would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception is not entirely new, as the authors acknowledge. However, the systematic framework proposed here—combining a flexible observer model with a KL divergence-based identifiability analysis—is novel.  The authors build on existing work in model identifiability but provide a tailored approach specifically for psychophysical models.  The application of this framework to guide experimental design is a particularly valuable contribution.  The generalization of the model and its ability to capture a large representational basis is also noteworthy.  It would strengthen the paper to explicitly compare this approach to other model identifiability techniques used in other fields (e.g., structural equation modeling).

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental challenge in interpreting Bayesian models of perception.  The ability to assess *a priori* the identifiability of model parameters is crucial for designing informative experiments and avoiding misleading conclusions.  The framework has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers in perception, cognition, and neuroscience who employ Bayesian modeling.  The case studies demonstrate the practical utility of the framework, suggesting that it can guide experimental design and interpretation of results.  The identification of parameters that are particularly difficult to identify (e.g., loss function parameters in the time interval estimation task) is a significant finding. While the current application is limited to specific tasks, the framework is generalizable and could be applied to a broader range of psychophysical phenomena.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical and often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
*   Demonstrates the utility of the framework through compelling case studies.
*   Provides insights into experimental design and parameter recovery.
*   The model is flexible and computationally tractable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and mathematically demanding, potentially limiting its accessibility.
*   The reliance on specific parameterizations may limit the generality of the findings.
*   The analysis is theoretical and does not include empirical validation with real behavioral data.
*   The effect of data/noise levels on identifiability is not explicitly quantified.



**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept.  This is a well-executed and significant contribution to the field. The framework presented is a valuable tool for researchers employing Bayesian models of perception, and the insights gained from the case studies are both theoretically interesting and practically relevant. The minor weaknesses identified above do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.
