

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper proposes a framework for assessing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a critical issue in the field: the non-uniqueness of solutions when inverting Bayesian observer models from behavioral data. The authors introduce a flexible observer model class and a method to explore the identifiability landscape a priori, with applications to two case studies in time and speed perception.

## Strengths

The paper addresses a genuinely important problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling. The authors correctly identify that the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is ill-posed without additional constraints, a limitation that has been noted in prior work [19] but not systematically analyzed. The framework for a priori identifiability analysis is well-motivated and could be valuable for experimental design in psychophysics. The case studies provide some interesting insights, particularly regarding the potential negative impact of constraining the loss function to be narrow (MAP-like) in interval timing tasks.

## Weaknesses

Despite the strong motivation, the paper contains numerous technical and formatting issues that undermine its credibility and clarity:

1. **Inconsistent mathematical notation**: The paper uses inconsistent spacing, formatting, and symbols throughout (e.g., "s s0" instead of "s/s0", "σ21" instead of "σ₁²", "σ`" instead of "σ'"). Equation (4) contains a critical formatting error: "a ≡ µmax − µmin M − 1" should be "a ≡ (µmax − µmin)/(M − 1)". These errors make it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations.

2. **Lack of precise definitions**: The paper claims parameters are "weakly identifiable" without defining what this means quantitatively. The phrase "reconstruction error" appears without a clear metric, making it difficult to assess the results of the case studies.

3. **Inadequate empirical justification**: The claim that "the baseline setup BSL does a relatively poor job at inferring the observers' priors" (section 4.1) is supported only by qualitative figures, not by numerical metrics or statistical tests. Similarly, the claim that speed perception inference is "relatively reliable" (section 4.2) lacks quantitative support.

4. **Theoretical gaps**: The paper states that "the more the trials, the more information to discriminate between models" without providing theoretical justification. The use of KL divergence as a metric for identifiability is not thoroughly justified given the asymmetry in the divergence.

5. **Inconsistent model assumptions**: The paper claims to "allow all model parameters to change freely" but then states "we keep only assumptions (d) and (g)" without explaining why these assumptions are valid. The handling of motor noise in equation (8) is inconsistent with the earlier statement that "we do not include the consequences of response error into the decision making part of the model."

6. **Formatting issues**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for parameters (e.g., "σ`" vs. "σ'"), inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions, and unclear notation (e.g., "s*" vs. "s*"). The phrase "log task space" (section 4.1) is confusing and should be clarified as "log-transformed task space."

7. **Lack of comparison to existing methods**: The paper claims to present "an efficient method" but doesn't compare it to existing approaches for identifiability analysis, making it difficult to assess its novelty or advantages.

## Major Concerns

The most significant issue is that the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence for its claims. The case studies are presented as "worked examples" but lack the quantitative rigor expected in NIPS papers. The paper makes several important claims about experimental design (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function "considerably worsens the quality of the reconstruction") but doesn't provide statistical evidence or error bars to support these claims.

Additionally, the paper's contribution to the field is limited by its lack of clear novelty. The model class presented is a combination of existing techniques rather than a truly novel framework. The framework for identifiability analysis is similar to existing approaches for model comparison [23], with no clear justification for why it's superior.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling, it fails to provide sufficient technical rigor, clear definitions, and empirical evidence to support its claims. The numerous mathematical errors, inconsistent notation, and lack of quantitative results undermine the paper's credibility. The case studies are presented as "worked examples" but lack the statistical depth expected in a NIPS paper.

The paper would benefit from substantial revisions to address these issues, including:
1. Correcting all mathematical notation inconsistencies
2. Providing clear quantitative metrics for "reconstruction error"
3. Adding statistical tests and error bars to the case study results
4. Clarifying the theoretical justification for using KL divergence as a metric
5. Comparing the proposed framework to existing methods for identifiability analysis

Given the substantial number of technical and formatting issues, I recommend rejection. The paper's core idea is valuable, but the execution is insufficiently rigorous for publication at NIPS.
