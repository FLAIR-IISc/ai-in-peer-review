This paper addresses the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing area of research where models are increasingly used to infer internal representations from behavior. The authors provide a framework for *a priori* investigation of identifiability, leveraging a novel, parameterized class of observer models. The core idea is to explore the parameter space and quantify the ambiguity in recovering model parameters from observed behavior. They demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies – time and speed perception – suggesting potential improvements to experimental design based on identifiability analysis.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the problem of non-identifiability and its implications for interpreting results from Bayesian modeling of perception. The extension of previous work to a more general observer model is a valuable contribution. The method proposed for mapping identifiability – using KL divergence and MCMC sampling – appears sound and computationally feasible. The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can be applied, revealing interesting insights into experimental design (e.g., the potentially detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function in time perception). The thoroughness in developing the observer model, detailing each stage from sensation to response, and providing mathematical formulations, is commendable. The inclusion of supplementary material to address detailed derivations and analyses is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the observer model is flexible, it relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., Gaussian distributions, specific functional forms for the sensory mapping and loss function). The extent to which these assumptions limit the generalizability of the findings is not fully discussed. A more thorough sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of deviations from these assumptions would be beneficial. The interpretation of results relies heavily on the choice of the reference observer (θ*). The justification for the specific parameter values chosen for θ* in the case studies is somewhat limited; a more explicit discussion of how different choices of θ* might affect the conclusions would strengthen the analysis. Although the authors acknowledge the theoretical nature of their analysis, a stronger connection to real-world experimental data would be valuable.  The claim that the framework allows one to "rank" experimental designs requires further clarification; the ranking appears to depend on the specific parameters of interest, and the framework doesn’t provide a universally optimal design.  The discussion section is a little brief, and does not sufficiently address the limitations of the proposed framework and potential avenues for future research.  Finally, while the mathematical formulation is extensive, it could be improved with more illustrative examples and figures to help readers understand the key concepts.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis is a significant contribution to the field, addressing a critical challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology, based on KL divergence and MCMC sampling, appears statistically valid and computationally practical.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, although some sections could benefit from further simplification.
*   **Significance:**  The ability to evaluate experimental designs *before* data collection has the potential to improve the efficiency and reliability of research in perceptual inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on several simplifying assumptions in the observer model may limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Reference Observer Dependence:** The results are sensitive to the choice of the reference observer, and the justification for the chosen parameters is limited.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of strong connection to real-world experimental data weakens the practical impact of the framework.
*   **Ranking Ambiguity:** The “ranking” of experimental designs is not universally applicable and depends on the parameters of interest.



**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable and technically sound contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The proposed framework addresses an important problem and offers a potentially useful tool for researchers. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding the reliance on simplifying assumptions and the dependence on the choice of the reference observer, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on addressing the limitations mentioned above through a more thorough sensitivity analysis, a more detailed discussion of the reference observer, and a stronger emphasis on the practical implications of the framework.
