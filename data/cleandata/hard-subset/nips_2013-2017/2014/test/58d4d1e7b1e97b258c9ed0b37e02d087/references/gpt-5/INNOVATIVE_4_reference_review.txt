Summary
This paper tackles a central and often underappreciated issue in Bayesian models of perception: parameter non-identifiability when inverting behavior to infer internal components (prior, likelihood, loss). The authors propose a framework to assess a priori identifiability for a broad class of 1D sensorimotor estimation tasks. The observer model is deliberately flexible yet computationally tractable: (i) a monotonic sensory mapping to an internal measurement space; (ii) measurement noise as a unimodal two-Gaussian mixture with moment constraints; (iii) priors represented as a Gaussian lattice mixture; (iv) loss as a mixture of inverted Gaussians; (v) lapse and motor noise models. A key technical contribution is to avoid repeated data simulation by analytically relating the expected log marginal likelihood under a reference observer θ* to a sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions presp(r|s;θ) across stimuli (Eq. 12). They then define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) and explore identifiability via MCMC.

Two case studies illustrate the framework. In interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010), the authors show that priors are poorly identified under baseline designs due to degeneracy between sensory and motor noise; encouraging a narrower (MAP-like) loss can further degrade inference; measuring motor noise in a separate task greatly improves identifiability. In speed perception (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), the slow-speed prior is robustly recoverable, including the prior exponent, even when decoupling internal from external noise, with modest biases.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of Bayesian decision theory in perception and model identifiability/optimal design (e.g., Myung, Pitt, Navarro; Kwon & Knill; DIC/AIC). The KL-based expected log-evidence is standard in information-theoretic design, but applying it at the level of full response distributions of Bayesian observers, enabled by a fast mixture-of-Gaussians machinery, is new and practically important. It complements prior empirical “Bayesian transfer” and recent cognitive tomography approaches by offering an a priori, design-oriented identifiability analysis.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and usefulness: A principled, efficient method to map identifiability before running experiments and to compare designs for recovering specific internal components.
- Unified, flexible observer class with closed-form computations: the Gaussian-mixture parameterizations for measurement, prior, and loss allow fast evaluation of presp and expected losses.
- Clear demonstrations: Two well-chosen case studies showing nontrivial design insights (e.g., MAP-like loss can harm prior recovery; fixing motor noise helps) and validating robustness for speed priors.
- Addresses a critical problem in the Bayesian perception literature (degeneracies across prior/likelihood/loss) with actionable guidance for experimenters.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope of model assumptions: The “two-Gaussian, unimodal, max-entropy” constructions for measurement and loss are plausible and flexible, but somewhat ad hoc. Please give more detail on: (i) how unimodality is enforced; (ii) the uniqueness/stability of the “locally max-entropy” choice; (iii) sensitivity to allowing >2 components or relaxing unimodality.
- E(θ|θ*) is not a true posterior: While appropriate for identifiability mapping, the paper should clarify its interpretation, its dependence on the implicit prior over θ, and how parameter volume/regularization affects conclusions.
- Computational details: Provide runtime, dimensionality, and MCMC diagnostics (especially with M≈15 prior weights plus many nuisance parameters). Without these, it’s hard to assess practical scalability.
- Dependence on the reference observer: Results are theoretical and may shift with θ*. The authors acknowledge this; it would strengthen the paper to include sensitivity analyses (e.g., varied lapse models, non-log sensory mapping, different loss shapes) and to quantify how rankings of designs change.
- Lapse model choice: Drawing lapse responses from the prior is not standard (often uniform or broad). Show that the main conclusions do not hinge on this choice.
- Link to optimal experimental design literature: The framework is close to maximizing expected information gain (or minimizing expected cross-entropy). Explicitly connecting to Bayesian optimal design would help position the contribution and suggest extensions (e.g., optimizing stimulus sets Pi or rewards to target parameters of interest).
- 2AFC conversion: The speed case repurposes 2AFC as estimation; provide more details or a check that inferences match those from an identifiability analysis done directly in 2AFC space (the authors mention a discrete-choice extension as future work).

Quality
Technically sound overall; the Eq. 12 derivation is standard and appropriate. The main caveats are around model assumptions, computational scalability, and sensitivity to θ*, which can be addressed by added analyses and clarity.

Clarity
Well organized and readable. Some implementation details are deferred to the supplement; bringing key points (unimodality enforcement, MCMC settings, computational cost) into the main text would help. Releasing code would substantially improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately high. The identifiability question is known, and information-theoretic design is not new, but the combination of (i) a unified, efficient observer class enabling closed-form expected losses and response distributions, with (ii) an a priori identifiability mapping used to compare experimental designs for Bayesian perception, is novel and valuable.

Significance
High for the Bayesian perception community and broadly relevant to computational cognitive science. The framework provides actionable guidance for experimenters and may prevent misleading inferences from ill-posed inverse problems.

Pros
- Timely, important problem; clear, principled framework
- Efficient, flexible modeling enabling practical identifiability mapping
- Nontrivial, actionable design insights in realistic case studies
- Potential to influence experimental practice

Cons
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions; limited sensitivity analyses
- Limited computational and convergence details
- E(θ|θ*) interpretation and relation to optimal design could be clarified
- No real-data demonstration (though not strictly necessary for this contribution)

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept to accept). The paper introduces a useful and novel framework with clear potential impact on how Bayesian observer models are validated and experiments are designed. Minor additions on robustness, computation, and positioning would further strengthen it.

Confidence
High.
