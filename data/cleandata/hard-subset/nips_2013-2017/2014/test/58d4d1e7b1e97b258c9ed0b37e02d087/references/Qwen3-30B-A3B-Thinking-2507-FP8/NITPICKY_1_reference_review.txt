

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper proposes a systematic framework for assessing identifiability of Bayesian models of perception before conducting experiments. The authors address a critical issue in the field: the degeneracy of Bayesian observer models, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral data. The paper introduces a general class of observer models parametrized by θ, and develops a method to explore identifiability using KL divergence to quantify model distinguishability.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies a genuine and significant problem in Bayesian perception modeling that has been noted in the literature (e.g., [17, 18, 19]) but lacks systematic analysis.
- The framework is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in experimental design for Bayesian models of perception.
- The two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) provide concrete demonstrations of the framework's utility.
- The authors clearly articulate how their approach could improve experimental design, which is valuable for the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous minor but significant technical errors that undermine confidence in the methodology. Equation (1) contains inconsistent spacing around the integral symbol, and the notation in Equation (3) uses inconsistent formatting for variances (σ21 instead of σ²₁). These small errors suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Incomplete Methodological Details:** The paper claims "Eqs. 3, 4, 5 and 6 combined yield an analytical expression for the expected loss that is a mixture of Gaussians" but provides no derivation or explanation of this claim. The maximum-entropy approach is mentioned multiple times without sufficient detail for replication.

3. **Misuse of Statistical Terminology:** The paper defines "support for observer model θ" as its log likelihood, log Pr(D|θ), but later refers to "the log (marginal) likelihood." The marginal likelihood (evidence) is different from the likelihood, and this confusion could mislead readers about the statistical foundation of the method.

4. **Inadequate Justification of Assumptions:** The choice of parametric forms for the sensory mapping (Equation 2) and loss functions (Equation 6) is not adequately justified. The authors state these forms "can approximate both Weber-Fechner's law and Steven's law" but don't demonstrate why these specific forms are necessary for their framework.

5. **Lack of Quantitative Evidence:** The paper states that "the reconstruction of both the prior and the characteristics of sensory noise is relatively reliable" in the speed perception case study but provides no quantitative measures of reliability (e.g., standard errors, confidence intervals, or error rates). The phrase "relatively reliable" is subjective and unquantified.

6. **Supplementary Material Dependency:** The paper repeatedly refers to "Supplementary Material" for key details (e.g., the "maximum-entropy approach" and the "derivation" of Equation 12), but the supplementary material is not provided in the submission. This prevents verification of critical claims.

7. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for standard deviations (σ² vs. σ2) and for the KL divergence (DKL vs. KL divergence), which creates confusion for readers.

8. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims the framework "covers a large representational basis with a single parametrization, while still allowing fast computation" but provides no evidence that this is faster than alternative approaches, nor does it compare computational efficiency with existing methods.

9. **Limited Scope Demonstration:** While the authors claim the framework "covers a large class of psychophysical tasks," the case studies only address two specific tasks. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the framework would generalize to other task types, such as categorical decision tasks or tasks with multiple sensory cues.

10. **Unsubstantiated Assumptions:** The paper assumes specific parameter values for the reference observer (e.g., λ = 0.03) without justification, which could bias the results of the identifiability analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a theoretically sound framework, the numerous technical inconsistencies, lack of methodological detail, and unsubstantiated claims significantly undermine the paper's quality. The minor methodological flaws, though seemingly small, collectively indicate a lack of rigor in the presentation of the work. The framework itself is interesting, but the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims or demonstrate its practical utility beyond the two case studies.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address the technical inconsistencies, provide complete methodological details, quantify all claims of reliability, and justify the assumptions made. As it stands, the paper's technical shortcomings prevent it from meeting NIPS' standards for quality and clarity.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the problem addressed is significant, the paper's technical flaws and lack of rigorous support for its claims prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field. The framework is promising but requires substantial revision to be presented as a high-quality scientific contribution.
