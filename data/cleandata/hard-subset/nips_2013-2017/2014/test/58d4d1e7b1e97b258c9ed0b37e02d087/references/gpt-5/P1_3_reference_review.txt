Summary
The paper tackles a central challenge for Bayesian models of perception: non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data. The authors present a general observer-model class for one-dimensional estimation tasks that (i) maps stimuli into an internal “measurement” space via a parametric monotonic transform, (ii) models sensory noise, prior, and loss as flexible mixtures of Gaussians chosen via a maximum-entropy criterion, (iii) includes lapses and reporting noise, and (iv) yields tractable response distributions. They propose an a priori identifiability analysis that, assuming a reference observer θ*, evaluates the expected log-likelihood of any θ averaged over datasets generated by θ*. This reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli, yielding an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩) that can be explored by MCMC to map parameter degeneracies and compare experimental designs. Two case studies illustrate the method: interval timing (where prior recovery is fragile unless motor noise is independently known; enforcing a MAP-like loss can worsen identifiability) and speed perception (where a slow-speed prior exponent and contrast-dependent noise are reliably recovered).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Bayesian observer models in perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli 2006; Girshick et al. 2011; Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014) and the longstanding identifiability critique (e.g., Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love; Navarro, Pitt & Myung). The central a priori metric is equivalent to expected cross-entropy and closely related to ideas in model discrimination and Bayesian experimental design (expected information gain). The modeling choices (mixtures in measurement and loss spaces; Gaussian-lattice prior basis) extend earlier parametric approaches while retaining computational tractability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The expected log-likelihood derivation (Eq. 12) as a KL sum is correct for i.i.d. data with known stimulus frequencies. Using this as a surrogate “posterior” for exploring identifiability is reasonable and computationally attractive. The observer model is internally consistent and general enough to capture many psychophysical tasks.
- Support: The two case studies demonstrate the framework’s use for design comparisons and reveal nontrivial interactions (e.g., MAP-like loss can hurt prior identifiability in interval timing). Results are carefully interpreted as a priori/synthetic rather than empirical fits.
- Caveats:
  - Strong parametric assumptions (two-Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss; Gaussian-lattice prior; monotone transform with translational invariance) may shape the identifiability conclusions. Sensitivity analyses to basis size (M), number of mixture components, and alternative lapse models would strengthen claims.
  - The “maximum-entropy” determination of mixture parameters is central yet deferred to the supplement; clearer specification in the main text would aid reproducibility.
  - The conversion from 2AFC to estimation tasks in speed perception is only sketched; the equivalence mapping should be spelled out and validated.
  - Computational details are sparse: MCMC setup, convergence diagnostics, and runtime scaling with parameter dimensionality should be reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear modular model and a crisp identifiability criterion. However:
- Notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent (e.g., occasional use of w1 for motor noise vs ρ1; q vs p; ŝ vs s* vs r). A notation table would help.
- Key algorithmic details (how E(θ|θ*) is sampled; parameter priors; proposal distributions; diagnostics) should be summarized in the main text.
- Some crucial assumptions (e.g., lapse responses sampled from the prior) deserve brief justification and discussion in the main text.

Originality
- The identifiability exploration via expected KL across response distributions is a principled instantiation of expected utility ideas for this modeling class. While the core metric is known in model discrimination and optimal design, integrating it with a flexible, computationally efficient observer model and using it to rank psychophysical designs is novel and valuable.
- The use of mixture-of-Gaussians to keep closed-form expected loss and fast evaluation is a pragmatic and technically interesting modeling contribution.

Significance
- Addresses an important barrier to interpreting Bayesian observer model fits. The framework can inform experimental design choices (e.g., which parameters to fix/measure, task conditions, reward structure) and anticipate non-identifiabilities before data collection.
- If code is released, this could become a widely used tool in perception and decision-making research.
- The case studies yield actionable insights (e.g., measure motor noise; beware of strong MAP-like reward feedback) and provide theoretical support for slow-speed priors.

Pros
- Clear formulation of an a priori identifiability metric with a direct link to KL divergence.
- Flexible, efficient observer model enabling tractable evaluation.
- Thoughtful application to two canonical domains with non-obvious conclusions for design.
- Addresses an active and consequential debate on Bayesian model identifiability.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric forms; limited sensitivity analyses.
- Sparse computational and algorithmic details; reproducibility depends on supplement/code.
- Limited connection to broader Bayesian experimental design literature; missed citations (e.g., Lindley 1956; Chaloner & Verdinelli 1995; Paninski 2005).
- 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence not demonstrated; assumptions about lapse and response noise could bias identifiability conclusions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise algorithm box describing computation of E(θ|θ*), MCMC settings, and runtime.
- Add sensitivity analyses: number of mixture components, prior-basis size M, alternative lapse distributions (uniform or heavy-tailed), non-Gaussian motor noise, and different loss parameterizations (e.g., Lp).
- Report Fisher information or local identifiability metrics at θ* to complement the global MCMC map.
- Explicitly connect to and cite Bayesian optimal experimental design and expected information gain; discuss relation to D/KL-optimal designs.
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and verify conclusions hold in a discrete-choice formulation.
- Clean up notation and fix minor inconsistencies (e.g., w1 vs ρ1). Complete references with years/pages.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (6/10). The contribution is timely and useful, and with added clarity, sensitivity checks, and broader contextualization, it can have significant impact.

Confidence: 0.7
