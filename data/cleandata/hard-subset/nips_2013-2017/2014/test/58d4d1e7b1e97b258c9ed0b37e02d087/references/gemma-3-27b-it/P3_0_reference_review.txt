## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical issue in the burgeoning field of Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. While Bayesian models have shown impressive explanatory power in understanding human behavior, the inverse problem of recovering internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from data is fundamentally ill-posed. The authors present a comprehensive framework for *a priori* assessing the identifiability of a broad class of Bayesian observer models, leveraging a novel parameterization that allows efficient exploration of the model space. They demonstrate the framework’s utility through case studies in time and speed perception, offering insights into experimental design for improved parameter recovery.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a long history of work applying Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) to perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 1996; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2010). It directly addresses concerns raised about the uniqueness of solutions when inverting Bayesian models (e.g., Bowers & Davis, 2012; Jones & Love, 2011).  Previous attempts to address identifiability have often focused on specific constraints (e.g., assuming particular noise models, Maloney & Mamassian, 2001), but lacked a systematic framework for assessing the effectiveness of these constraints. This work moves beyond ad-hoc solutions by offering a formal method for exploring the identifiability landscape. The development of a unified model parameterization also echoes efforts to create more flexible and computationally tractable Bayesian models (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

* **Formalization of a Crucial Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the issue of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a problem often glossed over in empirical studies. This is a vital contribution.
* **Comprehensive Framework:** The proposed framework provides a systematic and principled way to assess identifiability *before* running an experiment, which is a significant advantage.
* **Novel Model Parameterization:**  The continuous class of observer models is well-designed, offering a balance between flexibility and computational efficiency. The use of a parametric form for the sensory map (Eq. 2) is particularly clever.
* **Illustrative Case Studies:** The application to time and speed perception demonstrates the practical utility of the framework and provides concrete examples of how it can inform experimental design.
* **Thorough Mathematical Development:** The paper is mathematically rigorous, with clear derivations and justifications for key assumptions. The inclusion of supplementary material further enhances clarity.
* **Well-Written & Organized:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the complex methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors emphasize efficiency, exploring the identifiability landscape even with their framework is computationally demanding.  Scaling this to more complex models or higher-dimensional stimulus spaces could be prohibitive. This limitation isn’t fully addressed.
* **Assumptions in Model Simplifications:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, specific forms for loss functions, Gaussian reporting noise). While justified, the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions isn’t thoroughly explored. For example, the impact of deviating from Gaussian noise is not assessed, which could be important for some perceptual tasks.
* **Focus on Single-Dimensional Stimuli:** The model is primarily tailored for one-dimensional stimulus estimation. While the authors acknowledge potential extensions to multidimensional stimuli, the challenges associated with such extensions are not fully discussed.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation:** The paper outlines the theoretical framework but provides limited guidance on *how* researchers can readily implement it in their own studies. Providing example code or a user-friendly interface would greatly enhance its impact.
* **Interpretation of Identifiability:** The definition of "practical non-identifiability" based on KL-divergence is reasonable, but the choice of threshold for determining practical equivalence could be debated. Further sensitivity analysis on this threshold would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental and often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Presents a novel and promising framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
* Offers a well-developed and mathematically sound methodology.
* Demonstrates practical utility through compelling case studies.
* Has the potential to significantly improve the rigor and interpretability of research in Bayesian cognitive science.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost may limit its applicability to complex models.
* Reliance on simplifying assumptions requires further investigation.
* Limited discussion of practical implementation hinders wider adoption.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The framework presented is a significant step forward in addressing the challenges of parameter identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.  While the computational cost and simplifying assumptions are limitations, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not fundamentally detract from the paper’s significance. The clarity of the presentation and the rigorous mathematical development are particularly commendable. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper, with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. **Discuss computational scalability:**  Explicitly address the computational challenges associated with applying the framework to more complex models and propose potential strategies for mitigation (e.g., dimensionality reduction techniques, parallel computing).
2. **Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of key assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific loss function forms) on the identifiability results.
3. **Implementation Guidance:** Provide more concrete guidance on how researchers can implement the framework in their own studies, potentially including example code snippets or a description of a user-friendly interface.
4. **Clarify Identifiability Threshold:** Discuss the implications of different KL-divergence thresholds for defining practical non-identifiability.
