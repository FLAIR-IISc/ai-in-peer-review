Summary
The paper addresses a central challenge in using Bayesian decision-theoretic (BDT) models of perception: practical non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data. The authors (i) formalize a flexible, 1D sensorimotor estimation observer class with parametric sensory transform f(s), measurement noise as a two-Gaussian mixture, a Gaussian-mixture prior on a lattice, and a two-Gaussian “inverted” loss, plus lapses and motor noise; (ii) propose an a priori identifiability metric based on the expected log-likelihood of data generated by a reference observer θ* under a candidate θ, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions presp(r|s; θ*) and presp(r|s; θ); and (iii) sample the resulting approximate expected posterior E(θ|θ*) via MCMC to map identifiability and to compare experimental designs. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (recovery of priors across short/long contexts) and visual speed perception (recovery of a heavy-tailed slow-speed prior and noise characteristics). They find substantial degeneracies in interval timing unless motor noise is independently measured; surprisingly, narrowing the loss (MAP-like) worsens recovery. In contrast, speed priors/noise are recoverable under plausible designs, even when decoupling internal from external noise.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Bayesian observer modeling in psychophysics (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli 2006; Maloney & Mamassian 2009; Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al. 2013) and on identifiability/model distinguishability analyses in psychology (Navarro, Pitt, Myung 2004). The use of expected information-theoretic measures to assess design quality connects to broader literatures in optimal experimental design and information geometry in neuroscience/psychology (e.g., Paninski 2005; Myung & Pitt 2009), which could be cited and contrasted more explicitly.

Strengths
- Timely problem: a principled, computationally efficient approach to a priori identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models.
- Clear formalization: reduces expected support to a sum of KL terms over stimuli, enabling fast evaluation and MCMC exploration.
- Flexible observer class with shared analytic structure enabling tractable posterior expected loss and presp(r|s; θ) computation.
- Actionable design insights: shows when and why priors are unrecoverable in interval timing; highlights importance of independent motor noise calibration.
- Validates robustness of slow-speed prior inference and noise estimation under plausible conditions.

Weaknesses
- Heavy parametric assumptions (two-Gaussian mixtures, “local max-entropy” choices, specific f(s)) may limit generality; rationale and sensitivity analyses are limited.
- The identifiability metric uses expected log-likelihood without complexity penalties or priors over θ; relation to Fisher information, profile likelihoods, or other identifiability diagnostics is not explored.
- Case studies are purely synthetic; no real-data reanalysis to corroborate predictions.
- Limited computational details: sampler type, convergence diagnostics, run-times, effects of M (number of prior components) are not reported.
- Some clarity issues/typos (e.g., w1 used as “motor noise” in Fig. 2c discussion; earlier ρ0, ρ1 denote motor noise).
- Mapping 2AFC to estimation in speed perception is nontrivial; the assumptions and potential distortions deserve a more careful justification.

Quality
The modeling and derivations appear technically sound, and the KL-based expected support is appropriate for a priori distinguishability. The simulation studies are convincing within the assumed observer class. However, empirical validation and broader comparisons to alternative identifiability measures would strengthen the claims. The authors are commendably explicit about task assumptions and shared parameters across conditions.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, but dense. Key equations are clear, yet several crucial choices (max-entropy parameterization for mixtures, practical solution for t*(x)) are deferred to the supplement. Including more intuition about why MAP-like losses worsen inference and a small computational cost section would help. Fix minor notational issues and typos.

Originality
While identifiability analyses and design metrics exist in psychology/neuroscience, combining (i) a general, tractable BDT observer class, with (ii) an expected KL-based identifiability landscape sampled via MCMC, and (iii) its use to compare perceptual experiment designs, is a useful and reasonably novel contribution to NeurIPS’ computational neuroscience community.

Significance
If disseminated as a tool, the framework could materially influence experimental design in perception/cognition by revealing which parameters can be recovered and under what designs. The temporal context findings are particularly actionable. The lack of code and real-data demonstrations tempers immediate impact.

Constructive feedback and suggestions
- Provide code and reproducible scripts to compute presp and E(θ|θ*), and to reproduce figures. This would greatly increase utility.
- Compare against alternative identifiability diagnostics: Fisher information matrices, profile likelihoods, posterior curvature/sloppiness, and Jensen–Shannon divergence (symmetric) to check robustness of conclusions relative to KL asymmetry.
- Report sampler details (e.g., NUTS/HMC vs. MH), initialization, convergence diagnostics (R-hat, ESS), and computational costs; analyze sensitivity to M (prior granularity) and the two-Gaussian assumption.
- Clarify the “local maximum-entropy” criterion for fixing mixture degrees of freedom; discuss when mixtures may become multimodal or violate assumptions.
- For interval timing: quantify sample-complexity curves (Ntr vs. reconstruction error) and demonstrate which design manipulations (e.g., varying number of conditions, feedback shaping, double-pass to measure internal noise) most reduce degeneracy. Consider transfer manipulations to further constrain priors/likelihoods.
- For speed perception: justify the estimation/2AFC equivalence; probe confounds among s0, σ̃, and motor noise; explore whether adding more contrast levels further improves identifiability.
- Explicitly distinguish structural vs. practical identifiability; discuss how prior constraints (informative priors on θ) alter conclusions.
- Expand related work to include optimal experimental design for neural/psych models (e.g., Paninski 2005; Myung & Pitt 2009) and connect your expected-KL approach to that literature.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-discussed problem with practical design guidance.
- Methodologically sound with clear, interpretable metric.
- Flexible observer class, analytic efficiencies, and informative case studies.
- Likely to be used by the community if accompanied by code.

Cons for acceptance
- No real-data validation or released software.
- Strong parametric assumptions; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Sparse computational details and limited comparison to alternative identifiability tools.
- Some clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper provides a valuable, principled framework with actionable insights. With added clarity, minor fixes, and ideally public code, it would be a solid contribution to NeurIPS.
