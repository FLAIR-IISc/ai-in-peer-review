

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors demonstrate that the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these components can yield identical behavior. They propose a general framework for systematically analyzing identifiability a priori, before conducting experiments, to guide experimental design and interpretation.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretical and practical tool to assess identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors develop a flexible class of Bayesian observer models that encompasses a wide range of psychophysical tasks, with four key stages (sensation, perception, decision-making, and response) that allow for efficient computation. They then introduce a method to compute the expected support for different parameter values using KL divergence, which quantifies the average support for a model θ given a true model θ*. This approach enables researchers to systematically explore the identifiability landscape and evaluate how different experimental designs affect the ability to recover parameters of interest.

The paper applies their framework to two case studies: time interval estimation (with analysis of different experimental designs) and speed perception (re-examining the inference of slow-speed priors). For the time interval task, the framework reveals that standard experimental designs yield poor recovery of priors due to non-identifiability between sensory noise and prior parameters, while measuring motor parameters (MTR) significantly improves identifiability. For speed perception, the framework confirms that prior and noise characteristics can be reliably inferred, supporting previous findings.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses a fundamental, underappreciated problem in Bayesian modeling of perception that has significant implications for the field's methodological rigor.
2. The theoretical framework is mathematically sound, with clear derivations and proper statistical grounding (using KL divergence as a proxy for model support).
3. The case studies effectively demonstrate the practical utility of the framework for experimental design and interpretation.
4. The authors are transparent about assumptions (e.g., parametric forms for sensory mapping, likelihoods, and loss functions) and limitations.
5. The paper provides concrete guidance for researchers on how to design better experiments to avoid non-identifiability issues.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The framework's applicability is limited to specific types of psychophysical tasks (one-dimensional stimulus estimation), though the authors acknowledge this and suggest extensions.
2. The computational complexity of the framework for more complex models or larger parameter spaces is not thoroughly discussed.
3. The case studies are limited to two specific tasks; while the framework is general, more extensive validation across diverse perceptual domains would strengthen the argument for broad applicability.
4. The paper assumes a specific parametric form for the sensory mapping and likelihoods; it's unclear how sensitive the results are to these assumptions.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by introducing a systematic approach to identifiability analysis in Bayesian models of perception. While the underlying Bayesian models build on prior work (e.g., [9, 12, 24]), the specific framework for identifiability analysis is novel. The authors effectively distinguish their contribution from previous work, noting that while constraints to resolve degeneracy have been used, there has been no systematic analysis of their effectiveness.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem with significant implications for the validity of many studies in perception research. By providing a tool to assess identifiability before conducting experiments, the framework has the potential to improve the reliability of Bayesian modeling in perception. The case studies demonstrate concrete applications that could directly impact experimental design and interpretation in the field.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous mathematical derivations and clear presentation of the framework. The case studies provide strong empirical validation of the approach. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their framework.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to framework to applications. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts and results. The paper provides sufficient detail for replication, though some technical aspects (e.g., MCMC implementation details) could be expanded.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- Addresses a fundamental methodological issue in Bayesian perception modeling that has been overlooked
- Provides a theoretically sound and practically useful framework for experimental design
- Case studies demonstrate clear utility of the approach
- Well-written and technically rigorous
- Potential to improve reliability of Bayesian modeling in perception research

**Con:**
- Limited scope to specific psychophysical tasks (though the framework is general)
- Computational complexity not fully addressed
- Limited validation across diverse perceptual domains
- Some technical depth may limit accessibility for some readers

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a systematic approach to a critical methodological problem. The framework has clear potential to improve experimental design and interpretation in Bayesian modeling of perception. While the scope is somewhat limited to specific perceptual tasks, the authors provide clear pathways for extension to other domains. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, and represents an important step toward more rigorous Bayesian modeling in perception research. I recommend acceptance.
