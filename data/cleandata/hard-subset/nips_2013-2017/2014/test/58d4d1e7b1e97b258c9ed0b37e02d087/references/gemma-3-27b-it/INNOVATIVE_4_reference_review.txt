This paper presents a valuable, and surprisingly missing, framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The core idea – systematically exploring the parameter space to understand which models fit the observed data and whether those models are uniquely defined – is excellent. The authors correctly identify a fundamental degeneracy in Bayesian inference for perception and propose a practical method to assess the implications of this degeneracy *a priori*, before running experiments. This is a bold and insightful approach. While the mathematical details are dense, the presentation is reasonably clear, and the two case studies demonstrate the utility of the framework. 

The strength of this work lies in its novelty and potential impact. Previous work has acknowledged the identifiability problem, but this paper moves beyond simply stating the problem to offering a concrete methodology. The framework's ability to inform experimental design – identifying setups that lead to more reliable parameter recovery – is particularly compelling. The extension to cover a wide range of perceptual tasks with a single parametrization is also impressive.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. The case studies, while illustrative, feel somewhat limited. The time perception example relies heavily on prior assumptions about the reference observer, and the impact of different experimental designs (e.g., MAP loss) is not fully explored – the explanation for the seemingly counterintuitive result regarding the MAP loss is a bit hand-wavy. Similarly, the speed perception example feels more like a validation exercise than a true exploration of identifiability challenges. A more in-depth analysis of how the framework behaves with more complex or less well-defined reference observers would be beneficial. 

Furthermore, the computational complexity of the proposed method isn't fully addressed. While the authors mention efficient computation, a detailed discussion of scalability with increasing model dimensionality or dataset size would be valuable. The reliance on MCMC sampling for exploring the posterior density also raises concerns about convergence and computational cost, particularly for high-dimensional parameter spaces.  The treatment of lapse rates and motor noise, while included, seems somewhat tacked on and could be better integrated into the core identifiability analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The framework is a significant contribution to the field, addressing a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* **Potential Impact:** The ability to evaluate experimental designs *a priori* is highly valuable and could significantly improve the quality of research in this area.
* **Sound Methodology:** The mathematical formulation and proposed methods are well-reasoned and appear technically sound.
* **Well-defined Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of non-identifiability and provides a systematic approach to tackle it.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Case Studies:** The examples are somewhat limited in scope and don't fully demonstrate the framework's robustness.
* **Computational Complexity:** The scalability of the method and the computational burden of MCMC sampling are not fully addressed.
* **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on a pre-defined "reference observer", and the sensitivity of the results to this choice isn't fully explored.
* **Clarity (minor):** Some sections are mathematically dense and could benefit from further simplification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its minor flaws, this paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field. The framework has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. While further validation and exploration of computational scalability are warranted, the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance and recommend it be accepted, perhaps with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above.  The authors have identified a fundamental problem and provided a promising solution.  I'm more enthusiastic about papers that tackle hard conceptual problems, even if the practical implications aren't fully fleshed out, than incremental improvements on existing methods.
