- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models: multiple priors/likelihoods/losses can yield identical behavior, preventing unique parameter recovery.
- Proposes a systematic a priori identifiability framework using a flexible Bayesian observer model to test whether experiments can recover parameters.
- Uses KL divergence to quantify expected support for alternative models given a reference model.
- Implementation is computationally efficient and practically usable for identifiability analysis.
- Case study 1 (temporal interval estimation): standard designs show poor recovery of priors due to parameter degeneracy; MAP assumption can worsen prior recovery.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): speed perception models appear more robust to non-identifiability.
- Theoretical basis is clearly established and the framework provides useful diagnostics (e.g., sensory vs. motor noise degeneracy).
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, with effective figures (notably Figure 2) and reproducible supplementary materials.
- Contribution is original in applying identifiability analysis specifically to Bayesian models of perception and implementing an efficient observer model.
- Significant potential impact: can improve experimental design and prevent erroneous inferences about internal representations.
- Limitations: computational cost may be prohibitive for complex, high-parameter models and concrete benchmarks are not provided.
- Scope limited by case studies (only two perceptual tasks); broader task validation would strengthen generality.
- Does not fully address how to resolve detected non-identifiability beyond suggesting improved experimental designs.
- Needs more discussion connecting the framework to Bayesian model selection approaches (e.g., marginal likelihoods) and justification for using log likelihood as a proxy.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommended for acceptance given its practical utility and potential to become a standard tool in perceptual modeling.
