- Tackles non-identifiability of parameters in Bayesian models of perception and proposes an a priori assessment framework.
- Introduces a novel continuous parameterization enabling efficient exploration of a broad class of Bayesian observer models.
- Demonstrates framework utility with case studies in time and speed perception that inform experimental design.
- Builds on prior BDT and identifiability critiques, moving beyond ad-hoc constraints toward a systematic method.
- Unified parameterization echoes prior efforts to increase flexibility and computational tractability in Bayesian models.
- Clearly formalizes the identifiability problem often overlooked in empirical studies.
- Provides a comprehensive, principled framework for assessing identifiability before experiments.
- Proposes a clever parametric sensory map (Eq. 2) balancing flexibility and efficiency.
- Includes illustrative case studies showing practical implications for parameter recovery.
- Offers rigorous mathematical derivations with helpful supplementary material.
- Well written and logically organized, aiding comprehension of complex methods.
- Exploring the identifiability landscape remains computationally demanding and may not scale well to complex or high-dimensional models.
- Relies on simplifying assumptions (unimodal likelihoods, specific loss forms, Gaussian reporting noise) without thorough sensitivity analysis.
- Focuses on one-dimensional stimuli; extensions and challenges for multidimensional cases are under-discussed.
- Provides limited practical implementation guidance (e.g., example code or interfaces) for researchers.
- Definition of "practical non-identifiability" via KL-divergence is reasonable but threshold choice is debatable and needs sensitivity checks.
- Addresses a fundamental issue and presents a promising, novel framework for identifiability analysis.
- Methodology is mathematically sound and practically useful according to the case studies.
- Potential to significantly improve rigor and interpretability in Bayesian cognitive science.
- Applicability may be limited by computational costs and dependence on simplifying assumptions.
- Limited discussion of practical implementation could hinder wider adoption.
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution deserving acceptance with minor revisions.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, asking authors to (1) discuss computational scalability and mitigation strategies, (2) conduct sensitivity analyses for key assumptions, (3) provide implementation guidance or example code, and (4) clarify and analyze KL-threshold choices.
