This paper presents a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial problem given the increasing reliance on such models to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The authors identify the inherent degeneracy in mapping behavior to priors, likelihoods, and loss functions and propose a method to *a priori* assess identifiability based on exploring the parameter space. The framework is applied to two established psychophysical paradigms: time perception and speed perception.

While the motivation is excellent and the problem statement clearly articulated, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from imprecise technical details to questionable experimental setups and a generally overconfident tone given the simplifying assumptions made. The core idea of systematically exploring identifiability is valuable, but the execution feels incomplete and, in places, unconvincing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The issue of non-identifiability in Bayesian modeling of perception is critical and often overlooked. Addressing this is a significant contribution in principle.
*   **General Framework:** The attempt to create a unified framework applicable to various sensory estimation tasks is commendable.
*   **Analytical Approach:** The use of KL divergence to quantify identifiability, bypassing the need for extensive simulations, is a clever idea.
*   **Case Studies:** Applying the framework to existing paradigms (time and speed perception) provides concrete examples.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Imprecision:** Numerous technical aspects are glossed over. For instance, the “maximum-entropy approach” for defining likelihoods and loss functions is mentioned repeatedly but lacks sufficient detail. The justification for choosing two Gaussian mixtures for *pmeas(x|s)* feels arbitrary and doesn't convincingly address potential issues with other distributions. The derivation leading to Eq. 12 is stated but not fully elaborated, making it difficult to verify.
*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The model relies on numerous simplifying assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, specific functional forms for priors and losses, independent motor noise) that are not adequately justified. The impact of violating these assumptions on identifiability is not explored. The claim that the sensory mapping *f(s)* can be well approximated by the given parametric form without substantial justification is concerning.
*   **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The choice of reference observers (θ*) feels somewhat arbitrary. The parameter values used are taken from previous studies but are not thoroughly explained in the context of the current framework. The motivation for choosing specific contrasts in the speed perception example is weak.
*   **Interpretation of Results:** The interpretation of the results, particularly in the time perception example, seems overly definitive. The conclusion that a narrow loss function *worsens* reconstruction quality is presented as a clear-cut finding, yet the results (Figure 2) show considerable overlap in the posterior distributions. The discussion regarding the identifiability of σ` (loss width) is underdeveloped.
*   **Formatting and Clarity:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts, awkward phrasing) that detract from readability. Mathematical notation is not always clearly defined. Several sentences are convoluted and require multiple readings. Figure 1 is unclear and doesn't effectively convey the model's structure. The variable 'φ' used in the introduction is never used again.
*   **Missing Connections:** The paper doesn’t adequately connect to recent work on identifiability in Bayesian networks or the broader literature on model selection. Existing methods for addressing non-identifiability (e.g., regularization, prior elicitation) are mentioned but not thoroughly discussed in relation to the proposed framework.
*   **Unjustified Claims:** The claim that the model covers a "large representational basis" with a single parametrization is overstated, considering the numerous constraints imposed.
*   **Supplementary Material Reliance:** Critical details are relegated to the supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the validity of the approach without substantial effort.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a fundamentally important problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Presents a novel framework for systematically analyzing identifiability.

**Con:**

*   Numerous technical imprecisions and simplifying assumptions undermine the rigor of the analysis.
*   Experimental setups and interpretations are questionable.
*   The paper lacks clarity and suffers from formatting issues.
*   The framework feels incomplete and doesn’t adequately address the complexities of real-world data.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **rejection**. While the paper identifies a crucial issue and proposes a potentially valuable framework, the numerous shortcomings in technical detail, assumptions, and clarity significantly detract from its overall quality. The framework feels prematurely presented, requiring substantial refinement and validation before it can be considered a robust tool for analyzing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors need to address the aforementioned weaknesses, provide more rigorous justifications for their assumptions, and improve the clarity and presentation of their work. Even with improvements, the scope of this work might be better suited for a workshop than a main NIPS conference.
