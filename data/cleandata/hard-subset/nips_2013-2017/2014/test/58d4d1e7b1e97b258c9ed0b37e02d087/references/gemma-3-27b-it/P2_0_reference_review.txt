## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the crucial issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception – the problem that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can lead to identical observed behavior. The authors present a framework for *a priori* investigating the identifiability of such models, allowing researchers to assess experimental designs *before* data collection and potentially improve them. They introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model encompassing a wide range of perceptual tasks, and develop a method based on KL divergence to quantify identifiability. The framework is applied to two established perceptual scenarios – time interval estimation and speed perception – demonstrating its potential utility.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a fundamental problem:** Identifiability is a significant, often overlooked, challenge in fitting Bayesian models to behavioral data. This work directly confronts this issue.
* **Comprehensive framework:** The developed framework appears well-structured and capable of handling a broad class of psychophysical tasks. The model formulation is flexible and allows for representing a variety of sensory and decision-making processes.
* **A priori analysis:** The emphasis on *a priori* identifiability analysis is a major strength. It offers a proactive approach to experimental design, potentially saving time and resources.
* **Clear motivation and connection to literature:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation, contextualizes the problem within the existing literature on Bayesian modeling of perception, and acknowledges previous work on identifiability (albeit outside of this specific domain).
* **Demonstration through case studies:** Applying the framework to two well-known scenarios (time and speed perception) provides concrete examples and validates its functionality. The results appear insightful, particularly in the time perception case, highlighting potential pitfalls in experimental design.
* **Technical rigor:** The mathematical formulations, particularly the use of KL divergence, seem well-justified and appropriate for the task.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational complexity:** While the authors highlight efforts to improve computational efficiency, the framework still seems potentially demanding, particularly when exploring large parameter spaces.  The scaling of the MCMC sampling (used in the identifiability assessment) with model complexity isn’t explicitly discussed.
* **Model assumptions:** The framework relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., specific functional forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions). The sensitivity of the identifiability results to these assumptions needs further investigation. The authors acknowledge this to some extent but could elaborate on the impact of relaxing these assumptions.
* **Limited exploration of parameter space:** The chosen parameter ranges in the case studies aren’t fully justified. How representative are these ranges of plausible parameter values in real-world scenarios? A more systematic exploration of the parameter space might reveal additional identifiability issues.
* **Lack of comparison with other identifiability techniques:** While the paper acknowledges the existence of general techniques for model identifiability, it doesn't explicitly compare the proposed framework to these existing methods. A brief discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach relative to alternatives would be beneficial.
* **Focus on ideal observers:** The framework centers on “ideal” Bayesian observers. Real human observers are often sub-optimal. How might the framework be extended to account for noise in the decision-making process beyond the “lapse” parameter?



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper addressing a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception. The proposed framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and offers a valuable tool for researchers in the field. While computational complexity and model assumptions are potential limitations, the authors demonstrate the framework’s utility through compelling case studies.  The a priori analysis approach is particularly commendable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and underappreciated problem in the field.
* Presents a novel and well-developed framework for assessing identifiability.
* Demonstrates the framework’s utility with insightful case studies.
* Offers a proactive approach to experimental design.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential computational cost and scalability issues.
* Reliance on simplifying assumptions that may limit generalizability.
* Limited comparison with existing identifiability techniques.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Strong. The paper is technically sound, claims are well-supported, and the analysis is rigorous.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections (e.g., the mathematical derivations) could benefit from further clarification.
* **Originality:** High. The application of identifiability analysis to Bayesian models of perception is novel and represents a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** High. The framework has the potential to significantly improve the quality of research in Bayesian modeling of perception and to lead to more reliable inferences about internal representations.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper pending minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding computational complexity, the sensitivity to model assumptions, and a brief comparison with other identifiability techniques.  I believe this work will be highly valuable to the NIPS community.
