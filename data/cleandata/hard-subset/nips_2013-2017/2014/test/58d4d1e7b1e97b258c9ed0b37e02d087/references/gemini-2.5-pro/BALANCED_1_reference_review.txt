### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the critical issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. While Bayesian observer models are powerful tools for explaining behavior, their core components—prior, likelihood, and loss function—are often degenerate, meaning multiple combinations can produce identical behavior. This ambiguity undermines the goal of recovering an observer's true internal representations from data. The authors propose a novel computational framework to analyze this non-identifiability *a priori*, before an experiment is run, with the goal of improving experimental design.

The framework consists of two main contributions. First, the authors develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks. This model class uses parametric forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians) to represent a wide range of sensory mappings, priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. The parameterization is designed to make subsequent computations tractable. Second, they introduce an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape." Instead of running costly simulations, they derive an analytical expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, which is proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the candidate and a "ground truth" reference model. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" based on this quantity, they can visualize which parameters are well-constrained (identifiable) and which are not.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies from the literature: (1) recovering priors in a time interval estimation task, and (2) examining the inference of a "slow-speed prior" in visual motion perception. For the timing task, they show that recovering the prior is challenging due to degeneracies with sensory and motor noise parameters. Their analysis reveals non-trivial insights, such as that an experimental design encouraging a MAP-like loss function could paradoxically worsen the prior reconstruction, while independently measuring motor noise greatly improves it. For the speed perception task, the framework suggests that the inference of both the prior and noise characteristics is robust, providing theoretical support for the original findings.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The paper tackles a fundamental, pervasive, and often-underappreciated problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The ability to "invert" models to understand internal representations is a central goal of the field, and non-identifiability is a major obstacle. Providing a practical tool to address this a priori is a significant contribution that could improve the rigor and reliability of future research. The topic is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community, particularly those working on probabilistic models, cognitive science, and computational neuroscience.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** While the problem of model identifiability is not new, the authors' proposed framework is highly original. The combination of a flexible yet tractable model class with an efficient KL-divergence-based method for exploring the identifiability landscape constitutes a novel and powerful methodology. This moves beyond simply pointing out the problem to offering a concrete, computational solution for comparing and improving experimental designs.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The methodological approach is sound and elegant. The use of the expected log-likelihood, expressed via the KL divergence (Eq. 12), is a clever way to bypass the need for expensive data simulations, making the analysis of complex models feasible. The observer model class is well-conceived, encompassing many standard assumptions in the literature (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise, non-Gaussian distributions, lapses) within a unified parametric system.

4.  **Clarity and Empirical Demonstration:** The paper is well-written and the motivation is laid out very clearly. The case studies are excellent demonstrations of the framework's practical value. They provide concrete, non-obvious insights into well-known experimental paradigms, showing how the framework can be used not only to identify potential problems (as in the timing task) but also to build confidence in existing results (as in the speed perception task).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Parametric Assumptions:** The framework's conclusions are contingent on the specific parametric forms chosen for the model components (e.g., the sensory map `f(s)` in Eq. 2, the mixture-of-Gaussians representations). While these forms are flexible, it is unclear how robust the findings are to these choices. For instance, the counter-intuitive result that a narrow loss function harms prior recovery might be an artifact of the specific interaction between the parameterizations of the loss, prior, and noise. A discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Omission of Key Details:** The conversion of the 2AFC paradigm from the speed perception study into an equivalent estimation task is a critical step for the validity of the second case study. However, this is mentioned only in passing. Since this conversion is non-trivial and involves its own assumptions, providing more detail in the main text or supplementary material is important for the reader to fully evaluate the result.

3.  **Scope of Applicability:** The framework is currently developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of psychophysics experiments, the authors might briefly discuss the challenges in extending it to other common paradigms, such as multi-dimensional stimuli, classification tasks, or 2AFC paradigms directly, which would further highlight the value and potential of their approach.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   To address the dependence on parametric forms, the authors could add a short discussion in the conclusion acknowledging this limitation and speculating on how one might test for robustness (e.g., by comparing results with alternative parameterizations).
*   Please provide more details on the conversion of the 2AFC task to an estimation task for the speed perception case study. Even a few sentences outlining the mapping and its key assumptions in the main text would significantly improve clarity and reproducibility.
*   The term "approximate expected posterior density" (Eq. 13) is a useful construct but could be slightly confusing, as it's not a posterior in the traditional sense (i.e., conditioned on observed data). It may be helpful to briefly reiterate its interpretation as a tool for visualizing the identifiability landscape, emphasizing that it is derived by averaging over all possible datasets.
*   The impact of this work would be greatly magnified if the framework were made available as a software package. Mentioning the possibility of releasing code in the conclusion could signal this valuable future contribution to the community.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and novel contribution to an important problem. It is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates its utility with insightful case studies. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are largely related to clarity and scope and can be addressed. The work has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers designing experiments in computational perception and cognition. I strongly recommend acceptance.
