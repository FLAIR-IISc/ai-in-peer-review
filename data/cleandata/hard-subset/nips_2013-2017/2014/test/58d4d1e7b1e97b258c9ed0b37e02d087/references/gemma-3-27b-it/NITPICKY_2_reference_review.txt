This paper proposes a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue given the increasing use of these models to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The authors develop a continuous class of observer models and a method for exploring the parameter space to assess the degeneracy of solutions. They apply their framework to time and speed perception, offering insights into experimental design and the reliability of parameter recovery.

The paper is technically ambitious and attempts to address a significant problem in the field. The formulation of a general observer model, encompassing priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and response noise, is a commendable effort. The proposed method for mapping a priori identifiability, using KL-divergence and MCMC sampling, appears well-motivated. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples, though the interpretations are somewhat limited by the model assumptions.

However, despite the ambition and technical sophistication, numerous flaws detract from the paper’s overall quality. The writing is often dense and difficult to follow, with a significant amount of notation introduced without sufficient explanation. The structure feels somewhat disjointed; for instance, the extended discussion of the sensation stage in Section 2.1 feels overly detailed for a paper focused on identifiability. Several critical methodological choices lack justification. The assumption of unimodal sensory distributions (Eq. 3), while presented as a simplification, is not adequately defended and could significantly impact the results. Similarly, the choice of a mixture of Gaussians for the loss function (Eq. 6) appears arbitrary. While motivated by maximum entropy, the rationale for restricting the analysis to only *two* Gaussian components is weak. 

Furthermore, the validation of the framework is insufficient. While the authors demonstrate the framework's utility in identifying non-identifiable parameters, the conclusions drawn from the case studies are often speculative. For example, the claim that a narrower loss function *worsens* reconstruction accuracy in the time perception task is presented without a thorough exploration of the parameter space. The authors acknowledge this might be due to the model's assumptions, but this acknowledgement feels like a post-hoc rationalization rather than a careful consideration of model limitations. The comparison to existing literature is superficial; the authors cite relevant work but rarely engage in a critical discussion of how their framework extends or challenges previous findings.

A significant weakness is the lack of sensitivity analysis. The framework includes numerous parameters; however, the authors do not systematically investigate the impact of varying these parameters on the identifiability results. For instance, how sensitive are the conclusions to the choice of M (number of Gaussian components in the prior) or the specific form of the sensory mapping function (Eq. 2)? This lack of robustness analysis raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. 

Minor issues abound: Equation (13) is labelled "Eq. 13" in the text, but should be "Eq. 12". There are inconsistencies in the use of notation (e.g., θ sometimes represents a full parameter vector, and other times a single parameter). The Supplementary Material is frequently referenced but not readily available for review.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Presents a technically sophisticated framework for analyzing identifiability.
*   Provides potentially useful insights into experimental design.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Poorly written and difficult to follow.
*   Methodological choices lack justification.
*   Insufficient validation and sensitivity analysis.
*   Superficial comparison to existing literature.
*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the ambitious scope and potential significance, the numerous flaws in this paper, ranging from clarity issues to methodological weaknesses and insufficient validation, outweigh its strengths. The framework, while interesting, requires substantial refinement and more rigorous evaluation before it can be considered a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection**. The paper feels premature and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The authors should address the methodological concerns, improve the clarity of the writing, and conduct a more thorough validation of their framework before resubmitting.
