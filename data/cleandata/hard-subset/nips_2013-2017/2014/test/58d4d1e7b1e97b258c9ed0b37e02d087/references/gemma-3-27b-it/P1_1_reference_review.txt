## Peer Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a crucial, often overlooked issue in the growing field of Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate, theoretically, that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can lead to identical behavioral predictions within a Bayesian framework. They present a novel framework to *a priori* assess the identifiability of Bayesian observer models and demonstrate its utility through application to time and speed perception tasks. The core contribution is a method to explore the parameter space and assess the sensitivity of model predictions to variations in underlying assumptions, allowing for more informed experimental design.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a long line of work applying Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) to perception, citing foundational papers like Knill & Richards (2003) and more recent studies demonstrating near-optimal human behavior (e.g., Körding & Wolpert, 2004). It acknowledges criticisms regarding the uniqueness of solutions in Bayesian modeling (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) and seeks to provide a systematic approach to address these concerns, moving beyond ad-hoc constraints frequently employed in the literature (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian, 2001; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2012). The work connects to broader themes of model identifiability discussed in psychology (Anderson, 1983) and statistics (Navarro et al., 2004).

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of non-identifiability is fundamental to the interpretation of Bayesian models of perception. This paper tackles it head-on with a clear and rigorous analysis.
* **General Framework:** The authors develop a flexible framework applicable to a wide range of psychophysical tasks. The model’s ability to encompass different sensory mappings (Eq. 2) and loss functions (Eq. 6) is particularly impressive.
* **A Priori Analysis:**  The focus on *a priori* identifiability analysis is a major strength. By assessing potential ambiguities *before* data collection, the framework offers valuable guidance for experimental design.
* **Clear Methodology:** The proposed method, utilizing KL-divergence and MCMC sampling (Eqs. 12 & 13), provides a concrete and practical approach to explore the identifiability landscape.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The applications to time and speed perception provide compelling demonstrations of the framework’s utility.  The analysis of time perception revealed non-intuitive insights about the influence of loss function assumptions, which highlights the importance of this kind of analysis.
* **Well-written & Organized:**  The paper is generally clear, well-structured, and logically presented. The inclusion of equations and a flowchart (Figure 1) enhances understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors emphasize computational efficiency, exploring the high-dimensional parameter space (θ in Eq. 10) is still likely to be computationally demanding for complex models and datasets.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required and potential scalability issues.
* **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., unimodal sensory distributions, specific functional forms for priors and loss functions). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions could be further explored. While the authors acknowledge this, a more systematic analysis of the impact of relaxing these constraints would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Examples:** The two case studies, while insightful, might not fully represent the diversity of Bayesian modeling applications in perception.  Demonstrating the framework's effectiveness on a wider range of tasks would strengthen the claims.
* **Marginalization Complexity:** While the authors state the integral in Eq. 9 can be computed efficiently, a bit more details about this procedure could be helpful. Some readers may be concerned about the potential numerical instabilities involved in the marginalization process.
* **Connection to Neural Data:** While the paper focuses on behavioral data, a discussion of how the framework could be integrated with neural data (e.g., to constrain prior distributions) would broaden its impact.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental and often overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling.
* Presents a novel and practical framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
* Provides valuable insights into experimental design and model interpretation.
* Demonstrates the framework's utility through compelling case studies.
* The paper is well-written, technically sound, and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost may limit applicability to complex models and large datasets.
* Relies on simplifying assumptions that could impact the generality of the findings.
* The scope of the examples is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The authors have identified a critical issue, developed a promising framework for addressing it, and demonstrated its utility through insightful applications. While the computational cost and simplifying assumptions are legitimate concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work.  The framework has the potential to become a valuable tool for researchers in perception, cognition, and neuroscience.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the following minor points:

1.  Expand the discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the method.
2.  Provide a more systematic analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the model’s assumptions.
3.  Briefly discuss the potential for integrating the framework with neural data.
4. Provide further details about the numerical procedure used to calculate the integral in Eq. 9.




**Confidence:** 8/10
