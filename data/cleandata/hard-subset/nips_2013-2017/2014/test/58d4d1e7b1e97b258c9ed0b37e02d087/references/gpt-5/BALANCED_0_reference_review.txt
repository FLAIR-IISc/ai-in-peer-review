Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a central challenge for Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) when inferred from behavior. Building on a known degeneracy (multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and losses can yield the same optimal decision rule), the authors propose an a priori identifiability framework. They define a flexible 1D observer model with (i) a parametric stimulus-to-measurement transform f(s) that renders measurement noise approximately stationary, (ii) mixture-of-Gaussians representations for sensory likelihood and prior (the latter as a dense Gaussian basis), (iii) a flexible mixture-of-Gaussians loss function in internal space, and (iv) lapse and response noise. They then quantify expected evidence for an alternative parameterization θ when data are generated by a reference observer θ∗. The key result is an analytic expression for the expected log-likelihood difference as a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions presp(r|s;θ∗) and presp(r|s;θ), averaged over the experimental stimulus distribution. They sample from E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp(〈log Pr(D|θ)〉) via MCMC to map identifiability. Two case studies follow: (1) interval timing with short/long stimulus ranges, suggesting poor identifiability of priors unless motor noise is independently constrained and that MAP-like losses can harm identifiability; (2) speed perception with contrast-dependent noise, where estimates of a heavy-tailed slow-speed prior (and noise characteristics) are recovered robustly.

This paper sits in a line of work on the distinguishability of cognitive models and identifiability (Navarro, Pitt, Myung), critiques of over-flexible Bayesian modeling (Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis), and psychophysical Bayesian observer modeling (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Stocker & Simoncelli; Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.). The emphasis here is on an efficient, task-agnostic procedure to evaluate identifiability/experimental design a priori for continuous 1D estimation tasks, complementing empirical studies and “Bayesian transfer” tests.

Quality
- The expected log-likelihood formulation via KL divergences is standard and appropriate for a priori design analysis. Using presp rather than internal latent distributions correctly respects the observable.
- The observer model is engineered for computational tractability (mixture-of-Gaussians leading to analytical expressions for expected loss), which is a practical contribution.
- However, the framework hinges on strong parametric assumptions (two-component Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss; fixed Gaussian basis for priors; specific f(s)). It is unclear how sensitive conclusions are to these choices or to mis-specification of θ∗.
- Empirical validation is limited to simulations. There is no comparison to alternative identifiability/design diagnostics (e.g., Fisher information, expected information gain, posterior contraction under synthetic data, profile likelihood), nor a demonstration that the proposed E(θ|θ∗) predicts actual recovery error across datasets.
- Some presentation issues reduce confidence: several references are incomplete; a footnote appears mis-placed; minor symbol inconsistencies (e.g., w1 vs ρ1) occur; many core derivations are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear separation between model specification and identifiability analysis.
- The modeling choices (e.g., maximum-entropy moment-matched mixtures) are motivated but need more intuition and explicit formulas in the main text for the expected loss mixture result.
- Case studies are described clearly, but fuller details on MCMC settings, computational cost, and convergence diagnostics would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- While the concern and some tools are known, the combination of: (i) a general, fast observer model spanning priors/likelihoods/losses; (ii) a task-level expected-evidence metric for a priori identifiability; and (iii) explicit use for ranking experimental designs in psychophysics is novel and practically useful.
- The finding that narrow (MAP-like) loss can worsen prior identifiability in timing tasks is a non-obvious, interesting design insight.

Significance
- If robust, the framework could help psychophysics practitioners proactively diagnose degeneracies, choose stimuli/conditions, and decide which parameters to fix or measure separately.
- The speed-perception case supports prior empirical claims and may increase confidence in inferring slow-speed priors. The timing case highlights pitfalls that could reshape how interval timing experiments are designed.
- Impact is tempered by reliance on a specified reference observer and limited sensitivity analyses; generalization to alternative parameterizations or discrete-choice tasks is promised but not yet delivered.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-examined issue (a priori identifiability) with practical guidance for experimental design.
- Methodologically sound expected-evidence formulation; efficient implementation via mixtures.
- Insightful case studies yielding actionable recommendations (e.g., measure motor noise; beware narrow losses).
- Potentially broadly useful across Bayesian psychophysics.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong parametric assumptions; generality may be overstated without sensitivity analyses.
- No empirical validation on real data or prospective experiments to confirm design recommendations.
- Missing comparisons to established identifiability/design metrics; no optimization of stimuli distribution Pi.
- Presentation issues: incomplete references; some notation/footnote errors; core derivations relegated to supplement; code availability not stated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses: vary mixture complexity (number of components), f(s) forms, and θ∗ to assess robustness of identifiability conclusions.
- Validate the E(θ|θ∗) metric empirically: simulate datasets, run full inference under misspecified models, and correlate true recovery error with predicted identifiability; report calibration as Ntr varies.
- Compare against alternative criteria (e.g., expected information gain, Fisher information, posterior volume) and discuss trade-offs.
- Explicitly formulate optimal/near-optimal design procedures: optimizing stimuli sets Pi and condition structure to maximize minimal KL separation over parameter subsets of interest.
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence assumptions and provide a discrete-choice extension or at least a simulation bridging result.
- Improve clarity and completeness: fix references, unify notation, bring key derivations to the main text, and release code.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the ideas promising, but the current submission relies on strong modeling choices, lacks comparative/empirical validation, and has presentation gaps. With robustness checks, comparisons, and clearer exposition (ideally with code), this work could make a valuable contribution.
