Summary
The paper proposes a framework to study a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in 1D sensorimotor estimation tasks. The authors introduce a flexible parametric observer class: (i) a monotonic sensory mapping from stimulus s to internal coordinate t via a log/power family; (ii) a sensory likelihood in t as a unimodal mixture of Gaussians matched to specified moments; (iii) a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians; (iv) a loss function modeled as an inverted Gaussian mixture; (v) lapses and additive motor/report noise in s. A key analytical component is that the expected loss under posterior and mixture loss remains a tractable mixture, enabling fast evaluation of predicted response densities.

To assess practical identifiability without resampling many datasets, they derive the expected log-evidence of a candidate observer θ when data are generated by a reference θ*: the expectation equals a stimulus-frequency-weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions, up to a constant. They define an “approximate expected posterior” proportional to the exponentiated expected log-evidence and explore it via MCMC to map identifiability regions as a function of trial count and design.

Two case studies illustrate the method: interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen) and speed perception (Stocker & Simoncelli). For timing, they show strong trade-offs between sensory and motor noise and weak identifiability of the loss width; fixing motor noise markedly improves prior recovery, whereas encouraging near-MAP losses can harm identifiability. For speed, they recover the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters reasonably well across contrast conditions, even when decoupling internal and external noise models.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well grounded in Bayesian decision theory and the literature on model non-identifiability in cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung), Bayesian transfer (Maloney & Mamassian), and perceptual Bayesian modeling (Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert; Acerbi et al.). The invariance of Eq. 1 under joint reweightings of prior/likelihood/loss is a known source of degeneracy; here the contribution is to quantify practical identifiability under realistic designs via an expected-KL criterion and a fast, unified observer parametrization. This complements empirical fitting and cognitive tomography approaches that invert models from data.

Strengths
- Clear, principled identifiability objective: the expected log-evidence reduces to a sum of KL divergences, avoiding costly dataset resampling and aligning with classical information criteria.
- Flexible, unified observer family with analytic/numerically efficient components (mixture-of-Gaussians representations and tractable expected loss).
- Actionable design insights: shows when fixing motor noise or using multiple conditions helps, and when narrowing the loss can paradoxically degrade identifiability.
- Demonstrates both pessimistic and optimistic scenarios: challenging identifiability in timing vs. robust recovery in speed perception.
- Potentially useful as a pre-registration/design tool for psychophysics experiments seeking to infer internal representations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on strong parameterizations: identifiability conclusions may hinge on the specific choices (log/power mapping, Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss, lattice prior, Gaussian motor noise). Sensitivity analyses to these modeling choices are limited in the main text.
- Limited scope: 1D estimation tasks only; 2AFC paradigms are only indirectly addressed via an estimation surrogate. The mapping from 2AFC to estimation can itself affect identifiability conclusions.
- The “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) is not a true posterior; priors over θ and model complexity penalties are not incorporated. Implicit uniform priors and parameter bounds can bias identifiability landscapes.
- Experimental design optimization is not carried through. Given Eq. 12, one could derive or approximate optimal stimulus distributions Pi or contrast conditions to maximize expected discriminability; the paper focuses on a few handpicked designs.
- Some critical steps are relegated to the supplement (derivation of Eq. 12; moment-to-mixture mapping under unimodality and max-entropy; computational complexity and MCMC diagnostics).
- References are incomplete/malformed in places, which reduces clarity and traceability.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the expected log-evidence derivation is standard and appropriate; the modeling choices are reasonable and allow fast evaluation. The case studies are carefully set up and provide convincing illustrations of identifiability pitfalls. However, the strength of claims like “theoretical validation” of slow-speed prior inference should be tempered given modeling assumptions (estimation surrogate, assumed lapse/report noise forms). More detail on algorithms (runtime, convergence, effective sample sizes), parameter priors, and sensitivity to M and mixture order would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well structured, with a helpful decomposition into stages. Notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent (pmeas vs qmeas, task vs internal spaces), and the reader must consult the supplement for key implementation details. Figures (not visible here) appear central. The bibliography needs cleanup (years, venues).

Originality
A moderate but meaningful contribution. Using expected KL to assess identifiability is classical, but integrating it with a flexible, tractable observer class to proactively compare psychophysical designs is novel and useful for this community. The explicit exploration of prior/likelihood/loss degeneracies in applied tasks is timely.

Significance
Likely to influence experimentalists modeling perception under BDT, by enabling pre-experimental design vetting and highlighting when additional constraints (e.g., motor calibration) are essential. Broader impact beyond specific tasks depends on extending to multi-dimensional spaces and discrete-choice paradigms.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Connect to Bayesian optimal experimental design: can you optimize stimulus frequencies Pi and condition scheduling to maximize expected discriminability (e.g., minimize worst-case E, D-optimality, or maximize expected information gain)?
- Report computational details: wall-clock time per E(θ|θ*) evaluation, MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity to M (number of prior basis functions) and mixture order.
- Provide ablations on parameterizations: alternative loss families (e.g., generalized Gaussian), non-Gaussian report noise, different f(s) forms; quantify how identifiability conclusions change.
- Clarify the max-entropy mixture fitting from moments and the unimodality constraint; provide closed-form or algorithmic steps.
- For the speed case, justify the estimation surrogate for 2AFC and test robustness by simulating a genuine 2AFC observer with the same θ*.
- Consider adding local identifiability analyses (e.g., Fisher information, profile likelihoods) to complement the global expected-KL landscape.
- Release code and example configurations to facilitate adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-discussed problem (practical identifiability) with a principled, implementable approach.
- Offers concrete design guidance with nontrivial insights.
- Technically coherent and relevant to the NeurIPS community working on probabilistic perception/cognitive modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Scope limited to 1D estimation; 2AFC and higher-dimensional tasks are not yet handled.
- Conclusions may be sensitive to modeling choices; limited sensitivity analysis in the main paper.
- Missing details and bibliographic issues.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The framework is valuable and the case studies are informative; with improved exposition, sensitivity analyses, and a clearer link to optimal design, this could be a strong contribution.
