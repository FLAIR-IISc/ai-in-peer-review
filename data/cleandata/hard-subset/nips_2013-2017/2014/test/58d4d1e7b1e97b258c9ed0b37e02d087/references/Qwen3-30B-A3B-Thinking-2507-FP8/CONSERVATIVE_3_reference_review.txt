

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses an important problem in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors propose a framework to systematically assess identifiability a priori for a specific class of Bayesian observer models, using KL divergence as a proxy for model support to evaluate how well parameters can be recovered under different experimental designs.

**Strengths:**
The paper clearly articulates a genuine problem in the field (model non-identifiability) and provides a well-structured theoretical framework to address it. The case studies (temporal context and speed perception) demonstrate practical applications of the framework, showing how it could inform experimental design choices. The mathematical formulation is thorough and the paper is well-written with clear organization.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from significant issues with novelty and rigor that prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards. The core approach of using KL divergence to assess identifiability is a standard statistical technique that has been used in model comparison for decades (e.g., in AIC/BIC approaches and previous work on model distinguishability [23]). The paper fails to clearly distinguish its method from existing approaches to model identifiability, which weakens its central claim of providing a "systematic, a priori investigation" of identifiability.

The empirical validation is severely limited to just two case studies, both of which rely heavily on specific assumptions about the reference observer (e.g., assumed sensory mapping parameters, loss function properties). The authors do not demonstrate how their framework would improve experimental design in real-world settings, nor do they validate their approach against existing methods for addressing model non-identifiability (such as Bayesian transfer [5] or constraint-based approaches [12, 19]).

The paper overstates its contribution as filling a "gap" for "a large class of psychophysical tasks," when in fact the framework is specifically designed for a narrow class of one-dimensional estimation tasks. The authors acknowledge this limitation but fail to convincingly justify why this narrow framework represents a significant advance over existing methods.

The theoretical foundation has important limitations. The use of KL divergence as a proxy for model support (Equation 12) relies on approximations that are not thoroughly analyzed or validated. The paper does not provide error bounds or sensitivity analyses for these approximations, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the conclusions drawn from the framework.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the problem addressed is important, the paper's contribution lacks sufficient novelty and rigor to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The framework represents a specialized application of standard statistical techniques rather than a significant methodological advance. The limited empirical validation and theoretical shortcomings prevent the paper from demonstrating that this approach offers meaningful advantages over existing methods for addressing model identifiability.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: The paper identifies a genuine problem in Bayesian perception modeling and proposes a structured approach to address it.
- Pro: The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework could inform experimental design.
- Con: The proposed method is not sufficiently novel—essentially a standard statistical technique applied to a specific problem.
- Con: The empirical validation is limited to two narrow case studies without broader demonstration of utility.
- Con: The paper overstates the significance of its contribution without adequate justification.
- Con: The theoretical foundation lacks rigorous validation of key approximations.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper makes a modest contribution to a specific niche within Bayesian perception modeling but fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty, rigor, or broad applicability to meet NIPS's high standards. The framework is not sufficiently distinct from existing approaches to model identifiability, and the limited empirical validation does not convincingly demonstrate its utility beyond the specific scenarios presented.

**Suggested Improvements:**
1. Clearly distinguish the proposed framework from existing methods for model identifiability (e.g., [23]) and demonstrate concrete advantages.
2. Provide more extensive empirical validation across a wider range of tasks and experimental designs.
3. Include a critical analysis of the limitations of the KL divergence approximation and provide error bounds.
4. Acknowledge that the framework is limited to specific types of tasks and avoid overstating its general applicability.

The paper would benefit from a more critical assessment of its own limitations and a clearer demonstration of how it advances beyond existing work in the field. Without these improvements, the contribution does not meet the standards expected for publication at NIPS.
