

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses an important issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors propose a framework to systematically assess identifiability a priori through a general class of Bayesian observer models and an efficient method for exploring identifiability landscapes. The paper is well-motivated, with clear connections to previous work on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., [5, 6, 7, 9, 12, 24]) and relevant issues in model identifiability (e.g., [19, 22, 23]).

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound framework that addresses a genuine problem in the field. The mathematical formulation of the observer model is generally well-developed, though I identified several critical technical inconsistencies. The derivation of the expected log likelihood (Eq. 12) is flawed because it uses DKL(presp(r|si;θ*)||presp(r|si;θ)) rather than the reverse direction, which would be more appropriate for measuring how well θ fits data generated by θ*. The paper also contains several notational inconsistencies: in Eq. 2, the inverse function is incorrectly formatted as "s0 d √ e t−B A − 1" (should be s0*(exp((t-B)/A)-1)^(1/d)), and in Figure 2c, the authors refer to "motor noise, w1" when w1 is a prior weight (not a noise parameter). The claim that "the degeneracy is not surprising, as both sensory and motor noise... are approximately Gaussian" is vague and lacks mathematical justification. The paper also fails to provide quantitative metrics for "reliable" reconstruction in Section 4.2, making the claims about speed perception less convincing.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized, but several sections suffer from unclear notation and inconsistent terminology. The transformation between task space and internal measurement space is confusingly described, with inconsistent use of variables (s vs. t). The paper switches between using "σ`" and "σ_`" for the loss function parameter, and the description of the loss function (Eq. 6) as negative is counterintuitive for a loss function. The explanation of the MAP design variant is confusing: the paper states that a narrower loss function "worsens the quality of the reconstruction" but doesn't clearly explain why this is counterintuitive or provide evidence for the mechanism. The supplementary material appears necessary to fully understand the model, but the main text should be self-contained.

**Originality**: The paper's core contribution is a framework for a priori identifiability testing, which is valuable but not entirely novel. The authors cite [23] as a reference for existing approaches to model identifiability but don't clearly distinguish how their method differs or improves upon it. The specific implementation of the observer model (with the sensory transformation in Eq. 2 and the mixture-of-Gaussians approach) is an interesting technical contribution, but the framework itself doesn't represent a significant conceptual advance over existing model comparison techniques. The case studies are well-chosen but don't demonstrate novel empirical findings.

**Significance**: The problem of model non-identifiability is important for Bayesian models of perception, and the paper makes a valuable contribution to addressing it. However, the significance is somewhat limited because the paper doesn't provide a clear path for how the framework would be adopted by practitioners, and the case studies don't reveal unexpected insights beyond what would be expected from careful model analysis. The claim that "a design that encourages the observer to adopt a loss function closer to MAP considerably worsens the quality of the reconstruction" is interesting but not sufficiently motivated or explained.

**Strengths**: The paper identifies a real problem in the field, provides a well-structured framework for addressing it, and demonstrates its utility through two case studies. The model architecture is flexible and covers a broad class of perceptual tasks. The approach to using expected log likelihood as a proxy for identifiability is reasonable and practical.

**Weaknesses**: The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and notational errors that undermine its credibility. The derivation of Eq. 12 has a critical flaw in the direction of the KL divergence. The paper lacks sufficient quantitative metrics to support claims about "reliability" of reconstructions. The novelty is overstated relative to existing work on model identifiability. The case studies are limited to two examples without broader validation.

**Recommendation**: I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a generally sound framework, the technical inconsistencies and notational errors are too severe to overlook. The paper's claims about the effects of experimental designs (e.g., the negative impact of MAP loss functions) are not sufficiently supported by the analysis. The framework is not sufficiently novel to warrant publication at NIPS, and the technical flaws prevent the paper from making a compelling contribution to the field.

The authors should carefully rework the mathematical derivations, fix the notational inconsistencies, provide quantitative metrics for their claims about reconstruction quality, and more clearly distinguish their approach from existing model identifiability techniques before considering resubmission. The core idea has merit, but the execution is insufficiently rigorous for NIPS.
