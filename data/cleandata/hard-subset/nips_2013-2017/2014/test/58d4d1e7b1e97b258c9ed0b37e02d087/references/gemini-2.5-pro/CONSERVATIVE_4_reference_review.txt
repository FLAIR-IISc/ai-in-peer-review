**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a well-known issue where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions. The authors propose a novel framework to assess this identifiability *a priori*, with the goal of guiding experimental design. The core of their contribution consists of two parts: first, a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models that parameterizes the sensory, perceptual, decision-making, and response stages of a task; second, a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model under a "ground truth" reference model. This metric, which is shown to be proportional to the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the models' response distributions, is used to map out the "identifiability landscape" via MCMC sampling. The authors demonstrate their framework on two simulated case studies from the literature: inferring priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing priors and noise characteristics in a speed perception task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important Problem:** The problem of non-identifiability is a fundamental and often under-appreciated challenge in computational modeling of cognition. A systematic approach to diagnose and mitigate this issue before data collection would be a significant contribution to the field. The motivation is strong and well-articulated.
2.  **Principled Approach to Experimental Design:** The goal of using formal analysis to improve experimental design is laudable. The framework provides a concrete, quantitative method for comparing the potential of different experimental setups to resolve specific model parameters, moving beyond researcher intuition.
3.  **Elegant Formulation:** The proposed identifiability metric, based on the expected log-likelihood and its connection to the KL divergence, provides a theoretically grounded measure of distinguishability between two probabilistic models. This is a clean and sensible way to formalize the problem.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness of this work is that the proposed framework is evaluated entirely on simulated data generated from the model class it is designed to analyze. The "reference observers" in the case studies are not based on fits to real data but are constructed from parameters reported in the literature. While illustrative, this provides no evidence that the framework's conclusions are relevant for real-world experiments with all their attendant complexities and sources of noise not captured by the model. The claims about improving experimental design are, therefore, speculative. A convincing validation would require demonstrating the framework on existing datasets, showing that the *a priori* predicted degeneracies match the *a posteriori* correlations observed when fitting models to actual human data.
2.  **Over-reliance on a Specific Model Class:** The framework's machinery is tightly coupled to the proposed parametric observer model. The tractability of the approach relies heavily on the convenient choice of representing priors, likelihoods, and loss functions as mixtures of Gaussians. While flexible, this is a strong set of assumptions. The analysis can only reveal identifiability issues *within this specific model class*. It cannot speak to the possibility that the true generative process lies outside this class, which is a different and arguably more severe form of misspecification. The generality of the framework is thus more limited than suggested.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Established Methods:** The paper acknowledges the existence of generic techniques for identifiability analysis but dismisses them as intractable without providing a thorough justification or comparison. Methods based on the analysis of the Fisher Information Matrix, simulation-based calibration, or even extensive posterior predictive checks are standard practice for assessing identifiability. The authors do not demonstrate that these established methods fail for this problem, nor do they show that their proposed method provides more accurate or efficient results. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the novelty and necessity of the proposed framework. It appears to be a bespoke solution to a general problem, but its advantages over more general-purpose tools are not established.
4.  **Conclusions Remain Model-Specific:** The findings from the case studies are interesting, but they are ultimately statements about the authors' model rather than about perception itself. For instance, the conclusion that a narrow (MAP-like) loss function worsens the inference of the prior in the timing task is an intriguing result. However, it is unclear if this is a general principle or an artifact of the specific interactions between the parameterizations of the loss function, sensory noise, and motor noise within their model. Without grounding in empirical data, these conclusions risk becoming the kind of "just-so stories" that Bayesian modeling is sometimes criticized for.

**Recommendation**

While the ambition of this paper is commendable and the core idea is appealing, the work in its current form is premature for publication at NIPS. It presents an unproven methodological proposal. The primary criteria for a methodology paper should be a rigorous demonstration of its utility and, where applicable, its superiority to existing techniques. This paper falls short on both fronts. The lack of any validation on real experimental data is a critical omission that undermines the central claim of being a practical tool for experimental design. Furthermore, the failure to adequately compare the proposed method against established approaches for identifiability analysis leaves the contribution insufficiently contextualized.

The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a complete and validated piece of research. I would encourage the authors to apply their framework to several real datasets from the literature to demonstrate that its *a priori* predictions are borne out in practice. A detailed comparison with standard methods for identifiability analysis would also be necessary to justify the need for this new, model-specific framework. As it stands, the paper presents a novel idea but does not provide the extensive evidence and thorough validation required to endorse it.

**Final Decision: Reject**
