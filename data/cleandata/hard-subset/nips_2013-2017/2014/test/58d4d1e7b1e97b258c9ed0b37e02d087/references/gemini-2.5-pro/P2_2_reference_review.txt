**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors tackle a fundamental and well-known problem in the field: multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical observable behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover an observer's internal representations from data. The proposed solution consists of two main components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians to represent the sensory likelihood, prior, and loss function, allowing it to approximate a wide variety of observer characteristics within a single, computationally tractable framework. Second, they propose an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape" for a given experimental design. Instead of relying on computationally expensive data simulations, their method calculates the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model given a "true" reference model. This expectation elegantly reduces to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models, providing a measure of their distinguishability. By sampling from an approximate posterior defined by this measure, the framework can reveal which model parameters are well-constrained by a given experimental design and which are prone to degeneracy. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one on temporal interval estimation, which is used to compare different experimental designs, and one on speed perception, which provides theoretical validation for a classic finding in the literature.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of the problem is clear and well-motivated. The development of the flexible observer model is thoughtful, particularly the use of an internal measurement space to handle stimulus-dependent noise and the mixture-of-Gaussians approach to ensure tractability. The core methodological contribution—using the expected log-likelihood and its connection to KL divergence to assess identifiability—is both clever and sound. It provides a principled and efficient alternative to brute-force simulation. The claims are well-supported by the case studies, which yield non-trivial and interesting results. For instance, the finding that a MAP-like loss function could worsen prior recovery in the time perception task is a valuable and non-obvious insight that demonstrates the power of this *a priori* analysis. The authors are also careful to state the assumptions and limitations of their model.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out compellingly in the introduction, followed by a logical, step-by-step description of the four stages of the observer model (Sensation, Perception, Decision-making, Response). The mathematical formalism is presented clearly, and the connection between the theoretical framework (Section 3) and its application (Section 4) is seamless. The figures are informative and effectively illustrate the main results, such as the comparison of recovered priors (Fig. 2) and the identifiability of specific parameters (Fig. 3). The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the methodology and appreciate its implications.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution. While the problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models is not new (as acknowledged by the authors with citations to Bowers & Davis, Mamassian & Landy, etc.), the proposed solution is highly novel. Previous work has often pointed out the problem or used specific experimental tricks (like Bayesian transfer) to mitigate it on a case-by-case basis. This paper is the first to propose a systematic, general-purpose, and efficient framework to analyze identifiability *before* an experiment is run. The synthesis of a flexible parametric model with an analytical measure of distinguishability is a novel combination of techniques applied to a long-standing problem.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is potentially very high. The "Bayesian brain" hypothesis is a dominant paradigm in computational neuroscience and cognitive science, but it has faced valid criticism regarding model falsifiability and parameter degeneracy. This framework provides a concrete tool to address these criticisms head-on. It allows researchers to:
1.  Rigorously test whether their experimental design is capable of isolating the cognitive variable they claim to be measuring (e.g., the prior).
2.  Compare and optimize experimental designs "in silico" to maximize statistical power and minimize ambiguity.
3.  Re-evaluate the robustness of conclusions from past studies, as demonstrated in the speed perception case study.
If adopted, this approach could significantly improve the rigor and reproducibility of research that uses Bayesian observer models. It has the potential to become a standard tool in the methods toolkit of psychophysicists and computational modelers.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scope of Applicability:** The current framework is tailored to 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this is a common paradigm, a large portion of psychophysical research relies on discrete choice tasks, such as two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work, but the paper would be stronger if it briefly discussed the conceptual challenges or potential pathways to extending the framework to such tasks.

2.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer (`θ*`):** The entire analysis is conditioned on the choice of a "ground truth" reference observer, `θ*`. The conclusions about which experimental design is best are therefore dependent on the assumptions made about this observer. It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on the robustness of the findings. For example, how would the ranking of experimental designs in the time-perception task change if the true sensory noise (`p_meas`) had a different (e.g., heavier-tailed) form than assumed? While exploring this fully is beyond the scope of one paper, acknowledging this dependency more explicitly would be helpful.

3.  **Computational Cost:** While the KL-divergence approach is far more efficient than simulating datasets, the subsequent step of MCMC sampling from the approximate posterior to explore the landscape can still be computationally intensive. A brief mention of the typical computational resources required to run one of these analyses would add practical context for potential users.

**Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that addresses a critical methodological problem in a core area of NIPS research. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and highly original. The proposed framework is not merely a theoretical exercise but a practical tool that can be used to design better experiments and strengthen the inferences drawn from them. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to the scope of the current implementation and avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is a significant contribution to the field of computational cognitive science and is highly likely to be influential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem of non-identifiability in a principled way.
*   Presents a novel and computationally efficient method for *a priori* analysis of experimental designs.
*   The flexible observer model is a valuable contribution in itself.
*   The case studies demonstrate the framework's utility and produce non-obvious, important insights.
*   The work has high potential for impact, promoting more rigorous and reproducible research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The current framework is limited to 1D estimation tasks, which narrows its immediate applicability.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**
