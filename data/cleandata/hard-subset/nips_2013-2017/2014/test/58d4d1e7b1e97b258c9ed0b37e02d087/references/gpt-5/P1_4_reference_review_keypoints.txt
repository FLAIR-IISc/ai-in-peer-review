- Proposes a framework to study a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in 1D sensorimotor estimation tasks using a flexible parametric observer family.
- Observer class components: monotonic sensory mapping (log/power), unimodal mixture-of-Gaussians likelihood matched to moments, lattice Gaussian prior, inverted Gaussian-mixture loss, and lapses + additive motor/report noise.
- Key analytic result: expected posterior loss remains a tractable mixture, enabling fast evaluation of predicted response densities.
- Derives expected log-evidence of candidate θ given data from θ* as a stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences (up to constant).
- Defines an “approximate expected posterior” ∝ exp(expected log-evidence) and explores it via MCMC to map identifiability as a function of trial count and design.
- Case study 1 (timing): strong trade-offs between sensory and motor noise; loss width weakly identifiable; fixing motor noise improves prior recovery; near-MAP losses can worsen identifiability.
- Case study 2 (speed): reasonably good recovery of slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters across contrast conditions, even when internal/external noise are decoupled.
- Situates work within Bayesian decision theory and literature on model non-identifiability and perceptual Bayesian modeling.
- Notes known degeneracy under joint reweightings of prior/likelihood/loss and contributes by quantifying practical identifiability via expected-KL and a unified observer parameterization.
- Strength: clear, principled identifiability objective that avoids costly dataset resampling and aligns with information criteria.
- Strength: flexible, unified observer family with analytic/numerically efficient components.
- Strength: provides actionable design insights (e.g., fixing motor noise, multi-condition designs) and demonstrates both hard and easy identifiability scenarios.
- Strength: potential as a pre-registration/design tool for psychophysics experiments.
- Concern: conclusions depend on strong parameterizations (mapping choice, Gaussian mixtures, lattice prior, Gaussian motor noise) with limited sensitivity analyses in main text.
- Concern: limited scope to 1D estimation; 2AFC handled only via estimation surrogate which may affect identifiability conclusions.
- Concern: approximate expected posterior is not a true posterior—no parameter priors or model-complexity penalties explicitly incorporated, risking biased identifiability landscapes.
- Concern: experimental design optimization potential not fully explored despite relevance of Eq. 12.
- Concern: key derivations and implementation details (Eq. 12, moment-to-mixture mapping, computational complexity, MCMC diagnostics) relegated to supplement.
- Concern: incomplete/malformed references reduce clarity and traceability.
- Quality: technically sound derivations and reasonable modeling choices, but some claims (e.g., theoretical validation of slow-speed prior) should be tempered given assumptions.
- Clarity: generally clear and structured but dense notation, inconsistent terms, and reliance on supplement for important details.
- Originality: moderate but meaningful—integrates classical expected-KL with a tractable observer class for proactive design comparison.
- Significance: useful for experimentalists modeling perception under BDT; broader impact requires extension to higher dimensions and discrete choices.
- Suggestions: connect to Bayesian optimal experimental design, report computational/MCMC details, perform ablations on parameterizations, clarify moment-to-mixture fitting, validate 2AFC surrogate, add local identifiability analyses, and release code.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses practical identifiability with a principled, implementable approach and offers concrete, nontrivial design guidance.
- Cons for acceptance: limited to 1D estimation, sensitivity to modeling choices, and missing methodological/bibliographic details.
- Recommendation: borderline accept conditional on improved exposition, sensitivity analyses, clearer links to optimal design, and supplemental details.
