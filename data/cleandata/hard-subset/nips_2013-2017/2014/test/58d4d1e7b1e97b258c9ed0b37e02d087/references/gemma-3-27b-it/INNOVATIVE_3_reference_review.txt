This paper tackles a crucial, often overlooked, issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: identifiability. While the Bayesian framework is increasingly used as a process model, the inherent non-uniqueness of inverting behavior to recover internal representations is a significant concern. The authors present a framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis, allowing researchers to evaluate experimental designs *before* data collection. This is a bold and valuable contribution. The approach leverages a parameterized observer model encompassing priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and lapses, coupled with a clever method based on KL-divergence to assess the practical consequences of non-identifiability.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. The identification of the degeneracy problem is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The proposed framework is genuinely novel – existing approaches typically address identifiability *post hoc*, through regularization or constraints. This *a priori* approach is a significant shift in thinking. The parameterized observer model is reasonably general, capable of capturing a wide range of psychophysical phenomena. The application to time and speed perception provides concrete demonstrations of the framework’s utility, revealing interesting insights into experimental design (e.g., the potentially detrimental effects of imposing a narrow loss function).  The technical details, while dense, appear sound and are supported by supplementary material. The authors demonstrate awareness of related work in psychology and statistics regarding model identifiability.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. The complexity of the model and the resulting analysis make it difficult to fully grasp without significant effort. While the authors strive for generality, the assumptions made (e.g., specific functional forms for the priors, likelihoods, and loss functions) inevitably limit the scope. The validation is largely theoretical – applying the framework to existing paradigms and *simulated* data. While this is a reasonable starting point, empirical validation with real behavioral data is crucial. The discussion of the results, particularly for the time perception example, could be more nuanced; the authors attribute the poor reconstruction performance largely to non-identifiability, but other factors (e.g., model misspecification) cannot be ruled out. There’s a relative lack of discussion around the computational cost of applying this framework - how scalable is it to more complex models and datasets? Finally, the connection to broader discussions of Bayesian modeling in perception, and the implications for interpreting recovered representations, could be more strongly emphasized.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The *a priori* identifiability analysis framework is genuinely novel and addresses a critical issue in the field.
*   **Significance:** The framework has the potential to significantly improve experimental design and increase confidence in recovered internal representations.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model and analysis appear technically sound and well-motivated.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work could shift the mindset of researchers to proactively address identifiability concerns.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The paper is technically dense and may be inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The current validation relies heavily on simulations and existing paradigms.
*   **Assumptions:** The model relies on several assumptions that may limit its generality.
*   **Computational Cost:**  The scalability of the framework isn't clearly addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty and potential significance of this work outweigh the current limitations. The paper presents a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception, and the insights gained from applying this framework are likely to be substantial.  While empirical validation and further refinement are needed, this paper represents a significant step forward in addressing the challenges of model identifiability and interpreting recovered internal representations. The paper fulfills the NIPS criteria, particularly regarding originality and potential significance, even if clarity could be improved. I encourage the authors to consider addressing the scalability issue and providing more detailed guidance on applying the framework in future work.
