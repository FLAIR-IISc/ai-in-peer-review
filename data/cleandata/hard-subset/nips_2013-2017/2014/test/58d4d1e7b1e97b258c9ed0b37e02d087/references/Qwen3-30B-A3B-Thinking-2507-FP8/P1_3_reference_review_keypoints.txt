- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models and proposes an a priori identifiability testing framework using KL divergence to quantify expected parameter support.  
- Defines a flexible class of Bayesian observer models applicable to many psychophysical tasks and demonstrates the framework on time-interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Technically sound with a rigorous derivation (Eq. 12) and computationally efficient, making the method practical for researchers.  
- Novel contribution: first systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing in Bayesian perception modeling.  
- Significant methodological impact: can improve experimental design and prevent misleading inferences about priors, likelihoods, and loss functions.  
- Clear organization and writing; the four-stage observer model (sensation, perceptual, decision-making, response) aids conceptual clarity and figures effectively illustrate results.  
- Weakness: unclear how the framework scales to more complex models or designs with multiple interacting factors (e.g., multiple sensory cues, complex loss functions).  
- Weakness: case studies are limited to two perceptual tasks, reducing immediate evidence for broad applicability across domains.  
- Weakness: insufficient treatment of model misspecification — how to proceed if the true model lies outside the assumed model class.  
- Weakness: lacks explicit comparison with alternative identifiability methods (e.g., Fisher information, Monte Carlo parameter-recovery).  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Revision request 1: expand discussion of limitations and computational constraints, especially scalability to complex models.  
- Revision request 2: include a brief comparison with alternative identifiability analyses (Fisher information, Monte Carlo recovery).  
- Revision request 3: add discussion of how the framework handles model misspecification.  
- Revision request 4: consider adding a third case study or expanding existing ones to demonstrate broader applicability.  
- Pro summary: addresses a fundamental problem, is theoretically sound and efficient, demonstrated via case studies, well-written, and likely to improve reliability of studies.  
- Con summary: potential computational limits, narrow case-study scope, limited discussion of misspecification, and insufficient positioning relative to alternative methods.
