

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical methodological challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of these model components can produce identical behavioral outputs, making parameter recovery unreliable without appropriate constraints. The paper proposes a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis that allows researchers to evaluate experimental designs before conducting studies. The authors define a flexible class of Bayesian observer models that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks, then develop a method using KL divergence to quantify expected support for different parameter values given data generated by a reference model. They apply this framework to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception, demonstrating how it can inform experimental design to improve parameter recovery.

This work builds on foundational contributions to Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 1996; Knill, 2005; Maloney & Mamassian, 2002) while addressing a gap in systematic identifiability analysis that has been acknowledged but not rigorously examined (Mamassian, 2010; Bowers & Davis, 2012). It extends previous work on model identifiability in psychology (Navarro et al., 2004) to the specific context of Bayesian models of perception.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical foundations. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of their identifiability metric (Eq. 12) and demonstrate its application in two well-chosen case studies. The framework is both comprehensive and computationally efficient, making it practical for researchers. The case studies effectively illustrate the framework's utility, showing how it can reveal non-intuitive design trade-offs (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function may worsen prior recovery).

**Originality:** This is a novel contribution to the field. While non-identifiability has been recognized as a problem in Bayesian modeling of perception (Mamassian, 2010; Bowers & Davis, 2012), this paper provides the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing. The authors' flexible model class (Eqs. 2-8) that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks while maintaining computational efficiency is particularly impressive. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work by focusing on the experimental design aspect of the identifiability problem.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental methodological issue that affects the validity of many studies in computational neuroscience and psychophysics. By providing a tool to evaluate experimental designs before data collection, the framework has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of Bayesian model fitting in the field. The case studies demonstrate concrete applications where the framework could prevent misleading conclusions (e.g., questioning claims about loss functions in interval timing).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The four-stage construction of the observer model (sensation, perceptual, decision-making, response stages) provides an excellent conceptual framework. The case studies are presented with sufficient detail to understand the application of the framework. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Scope:** While the authors demonstrate the framework's utility, they don't fully address how it would scale to more complex models or experimental designs with multiple interacting factors. For instance, how would the framework handle designs with multiple sensory cues or more intricate loss functions?

**Case Study Limitations:** The two case studies, while well-chosen, are somewhat limited in scope. Additional examples from other domains of perception (e.g., spatial perception, auditory perception) would strengthen the argument for the framework's broad applicability.

**Model Specification:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss how the framework would handle cases where the true model isn't within the assumed model class. This is a common issue in model selection that could affect the framework's reliability.

**Comparison with Alternatives:** The authors could better position their framework against existing approaches to model identifiability (e.g., Fisher information analysis, Monte Carlo simulations of parameter recovery). While they reference Navarro et al. (2004), a more explicit comparison would strengthen the contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. This is a strong, methodologically important contribution that addresses a critical problem in the field. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution with significant potential impact on experimental design in computational neuroscience.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Expand the discussion of the framework's limitations and computational constraints, particularly regarding scalability to more complex models.

2. Add a brief comparison with alternative identifiability analysis approaches (e.g., Fisher information, Monte Carlo parameter recovery studies) to better position the paper's contribution.

3. Include a short discussion of how the framework would handle model misspecification (i.e., when the true model isn't within the assumed model class).

4. Consider adding a third case study or expanding the discussion of the two presented case studies to better demonstrate the framework's applicability across different perceptual domains.

## Summary of Pro and Con Arguments

**Pro-acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental methodological challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception
- Provides a theoretically sound, computationally efficient framework for a priori identifiability testing
- Case studies clearly demonstrate practical utility for experimental design
- Well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field
- Potential to improve the reliability of many existing studies in computational neuroscience

**Con-acceptance:**
- Framework's computational requirements may limit adoption for very complex models
- Case studies are limited to two specific perceptual tasks
- Limited discussion of how framework handles model misspecification
- Could better position against alternative identifiability analysis methods

This paper is a valuable contribution to the methodological toolkit of researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. It provides a clear path forward for addressing a fundamental issue that has the potential to undermine many existing studies. With minor revisions to address the suggested points, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.
