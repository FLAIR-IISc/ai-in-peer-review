- Paper demonstrates non-identifiability: multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral data.
- Proposes a framework to assess identifiability a priori using a flexible Bayesian observer class and metrics (KL divergence, expected log likelihood).
- Validated framework on two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception, showing how design choices affect parameter recovery.
- Strength: technically rigorous with solid mathematical derivations (notably Eq. 12) and well-executed case studies.
- Strength: original — first systematic identifiability framework targeted at Bayesian perceptual models, distinct from prior instance-specific work.
- Strength: high practical significance — helps prevent uninformative experiments and reveals counterintuitive design effects (e.g., narrow loss can hurt prior recovery).
- Strength: clear organization and explanations; figures effectively illustrate results; minor omission in MCMC implementation detail.
- Weakness: limited scope — currently restricted to continuous one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks.
- Weakness: computational concerns — MCMC-based exploration may not scale well to high-dimensional or complex models.
- Weakness: sensitivity to choice of reference observer (θ*) and limited analysis of robustness to that choice.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant methodological contribution with practical utility, despite scope and computational limits.
- Recommendation: Accept.
