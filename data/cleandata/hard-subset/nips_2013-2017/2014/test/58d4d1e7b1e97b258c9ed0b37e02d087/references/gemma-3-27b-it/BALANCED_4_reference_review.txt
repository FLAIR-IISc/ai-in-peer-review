This paper addresses the crucial issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing area of research aiming to link behavior to underlying cognitive mechanisms. The authors rightly point out the degeneracy inherent in inverting behavioral data to recover priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, and propose a framework to *a priori* assess the identifiability of these components for a specific observer model. The core contribution lies in providing a method to explore the parameter space and quantify the overlap between different parameter settings that yield the same behavioral predictions. The framework is well-motivated, grounded in theoretical considerations, and demonstrated through two compelling case studies – time and speed perception.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The problem of non-identifiability is a fundamental limitation of Bayesian modeling, and this work directly tackles it. Addressing this issue is crucial for the reliability and interpretability of inferences drawn from these models.
* **Comprehensive Framework:** The authors present a well-defined and flexible observer model encompassing a wide range of psychophysical tasks, built on a continuous parametrization. This allows for systematic exploration of the parameter space.
* **Practical Methodology:** The proposed method for mapping *a priori* identifiability, utilizing KL-divergence and MCMC sampling, offers a concrete and potentially valuable tool for researchers.
* **Illustrative Case Studies:** The application to time and speed perception provides convincing demonstrations of the framework’s utility. The results are insightful, highlighting the conditions under which identifiability is compromised and offering guidance for experimental design. For example, the finding regarding the detrimental effect of a narrowly defined loss function in the time interval estimation task is particularly noteworthy.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical derivations are reasonably clear, and the figures are informative.
* **Connections to Literature:** The authors appropriately cite relevant work in Bayesian modeling, perception, and identifiability analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the framework is presented as efficient, exploring the high-dimensional parameter space via MCMC can still be computationally demanding, particularly for complex models or large datasets. The paper doesn’t fully address the practical limitations of scaling this approach.
* **Model Assumptions:** The proposed observer model, while flexible, relies on several assumptions (e.g., Gaussian distributions, specific functional forms for priors and likelihoods). The sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to these assumptions is not extensively explored.  For instance, the choice of a two-component Gaussian mixture for sensory likelihoods might not be appropriate for all scenarios.
* **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** The two case studies, while illustrative, focus on relatively well-studied phenomena. Applying the framework to more complex or less understood perceptual tasks would further demonstrate its generality and robustness.
* **Interpretation of KL-Divergence:**  The use of KL-divergence as a proxy for identifiability is reasonable, but its interpretation requires careful consideration.  KL-divergence is asymmetric, and the choice of reference model (θ*) can influence the results.  The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion on the limitations of this approach would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Identifiability Methods:** The paper primarily focuses on its proposed framework without explicitly comparing it to existing techniques for assessing model identifiability (e.g., profile likelihood analysis, bootstrapping). A comparative analysis would strengthen the paper’s contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and increasingly important challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* The proposed framework provides a novel and practical approach to assess *a priori* identifiability.
* The case studies demonstrate the utility of the framework and offer insightful guidance for experimental design.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and grounded in theoretical principles.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the method could limit its applicability to complex models.
* The framework relies on several assumptions that may not hold for all perceptual tasks.
* The case studies, while illustrative, are somewhat limited in scope.
* The paper does not comprehensively compare its approach to existing identifiability methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The proposed framework offers a valuable tool for researchers aiming to draw reliable inferences about underlying cognitive mechanisms. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance with minor revisions. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Computational Considerations:**  Discuss the computational challenges associated with scaling the method to more complex models and explore potential optimizations.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:**  Investigate the sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to key model assumptions (e.g., the choice of distribution families).
* **Broader Application:** Apply the framework to a wider range of perceptual tasks to demonstrate its generality and robustness.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:**  Include a brief discussion of how the proposed framework compares to existing techniques for assessing model identifiability.
* **Clarify KL-Divergence:** Expand on the limitations and potential biases associated with using KL-divergence as a measure of identifiability.
