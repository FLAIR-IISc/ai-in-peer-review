- Tackles non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models and proposes an a priori testing framework.  
- Uses Kullback-Leibler divergence and MCMC sampling to quantify recoverability of true parameters versus behaviorally-equivalent alternatives.  
- Defines a flexible class of observer models covering many sensory estimation tasks (varied priors, likelihoods, loss functions).  
- Demonstrates framework on time and speed perception tasks, yielding insights for experimental design.  
- Clear, thorough mathematical formulation and comprehensive supplementary material.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian perception literature and addresses longstanding identifiability concerns with a computational, pre-data approach.  
- KL divergence choice aligns with established Bayesian model comparison methods.  
- Novelty: provides a practical tool for assessing identifiability before data collection, extending beyond purely theoretical analyses.  
- Strength: important, fundamental problem with potential to improve inference validity in perceptual modeling.  
- Strength: generality and flexibility of model class increases applicability across tasks.  
- Weakness: computational cost of MCMC exploration may limit applicability to high-dimensional or complex models.  
- Weakness: relies on specific model assumptions (e.g., parametric sensory map, Gaussian mixtures) that may not generalize.  
- Weakness: identifiability results depend on choice of reference observer (θ*), requiring sensitivity checks.  
- Weakness: case studies limited to well-understood tasks; applicability to multi-cue or ambiguous stimuli not demonstrated.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion connecting identifiability results to interpretation of neural data and neural representations.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—address computational limitations, sensitivity to model assumptions and reference observer choice, and discuss neural implications.  
- Overall judgment: strong contribution, recommend acceptance (rating 8/10).
