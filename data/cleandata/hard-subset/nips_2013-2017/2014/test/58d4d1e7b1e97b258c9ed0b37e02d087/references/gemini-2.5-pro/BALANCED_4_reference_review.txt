This paper addresses the critical issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that since multiple combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavior, recovering these representations from data is an ill-posed inverse problem. The core contribution is a novel computational framework to analyze the identifiability of a given Bayesian observer model *a priori*, before an experiment is run. This allows a researcher to assess how reliably specific model parameters can be recovered and to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs.

The framework consists of two main components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model systematically parameterizes the sensation, perception, decision, and response stages, using mixtures of Gaussians to represent the observer's prior, likelihood, and loss function, allowing for a wide range of assumptions to be captured. Second, they propose a principled metric to quantify the distinguishability between a "ground truth" reference observer and other candidate observers. This metric is based on the expected log-likelihood over datasets generated by the reference observer, which elegantly simplifies to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the models' response distributions. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" derived from this metric, the framework can map out the "identifiability landscape" for the parameters of interest.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies. In a time interval estimation task, they show that a standard experimental design may be insufficient to reliably recover the observer's prior due to a degeneracy between sensory and motor noise. Their analysis reveals how different design choices, such as independently measuring motor noise, can break this degeneracy. Counter-intuitively, they also find that a design encouraging a MAP-like loss function could worsen prior recovery. In a second case study on speed perception, they provide theoretical validation for the classic finding of a "slow-speed prior," showing that the key parameters of this model are robustly identifiable.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and often-understated problem in computational modeling of cognition. As Bayesian models become more complex, the risk of non-identifiability grows, potentially undermining the scientific conclusions drawn from them. This work provides a much-needed, practical tool for researchers to proactively address this issue, leading to better experimental design and more robust inference.

2.  **Originality and Quality:** The proposed framework is novel, principled, and technically sound. While the problem of identifiability is well-known, this is the first work to our knowledge that provides a systematic, computational method for *a priori* analysis in the context of Bayesian perception models. The construction of the flexible observer model is clever, and the use of the KL divergence as a measure of distinguishability is well-justified. The case studies are insightful and convincingly demonstrate the framework's power.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented logically. Figure 1 provides an excellent conceptual overview of the model structure.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The conclusions of the analysis are conditional on the choice of the "reference observer" θ*. For example, the finding that a narrow loss function harms prior recovery is a powerful claim, but it's shown for one specific θ*. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a robustness analysis demonstrating that this conclusion holds for a reasonable range of plausible reference observers. Even a small-scale analysis showing this for a few key parameters of θ* would add considerable weight to the findings.

2.  **Model Complexity:** The proposed observer model is highly flexible, which is a strength, but also very complex, with over 20 free parameters (Eq. 10). This raises the question of whether the framework's own analysis is well-posed. A brief discussion of the trade-offs of this complexity would be beneficial. For instance, are the results sensitive to the choice of parameterization (e.g., mixtures of *two* Gaussians, M=15 for the prior)? While exhaustive testing is not feasible, acknowledging these choices and their potential impact would be valuable.

3.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently limited to 1D continuous estimation tasks. Many foundational studies in this area use discrete-choice paradigms like two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors suggest their framework can be applied by converting a 2AFC task into an "equivalent estimation task," but this is a non-trivial step that warrants more careful justification. The decision processes, noise sources, and potential biases can differ significantly between these paradigms, which could have major implications for an identifiability analysis. This limitation on scope should be more clearly stated, and the proposed extension to 2AFC tasks should be treated with more caution.

4.  **Contextualization with Other Methods:** The paper mentions that generic identifiability analysis techniques exist but argues its method is more efficient. It would be helpful to make this comparison more concrete. A brief discussion of how the proposed method relates to or improves upon other approaches (e.g., those based on the Fisher Information Matrix or simulation/resampling methods) would help readers better situate the contribution within the broader literature on model identifiability.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a critical, fundamental problem in a major subfield of computational neuroscience and cognitive science.
    *   Provides a novel and practical computational tool for improving experimental design and model-based inference.
    *   The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated.
    *   The case studies provide insightful and non-obvious results, demonstrating the framework's value.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The conclusions are conditional on a single, assumed "reference observer" without a robustness analysis.
    *   The scope is currently limited to estimation tasks, and the proposed extension to other paradigms needs more support.

**Decision:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the scope of the claims and could be addressed through discussion and modest additional analysis. The framework presented here has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers designing and interpreting experiments through the lens of Bayesian observer models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
