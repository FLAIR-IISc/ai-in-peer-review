

# Peer Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors correctly identify that the inverse mapping from observed behavior to model components is degenerate, as multiple combinations of model parameters can produce identical behavior. While this issue has been noted in the literature (e.g., [19, 22]), the authors present the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis. Their work builds on previous Bayesian perception models [7, 9, 12] but extends them by providing a method to assess whether model parameters can be reliably recovered before conducting experiments. This is a significant contribution that bridges theoretical concerns with practical experimental design, addressing a gap that has been acknowledged but not systematically tackled in the field.

## Strengths

The paper's primary strength lies in its technical rigor and practical utility. The authors develop a flexible yet computationally efficient observer model that can represent a wide range of sensory estimation tasks while enabling systematic identifiability analysis. Their method for quantifying identifiability using expected log likelihood (Equation 12) is theoretically sound and computationally feasible, making it practical for researchers. 

The two case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility: in temporal interval estimation, they reveal that experimental designs that force observers to adopt a "narrower" loss function (closer to MAP) actually worsen prior recovery, a non-intuitive finding that would be difficult to anticipate without their framework. In speed perception, they validate the robustness of prior recovery, aligning with previous findings while providing theoretical justification. This demonstrates how the framework can guide experimental design decisions, moving beyond the ad hoc approaches commonly used in the field.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. The mathematical derivations are thorough, and the case studies are presented with sufficient detail to allow replication. The authors also thoughtfully acknowledge limitations, such as the assumption that the reference model is correctly specified.

## Weaknesses

The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical validation with real behavioral data. While the case studies use simulated data based on reference models, the framework's practical utility would be stronger if validated with actual human or animal behavior data. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't address how their framework would perform with noisy, real-world data where the reference model may be misspecified.

The paper also underutilizes related work on model identifiability in other fields. The framework's approach to identifiability analysis bears similarities to techniques in statistics and machine learning (e.g., [23]), but the authors don't adequately contextualize their work within this broader literature. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

Another limitation is the scope of the framework. While the authors mention potential extensions to discrete-choice paradigms and multiple cues, they don't demonstrate how their framework would handle these more complex scenarios. The paper focuses on estimation tasks, but many perception studies use discrete-choice paradigms (e.g., 2AFC), where the identifiability structure may differ significantly.

## Significance and Impact

This work addresses a critical issue that has plagued Bayesian models of perception for decades. By providing a systematic method to evaluate identifiability before conducting experiments, the framework has the potential to improve the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian modeling in perception research. The case studies demonstrate that the framework can identify suboptimal experimental designs and guide improvements in experimental setup, which could prevent wasted resources and misinterpretations of data.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by moving beyond the common practice of "fitting models and hoping for the best" to a more principled approach to experimental design. This is particularly valuable given the increasing popularity of Bayesian modeling in perception research.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong technical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. The framework is well-developed, theoretically sound, and has clear practical implications for experimental design. While the lack of empirical validation with real data is a limitation, the paper's theoretical contributions and practical utility for guiding experimental design are substantial.

I recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), provides a novel framework for a well-known problem (Originality), and addresses a significant methodological challenge with clear implications for the field (Significance). The framework has the potential to become a standard tool in the Bayesian perception modeling toolkit, helping researchers design experiments that can reliably recover internal representations.

The main suggestions for improvement would be to: 1) provide more discussion of how the framework might perform with real data where the reference model is misspecified, and 2) better contextualize the work within the broader literature on model identifiability. These would strengthen an already strong paper.
