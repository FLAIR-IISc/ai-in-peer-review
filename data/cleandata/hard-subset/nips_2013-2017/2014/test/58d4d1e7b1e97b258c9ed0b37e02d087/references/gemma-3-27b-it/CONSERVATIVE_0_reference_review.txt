This paper attempts to provide a framework for assessing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The core idea – that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can lead to identical behavior, creating a degeneracy problem – is well-established (citations [17, 18, 19] are appropriate). The authors aim to offer a method for *a priori* evaluation of identifiability, and to use this to improve experimental design. They introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model and a method based on KL divergence to quantify the risk of non-identifiability. They apply this framework to two scenarios: time perception and speed perception.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical formulations are generally accessible, though the density of equations requires careful reading. The extension of existing models to a unified parametrization is a positive step towards practical application. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the problem identified is valid, the proposed solution feels more like a restatement of the problem with a computationally intensive method for quantifying something already known, rather than a truly novel and impactful contribution to NIPS.

**Strengths:**

*   **Identifies a critical issue:** The non-identifiability of Bayesian models is a fundamental concern, and highlighting the need for careful consideration of this issue is valuable.
*   **Unified Model:** The proposed Bayesian observer model appears flexible and attempts to encompass a broad range of scenarios.
*   **KL Divergence Approach:** The use of KL divergence to quantify the support for different parameter combinations is a reasonable, albeit standard, approach.
*   **Case Studies:** The application to time and speed perception provides concrete examples, although the results are not entirely surprising.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core idea isn't new. The paper largely repackages known limitations of Bayesian modeling. The proposed method doesn’t offer a fundamentally new way to *resolve* identifiability issues, merely a way to quantify them *a priori*.
*   **Computational Cost:** The method, relying on MCMC sampling of the approximate posterior, is computationally expensive. This limits its practical applicability, particularly for complex models. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational limitations or explore potential approximations.
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The framework relies on a number of strong assumptions about the form of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. While the authors attempt to justify these, they significantly restrict the generality of the approach. The sensitivity of results to these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Validation:** The case studies, while illustrative, lack rigorous validation. The authors compare their framework to existing work but don't demonstrate a clear advantage or new insight gained. The impact of the proposed methodology on experimental design is asserted rather than demonstrated with compelling results.
*   **Unclear Practical Guidance:** While the paper identifies potential issues with experimental designs (e.g., the negative impact of a narrow loss function in the time perception example), the practical guidance for researchers is limited. It's unclear how researchers should use this framework to optimize their experimental designs beyond simply recognizing the potential for degeneracy.
*   **Overly Idealized:** The model, while flexible, remains highly idealized. Real-world perceptual processes are likely more complex and less constrained than those assumed in the framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   The framework provides a systematic way to quantify identifiability.
*   The case studies illustrate the application of the framework.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty is limited, primarily a quantification of a known problem.
*   The computational cost is high, limiting practical applicability.
*   The framework relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in real-world scenarios.
*   The practical guidance for researchers is limited.
*   The paper doesn't demonstrate a clear advance over existing methods.



**Overall:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the authors correctly identify an important issue, the proposed solution is not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The framework feels like a complex way to state a well-known problem, without offering a practical solution or demonstrating a significant advantage over existing approaches. The computational burden and strong assumptions further limit its usefulness. The paper lacks the originality and significance expected of a NIPS contribution. I suggest the authors focus on reducing the computational complexity of the method and demonstrating its practical value through more rigorous validation and real-world applications. They should also explore the sensitivity of their results to the underlying assumptions and provide more concrete guidance for experimental design.
