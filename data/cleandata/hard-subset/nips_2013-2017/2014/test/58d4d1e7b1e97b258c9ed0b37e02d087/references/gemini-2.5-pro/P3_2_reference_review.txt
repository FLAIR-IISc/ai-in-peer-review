### Summary

This paper presents a novel and much-needed computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors correctly identify a fundamental weakness in the application of Bayesian models as process models: the degeneracy between internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function), which makes it difficult to uniquely recover these components from behavioral data. To address this, they propose a two-part solution. First, they develop a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians to flexibly represent priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. Second, they introduce an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, given data generated by a known "reference" model. This expectation is elegantly shown to be a function of the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the models' response distributions, avoiding the need for costly data simulation. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two well-chosen case studies from the literature: interval timing and speed perception. They show how their framework can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs and to provide theoretical validation for previous findings about observers' internal representations.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a critical and often-overlooked issue in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. As Bayesian models move from "as if" descriptive tools to "process models" from which we claim to recover latent cognitive constructs, the question of whether this recovery is unique and reliable becomes paramount. This paper tackles this "elephant in the room" head-on, providing a principled and practical tool for researchers. This work is highly significant for the field and aligns well with NIPS's interest in the foundations of machine learning and its application to understanding intelligence.

2.  **Methodological Novelty and Soundness:** The core technical contribution is the combination of a flexible observer model with an efficient, KL-divergence-based metric for identifiability. The parameterization of the prior, likelihood, and loss function as mixtures of Gaussians is a clever engineering choice that balances expressiveness with the need for analytic tractability (e.g., in calculating the expected loss). The derivation of the expected log-likelihood in terms of KL divergence (Eq. 12) is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach, making the exploration of the identifiability landscape computationally feasible.

3.  **Practical Utility and Insightful Case Studies:** The paper is not merely a theoretical exercise; it provides a framework that can be directly used by experimentalists to design better experiments. The case studies are excellent demonstrations. The analysis of the interval timing task yields a non-obvious and important insight: an experimental design that forces a narrower (MAP-like) loss function might, counter-intuitively, *worsen* the ability to recover the observer's prior. This highlights a subtle trade-off that would be difficult to intuit without such a framework. Conversely, the validation of the recoverability of the slow-speed prior in the second case study strengthens confidence in those seminal findings.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, the model is built up in a step-by-step manner, and the results are presented and interpreted cogently. The authors do a good job of explaining complex concepts in an accessible way.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **The Reference Observer Assumption:** The entire analysis is conditioned on a "reference observer" θ*, which is assumed to be the ground truth. The paper suggests this can be chosen based on "pilot experiments, the literature, or educated guesses." However, the conclusions about which experimental design is best might be sensitive to this choice. For instance, would the MTR design still be superior if the true observer had a highly non-Gaussian motor noise profile? A discussion on the robustness of the framework's conclusions to the specific choice of θ* would significantly strengthen the paper. Perhaps a brief analysis showing that the ranking of experimental designs is stable across a plausible range of reference observers would be beneficial.

2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently developed for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this is a common paradigm, a large portion of psychophysics research relies on discrete choice tasks, particularly two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors briefly mention extending the framework to 2AFC in the conclusion, but the current limitation is significant. The conversion of the Stocker & Simoncelli (2006) 2AFC study into an "equivalent estimation task" is asserted rather than explained, which feels like a gloss over a non-trivial issue. Providing more detail on this conversion, and discussing the challenges of a direct extension to discrete choice models, would be valuable.

3.  **Potential for Hidden Degeneracies:** The proposed observer model is highly flexible, with a large number of parameters (Eq. 10). While the case studies reveal some key degeneracies (e.g., sensory vs. motor noise), there may be others lurking. For example, can the skewness of the likelihood (γ̃) be traded against the skewness of the loss function (γ`)? The framework has the power to explore these higher-order interactions, but the current analysis focuses on a few key parameters. A more comprehensive visualization of the covariance structure of the approximate posterior `E(θ|θ*)` could reveal more about the complex trade-offs inherent in this flexible model class.

4.  **Clarity on Computational Cost:** While the KL-divergence approach is lauded as "efficient," no details are provided about the actual computational cost. How long does it take to run the MCMC sampling to map the identifiability landscape for a given experimental design? Providing some sense of the runtime would be helpful for other researchers who might wish to adopt this methodology for their own experimental design process.

### Overall Assessment

This is a strong, original, and significant contribution. The paper addresses a foundational problem in a methodologically sound and innovative way. It provides both a theoretical framework and a practical tool that could have a substantial positive impact on the rigor of research using Bayesian observer models. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations in scope or areas where the discussion could be deepened, rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The work is a perfect fit for the NIPS community, bridging theory, methodology, and scientific application. I strongly recommend acceptance.
