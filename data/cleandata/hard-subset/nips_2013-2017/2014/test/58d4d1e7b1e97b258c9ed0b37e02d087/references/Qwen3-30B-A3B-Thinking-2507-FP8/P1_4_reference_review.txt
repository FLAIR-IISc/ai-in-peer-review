

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs, making it impossible to recover internal representations from data without appropriate constraints. They propose a systematic framework to assess identifiability *a priori* before conducting experiments, which allows researchers to evaluate how different experimental designs affect the ability to recover model parameters. The framework builds on previous work by Maloney and Mamassian (2009) and Stocker and Simoncelli (2006), but provides a novel method for systematically analyzing identifiability across the parameter space. The paper introduces a flexible Bayesian observer model that covers a wide range of perceptual tasks and demonstrates the framework's utility through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a critical issue in the field that has been noted in the literature (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) but has lacked systematic analysis. The authors clearly articulate why non-identifiability is a problem for the field's ability to draw valid conclusions about internal representations.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The framework is well-founded in information theory, using KL divergence as a principled measure of identifiability. The derivation of the expected log likelihood (Equation 12) is mathematically rigorous and provides a clear theoretical foundation for the approach.

3. **Practical Utility**: The case studies demonstrate concrete applications of the framework to real experimental designs. The findings about the impact of experimental constraints (e.g., measuring motor parameters) provide actionable guidance for experimental design that could improve reproducibility in the field.

4. **Model Flexibility**: The proposed observer model is sufficiently general to cover a wide range of perceptual tasks while maintaining computational tractability, which is crucial for exploring the complex parameter space.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the theoretical framework, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Validation**: While the case studies are compelling, the framework's generalizability across different experimental paradigms remains untested. The paper focuses on estimation tasks but doesn't address how the framework would apply to choice-based paradigms like 2AFC, which are common in perception research.

2. **Computational Cost**: The approach requires MCMC sampling from the approximate posterior density, which could be prohibitively expensive for more complex models or larger parameter spaces. The paper doesn't provide guidance on computational trade-offs or approximations that could make the method more scalable.

3. **Assumption Sensitivity**: The framework's results are sensitive to the assumed reference observer (θ*), as noted in Section 4.1. The authors don't systematically explore how varying these assumptions affects the identifiability conclusions, which limits the framework's robustness.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Methods**: While the paper references prior work on model identifiability (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), it doesn't directly compare its approach to existing methods for assessing identifiability in Bayesian models. A brief comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Practical Implementation Guidance**: The paper provides theoretical insights but offers limited practical guidance on how to implement this framework in standard experimental practice. For instance, how many trials would be needed for reliable identifiability assessment in a real-world experiment?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic method for addressing the critical issue of parameter non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The framework is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrated through relevant case studies. While the computational cost and limited scope of validation are concerns, these are common challenges in methodological papers and do not undermine the paper's core contribution.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a fundamental problem that could undermine many existing studies in the field. By providing a tool to evaluate identifiability *before* conducting experiments, the framework has the potential to improve the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian modeling of perception. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by offering a practical, computationally efficient approach to a problem that has previously lacked systematic analysis.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field with a well-executed methodological approach. The framework provides a valuable tool for researchers to improve experimental design and interpretation of Bayesian models of perception. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility, and the paper is written clearly and professionally.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider extending the framework to handle choice-based paradigms (e.g., 2AFC), which are common in perception research but not covered in the current analysis.

2. Include a brief comparison to alternative methods for assessing identifiability, highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach.

3. Discuss the computational requirements more explicitly, including potential approximations that could make the method more scalable for larger models.

4. Address how the framework might be applied to handle partially identifiable parameters, which is likely to be common in practice.

5. In the case studies, more explicitly discuss the implications of the findings for existing literature (e.g., the surprising finding that a narrow loss function worsens prior recovery).

This paper represents a significant step toward improving the rigor and reliability of Bayesian modeling in perception research, and I believe it would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
