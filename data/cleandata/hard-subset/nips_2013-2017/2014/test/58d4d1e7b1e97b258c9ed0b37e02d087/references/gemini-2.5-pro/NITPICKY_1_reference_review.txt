### **Summary**

This paper introduces a framework for assessing, *a priori*, the practical identifiability of parameters in Bayesian models of perception. The authors' contribution is twofold. First, they develop a flexible class of one-dimensional Bayesian observer models that aims to be both broadly applicable and computationally tractable. This model parameterizes the key components of Bayesian Decision Theory—sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss function—using flexible functional forms like mixtures of Gaussians. Second, they propose a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, averaged over datasets generated by a known "ground truth" reference model. This metric, equivalent to a Kullback-Leibler divergence, is used to map an "identifiability landscape" via MCMC sampling. The authors demonstrate their framework with two case studies: (1) recovering priors in a time interval estimation task, where they compare different experimental designs, and (2) re-examining the inference of a slow-speed prior in visual perception, largely validating previous findings. The overarching goal is to provide a tool for researchers to optimize experimental designs and understand potential model degeneracies before collecting data.

### **Strengths**

*   **Addresses a critical problem:** Model non-identifiability is a pervasive and often-underappreciated issue in computational modeling, particularly for complex Bayesian observer models where priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can trade off against each other. The paper’s focus on providing a systematic, *a priori* tool to tackle this problem is both timely and significant.
*   **Novel and principled framework:** The core idea of using the expected KL divergence to map out an identifiability landscape is elegant and well-founded. It provides a practical methodology for exploring how well an experimental design can constrain model parameters, moving beyond post-hoc identifiability analyses.
*   **Flexible and efficient model class:** The synthesis of a flexible sensory mapping function (approximating both Weber-Fechner and Stevens's laws) with mixture-of-Gaussians representations for the prior, likelihood, and loss function is a strong engineering contribution. The fact that this complex model still permits fast computation is crucial for the feasibility of the proposed MCMC-based exploration.
*   **Illustrative case studies:** The applications provide compelling demonstrations. The time perception example shows how the framework can generate non-obvious predictions (e.g., that a MAP-like loss function might hinder prior recovery) and rank experimental designs. The speed perception example serves as a useful validation, providing theoretical support for a well-established empirical finding.

### **Weaknesses**

The paper, while built on a strong premise, suffers from a lack of rigor and precision in its execution. The accumulation of minor-to-moderate flaws detracts significantly from its quality and calls into question the reliability of its conclusions.

**1. Lack of Technical Precision and Clarity:**

*   **Inconsistent Notation:** In the introduction's degeneracy argument, the loss function is written as `L(s, ŝ)` in the text of Eq. 1, but as `L(ŝ, s)` in the subsequent description of the degenerate triplet. This is a small but critical error in a foundational part of the argument.
*   **Variable Mismatch:** In Eq. (8), the response variance `σ²report` is defined as a function of the estimate `s*`, but its parametric form is given as a function of the stimulus `s`. It should be `σ²report(s*) = ρ²₀ + ρ²₁s*²`.
*   **Overly Broad Claims:** The assertion that the model encompasses "any sensorimotor estimation task" (Section 2) is immediately contradicted by the qualifications that follow (e.g., requires modifications for angular variables). Similarly, stating that Eq. 4 can approximate "any observer's prior" is an overstatement; it is a flexible approximation whose quality depends on `M`.
*   **Misleading Terminology:** The term "approximate expected posterior density" for `E(θ|θ*)` in Eq. (13) is confusing. This is not a posterior in the Bayesian sense (i.e., `p(θ|D)`). It is a similarity metric between the output distributions of two parameter vectors, `θ` and `θ*`. A more precise term, such as "expected likelihood profile" or "identifiability landscape," would be more appropriate and less ambiguous.
*   **Insufficient Justification for Model Choices:** The choice of `M=15` basis functions for the prior is presented without justification. How sensitive are the results to this choice? What are the computational vs. accuracy trade-offs? Similarly, the "maximum-entropy approach" for parametrizing the likelihood and loss is vaguely described. The meaning of "locally maximizes the differential entropy" is unclear and lacks the mathematical formalization expected at NIPS.

**2. Significant Methodological Omissions:**

*   **The 2AFC-to-Estimation Conversion:** The second case study hinges on converting a 2AFC paradigm from the original study [9] into an "equivalent estimation task." This is a highly non-trivial step that is dismissed in a single sentence with a citation. The assumptions and potential pitfalls of this conversion are critical to the validity of the case study's conclusions, yet they are completely unaddressed. For a paper focused on methodological rigor and identifiability, this omission is a major flaw.
*   **Explanation of Key Results:** The surprising finding that a narrow (MAP) loss function worsens prior recovery in the first case study is a key result. The explanation is relegated to a footnote, which cursorily points to a degeneracy between sensory and motor noise. This crucial insight deserves a full explanation in the main text, explicitly linking the properties of the loss function to the exacerbation of this specific degeneracy.

**3. Presentation and Formatting Issues:**

*   **Use of Footnotes:** As mentioned, the footnote on page 4 contains a critical piece of the scientific argument and should be integrated into the main text.
*   **Citation Style:** The long string of citations, e.g., "[7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14]", is stylistically poor and hinders readability.

### **Decision**

This paper addresses an important problem with a promising approach. The core idea is valuable, and the framework could become a useful tool for the community. However, the current manuscript is marred by a significant number of inaccuracies, methodological gaps, and instances of imprecise language. The failure to detail the 2AFC-to-estimation conversion is particularly concerning for a paper whose central theme is methodological diligence. While the contribution is potentially significant, the execution does not meet the high standards of technical soundness and clarity expected for NIPS. The paper preaches methodological rigor but fails to practice it in several key aspects. Therefore, I recommend rejection in its current form. I would, however, be very open to reviewing a substantially revised version that addresses these numerous shortcomings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in computational modeling.
*   The proposed framework is novel, principled, and potentially very useful for experimental design.
*   The core technical idea of the flexible observer model is well-conceived.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Numerous notational and terminological inaccuracies throughout the paper.
*   A major methodological step (2AFC-to-estimation conversion) is completely unexplained, undermining the validity of a key case study.
*   Key modeling choices (e.g., `M=15`) are arbitrary and unjustified.
*   The paper's claims of generality are overstated.
*   The overall lack of precision and detail is at odds with the paper's focus on methodological rigor.
