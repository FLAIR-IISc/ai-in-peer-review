- Addresses non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavioral data and proposes a framework to assess identifiability a priori using expected log-likelihood and KL divergence.
- Applies the framework to two psychophysical tasks (time interval estimation and speed perception) to show practical implications.

Strengths:
- Tackles a fundamental, longstanding methodological problem in Bayesian models of perception.
- Framework is theoretically grounded in Bayesian decision theory and maximum entropy; derivation of expected support (Eq.12) is elegant and tractable.
- Demonstrates practical utility for experimental design, revealing non-intuitive effects (e.g., narrow loss worsens prior recovery).
- Model structure (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) is clear, flexible, and computationally efficient.
- Authors transparently acknowledge limitations and discuss potential extensions.

Weaknesses:
- Limited comparison to other identifiability methods (e.g., profile likelihood, Fisher information); novelty relative to prior methods not fully clarified.
- Insufficient implementation details for MCMC sampling (proposal distributions, convergence diagnostics) hindering reproducibility.
- Explanation for why narrow loss functions worsen prior recovery is superficial and needs deeper analysis of degeneracy with sensory noise.
- Unclear scalability to multidimensional stimuli or multi-cue models; discussion of these limitations is brief.
- Could better connect to recent NIPS work on Bayesian models to position contributions within the field.

Overall assessment:
- Strong, technically sound paper that makes a novel, valuable methodological contribution with clear practical impact despite minor deficiencies.

Recommendation:
- Accept.

Key arguments for acceptance:
- Solves a core non-identifiability problem affecting many studies.
- Provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient a priori identifiability framework.
- Case studies demonstrate clear, non-obvious insights relevant to experimental design.
- Well-written, logically structured, and transparent about limitations.
- High potential impact for computational cognitive science and neuroscience.

Key suggestions for improvement before publication:
- Clarify differences and advantages relative to existing identifiability methods.
- Provide full MCMC implementation details and diagnostics for reproducibility.
- Deepen the analysis explaining the degeneracy between loss width and sensory noise.
- Explicitly relate and compare the work to recent relevant NIPS contributions.
