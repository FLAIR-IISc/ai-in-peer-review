

# Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors demonstrate that for a given behavioral output, multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, rendering standard model fitting unreliable. They propose a systematic framework to assess identifiability a priori, allowing researchers to evaluate experimental designs before collecting data. The framework combines a flexible class of Bayesian observer models with a method to quantify identifiability using KL divergence and expected log likelihood. The authors validate their approach through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception, demonstrating how their framework can guide experimental design to improve parameter recovery.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a well-identified problem in the field. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for their identifiability framework, with careful derivations of key equations (particularly Eq. 12, which connects identifiability to KL divergence). The case studies are well-executed, with appropriate model specifications and clear results. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the problem's nuances, acknowledging limitations (e.g., the framework assumes the true model is within the model class) and discussing how to extend the approach.

**Originality (Strong):** While model identifiability has been discussed in psychology (e.g., [22, 23]), this paper presents the first systematic framework specifically designed for Bayesian models of perception. Previous work has focused on specific instances of the problem (e.g., [12, 19]), but this paper provides a unified approach applicable to a wide range of psychophysical tasks. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior contributions, emphasizing the lack of a systematic analysis of identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models.

**Significance (High):** The framework has immediate practical value for perception researchers. By enabling a priori assessment of experimental designs, it can prevent wasted resources on studies that cannot recover the parameters of interest. The case studies reveal counterintuitive insights (e.g., that a "narrow loss function" can worsen prior recovery), which would be difficult to discover through trial-and-error experimentation. This work directly addresses a critical gap in the field, as noted by the authors: "there has been no systematic analysis – neither empirical nor theoretical – of their effectiveness, nor a framework to perform such study a priori."

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized, with clear sections that progress logically from problem statement to method to validation. The mathematical derivations are presented carefully, and the case studies effectively illustrate the framework's application. The figures (particularly Figures 2 and 3) are informative and help clarify key findings. A minor improvement could be to provide more detail on the MCMC implementation for the posterior density approximation (Eq. 13), but the paper is otherwise clear.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations:** The framework is restricted to continuous one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and note potential extensions (e.g., to "discrete-choice paradigms"), the scope is narrower than the title might suggest. For a conference like NIPS that values broad applicability, this limits the framework's potential impact.

**Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim efficiency, the MCMC-based approach to explore the identifiability landscape could become computationally intensive for high-dimensional parameter spaces or complex models. The paper doesn't thoroughly address the scalability of their approach to more complex models that might be of interest to NIPS researchers.

**Assumption Sensitivity:** The framework's results are sensitive to assumptions about the reference observer (θ*). The authors acknowledge this, but the paper doesn't explore how robust their conclusions are to different choices of θ*. This is particularly important since the reference model is often based on pilot data or educated guesses, which may contain errors.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality, significant contribution that addresses a critical methodological gap in Bayesian modeling of perception. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and offers a practical tool that could substantially improve experimental design in the field. While the scope is somewhat limited to specific psychophysical tasks, the framework is general enough to be adapted to many related problems. The case studies provide convincing evidence of the framework's utility, revealing non-intuitive insights about experimental design.

The main limitation is the restricted scope to one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks, but the authors acknowledge this and outline potential extensions. The computational considerations are noted but not deeply explored, though this is a reasonable trade-off given the paper's focus on the conceptual framework rather than implementation details.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a systematic method to address a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception. The framework is novel, well-motivated, and has clear practical value. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility, revealing insights that would be difficult to obtain through other means. While the scope is limited, the authors clearly articulate the boundaries of their approach and suggest promising directions for future work.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical and methodological advances in machine learning and cognitive science. It provides a valuable tool for researchers in perception science and could inspire similar approaches in other domains where model identifiability is a concern. The work is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers (e.g., compared to [12, 19]), making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
