Summary
The paper tackles a central and underappreciated problem in Bayesian models of perception: identifiability of latent components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data. The authors (i) propose a flexible, computationally tractable class of 1D Bayesian observer models that standardizes internal representations via a parametric sensory mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians formulations for likelihoods and loss functions, and a simple response noise model; (ii) derive an a priori identifiability measure based on the expected log-likelihood of data generated by a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions; and (iii) explore the induced “approximate expected posterior” over model parameters via MCMC to visualize and quantify identifiability under different experimental designs. They apply the framework to interval timing and speed perception, finding, e.g., that (a) in timing, priors are poorly recovered without additional constraints but become recoverable if motor noise is independently measured; and (b) in speed perception, the slow-speed prior can be reliably inferred under typical designs.

Relation to prior work: The non-identifiability of priors/likelihood/loss is known conceptually (e.g., invariances noted in BDT), and model distinguishability/identifiability has a long tradition in cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung; Bayesian OED notions of expected information/KL). This paper’s novelty lies in packaging those ideas into a practical, unified, and efficient framework targeted at Bayesian perception models, with a parameterization explicitly designed to support a priori design comparison at scale.

Strengths
- Novel and useful direction: a principled, pre-experimental procedure for probing identifiability in popular Bayesian observer models; likely to influence practice and improve experimental design.
- Elegant analytic core: the expected log-likelihood reduces to a sum of KL divergences over stimuli, enabling efficient evaluation and MCMC exploration of identifiability landscapes.
- Modeling design is broad yet computationally pragmatic: unified parameterization (sensory map, likelihood, prior basis, loss), enabling fast predictions and reuse across tasks.
- Insightful case studies: convincingly shows non-obvious consequences (e.g., enforcing MAP-like losses can degrade prior recovery in timing; measuring motor noise helps), and that speed priors can be robustly inferred even under relaxed assumptions.
- Emphasis on a priori analysis and design ranking is timely and valuable to the community.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope limitations: The framework is currently restricted to 1D estimation tasks; 2AFC and multi-dimensional/cue-integration cases are mentioned as future work but are central in the literature.
- Strong parametric assumptions: two-component Gaussian mixtures and the specific f(s) parametric form may bias identifiability conclusions under mis-specification. Sensitivity analyses beyond a few scenarios are limited.
- “Approximate expected posterior”: While intuitive, sampling from E(θ|θ*) conflates identifiability with the assumed parametric family and may not reflect structural identifiability under model mismatch; discussion of local vs global identifiability would help.
- Limited connection to design optimization: The KL-based objective suggests a natural link to Bayesian optimal experimental design, but the paper only compares a few hand-picked designs rather than optimizing stimuli/conditions formally.
- Presentation issues: a few notation slips (e.g., “w1” seemingly used for motor noise vs prior weights wm), and some references are incomplete. The mapping of the speed 2AFC setting to an equivalent estimation task is only sketched.
- Reproducibility details could be sharper: computational cost, MCMC specifics (priors, chain lengths, diagnostics), and code availability are not clearly provided.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the KL derivation for expected log-likelihood is standard and appropriate. The observer model is internally consistent and the case studies are thoughtfully constructed. However, empirical validation with real datasets or stronger robustness checks would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. The modeling pipeline is easy to follow. Clarity would improve by tightening notation (p vs q; “true” vs “internal”), fixing minor typos, and adding a compact table of parameters. More explicit MCMC and runtime details are needed for reproducibility.

Originality
- The individual components (KL-based expected evidence; identifiability awareness; mixture parameterizations) are known, but their synthesis into a practical, reusable framework for a priori identifiability analysis in Bayesian perception is novel and valuable.

Significance
- High potential impact: if adopted, this could shift how labs design perceptual experiments and interpret parameter recovery. The counterintuitive design insights are especially compelling.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify the apparent “w1” typo and ensure consistent notation for prior weights vs motor noise parameters.
- Provide MCMC details (priors, number of chains, iterations, adaptation, diagnostics) and report computational cost. Consider releasing code.
- Discuss robustness to model mis-specification: e.g., heavy-tailed sensory noise, non-monotonic f(s), or non-Gaussian loss beyond two-component mixtures.
- Contrast with Fisher-information/local identifiability analyses, and relate your KL-based utility to Bayesian optimal experimental design; consider a proof-of-concept design optimization.
- For the speed example, elaborate on the mapping from 2AFC to estimation and its implications for identifiability.
- Outline how your framework would extend to 2AFC and multi-cue/integrative tasks and any anticipated pitfalls.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a practical, general framework addressing an important and timely problem.
- Provides actionable, non-trivial design guidance.
- Likely to catalyze better experimental practices in Bayesian perception.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scope (1D estimation) and strong parametric assumptions.
- Lack of empirical validation and limited robustness analyses.
- Missing connection to formal design optimization.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a meaningful conceptual and practical advance with promising implications for the field. While not flawless, its novelty and potential impact outweigh the limitations.
