**Summary**
This paper addresses the well-known non-identifiability problem in Bayesian models of perception, where different combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss function can produce identical observable behavior. The authors propose a framework to assess, *a priori*, whether a given experimental design can successfully disambiguate these internal components. The framework consists of two main parts: first, a flexible and computationally tractable parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of representing a wide range of sensory mappings, noise characteristics, priors, and loss functions. Second, a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model under a "true" reference model, which cleverly reduces to computing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between their respective response distributions. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility on two case studies from the literature: interval timing and speed perception, showing how it can be used to compare experimental designs and probe the robustness of previous scientific claims.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper tackles a fundamental and critical methodological problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The non-identifiability of model components is a pervasive issue that threatens the validity of many studies aiming to "recover" internal representations from behavioral data. A systematic approach to address this before data collection is highly desirable.
2.  The proposed method for mapping the identifiability landscape via the expected log-likelihood (and its connection to KL-divergence) is elegant and computationally efficient. It provides a principled way to assess expected model distinguishability without resorting to brute-force simulations of thousands of datasets, which would be intractable for models of this complexity.
3.  The case studies effectively illustrate the potential of the framework. The analysis of the interval timing task, for instance, yields non-trivial predictions, such as the potential negative impact of a MAP-like loss function on prior recovery and the critical importance of independently measuring motor noise. This demonstrates that the framework can provide concrete, actionable guidance for experimental design.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Over-reliance on a specific, complex model class:** The entire framework is built upon a single, highly parameterized class of observer models (Eq. 10). While flexible, this model is laden with specific assumptions (e.g., mixtures of two Gaussians for likelihood and loss, a specific parametric form for the sensory map). The conclusions drawn from the framework are therefore conditional on the true observer behaving according to this specific model class. The paper provides little justification for these choices beyond computational convenience and does not assess how the conclusions might change if the true data-generating process lies outside this assumed class. This is a significant limitation, as the framework's utility depends entirely on the validity of its foundational model.
2.  **Lack of robustness analysis:** The analysis in the case studies depends on the choice of a "reference observer" (θ*), which is set based on prior literature or "educated guesses." However, the paper does not explore the robustness of its conclusions to variations in this reference observer. For example, in the interval timing task, would independently measuring motor noise still be the optimal strategy if the true sensory noise were significantly higher or non-Gaussian? Without such sensitivity analysis, the specific recommendations for experimental design feel fragile and potentially idiosyncratic to the chosen θ*.
3.  **Absence of comparison to established methods:** The problem of model identifiability is not new, and various statistical techniques exist for its analysis (e.g., based on the Fisher Information Matrix, profile likelihoods, or simulation-based approaches). The paper presents its framework in isolation, without discussing how it relates to or improves upon these more established, general-purpose methods. A comparison would be essential to understand the unique contribution and potential trade-offs of this domain-specific approach.
4.  **Purely theoretical validation:** The case studies are simulations. While they are useful for demonstrating the mechanics of the framework, they do not constitute empirical validation. The work would be substantially stronger if it presented an example where the framework was used to design a novel experiment, which was then shown to yield better parameter recovery than a baseline design when fit to real human data. As it stands, the framework is an unproven, though promising, theoretical proposal.

**Overall assessment**
This paper presents an interesting and computationally clever approach to a significant problem. The idea of using a KL-divergence-based metric to guide experimental design *a priori* is compelling. However, the work in its current form feels more like a proof of concept than a validated, ready-to-use methodology. The primary concern is its heavy reliance on a specific and complex set of modeling assumptions without sufficient justification or robustness analysis. While the persona of a generic Bayesian observer is a powerful tool, the specific implementation here may be too rigid. An experimentalist might be hesitant to redesign their experiment based on recommendations from a framework whose own assumptions have not been rigorously tested.

For the paper to be acceptable, the authors would need to, at a minimum, provide a more thorough justification for their model class and, crucially, demonstrate that their key findings are robust to reasonable variations in the assumed "reference observer." A discussion of how their method compares to other standard identifiability analysis techniques would also be necessary to contextualize the contribution. While the ambition is laudable, the work currently lacks the rigorous validation required to endorse it as a reliable tool for the community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved methodological problem in a popular research area.
*   The proposed computational technique (using KL-divergence) is novel in this context and efficient.
*   The framework has clear potential to improve the rigor of experimental design in its target domain.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The framework's conclusions are contingent on a complex, unvalidated, and highly specific class of observer models.
*   Key results from the case studies lack a robustness analysis with respect to the assumed "ground truth" observer.
*   The work is presented without comparison to or discussion of existing, more general methods for identifiability analysis.
*   The validation is purely theoretical, with no empirical evidence to support the framework's predictions.
