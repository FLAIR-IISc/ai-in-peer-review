- Overall assessment: Strong, timely contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions.
- Core problem: Tackles non-identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models where different parameter combinations yield identical behavior.
- Contribution: Proposes a systematic, a priori framework for testing identifiability using a flexible parameterized observer class.
- Method: Uses KL-divergence and MCMC sampling to quantify and explore the identifiability landscape.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on and generalizes previous experimental-design and auxiliary-measurement approaches to identifiability.
- Examples: Demonstrates utility on time and speed perception, revealing concrete and sometimes counterintuitive insights (e.g., MAP loss finding).
- Strength — importance: Addresses a fundamental limitation affecting validity of inferences from Bayesian models.
- Strength — systematic approach: Provides a structured, quantitative method over ad-hoc constraints.
- Strength — model flexibility: Single parameterization covers a wide range of perceptual tasks and components.
- Strength — clarity and derivations: Key equations and derivations are well-explained (supplementary material supports main text).
- Strength — thoroughness: Acknowledges limitations and discusses potential extensions.
- Weakness — mathematical complexity: Dense formalism may limit accessibility to non-experts.
- Weakness — computational cost: MCMC exploration can be expensive and scaling to high-dimensional spaces is a concern.
- Weakness — reference observer: Results depend on the chosen reference observer (θ*); choice can be arbitrary and affect conclusions.
- Weakness — simplifying assumptions: Gaussian noise and specific functional forms may limit generalizability.
- Revision suggestion — clarity: Add an intuitive KL-divergence explanation and a simple illustrative example in the main text.
- Revision suggestion — computation: Discuss computational demands and mitigation strategies (dimensionality reduction, approximate inference).
- Revision suggestion — sensitivity: Perform sensitivity analysis for different choices of the reference observer (θ*).
- Revision suggestion — assumptions: Discuss impact of simplifying assumptions on generalizability.
- Revision suggestion — future work: Expand on applying the framework to more complex phenomena (e.g., multisensory integration, decision-making under uncertainty).
- Pro: Framework is technically sound, practically useful, and influential for experimental design in Bayesian perception.
- Con: Accessibility, computational barriers, and reliance on an arbitrarily chosen reference observer are notable drawbacks.
