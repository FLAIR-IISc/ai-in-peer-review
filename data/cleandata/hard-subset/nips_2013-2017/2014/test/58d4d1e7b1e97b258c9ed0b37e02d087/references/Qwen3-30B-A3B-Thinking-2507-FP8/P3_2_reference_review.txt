

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical yet underappreciated issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of key model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, making model inversion unreliable. They propose a systematic framework to assess identifiability a priori by analyzing the expected log likelihood of alternative models given a reference model and experimental design. The framework is implemented through a flexible Bayesian observer model with four stages (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) and applied to two well-known case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. The authors show how their framework can inform experimental design by identifying which constraints are most effective for recovering specific model components.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a fundamental problem**: The paper correctly identifies that non-identifiability is a serious threat to the validity of Bayesian models in perception, building on earlier work by [19] but providing a systematic approach to address it.

2. **Theoretical soundness**: The derivation of the expected log likelihood as a proxy for identifiability (Equation 12) is mathematically rigorous, building on established concepts in model comparison (AIC, BIC) and information theory (KL divergence).

3. **Practical utility**: The framework provides concrete guidance for experimental design, as demonstrated in the two case studies. For instance, the analysis reveals that assuming knowledge of motor parameters (MTR) significantly improves prior recovery in interval timing tasks, while imposing a narrow loss function (MAP) can actually worsen recovery.

4. **Well-executed case studies**: The applications to temporal context and speed perception are relevant, well-chosen examples that demonstrate the framework's utility. The findings align with empirical observations while providing theoretical justification.

5. **Appropriate technical depth**: The authors carefully balance theoretical development with practical implementation details, making the framework accessible to both methodologists and applied researchers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the observer model**: While the model is flexible, it's restricted to one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately discuss how their framework might extend to more complex scenarios (e.g., multidimensional stimuli, cue integration) that are common in perception research.

2. **Insufficient engagement with identifiability literature**: The paper cites [23] as a prior work on identifiability, but doesn't thoroughly compare their approach to existing methods for assessing model identifiability in psychology. This weakens the claim of novelty.

3. **Computational concerns**: The paper doesn't address the computational burden of the MCMC sampling required for the identifiability analysis, especially for more complex models or larger experimental designs. This could limit practical applicability.

4. **Assumption sensitivity**: The results in both case studies depend heavily on assumptions about the reference model (θ*). The paper doesn't systematically explore how robust the identifiability conclusions are to reasonable variations in these assumptions.

5. **Lack of empirical validation**: The framework is demonstrated theoretically, but it would be valuable to see how it would perform with actual human data to confirm the theoretical predictions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong contribution that addresses a genuine and important problem in the field. The framework provides a valuable tool for researchers designing experiments with Bayesian models of perception, and the case studies convincingly demonstrate its utility. While the scope is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, the methodological approach is generalizable and could inspire extensions to more complex scenarios.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the methodological toolkit for Bayesian modeling in perception. The findings have direct implications for experimental design and interpretation of behavioral data in the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear contribution to a fundamental methodological issue in Bayesian modeling of perception, with practical implications for experimental design. The framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and effectively demonstrated through relevant case studies. While there are limitations in scope and some unaddressed computational concerns, these don't undermine the paper's core contribution, which is novel, significant, and well-executed.

The authors have made a substantial contribution to the methodological foundations of Bayesian modeling in perception research, which is exactly the kind of work NIPS should be supporting. The paper advances the field by providing a systematic approach to a problem that has been acknowledged but not properly addressed in previous work.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the discussion, better articulate how this framework differs from and improves upon existing methods for model identifiability in psychology (e.g., [23]).

2. Briefly discuss the computational complexity of the framework and potential strategies for scaling to larger model spaces.

3. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the framework might be extended to handle multidimensional stimuli or cue integration, even if only as a future work direction.

4. Provide more detail about the sensitivity of results to assumptions about the reference model (θ*), perhaps through a sensitivity analysis in the supplementary material.

This is a valuable paper that will help improve the rigor of Bayesian modeling in perception research, and I believe it would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
