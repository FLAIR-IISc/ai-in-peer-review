

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a genuine and important issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability problem where multiple combinations of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors propose a systematic framework for analyzing identifiability a priori by introducing a flexible class of Bayesian observer models and using KL divergence to quantify the expected support for alternative parameterizations. They apply this framework to two case studies in time perception and speed perception.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a well-known problem in the field. The mathematical derivations are clear and properly justified, particularly the elegant use of KL divergence to quantify identifiability. The parametrization of the observer model (Eqs. 2-8) is sufficiently general to cover many psychophysical tasks while maintaining computational tractability. The case studies demonstrate practical utility - for example, showing that measuring motor noise parameters (MTR) significantly improves identifiability in time perception tasks, which aligns with experimental best practices. The framework's ability to rank experimental designs based on identifiability (e.g., showing that "MAP" designs with narrow loss functions can worsen reconstruction quality) provides valuable theoretical insights for experimental design.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the technical soundness, I have significant concerns about the paper's validation and significance. The authors present a framework for theoretical identifiability analysis but provide no empirical validation against real data or established baselines. The case studies are entirely simulation-based, with the "reference observer" parameters derived from literature assumptions rather than empirical measurement. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to "improve experimental design" - without demonstrating that the framework correctly identifies which experimental designs actually yield better parameter recovery on real data, the practical utility remains unproven.

The paper's scope is also quite narrow. While the authors claim their framework covers "a large class of psychophysical tasks," the case studies are limited to two specific tasks (time interval estimation and speed perception), and the framework's applicability to more common machine learning contexts (where Bayesian models are frequently used) is not addressed. The paper makes no connection to standard statistical identifiability methods used in machine learning (e.g., Fisher information analysis, which is standard for assessing parameter recoverability), which would have strengthened the theoretical grounding.

The most concerning aspect is the paper's lack of comparison with existing approaches to identifiability analysis. The authors cite previous work on identifiability [22,23] but fail to demonstrate how their method improves upon or differs from established statistical techniques. Without this comparison, it's unclear what the paper contributes beyond rederiving known concepts in a more specialized context. The paper claims to "fill a gap" but doesn't convincingly show that previous work has failed to address this issue adequately.

**Significance and Originality:**
While the problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception is valid and important, the paper's contribution is largely methodological rather than conceptual. The framework is a useful tool for psychophysicists but lacks the broader significance expected at NIPS. The paper does not advance the state of the art in machine learning or statistical methods - it applies standard statistical concepts (KL divergence, MCMC) to a specific domain without demonstrating novel insights that would benefit the wider ML community. The connection to NIPS is tenuous at best, as the paper doesn't address machine learning problems or demonstrate how this framework could improve learning algorithms.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound framework for a specific problem in psychophysics, it fails to meet NIPS's standards for empirical validation and broader significance. The lack of empirical validation on real data, the narrow scope limited to psychophysics, and the failure to demonstrate how this framework improves upon existing statistical methods for identifiability analysis are critical shortcomings. The paper would be better suited for a specialized journal in perception science rather than NIPS, where the expectation is for work that advances machine learning or computational neuroscience with broader applicability.

The authors have identified an important problem, but their solution lacks the rigorous empirical validation and broader impact that would justify publication at NIPS. The paper would be significantly strengthened by (1) demonstrating the framework's utility on real experimental data, (2) comparing it to standard statistical identifiability techniques, and (3) showing how it could be applied to more general machine learning problems beyond psychophysics. Without these elements, the contribution remains limited to a niche area without clear relevance to the broader NIPS community.
