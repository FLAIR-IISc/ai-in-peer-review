This paper tackles the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue given the increasing use of these models to infer internal representations from behavior. The authors provide a comprehensive framework for analyzing identifiability a priori, along with a method for exploring the parameter space and improving experimental design. The work is well-motivated, building on a substantial body of literature in Bayesian perception and model fitting. The introduction clearly articulates the challenges posed by the degeneracy of solutions and the need for a systematic approach to address it.

The core strength of the paper lies in its formalization of the identifiability problem and the development of a practical method based on KL divergence. The proposed observer model, while complex, appears to be a useful generalization of existing models, incorporating key elements like sensory mapping and lapse rates. The application to time and speed perception tasks provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used to evaluate experimental designs and highlight potential issues with parameter recovery. The careful consideration of different experimental setups (e.g., varying the number of trials, manipulating the loss function, incorporating prior knowledge of motor noise) is commendable.  The inclusion of supplementary material detailing derivations and computational procedures enhances the reproducibility of the work.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the complexity of the model makes it difficult to fully grasp the implications of the analysis. While the authors aim for generality, the sheer number of parameters can obscure the key drivers of identifiability. A more focused analysis on a smaller subset of critical parameters might have provided clearer insights. Second, the assumption of Gaussian distributions throughout the model (for likelihoods, priors, and noise) feels restrictive. While justified as a starting point, the authors do not sufficiently discuss the potential impact of deviations from Gaussianity on their results. Third, the interpretation of the KL divergence as a measure of practical non-identifiability feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more rigorous connection to statistical power or prediction error would strengthen the argument. Fourth, the discussion of the MAP loss function experiment feels somewhat dismissive. While the authors find that it *decreases* identifiability in their model, it's possible this is a specific artifact of their model assumptions, and doesn’t necessarily invalidate the use of MAP-like loss functions in general. The sensitivity analysis on this assumption should be more thorough. Fifth, while the authors present a framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis, they do not fully address the issue of *post hoc* model validation. Even with a well-designed experiment, it is crucial to assess the fit of the model to the data and to consider alternative explanations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and important problem: Addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Comprehensive framework: Provides a systematic approach to analyzing identifiability.
*   Practical method: Offers a concrete technique for exploring parameter space and optimizing experimental design.
*   Well-motivated: Builds on a strong foundation of existing literature.
*   Clear presentation: The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Model complexity: The large number of parameters can obscure key insights.
*   Restrictive assumptions: Reliance on Gaussian distributions may limit the generalizability of the results.
*   Interpretation of KL divergence: The connection to practical non-identifiability could be strengthened.
*   Limited post-hoc validation: The paper does not adequately address model validation and alternative explanations.
*   Dismissive discussion of MAP loss: The potential benefits of MAP-like loss functions are downplayed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The framework proposed by the authors is a valuable tool for researchers seeking to infer internal representations from behavioral data. However, the complexity of the model and some limitations in the analysis prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. 

**Suggestions for revision:**

1.  **Simplify the model:** Focus on a smaller subset of critical parameters and explore their interactions in more detail.
2.  **Investigate non-Gaussian distributions:** Explore the impact of deviations from Gaussianity on identifiability.
3.  **Strengthen the connection to statistical power:** Provide a more rigorous justification for using KL divergence as a measure of practical non-identifiability.
4.  **Expand the discussion of post-hoc validation:** Discuss methods for assessing model fit and considering alternative explanations.
5.  **Re-evaluate the MAP loss function experiment:** Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis to understand why it leads to decreased identifiability in the current model.
6.  **Consider a worked example with synthetic data:** Demonstrate how the framework can be used to design an experiment that successfully recovers the ground-truth parameters.
