Summary
The paper tackles a central problem in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions when fitting observer models to behavioral data. The authors (i) construct a flexible, unified observer model for 1D estimation tasks with parametric sensory mapping, measurement noise (two-Gaussian mixtures), priors (Gaussian mixture basis), loss (mixture of inverted Gaussians), lapses, and motor noise; (ii) derive an efficient, a priori identifiability analysis by computing the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model θ under data generated by a reference observer θ*, which equals a negative weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions (plus a constant); and (iii) sample from an “approximate expected posterior” to map regions of practical non-identifiability and compare experimental designs. Two case studies (interval timing and speed perception) illustrate how the framework can reveal confounds (e.g., sensory vs motor noise; weak identifiability of loss width) and provide actionable design guidance (e.g., measure motor noise; cautious use of narrow loss). The approach relates to prior work on model distinguishability and Bayesian experimental design but offers a concrete, task-tailored instantiation for Bayesian observer models.

Quality
- Positive: The expected support derivation (expected log-likelihood as −Ntr Σ P_i KL) is correct and appropriate for a priori identifiability. The observer model is carefully engineered so that expected loss is tractable (mixtures lead to closed-form expressions), making the exploration feasible. The two case studies are thoughtfully designed and yield interpretable, nontrivial conclusions (e.g., narrow loss can harm prior recovery in timing; slow-speed prior recovery is robust).
- Caveats: The framework relies on strong parametric assumptions (two-Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss; fixed M=15 Gaussian basis for priors; specific f(s) mapping). “Local maximum entropy” choices for mixture parameters are under-specified in the main text. The analysis focuses on practical identifiability with respect to a single θ*; sensitivity to θ* is discussed qualitatively but could be quantified more systematically. The mapping of 2AFC to estimation is assumed but not detailed. MCMC diagnostics, computational cost, and sensitivity to M, mixture count, stimulus discretization, and Ntr are not reported. No formal link to Fisher Information/local identifiability or to optimal design (e.g., maximizing expected information gain) is made, although the setup naturally permits it.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with a clear motivation (degeneracy), method (expected support via KL), and applications. Equations and modeling choices are explained, though several key implementation details are deferred to the supplement (derivation of Eq. 12; maximum-entropy construction for mixtures; numerical solution details; MCMC settings). The reference list has incomplete bibliographic details in places. Suggestions:
  - Provide a brief derivation sketch of Eq. 12 in the main text.
  - Clarify the “local maximum entropy” criterion and constraints for choosing mixture parameters.
  - Report MCMC configuration, convergence diagnostics, and runtimes.
  - Add an ablation on the number of prior components M and on the number of Gaussian components for likelihood/loss.

Originality
- The paper combines known ingredients—Bayesian observer modeling, KL-based expected evidence, and mixture approximations—into a unified, efficient framework specifically for a priori identifiability and experimental design assessment in psychophysics. Prior work has discussed non-identifiability and model distinguishability (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung) and Bayesian experimental design (Lindley; Chaloner & Verdinelli; Paninski). This paper’s novelty lies in the concrete, tractable parameterization enabling broad coverage of observer models and the operationalization of identifiability mapping for realistic perceptual tasks. The case studies provide new insights and design recommendations.

Significance
- Practical: The framework can influence how experiments are designed and interpreted in Bayesian perception, highlighting when inferred components (e.g., priors, loss width) are or are not recoverable. The timing and speed examples address prominent debates and are likely to be used by practitioners.
- Scientific: Provides a principled, quantitative way to reason about degeneracy and to compare designs before data collection. Potential to be extended to adaptive design and to other paradigms (multi-cue, 2AFC).
- Scope limitations (1D tasks, specific parametrizations) reduce generality, but extensions appear feasible.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Parametric rigidity: Explore robustness to mis-specification (e.g., true loss not in the mixture family, likelihood with heavier tails). Show recovery degradation when the generative model departs from the inference family.
- Local identifiability analysis: Compute the Fisher Information Matrix at θ* or the Hessian of the expected log-likelihood to visualize ill-conditioned directions and to complement MCMC-based landscapes.
- Design optimization: Connect the KL-based expected support to Bayesian experimental design (expected information gain) and illustrate how to optimize stimuli/conditions (beyond comparing a few hand-picked designs).
- 2AFC conversion: Provide the mapping from 2AFC to estimation and validate equivalence on simulated data.
- Real-data validation: Demonstrate at least one prediction on real datasets (e.g., that fixing motor noise improves prior recovery in timing).
- Reporting: Include code, detailed supplement derivations, and additional diagnostics.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem (a priori identifiability) with a principled, efficient method.
- Clear, actionable insights for experimental design; compelling case studies.
- Technically sound core derivation; thoughtful model construction enabling tractability.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on parametric assumptions; limited robustness analyses.
- Missing methodological details in main text; limited quantitative diagnostics.
- No formal link to optimal design or local identifiability; scope limited to 1D estimation.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The contribution is timely and useful for the computational neuroscience/psychophysics community, with solid methodology and impactful insights. Strengthening robustness analyses, methodological details, and connections to optimal design would further increase its value.
