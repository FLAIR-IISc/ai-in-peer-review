Summary
This paper proposes a practical, a priori framework to assess identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors (i) introduce a flexible, computationally efficient observer model class for 1D estimation tasks: a parametric stimulus-to-internal mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians likelihood and loss, and a lattice mixture-of-Gaussians prior; (ii) derive an expected log-likelihood under a reference observer θ∗ that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions (Eq. 12), avoiding Monte Carlo over datasets; (iii) define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩ and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability landscapes; and (iv) use the framework to compare experimental designs and interrogate parameters that drive non-identifiability. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (showing poor prior identifiability unless motor noise is independently constrained; encouraging MAP-like losses can hurt identifiability) and speed perception (recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters is robust).

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to ideal observer modeling and Bayesian cognition (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert), and to generic identifiability/model distinguishability analyses (Myung, Pitt & Navarro). The degeneracy between prior, likelihood, and loss in BDT is known; what’s novel here is a unifying, tractable observer model class with analytic/numerically cheap computations that allow pre-experiment identifiability mapping and design comparison. The expected log-likelihood/KL identity is standard, but its application here to complex Bayesian observer models, coupled with a computationally efficient instantiation and design-level insights, is new and useful for this community.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and ambition: a general, actionable framework to probe identifiability and guide experimental design before data collection.
- Technical design enabling efficiency: mixture-of-Gaussians forms for likelihood and loss and a prior lattice yield closed-form expected losses and tractable response distributions, making the KL-based objective usable in practice.
- Clear utility: The analyses produce concrete, sometimes counterintuitive design recommendations (e.g., narrow losses can degrade prior identifiability in timing; measuring motor noise largely resolves key degeneracies).
- Breadth and validation: Two distinct domains (interval timing and speed perception) demonstrate that the method can both diagnose fragile inferences and corroborate robust ones (slow-speed prior).
- Addresses an important, timely issue (identifiability/degeneracy) that impacts many Bayesian modeling studies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Model-class dependence: Identifiability is characterized within a specific parametric family (log-like mapping, 2-Gaussian mixtures, fixed prior lattice M=15, Gaussian motor noise). Conclusions about design quality may shift under different families (e.g., heavier-tailed likelihoods, different response noise, different prior bases).
- Limited robustness analysis over θ∗: Case studies hinge on single reference observers and a few variants. A broader sweep (or hierarchical distribution) over θ∗ would strengthen generality claims and design rankings.
- 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence: The speed example relies on an equivalence that is non-trivial; this mapping deserves clearer justification and limitations.
- Loss identifiability: The finding that σℓ is weakly identifiable in timing could be (partly) an artifact of the chosen loss family; more ablations (e.g., single-Gaussian vs mixture, asymmetric penalties) would help interpretability.
- Practicalities under-specified: How are KLs between continuous response distributions computed (grid resolution, quadrature accuracy)? MCMC diagnostics, computational cost, and sensitivity to M (prior grid size) are not reported.
- The degeneracy argument in Eq. 1: As written, scaling qprior, qmeas, and L by φi(s) with product constant preserves the argmin, but these φi may break normalization of qprior and qmeas. Clarify that this is an observational equivalence argument at the level of the decision rule, not necessarily a valid probabilistic reparameterization unless renormalized.
- Presentation: Some references appear incomplete; a quick pass to ensure proper citations and to relate to Bayesian optimal experimental design and Fisher information approaches would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide code or pseudocode and report computational cost (time/cores) and MCMC diagnostics. Include details on numerical KL estimation.
- Sensitivity analyses: vary M (prior basis granularity), number of mixture components, alternative motor noise models, and different t-mappings (e.g., Stevens vs Weber–Fechner regimes).
- Explore design optimization: since Eq. 12 is available, consider using expected KL/information gain to automatically propose optimal stimulus schedules or conditions.
- Compare to Fisher information/profile likelihood or sloppiness analyses to triangulate identifiability conclusions.
- For the 2AFC mapping, add a derivation or cite a concrete equivalence result and conditions under which it holds.
- Clarify the scope: explicitly state that identifiability conclusions are conditional on the model class and discuss how to detect class misfit.

Evaluation
- Quality: Solid and careful, with reasonable assumptions and insightful case studies; would benefit from deeper robustness checks and more computational detail.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and readable, though some technical derivations and numerical details are left to the supplement; polish references.
- Originality: High. While components exist in the literature, the integration into an efficient, practical framework for a priori identifiability and design is novel.
- Significance: High. This tool can materially impact how Bayesian observer studies are designed and interpreted, potentially averting misinference.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, timely contribution addressing a pervasive problem.
- Technically sound framework yielding actionable insights.
- Broad applicability across psychophysics and computational neuroscience.

Cons against acceptance
- Dependence on specific parametric assumptions with limited robustness analyses.
- Some methodological and numerical details underreported.
- 2AFC mapping and degeneracy normalization need clarification.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some incomplete validations and missing details, the paper introduces a significant, broadly useful framework that can shape experimental practice and interpretation in Bayesian perception modeling. My confidence is reasonably high.
