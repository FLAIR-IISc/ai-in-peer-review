Summary
The paper tackles a central and underappreciated problem in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions when inferring internal representations from behavior. The authors propose a general, computationally efficient framework to assess a priori identifiability for a broad class of one-dimensional estimation tasks. Key ingredients are: (i) a richly parameterized observer class that unifies sensory mapping, measurement noise, prior, loss, lapses, and motor noise (with priors/likelihoods/losses expressed as mixtures of Gaussians in an internal space defined by a monotonic transform f); (ii) an analytic treatment that yields closed-form mixtures for expected loss and fast computation of predicted responses; and (iii) an identifiability metric derived from the expected log marginal likelihood of a candidate parameter vector relative to a reference observer, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimuli. They then sample from an approximate “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp⟨log p(D|θ)⟩ to map degeneracies and compare experimental designs. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (where priors are hard to recover unless motor noise is independently constrained and narrow loss functions can hurt identifiability) and visual speed (where the slow-speed prior and contrast-dependent noise are more robustly recoverable). The work relates to earlier concerns about degeneracy in Bayesian inversion (e.g., Myung/Pitt/Navarro; critiques by Jones & Love, Bowers & Davis) and to Bayesian decision theory in perception.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A unified, a priori identifiability framework that emphasizes design-level decisions, going beyond post hoc model fits. The KL-based expected support is elegant and principled.
- Technical contribution: Clever parameterization (mixture-of-Gaussians for likelihood/prior/loss in a normalized internal space) allows analytic expected loss and fast evaluation—critical for sweeping high-dimensional parameter spaces via MCMC.
- Practical impact: Provides concrete design guidance (e.g., measure motor noise; don’t assume MAP-like losses will necessarily help) and actionable diagnostics about which parameters are weakly identifiable.
- Insightful case studies: The interval timing example reveals non-obvious failures (σℓ weakly identifiable; prior SD biased) and the speed example offers theoretical validation of slow-speed prior recovery even when decoupling internal from external noise.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured, with assumptions explicit and derivations (promised) in the supplement.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Approximation choice: The “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) is a surrogate obtained by exponentiating the expected log-likelihood under θ*. It ignores parameter priors and does not quantify full posterior uncertainty over datasets; calibration of this proxy (e.g., by comparison to repeated simulated datasets or to Fisher information) would strengthen claims.
- Model class constraints: Focus is on 1D estimation and unimodal mixtures in a specific internal metric. It is unclear how sensitive identifiability conclusions are to the chosen transform f(s), mixture order, or the maximum-entropy constraints used to set mixture parameters for a given moment set.
- Overparameterization and mixture issues: With M=15 prior weights plus mixture params, label-switching, implicit regularization, and constraints on weights (non-negativity, normalization) need to be discussed. How is overfitting avoided when exploring identifiability?
- Empirical grounding: Results are synthetic. A small demonstration on real datasets (e.g., reproducing a published interval timing or speed result) to show that the a priori rankings predict actual recovery performance would considerably boost significance.
- Design optimality: While the KL-based metric naturally connects to optimal design, the paper does not exploit it to produce optimized stimulus schedules; a comparison to classical D/KL/Bayes-optimal designs or local Fisher-information analyses would contextualize the benefits.
- Generality claims: Extensions to multi-cue, angular variables, and 2AFC are mentioned but not demonstrated. Given that 2AFC dominates perceptual studies, an explicit discrete-choice version (or a small demo) would increase impact.
- Reporting details: Computational cost, MCMC mixing diagnostics, and sensitivity to Ntr and stimulus grids deserve more quantitative treatment.

Comments and questions for the authors
- How sensitive are identifiability maps to the choice of M (number of prior components) and to the lattice spacing/range? Do results stabilize beyond M=15?
- Can you compare your identifiability landscape to local measures (Fisher information, profile likelihood) to verify that flat directions coincide?
- How do you handle mixture label-switching in MCMC, and do you impose priors (e.g., Dirichlet on weights) when sampling E(θ|θ*)?
- The finding that narrower losses degrade prior identifiability is intriguing. Is this due to increased effective determinism shrinking the diagnostic variance? Could targeted stimulus placement rescue identifiability under MAP-like losses?
- The decoupled likelihood variant (UNC) underestimates σ̃High and s0. Can you explain the mechanism (e.g., trade-off with prior scale) and suggest design fixes?
- Can your KL objective be used for adaptive/optimal experimental design (e.g., choose si to maximize expected KL between confusable parameter regions)? Even a small simulation would be valuable.
- Please clarify whether the identifiability conclusions are invariant to monotone reparameterizations of s (beyond your chosen f(s)).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely challenge (identifiability) with a principled, general, and efficient framework.
- Offers non-obvious, actionable design recommendations supported by analyses.
- Methodologically bold: unifies prior/likelihood/loss within a single parametric machinery conducive to exploration.
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work in experimental design and model criticism in computational perception.

Cons against acceptance
- Empirical validation and breadth of demonstrations are limited.
- Some technical choices (expected-posterior surrogate, mixture parameterization) deserve further justification and calibration.
- Missing comparison to classical optimal design and local identifiability tools.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper introduces a conceptually fresh and practically useful framework for a priori identifiability in Bayesian perception, with clear implications for experimental design. Despite limited empirical validation and some modeling choices that could be further calibrated, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance. I encourage the authors to add small-scale validations, comparisons to Fisher/optimal design baselines, and a brief discrete-choice demonstration if space permits.

Reviewer confidence: High on modeling/identifiability; moderate on specific psychophysical domains.
