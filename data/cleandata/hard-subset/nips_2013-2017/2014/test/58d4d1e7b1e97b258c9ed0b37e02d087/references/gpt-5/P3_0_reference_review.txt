Summary
The paper tackles a central and under-discussed issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: practical non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions when fitting observer models to behavioral data. The authors propose a two-part contribution: (i) a flexible, computationally efficient class of 1D Bayesian observer models with parametric mappings from task to internal space, mixture-of-Gaussians representations for measurement noise and loss, and a Gaussian-mixture basis for priors; and (ii) an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log-marginal likelihood under a reference observer θ*, which reduces to a sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions across stimuli. They sample from an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) to visualize identifiability. Two case studies—interval timing and speed perception—illustrate how the framework can compare designs and reveal parameter confounds.

Strengths
- Important problem: Provides a principled, task-general way to evaluate parameter recoverability before running experiments, addressing documented degeneracies in Bayesian BDT models.
- Methodological clarity: Derivation of the expected evidence as a KL sum (Eq. 12) is sound and insightful; sampling E(θ|θ*) is a practical surrogate for costly dataset-level simulations.
- Modeling flexibility and computational tractability: The mixture representations and internal-space mapping enable fast repeated evaluation across high-dimensional θ, which is crucial for design exploration.
- Compelling case studies:
  - Interval timing: convincingly shows confounds between sensory and motor noise and how fixing motor noise helps; counterintuitive insight that a MAP-like narrow loss can worsen prior recovery.
  - Speed perception: corroborates robustness of inferring a slow-speed prior and noise parameters, even with decoupled internal likelihood.
- Practical value: Provides guidance for experimental design (e.g., importance of calibrating motor noise; multi-condition vs single-condition trade-offs).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Relation to prior work on identifiability and design is underdeveloped. The approach is closely related to expected log-likelihood, Fisher information, and Bayesian optimal experimental design (e.g., Lindley, Chaloner & Verdinelli) and to cognitive-model diagnostics (Myung, Pitt, Navarro). Positioning against expected information gain, D-/A-optimality, and profile likelihood/Fisher-based local identifiability would strengthen novelty claims and help connect to design optimization methods.
- “Maximum-entropy” mixture construction: The paper asserts selecting mixture parameters (for measurement and loss) by locally maximizing entropy under moment and unimodality constraints. This is unusual and under-specified. How is unimodality enforced? Is the maximum unique and numerically stable? Provide derivations and algorithmic details; otherwise reproducibility and robustness are unclear.
- Parameter dimensionality and priors: θ is high-dimensional (e.g., 15 prior weights plus many nuisance parameters). E(θ|θ*) omits a prior over θ and acts like a cross-entropy-based pseudo-posterior. This can overstate identifiability in directions regularized in practice (e.g., smoothness of priors). Consider reporting local curvature (Hessian) at θ* to quantify “sloppiness,” and/or incorporating weakly informative priors (e.g., Dirichlet plus roughness penalty on wm) to reflect realistic constraints.
- Assumptions on loss and mapping: The loss depends only on t̂−t in internal space; many tasks impose loss in task space and/or asymmetries that depend on s. The mapping f(s) shares variance/scale with measurement noise, creating potential scale confounds. Discuss identifiability of (s0, d, σ) when A, B are set by range normalization.
- Design scope: The case studies compare a few hand-crafted designs. Given Eq. 12, the framework is positioned to optimize stimulus placements and frequencies Pi to maximize discriminability (e.g., maximize expected KL or EIG). Lack of an explicit design optimization is a missed opportunity.
- Mapping 2AFC to estimation in the speed case: This conversion is asserted but not detailed. Identifiability properties can change under discretization/choice noise. At minimum, outline the equivalence assumptions.
- Empirical details: MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, computation time, and numerical stability are not reported. Given the model size, these are important for reproducibility and for convincing the reader that the approach scales.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a clear and correct core derivation (Eq. 12). Case studies are thoughtfully constructed and provide interpretable conclusions. However, important implementation details (entropy-based mixture construction, MCMC, computational cost) are missing, and several strong modeling assumptions are not stress-tested for robustness to misspecification.

Clarity
Generally well written and structured. Notational choices (p vs q) are consistent but dense. Some text glitches (e.g., footnote in Sec. 4.2) and many incomplete references (missing years/venues) should be fixed. Please expand on the “maximum-entropy” procedure and provide algorithmic details in the supplement.

Originality
The modeling class is a sensible synthesis of known components; the core identifiability measure is classical (expected log-likelihood/KL), but its application as a practical, general a priori identifiability tool for Bayesian perception models with efficient mixture machinery is valuable. The design-comparison angle is useful, though not fully novel relative to Bayesian design literature.

Significance
Addresses a pervasive problem that affects interpretation of many Bayesian observer studies. The framework can influence experimental practice by encouraging pre-registered design checks and by highlighting which nuisance parameters must be independently calibrated. Potential to become a standard tool if released as code and extended to design optimization and discrete-choice tasks.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide full details on the entropy-based mixture parameterization and unimodality enforcement; consider ablation showing its impact vs simpler single-Gaussian models.
- Report Hessian/Fisher-based local identifiability around θ*; compare with the MCMC-based landscape to validate conclusions.
- Demonstrate a simple design optimization (e.g., optimize Pi) to illustrate how Eq. 12 can directly guide stimulus allocation.
- Clarify how prior weights wm are constrained (simplex, smoothness); consider regularization to avoid pathological priors.
- Explore robustness to model mismatch (e.g., true observer has non-mixture loss or non-log mapping).
- Elaborate the 2AFC→estimation equivalence and discuss how results might change in a native discrete-choice formulation.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely methodological gap; actionable insights for experimentalists.
- Solid, general framework with clear derivation and informative case studies.
- Potential to standardize a priori identifiability analyses in perception.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited positioning relative to established identifiability/design literatures.
- Missing technical details and empirical diagnostics.
- No actual design optimization despite having the necessary objective.

Recommendation
Weak accept. With improved exposition of the mixture/entropy construction, added diagnostics, and stronger links to optimal design/identifiability theory, this could be a very impactful contribution.
