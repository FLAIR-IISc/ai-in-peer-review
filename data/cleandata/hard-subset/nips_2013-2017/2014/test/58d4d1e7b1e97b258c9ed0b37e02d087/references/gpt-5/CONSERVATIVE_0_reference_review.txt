Summary
This paper tackles the long-standing problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors (i) propose a flexible 1D observer-model class that parameterizes the measurement mapping, likelihood (as a two-Gaussian mixture with moment constraints), prior (as a lattice mixture of Gaussians), loss (as an “inverted-mixture” family), lapses, and motor noise; and (ii) define an a priori “identifiability” measure by averaging the log-likelihood of data generated from a reference observer θ* over all datasets of fixed size, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli. They then sample from an “approximate expected posterior” proportional to the exponentiated expected log-likelihood to explore parameter regions that are practically indistinguishable from θ*. Two simulated case studies (interval timing and speed perception) illustrate how the framework can rank experimental designs and reveal degeneracies (e.g., confounding between sensory and motor noise) and suggest when recovery of priors and noise is feasible.

Strengths
- Important problem: practical non-identifiability of priors/likelihoods/loss is a core concern in Bayesian perception; the paper addresses it head-on.
- Coherent framework: reduces expected evidence to stimulus-weighted KL divergences of response distributions, a principled and familiar quantity.
- Flexible 1D model class: unifies several psychophysical tasks; mixture-based parameterizations enable analytic integrals and fast numerics (in 1D).
- Design insight: demonstrates how fixing motor noise or using multiple conditions improves identifiability; cautions that narrowing the loss (MAP-like feedback) can harm recovery in interval timing.
- Validation case: slow-speed prior recovery appears robust under their framework, lending theoretical support to established findings.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited to simulations; no demonstration that the identifiability metric predicts posterior concentration or recovery in actual fits to real human datasets or even to synthetic datasets fitted with a realistic inference pipeline.
- The “approximate expected posterior” is essentially exp(−Ntr×KL), not a posterior in a Bayesian sense; it omits model priors/complexity penalties and may not reflect finite-sample pathologies. The link between this quantity and practical parameter recovery (coverage, bias) is not established.
- Computational details are underspecified. KL between response densities (mixtures convolved with noise) has no closed form; numerical quadrature is implied, but stability, accuracy, and runtime are not reported. Convergence diagnostics for MCMC over a deterministic objective are missing.
- Strong structural assumptions drive conclusions: existence of a transform to translationally invariant noise; specific mixture parameterizations; tying internal and external likelihoods in some setups. Sensitivity analyses (e.g., number of prior components M, mixture skew/kurtosis, lapse structure) are scant.
- The “maximum-entropy” selection for mixture parameters and the unimodality constraint are relegated to the supplement; the rationale and implications for identifiability are unclear in the main text.
- The connection to well-established optimal experimental design (e.g., maximizing expected KL for model discrimination) is not made explicit; the paper stops short of proposing or optimizing designs using the presented criterion.
- Claims of novelty for the observer-class seem incremental relative to prior work using flexible priors/likelihoods and mixture models; related literatures on parameter identifiability (e.g., Fisher information/profile likelihood, sloppiness) and design for discriminability could be better integrated.
- Minor but concerning: a notation error (“motor noise, w1” likely intended ρ1), incomplete/irregular references, and heavy reliance on the supplement for essentials.

Quality
Technically mostly sound at a high level, but important derivations and numerical procedures are deferred. The central identifiability criterion is principled, yet the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that it predicts actual parameter recovery quality under realistic inference. Case studies are illustrative but limited and assumption-dependent.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear motivation. However, many critical choices (KL computation, maximum-entropy construction, optimization of t*, MCMC setup) lack detail in the main text. Some notation slips and incomplete references reduce polish.

Originality
The use of expected KL to assess model distinguishability is standard; the contribution is the specific observer-class and the application to psychophysical design. Novelty is moderate; the broader literature on identifiability/design is not fully engaged.

Significance
If strengthened, the framework could become a useful tool for psychophysics labs to sanity-check model/design identifiability. As is, the impact is limited by lack of empirical validation and unclear generality beyond 1D tasks with the assumed structure.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Provide empirical calibration: simulate datasets from θ* and fit the full model with a standard Bayesian pipeline; demonstrate correlation between your identifiability measure and posterior concentration/parameter recovery (coverage, bias) across Ntr.
- Report computational details: how is KL computed (grid resolution over r, integration scheme), runtimes, and numerical stability? How is t* computed efficiently across x?
- Clarify MCMC: target density, proposal, diagnostics, multimodality; why MCMC rather than direct optimization of the (deterministic) objective?
- Sensitivity analyses: number of prior components M; mixture skew/kurtosis; lapse model; dependence on the assumed transform f(s). How robust are conclusions across these?
- Connect to and possibly leverage optimal experimental design: can you optimize the stimulus set or task condition schedule to maximize expected discriminability?
- Expand to discrete-choice (2AFC) as claimed, or at least provide a worked example.
- Fix notation and reference formatting; clarify the “maximum-entropy” construction for mixtures in the main text.

Pros
- Addresses an important, under-discussed issue.
- Principled criterion (expected KL).
- Offers actionable design insights in case studies.
- Flexible, potentially reusable modeling framework.

Cons
- Limited validation; unclear predictive value for real inference.
- Heavy reliance on assumptions; sensitivity not fully explored.
- Missing computational and methodological details.
- Moderate novelty relative to existing identifiability/design literatures.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With stronger empirical validation, clearer computational details, and a tighter connection to optimal design and identifiability theory, this work could be impactful. As submitted, it does not yet meet the bar for rigor and demonstrated utility expected at NeurIPS.
