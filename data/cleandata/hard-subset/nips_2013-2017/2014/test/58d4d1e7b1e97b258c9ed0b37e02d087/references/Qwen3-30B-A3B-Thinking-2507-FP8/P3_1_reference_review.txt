

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors correctly identify that multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, undermining the validity of inferences about internal representations. While previous work has proposed ad hoc solutions to this degeneracy (e.g., theoretical constraints, experimental manipulations), the authors present the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing. They develop a flexible Bayesian observer model class and a method to explore identifiability landscapes, demonstrating its utility in two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

This work directly addresses a critical issue in the field that has been acknowledged in prior literature (e.g., [17, 18, 19]) but lacked systematic analysis. It builds on previous work in Bayesian modeling of perception [5, 9, 12, 14] while introducing a novel methodological contribution that could significantly improve experimental design in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively articulates the identifiability problem with a compelling mathematical demonstration (Eq. 1), showing why multiple model combinations can produce identical behavior. This is a critical contribution to the field.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The authors' approach using expected log-likelihood (Eq. 12) as a proxy for identifiability is theoretically well-grounded, leveraging KL divergence to measure differences between model predictions. The derivation is rigorous and properly accounts for experimental design.

3. **Practical Utility**: The framework is immediately applicable to experimental design. The case studies (Section 4) effectively demonstrate how the framework can identify problematic experimental designs (e.g., narrow loss functions worsening prior recovery) and suggest improvements (e.g., measuring motor parameters).

4. **Model Flexibility**: The proposed observer model class (Section 2) is well-constructed to balance flexibility and computational tractability, covering a broad range of sensory estimation tasks with a single parametrization.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow, appropriate mathematical notation, and effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Model Class**: While the authors claim generality, the model assumes a unidimensional stimulus space and specific parametric forms for sensory mapping and mixture models. The paper doesn't adequately address how this framework would extend to more complex perceptual tasks (e.g., multidimensional stimuli, multiple sensory cues) that are increasingly common in the field. The authors mention these extensions in Section 5 but don't demonstrate their feasibility.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of exploring the identifiability landscape, which could be prohibitive for high-dimensional parameter spaces. This is particularly relevant since the framework requires sampling from the approximate posterior density (Eq. 13) across multiple parameter dimensions.

3. **Case Study Limitations**: The two case studies are well-executed but limited in scope. More diverse examples (e.g., visual orientation perception, multi-cue integration) would better demonstrate the framework's generality. The speed perception case study also assumes a "natural" prior rather than one learned from experimental distribution, which might not reflect typical experimental conditions.

4. **Practical Implementation Guidance**: While the framework is theoretically sound, the paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how researchers should implement this in practice. For instance, how should researchers choose the reference observer θ*? What constitutes a "sufficient" level of identifiability?

5. **Comparison to Existing Identifiability Methods**: The paper claims novelty in systematic identifiability analysis but doesn't sufficiently contrast with existing methods (e.g., [23]). A brief comparison would strengthen the argument for the framework's unique contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of computational modeling of perception. The framework addresses a genuine and important problem with practical implications for experimental design. While the scope is somewhat limited to unidimensional estimation tasks, the core methodology is sound and could be extended to broader applications.

The paper's greatest strength is its practical utility: it provides researchers with a tool to evaluate whether their experimental design can support the recovery of specific model components before conducting expensive experiments. This could prevent wasted resources on experiments that cannot yield reliable inferences about internal representations.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a valuable methodological contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception. The framework is well-developed, theoretically sound, and practically useful. While the case studies could be expanded, the core contribution is significant and would benefit the entire field. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (quality), well-written (clarity), presents a novel approach to a well-known problem (originality), and has clear implications for experimental design in perception research (significance).

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following points:
1. Briefly discuss computational requirements for implementing the framework
2. Expand the discussion of model limitations, particularly regarding extensions to more complex perceptual tasks
3. Add a short comparison to existing identifiability methods in the field
4. Provide more concrete guidance on implementing the framework in practice

The paper provides a valuable tool for the field, and I believe it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
