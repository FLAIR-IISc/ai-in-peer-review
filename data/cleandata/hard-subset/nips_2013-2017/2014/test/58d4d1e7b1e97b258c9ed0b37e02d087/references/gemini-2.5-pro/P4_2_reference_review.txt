### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a computational framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors highlight a well-known degeneracy in these models: identical behavioral data can be produced by multiple combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, and loss functions). This non-identifiability poses a significant challenge to the goal of recovering an observer's mental representations from behavior. While this issue has been previously acknowledged and addressed through various experimental constraints, the authors argue that there has been no systematic method to evaluate the effectiveness of these constraints before conducting an experiment.

The core contribution is a two-part framework to address this gap. First, the authors develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. This model uses parametric forms, such as mixtures of Gaussians, to represent the sensory likelihood, prior, and loss function, allowing it to capture a wide range of observer strategies. Crucially, the model's structure permits a fast, semi-analytical computation of the observer's predicted response distribution.

Second, the paper proposes a method to quantify the confusability between a "ground truth" reference model (θ*) and other models (θ) in parameter space. Instead of relying on computationally intensive simulations of many datasets, the authors derive a metric based on the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model. This expectation is shown to be directly related to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions predicted by the reference and candidate models. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior density" derived from this metric using MCMC, the framework allows for an efficient exploration of the regions in parameter space that are practically non-identifiable from the reference model under a given experimental design.

The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two case studies based on prior work. The first, on interval timing, shows how the framework can be used to compare different experimental designs and reveals that recovering the observer's prior is challenging. It provides a non-obvious prediction that encouraging a MAP-like loss function could worsen identifiability. The second case study, on speed perception, re-examines the inference of a "slow-speed prior" and concludes that, in contrast to the timing task, the recovery of both the prior and noise characteristics is theoretically robust.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental and pervasive problem in computational modeling of cognition. While non-identifiability is a known issue (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2001; Jones & Love, 2011), this work provides the first systematic, quantitative, and *a priori* framework for analyzing it in the context of Bayesian perceptual models. This moves the field beyond post-hoc justifications or qualitative arguments, offering a principled tool to guide the design of more informative experiments. The potential impact is high, as it could improve the reliability of inferences made about latent cognitive variables.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed methodology is technically sound and well-conceived. The observer model is thoughtfully constructed to be both flexible (using mixtures of Gaussians and parametric sensory maps) and computationally tractable, which is essential for the feasibility of the analysis. The core idea of using the expected log-likelihood, and linking it to the KL divergence, is an elegant and efficient solution that avoids the brute-force approach of simulating numerous datasets.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors provide a concise explanation of the non-identifiability problem, followed by a step-by-step description of their model and analysis method. The case studies are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the framework's practical utility, yielding concrete and sometimes counter-intuitive insights into experimental design.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Model Class:** The framework's conclusions are conditional on the specific, albeit flexible, class of observer models defined by the authors. The parametric forms chosen for the sensory mapping, prior, likelihood, and loss function are assumptions. The analysis assesses identifiability *within* this class but does not address the possibility of structural mismatch, where the true observer's internal representations might have a functional form not well-captured by the model. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the potential sensitivity of the conclusions to these structural assumptions.

2.  **Details of Implementation and Task Equivalence:** Some important details are either omitted or not fully justified in the main text. For instance, the second case study re-examines a finding from a 2AFC task (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006) by converting it into an "equivalent estimation task." This conversion is a critical step, as the identifiability properties of 2AFC and estimation paradigms can be very different. The paper provides little justification or detail on how this equivalence was established, which could impact the validity of the conclusions drawn for that case study. Furthermore, the practical computation of the KL divergence between the complex response distributions (Eq. 9) is not detailed.

3.  **Limited Scope:** The current framework is developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of psychophysical research, key areas where identifiability is also a major concern, such as discrete-choice paradigms (e.g., 2AFC) and multi-cue integration studies, are not covered. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the challenges involved in extending the framework to these other domains.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses a critical and foundational methodological problem in a large subfield of computational neuroscience and cognitive science.
*   The proposed framework is novel, principled, and offers significant practical utility for designing more robust experiments.
*   The technical approach is sound, and the case studies effectively demonstrate the power of the method.
*   The topic is highly relevant to the NIPS community, bridging machine learning methods (Bayesian modeling, MCMC) with scientific applications (neuroscience, perception).

**Arguments against:**
*   The analysis is constrained by the assumptions of the chosen model class, and the robustness of the findings to model mismatch is unexplored.
*   The treatment of the speed perception case study, involving a conversion from a 2AFC task, lacks sufficient detail and justification.
*   The scope is currently restricted to a specific type of experimental paradigm.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant and original contribution. The framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis is a valuable tool that could have a substantial impact on how model-based cognitive science is conducted. The weaknesses, while notable, primarily relate to the scope and the need for additional clarification rather than fundamental flaws in the core methodology. The strengths decisively outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance. Minor revisions to clarify the task equivalence in the second case study and to discuss the limitations imposed by the model class would further improve the manuscript.
