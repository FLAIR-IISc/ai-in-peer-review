This paper proposes a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue given the increasing reliance on these models to infer internal representations from behavior. The authors correctly identify the inherent degeneracy in inverting Bayesian models – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavioral predictions. They present a method for *a priori* assessing identifiability and demonstrate its application to time and speed perception tasks. The work builds on established concepts in Bayesian modeling and statistical inference, addressing a long-standing concern within the field.

The strengths of this paper lie in its formalization of a known problem. The authors move beyond simply acknowledging the non-identifiability issue and attempt to provide a practical methodology for evaluating it. The proposed framework appears mathematically sound, extending previous work to a more general class of observer models. The application to both time and speed perception, while not groundbreaking in terms of new empirical findings, serves as a useful illustration of the framework's capabilities. The connection to experimental design – using identifiability analysis to inform better experiments – is a potentially valuable contribution.

However, I have significant reservations about the paper's overall significance and its readiness for NIPS. While the framework *is* a step forward, its practical impact remains unclear. The authors rely heavily on theoretical analysis and simulation. The "results" section essentially validates the framework by showing it behaves as expected – identifying known issues in existing literature (e.g., the difficulty of inferring loss functions). There is a lack of novel empirical validation; the framework isn't used to uncover genuinely unexpected or counterintuitive results. The reliance on a specific, relatively complex observer model (Eqs. 2-8) feels limiting. The framework's generality is claimed, but the implementation seems tightly coupled to this particular model structure. This raises concerns about its applicability to more diverse perceptual tasks or different modeling approaches.

Furthermore, the framework’s computational cost is not adequately addressed. While the authors mention efficiency, the MCMC sampling step for approximate posterior density estimation (Eq. 13) could be computationally prohibitive for more complex models or larger parameter spaces. A thorough discussion of scalability and computational limitations is missing. The sensitivity analysis of the parameters – while showing how identifiability changes with certain experimental setups – does not provide a clear recipe for researchers to avoid identifiability issues. What constitutes "sufficient" identifiability? The paper lacks concrete guidance on this. The claim that the framework can “rank” experimental designs feels overly strong without a more rigorous metric or analysis. The examples chosen, while relevant, feel somewhat contrived and don’t represent the full spectrum of challenges faced in perceptual inference.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important theoretical problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Presents a mathematically sound framework for assessing identifiability.
*   Provides a potentially useful tool for informing experimental design.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks novel empirical validation; primarily a theoretical contribution.
*   The framework’s generality and scalability are questionable.
*   The practical impact and usefulness for researchers remain unclear.
*   The computational cost is not adequately addressed.
*   The connection to existing literature is not always strongly articulated.

**Overall assessment:** I believe this paper, in its current form, is below the threshold for acceptance at NIPS. While the topic is relevant and the authors demonstrate technical competence, the lack of novelty, empirical validation, and a clear path to practical application outweigh the strengths. The paper feels more suited to a specialized journal in cognitive science or computational perception than a conference focused on cutting-edge machine learning and neural computation. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to consider further developing the framework, perhaps by applying it to real-world datasets and demonstrating its ability to resolve previously intractable problems in perceptual inference. A more detailed discussion of computational limitations and scalability would also be beneficial.
