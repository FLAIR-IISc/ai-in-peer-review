- Paper addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models, showing multiple internal-model combinations can produce identical behavior.  
- Proposes a framework to analyze identifiability a priori by quantifying expected support for models when data come from a reference model.  
- Uses KL divergence to measure identifiability and MCMC sampling to explore parameter space.  
- Applies framework to two perceptual tasks (temporal interval estimation and speed perception) to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Provides rigorous mathematical derivations (notably equation 12) and a well-justified observer model building on prior work.  
- Case studies are well-designed and show how the framework can guide experimental design and reveal parameter confounds.  
- Authors acknowledge framework limitations, including applicability to a specific class of models and dependence on the reference model.  
- Supplementary material contains sufficient detail for replication.  
- Paper could strengthen theoretical grounding by more explicitly comparing to existing identifiability methods [22,23].  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized presentation with a logically constructed observer model and effective figures.  
- Would benefit from a more explicit upfront statement of limitations.  
- Original contribution: focuses specifically on identifiability in perceptual Bayesian models and adapts general identifiability ideas to this domain.  
- Differentiates clearly from prior Bayesian perception work and fills a recognized methodological gap.  
- Significant impact: tool for evaluating and improving experimental designs, with implications for interpreting many existing studies.  
- Reveals non-intuitive experimental-design insights (e.g., narrow loss functions can worsen identifiability in interval timing).  
- Strengths include addressing a fundamental gap, theoretical soundness, computational efficiency, practical case studies, and clear writing.  
- Weaknesses include restriction to one-dimensional stimulus estimation, reliance on a known reference model, limited case-study diversity, and incomplete comparison to alternative identifiability approaches.  
- Recommendation: accept at NeurIPS — solid, valuable contribution with immediate applicability and potential for broader extension.
