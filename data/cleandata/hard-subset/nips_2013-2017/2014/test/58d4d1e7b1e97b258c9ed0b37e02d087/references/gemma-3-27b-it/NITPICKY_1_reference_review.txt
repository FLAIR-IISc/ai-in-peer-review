This paper presents a framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue given the increasing reliance on these models for understanding cognitive processes. The authors correctly identify the inherent degeneracy in inferring priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavior and propose a method to *a priori* assess identifiability based on Kullback-Leibler divergence. They apply this framework to two established psychophysical scenarios – time and speed perception.

The motivation is well-articulated, and the problem of non-identifiability is clearly explained. The proposed observer model is a commendable attempt at generality, incorporating several key elements of perceptual inference within a single parametrization. The framework’s potential to inform experimental design *before* data collection is a significant strength.

However, despite these positive aspects, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, both substantial and minor, that ultimately lead me to recommend rejection. My concerns range from a lack of rigorous justification for modeling choices to inconsistencies in notation and a disconcerting reliance on “convenience” throughout the derivations. 

Firstly, the mathematical derivations, while presented, lack sufficient explanation. Several steps, particularly within the supplementary material, are asserted rather than demonstrated. For example, the justification for choosing the specific parametric form of the sensory map (Eq. 2) – approximating both Weber-Fechner and Stevens' law – feels hand-wavy and relies on undocumented observations. Similarly, the claim of a maximum-entropy approach for defining the measurement distribution lacks detail. What constraints are truly being maximized? The authors simply state they are fixing the first four moments, which is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a unique maximum-entropy distribution.

Secondly, the choice of a two-Gaussian mixture for both the likelihood and the loss function, while simplifying computations, feels arbitrarily constrained. The authors acknowledge this, but the justification (“allows for a fast numerical solution”) prioritizes computational ease over theoretical grounding. A sensitivity analysis to explore the impact of using more complex distributions is absent. Further, the assertion that the loss function is well-behaved (smooth, unique minimum) is not universally true for all parameter settings within the Gaussian mixture framework.

Thirdly, the application to time perception reveals a concerning result: the experimenter attempting to impose a narrower loss function (MAP) *worsens* the quality of inference. This counterintuitive finding isn’t adequately explored. The authors attribute it to the low identifiability of σ`, but provide little mechanistic explanation. This lack of critical analysis weakens the paper’s conclusions.

Fourthly, the notation is inconsistent. For instance, 't' represents both a variable in internal measurement space and the time stimulus. While context sometimes clarifies the meaning, it introduces unnecessary ambiguity. The same holds for the use of 's' to represent both the stimulus and the stimulus magnitude.

Fifthly, and perhaps most critically, the “efficient” method for exploring identifiability relies heavily on approximations and simplifying assumptions. The use of the KL divergence as a proxy for posterior probability is reasonable, but the reliance on the approximate expected posterior (Eq. 13) and subsequent MCMC sampling lacks a thorough discussion of convergence diagnostics and potential biases. The validity of this approximation is not rigorously established. The paper repeatedly states “for computational convenience” as justification for choices, suggesting a prioritization of tractability over methodological rigor.

Finally, there are numerous minor flaws: the statement that the loss function is not a distribution, yet parametrized using statistics of a corresponding distribution, is confusing; the definition of θ (Eq. 10) lacks clarity regarding the organization of parameters; the references are not consistently formatted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem of identifiability in Bayesian models is crucial and timely.
*   The framework provides a potential tool for *a priori* experimental design.
*   The model itself is a reasonable attempt at generality.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous mathematical justification for key modeling choices.
*   Arbitrary constraints (e.g., two-Gaussian mixtures) with insufficient exploration of alternatives.
*   Counterintuitive results (e.g., MAP loss worsening inference) without adequate explanation.
*   Inconsistent notation and a reliance on “convenience” over methodological rigor.
*   Insufficient discussion of convergence and biases in the MCMC sampling.
*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and notation.



In conclusion, while the authors address an important problem and offer a potentially valuable framework, the numerous methodological shortcomings and the lack of rigorous justification ultimately outweigh the positive contributions. The paper reads more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and polished piece of work ready for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection and encourage the authors to address these concerns before resubmission.
