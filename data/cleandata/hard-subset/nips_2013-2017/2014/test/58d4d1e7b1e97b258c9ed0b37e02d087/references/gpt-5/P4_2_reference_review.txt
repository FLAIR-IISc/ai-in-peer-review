Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. It introduces a flexible, parametric observer model for estimation tasks with a one-dimensional stimulus, decomposed into sensation, perception, decision, and response stages. Key components include: (i) a parametric sensory mapping f(s) that renders noise approximately stationary in an internal space; (ii) a sensory likelihood modeled as a two-component Gaussian mixture with parameters matched to the first four moments under a maximum-entropy prescription; (iii) a prior represented as a mixture of Gaussians on a uniform lattice in internal space; (iv) a smooth loss function modeled as an inverted mixture of Gaussians; (v) lapse probability and motor/reporting noise. The observable response density is computed by marginalizing over latent variables.

To assess identifiability a priori, the authors derive the expected log-likelihood of a candidate parameter vector θ when data are generated by a reference observer θ∗. The result is proportional to minus the expected KL divergence between response distributions presp(r|s; θ∗) and presp(r|s; θ), weighted by the experimental stimulus distribution and number of trials. They then define E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp(⟨log Pr(D|θ)⟩), sample it via MCMC, and use the resulting distributions (e.g., over prior parameters) to judge practical identifiability and to compare experimental designs.

Two case studies are presented: interval timing (estimating priors under different task designs) and visual speed perception (recovering a slow-speed prior exponent and noise characteristics). The timing example highlights confusability between sensory and motor noise and shows that constraining motor parameters improves recovery of priors, while encouraging near-MAP losses can worsen reconstruction. The speed example shows robust recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent across conditions.

This work is related to identifiability and model distinguishability analyses in cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung, 2004) and to Bayesian experimental design that uses information-theoretic criteria. It builds on Bayesian observer modeling in perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, Jazayeri & Shadlen), and “cognitive tomography” approaches to recovering internal representations, but focuses specifically on a priori identifiability and experimental design within a unified, fast-evaluable observer class.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical problem: non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and losses in Bayesian observer models.
- Provides a coherent, general-purpose observer model with closed-form expressions that support efficient computation and exploration.
- Uses an analytically derived expected log-likelihood/KL criterion to avoid repeated dataset simulation, enabling scalable a priori analysis.
- Demonstrates utility on two canonical domains, yielding actionable design insights (e.g., benefits of measuring motor noise; risks of narrow loss functions in timing).
- Clearly links identifiability to experimental choices (stimulus distributions, number of trials, shared parameters).

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope relies on a specific parametric family (two-Gaussian mixtures for likelihood and loss; lattice Gaussian mixture prior). Identifiability conclusions may depend on this choice; sensitivity to model class complexity (e.g., number of components M) is not evaluated.
- The “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ∗) is a scoring function (expected log-likelihood) rather than a true posterior; discussion of its interpretation and limitations would be helpful (e.g., absence of parameter priors or complexity penalties).
- Limited details on MCMC (sampler, convergence diagnostics, mixing in high dimensions), despite potentially large parameter spaces (e.g., 15 mixture weights plus other parameters).
- Some notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., motor noise referred to as w1 in text vs ρ1 earlier; switches between qprior and pprior) that can impede clarity.
- The second case converts 2AFC to estimation; implications of this transformation for identifiability are not discussed in the main text.
- Assumptions like lapse drawing from the prior (rather than uniform) and loss depending only on t̂ − t should be examined for robustness; alternative choices could alter identifiability.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The derivation connecting expected log-likelihood and KL divergence is standard and sound. The observer model is well-specified and computationally tractable. However, empirical validation of the MCMC procedure (run times, diagnostics) and sensitivity analyses to model class choices are limited in the main text. The conclusions are plausible and consistent with known degeneracies (e.g., sensory vs motor noise) but would benefit from more systematic robustness checks.
- Clarity: Generally clear exposition of the modeling framework and identifiability metric. Some notational issues and reliance on the Supplementary for key details (maximum-entropy mixture fitting, optimization of expected loss) reduce self-containment. Figures are referenced appropriately but are essential for assessing the results.
- Originality: The combination of (i) a unified, flexible observer class with (ii) an analytically tractable expected support measure for (iii) a priori identifiability mapping and experimental design comparison appears novel within computational psychophysics. The information-theoretic core is known, but its packaging and application here are original and useful.
- Significance: The framework can materially influence experimental design and interpretation in studies that fit Bayesian observer models. Insights such as the value of measuring motor noise separately and potential pitfalls of narrow loss functions in timing are practically relevant. Broader impact will depend on generalization (e.g., to multi-cue, discrete-choice tasks) and availability of tools.

Suggestions for improvement
- Connect more explicitly to Bayesian optimal experimental design and expected information gain literature; discuss how Eq. 12 could guide stimulus selection (not just design comparison).
- Provide sensitivity analyses: effect of M (prior basis size), alternative likelihood/loss families, different lapse models, and misspecification (true observer outside model class).
- Include MCMC details and diagnostics; consider local approximations (e.g., Fisher information/Hessian) to complement global sampling and quantify parameter trade-offs.
- Clarify notational inconsistencies and define constraints on mixture weights (non-negativity, normalization).
- Discuss the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping’s implications for identifiability.
- If possible, release code to facilitate adoption.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a widely recognized but under-systematized problem with a practical, general, and efficient framework.
- Provides concrete, nontrivial design guidance validated on canonical tasks.
- Methodologically clean linkage between expected log-likelihood, KL divergence, and identifiability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on specific parametric choices without thorough sensitivity analysis may limit generality.
- Limited methodological details on sampling and computational performance.
- Some clarity and notation issues; empirical demonstrations remain theoretical/simulated and partially rely on task transformations.

Overall, this is a technically solid and useful contribution with clear relevance to the NeurIPS community working on Bayesian models of perception and experimental design, though it would benefit from expanded robustness analyses and clearer methodological details.
