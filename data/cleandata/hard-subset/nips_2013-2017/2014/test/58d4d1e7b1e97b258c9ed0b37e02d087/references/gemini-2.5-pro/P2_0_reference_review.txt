### **Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a fundamental and well-known problem in cognitive science and computational neuroscience: the non-identifiability of Bayesian observer models. The authors correctly point out that identical behavioral data can be produced by multiple combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, and loss functions), which undermines the goal of using these models to uncover the brain's internal computations. The central contribution is a novel computational framework to assess, *a priori*, the practical identifiability of a given Bayesian model within a specific experimental design.

The proposed framework consists of two main components:
1.  A flexible and computationally efficient class of parametric Bayesian observer models. This model class can represent a wide range of sensory mappings, priors, likelihoods, and loss functions (using mixtures of Gaussians and other functional forms) for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks.
2.  An efficient method for mapping the "identifiability landscape." Instead of relying on computationally expensive simulations, the authors propose using the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, given data generated by a "true" reference model. This quantity is shown to be proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from an approximate expected posterior density defined by this metric, one can visualize which parameters are well-constrained by the experimental design and which are degenerate.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two compelling case studies. First, in a time interval estimation task, the authors show how their framework can be used to compare different experimental designs. They find that a baseline design struggles to disentangle sensory and motor noise and that, counter-intuitively, forcing a MAP-like loss function can worsen the recovery of the prior. Second, they re-examine the classic "slow-speed prior" in visual perception and find that, within their framework, the key parameters (including the prior's shape and noise characteristics) are robustly identifiable, thus providing theoretical validation for previous empirical findings.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a critical and persistent challenge for the field of computational cognitive science. It is often acknowledged but rarely tackled with such a systematic and practical approach. This paper moves beyond simply stating the problem and provides a concrete tool for experimentalists and modelers to proactively design better experiments. This work has the potential to significantly improve the rigor and reliability of research using Bayesian observer models.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Methodology:** The core idea of using the KL divergence between response distributions as a proxy for the expected posterior is both clever and computationally efficient. It sidesteps the need for massive simulations of data generation and fitting, making the analysis of complex models tractable. The construction of the flexible observer model, which uses well-motivated parameterizations (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians for priors/likelihoods/losses), is a strong piece of engineering that enables the broader analytical framework.

3.  **Convincing and Insightful Case Studies:** The two applications are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the framework's power. The time perception example is particularly strong because it produces non-obvious recommendations for experimental design (e.g., the negative impact of a narrow loss function and the crucial benefit of independently measuring motor noise). This shows the framework is not just a confirmatory tool but can generate new, testable hypotheses about how to best probe internal representations. The speed perception example provides a valuable theoretical sanity check for a landmark finding in the field.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is built up in logical stages, the identifiability metric is well-justified, and the results are presented in a clear and interpretable manner.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer `θ*`:** The entire analysis is conditioned on a chosen "reference" or ground-truth observer model, `θ*`. The conclusions about which experimental design is better are therefore contingent on this choice being accurate. While the authors mention that the framework allows testing robustness by modifying `θ*`, the paper does not present any such sensitivity analysis. It would strengthen the claims if the authors could show, for example, that the ranking of experimental designs in the time perception task is robust to reasonable variations in the parameters of the reference observer.

2.  **Limited Scope of the Model:** The current framework is tailored to 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of psychophysical experiments, many influential studies use discrete-choice paradigms like two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). The authors address the speed perception study (originally 2AFC) by converting it to an "equivalent estimation task," but this crucial step is mentioned only briefly. The assumptions and validity of such a conversion warrant more detailed discussion, as the identifiability properties of 2AFC designs may differ substantially from estimation tasks. An extension of the framework to directly handle discrete-choice models would be a major future contribution.

3.  **Computational Details:** While the overall method is clear, some details of the implementation are left to the supplementary material. For example, a bit more intuition in the main text about the MCMC sampling from the "approximate expected posterior density" would be beneficial. It is not a standard posterior, and its properties and interpretation could be elaborated upon slightly more for the reader.

**Evaluation against NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations are solid, the model is well-conceived, and the claims are well-supported by the results of the case studies. The authors are careful to frame their contribution as a tool for *a priori* analysis, which is an honest and accurate representation.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is a model of clear scientific writing. The progression from problem to solution to application is logical and easy to follow. The figures are informative and support the main text effectively.
*   **Originality:** High. While the problem of non-identifiability is not new, the proposed framework for systematically and efficiently analyzing it *before* running an experiment is highly original. It synthesizes known concepts (Bayesian models, KL divergence) into a novel and powerful tool. This work is clearly distinct from previous literature that either points out the problem anecdotally or uses generic, post-hoc model comparison techniques.
*   **Significance:** Very High. This work addresses a deep, foundational issue in a field of great interest to the NIPS community (understanding intelligence, both human and artificial). It provides a practical methodology that could, and should, be adopted by researchers designing experiments to probe cognitive representations. It has the potential to shift the standard for rigor in computational modeling of perception and cognition.

**Conclusion**

This is an outstanding paper that makes a significant and timely contribution. It tackles a difficult and important problem with a method that is both theoretically principled and practically useful. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and its potential impact on the design and interpretation of psychophysical experiments is substantial. I strongly recommend acceptance.
