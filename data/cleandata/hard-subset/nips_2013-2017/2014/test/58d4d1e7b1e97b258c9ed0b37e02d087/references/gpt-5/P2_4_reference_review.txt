Summary
The paper addresses a central and persistent problem in Bayesian models of perception: non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data. The authors (i) formalize how degeneracy arises in decision-theoretic models, (ii) propose a flexible but computationally tractable class of 1D observer models (mixture-based parameterizations of sensory mapping, likelihood, priors, loss, lapses, and reporting noise), and (iii) introduce an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log marginal likelihood under a reference observer. This yields a quantity proportional to exp of a sum of KL divergences between response distributions that can be sampled via MCMC to map regions of practical non-identifiability. They apply the framework to interval timing (showing poor identifiability of priors unless motor noise is constrained; narrower loss functions can worsen inference) and speed perception (showing robust recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and connects several strands: ideal observer and BDT in perception (Geisler; Knill & Richards), prior/likelihood/loss inference in psychophysics (Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert; Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.), and model identifiability/model selection (Navarro, Pitt & Myung). The formalization of degeneracy echoes earlier critiques (Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis), but the main contribution here is a practical, task-specific framework to quantify and compare identifiability across experimental designs before data collection. The expected evidence criterion is closely related to classic KL-based model discrimination in optimal experimental design, and to cross-entropy risk.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation that the expected log-likelihood under the reference observer reduces to −Ntr times a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions is correct and appropriate for a priori model discrimination. The mixture parameterizations, the internal monotone mapping to stabilize noise, and the analytical tractability of the expected loss for Gaussian mixtures are sensible and well-motivated.
- Evidence: The two case studies are simulation-based, which is appropriate for an a priori framework. The interval timing analysis identifies specific parameter confounds (sensory vs motor noise; weak identifiability of loss width) and shows how fixing motor noise restores identifiability. The speed perception analysis shows recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent across conditions, and some biases (underestimation of s0 and σ̃High).
- Caveats: Conclusions depend on the assumed reference observer and the parametric class. Robustness checks (varying mixture complexity M, non-Gaussian/heavy-tailed noise, alternative loss shapes) are mentioned but not shown in depth. MCMC details, convergence diagnostics, and computational cost are not reported. The conversion of a 2AFC paradigm to an equivalent estimation task is plausible but not validated here; a 2AFC-native variant is deferred to future work.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and clear. The model is laid out in stages with explicit equations. However:
- The intuition behind Eq. 12 and the interpretation of E(θ|θ*) would benefit from a short paragraph connecting it to cross-entropy and KL-based model discrimination/experimental design.
- Provide a short algorithm box or pseudo-code for the identifiability-mapping procedure (inputs: θ*, stimuli, Ntr; output: MCMC samples, metrics).
- Summarize key MCMC settings (samplers, priors over θ, numbers of iterations, diagnostics) and runtime.
- Some key technical steps are deferred to the supplement (maximum-entropy construction of mixtures; analytic expected loss); a one- or two-sentence sketch would help.

Originality
- The identifiability problem is well-known, but this work’s contribution is a unified, efficient, and practical framework that:
  - Uses a flexible observer class supporting analytic or fast computation,
  - Formalizes a priori identifiability via expected evidence over datasets,
  - Compares experimental designs by the identifiability of target components.
- To my knowledge, this combination targeted at Bayesian perception with concrete design guidance is novel and fills a real gap.

Significance
- Practical impact: This framework can inform experimenters about which parameters are recoverable under specific designs, and how to modify tasks (e.g., fix motor noise, add conditions) to resolve confounds. The non-trivial finding that narrower loss functions can worsen prior identifiability is valuable.
- Scientific impact: Provides theoretical support for robustness of slow-speed prior inference; highlights questionable identifiability of loss width in interval timing unless independently constrained.
- Broader applicability: Extensible to multi-cue and discrete-choice tasks, though those extensions remain future work.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of degeneracy and concrete, task-relevant identifiability metric.
- Flexible, efficient observer model with analytical components enabling MCMC exploration.
- Actionable design insights demonstrated on two influential domains.
- Proper positioning relative to prior literature; balanced discussion of limitations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on parametric assumptions: Include robustness analyses where the true observer lies outside the model class (e.g., non-unimodal posterior, non-Gaussian loss, heavier tails), and report how conclusions change.
- Limited reporting of computational aspects: Provide sampler choice, priors on θ, initialization, convergence checks, and compute time to support claims of efficiency.
- Link to optimal experimental design: Discuss relationships to KL-optimal design and Bayesian design for model discrimination; consider suggesting a next step to actively optimize stimuli/conditions to maximize identifiability.
- 2AFC mapping: Either justify more thoroughly or add an experiment showing that identifiability conclusions carry over to a discrete-choice variant.
- Quantify sensitivity to Ntr, Nexp, and M (mixture lattice size), and report how results scale with these.
- Code/data: Sharing code would greatly increase impact and adoption.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a practical, general framework.
- Technically sound core; produces non-obvious, actionable insights for experimental design.
- Likely to be used by researchers modeling perception and designing psychophysics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical validation limited to simulations under the authors’ model class; robustness to misspecification not thoroughly studied.
- Missing computational details and limited connection to the broader experimental design literature.
- Extensions to common task formats (e.g., 2AFC) are not yet implemented.

Overall
I lean positive. With added robustness checks, computational details, and a clearer bridge to optimal design, this work would make a strong NeurIPS contribution.
