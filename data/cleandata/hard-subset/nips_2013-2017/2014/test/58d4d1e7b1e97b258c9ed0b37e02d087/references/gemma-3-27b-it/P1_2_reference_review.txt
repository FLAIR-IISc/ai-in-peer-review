## Peer Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical, often overlooked issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the inherent non-identifiability of these models. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can generate the same observed behavior, leading to ambiguity in inferring underlying cognitive representations. They present a novel framework for *a priori* analysis of identifiability, providing a method to systematically explore the parameter space and assess the impact of different experimental designs. The framework is implemented within a flexible class of Bayesian observer models covering a range of sensory estimation tasks. Two case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception – illustrate the utility of the framework, highlighting the importance of experimental constraints and revealing potential pitfalls in model inference.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a long history of using Bayesian models to explain perceptual phenomena (e.g., [1, 2, 3]). It acknowledges the growing trend of interpreting Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) as a process model, seeking to recover internal representations from behavior [4, 5, 6].  However, it directly addresses concerns raised by researchers highlighting the ill-posed nature of inverse problems in this context [17, 18, 19]. The work distinguishes itself by offering a systematic, rather than ad-hoc, approach to assessing identifiability, moving beyond simply imposing constraints (e.g., [12, 19]) and providing a means to *predict* the effectiveness of those constraints before data collection. The connection to broader literature on model identifiability in psychology [22, 23] is acknowledged, and the presented approach is tailored to the specific structure of Bayesian models of perception.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The paper tackles a core issue undermining the interpretability of Bayesian models – the ambiguity in recovering internal representations.  This is a critical contribution to the field.
* **Systematic Framework:** The proposed framework offers a principled and quantifiable approach to assess identifiability *a priori*. This is a significant advantage over purely post-hoc justifications for model choices.
* **Flexible Model Class:** The Bayesian observer model developed is general enough to encompass a broad range of sensory estimation tasks, enhancing the applicability of the framework. The use of parametric forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, while simplifying computation, allows for good approximation of diverse representations.
* **Practical Demonstrations:** The two case studies convincingly demonstrate the framework's utility and highlight the potential for improving experimental design. The results relating to the impact of loss function assumptions in time perception are particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical derivations are concise, and the supplementary material seems comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the framework is presented as efficient, exploring the full parameter space remains computationally demanding. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a more detailed discussion of computational scaling and practical limitations would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions of the Observer Model:**  The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, specific functional forms for priors and loss functions). While these are justified for tractability, they may limit the applicability of the framework to certain scenarios. A discussion of how violations of these assumptions might affect identifiability would be valuable.
* **Focus on Parameter Identifiability:** The framework focuses primarily on the identifiability of model *parameters*. While crucial, it doesn't directly address the identifiability of broader theoretical constructs or qualitative features of the internal representations.  
* **Limited Exploration of Experimental Designs:** The case studies present a relatively small number of experimental designs. A more comprehensive exploration of the design space could reveal additional insights.
* **Interpretation of KL Divergence:** The use of KL divergence as a measure of model support might be sensitive to the choice of distributions. Discussing the limitations of KL divergence and potentially exploring alternative metrics could strengthen the analysis.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The technical aspects of the paper are sound, and the analysis is well-supported. The framework is a significant contribution and tackles a challenging problem.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is clearly written and well-organized.  The mathematical notation is manageable.
* **Originality:** High. The framework presented represents a novel approach to a long-standing problem.
* **Significance:** High. This work has the potential to significantly impact the field by providing a principled way to evaluate and improve the interpretability of Bayesian models of perception. Researchers using Bayesian models will benefit from this framework.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation of Bayesian modeling in perception.
* Presents a novel and systematic framework for assessing identifiability.
* Demonstrates the framework's utility with compelling case studies.
* Is well-written and technically sound.
* Has the potential for significant impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost could be a barrier to widespread adoption.
* Reliance on simplifying assumptions within the observer model.
* Primarily focuses on parameter identifiability, not broader theoretical constructs.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field.  The proposed framework provides a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality and importance of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  I suggest the authors address the limitations regarding computational cost, model assumptions, and the scope of experimental design exploration. Further clarification on the interpretation of the KL divergence metric would also be helpful.
