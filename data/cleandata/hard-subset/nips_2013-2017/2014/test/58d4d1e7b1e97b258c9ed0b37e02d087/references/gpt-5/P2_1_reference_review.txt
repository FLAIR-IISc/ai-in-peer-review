Summary
The paper proposes an a priori framework to assess and improve identifiability of Bayesian observer models in 1D estimation tasks. The authors define a flexible observer class with: (i) a parametric sensory mapping f(s) that renders measurement noise approximately stationary in an internal space; (ii) measurement and internal likelihoods modeled as unimodal mixtures of two Gaussians, parameterized by variance, skewness, and kurtosis via moment constraints; (iii) priors represented as mixtures of Gaussians on a regular lattice; (iv) a smooth loss modeled as a mixture of inverted Gaussians; (v) lapse rate and motor noise. This yields tractable computation of response distributions. For identifiability, they derive the expected log-likelihood of a model θ under data generated by a “reference observer” θ*, which equals a constant minus the sum over stimuli of KL divergences between response distributions. They then sample from an “expected posterior” proportional to the exponentiated expected log-likelihood to map the identifiability landscape as a function of trial count and design choices. Two case studies examine interval timing and speed perception designs; the first reveals strong degeneracies (notably sensory vs motor noise, and weak identifiability of loss width) and shows that independent motor noise calibration markedly improves prior recovery; the second indicates that the slow-speed prior exponent and noise characteristics are reasonably identifiable under realistic designs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The observer class is carefully constructed to make inference and forward simulation efficient (mixture-of-Gaussians algebra; internal-space mapping). The expected log-likelihood derivation as a sum of KL divergences is correct and appropriate for “a priori” identifiability. The case studies are plausible and reveal non-trivial design trade-offs.
- Evidence/validation: Results are simulation-based (appropriate for an a priori design tool). The interval-timing findings (degeneracy between sensory and motor noise; poor identifiability of loss width) are supported by posterior landscape inspections; the speed-perception case recovers the slow-speed prior exponent and noise differences across contrast. However, quantitative details about MCMC (convergence diagnostics, computational cost, sensitivity to the number of prior basis components M) and robustness across multiple θ* are missing. The “maximum-entropy” description for setting mixture parameters is somewhat imprecise: maximum-entropy with fixed moments does not generally yield Gaussian mixtures; here it seems used as a heuristic to fix remaining degrees of freedom—this should be clarified.
- Limitations acknowledged: The authors note the dependence on θ*, the 1D restriction, and that 2AFC requires adaptation. They also flag that narrow losses (MAP-like) can harm prior identifiability, and that independent motor measurements help—usefully candid.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The modeling pipeline and parameterization are clear, and the identifiability objective is well-motivated. Notation alternates between p and q; a concise notation table would help. Several key implementation details and derivations are deferred to the supplement (e.g., “maximum-entropy” mixture construction, efficient argmin for the expected loss), which may hinder reproducibility if not provided with code. Figures are referenced to make arguments but are essential to fully judge conclusions.

Originality
- The identity relating expected log-likelihood and KL divergences is standard, and the goal of assessing model distinguishability has precedents in psychology/cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, and Myung) and in optimal experimental design/information gain. The novelty here lies in (i) assembling a flexible, computationally efficient observer class that captures priors, likelihoods, loss, lapses, and motor noise within one parametrization; and (ii) deploying an MCMC-based exploration of the expected-identifiability landscape to compare concrete psychophysical designs. This is a useful and practical synthesis, though the paper should better situate itself relative to Bayesian optimal experimental design (e.g., expected information gain) and Fisher-information-based identifiability.

Significance
- The framework addresses a real and pressing issue: non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception and the risk of drawing unwarranted conclusions about internal representations. The ability to compare designs a priori and highlight which parameters (e.g., motor noise, loss width) threaten identifiability is practically valuable to experimenters. The case studies produce actionable guidance (e.g., independently measure motor noise; be cautious interpreting loss parameters in interval timing; robustness of slow-speed prior inference). Given the broad use of BDT models, this can influence best practices.

Strengths
- Clear, principled identifiability objective with an efficient computational instantiation.
- Flexible observer class that unifies many modeling choices and admits fast forward models.
- Insightful case studies with non-obvious conclusions (narrow loss can hurt prior recovery; motor calibration helps).
- Emphasis on experimental design implications.

Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of connections to—and differences from—optimal experimental design and information-theoretic metrics beyond expected log-likelihood.
- Reliance on a single θ* per scenario; limited exploration of robustness across observers.
- “Maximum-entropy” terminology for Gaussian mixtures is potentially misleading; clarify method.
- 2AFC-to-estimation mapping is assumed; details and caveats are not fully provided.
- Missing practical details: computational cost, MCMC diagnostics, code availability.
- Reference list appears incomplete (years/pages missing).

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the approach with respect to expected information gain, D-optimal design, and Fisher information; discuss why the chosen objective is suitable here.
- Provide sensitivity analyses across multiple θ* and prior basis size M.
- Clarify the mixture-construction procedure and the role of “maximum-entropy.”
- Detail the 2AFC-to-estimation conversion assumptions and potential biases.
- Add computational benchmarking and convergence diagnostics; release code.
- Fix and complete the references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-served need with a practical, usable framework.
- Technically sound and thoughtfully designed observer class enabling tractability.
- Produces actionable design insights in canonical tasks.
- Likely to be used by researchers designing Bayesian psychophysics experiments.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual core (expected log-likelihood/KL) is known; novelty is more in packaging and application.
- Some methodological claims (maximum-entropy mixtures) and practical details are underspecified.
- Evaluation confined to simulations with limited robustness checks; no real-data validation.

Overall recommendation: Accept (weak accept). The contribution is solid and useful to the NeurIPS community interested in computational cognitive science and experimental design, with room to strengthen positioning and technical clarity.
