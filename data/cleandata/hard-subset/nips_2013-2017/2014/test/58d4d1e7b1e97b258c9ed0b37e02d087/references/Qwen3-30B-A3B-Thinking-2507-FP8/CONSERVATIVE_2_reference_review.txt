

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses an important issue in cognitive science: the non-identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral data. The authors propose a framework for systematically investigating model identifiability a priori, which they demonstrate through two case studies in temporal and speed perception.

The paper builds on established concepts in Bayesian inference and model identifiability. The core idea of using KL divergence to quantify model distinguishability (Equation 12) is not novel - it's a standard technique in model comparison (see Spiegelhalter et al., 2002, for similar approaches in Bayesian model selection). The authors' contribution lies in applying this established technique to the specific problem of Bayesian models of perception, which has been a topic of interest in cognitive science for decades.

**Strengths:** The paper identifies a genuine problem in the field that has been noted in the literature (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2002; Jones & Love, 2011). The proposed framework is theoretically sound, and the case studies provide concrete illustrations of how the framework can be applied to practical problems. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of their approach and the assumptions required for their framework to work.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weakness is its lack of empirical validation against established baselines. The authors present a theoretical framework but provide minimal evidence that this approach would actually improve experimental design in practice. While they demonstrate the framework on two case studies, these are hypothetical simulations rather than real empirical tests. The paper does not compare their approach to existing methods for addressing identifiability issues in cognitive science, such as those proposed by Navarro et al. (2004) or Bowers & Davis (2012).

The Bayesian observer model proposed in Section 2 is overly complex without sufficient justification. The authors claim it's "flexible and efficient," but the model has 20+ parameters (Equation 10), making it difficult to interpret and prone to overfitting. For instance, the sensory mapping function (Equation 2) and the mixture of Gaussians for likelihood and loss functions appear to add unnecessary complexity without clear evidence that they are required for the problem at hand. This complexity undermines the paper's claim of efficiency.

The paper also makes several untested assumptions about the reference observer θ* in their case studies. In the time perception example, they assume a specific form for the prior (smoothed uniform distribution) without justification for why this is the "typical" observer. The speed perception example similarly relies on assumptions about the prior that are not validated against actual human data.

Most critically, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims about the effectiveness of the proposed framework. The authors state that "our framework can be used to rank candidate experimental designs," but they provide only theoretical arguments without demonstrating that their method would actually improve experimental outcomes in real-world scenarios. The paper would benefit from a direct comparison between the experimental designs recommended by their framework and those used in actual published studies.

The paper also fails to adequately address how this framework would be adopted by researchers in cognitive science. The proposed method requires sophisticated Bayesian modeling expertise, which is not common among experimental psychologists who study perception. The paper does not provide practical guidance for implementing this framework in standard experimental workflows.

While the problem addressed is important, the paper's approach is not sufficiently novel or validated. The framework is built upon established statistical techniques without providing substantial new insights or evidence that it would be practically useful for the target audience of cognitive scientists. The authors' claim that their method "allows a modeller to perform a systematic, a priori investigation of identifiability" is overstated - the same could be said for many existing model comparison approaches.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a legitimate problem in cognitive science, it presents an unproven approach that lacks sufficient empirical validation against established baselines. The framework appears to be a straightforward application of existing statistical techniques to a specific domain, without demonstrating significant advantages over current practice. The paper would benefit from extensive empirical validation in real experimental contexts before being considered for publication at NIPS.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
*Pro:* Identifies an important problem in cognitive science, theoretically sound framework, provides concrete case studies.
*Con:* Lack of empirical validation, overly complex model without justification, untested assumptions, limited comparison to existing methods, minimal practical guidance for adoption.
