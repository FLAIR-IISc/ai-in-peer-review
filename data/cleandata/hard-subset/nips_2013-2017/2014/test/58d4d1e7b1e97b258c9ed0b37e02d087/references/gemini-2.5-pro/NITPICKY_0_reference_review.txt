### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the critical issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly note that different combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss function can produce identical observable behavior, posing a fundamental challenge to studies that aim to recover an observer's internal representations from data. The central contribution is a framework for performing an *a priori* identifiability analysis to guide experimental design. This framework has two main components: 1) a flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of representing a wide variety of sensory mappings, likelihoods, priors, and loss functions within a unified structure, and 2) a method to quantify the distinguishability between models by calculating the expected log-likelihood, which is shown to be proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the models' response distributions. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility with two case studies: one on interval timing, used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs for recovering priors, and another on speed perception, used to validate the recoverability of a "slow-speed prior."

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper tackles a significant and often underappreciated problem in computational modeling of cognition. The goal of providing a tool to assess model identifiability before data collection is highly laudable and could be of great value to the field. The proposed framework is ambitious, and the combination of a flexible model class with a principled identifiability metric is novel. The case studies are well-chosen to illustrate the potential applications of the method.

However, the paper is marred by a substantial number of technical imprecisions, questionable modeling choices, and missing methodological details that undermine confidence in the presented results and conclusions.

**Quality & Clarity:**

The paper's technical quality suffers from a lack of rigor and precision throughout.

1.  **Notational and Mathematical Imprecision:**
    *   The description of the fundamental degeneracy in the introduction is imprecise. The functions `φi(s)` multiply terms inside an integral that is a function of `s`, while the loss function `L(s, ŝ)` depends on both `s` and the optimization variable `ŝ`. The statement that `∏φi(s) = c` guarantees an identical solution to `argmin_ŝ` requires more careful justification than is provided.
    *   Mathematical notation is frequently non-standard or ambiguous. For instance, `d√...` in Eq. 2 is an unusual way to write a d-th root. In Eq. 6, the variance terms are written as `σ`1 2` instead of the standard `σ²_{`1}`. The definition of `a` in Eq. 4 is awkwardly placed. The notation for the prior is inconsistent: it is defined as `q_prior(t)` in internal space (Eq. 4), but then used as `q_prior(s*)` in task space in the lapse term (Eq. 7) without explicitly noting the change of variables. In the caption for Fig 2c, motor noise is referred to as `w₁`, while the parameter is defined as `ρ₁` in the text. These are minor points individually, but their cumulative effect is a sense of carelessness.

2.  **Unsupported Modeling Assumptions:**
    *   The model architecture, while flexible, is built on a series of strong and specific parametric assumptions. The sensory map `f(s)`, the mixture-of-two-Gaussians form for the likelihood (`p_meas`) and loss (`L`), and the fixed-grid mixture model for the prior (`q_prior`) are all convenient but restrictive choices. The "maximum-entropy" justification is mentioned but not sufficiently detailed to be convincing; for example, what does it mean to "locally" maximize entropy in this context?
    *   A significant inconsistency exists in the response stage. The entire perceptual and decision process is carefully mapped to an internal measurement space `t`, yet the final motor noise (Eq. 8) is modeled as Gaussian in the original task space `s`. This mixing of representational spaces is unmotivated and potentially problematic. A more consistent model would define all noise sources in the same internal space.
    *   The choice of M=15 for the prior basis functions is arbitrary and its impact on the results is not explored.

3.  **Missing Methodological Details:**
    *   The computation of the KL divergence in Eq. 12 is a critical step. The response distribution `p_resp(r|s;θ)` in Eq. 9 is a complex integral. It is not stated how the KL divergence, which itself is an integral over `r`, is computed. Is this done numerically? Via sampling? This omission makes the core of the analysis irreproducible.
    *   Similarly, the description of the MCMC sampling is entirely superficial. No details are provided on the algorithm used, the number of samples, or how convergence was assessed. These details are essential for evaluating the validity of the posterior distributions shown in the figures.

**Originality & Significance:**

The core idea is original and significant. A systematic, *a priori* approach to identifiability would be a major advance. However, the execution here is flawed. The conclusions drawn from the case studies seem fragile and heavily dependent on the specific, un-interrogated assumptions of the reference observer model `θ*`.

*   In the interval timing study, the conclusion that a MAP-like loss function worsens prior recovery is presented as a surprising insight into experimental design. However, it could equally be interpreted as a pathological interaction within the authors' specific model class, where the weakly identifiable loss parameter `σ` trades off against the prior's parameters. Without testing robustness to different model architectures, this conclusion is speculative.
*   The framework is only capable of testing identifiability *within* its own assumed parametric class. It cannot address the possibility that a true observer's internal representations have a fundamentally different functional form that is mimicked by a particular parameterization of the authors' model. This is a profound limitation on the generality of the conclusions.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, important, and difficult problem in computational cognitive science.
*   The proposed idea of an *a priori* identifiability analysis framework is novel and potentially very useful for experimental design.
*   The model is ambitious in its attempt to provide a single, flexible parametrization for a wide range of observer components.

**Con:**
*   The paper is rife with minor, but collectively significant, notational and mathematical imprecisions.
*   Key methodological details regarding the KL divergence computation and MCMC sampling are absent, rendering the work non-reproducible.
*   The model relies on a series of strong, specific, and insufficiently justified parametric assumptions.
*   The conclusions are derived from single instances of a "reference observer" and their robustness to the model's own structural assumptions is not tested, limiting their generality.

### Recommendation

While the central research question is important and the proposed direction is promising, the execution in this manuscript falls short of the standards required for publication at NIPS. The accumulation of minor technical flaws, missing methodological details, and strong, unexamined assumptions severely undermines the confidence one can place in the results. The work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete, rigorous scientific contribution. I recommend **rejection**, but I would strongly encourage the authors to refine the technical presentation, provide the missing methodological details, and perform a more thorough analysis of the framework's limitations and robustness to its own assumptions for a future submission.
