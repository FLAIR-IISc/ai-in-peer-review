This paper presents a novel and valuable framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The core problem it addresses is a well-known but often-underappreciated degeneracy in Bayesian modeling: a given pattern of behavior can be explained by multiple, distinct combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, and loss functions). This ambiguity undermines the primary goal of such models, which is to faithfully recover these latent cognitive components. The authors propose a principled and efficient method to map out this "identifiability landscape" for a given experimental design before data collection, allowing researchers to optimize their experiments for parameter recovery.

The proposed framework consists of two main components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model is comprehensive, encompassing stages of sensation (including nonlinear sensory mapping and non-Gaussian noise), perception (flexible priors represented as Gaussian mixtures), decision-making (flexible loss functions, also modeled as inverted Gaussian mixtures), and response (motor noise). The use of Gaussian mixtures for key components is particularly clever, as it allows for the approximation of arbitrary functional forms while retaining analytical tractability for fast computation of the expected loss. Second, to assess identifiability, the authors propose a metric based on the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model `θ` given data generated by a "true" reference model `θ*`. They show this can be computed efficiently via the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions predicted by the two models, avoiding the prohibitive cost of repeated data simulation. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" derived from this KL divergence, one can visualize which regions of the parameter space are indistinguishable from the reference model. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies from the literature on time perception and speed perception, yielding actionable insights into experimental design.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The paper tackles a fundamental, critical issue in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The proliferation of Bayesian observer models has been a major success story, but concerns about their identifiability [17, 18, 19] are significant. This work provides the first systematic, practical tool for researchers to proactively address these concerns, moving the field towards more robust and rigorous modeling practices.
2.  **Methodological Innovation:** The proposed method for assessing identifiability is both elegant and pragmatic. Bypassing explicit data simulation by using the KL divergence between response distributions is a key insight that makes the analysis computationally feasible. This connects the problem of model identifiability to the well-established information-theoretic concept of statistical distance.
3.  **Comprehensive Observer Model:** The parametric observer model is a significant contribution in its own right. It is remarkably flexible, able to capture many key features discussed in the literature, such as stimulus-dependent noise (via the sensory map `f(s)`), non-Gaussian sensory likelihoods, arbitrary priors, asymmetric loss functions, and lapses. This generality is crucial for the framework's applicability.
4.  **Actionable Insights from Case Studies:** The two applications are well-chosen and demonstrate the framework's power. The time perception example yields a non-obvious and important conclusion: under their model assumptions, encouraging a MAP-like strategy (narrow loss function) can actually *worsen* the recovery of the prior due to degeneracies with other parameters. The finding that independently measuring motor noise (MTR condition) provides the best recovery is an empirically testable recommendation. The validation of the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference [9] provides theoretical support for a landmark finding in the field.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer (`θ*`):** The entire analysis is conditioned on a chosen "ground truth" reference observer `θ*`. The conclusions about which experimental design is best are therefore dependent on the assumptions made about this observer. For example, the conclusion that a MAP-like loss function is detrimental relies on the specific degeneracies present in the chosen `θ*`. The framework tests identifiability *within* the specified model class but does not address the issue of model misspecification. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the conclusions to the choice of `θ*` would strengthen the paper. For instance, how would the results for the time perception task change if the true sensory noise was assumed to be more complex?
2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently developed for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a large class of psychophysical experiments, many influential studies use discrete choice paradigms, most notably two-alternative forced choice (2AFC). It is not immediately obvious how the framework would apply to 2AFC tasks, where the observable is a binary choice rather than a continuous response distribution. The authors briefly mention this as future work, but given the prevalence of 2AFC (e.g., in the original Stocker & Simoncelli paper they analyze), this is a significant limitation. The conversion of the 2AFC speed perception task to an "equivalent" estimation task is a critical step that deserves more justification.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While generally well-written, some technical details are quite dense. The parameterization of the likelihood and loss functions via their first four moments is clever, but the process of fixing the remaining degrees of freedom by maximizing entropy could be explained more intuitively. Similarly, the exact form of the analytical expression for the expected loss is deferred to the supplement but is central to the model's computational efficiency; a brief sketch of this result in the main text could be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The flexibility of the prior is determined by the number of mixture components, M. You set M=15. How was this value chosen, and how sensitive are the results to this hyperparameter? Is there a risk that M is too small to capture a complex prior, or so large it introduces its own identifiability issues?
*   Regarding the speed perception case study: Could you elaborate on the "equivalent estimation task" used to model the 2AFC experiment? The mapping from a comparative judgment (2AFC) to an absolute estimate is non-trivial, and any assumptions made here could impact the identifiability analysis. Could the identifiability properties in the original 2AFC paradigm be substantially different?

In summary, this is a high-quality, technically sound, and highly significant paper. It provides a novel, principled, and much-needed tool for a large community of researchers. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations in scope and opportunities for clarification, rather than fundamental flaws. The work represents a substantial step forward in improving the rigor of computational modeling in perception and cognition. It is likely to be widely read, cited, and used by others in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
