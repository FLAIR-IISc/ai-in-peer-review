

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that produce identical behavioral data. The authors present a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis, which could significantly improve the reliability of inferences about internal representations in perception research. The work builds on previous discussions of identifiability problems in Bayesian models (e.g., [19, 23]) but provides a general, computationally efficient method applicable to a wide range of psychophysical tasks, extending the approach beyond specific cases addressed in prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Problem Addressed:** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in a widely used modeling approach (Bayesian observer models) that has been noted in the literature but not systematically addressed ([17, 18, 19]). The authors clearly articulate why non-identifiability matters for understanding perceptual processes.

2. **Rigorous Framework Development:** The authors develop a mathematically sound framework based on KL-divergence of response distributions, which provides a theoretically grounded way to evaluate identifiability. The derivation of the expected log-likelihood (Eq. 12) is elegant and well-supported.

3. **Practical Demonstrations:** The two case studies (temporal interval estimation and speed perception) effectively demonstrate the framework's utility. The analysis of experimental design variations (e.g., BSL vs. SRT vs. MAP vs. MTR) provides concrete, non-obvious insights about how experimental choices affect identifiability.

4. **Computational Efficiency:** The authors successfully balance model flexibility with computational tractability, making the framework practical for researchers. The use of mixture models for sensory and loss functions is particularly well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the framework is demonstrated through simulation, the authors provide no empirical validation on real behavioral data. This limits the claim that the framework "can be used to improve experimental design" as it's currently presented. A comparison with actual experimental data would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** The two case studies focus on specific perceptual domains (time and speed perception). While these are well-chosen, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore whether the framework would generalize to other perceptual domains or more complex models (e.g., those involving multiple sensory cues).

3. **Parametric Assumptions:** The framework relies on specific parametric forms for the sensory map (Eq. 2), likelihood (Eq. 3), and loss function (Eq. 6). While these choices are well-motivated, the paper doesn't thoroughly address how the framework would need to be modified for different model structures.

4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim computational efficiency, they don't provide a detailed analysis of how the framework scales with model complexity or number of parameters, which would be valuable for potential users.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. **Include Empirical Validation:** Add a section demonstrating the framework's utility on real experimental data (even if from prior studies), showing how it could have improved experimental design or interpretation.

2. **Expand Case Studies:** Consider adding a brief discussion of how the framework might apply to other perceptual domains (e.g., visual orientation, spatial localization) to better establish the scope of applicability.

3. **Clarify Limitations:** More explicitly discuss the framework's limitations regarding model complexity. A table comparing the framework's capabilities against other identifiability methods (e.g., [23]) would be valuable.

4. **Enhance Practical Guidance:** Provide more concrete recommendations for experimental design based on the framework's output. For example, a flowchart or checklist for researchers to apply the framework to their specific tasks.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a theoretically strong and potentially impactful contribution to the field of perception science. The framework addresses a genuine methodological gap in Bayesian modeling of perception, and the case studies demonstrate its utility in a compelling way. The paper meets the quality standard with its rigorous mathematical foundation and clear presentation.

The significance of this work is high for the perception research community, as it directly addresses a fundamental limitation in a widely used modeling approach. If implemented, the framework could lead to more reliable inferences about internal representations in perception.

However, the lack of empirical validation on real data and the limited scope of the case studies represent important gaps. These limitations prevent the paper from making a definitive claim about the framework's utility in actual research settings.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, and the weaknesses identified can be addressed with relatively straightforward additions to the manuscript. The framework is sufficiently novel and well-developed to warrant publication at NeurIPS, especially given the importance of the problem it addresses.

The authors should prioritize adding empirical validation (even if limited to reanalysis of existing datasets) and expanding the discussion of the framework's applicability to broader contexts. These additions would strengthen the paper's impact and ensure it meets the high standards expected at NeurIPS.
