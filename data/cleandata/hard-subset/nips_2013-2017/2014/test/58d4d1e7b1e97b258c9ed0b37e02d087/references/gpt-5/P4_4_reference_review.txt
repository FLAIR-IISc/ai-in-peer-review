Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception: different combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss can yield indistinguishable behavior. Building on classical Bayesian Decision Theory and known equivalence issues (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung, and critiques in cognitive modeling), the authors propose an a priori framework to assess practical identifiability before running experiments. They introduce a flexible 1D estimation observer model: (i) a parametric sensory mapping f(s) to an internal space where noise is approximately translation-invariant; (ii) measurement noise modeled as a mixture of two Gaussians with moments (variance, skewness, kurtosis); (iii) priors represented as mixtures of fixed-width Gaussians over a lattice; (iv) a loss function modeled as a mixture of inverted Gaussians, with a lapse process; and (v) response noise in task space. A key analytic ingredient is computing the expected log-likelihood of a model θ under a generating model θ*, which reduces to a stimulus-frequency–weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions, scaled by the number of trials. They define a pseudo-posterior E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(expected log-likelihood) and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability.

Two case studies illustrate the framework. For interval timing (modeled after Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.), they investigate recovery of the prior under different designs (two ranges vs one range, MAP-like loss, known motor noise). They find poor identifiability under baseline; motor-sensory noise degeneracy harms prior recovery; known motor noise helps; and encouraging a narrow/MAP-like loss can degrade prior recovery. For speed perception (modeled after Stocker & Simoncelli), they translate a 2AFC paradigm into an equivalent estimation task and probe reconstruction of a heavy-tailed slow-speed prior and contrast-dependent noise. Here, both prior exponent and noise are recovered robustly across conditions, even when decoupling internal from external noise, supporting earlier inferences in that literature.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The expected support derivation (expected log-likelihood equals a constant minus Ntr times a sum of KL divergences) is standard and appropriate for assessing average discriminability under θ*. The observer class is internally coherent, and the analytic mixture structure expedites computation of expected loss. The two case studies are carefully set up with reasonable reference parameters and shared vs condition-specific constraints.
- Limitations: (a) The invariance argument in Eq. 1 relies on multiplicative transformations that preserve the product prior×likelihood×loss up to normalization; the precise conditions under which normalization and functional constraints hold (e.g., ensuring valid densities) should be made explicit. (b) The identifiability assessment uses a pseudo-posterior based on expected log-likelihood; the link to actual posterior concentration with finite datasets is not empirically validated. (c) Computational details (integration over r, discretization, MCMC settings/diagnostics, runtime, sensitivity to M, lattice spacing) are deferred or omitted. (d) The approximation of the 2AFC task by an estimation model is asserted but not demonstrated; a direct comparison would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized: model, metric, and applications are clearly delineated. The narrative ties conclusions to design choices (e.g., known motor noise, loss width). However, some expository elements could be improved:
  - Clarify how A and B are chosen in f(s) and discuss sensitivity to f(s) misspecification; justify the moment-based, maximum-entropy construction for the measurement and loss mixtures and provide the algorithmic procedure in the main text.
  - State constraints on the prior weights (wm ≥ 0, sum to one) and how they are sampled; describe how mixture unimodality is enforced.
  - Provide a brief derivation or intuition for the 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence and note any changes in identifiability it may induce.
  - Some references in the list are incomplete; ensure full bibliographic details.

Originality:
- The identifiability problem and use of KL-based expected support have precedents, but the combination of: a unified, computationally efficient observer class; an explicit E(θ|θ*) sampling procedure; and design-oriented case studies focused on priors/likelihood/loss in perception is a useful and, to this community, novel contribution. The non-obvious design insight regarding the detrimental effect of narrow loss functions (in this modeling context) is particularly interesting.

Significance:
- The framework is potentially impactful for designing psychophysics to estimate internal representations. The speed-perception case gives theoretical backing to a widely cited result. The interval-timing case provides concrete guidance (e.g., measure motor noise; be cautious about MAP-like feedback) and identifies weakly identifiable dimensions (e.g., loss width). Broader significance would increase with empirical validation and extensions to common paradigms (2AFC, multi-cue).

Pros:
- Addresses a central, under-examined issue (parameter/model non-identifiability) with a principled, quantitative approach.
- Flexible observer model enabling fast expected-loss computation; clear link between design variables and identifiability.
- Actionable findings in two canonical domains; highlights specific confounds and remedies.

Cons:
- Validation gap: no demonstration that E(θ|θ*) predicts actual parameter recovery from finite synthetic datasets using the intended fitting pipeline.
- Dependence on specific parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures; fixed-width prior basis; symmetric loss in t̂−t); generality not fully established.
- Limited methodological details and diagnostics; 2AFC equivalence assumed rather than shown.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a model-recovery study: generate datasets at several Ntr and fit with the full inference procedure to show correspondence between E(θ|θ*) and empirical recovery accuracy.
- Provide implementation details (integration grids, MCMC setup, constraints on wm, mixture unimodality checks) and sensitivity analyses (number of basis components M, choice of f(s)).
- Clarify the invariance conditions in Eq. 1 and the normalization steps, and give a concise derivation of the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping.
- Outline an information-theoretic design optimization (e.g., choose si and Pi to maximize expected KL separation of parameters of interest).

Overall, this is a solid, timely contribution with clear potential to influence experimental design in Bayesian perception modeling, pending additional validation and clarifications.
