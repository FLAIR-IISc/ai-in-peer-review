This paper presents a computational framework to assess the *a priori* practical identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception. The authors correctly identify a fundamental challenge in Bayesian cognitive science: multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover these latent "internal representations" from data. While this degeneracy is known, previous work has relied on task-specific constraints to address it, without a systematic way to know if these constraints are sufficient before running an experiment. The core contribution of this work is a principled and efficient method to do exactly that. The framework consists of two main components: 1) a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models that can capture a wide range of sensory processes, priors, and decision strategies; and 2) a metric, based on the expected Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of a posited "true" model and a candidate model, to quantify the expected statistical evidence for distinguishing between them for a given experimental design. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two well-chosen case studies from the literature on time and speed perception, showing how it can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs and validate the robustness of previous scientific claims.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and pervasive problem in computational modeling of cognition. The issue of model non-identifiability is often acknowledged but rarely tackled with such a systematic, a priori approach. This work moves beyond simply fitting models to proposing a tool to design better experiments for model-based inference. This is a significant methodological contribution that could have a substantial impact on how research in this area is conducted. The approach of using a flexible parametric model combined with an efficient KL-divergence-based metric for identifiability is novel and powerful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and cleverly designed. The parameterization of the model components (sensory mapping, likelihoods, priors, loss functions) as mixtures of Gaussians is a key strength. It provides the necessary flexibility to approximate a wide variety of functional forms while crucially retaining computational tractability, enabling fast calculation of the observer's expected behavior (Eq. 9) and the KL-divergence (Eq. 12). The derivation of the identifiability metric from the expected log-likelihood is principled and provides a more efficient alternative to computationally prohibitive simulation-based approaches.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction, establishing the problem of non-identifiability with a formal argument. The four-stage observer model is described systematically, and the rationale for each modeling choice is well-justified. The case studies are presented effectively, demonstrating how the framework can yield non-trivial insights into experimental design. For example, the finding that a MAP-like loss function could worsen prior recovery in the timing task is a compelling and non-obvious result.

4.  **Impactful Application:** The case studies are not mere toy examples; they engage with prominent findings in the perception literature. The analysis of the time perception task provides concrete, actionable advice for experimentalists (e.g., the high value of independently measuring motor noise). The re-examination of the slow-speed prior provides a novel theoretical validation for a classic result, strengthening the original conclusions. This demonstrates the framework's practical utility for both prospective experimental design and retrospective analysis of existing claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Sensitivity to the Reference Observer:** The entire analysis is conditioned on a "reference observer" θ*, which represents the assumed ground truth. The authors note this can be set from pilot data or educated guesses. However, the conclusions about the relative merits of different experimental designs might themselves be sensitive to this choice. A brief discussion on the robustness of the identifiability landscape to perturbations in θ* would strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, would the conclusion that the MTR design is superior hold if the true motor noise was significantly different from the assumed value?

2.  **Scope of the Model:** The framework, as presented, is tailored to one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant portion of psychophysics experiments, the authors themselves note that extensions to other common paradigms like discrete-choice (e.g., 2AFC) or multi-cue integration would be valuable. While the paper mentions these as future work, a short discussion of the potential challenges in such extensions (e.g., how the KL-divergence metric would apply to discrete responses) would be helpful for readers looking to build on this work.

3.  **Complexity of the Observer Model:** The flexibility of the proposed observer model is a strength, but it also comes with high parametric complexity (Eq. 10). This raises the question of whether some of the observed non-identifiability might be specific to this particular, very rich model class. It would be useful to briefly address whether a simpler, more constrained model might exhibit different identifiability properties, and how the framework could help in deciding the appropriate level of model complexity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, important, and often-overlooked problem in a major area of NIPS-relevant research (Bayesian models of cognition).
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and computationally efficient framework for a priori analysis of model identifiability.
*   The technical execution is strong, with a well-designed flexible model class that remains tractable.
*   The work has clear practical implications, providing a tool to improve experimental design and rigorously test the assumptions underlying model-based cognitive science.
*   The case studies are compelling and demonstrate the utility of the framework on real scientific problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis is contingent on the choice of a "true" reference observer, and the sensitivity to this choice is not fully explored.
*   The framework's current implementation is limited to 1D estimation tasks, though the core ideas are likely generalizable.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong methodological contribution to the field of computational cognitive science and machine learning. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem and the novelty and quality of the proposed solution, far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to scope and potential avenues for future exploration. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and likely to be highly influential for researchers using Bayesian models to understand perception and cognition. It is a clear candidate for acceptance.
