

# NIPS Peer Review

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors argue that without proper constraints, multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, making it impossible to reliably recover internal representations from data. They present a systematic framework for analyzing identifiability *a priori* through expected log-likelihood and KL divergence, applied to two well-known psychophysical tasks (time interval estimation and speed perception). This work builds on foundational NIPS contributions to Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Knill & Richards [2], Körding & Wolpert [7], Stocker & Simoncelli [9]) while addressing a significant gap in the methodology for interpreting these models.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a fundamental methodological issue that has plagued Bayesian modeling of perception for decades. The authors correctly identify that the "inverse problem" is ill-posed without constraints, and their framework provides a principled way to evaluate whether experimental designs can overcome this degeneracy.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The framework is built on solid theoretical foundations (Bayesian Decision Theory, maximum entropy principles). The derivation of Equation 12 (expected support for model θ) is particularly elegant, providing a tractable measure of identifiability that avoids the intractable sampling of all possible datasets.

3. **Practical Utility**: The case studies demonstrate the framework's utility in guiding experimental design. For time estimation, they show that assuming a narrower loss function (closer to MAP) can actually *worsen* prior recovery, a non-intuitive finding that would be difficult to discover without their framework. For speed perception, they validate previous findings about slow-speed priors.

4. **Well-Structured Contribution**: The four-stage observer model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) is logically organized and clearly explained. The parametrization is flexible yet computationally efficient, making it suitable for exploring the complex model space.

5. **Transparency**: The authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., the framework currently handles one-dimensional stimuli) and discuss how it could be extended.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Identifiability Methods**: While the paper cites [23] as a reference for identifiability analysis, it doesn't clearly distinguish how their approach differs from or improves upon existing methods. A brief comparison to other approaches (e.g., profile likelihood, Fisher information) would strengthen the originality claim.

2. **Implementation Details**: The MCMC sampling procedure (Equation 13) is mentioned but not sufficiently explained. For a computational paper, more details on how they implemented the sampling (e.g., proposal distributions, convergence diagnostics) would be valuable for reproducibility.

3. **Case Study Explanations**: The explanation for why a narrow loss function (MAP) worsens prior recovery in the time estimation task (Figure 2d) is somewhat superficial. A more detailed discussion of the degeneracy between loss width and sensory noise would strengthen this insight.

4. **Scope Limitations**: While the authors note that their framework handles one-dimensional stimuli, it's unclear how it would scale to more complex models (e.g., multidimensional stimuli or multiple cues). A brief discussion of these limitations would be helpful.

5. **NIPS Context**: The paper could better connect to recent NIPS work on Bayesian models (e.g., [11, 12, 14, 15, 16]). While they cite some relevant work, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how this work advances the field beyond previous NIPS contributions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that addresses an important methodological challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception. The framework is novel, well-motivated, and has clear practical value for experimental design. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility in revealing non-intuitive insights about experimental setups. While the paper has minor weaknesses in implementation details and contextualization within recent NIPS work, these do not detract from the core contribution.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a significant contribution to the methodology of Bayesian modeling in perception. It provides a much-needed systematic approach to a problem that has been acknowledged but not properly addressed in the literature. The framework has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of inferences drawn from Bayesian models in perception, which would benefit the broader computational cognitive science and neuroscience communities.

## Key Arguments for Acceptance

- Solves a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception (non-identifiability) that affects many existing studies
- Provides a theoretically sound, computationally efficient framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis
- Demonstrates practical utility through well-chosen case studies with non-obvious insights
- Well-written, logically structured, and transparent about limitations
- Has clear potential for impact on experimental design in psychophysics

## Key Arguments for Improvement (Before Acceptance)

- Clarify how this framework differs from existing identifiability analysis methods
- Provide more details about the MCMC implementation for reproducibility
- Deepen the explanation of the non-intuitive finding regarding narrow loss functions
- Explicitly connect to recent NIPS work on Bayesian models of perception

This paper represents a valuable methodological advance that would be highly relevant to the NIPS community, particularly those working at the intersection of Bayesian modeling, perception, and cognitive science. The framework has the potential to improve the reliability of inferences drawn from Bayesian models across multiple domains.
