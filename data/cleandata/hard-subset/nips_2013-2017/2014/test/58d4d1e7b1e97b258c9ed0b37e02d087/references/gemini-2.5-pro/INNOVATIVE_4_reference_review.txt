This paper addresses the critical and often-understated problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that since different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, the scientific conclusions drawn from fitting such models are potentially fragile. The main contribution is a novel and ambitious computational framework to assess, *a priori*, the identifiability of a model's parameters for a given experimental design. This is achieved by first constructing a highly flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of capturing a wide range of perceptual and decisional phenomena. Second, the authors propose an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood between a reference model and other models in the parameter space, which cleverly reduces to computing the KL divergence between their respective response distributions. The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two compelling case studies in time and speed perception, which show how it can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs and to provide theoretical validation for existing findings.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and significance. The problem of model degeneracy in Bayesian cognitive science is a well-known elephant in the room. While often acknowledged, it is typically addressed through simplifying assumptions or post-hoc arguments. This work represents a bold and much-needed attempt to move beyond this status quo by providing a principled, quantitative tool for *prospective* analysis. The idea of using a computational framework to perform *in silico* experiments to optimize the design of *in vivo* experiments is powerful and could have a substantial impact on the field. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method; it is a new conceptual and methodological direction for the field.

The technical implementation is also impressive. The unified observer model is thoughtfully constructed to be both flexible and computationally tractable. By parameterizing sensory maps, noise distributions, priors, and loss functions using flexible forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians), the framework can approximate a vast space of possible observer models. The derivation of an analytical expression for the expected loss and the use of KL divergence as an efficient proxy for model distinguishability are key enablers that make the exploration of this high-dimensional space feasible.

Finally, the case studies effectively demonstrate the practical value of the framework. The analysis of the time perception task yields non-obvious insights, such as the potentially detrimental effect of a MAP-like loss function on prior recovery and the critical importance of independently measuring motor noise. This provides a concrete example of how the framework can guide researchers toward more robust experimental designs. The validation of the slow-speed prior finding in the second case study shows that the framework can also be used to increase our confidence in previous scientific conclusions.

### Weaknesses

While the work is conceptually strong, there are a few points that could be further clarified or strengthened.

1.  **Dependence on Parametric Forms:** The framework's flexibility is built upon a series of specific parametric assumptions (e.g., the sensory map in Eq. 2, mixtures of two Gaussians for noise and loss, a basis of 15 Gaussians for the prior). The conclusions drawn from the framework are necessarily conditioned on this chosen model class. While the choices are reasonable, the paper would benefit from a discussion of their potential limitations. For instance, how sensitive are the identifiability landscapes to the number of mixture components or the choice of basis functions for the prior?

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Identifiability Metric:** The proposed metric, based on KL divergence, quantifies the distinguishability of models in the limit of infinite data. While the number of trials `N_tr` scales the resulting "expected posterior," the fundamental shape of the identifiability landscape is determined by the KL divergence. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the correspondence between this asymptotic measure and the practical identifiability one might encounter with realistic, finite datasets. A small simulation showing that the KL-based landscape predicts the posterior distributions recovered from a few simulated finite datasets could further bolster the authors' claims.

3.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. The authors mention that it can be extended, but the current scope is a limitation. For instance, the conversion of the 2AFC task from the speed perception study into an "equivalent estimation task" is mentioned without sufficient detail, leaving the reader to wonder about the assumptions and validity of this conversion.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is an excellent paper that introduces a highly novel and significant contribution to the field of computational modeling. It tackles a fundamental methodological problem with a creative and powerful solution. The proposed framework has the potential to become a standard tool for designing and validating psychophysical experiments that use Bayesian observer models. While there are minor weaknesses related to the specificity of the model assumptions and the asymptotic nature of the analysis, these are far outweighed by the boldness of the vision and the importance of the problem being solved. This work is thought-provoking, technically solid, and likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research. It is a clear accept.
