- Addresses the non-identifiability problem in Bayesian perceptual models and proposes testing identifiability a priori.
- Introduces a flexible Bayesian observer model class able to represent many psychophysical tasks.
- Proposes an efficient identifiability exploration method using KL divergence and expected log likelihood.
- Applies the framework to two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) to guide experimental design and parameter recovery.
- Provides rigorous mathematical derivations (notably Eqs. 11–13) and a sound theoretical foundation.
- Appropriately uses KL divergence as an identifiability measure and distinguishes theoretical vs. practical identifiability.
- Case studies are well-designed and demonstrate the framework’s utility and non-intuitive experimental implications.
- Clear, well-organized writing and effective figures (e.g., Figure 2); supplementary material supports replication.
- Novel contribution: systematically addresses identifiability specifically for Bayesian models of perception.
- Modeling choices (parametric sensory map, mixture models for likelihood and loss) balance flexibility and tractability.
- Significant potential impact across computational neuroscience, psychology, and cognitive science.
- Strengths: addresses an important problem, practical framework, validated by case studies, careful assumptions, clear presentation.
- Weaknesses: assumes a specific internal measurement-space structure which may limit applicability.
- Weaknesses: only two case studies—broader validation would strengthen claims.
- Weaknesses: does not fully tackle how to resolve identified degeneracies beyond ranking experimental designs.
- Weaknesses: computational complexity for very large parameter spaces is not explicitly discussed.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, significant contribution; recommend acceptance with minor suggestions to discuss computational complexity and add broader validation.
