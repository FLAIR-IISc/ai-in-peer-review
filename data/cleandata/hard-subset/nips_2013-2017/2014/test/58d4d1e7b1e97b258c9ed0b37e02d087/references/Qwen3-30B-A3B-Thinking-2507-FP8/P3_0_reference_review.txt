

# NIPS Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors demonstrate that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs, making it impossible to uniquely recover internal representations from data. They propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis that allows researchers to evaluate whether their experimental design can reliably recover model parameters before conducting costly experiments. The framework is implemented through a flexible Bayesian observer model and uses KL divergence to quantify the expected support for alternative models given a reference model.

The paper makes two case studies: one on temporal interval estimation and another on speed perception. In the first case, they show that standard experimental designs lead to poor recovery of priors due to parameter degeneracy, while in the second, they demonstrate that speed perception models are more robust to non-identifiability. The work builds on previous identifiability literature (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004) but applies it specifically to Bayesian models of perception with greater computational efficiency.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors clearly establish the theoretical basis for parameter non-identifiability in Bayesian models, and their framework provides a practical solution. The model specification is comprehensive yet computationally efficient, and the use of KL divergence to quantify expected model support is appropriate. The case studies are well-designed and demonstrate the framework's utility. 

One notable strength is the careful consideration of how different experimental designs affect identifiability. The analysis of the temporal interval estimation task reveals counterintuitive findings—such as how a narrower loss function (MAP assumption) actually degrades prior recovery—which would be difficult to discover without this framework. The authors also provide a useful diagnostic tool for identifying which parameters are causing degeneracy (e.g., sensory vs. motor noise in the temporal case).

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively explain the technical details of their framework, and the case studies are presented in a logical manner. The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively communicate the key findings. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper could be even clearer about the limitations of the framework—specifically, how it handles cases where non-identifiability cannot be resolved through experimental design alone.

## Originality

The paper's core contribution is not entirely novel in the methodological sense—it applies existing identifiability concepts to a specific domain—but it is highly original in its application to Bayesian models of perception. Previous work on model identifiability (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004) has not been systematically applied to this particular problem in the perceptual domain. The authors' specific implementation of a flexible observer model with computational efficiency for identifiability analysis is a significant contribution. The paper also makes a novel contribution by showing that identifiability concerns can have counterintuitive implications for experimental design (e.g., MAP assumption worsening recovery).

## Significance

This work addresses a fundamental problem that affects the validity of numerous Bayesian models in perception research. The framework has the potential to significantly improve experimental design in the field, preventing researchers from drawing unwarranted conclusions about internal representations. The case studies demonstrate concrete applications that could influence how researchers approach time perception and speed perception experiments. While the framework is currently limited to specific perceptual tasks, the authors suggest extensions to other domains, broadening its potential impact.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, but there are some limitations to consider. First, while the framework is theoretically sound, its computational cost might be prohibitive for complex models with many parameters. The authors acknowledge this in the supplementary materials but don't provide concrete benchmarks. Second, the case studies are limited to two specific perceptual tasks—extending the framework to more diverse tasks would strengthen the case for broader adoption. Third, the paper doesn't fully address how to resolve non-identifiability when it's detected, beyond suggesting better experimental designs.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently discuss how the framework relates to recent advances in Bayesian model selection (e.g., using marginal likelihoods for model comparison). While they acknowledge the use of log likelihood as a proxy, a more thorough discussion of this choice would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant problem in the field with a practical solution. The framework has the potential to improve the rigor of Bayesian modeling in perception research. The technical execution is sound, and the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility. While not groundbreaking in methodology, it represents an important application of existing concepts to a critical problem in the field.

**Pro**: Addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian models of perception; provides a practical framework for a priori identifiability analysis; case studies demonstrate clear utility; computationally efficient implementation.

**Con**: Limited to specific perceptual tasks; computational cost not fully addressed; doesn't provide complete solutions for non-identifiability cases.

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a much-needed tool for assessing the validity of Bayesian models of perception. It will help researchers design better experiments and draw more reliable conclusions from their data. The framework has the potential to become a standard tool in perceptual modeling, and the authors have made a compelling case for its utility.
