- Addresses the overlooked problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models and the ill-posed inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations.  
- Introduces a systematic framework to evaluate identifiability a priori using a flexible observer model class and information-theoretic measures.  
- Uses KL divergence and MCMC sampling to explore identifiability landscapes efficiently.  
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies (interval timing and speed perception) showing non-trivial, actionable insights for experimental design.  
- Theoretical foundations are rigorous, deriving expected log-likelihood as a proxy for identifiability (Eq. 12).  
- The proposed observer model is sufficiently flexible for many psychophysical tasks while remaining computationally tractable.  
- Clear presentation with well-organized figures and enough detail for expert replication.  
- MCMC-based exploration can be computationally expensive for high-dimensional models or complex designs.  
- Conclusions depend strongly on the assumed reference model (θ*), with limited analysis of sensitivity to mis-specification.  
- Case studies, though informative, are limited in scope and don't fully demonstrate generality across sensory domains.  
- Paper lacks concrete, practical implementation guidance for researchers applying the framework to specific paradigms.  
- Limited discussion connecting behavioral identifiability issues to neural representations or computational neuroscience.  
- Overall, a strong, significant methodological contribution that improves reliability of Bayesian model interpretation.  
- Recommendation: accept — important, well-executed work despite some scope and implementation limitations.
