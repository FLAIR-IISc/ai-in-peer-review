This paper addresses the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors provide a framework to a priori investigate whether parameters of a Bayesian observer model can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, given a specific experimental design. They introduce a flexible class of observer models and a method to efficiently explore the identifiability landscape. The framework is applied to two scenarios, time perception and speed perception, providing insights into experimental design and parameter estimation.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the issue of non-identifiability arising from the degeneracy of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions in Bayesian models. It correctly relates this to previous work demonstrating successful applications of Bayesian modeling in perception, while also acknowledging the potential pitfalls of unconstrained model fitting. The introduction effectively summarizes the problem and highlights the need for a systematic approach to assessing identifiability.

The proposed observer model is a significant strength. Its flexibility, achieved through parametric functions for sensory mapping and distributions (priors, likelihoods, losses), is commendable. The choice of analytical forms allows for relatively efficient computation, crucial for exploring the parameter space. The extension to one-dimensional stimulus magnitude variables, covering tasks like duration, distance, and speed, is also a positive aspect.

The method for mapping a priori identifiability, using KL divergence and MCMC sampling, is a sound approach. The connection to the expected posterior density provides a clear interpretation of the results. Applying this framework to two established scenarios (time and speed perception) demonstrates its utility and provides concrete examples of how it can be used to assess experimental designs. The analysis of the time interval estimation task, in particular, yields non-trivial insights regarding the impact of loss function constraints and independent measurements of motor noise.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The mathematical derivations, while present in the supplementary material, are not always fully transparent within the main text. The equations are dense and could benefit from more detailed explanations of the key steps. The notation is somewhat heavy, potentially hindering readability for readers not deeply familiar with the field. The analysis assumes a specific parametric form for several key distributions (e.g., Gaussian mixtures). While justified by computational convenience, it’s important to acknowledge the potential limitations of this assumption and to consider the sensitivity of the results to alternative distributional choices.

The discussion of the experimental design choices is somewhat limited. The authors primarily focus on manipulating individual parameters (e.g., loss function width, motor noise). A more comprehensive exploration of the interactions between different experimental factors could provide further valuable insights. For example, a more systematic investigation of the trade-offs between the number of conditions, the number of trials per condition, and the complexity of the model would be beneficial.

The claim of providing a ‘novel’ class of observer models should be toned down. While the combination of existing techniques within a single parameterized framework is useful, many of the individual components (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, logarithmic sensory mappings) are well-established in the literature. The novelty lies more in the systematic integration and the application to identifiability analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical, often overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling of perception: identifiability.
*   Presents a flexible and computationally efficient framework for a priori identifiability analysis.
*   Provides concrete examples and insights into experimental design using established perceptual tasks.
*   Offers a valuable tool for researchers developing and applying Bayesian models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Mathematical derivations and notation can be dense and challenging to follow.
*   Relatively strong assumptions about distributional forms (e.g., Gaussian mixtures).
*   Limited exploration of interactions between experimental design factors.
*   The claim of a ‘novel’ model class is overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field. It tackles an important issue with a well-defined framework and demonstrates its utility through compelling examples. While there are areas for improvement in terms of clarity and comprehensiveness, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve readability and address the concerns outlined above. Specifically, the authors should consider providing more detailed explanations of the mathematical derivations, simplifying the notation where possible, and acknowledging the limitations of their distributional assumptions. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between different experimental design choices would also strengthen the paper.
