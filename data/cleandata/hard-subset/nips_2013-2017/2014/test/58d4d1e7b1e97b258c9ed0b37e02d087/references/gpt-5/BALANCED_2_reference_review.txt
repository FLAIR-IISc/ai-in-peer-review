Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a central challenge in fitting Bayesian observer models to psychophysical data: non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavior. The authors formalize an a priori identifiability analysis: given a reference observer θ* and an experimental design (stimulus set and trial counts), they evaluate the expected support for alternative observers θ by integrating the log-likelihood over datasets generated by θ*. This leads to an expected log-evidence equal to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions presp(r|s; θ*) and presp(r|s; θ), scaled by the number of trials (Eq. 12). They then define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp〈log Pr(D|θ)〉 and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability.

A key enabling contribution is a flexible yet computationally tractable observer family: a stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) with Weber/Stevens-like flexibility; mixture-of-Gaussians parameterizations for sensory likelihood and loss (with “max-entropy” choices given four moments); a Gaussian-mixture lattice prior in internal space; lapses; and additive motor/report noise. The mixture structure yields analytic expected losses and fast evaluation of presp.

Two case studies are presented. In interval timing (two duration ranges), the framework shows that baseline designs recover prior means/skewness but underestimate prior variance; identifiability is degraded by a trade-off between sensory and motor noise, and worsens when the loss is narrow (MAP-like). Measuring motor noise in a separate task substantially improves recovery. In speed perception (two contrasts), the method recovers the slow-speed prior exponent and noise parameters robustly, even when the internal likelihood is decoupled from the objective one, corroborating prior findings (Stocker & Simoncelli).

This work connects to a substantial literature on model identifiability and experimental design in cognitive science and statistics, including parameter recovery/diagnosability (Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004), Bayesian optimal experimental design (e.g., Lindley; Chaloner & Verdinelli, 1995), information-based design criteria using KL divergence, and adaptive design optimization in cognitive modeling (Cavagnaro, Myung & Pitt). Within Bayesian perception, concerns about degeneracy among priors/likelihoods/losses are well known; this paper offers a concrete, general, and computationally efficient procedure to probe them before data collection.

Quality
- The mathematical formulation of expected log-evidence (Eq. 12) is sound and ties to standard pre-posterior utility. The observer class is expressive and designed for computational tractability.
- Case studies are thoughtfully set up and yield nontrivial insights (e.g., the detrimental effect of MAP-like losses on prior recovery in timing; the value of measuring motor noise).
- Some technical details critical for reproducibility are deferred to the supplement: construction of the “max-entropy” two-Gaussian mixtures from moments, computing KL between response distributions (which are mixtures), MCMC specifics, constraints on prior weights. Given the dimensionality (M=15 prior weights plus many nuisance parameters), evidence of chain mixing, identifiability diagnostics, and runtime would strengthen confidence.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and structured. The modular observer model is clearly explained, and the design levers are explicit.
- A few notational ambiguities and minor issues: “w1” appears to refer to motor noise but conflicts with prior weights wm; clarify. Clarify whether KLs are computed analytically or numerically. The mapping of a 2AFC experiment to an equivalent estimation task needs more detail (assumptions and limits of equivalence).
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues) and should be corrected. A more explicit discussion of how this approach relates to and differs from OED/ADO literature would improve positioning.

Originality
- Using pre-posterior expected log-evidence/KL to quantify identifiability is not new in statistics or cognitive modeling, but tailoring it to a broad, analytically tractable class of Bayesian observer models and demonstrating concrete design implications in perception is a useful and timely contribution.
- The particular observer parameterization (moments-to-mixture “max-entropy” approximations for both likelihood and loss; lattice prior) is a practical design choice that balances flexibility and speed.

Significance
- The framework is practically valuable: it enables researchers to anticipate parameter trade-offs, rank designs, and decide which components to constrain or measure independently. The timing and speed case studies target influential literatures and illustrate how conclusions about priors or losses can hinge on design and nuisance parameters.
- With code and clearer guidance, this could become a standard pre-registration tool for Bayesian modelers in perception.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and persistent problem (non-identifiability) with a principled, computationally efficient framework.
- Clear, modular observer model; broadly applicable beyond the two tasks studied.
- Yields actionable design insights (e.g., why measuring motor noise helps; when MAP-like feedback hurts).
- Provides theoretical validation of prior empirical inferences in speed perception.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited comparison to established optimal design/identifiability methodologies (OED/ADO, Fisher information approaches), and no adaptive design demonstration.
- Some methodological pieces are under-specified in the main text (mixture construction, KL computation, MCMC details), raising reproducibility concerns.
- The 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence is asserted but not demonstrated; conclusions for speed perception would be stronger with either an explicit 2AFC variant or a real-data check.
- Sensitivity analyses (e.g., number of prior mixture components M, misspecification of observer class) are limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the approach within the OED/ADO literature; discuss relations and differences (e.g., your criterion vs. KL/expected information criteria; potential for adaptive design).
- Provide implementation details in the main text or ensure the supplement includes: algorithm to map moments to unimodal two-Gaussian mixtures, KL computation for presp (analytic vs. quadrature), MCMC setup and diagnostics, constraints on prior weights.
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and its assumptions; consider adding a discrete-choice variant of the framework or a small real-data reanalysis.
- Add sensitivity analyses: impact of M, alternative prior/likelihood parameterizations, and degree of observer-model mismatch.
- Release code to facilitate adoption.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is valuable and timely, and the case studies are insightful, but the paper under-positions itself relative to existing design/identifiability work and lacks some critical technical clarity. With stronger connections to OED/ADO, clearer computational details, and either an explicit discrete-choice formulation or empirical validation, this would be a strong submission.
