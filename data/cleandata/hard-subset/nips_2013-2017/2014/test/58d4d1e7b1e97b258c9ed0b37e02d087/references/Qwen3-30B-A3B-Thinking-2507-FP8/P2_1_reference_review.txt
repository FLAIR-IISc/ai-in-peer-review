

# Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability problem, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors develop a systematic framework to investigate this identifiability problem *a priori* before conducting experiments, which is a significant gap in the current literature. The paper introduces a flexible Bayesian observer model class that can represent a wide range of psychophysical tasks, and proposes a method to efficiently explore the identifiability landscape using KL divergence and expected log likelihood. The framework is applied to two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) to demonstrate how it can guide experimental design and parameter recovery.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-justified. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for their framework, with clear derivations of the key equations (particularly Equations 11-13). The use of KL divergence as a measure of identifiability is appropriate and well-motivated. The case studies are well-designed and provide concrete evidence of the framework's utility. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of the problem's nuances, such as the distinction between theoretical and practical identifiability, and the impact of different experimental designs on parameter recovery.

The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly regarding the assumptions about the internal measurement space and the need for additional validation. The authors properly distinguish their work from prior identifiability studies (e.g., [23]) by focusing specifically on Bayesian models of perception, which have unique challenges due to the degeneracy in the inverse problem.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define the problem, present their solution in a logical sequence, and provide sufficient detail for replication. The mathematical exposition is precise without being overly technical, and the figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively illustrate the key findings. The transition from problem statement to framework to case studies is smooth and logical. The supplementary material appears to provide additional detail for those interested in implementation specifics.

## Originality

The core contribution—systematically addressing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception—is novel. While identifiability is a well-known statistical problem, its application to Bayesian models of perception has been largely overlooked in the literature. The authors clearly position their work within the field, citing relevant prior work on Bayesian models of perception [1-16] and identifiability [23] while highlighting the gap they fill.

The framework is sufficiently general to cover a wide range of psychophysical tasks while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors' approach to modeling the sensation stage with a parametric sensory map (Equation 2) and using mixture models for likelihood and loss functions (Equations 3 and 6) is a clever way to balance flexibility with tractability.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The non-identifiability problem has long plagued Bayesian modeling in perception, potentially invalidating conclusions about internal representations. The authors' framework provides a concrete tool to address this issue, with direct implications for experimental design and interpretation of results. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can reveal non-intuitive findings—for example, that imposing a narrow loss function (MAP) may actually degrade prior recovery in interval timing tasks.

This work could have broad impact across computational neuroscience, psychology, and cognitive science. It provides a methodological advance that could improve the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian models in these fields. The framework is directly applicable to many existing studies and could become a standard tool in the field.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a genuine, important problem in the field with a well-justified solution
- Provides a practical framework that can be immediately applied to experimental design
- Demonstrates utility through two well-chosen case studies
- Careful treatment of assumptions and limitations
- Clear, well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- The framework assumes a specific structure for the internal measurement space, which may limit applicability to some tasks (though the authors acknowledge this)
- The case studies are limited to two specific tasks; broader validation would strengthen the paper
- The paper doesn't fully address how to resolve degeneracies once identified (beyond ranking experimental designs)
- The computational complexity of the framework isn't explicitly addressed for very large parameter spaces

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It addresses a real and important problem with a technically sound, well-motivated solution. The framework is novel, practical, and well-validated through case studies. While there are minor limitations in scope, the paper's contribution is substantial and likely to have a meaningful impact on how Bayesian models of perception are designed and interpreted.

The paper meets all NIPS review criteria: it's of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant. The authors have made a valuable methodological contribution that addresses a critical problem in the field. I recommend acceptance.

The paper could be strengthened by briefly discussing computational complexity for larger parameter spaces and by including one additional case study to demonstrate broader applicability. However, these are minor suggestions for an already strong paper.

This work represents an important step toward more rigorous Bayesian modeling in perception research, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
