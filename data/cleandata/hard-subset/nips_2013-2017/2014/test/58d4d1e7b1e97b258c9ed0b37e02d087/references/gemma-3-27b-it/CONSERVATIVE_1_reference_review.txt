This paper tackles the important, and often overlooked, issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in inverting behavioral data to recover internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) and propose a framework for *a priori* assessment of identifiability. The motivation is well-articulated, and the problem statement is clear. The extension of existing models into a unified parametrization is a positive step, and the attempt to provide a practical tool for experimental design is commendable. However, despite the relevance of the problem, the paper suffers from a lack of extensive validation and relies heavily on theoretical analysis without sufficient demonstration of its practical impact.

The core idea – quantifying identifiability using KL divergence and MCMC sampling – is reasonable. The authors appropriately identify that the degeneracy can be addressed through constraints, but the analysis of *how* these constraints impact identifiability feels somewhat limited. The two case studies, while illustrating the framework, don't provide overwhelmingly convincing evidence of its utility. The time perception example reveals potentially counterintuitive results (narrowing the loss function *decreases* identifiability) that are not fully explored, and the speed perception example largely confirms existing understanding without groundbreaking new insight. The reliance on a single “reference observer” in the MCMC sampling, while simplifying the analysis, introduces a strong assumption that might limit the generalizability of the findings. The paper acknowledges this somewhat but doesn't adequately address how sensitive the conclusions are to the choice of this reference observer.

A significant weakness is the limited exploration of the parameter space. The authors fix several parameters (e.g., M=15 for the prior) without a thorough justification. While computational constraints are mentioned, a sensitivity analysis to these fixed parameters would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, the paper lacks a systematic comparison to simpler identifiability checks that are already available. For instance, a straightforward parameter sweep combined with goodness-of-fit measures could have provided a baseline for evaluating the proposed framework's efficiency and accuracy. The connection to other work in model identifiability (e.g., structural identifiability in systems biology) is superficial. The authors mention generic techniques, but do not demonstrate how their approach improves upon or differs substantially from these established methods. The empirical validation remains a key concern. While the framework proposes a way to *assess* identifiability, it doesn't inherently *guarantee* that the recovered parameters are accurate or meaningful.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: identifiability.
*   Presents a unified and flexible observer model.
*   Proposes a potentially useful framework for *a priori* experimental design.
*   Clear motivation and problem statement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited empirical validation. The case studies don’t provide strong evidence of practical benefit.
*   Reliance on a single reference observer and fixed parameters.
*   Lack of comparison to simpler identifiability checks.
*   Superficial connection to broader literature on model identifiability.
*   Counterintuitive results (time perception example) are not fully explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important and relatively unexplored topic.
*   The framework, while needing further development, has the potential to improve experimental design in Bayesian modeling.
*   The unified observer model is a useful contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of extensive validation raises serious concerns about the practical utility of the framework.
*   The analysis feels incomplete and relies heavily on theoretical arguments without sufficient empirical support.
*   The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods for assessing model identifiability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. The paper identifies a crucial issue but doesn't provide sufficiently compelling evidence that the proposed framework offers a significant and practical solution. The theoretical analysis is sound, but the lack of robust empirical validation and comparison to existing methods weakens the contribution. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and convincing piece of work. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by conducting more extensive simulations and comparisons to existing methods, before resubmitting. A more thorough sensitivity analysis and exploration of the parameter space are also crucial.
