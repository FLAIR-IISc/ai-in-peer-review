

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that produce identical behavioral data. The authors propose a theoretical framework to systematically assess identifiability a priori for a class of Bayesian observer models, which they argue can guide experimental design to improve parameter recovery. The paper develops a flexible observer model with a rich parameterization, and proposes a method to quantify identifiability through expected log-likelihood differences between models.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) a general class of Bayesian observer models that can represent a wide range of sensory estimation tasks, (2) a method to explore identifiability landscapes using expected log-likelihood differences, and (3) two case studies demonstrating how the framework can evaluate experimental designs for time interval estimation and speed perception.

**Strengths:** The paper tackles a genuine methodological challenge in the field that has been acknowledged in prior work (e.g., [17, 18, 19]). The theoretical foundation is sound, building on established concepts in model identifiability and Bayesian inference. The case studies provide concrete illustrations of how the framework could inform experimental design, and the authors demonstrate reasonable attention to the limitations of their approach. The framework is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and thorough supplementary materials.

**Weaknesses:** While the problem is well-motivated, the paper falls short in several key areas that would be expected of a NIPS submission. The most significant deficiency is the lack of empirical validation. The case studies are entirely theoretical simulations, not grounded in real experimental data. Without demonstrating how this framework would actually improve parameter recovery in real datasets compared to existing approaches, the practical utility remains speculative. The paper claims to "fill a gap" in systematic analysis of identifiability, but doesn't properly compare their method to existing techniques for addressing non-identifiability (e.g., [23]), making it unclear what new contribution they're making.

The proposed framework is also excessively complex. The parameter vector θ contains 21 free parameters (Equation 10), and the authors claim they need to fix M=15 for the prior mixture. This level of complexity would make the framework impractical for most experimental psychologists, who typically work with simpler models. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their method would outperform more straightforward approaches like model comparison with BIC or AIC, or the use of informative priors based on prior knowledge.

Furthermore, the authors don't adequately address whether their framework would generalize to the most common experimental paradigms in the field. For example, they convert a 2AFC speed perception task into an estimation task (Section 4.2), but don't justify why this conversion is valid or demonstrate how the framework would work with the actual 2AFC paradigm. This undermines their claim that their approach "can be used to improve experimental design" for real-world studies.

**Comparative Analysis:** The paper's approach is theoretically interesting but resembles other work on model identifiability (e.g., [23]), with only incremental improvements in computational efficiency. The authors don't demonstrate any significant performance gains over existing methods. The field has already developed practical approaches to address non-identifiability (e.g., using experimental manipulations to constrain parameters [5, 12]), and this paper doesn't convincingly argue why their framework would be preferable.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper identifies an important methodological issue in Bayesian modeling of perception, but fails to provide sufficient evidence that their proposed solution is both practical and superior to existing approaches. The theoretical framework is well-developed but remains untested on real data, which is a critical omission for a methodological paper. Without empirical validation against established baselines or demonstration of clear advantages over simpler methods, the paper's contribution is limited.

I am skeptical of the paper's claim that this framework will "fill this gap" in the literature. The field has long recognized the identifiability problem, and while systematic analysis is valuable, the authors have not demonstrated that their method offers a significant improvement over existing solutions. The paper would benefit from substantial empirical validation, including comparison to established methods on real datasets.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's theoretical contribution is not sufficiently novel or validated. While the problem is important, the proposed solution lacks the empirical grounding and comparative analysis required to demonstrate its value to the field. The framework appears to be more complex than necessary without clear advantages over simpler, established approaches to addressing identifiability issues in Bayesian models.
