Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the longstanding non-identifiability problem in Bayesian models of perception: multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce indistinguishable behavior. It formalizes an a priori identifiability framework for a broad class of 1D sensorimotor estimation tasks. The authors propose a flexible observer model comprising: (i) a stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) designed to render measurement noise approximately stationary; (ii) a measurement distribution expressed as a two-component Gaussian mixture with specified central moments; (iii) a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians; (iv) a loss function represented as an inverted Gaussian mixture; (v) lapses and motor noise in the response stage. They derive an expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model θ under data generated by a reference θ*, showing it equals a constant minus the expected KL divergence between predicted response distributions, averaged over stimuli and trials. They define E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp〈log Pr(D|θ)〉 and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability.

Two case studies are presented. In interval timing (following Jazayeri & Shadlen and Acerbi et al.), they show that standard designs poorly identify priors due to confounds between sensory and motor noise, that encouraging MAP-like loss worsens prior recovery, and that independently measuring motor noise improves identifiability. In speed perception (Stocker & Simoncelli), they find that the slow-speed prior exponent and contrast-dependent noise are well-identifiable under their framework, even when decoupling internal from external noise. The work builds on prior concerns about degeneracy in Bayesian cognitive models and distinguishability analyses (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung), and complements empirical “cognitive tomography” approaches by offering a computationally efficient, model-based a priori analysis.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The key identity (Eq. 12) is standard: the expected log-likelihood under the true model equals negative cross-entropy plus a constant, yielding a sum of KL divergences across stimuli weighted by their frequencies. Using this to define a pseudo-posterior E(θ|θ*) is reasonable for identifiability exploration.
- The observer model is internally consistent and computationally tractable: mixture-of-Gaussians forms allow analytic expected loss and efficient marginalization. The lapse and motor noise models are conventional.
- Assumptions are explicit: stationary noise in transformed space, mixture forms for likelihood and loss, Gaussian-lattice prior representation, and separable response noise.
- The case studies are coherent and reproduce known qualitative findings: the timing task shows sensory–motor confounds and loss-function sensitivity; the speed task shows robust recovery of a slow-speed prior.
- Potential limitations: the identifiability conclusions depend on the chosen parametric bases (e.g., two-component mixtures, Gaussian lattice with M=15, specific f(s)). The “approximate expected posterior” is not a true posterior (no prior over θ, no integration over datasets), so it is a heuristic landscape for identifiability. Robustness to model mismatch and alternative choices (e.g., spline priors, different lapse models) is only partially explored.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly organized and methodologically transparent. The modeling assumptions and parameterizations are stated, and the rationale for using mixtures is clear. The derivation of Eq. 12 is deferred to the supplement, which is appropriate.
- Some notation issues could be clarified (e.g., reuse of symbols such as w1; clearer distinction between internal vs external likelihood parameters). Including computational cost and MCMC diagnostics would improve clarity on feasibility.
- The conversion of a 2AFC setup to an estimation-equivalent task in the speed case is plausible but would benefit from more detail or a citation in the main text.

Originality
- The main novelty lies in combining: (i) a unified, efficient observer model family supporting closed-form expected losses; and (ii) an expected-KL-based exploration of identifiability to guide experiment design. While expected KL/cross-entropy and distinguishability analyses are established, their tailored application to complex Bayesian observer models with an emphasis on practical design trade-offs is a useful contribution.
- The demonstration that narrowing the loss (MAP-like) can degrade prior identifiability in timing, and that measuring motor noise can restore it, provides new, actionable insights for experimentalists.

Significance
- The framework has potential to affect experimental design in perception and decision-making by diagnosing parameter confounds before data collection. It can help adjudicate which parameters require independent measurement and which task manipulations enhance identifiability.
- The case studies address influential paradigms (interval timing, speed perception), suggesting broader applicability. However, empirical validation on real datasets and comparisons with alternative design metrics (e.g., Fisher information, expected information gain) would strengthen impact.

Strengths
- Clear, general observer model with efficient computations (mixture-based).
- Sound identifiability metric based on expected KL divergences.
- Actionable insights on design choices (e.g., motor noise measurement; caution with MAP-like feedback).
- Reassurance for prior recovery in speed perception under plausible assumptions.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on specific parametric families; limited robustness analyses to alternative bases.
- “Approximate expected posterior” is a heuristic; no principled thresholding for identifiability.
- Lack of empirical demonstrations with real data and runtime/convergence reporting.
- Limited exploration of inter-subject variability, model-class comparisons (e.g., non-Bayesian heuristics), and discrete-choice paradigms (though mentioned as future work).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses to alternative prior bases (e.g., splines), different lapse models, and different f(s), and report how conclusions change.
- Include computational complexity, MCMC diagnostics, and code to facilitate adoption.
- Compare with Fisher information or expected information gain to contextualize the method.
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and assess identifiability directly in discrete-choice settings.
- Consider group-level variability and hierarchical analyses in the identifiability framework.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-examined issue (identifiability) with a practical, general framework.
- Methodologically sound and clearly presented; provides nontrivial design guidance.
- Case studies connect to influential results and validate known phenomena.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavily dependent on parametric choices; limited robustness analyses and no empirical validation on real data.
- The identifiability measure is heuristic and not contrasted with established optimal design criteria.
- Scope limited to 1D estimation with specific mixture forms; generalization claims would benefit from additional demonstrations.
