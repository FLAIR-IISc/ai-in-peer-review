Summary
The paper tackles a critical and under-discussed issue in Bayesian models of perception: practical non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss). It proposes (i) a flexible parametric class of Bayesian observer models for 1D estimation tasks (sensation via a monotonic transform and mixture-Gaussian noise; priors as a Gaussian lattice mixture; loss as an “inverted mixture of Gaussians”; lapses; reporting noise), and (ii) an a priori identifiability assessment based on the expected log marginal likelihood under a reference observer, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response densities (Eq. 12). The authors approximate an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp(⟨log p(D|θ)⟩) and sample it with MCMC to map identifiability and compare designs. Case studies in interval timing and visual speed illustrate that: (a) in timing, recovering priors is fragile unless motor noise is known; inducing narrow loss (MAP-like) can worsen identifiability; (b) in speed perception, a slow-speed prior and noise parameters appear recoverable.

Strengths
- Important problem: a priori identifiability assessment for Bayesian observer models is highly relevant to neuroscience/psychophysics and to NeurIPS.
- General modeling class spans many tasks, and the conjugacy/product-of-mixtures structure can make computations tractable.
- The expected log-likelihood → KL formulation (Eq. 12) is principled and aligns with cross-entropy/information-theoretic design ideas.
- Interesting design insights (e.g., measuring motor noise helps; narrow loss can harm identifiability).
- Potentially useful for pre-registered design comparison and prioritization.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/methodological
- Incomplete derivations and key constructions deferred to Supplementary Material:
  - Moment-matching of a two-Gaussian mixture under unimodality plus a “local maximum-entropy” criterion (for both likelihood and loss) is non-standard and can be ill-posed. Please provide explicit algorithms, feasibility conditions, and diagnostics.
  - Analytic form for expected loss (Sec. 2.3) when posterior is a mixture and loss is an inverted mixture: product/normalization steps and computational complexity need to be spelled out; otherwise “fast” remains unsubstantiated.
  - Numerical evaluation of KL between continuous presp(r|s; θ) and presp(r|s; θ∗): quadrature/discretization error, stability, and cost are not reported.
- Parameterization and constraints:
  - Prior mixture weights {wm} must be positive and sum to one. No reparameterization (e.g., softmax) or priors on weights are specified; how is MCMC performed in a constrained high-dimensional simplex?
  - Many parameters must be positive (variances, s0, d). Parameter bounds and priors are not described; without this, E(θ|θ∗)-MCMC can misbehave.
- The transform f(s): A and B are chosen so that the discrete set {f(si)} maps to [−1,1] “without loss of generality.” This implies experiment-dependent scaling of the observer’s internal space, which is psychologically questionable and can confound design comparisons. If this is purely a computational reparameterization, show invariance of the identifiability conclusions to linear rescaling and how priors/noise/loss parameters are co-transformed.
- Lapse model: drawing lapses from the prior (Eq. 7) is not standard; many studies use uniform response or stimulus-dependent hazards. Lapses that mirror the prior will strongly couple lapse rate with prior shape and may bias identifiability. At least test uniform-lapse as a sensitivity analysis.
- Response noise: σreport^2(s) = ρ0^2 + ρ1^2 s^2. This is a specific, multiplicative-like form; why not (ρ0 + ρ1 s)^2? Do results depend on this choice?
- Eq. 1 degeneracy argument: multiplying prior/likelihood/loss by φi(s) with ∏ φi(s) = c requires careful handling of normalization constants, especially for qprior and qmeas; please provide a rigorous proof and clarify where normalization is absorbed (Z in the posterior vs loss scaling).
- The “expected posterior” E(θ|θ∗) is not a posterior unless a parameter prior is included. If a flat prior is assumed, please state it. Also justify MCMC over E vs. using local Fisher information/Hessian approximations to map local identifiability; compare results.

Empirical validation and scope
- The approach is never validated against actual parameter recovery on simulated datasets using full inference (i.e., does high E-peakedness predict accurate recovery with finite data and realistic noise/model mismatch?). A small-scale check comparing E-based predictions to recovery from synthetic data would strengthen claims.
- Second case study converts a 2AFC paradigm to estimation “equivalently,” which is nontrivial. The mapping should be specified and justified, or the method should be applied directly to a 2AFC observer model.
- The Gaussian-lattice prior (M = 15, fixed variance tied to spacing) may approximate uniform distributions but may struggle with heavy tails/power laws. In the speed case, is the power-law prior approximated in t- or s-space? Sensitivity to M/variance assumptions should be shown.
- Design optimization: the paper stops at qualitative comparisons; connecting to established Bayesian optimal experimental design (expected information gain, D-/A-optimality) would clarify how to choose stimuli/protocols systematically.

Clarity/organization
- Several notational inconsistencies and likely typos that impede readability:
  - L(s, ŝ) vs L(ŝ, s) used interchangeably; fix the argument order throughout.
  - In Sec. 4.1, “non-identifiability of the sensory noise, σ, and the motor noise, w1” — likely ρ1, not w1 (wm denote prior mixture weights).
  - Inverse of Eq. 2: write explicitly s0 [exp((t−B)/A) − 1]^{1/d}; current “d √” is ambiguous.
  - “parameteric” → “parametric”; spacing inconsistencies (ρ 2 0, etc.); a mid-sentence break in Sec. 4.2 (“consider-”).
- The manuscript depends heavily on the Supplement; essential pieces should be in the main text, or at least summarized with clear algorithms.

Related work
- Missing connections to Bayesian optimal experimental design and information-theoretic criteria: Lindley (1956), Chaloner & Verdinelli (1995), Atkinson & Donev (1992), MacKay (information gain), Myung & Pitt (model distinguishability), Fisher information-based identifiability, and “sloppiness” literature (e.g., Transtrum et al.).
- Broader cognitive modeling identifiability work (e.g., parameter recovery, posterior predictive checks) should be discussed.

Formatting/references
- References are severely incomplete/inconsistent (missing years, venues, volumes/pages; some with dangling commas; duplicate/inaccurate entries). This is below NeurIPS standards and must be fixed.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem with a principled, general framework.
- Offers actionable design insights; could influence how labs plan psychophysics experiments.
- Technical idea (expected log-likelihood as KL sum) is sound and efficient to compute once presp is available.

Cons for acceptance
- Many missing derivations/algorithms; unclear parameterization and constraints; reliance on Supplement.
- Questionable modeling choices (lapse; scaling of internal space) not justified or stress-tested.
- No validation that E-based identifiability predicts actual parameter recovery.
- Substantial clarity/notation issues and severely incomplete references.

Recommendation
Reject. The contribution is promising, but the current manuscript has too many methodological gaps, unclear specifications, and presentation/formatting problems. With complete derivations, clearer parameterization and constraints, validation on synthetic datasets (and/or direct 2AFC modeling), sensitivity analyses (lapse, transform scaling, prior basis), and corrected references, this work could be strong.

Reviewer confidence: High.
