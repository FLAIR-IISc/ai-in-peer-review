This paper presents a novel and powerful framework for assessing, a priori, the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly identify a fundamental challenge in computational cognitive science: the behavioral equivalence of different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions makes it difficult to uniquely recover these components from data. This "inverse problem" undermines the interpretation of such models as veridical descriptions of internal representations. The proposed solution is a computational framework that allows researchers to simulate an experiment and analyze the "identifiability landscape" of a model's parameters before collecting any data. This is achieved by (1) defining a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models, and (2) introducing a metric based on the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of a "ground truth" reference observer and other candidate observers. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two compelling case studies in time and speed perception, which yield non-trivial insights into experimental design.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a critical and long-standing issue that has often been noted but rarely addressed with a systematic, prospective tool. This paper moves beyond post-hoc critiques of Bayesian modeling by providing a concrete, principled framework for improving experimental design. The approach of using the expected log-likelihood (formalized via KL divergence) to map the identifiability landscape is both elegant and novel, representing a significant methodological contribution.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed observer model is a major strength. The use of mixtures of Gaussians to flexibly represent priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, combined with a parametric sensory map that can approximate both Weber's and Steven's laws, is powerful. Crucially, the authors have ensured computational tractability, particularly by deriving an analytical expression for the expected loss, which makes the required MCMC sampling feasible. This is a sophisticated piece of modeling.
3.  **Clarity and Impact:** The paper is well-written and the motivation is clearly articulated. The case studies are particularly effective at demonstrating the framework's value. The analysis of the time perception task provides actionable advice: it highlights a specific degeneracy between sensory and motor noise and shows that independently measuring motor noise is far more effective at improving prior recovery than simply increasing trial counts or, surprisingly, enforcing a MAP-like loss function. The validation against the well-known "slow-speed prior" result provides theoretical support for prior work and demonstrates that not all Bayesian models are hopelessly degenerate. This framework has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers in this field, guiding them toward more informative experiments.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Justification of the Identifiability Metric:** The paper defines the "approximate expected posterior density" as `E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(<log Pr(D|θ)>)`. While this is an intuitive and useful heuristic for visualizing the identifiability landscape, its formal statistical justification is somewhat thin. It is not a true posterior distribution, as it lacks a prior over `θ` and is not conditioned on observed data `D`. It would strengthen the paper to be more precise about the interpretation of this quantity, perhaps framing it as a "recoverability landscape" or "expected evidence landscape" to avoid confusion with a Bayesian posterior.
2.  **Simplification of the Speed Perception Task:** A critical detail in the second case study is the conversion of the original 2AFC paradigm from Stocker & Simoncelli (2006) into an "equivalent estimation task." This step is mentioned only in passing. However, the information available from binary choices (2AFC) is fundamentally different and often less constraining than that from continuous reports (estimation). The validity of the framework's conclusions as a "theoretical validation" for the original study hinges entirely on this equivalence. The authors should provide a more detailed justification for this conversion or, alternatively, be more cautious in their claims, stating that their analysis applies to a hypothetical estimation version of the task.
3.  **Robustness to the Reference Observer:** The analysis is conditioned on a single, fixed "reference observer" `θ*`, which is based on "pilot experiments, the literature, or educated guesses." However, the conclusions about which experimental design is best might themselves depend on the specific choice of `θ*`. A small sensitivity analysis, showing that the main conclusions (e.g., the ranking of experimental designs in the timing task) are robust to reasonable variations in the parameters of `θ*`, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
4.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this is a common paradigm, the authors should be more explicit about the challenges in extending it. For instance, extending the model to discrete-choice paradigms like 2AFC is non-trivial and would require a different likelihood function over the data, fundamentally changing the KL-divergence calculation.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pros for Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a fundamental, high-impact problem in cognitive and computational neuroscience.
    *   Presents a novel and technically sound methodological framework.
    *   The flexible observer model is a significant contribution in itself.
    *   Demonstrates clear utility through insightful and non-obvious results in well-chosen case studies.

*   **Cons for Acceptance:**
    *   The statistical interpretation of the core identifiability metric could be clarified.
    *   The unelaborated conversion of a 2AFC task to an estimation task is a potential weak point in the second case study's claims.
    *   The analysis lacks a demonstration of robustness to the choice of the reference model.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant and timely contribution to the field. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the framework and its potential to improve the rigor of experimental research in perception and cognition, far outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily points that could be clarified or addressed in future work, rather than fatal flaws.
