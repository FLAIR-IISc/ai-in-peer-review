### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and powerful framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis of Bayesian observer models in perception. The authors tackle the well-known but challenging problem that different combinations of internal model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical observable behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover these components from experimental data. The contribution is twofold: first, they develop a flexible, general-purpose class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks that is computationally tractable. Second, they propose an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape" of a model's parameters for a given experimental design. This method calculates the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model under a "true" reference model, which is elegantly shown to be related to the KL divergence between their respective response distributions. This allows researchers to assess potential model degeneracies and compare the power of different experimental designs before collecting any data. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two compelling case studies: (1) revealing significant challenges in recovering priors in a time-perception task and showing how design choices (like independently measuring motor noise) can resolve them, and (2) providing theoretical validation for the robust recovery of a "slow-speed prior" in models of vision.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The primary strength of this paper lies in its **boldness and novelty**. The problem of non-identifiability is a specter that has long haunted Bayesian cognitive science, often relegated to footnotes or addressed with ad-hoc assumptions. This paper confronts the issue head-on, not with another critique, but with a constructive, principled, and quantitative tool. The idea of formalizing *a priori* identifiability analysis for this class of models is a significant conceptual leap. It has the potential to shift the standard of practice in the field, encouraging researchers to design experiments that are explicitly powered to distinguish between competing hypotheses about internal representations. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method; it is the creation of a new and much-needed methodological direction.

The technical approach is both clever and pragmatic. The development of a flexible, parametric observer model using mixtures of Gaussians is a solid piece of engineering that makes the subsequent analysis possible. More impressively, the method for mapping the identifiability landscape by sampling from an approximate expected posterior is a brilliant simplification. By linking expected log-likelihood to KL divergence, the authors circumvent the intractable need for massive Monte Carlo simulations of experiments, creating a tool that is genuinely usable.

The significance of the work is clearly demonstrated through the case studies, which yield non-obvious insights. The finding that, in the time perception model, forcing a narrower (MAP-like) loss function could actually *worsen* the ability to recover the prior is a fantastic example of the framework's power to challenge intuition. Conversely, the validation of the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference provides a new kind of theoretical support for a classic finding. These results showcase the framework not just as a theoretical curiosity, but as a practical tool for discovery and validation.

The paper does have some weaknesses, though they are primarily limitations of scope rather than fundamental flaws.

1.  **Limited Model Domain:** The framework is currently specified for one-dimensional, continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a substantial portion of the psychophysics literature, its applicability to other common paradigms like discrete choice (e.g., 2AFC), multi-cue integration, or tasks with categorical variables is not yet established. The authors acknowledge this, but it does constrain the immediate reach of the proposed method.

2.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The entire analysis is conditioned on a chosen "reference observer" (θ*), which represents the assumed ground truth. The conclusions about a given experimental design are therefore only as good as the assumptions about this reference model. While one can explore the robustness of the conclusions by varying θ*, the paper presents results for a single reference in each case study, leaving the sensitivity of the identifiability landscapes to these ground-truth assumptions largely unexplored.

3.  **Methodological Approximations:** The use of the expected log-likelihood is a well-justified but nonetheless simplified proxy for the full dynamics of model fitting with finite data. It captures the global similarity between the predictions of two models but may not fully reflect the complexities of the likelihood surface (e.g., local minima, ridge-like degeneracies) that would be encountered by an inference algorithm on a single, noisy dataset.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's strengths are overwhelming. The limitations are characteristic of any ambitious new framework and represent exciting avenues for future work rather than reasons for rejection. The core idea is too novel and important to be held back by what are, in essence, issues of completeness.

### Arguments for acceptance:
-   **High Originality and Significance:** Proposes a novel, foundational framework to address a critical, long-standing problem in computational modeling. This is a pioneering work.
-   **Conceptual Advance:** Shifts the focus from post-hoc justification of model assumptions to principled, *a priori* experimental design.
-   **Practical Utility:** Provides a concrete, computationally efficient tool that researchers can immediately use to design better, more informative experiments.
-   **Strong Demonstration:** The case studies are well-executed and generate non-trivial, valuable insights that showcase the power of the approach.

### Arguments against acceptance:
-   The framework's scope is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks.
-   The analysis is conditional on assumptions about the "true" reference observer, and sensitivity to these assumptions is not fully explored.
-   The identifiability metric is a pragmatic approximation.

### Recommendation

This paper is a standout. It is precisely the kind of bold, concept-driven work that pushes a field forward. While the methodology is not without its limitations and simplifying assumptions, these are minor in comparison to the novelty and significance of the core contribution. The authors have developed a tool that could, and should, change the way a generation of computational cognitive scientists design and interpret their experiments. I strongly recommend acceptance.
