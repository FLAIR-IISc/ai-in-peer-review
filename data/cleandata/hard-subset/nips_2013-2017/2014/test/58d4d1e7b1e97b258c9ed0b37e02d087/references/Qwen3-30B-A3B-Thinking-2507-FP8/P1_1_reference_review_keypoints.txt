- Paper addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models and presents a general framework plus an efficient method to assess identifiability a priori, validated on temporal interval estimation and speed perception case studies.  
- Originality: novel systematic application of identifiability analysis to Bayesian models of perception, moving beyond ad hoc constraints.  
- Quality: rigorous mathematical development (e.g., expected log-likelihood derivation), computationally tractable methods, and strong empirical validation; assumes Gaussian motor noise.  
- Significance: directly tackles a major problem that can undermine prior studies and provides a practical tool to improve experimental design and interpretation.  
- Clarity: well-organized, clearly written, with thorough derivations and reproducible details (including supplementary material).  
- Scope limitation: framework is currently limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks and does not directly handle multi-dimensional stimulus spaces.  
- Computational cost: requires MCMC sampling per experimental design and may be computationally intensive for high-dimensional parameter spaces; scalability is not quantified.  
- Assumption sensitivity: conclusions rely on the chosen reference observer θ*, but sensitivity to deviations from θ* is not fully explored.  
- Specific comment (1): interval estimation case study reveals the non-intuitive result that a MAP-like loss can worsen prior recovery.  
- Specific comment (2): speed perception case study demonstrates that some Bayesian models are inherently more robust to non-identifiability, corroborating prior work.  
- Specific comment (3): the paper could strengthen claims by discussing extensions to more complex loss functions beyond the two-component Gaussian mixture.  
- Specific comment (4): the authors should relate their framework to recent neuroscience model-selection work that also addresses identifiability from a different perspective.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically strong, original, significant, and practically useful despite minor limitations.  
- Final thought: the framework meaningfully fills a methodological gap in Bayesian perception modeling and motivates future extensions to more complex experimental paradigms.
