

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors recognize that the inverse mapping from observed behavior to internal representations is fundamentally ill-posed, as multiple combinations of model parameters can yield identical behavior. They propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis, building on previous work by [19] while introducing a novel class of observer models and an efficient method to explore identifiability landscapes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Problem Addressed:** The paper tackles a genuine and important problem in the field that has been acknowledged but not systematically resolved (e.g., [17, 18, 19]). The authors clearly demonstrate that without proper identifiability analysis, conclusions about internal representations could be invalid.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The framework is theoretically rigorous, using KL divergence to quantify expected model support and deriving an efficient computational approach. The mathematical formulations are precise and well-motivated, with the authors carefully addressing the degeneracy issue through their parametrization.

3. **Practical Utility:** The two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) effectively demonstrate the framework's utility. They show how the approach can guide experimental design (e.g., identifying that measuring motor parameters improves identifiability, while imposing a narrow loss function may worsen it), providing concrete, actionable insights for experimentalists.

4. **Well-Structured Contribution:** The paper builds logically from problem statement to framework to validation. The generative model is sufficiently flexible to cover many psychophysical tasks while maintaining computational tractability, and the identifiability analysis method is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Applicability:** The framework focuses on one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. While the authors note potential extensions (e.g., to multiple cues or discrete-choice paradigms), the current work doesn't demonstrate how the framework would handle more complex scenarios, such as those involving multiple sensory cues or categorical decisions, which are common in psychophysics.

2. **Lack of Robustness Analysis:** The case studies assume the reference model is correctly specified. The paper doesn't address how the framework would perform when the true model lies outside the assumed model class (a common scenario in practice), which could limit its practical utility.

3. **Computational Considerations:** The authors don't discuss the computational cost of applying their framework to more complex models or larger datasets. For the MCMC sampling approach described, this could become prohibitive for models with many parameters or high-dimensional stimulus spaces.

4. **Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper references [23] on model distinguishability, it doesn't fully contrast its approach with existing methods for identifiability analysis in other fields (e.g., system identification in engineering). A more thorough comparison would better establish the novelty and advantages of their approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic framework for addressing the critical problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The technical approach is sound, the case studies are well-chosen, and the insights for experimental design are practically useful. While the scope is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks, the framework could be extended to broader applications.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. The problem addressed is important, the solution is theoretically sound and practically applicable, and the work advances the state of the art in experimental design for psychophysical studies. The authors have provided a valuable tool that could prevent many studies from drawing invalid conclusions about internal representations.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. In the revision, include a brief discussion of how the framework might be extended to handle more complex experimental paradigms (e.g., multiple cues, categorical decisions).

2. Add a short section discussing the computational complexity of the approach and potential approximations that could make it feasible for larger models.

3. Provide a more detailed comparison with existing approaches to identifiability analysis in related fields to better establish the novelty of the proposed framework.

4. Consider adding a brief analysis of how the framework would behave when the true model is outside the assumed model class, perhaps through a small simulation study.

This paper provides a valuable methodological contribution that has the potential to significantly improve the reliability of conclusions drawn from Bayesian models of perception. The framework is well-developed, the case studies are convincing, and the insights for experimental design are practical and important.
