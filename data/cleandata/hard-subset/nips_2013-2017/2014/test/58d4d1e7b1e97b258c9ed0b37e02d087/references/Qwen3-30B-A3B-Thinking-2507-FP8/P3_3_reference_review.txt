

# Peer Review: "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a fundamental yet often overlooked issue in computational models of perception: the non-identifiability of Bayesian observer models. The authors rightly point out that the inverse mapping from behavioral data to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior. While previous work has acknowledged this degeneracy [19, 22], no systematic framework existed for investigating it a priori before experimental design. The paper builds on the rich tradition of Bayesian models in perception [1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 9, 12, 14, 24, 29], but makes a significant contribution by providing a general method to evaluate identifiability across model parameters before collecting data.

The authors propose a flexible Bayesian observer model class (with a rich parameterization of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions) and develop a method to efficiently explore the identifiability landscape using KL divergence and MCMC sampling. They demonstrate the framework's utility through two case studies: interval timing and speed perception. This work aligns with the growing trend in computational cognitive science to focus on model validation and identifiability [23, 28], but provides a more systematic approach specifically tailored to Bayesian models of perception.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a genuine methodological gap in the field. Many papers in perception research claim to recover internal representations using Bayesian models, yet without proper identifiability analysis, these claims may be unfounded. The authors correctly identify this as a critical issue that has been largely ignored.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The framework is mathematically rigorous, with clear theoretical foundations in Bayesian Decision Theory and information theory. The derivation of the expected log-likelihood as a proxy for identifiability (Eq. 12) is elegant and well-justified.

3. **Practical Utility**: The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can inform experimental design. The findings are non-trivial (e.g., the counterintuitive result that a "narrower loss function" can worsen prior recovery), demonstrating the framework's value for researchers.

4. **Comprehensive Model**: The proposed observer model is sufficiently flexible to encompass many psychophysical tasks while remaining computationally tractable, making it a useful tool for the field.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear figures and sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially replicate the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Cost**: The MCMC-based approach to explore identifiability landscapes may be prohibitively expensive for models with many parameters or complex experimental designs. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't propose practical alternatives for large-scale applications.

2. **Assumption Sensitivity**: The framework's conclusions depend heavily on the assumed reference model (θ*). The paper doesn't sufficiently explore how sensitive the identifiability analysis is to deviations from the assumed reference model. For example, in the time interval experiment, the authors note "the analysis we performed is theoretical," but the practical relevance depends on how well the reference model approximates reality.

3. **Limited Scope of Case Studies**: While the two case studies are well-chosen, they don't fully demonstrate the framework's versatility. More diverse examples (e.g., visual orientation perception, auditory tasks) would better establish the method's general applicability.

4. **Lack of Practical Implementation Guidance**: The paper is strong on theory but weak on implementation. It doesn't provide clear guidance on how a researcher could actually implement this framework for their specific experimental paradigm.

5. **Limited Discussion of Neural Connections**: The paper focuses on behavioral models but doesn't connect the identifiability issues to neural representations or computational neuroscience, which would strengthen the significance for NIPS readers.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the methodology of computational models in perception. The problem addressed is important, the solution is theoretically sound, and the case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility. While there are some limitations in scope and implementation, these don't detract from the paper's core contribution.

The paper clearly advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It provides researchers with a tool to improve the reliability of their Bayesian model interpretations, which has direct implications for the validity of many findings in the field. The framework could be extended to other domains of cognitive science beyond perception, further increasing its significance.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance**. This paper addresses a critical methodological gap with a well-developed solution that has the potential to improve the quality of research across the field. While some limitations exist, they don't undermine the core contribution. The paper is of high quality, clear in its presentation, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Pro:** Addresses a fundamental methodological issue in Bayesian modeling of perception, provides a theoretically sound and practically useful framework, well-executed case studies.

**Con:** Limited exploration of implementation challenges, scope of case studies could be broader, some dependence on reference model assumptions.

This work represents an important step toward more rigorous Bayesian modeling in perception research, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
