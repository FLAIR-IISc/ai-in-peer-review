### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the critical and well-known problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that a given behavioral pattern can often be explained by multiple, distinct combinations of an observer's internal prior, likelihood, and loss function. This degeneracy threatens the core goal of using such models to recover and understand an observer's internal representations. The paper's main contribution is a novel computational framework designed to assess, *a priori*, the identifiability of model parameters for a given experimental design.

The framework consists of two key components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, yet computationally efficient, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. This model uses mixtures of Gaussians to represent the prior, likelihood, and loss function, and a flexible sensory mapping function, allowing it to capture a wide range of observer characteristics (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise, asymmetric priors/losses). Second, they propose an efficient method to explore the "identifiability landscape." Instead of relying on computationally prohibitive simulations, they derive an analytical expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, which is proportional to the negative Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the candidate and a "true" reference model. This allows them to sample from an approximate expected posterior distribution using MCMC, where the spread of the posterior for a given parameter reveals its identifiability.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies from the psychophysics literature: (1) recovering priors in a time interval estimation task, and (2) recovering the slow-speed prior and noise characteristics in visual speed perception. The results show how the framework can be used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs and to provide theoretical validation for previous empirical findings.

### Review and Justification

This is an excellent paper that addresses a fundamental methodological problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. It is technically sound, novel, and of high significance to the NIPS community, particularly those interested in Bayesian models of cognition and brain function.

**Quality: High**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed observer model is a significant contribution in itself. By parameterizing the prior, likelihood, and loss function as mixtures of Gaussians within a transformed "internal space," the authors achieve impressive flexibility while maintaining computational tractability (e.g., enabling an analytical expression for the expected loss). This is a clever piece of engineering that makes the subsequent identifiability analysis possible.

The core methodological innovation—using the KL divergence between response distributions as a proxy for the expected log-likelihood over datasets—is elegant and well-justified. It provides a principled and efficient way to circumvent the need for massive simulations, which would be intractable for a model of this complexity. The claims are well-supported by the two case studies, which are thoughtfully chosen and effectively demonstrate the framework's power. For instance, the analysis of the time perception task yields a non-obvious and important insight: that an experimental design enforcing a near-MAP loss function could, paradoxically, *worsen* the identifiability of the prior due to a trade-off with the loss width parameter. This is exactly the kind of counter-intuitive result that makes an *a priori* analysis framework so valuable.

A minor point for improvement would be a more detailed discussion of the assumptions made about the "reference observer" (`θ*`). The conclusions drawn are contingent on this choice, and it would be useful to discuss the robustness of the findings to variations in this reference model.

**Clarity: Very Good**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem is motivated effectively in the introduction, and the four-stage observer model is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The figures are illustrative and support the main conclusions well. Figure 2, in particular, does an excellent job of visualizing the practical consequences of different experimental designs on the recovered prior distributions.

While the mathematical formalism is dense, it is necessary for the topic. The authors have done a good job of explaining the key components. The main text could perhaps be strengthened with a more intuitive explanation of how the expectation over all datasets in Eq. 11 simplifies to the KL divergence in Eq. 12, even if the full derivation remains in the supplement. Additionally, the conversion of the 2AFC task from the Stocker & Simoncelli study into an equivalent estimation task is a key step that is mentioned briefly; a sentence or two more on this in the main text would be helpful for readers not deeply familiar with that literature.

**Originality: High**

The problem of model non-identifiability is not new, as the authors acknowledge. However, previous work has often either pointed out the problem in specific cases or proposed generic statistical techniques that are hard to apply to complex, domain-specific models. This paper's contribution is a novel, concrete, and purpose-built framework that is tailored specifically for the class of Bayesian observer models widely used in perception research. The combination of the flexible generative model with the efficient KL-based analysis method is, to my knowledge, entirely new. It moves the field from a general awareness of the identifiability problem to providing a practical tool to actively diagnose and mitigate it during the experimental design phase.

**Significance: High**

The significance of this work is substantial. Bayesian observer models are a cornerstone of modern research in perception, sensorimotor control, and cognitive science. However, the field has been criticized for the potential of "just-so stories," where model flexibility allows for post-hoc explanations of any dataset. This paper provides a rigorous method to address this criticism head-on. If adopted, this framework could lead to:
1.  **Better Experimental Design:** Researchers could simulate the identifiability of their parameters of interest *before* collecting data, saving time and resources and leading to more robust conclusions.
2.  **Increased Model Scrutiny:** It provides a tool to re-evaluate the claims of previous studies, as demonstrated in the case studies.
3.  **Improved Modeling Practices:** It encourages a more thoughtful and principled approach to model building and fitting, forcing researchers to consider potential degeneracies explicitly.

The work is directly relevant to the NIPS audience, bridging machine learning methods (MCMC, probabilistic modeling) with cognitive science and neuroscience, a key area of interest for the conference.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, critical, and often-ignored problem in a popular modeling paradigm.
*   Provides a novel and technically sound computational framework as a solution.
*   The proposed observer model is a flexible and powerful contribution in its own right.
*   The case studies are compelling and demonstrate the practical utility of the framework, yielding non-trivial insights.
*   High potential for significant impact on how research using Bayesian observer models is designed and interpreted.

**Con:**
*   The framework is currently limited to 1D continuous estimation tasks, though the authors note this.
*   The analysis relies on a "reference observer," and the robustness of conclusions to this choice could be explored further.
*   The complexity of the model itself raises questions about the identifiability of its own hyperparameters (e.g., the number of mixture components), which is not discussed.

In conclusion, this is a strong, well-executed, and important paper. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future work. It represents a significant methodological advance for a key area of research represented at NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
