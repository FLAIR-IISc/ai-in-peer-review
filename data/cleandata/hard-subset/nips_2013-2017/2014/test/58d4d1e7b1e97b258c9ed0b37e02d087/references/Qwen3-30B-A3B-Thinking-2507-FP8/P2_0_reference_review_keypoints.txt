- Paper addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models and proposes an a priori framework using expected log likelihood to assess model distinguishability.
- Derives a theoretically grounded relation (Eq. 12) between expected log likelihood and KL divergence, giving solid mathematical footing.
- Presents a flexible, computationally efficient parametrization of Bayesian observer models enabling model-space exploration.
- Validates the framework with two empirical case studies (interval timing and speed perception) showing how experimental design affects identifiability.
- Novel application of identifiability analysis tailored to Bayesian perception models, emphasizing a priori experimental design.
- Exceptionally clear writing, organization, and visualizations (Figures 2–3) with sufficient supplementary details for reproducibility.
- Demonstrates practical significance by revealing counterintuitive effects (e.g., narrow loss functions can worsen prior recovery) that impact experimental design.
- Scope limited to one-dimensional stimulus estimation with specific parametric assumptions, so results may not generalize to multi-dimensional or discrete-choice tasks.
- Results depend on a chosen reference observer model (θ*), and robustness to misspecification of that reference is not explored.
- Computational burden may grow for high-dimensional parameter spaces due to MCMC sampling from the approximate posterior, with no clear scaling guidance.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-executed, and novel methodological contribution that addresses a widely overlooked issue in perception modeling.
- Recommendation: accept — the paper substantially improves methodology for Bayesian perception research and should be adopted by the community.
- Pro-accept: addresses a fundamental methodological issue, provides systematic a priori identifiability analysis, strong theory and empirical validation, practical experimental implications, and clear presentation.
- Con-reject: limited scope for more complex paradigms, some computational cost concerns, and reliance on assumptions about the reference observer.
