- Tackles practical non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss in Bayesian observer models and proposes a two-part solution: flexible 1D model class + an a priori identifiability analysis.

- Model contributions: parametric task→internal mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians measurement/noise and loss, and Gaussian-mixture priors enabling fast evaluation over high-dimensional θ.

- Identifiability contribution: derives expected log-marginal likelihood as a sum of KL divergences across stimuli (Eq. 12) and samples an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) to visualize confounds.

- Case studies (interval timing, speed perception) demonstrate design comparisons and reveal parameter confounds (e.g., sensory vs motor noise; MAP-like loss hurting prior recovery).

- Strength: addresses an important, under-discussed methodological problem with a task-general, pre-experiment identifiability tool.

- Strength: core derivation is sound and sampling E(θ|θ*) is a practical surrogate for costly dataset-level simulations.

- Strength: modeling choices are flexible and computationally tractable, enabling high-dimensional design exploration.

- Strength: case studies provide interpretable, actionable guidance for experiment design (e.g., calibrating motor noise, multi-condition trade-offs).

- Weakness: inadequate positioning relative to prior identifiability/design literature (Fisher information, expected information gain, profile likelihood, cognitive-model diagnostics).

- Weakness: “maximum-entropy” mixture construction is under-specified—unclear how unimodality is enforced, whether solutions are unique, and whether procedure is numerically stable.

- Weakness: E(θ|θ*) omits priors over θ and may overstate identifiability; need to report local curvature (Hessian/Fisher) or incorporate weakly informative priors/regularization.

- Weakness: strong modeling assumptions (loss depends only on t̂−t in internal space; mapping f(s) shares scale with noise) create potential scale/confound issues that are not discussed.

- Weakness: potential to optimize stimulus placements/frequencies using Eq. 12 is not exploited—no explicit design optimization demonstrated.

- Weakness: mapping of 2AFC to estimation in the speed case is asserted but not detailed; discretization and choice noise could alter identifiability.

- Weakness: missing empirical/implementation details (MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, runtimes, numerical stability) important for reproducibility and scalability claims.

- Quality: technically sound core derivation and thoughtful case studies, but lacking implementation details and robustness checks.

- Clarity: generally well written but dense; notation is consistent yet heavy; some text glitches and incomplete references need fixing; supplement should detail the entropy procedure.

- Originality: sensible synthesis of known components; expected log-likelihood/KL measure is classical but useful in this practical application with efficient mixture machinery.

- Significance: addresses a pervasive problem and could influence experimental practice if code is released and the framework is extended.

- Suggestions: provide algorithmic details for entropy-based mixtures and unimodality enforcement; report Hessian/Fisher and compare to MCMC landscapes; demonstrate a simple stimulus-design optimization; clarify prior-weight constraints and add regularization; test robustness to model mismatch; detail 2AFC→estimation equivalence.

- Pros for acceptance: important methodological gap, solid framework, informative case studies, and high practical value for experimenters.

- Cons for acceptance: limited connection to existing identifiability/design literatures, missing technical diagnostics, and no demonstrated design optimization.

- Recommendation: weak accept conditional on improved exposition of mixture/entropy methods, added diagnostics (Hessian/MCMC), and stronger links to optimal design/identifiability theory.
