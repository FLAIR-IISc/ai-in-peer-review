This paper tackles the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue given the increasing reliance on these models to infer underlying cognitive mechanisms. The authors correctly point out the degeneracy inherent in the Bayesian framework – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can lead to the same observed behavior – and propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability a priori. The framework centers on efficiently exploring the parameter space of a generalized Bayesian observer model and quantifying the overlap in predicted behavior for different parameter settings. They demonstrate the utility of their approach with two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is fundamental to the validity of inferences drawn from Bayesian models. Addressing this issue is a significant contribution.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework offers a systematic way to assess identifiability, moving beyond ad-hoc approaches.  The use of KL-divergence to quantify similarity of predictions is appropriate and well-justified.
* **Generalizable Model:** The Bayesian observer model introduced in Section 2 appears flexible and capable of encompassing a wide range of perceptual tasks. The parametrization, while complex, aims for computational efficiency, which is crucial for the exploration of the parameter space.
* **Illustrative Examples:** The two case studies effectively demonstrate the application of the framework and highlight its potential to inform experimental design. The time interval estimation example is particularly compelling, showing how the framework can reveal weaknesses in certain experimental setups.
* **Thorough Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed method, with clear derivations and justifications for the chosen approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors emphasize computational efficiency, exploring the parameter space of even this relatively constrained model will be computationally demanding for more complex tasks or models.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required and potential scalability issues.
* **Model Assumptions:** The framework relies on several assumptions about the observer model (e.g., Gaussian distributions, specific functional forms for priors and likelihoods). While these assumptions are reasonable, their impact on the identifiability results is not fully explored. A sensitivity analysis varying these assumptions would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** The case studies, while insightful, are relatively simple. Applying the framework to more complex perceptual tasks (e.g., involving multiple cues, more intricate prior structures, or dynamic stimuli) would further validate its generalizability.
* **Interpretation of Results:** The link between the identifiability analysis and concrete experimental recommendations could be stronger. For example, in the time interval estimation example, while the authors identify that measuring motor parameters improves identifiability, they don’t provide explicit guidelines for *how* to best measure those parameters.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, some of the notation is dense and requires careful attention.  A clearer organization and potentially a table of notation would improve readability.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear and logical presentation of the framework and results. The claims are generally well-supported by theoretical analysis and the case studies. While it doesn't represent a complete solution to the identifiability problem, it is a substantial step forward.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly clearly written, but some sections (particularly those with heavy mathematical notation) could benefit from further simplification. The organization is generally good, with a logical flow from motivation to method to results.

**Originality:** The framework proposed in this paper is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. While the issue of identifiability has been recognized before, the authors provide the first systematic method for addressing it a priori.

**Significance:** The results of this paper have the potential to significantly impact the way Bayesian models of perception are used and interpreted. By providing a tool for assessing identifiability, the authors can help researchers design more informative experiments and draw more reliable conclusions about underlying cognitive mechanisms.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial and often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* Proposes a novel and theoretically sound framework for assessing identifiability.
* Demonstrates the utility of the framework with compelling case studies.
* Has the potential to significantly improve the rigor and interpretability of research in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity could limit the applicability of the framework to more complex models.
* Relies on several assumptions that may not hold in all cases.
* The link between identifiability analysis and concrete experimental recommendations could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to NIPS and recommend acceptance. The strengths of the paper – its theoretical rigor, novel framework, and potential impact – outweigh its weaknesses. Addressing the minor points raised above (e.g., discussing computational complexity, conducting a sensitivity analysis, and strengthening the experimental recommendations) would further enhance the paper's quality.  This work will be valuable to researchers working with Bayesian models of perception and contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the limitations and possibilities of this powerful approach.
