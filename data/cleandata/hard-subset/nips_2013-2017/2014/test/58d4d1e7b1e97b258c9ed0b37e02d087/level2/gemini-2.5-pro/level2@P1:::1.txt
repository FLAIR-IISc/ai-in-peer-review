This paper addresses the critical and often-underappreciated issue of model identifiability within the popular framework of Bayesian observer models for perception. The authors correctly point out that since different combinations of internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function) can lead to identical observed behavior, the "inverse problem" of recovering these representations from data is fundamentally ill-posed. The main contribution is a novel framework to systematically and quantitatively assess the identifiability of a model's parameters *a priori*, before an experiment is run. This allows researchers to compare and improve experimental designs to maximize the chances of reliably recovering the parameters of interest.

The proposed framework consists of two core components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible yet computationally tractable class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks. This model parameterizes the sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss function using flexible forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians) that are defined by their statistical moments, allowing a wide range of observer strategies to be captured. Second, they introduce an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape." Instead of relying on computationally expensive simulations, they derive an analytical expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model `θ` given a "ground truth" reference model `θ*`. This expectation is shown to be a function of the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" based on this quantity, one can visualize which regions of the parameter space are confusable with the true model for a given experimental design.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two compelling case studies. In a time interval estimation task, the analysis reveals a significant degeneracy between sensory noise and motor noise, which hinders the accurate recovery of the observer's prior. The framework is used to show that an experimental design where motor noise is measured independently is far superior to simply increasing the number of trials. Interestingly, the analysis also suggests that enforcing a MAP-like loss function can, perhaps counter-intuitively, worsen the recovery of the prior. In a second study on speed perception, the framework is used to re-examine the classic finding of a "slow-speed prior." Here, the analysis shows that the inference of both the prior and the sensory noise characteristics is quite robust, providing additional theoretical support for the original conclusions.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is well-founded, and the derivation of the expected log-likelihood in terms of KL divergence is elegant and correct. The parameterization of the observer model components is a clever engineering choice that balances flexibility with computational tractability, which is essential for the feasibility of the MCMC exploration. The claims are well-supported by the results of the case studies, which provide clear and insightful demonstrations of the framework's power. The authors are careful to frame this as an *a priori* tool, which is its main strength.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for tackling the identifiability problem is laid out compellingly in the introduction. The four-stage observer model and the identifiability analysis method are explained in a logical, step-by-step manner. The figures, particularly Figures 2 and 3, are effective at visualizing the identifiability landscapes and comparing the outcomes of different experimental designs. The connection between the abstract framework and its practical application is made very clear through the case studies.

**Originality:** While the problem of non-identifiability in cognitive models is not new, previous approaches have often relied on making strong, simplifying assumptions or applying ad-hoc fixes. The primary novelty of this work lies in providing a systematic, general, and quantitative framework for analyzing the problem before data collection. The idea of using the expected log-likelihood to define an "identifiability landscape" that can be explored via MCMC is a significant and original methodological contribution. This moves the field from simply acknowledging the problem to having a concrete tool to address it proactively.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. Bayesian observer models are a cornerstone of modern computational cognitive science and neuroscience. However, the validity of the conclusions drawn from them hinges on the ability to reliably identify their components. This paper provides a much-needed tool for ensuring the robustness of such inferences. The framework has the potential to change how experiments are designed in the field, encouraging researchers to explicitly test for and mitigate potential degeneracies in their models. The insights from the case studies (e.g., the critical importance of constraining motor noise) are in themselves valuable contributions to their respective domains. This work is likely to be highly influential and widely used by other researchers.

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, important, and often-ignored methodological problem in a popular modeling paradigm.
*   Proposes a novel, powerful, and computationally efficient framework for a priori identifiability analysis.
*   Provides a practical tool that can be used to compare and improve experimental designs.
*   The case studies are well-chosen and yield non-trivial insights that are valuable in their own right.

**Weaknesses/Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The observer model, while flexible, is also complex, with a large number of parameters. The success of the analysis may depend on having a reasonable "reference observer" `θ*` to begin with. A brief discussion on the robustness of the conclusions to the choice of this reference model would be welcome.
*   The framework is presented for 1D estimation tasks. While the authors mention potential extensions, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the challenges or modifications needed to apply this framework to other common paradigms, such as discrete-choice (e.g., 2AFC) or multi-dimensional tasks.
*   The conversion of the 2AFC task from the original speed perception study [9] into an estimation task is a necessary simplification for the current framework. It would be beneficial to explicitly acknowledge and briefly discuss the potential implications of this modeling choice.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong methodological contribution to the field. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of high significance. The proposed framework provides a valuable new tool for any researcher using Bayesian models to infer latent cognitive structures.
