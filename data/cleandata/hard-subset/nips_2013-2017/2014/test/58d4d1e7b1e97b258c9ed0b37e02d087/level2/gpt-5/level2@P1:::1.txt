Summary
The paper addresses a central and under-discussed issue in Bayesian observer modeling for perception: identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss) from behavioral data. The authors propose (i) a flexible, unified parametric class of 1D estimation tasks that captures sensation, prior, loss, lapses, and response noise with analytic or fast-to-evaluate components, and (ii) an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model θ given data from a “true” observer θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions across stimulus conditions. They then define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(E log L) and explore identifiability via MCMC. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (where reconstruction of priors is fragile unless additional constraints, e.g., known motor noise, are imposed) and visual speed perception under a slow-speed prior (where recovery of prior exponent and noise characteristics is relatively robust). The framework also supports comparing alternative experimental designs.

Relation to prior work
The identifiability question is related to classical analyses of distinguishability and model selection in cognitive science (e.g., Myung/Pitt/Navarro) and to ongoing debates on the limits of Bayesian modeling in perception (e.g., Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love). The construction of the observer class builds on psychophysical work on priors and noise (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.; Girshick et al.). The technical reduction to expected log-likelihood equals negative KL is standard, but its packaging into a practical identifiability-mapping tool for Bayesian observer models, combined with a flexible parametric family, is novel and useful in this domain.

Quality
The derivation linking expected log-likelihood to stimulus-weighted KL divergences is sound, and the use of that quantity to probe identifiability is appropriate. The observer class is thoughtfully designed: a monotone sensory map to stabilize likelihood scale, mixtures to capture asymmetry/kurtosis, a tractable loss family with analytic expected loss, and explicit reporting noise and lapses. The two case studies are carefully specified and the qualitative conclusions are plausible (e.g., confusability of sensory vs motor noise; sensitivity to the loss width; robustness of speed prior exponent). Some caveats: conclusions necessarily depend on the assumed parametric families (two-Gaussian mixtures, fixed lattice prior representation, specific sensory map). Computational cost and MCMC diagnostics are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a logical flow from model construction to identifiability mapping to applications. The assumptions in each case study are explicit, and the role of experimental design choices is well explained. It would help to more clearly separate what is fixed vs inferred in each scenario, and to summarize sensitivity analyses in the main text.

Originality
While each component (parametric observer, KL-based expected evidence, simulation-based parameter recovery) has precedents, their integration into a systematic, a priori identifiability framework for Bayesian perception models is original and timely. The design-oriented insights (e.g., when narrowing the loss harms identifiability) are particularly valuable.

Significance
The work addresses a real need in a field where inferences about internal representations are widespread but often underconstrained. Providing a practical tool to assess identifiability and compare experimental designs can impact how experiments are planned and interpreted. The scope is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks, but the approach is extensible.

Specific strengths
- Clear, principled identifiability metric tied to expected log-evidence, with intuitive dependence on Ntr and stimulus distribution.
- Flexible observer class enabling rich priors/likelihoods/losses while retaining computational tractability.
- Concrete, design-relevant insights in two canonical domains; identifies key degeneracies (sensory vs motor noise; weak identifiability of loss width).
- Transparent assumptions and explicit sharing of parameters across conditions.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Model dependence: Results may hinge on the choice of two-component mixtures and the prior lattice. Suggest reporting sensitivity to the number of mixture components and to alternative families (e.g., log-normal, Student-t, heavy-tailed losses).
- Local vs global identifiability: Consider complementing the KL-based global mapping with local analyses (Fisher information; profile likelihoods) to diagnose which parameters are weakly constrained.
- Experimental design optimization: The framework naturally suggests optimizing over stimulus sets and frequencies Pi (e.g., D- or A-optimal designs). Including an example would increase impact.
- Computational details: Please report MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, effective sample sizes, and runtime to assess practicality.
- Prior distributions over parameters: E(θ|θ*) omits parameter priors. For design ranking, this is fine, but including reasonable priors could stabilize exploration and reflect realistic constraints.
- Interval timing claims: The sensitivity of prior recovery to σℓ suggests caution in inferring loss properties; consider validating via independent tasks or including control analyses where loss is externally manipulated.
- Speed prior case: The assumption of no task-induced learning is strong. A robustness check with mild adaptation, or discussion of how within-experiment learning would affect identifiability, would be helpful.
- Reporting noise: The parametric σreport(s) may confound with sensory noise in some regimes; quantify where these are separable in your KL landscape.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are your conclusions to the choice M = 15 for the prior lattice and to the mixture assumption (two vs more Gaussians) for likelihood and loss?
- Can you quantify identifiability via credible region widths or Cramér–Rao bounds for key parameters (e.g., σ, ρ1, σℓ, kprior) as a function of Ntr?
- Have you explored optimizing stimulus placements Pi using your KL criterion to automatically propose better designs?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, pervasive problem with a principled and practical framework.
- Generates actionable design guidance and nontrivial insights.
- Technically sound and well integrated with existing literature.
- Likely to be used by researchers modeling perceptual behavior.

Cons for acceptance
- Results are simulation-based and somewhat model-class dependent; no empirical validation of predicted design improvements.
- Scope limited to 1D estimation tasks; extensions to discrete choice (2AFC) and multi-cue integration are deferred.
- Missing computational/practical details and design optimization demonstration.

Overall evaluation
A solid and useful contribution that should stimulate better practices in modeling and experimental design in Bayesian perception. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing sensitivity analyses, computational details, and (if feasible) a brief design optimization example.
