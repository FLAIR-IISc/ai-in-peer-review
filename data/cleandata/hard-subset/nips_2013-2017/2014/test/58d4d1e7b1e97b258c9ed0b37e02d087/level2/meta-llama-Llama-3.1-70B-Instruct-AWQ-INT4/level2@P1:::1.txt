This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors develop a flexible and efficient class of Bayesian observer models that can be used to model a wide range of psychophysical tasks. They then introduce a method for exploring the identifiability of these models within their framework, which involves computing the expected support for a given model as the log likelihood of the data given the model.

The authors apply their framework to two case studies: the inference of priors in a time interval estimation task and the reconstruction of prior and noise characteristics in speed perception. The results show that the framework can be used to rank experimental designs by their ability to resolve the underlying degeneracy of parameters of interest. For example, in the time interval estimation task, the authors find that experimentally imposing a narrow loss function can be detrimental to the inference of priors.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of psychophysics by providing a systematic approach to testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The framework is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their methods and results. The case studies are well-chosen and demonstrate the utility of the framework in practice.
