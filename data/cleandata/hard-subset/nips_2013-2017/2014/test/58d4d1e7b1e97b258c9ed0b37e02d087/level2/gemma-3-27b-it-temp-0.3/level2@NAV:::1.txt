## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the inherent non-identifiability of model parameters. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing the identifiability of a class of Bayesian observer models, aiming to improve experimental design and ensure reliable recovery of internal representations. The core idea – leveraging KL divergence to quantify the practical indistinguishability of models – is well-motivated and potentially impactful. 

The paper builds upon the established success of Bayesian models in explaining perceptual phenomena (citing key works like Knill & Richards, Körding & Wolpert) and acknowledges the degeneracy problem highlighted by previous research (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli). The proposed framework is presented clearly, with a well-defined observer model incorporating priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and noise. The analytical formulation, particularly the derivation linking identifiability to KL divergence, appears sound. 

The strength of the paper lies in its application to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. The time interval estimation results are particularly insightful, demonstrating how experimental design (specifically, incorporating motor noise measurements) can significantly improve identifiability. The validation against existing findings in speed perception further strengthens the framework’s credibility.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the framework is presented generally, the specific observer model relies on several assumptions (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, specific functional forms for sensory mapping) that may limit its applicability. The analysis focuses heavily on parameter identifiability, potentially overlooking other sources of non-identifiability related to model structure itself. The discussion of the MAP condition in the time interval estimation task feels somewhat underdeveloped – why does a narrower loss function *worsen* reconstruction? Further exploration of this counterintuitive finding would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for assessing identifiability.
*   Provides practical insights into experimental design.
*   Demonstrates the framework’s utility through compelling case studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on specific assumptions within the observer model.
*   Limited exploration of non-identifiability beyond parameter estimation.
*   Some results (e.g., MAP condition) require further explanation.

**Overall:**

This is a valuable contribution to the field. While the framework isn’t a panacea for all identifiability issues, it provides a powerful tool for researchers to critically evaluate their Bayesian models and design more informative experiments. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a more detailed discussion of the MAP condition and a clearer articulation of the framework’s limitations. The authors should also consider encouraging further work on extending the framework to more complex models and tasks.
