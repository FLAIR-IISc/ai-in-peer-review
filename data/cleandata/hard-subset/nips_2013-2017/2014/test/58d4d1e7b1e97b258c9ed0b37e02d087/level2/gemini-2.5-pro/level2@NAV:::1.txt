This paper addresses the critical and often-overlooked problem of model identifiability in the context of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that the inverse problem of inferring an observer's internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function) from behavior is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavioral data. The central contribution is a novel computational framework for systematically testing the *a priori* identifiability of these models. The framework consists of a flexible, parametric class of observer models and an efficient method, based on the Kullback-Leibler divergence between response distributions, to map the "identifiability landscape" for a given experimental design. This work builds upon the large body of research using Bayesian models to understand perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010) but provides a much-needed tool for assessing the validity and uniqueness of the inferences drawn from such models.

The paper’s main strength lies in its significance and originality. It provides researchers with a principled tool to evaluate and improve experimental designs *before* data collection, potentially saving significant resources and preventing erroneous conclusions. The technical approach is sound, and the two case studies convincingly demonstrate the framework's utility. The analysis of the time interval estimation task yields a non-obvious and important insight: an experimental design that encourages a MAP-like loss function may, under the model's assumptions, actually worsen the ability to recover the observer's prior. Conversely, the successful validation of the robust inference of a slow-speed prior serves as an excellent positive control, lending credibility to the method.

The primary weakness concerns the model's complexity and current scope. The proposed observer model has many parameters, which could be a barrier to adoption. Releasing the code for the framework would significantly increase its impact and aid in reproducibility. Furthermore, the framework is currently limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks. While the authors acknowledge this, the conversion of the 2AFC paradigm from the speed perception literature into an equivalent estimation task is mentioned without sufficient detail, and the assumptions underlying this equivalence could affect the conclusions. A brief discussion on the robustness of the findings to the choice of the "reference observer" would also strengthen the analysis.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution. It tackles a fundamental issue in computational cognitive science with a novel and practical solution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
