This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors present a general class of observer models that can be used to model a wide range of psychophysical tasks, and develop an efficient method for exploring the identifiability of these models. The framework is applied to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The introduction provides a thorough review of the relevant literature and clearly motivates the need for a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.

The authors' approach to modeling the observer is flexible and efficient, allowing for the exploration of a large class of models. The use of a maximum-entropy approach to determine the shape of the measurement distribution and the loss function is a nice touch, as it allows for the incorporation of prior knowledge while still maintaining a degree of flexibility.

The application of the framework to the two case studies is well-done, and the results are interesting and non-trivial. The authors' finding that the reconstruction of internal representations in interval timing requires strong experimental constraints and validations is particularly noteworthy, as it highlights the importance of careful experimental design in this field.

The paper's strengths include its clear and concise writing style, its thorough review of the relevant literature, and its well-executed application of the framework to the two case studies. The authors' approach to modeling the observer is also a major strength, as it allows for the exploration of a wide range of models and provides a flexible framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors' framework may not be directly applicable to all types of psychophysical tasks. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest several modifications that could be made to increase the scope of the framework. However, it is not entirely clear how these modifications would affect the results of the case studies, and further work would be needed to fully explore the limitations of the framework.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of psychophysics. The authors' framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception is a valuable tool that could be used to improve experimental design and advance our understanding of human perception.
