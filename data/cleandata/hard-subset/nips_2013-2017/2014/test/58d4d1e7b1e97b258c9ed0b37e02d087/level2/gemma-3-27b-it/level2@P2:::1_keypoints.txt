- Addresses the important and under-explored problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.  
- Proposes a novel, concrete framework for systematically testing identifiability a priori to guide experimental design.  
- Develops a general yet tractable Bayesian observer model using a parametric sensory map (Eq. 2).  
- Paper is well-written with clear mathematical derivations and supporting supplementary material.  
- Two illustrative case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) demonstrate the framework’s utility and practical insights.  
- Time-interval case highlights experimental design importance (e.g., measuring motor noise) for reliable parameter recovery.  
- Validation on speed perception and link to prior work strengthens credibility of the approach.  
- Exploring identifiability via MCMC remains computationally demanding, limiting scalability to high-dimensional models.  
- Results are sensitive to the choice of reference observer (θ*); robustness across different reference observers is not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Simplifying assumptions (unimodal likelihoods, Gaussian noise) may restrict generalizability; non-Gaussian cases are not addressed.  
- Experimental design analysis is limited in scope; broader manipulations (stimulus sampling, number of conditions, instructions) are needed.  
- Paper could better connect to existing identifiability methods (e.g., profile likelihood, information criteria) from statistics/ML.  
- Request more details on computational implementation: KL divergence calculation, MCMC specifics, and runtime estimates.  
- Ask for quantitative measures of reconstruction error (e.g., RMSE, confidence intervals) instead of vague statements.  
- Suggest discussion of why speed perception appears more robust to identifiability issues than time-interval estimation.  
- Recommend expanding how the framework could guide model selection beyond parameter identifiability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical problem, offers a novel practical framework, demonstrates utility via case studies, and is clearly presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, sensitivity to reference observer, and simplifying assumptions may limit applicability.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution with strengths outweighing weaknesses; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address computational, sensitivity, and limitation concerns.
