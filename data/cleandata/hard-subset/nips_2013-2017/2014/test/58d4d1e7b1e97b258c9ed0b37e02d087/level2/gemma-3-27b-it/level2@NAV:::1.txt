## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a problem arising from the inherent degeneracy where multiple parameter combinations can yield the same observed behavior. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing this identifiability, with the ultimate goal of improving experimental design. They demonstrate its utility through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. 

The core contribution lies in providing a practical method, leveraging Kullback-Leibler divergence, to explore the parameter space and assess the sensitivity of model inference to different experimental setups. The framework is built on a flexible observer model incorporating key elements of Bayesian perception, allowing for efficient computation and exploration of the model space. 

The paper’s strengths are its clear articulation of the identifiability problem, the well-motivated methodological approach, and the concrete examples demonstrating its application. The time interval estimation example yields actionable insights – specifically, that relying on narrow loss functions can hinder accurate prior reconstruction and that independent measurement of motor noise is crucial. The validation on speed perception, reaffirming existing findings, lends credibility to the framework. The inclusion of supplementary material detailing derivations and implementation details enhances reproducibility. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the framework is presented as general, the choice of Gaussian distributions for priors and likelihoods feels limiting.  Exploring the impact of different distributional assumptions would strengthen the robustness claim. Furthermore, the analysis largely focuses on a specific observer model; the extent to which the findings generalize to other Bayesian models within perception remains unclear. The discussion of the MAP condition is slightly underwhelming, and the reason for the worsened reconstruction could be further investigated. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper tackles an important methodological challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception, provides a novel and practical framework, and delivers actionable insights through well-chosen case studies. The clarity of writing and strong theoretical foundation make it a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:** The framework’s reliance on Gaussian assumptions and limited generalization to other model structures represent potential limitations. More extensive validation, beyond the two provided examples, would bolster the claims.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations related to distributional assumptions and explore the generalizability of their findings. Encouraging them to publicly release the implementation code would further enhance the impact of this work.
