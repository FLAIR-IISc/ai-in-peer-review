Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses a central and timely issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: practical non-identifiability of latent components (priors, likelihoods, loss) from behavioral data. Building on the observation that multiple combinations of these elements can yield indistinguishable optimal decisions (and formalizing a multiplicative φ1·φ2·φ3 degeneracy), the work proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability for a broad class of one-dimensional estimation tasks. The framework comprises: (1) a flexible, computationally efficient observer model that parameterizes sensory mapping (via a log/Weber-Fechner–like transform), measurement noise (mixtures of Gaussians with moment constraints), priors (Gaussian mixture lattice), loss functions (mixtures of inverted Gaussians), lapses, and motor/report noise; and (2) an identifiability metric based on the expected log marginal likelihood under a reference observer θ∗, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli (Eq. 12). The resulting “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp〈log Pr(D|θ)〉 is explored by MCMC to map regions of parameter space that are indistinguishable given a design (stimulus set, trial counts, shared parameters).

Two case studies illustrate the approach. For interval timing, identifiability of the prior is fragile under common designs; fixing motor noise dramatically improves recovery, whereas making the loss narrow (MAP-like) can worsen identifiability. For speed perception with a slow-speed prior, the reconstruction of the prior exponent and noise parameters is comparatively robust, even when decoupling internal from objective likelihoods. The framework relates to model distinguishability and design informativeness (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, Myung, 2004) but is tailored to Bayesian observer models with explicit computational efficiency.

Strengths:
- Tackles an important gap: how to evaluate, before data collection, whether a Bayesian observer model’s components can be uniquely inferred under a given design.
- Clear, principled identifiability measure derived from expected log likelihood (KL-based), avoiding costly dataset resampling.
- A flexible yet efficient observer model enables large-scale exploration (analytic mixture structure for expected loss and tractable response predictions).
- Actionable insights for experimental design in interval timing (e.g., benefit of independently measuring motor noise; potential pitfalls of narrow loss) and validation in speed perception (robust recovery of slow-speed prior).
- Thoughtful discussion of how design choices (conditions, shared parameters, feedback shaping) impact identifiability.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Parametric scope and robustness. The modelling choices (two-component Gaussian mixtures for measurement and loss, fixed lattice prior basis, specific f(s) family) may constrain conclusions. A sensitivity analysis is encouraged: vary the number of mixture components, prior lattice spacing/range, and the form of f(s), and report how design rankings and key identifiability conclusions change.
- Relation to optimal experimental design. The KL-based criterion is closely related to expected information gain. A brief comparison and, if feasible, a demonstration of design optimization (e.g., selecting stimulus locations or frequencies to maximize expected KL/entropy reduction) would materially strengthen the contribution and move beyond post hoc design ranking.
- Scope beyond 1D estimation. Although future extensions are mentioned, many psychophysical studies use discrete-choice paradigms (2AFC). Providing a direct version of the framework for 2AFC (even in Supplementary) or an empirical check that the estimation surrogate yields equivalent identifiability would increase impact.
- Lapse modeling. Lapses are drawn from the prior; alternative lapse models (e.g., uniform in response space, or condition-dependent distributions) might alter identifiability conclusions. Including a brief robustness check would be helpful.
- Practical guidance and scaling. The MCMC-based exploration in a high-dimensional space (including many prior mixture weights) invites questions on computational cost and convergence. Reporting run times, diagnostics, and sample sizes, and providing rules-of-thumb for required trial numbers (curves of identifiability vs Ntr) would improve usability for practitioners.
- Empirical corroboration. The study is theoretical/simulated. A small empirical demonstration (e.g., reanalysis of an existing dataset to test predicted gains from fixing motor noise) would add credibility. If not feasible, a stronger argument for external validity via simulation diversity (multiple θ∗ draws rather than single exemplars) would help.

Quality:
The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated. The KL-based derivation is appropriate, and the analytic mixture machinery supports efficient computation. Some claims about generality would benefit from additional robustness analyses, but overall the work is careful and transparent about assumptions.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The four-stage observer decomposition is helpful. A compact summary figure of the framework (assumptions, design inputs, outputs) and a table of parameters could aid readability. More explicit algorithmic details (sampler, priors, convergence criteria) would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
The integration of a general observer model with an a priori identifiability mapping tailored to perceptual Bayesian models appears novel. While identifiability and design informativeness have prior treatments, this work provides a specialized, computationally tractable approach for Bayesian observer models that is not present in prior NIPS proceedings to this reviewer’s knowledge.

Significance:
The contribution is practically important for a large community using Bayesian models in perception and decision-making. The framework can influence how experiments are designed and interpreted, helping avoid overconfident inferences about internal representations. The case studies offer concrete guidance with potential to shape future work.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and under-discussed problem with a principled, usable framework.
- Provides new insights into design choices for canonical tasks.
- Methodologically solid with clear connections to information-theoretic principles.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric forms; limited exploration of robustness.
- No automated design optimization or empirical validation; evaluations are simulation-based.
- Sparse details on computational efficiency and convergence.

Recommended improvements (feasible pre-camera-ready):
- Add sensitivity analyses for mixture complexity and f(s) form; report impact on design ranking.
- Provide computational details and convergence diagnostics; include identifiability vs Ntr curves.
- Clarify relation to expected information gain and, if possible, include a small design optimization example.
- Brief robustness check for alternative lapse models.

Overall recommendation:
A strong and useful methodological contribution with clear relevance to the NIPS community. With added robustness analyses and clearer ties to optimal design, this work would be highly impactful. Lean accept.
