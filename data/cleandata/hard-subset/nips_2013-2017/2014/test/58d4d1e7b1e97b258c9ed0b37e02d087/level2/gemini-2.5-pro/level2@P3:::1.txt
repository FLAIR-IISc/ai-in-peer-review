This paper presents a computational framework for systematically assessing, *a priori*, the identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The work addresses a fundamental degeneracy in Bayesian Decision Theory, where multiple combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce empirically indistinguishable behavior. The proposed solution consists of two main components: first, a flexible and computationally efficient class of observer models capable of representing a wide range of internal components within a single parametric form; second, a method to explore the "identifiability landscape" by sampling from an approximate expected posterior density, which is shown to be related to the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of a reference model and a candidate model. The framework's utility is demonstrated through two case studies: one in time interval estimation, used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs in resolving parameter degeneracies, and another in speed perception, used to validate the robustness of classic findings about a slow-speed prior. This work is timely, as the use of Bayesian models to infer mental representations is widespread, and concerns about their theoretical underpinnings and potential for over-interpretation have been raised (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

**Quality & Significance**

The paper makes a significant and high-quality contribution to the field of computational modeling of cognition. The problem of model non-identifiability is a critical and often under-appreciated issue that can undermine the conclusions drawn from fitting complex models to behavioral data. By providing a principled and practical tool to probe these issues before an experiment is even run, this work has the potential to improve the rigor and design of future studies in the field.

Strengths of the paper include:
*   **Problem Significance:** The work tackles a core challenge to the interpretation of Bayesian cognitive models. The ability to assess which model parameters can be reliably recovered from data, given a particular experimental design, is of great practical importance.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** The proposed framework is original. While identifiability analysis is a known field, the development of a specific, efficient, and systematic framework tailored to the common structure of Bayesian observer models is a novel contribution. The connection between the expected log-likelihood and the KL divergence provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The four-stage observer model is well-conceived, offering impressive flexibility through its use of parametric function classes (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians for distributions, a flexible sensory mapping). This flexibility is crucial for exploring a wide space of possible observer models. The use of this model to evaluate and rank different experimental designs in the time-perception case study is a compelling demonstration of the framework's power.
*   **Insightful Results:** The case studies yield non-trivial insights. The finding that independently measuring motor noise is the most effective way to break degeneracies in the time estimation task is plausible and provides concrete guidance. The more surprising finding that a narrow, MAP-like loss function could actually *worsen* the recovery of the prior is an excellent example of a non-obvious prediction that such an *a priori* analysis can provide. The validation of the robustness of the slow-speed prior finding is also a valuable result.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for the work is established effectively in the introduction, and the four-stage model is described in a logical, step-by-step manner. The figures are informative and support the main conclusions of the case studies.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but could be improved further with minor revisions. The following suggestions are offered to enhance the clarity and impact of the work:

1.  **Sensitivity to the Reference Observer:** The framework's analysis depends on the choice of a "reference observer" model, θ*. The conclusions drawn (e.g., about the degeneracy between sensory and motor noise) might depend on this specific choice. For instance, in the timing task, the degeneracy may be particularly strong because both noise sources in the reference model are approximately Gaussian in the internal space. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the main conclusions to the choice of θ* would be beneficial. It would strengthen the paper to comment on whether the identified degeneracies are general properties of the task structure or specific artifacts of the chosen reference parameters.

2.  **Practicality and Computational Cost:** The paper rightly emphasizes the efficiency of the framework, which is key to its practical utility. However, no quantitative information about the computational cost is provided. Including a rough indication of the runtime for the MCMC analyses (e.g., in the supplementary material) would give readers a much better sense of the framework's practicality for their own experimental design problems.

3.  **Scope and Generalization:** The conversion of the 2AFC task from the speed perception literature into an "equivalent estimation task" is a key step for the second case study. While plausible, this step is stated without much justification. A sentence or two clarifying the assumptions behind this equivalence would be helpful, as 2AFC and other discrete-choice paradigms are very common. This would help the reader better understand the current scope and potential for generalization of the framework.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and highly valuable tool for the computational modeling community. It addresses a difficult and important problem with a method that is both theoretically principled and practically useful. The strengths in originality, quality, and significance are substantial. The suggested revisions are minor and intended to further clarify the excellent work presented.
