- Paper addresses practical identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and losses in Bayesian observer modeling.
- Proposes a general observer family: mixture-based likelihoods and losses plus parametric sensory warping for flexibility and tractability.
- Introduces a priori identifiability measure via expected log-likelihood under a reference observer, reducing to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimuli.
- Uses MCMC to sample an approximate posterior over model parameters and ranks experimental designs by recovery performance.
- Case study 1 (interval timing): shows fragility of prior/likelihood/loss recovery and motor noise confounds.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): shows robustness of inferred slow-speed prior.
- Mathematical derivation linking expected evidence to KL is sound and appropriate for design evaluation.
- Results are entirely simulation-based; no empirical re-analyses or prospective experimental validation provided.
- Sensitivity to model misspecification is only qualitatively discussed, not systematically tested.
- Paper is generally well organized and models are clearly specified, but some implementation choices are deferred to the supplement.
- Requested summaries: MCMC setup, convergence diagnostics, and computational costs should be included in the main text to improve reproducibility.
- Using expected evidence/KL for identifiability is known, but the tailored observer class and its use for ranking psychophysical designs is novel and practical.
- The connection to Bayesian optimal design (e.g., expected information gain) should be made more explicit.
- High potential impact for perceptual modeling and experiment planning; findings may change interpretation of prior/likelihood/loss fits.
- Suggested validations: re-fit classic datasets and test predicted design rankings prospectively.
- Suggested robustness checks: alternative loss families, non-mixture likelihoods, nonstationary priors, decision noise.
- Report sensitivity analyses for number of mixture components M, parameter tying, and choice of f(s); provide code and convergence diagnostics.
- Consider complementing the KL approach with information-theoretic design criteria (EIG) and local identifiability (Fisher information).
- Pros: tackles important problem, provides clear general framework, and gives actionable design recommendations.
- Cons: lacks empirical validation, may be over-parameterized/model-class dependent, and has limited discussion of computational scaling and robustness.
