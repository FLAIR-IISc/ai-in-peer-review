

This paper addresses a critical methodological gap in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to the degeneracy in the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations. The authors propose a systematic framework for *a priori* identifiability testing, enabling researchers to evaluate experimental designs before data collection. The framework leverages the KL divergence between response distributions under different parameter settings to quantify identifiability, avoiding computationally intractable Monte Carlo integration. Two case studies—time interval estimation and speed perception—demonstrate the framework's utility in ranking experimental designs and identifying problematic parameter confounds.

**Strengths:**  
The paper's core contribution—providing a practical, theoretically grounded method for identifiability analysis—is highly valuable and timely. The authors clearly articulate the problem (Section 1), distinguish their work from prior efforts (e.g., [12,19] focus on model fitting, not design), and validate their approach through two compelling case studies. The time interval example reveals counterintuitive insights (e.g., imposing a MAP-like loss *worsens* prior recovery), while the speed perception study confirms robustness of prior inferences, aligning with prior findings [9,29]. The framework's flexibility (e.g., handling multiple conditions, motor noise) and computational efficiency (via analytical KL divergence) are well-justified. The writing is clear, the model is well-motivated, and the case studies effectively showcase the framework's practical impact on experimental design.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Computational Scalability:** While the authors claim "fast computation," they omit benchmarking against alternative methods (e.g., grid search or MCMC-based identifiability checks). For complex models (e.g., with high-dimensional θ), the MCMC sampling in Eq. 13 could become prohibitive. A brief discussion of scaling with model complexity (e.g., M, number of parameters) would strengthen the claim.  
2. **Assumption Justification:** The sensory mapping (Eq. 2) is chosen without justification beyond "approximating Weber-Fechner’s law." Why not test alternative mappings (e.g., power-law with fixed exponent)? The paper should acknowledge that the framework's validity depends on this parametrization.  
3. **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** The speed perception case study assumes a "natural" prior, but the framework’s applicability to *learned* priors (e.g., in context-dependent tasks [11,12]) is unexplored. The authors note this in Section 5 but should explicitly address how their framework handles such scenarios.  
4. **Omission of Key Prior Work:** The paper cites Navarro et al. [23] on model distinguishability but misses the 2012 NIPS paper by Myung et al. on "model selection" (which addresses similar identifiability challenges in cognitive science). This omission weakens the context for the contribution.  

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
- Add a subsection (e.g., "Computational Considerations") discussing runtime scaling with θ dimensionality and proposing heuristics for high-dimensional cases (e.g., dimensionality reduction).  
- In Section 2.1, briefly justify Eq. 2’s form (e.g., "We adopt this form as it encompasses common sensory models in psychophysics [9,24] and enables analytical tractability").  
- Extend the speed perception case study to include a scenario where priors are *learned* (e.g., from prior trials), testing if the framework identifies confounds between learning and prior recovery.  
- Cite Myung et al. (2012) in the related work to better position the contribution against existing model-identifiability literature.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong, methodologically rigorous paper with significant potential to improve experimental design in perceptual modeling. The framework fills a critical gap, and the case studies convincingly demonstrate its utility. While minor weaknesses exist (mainly around scalability and assumption justification), they are easily addressable. The work is original (not covered in recent NIPS proceedings), significant (directly impacts how Bayesian models are validated), and technically sound. The clarity of presentation and practical focus make it highly suitable for NIPS.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper’s contribution is timely, well-executed, and addresses a fundamental challenge in the field. The suggested revisions are minor and would further enhance its impact.
