- Paper addresses practical non-identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian perceptual models and formalizes a multiplicative φ1·φ2·φ3 degeneracy.  
- Proposes a framework for a priori identifiability in 1D estimation tasks using a flexible observer model and an expected-log-marginal-likelihood identifiability metric.  
- Observer model parameterizes sensory mapping (Weber–Fechner–like f), measurement noise (Gaussian mixtures), priors (Gaussian-mixture lattice), loss (inverted-Gaussian mixtures), lapses, and motor/report noise.  
- Identifiability metric reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions and is explored via MCMC to map indistinguishable parameter regions.  
- Case study 1 (interval timing): prior identifiability is fragile; fixing motor noise helps, while narrow (MAP-like) loss can worsen identifiability.  
- Case study 2 (speed perception): recovery of slow-speed prior exponent and noise is relatively robust, even when decoupling internal and objective likelihoods.  
- Strength: tackles an important practical gap for inferring internal model components before data collection.  
- Strength: principled KL-based identifiability measure that avoids costly data resampling.  
- Strength: computationally efficient, flexible observer model enabling large-scale exploration.  
- Strength: provides actionable experimental-design insights (e.g., measure motor noise; beware narrow loss).  
- Strength: thoughtful discussion of how design choices and shared parameters affect identifiability.  
- Weakness: restrictive parametric choices (two-component mixtures, fixed lattice, specific f(s)) may limit generality—sensitivity analyses recommended.  
- Weakness: limited engagement with optimal experimental design literature and no demonstration of design optimization.  
- Weakness: framework currently limited to 1D estimation; lacks direct extension or validation for 2AFC/discrete-choice tasks.  
- Weakness: lapse model (drawn from prior) may be unrealistic; robustness checks for alternative lapse forms are needed.  
- Weakness: MCMC exploration in high-dimensional space raises computational/convergence concerns; report runtimes, diagnostics, and identifiability vs. Ntr curves.  
- Weakness: entirely simulation-based with no empirical corroboration; recommend reanalysis of existing data or broader θ∗ sampling.  
- Quality: methodologically sound and transparent, but claims of generality need more robustness analyses.  
- Clarity: generally clear; would benefit from a summary figure, parameter table, and more algorithmic/sampler details for reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel integration of a general observer model with a priori identifiability mapping tailored to Bayesian perceptual models.  
- Significance: practically important for experimental design and interpretation in perception and decision-making communities.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an under-discussed problem with a principled, usable framework and yields new, actionable insights.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on specific parametric forms, lack of automated design optimization, and sparse computational/empirical validation.  
- Recommended improvements: add sensitivity analyses for mixture complexity and f(s), provide computational/convergence details and identifiability vs. Ntr curves, relate criterion to expected information gain and include a small design-optimization example, and check alternative lapse models.  
- Overall recommendation: strong, useful methodological contribution; lean accept pending added robustness analyses and clearer ties to optimal design.
