This paper addresses the critical and often-overlooked problem of model identifiability in the context of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that since different combinations of internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function) can lead to identical observed behavior, conclusions drawn from fitting such models are potentially invalid. The main contribution is a novel, principled framework for systematically testing the identifiability of a model's parameters *a priori*, with the goal of improving experimental design.

The proposed framework consists of two core components. First, the authors develop a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model parameterizes the sensory mapping, sensory noise (likelihood), prior, and loss function using mixtures of Gaussians characterized by their first four moments, allowing for a wide range of functional forms. Second, they introduce an efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape." Instead of relying on computationally expensive simulations of many datasets, they derive an analytical expression for the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model `θ`, given data generated from a true "reference" model `θ*`. This expectation is shown to be proportional to the negative Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions of the two models. By sampling from a density based on this expected log-likelihood, one can explore which parameters are well-constrained and which are degenerate for a given experimental design.

The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two well-chosen case studies. In a time interval estimation task, the analysis reveals significant non-identifiability, particularly a degeneracy between sensory and motor noise parameters. The framework is used to compare several experimental designs, yielding the practical and non-obvious conclusion that independently measuring motor noise is far more effective at improving identifiability than simply increasing the number of trials or, surprisingly, enforcing a MAP-like loss function. In contrast, a second case study on speed perception shows that the inference of a "slow-speed prior," a classic result in the field, is theoretically robust and not prone to major degeneracies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem for a popular and influential class of models in computational neuroscience and psychology. While the issue of non-identifiability is known, this work is highly original in providing a systematic, quantitative, and *a priori* framework to analyze it. This shifts the focus from post-hoc model selection to principled experimental design, which could have a major positive impact on the field.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is elegant and technically sound. The formulation of expected evidence in terms of KL divergence is a clever move that makes the problem computationally tractable. The parameterization of the model components (likelihood, prior, loss) as mixtures of Gaussians defined by their moments is a powerful way to achieve representational flexibility without sacrificing the ability to perform efficient computation.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The problem is introduced effectively, and the proposed solution is laid out in a step-by-step manner. The case studies are well-explained and effectively illustrate the power and practical utility of the framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Model Assumptions:** The framework makes several simplifying assumptions. The restriction of likelihoods and loss functions to be unimodal (by constraining the mixture of Gaussians) seems potentially limiting, as multimodal representations could certainly exist. It would be helpful if the authors could comment on why this assumption was made and whether the framework could be extended to relax it. Similarly, the extension to tasks beyond 1D estimation, such as 2AFC or multidimensional stimuli, is mentioned as future work but its difficulty is unclear. A brief discussion of the primary challenges would be welcome.
*   **Intuition behind Results:** Some of the key results, while compelling, could benefit from more intuition. For instance, the finding that a narrow, MAP-like loss function *worsens* the recovery of the prior in the time-estimation task is counterintuitive and very interesting. The authors link this to the weak identifiability of the loss width parameter `σ_l`, but a more detailed explanation of why this interaction occurs would strengthen the paper's message.
*   **Practicality:** While the framework is powerful, its complexity may present a barrier to adoption by the broader psychophysics community. The impact of this excellent work would be magnified substantially if the authors were to make a software package implementing the framework publicly available.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and critical problem in a popular modeling field.
*   Proposes a novel, technically sound, and computationally efficient framework for a priori identifiability analysis.
*   Provides clear, practical demonstrations of how the framework can be used to evaluate and improve experimental design.
*   The results from the case studies provide both a valuable cautionary tale (for time perception) and an important theoretical validation (for speed perception).

**Cons:**
*   The applicability is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks.
*   Some of the model's simplifying assumptions (e.g., unimodality) could be better justified or discussed.
*   The intuition behind some of the more surprising results could be further elaborated.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the methodology of computational modeling in perception and cognitive science. It is original, technically strong, and addresses a problem of high importance. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with small revisions. The proposed framework has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers designing experiments to probe internal representations.
