

This paper proposes a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, addressing a critical gap in the field. The authors clearly articulate their main contribution: a method to assess whether model parameters (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can be reliably recovered from behavioral data before conducting experiments. This is particularly important given the well-known degeneracy in Bayesian observer models where multiple parameter combinations produce identical behavior.

The paper makes strong theoretical contributions by formalizing a priori identifiability through KL divergence (Equation 12), providing a practical tool for experimental design. The framework is well-motivated and addresses a genuine problem in the field—many Bayesian inference studies in perception lack sufficient constraints to ensure model components are uniquely identifiable. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility through two well-chosen case studies: time interval estimation (Section 4.1) and speed perception (Section 4.2). Their analysis reveals non-intuitive findings, such as how imposing a narrow loss function may actually degrade prior recovery in interval timing tasks, and how speed perception inferences are relatively robust to degeneracy.

The paper excels in quality, with rigorous mathematical derivations (Section 3) that are well-supported by the case studies. The clarity is excellent, with the four-stage observer model (Section 2) providing a coherent structure for the framework. The significance is clear—this work directly addresses a fundamental methodological challenge that could improve the reliability of Bayesian model-based inference across perception research.

The main limitations are that the framework is currently limited to continuous one-dimensional estimation tasks (though the authors acknowledge extensions in Section 5), and the case studies remain theoretical without empirical validation. However, the paper correctly identifies that the framework's primary value is in guiding experimental design before data collection.

The authors demonstrate strong field knowledge, appropriately citing key work in Bayesian perception (e.g., Knill, Maloney, Wolpert) and correctly framing their contribution as addressing a gap in systematic identifiability analysis. While the paper would benefit from brief discussion of how their approach compares to existing methods for model identifiability (e.g., Navarro et al.'s work on distinguishability), this is a minor point.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that should be accepted. The framework has clear potential to improve experimental design in Bayesian modeling of perception, and the case studies effectively demonstrate its utility. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses an important methodological need in the field.
