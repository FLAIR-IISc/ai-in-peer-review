- Tackles the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.  
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework for testing identifiability a priori.  
- Framework explores parameter space of a generalized Bayesian observer model and quantifies overlap in predicted behavior.  
- Uses KL-divergence to measure similarity of model predictions (appropriate and well-justified).  
- Demonstrates utility on two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Time interval estimation example effectively shows how experimental setups can reveal identifiability weaknesses.  
- Bayesian observer model is flexible and generalizable, with a parametrization aimed at computational efficiency.  
- Provides a thorough theoretical foundation with clear derivations and justifications.  
- Main concern: computational complexity and potential scalability issues for more complex models/tasks.  
- Reliance on modeling assumptions (e.g., Gaussian forms) not fully explored; lacks sensitivity analysis.  
- Case studies are relatively simple; applying the framework to more complex tasks would strengthen claims.  
- Connection between identifiability analysis and concrete experimental recommendations is weak (e.g., how to measure motor parameters).  
- Notation can be dense; would benefit from clearer organization or a notation table.  
- Paper is technically sound and mostly clearly written, though some math-heavy sections need simplification.  
- The framework is original and represents a significant contribution to the field.  
- Results are potentially significant for improving experimental design and interpretation of Bayesian perceptual models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a crucial issue, offers a novel method, and provides compelling demonstrations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational demands, strong modeling assumptions, and limited practical guidance for experiments.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with suggestions to address computational, sensitivity, and clarity concerns.
