This paper tackles the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue given the increasing use of these models to infer internal representations. The authors propose a framework for systematically testing identifiability and demonstrate its utility through two case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception. The work is well-motivated, recognizing the degeneracy inherent in the Bayesian framework and the potential for unreliable conclusions if this is not addressed.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in the field – the non-identifiability of Bayesian models.  This issue is often acknowledged but rarely systematically investigated.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a concrete methodology for exploring identifiability *a priori*. This is a significant contribution as it allows researchers to design experiments that are more likely to yield interpretable results.
* **Comprehensive Model:** The Bayesian observer model developed is sufficiently general to encompass a wide range of psychophysical tasks, while remaining computationally tractable for identifiability analysis. The use of a parametric form for the sensory map (Eq. 2) is a smart choice, balancing flexibility and manageability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The mathematical derivations, while complex, are presented clearly and supplemented with details in the Supplementary Material.
* **Illustrative Case Studies:** The two case studies effectively demonstrate the application of the framework. The results from the time interval estimation task are particularly insightful, highlighting the importance of experimental design (e.g., measuring motor noise) for reliable parameter recovery. The validation on the speed perception task, linking to prior work [9], strengthens the approach.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors aim for efficiency, exploring the identifiability landscape via MCMC (even with approximations) remains computationally demanding, especially for high-dimensional parameter spaces. The paper doesn’t fully convey the practical limitations of applying this framework to more complex models.
* **Sensitivity to Reference Observer:** The results are heavily dependent on the choice of the reference observer (θ*).  The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the conclusions to different reference observer assumptions. A more robust analysis might involve exploring a distribution of plausible reference observers.
* **Simplifying Assumptions:** The model relies on several simplifying assumptions (e.g., unimodal likelihoods, Gaussian noise). While justifiable for demonstration purposes, the extent to which these assumptions limit the generalizability of the framework is not fully discussed.  Specifically, the limited treatment of non-Gaussian noise profiles and the impact of violations of those assumptions are not addressed.
* **Limited Scope of Experimental Design Considerations:** The analysis of experimental design focuses on a relatively small set of manipulations. A more comprehensive exploration of different experimental factors (e.g., stimulus sampling, number of conditions, task instructions) would be valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Literature on Identifiability:** The paper mentions the broader problem of model non-identifiability but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of existing techniques from statistics and machine learning (e.g., profile likelihood, information criteria) that could be integrated into the framework.

**Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Section 3:** Provide more details about the computational challenges of calculating the KL divergence and the specific MCMC implementation used.  Quantify the computational cost (e.g., running time) for the example scenarios.
* **Section 4.1:** The statement that the reconstruction error is “generally acceptable” is vague. Provide more quantitative measures of reconstruction error (e.g., RMSE) and confidence intervals.
* **Section 4.2:** Discuss the potential implications of the robustness observed in the speed perception task. Why is this task less susceptible to identifiability issues than time interval estimation?
* **General:** Consider adding a discussion of how the framework could be used to guide model selection, beyond simply assessing parameter identifiability.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical and under-explored problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
* The proposed framework offers a novel and potentially valuable methodology for improving experimental design.
* The case studies demonstrate the practical utility of the framework and provide insightful results.
* The paper is well-written, technically sound, and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the framework may limit its applicability to more complex models.
* The sensitivity to the choice of the reference observer raises concerns about the robustness of the conclusions.
* The simplifying assumptions made in the model could limit its generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The proposed framework has the potential to become a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on addressing the concerns regarding computational cost, sensitivity analysis, and discussion of limitations.
