- Addresses model identifiability in Bayesian observer models and the ill-posed inverse problem of recovering prior, likelihood, and loss from behavior.
- Main contribution: a framework to assess parameter identifiability a priori to guide experimental design.
- Flexible, tractable parameterization of 1D Bayesian observer models using moment-defined components (sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, loss).
- Analytical derivation: expected log-likelihood between candidate θ and true θ* reduces to a function of the KL divergence between their response distributions.
- Efficient method to map the "identifiability landscape" by sampling an approximate expected posterior, revealing confusable parameter regions.
- Case study 1 (time interval estimation): major degeneracy between sensory and motor noise impedes prior recovery; independently measuring motor noise outperforms simply increasing trials.
- Case study 1 also finds enforcing a MAP-like loss can paradoxically worsen prior recovery.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): inference of prior and sensory noise is robust, supporting the slow-speed prior conclusion.
- Technical quality: derivations are elegant and correct; parameterization balances flexibility and tractability enabling feasible MCMC.
- Clarity: well-written, logically organized, with effective figures illustrating identifiability landscapes and design comparisons.
- Originality: provides a systematic, quantitative a priori identifiability tool and the novel idea of using expected log-likelihood/KL to explore identifiability via MCMC.
- Significance: high — tool can change experiment design practices and improve robustness of Bayesian model inferences.
- Strengths: tackles a fundamental methodological problem, offers a powerful computationally efficient framework, practical for comparing designs, and demonstrates nontrivial domain insights.
- Weakness: model complexity and many parameters may make results sensitive to choice of reference observer θ*; robustness to that choice should be discussed.
- Weakness: currently limited to 1D estimation tasks; discussion needed on extending to discrete-choice (2AFC) or multidimensional paradigms.
- Weakness: simplifying the original 2AFC speed task into an estimation task is necessary but its implications should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.
