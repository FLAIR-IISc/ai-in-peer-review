Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a core challenge for Bayesian observer models in perception: non-identifiability of internal components (priors, likelihoods, loss) from behavioral data. The authors formalize a general class of estimation tasks with a one-dimensional stimulus and propose a flexible parametric observer model: (i) a monotonic sensory mapping to an internal space (Eq. 2), (ii) likelihoods and loss functions represented as unimodal mixtures of Gaussians determined by moment constraints and a local maximum-entropy criterion (Eqs. 3, 6), (iii) priors approximated as mixtures of regularly spaced Gaussians (Eq. 4), and (iv) a response stage with additive reporting noise (Eq. 8) and lapses. They derive an a priori identifiability metric for a given experimental design by computing the expected log-likelihood of model θ on data generated by a reference θ*, showing it reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions for each stimulus (Eq. 12). They then sample an “expected posterior” over parameters proportional to exp(expected log-likelihood) (Eq. 13) to explore non-identifiability structure.

Two case studies illustrate the approach: (1) interval timing under short/long stimulus ranges, where they compare design variants (two ranges vs single range with more trials; narrower loss; known motor noise) and show prior recovery is fragile unless motor noise is constrained; and (2) speed perception with a slow-speed prior, where they find robust recovery of the prior exponent and noise properties across contrasts, even when decoupling internal and external likelihoods. The work connects to concerns about “Bayesian degeneracy” (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love) and builds on identifiability/distinguishability analyses in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung), and on Bayesian observer literature (Stocker & Simoncelli; Acerbi et al.; Girshick et al.).

Strengths
- Important problem: Provides a principled, quantitative framework for a priori identifiability analysis of Bayesian observer models, directly relevant to experimental design and interpretation.
- General and efficient model class: The mixture-of-Gaussians parametrizations enable fast evaluation and analytic expressions for expected loss, facilitating large-scale exploration.
- Clear operational identifiability criterion: The expected log-likelihood under θ* leading to a KL divergence decomposition is elegant, interpretable, and scales with trial number.
- Practical design insights: The interval timing study yields non-obvious guidance (e.g., MAP-like losses can hurt prior recovery; measuring motor noise can resolve degeneracy).
- Validation by re-analysis: The speed-perception results support the robustness of prior inference in a canonical paradigm, aligning with prior empirical conclusions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling assumptions may drive identifiability conclusions. The reliance on: (a) a specific monotone map and mixture-of-two Gaussians for likelihood and loss; (b) a local “maximum-entropy under unimodality” procedure to fix degrees of freedom; and (c) a fixed prior basis (M=15) could bias identifiability maps. Sensitivity analyses to these modeling choices are not shown.
- “Expected posterior” lacks priors over θ and a normalization constant. Although suitable as a landscape-exploration device, calling Eq. 13 an approximate posterior without a parameter prior may mislead readers; moreover, MCMC convergence/mixing on such a high-dimensional space (especially with {wm}) is nontrivial. Runtime, chain diagnostics, and robustness to initialization are not reported.
- Limited scope of misspecification tests. The method assumes data are generated by the same model class. It would be informative to test recovery under realistic misspecification (e.g., heavier-tailed noise, non-unimodal losses, different sensory transforms), since design guidance may change.
- Experimental design optimization is implicit. While the framework can rank designs, the paper stops short of optimizing stimulus sets or feedback policies. This connection to Bayesian optimal experimental design (expected information gain; Fisher information criteria) is not developed.
- Reporting of results is qualitative in places. Quantitative summaries (confidence intervals over metrics such as symmetric KL, recovery error vs Ntr curves) would help assess effect sizes and the benefit of design changes.
- Some details deferred to Supplementary Material are critical. The specification and computation of the maximum-entropy mixture parameters, constraints on weights {wm} (e.g., regularization/sparsity), and numerical stability in computing presp(r|s;θ) should be summarized in the main text for reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The derivation of Eq. 12 is standard and appropriate; using KL as a measure of distinguishability is well-founded. The observer model is coherent and computationally efficient. However, empirical validation lacks diagnostics (MCMC convergence, sensitivity to M and mixture order), and the effect of model misspecification is not assessed.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful modular description of the model and explicit parameterization. Some notational choices (e.g., dual use of σ parameters across stages) could be clarified. The “maximum-entropy under unimodality” procedure should be summarized in the main text. Figures referenced (e.g., Fig. 2c–d, Fig. 3c) suggest informative posterior visualizations; ensure these include scales and credible intervals.

Originality
The specific combination of (i) a broadly parameterized, mixture-based observer model; (ii) an a priori identifiability mapping via expected log-likelihood/KL; and (iii) design-oriented comparisons within canonical tasks appears novel within the NeurIPS literature. Related ideas exist in distinguishability (Navarro et al.) and OED, but this targeted application to Bayesian perception with concrete design guidance is original.

Significance
High practical relevance for researchers fitting Bayesian observer models. The framework can prevent over-interpretation of inferred priors/likelihoods, inform auxiliary measurements (e.g., motor noise), and motivate better designs. The case studies touch widely cited paradigms (interval timing, speed perception), increasing impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses: vary M (prior basis), number of Gaussian components, and the sensory transform family; report how identifiability conclusions change.
- Include diagnostics: MCMC settings, convergence metrics, runtime; ablation of free parameters; report credible intervals for recovery metrics.
- Assess robustness under misspecification: generate data from models with heavier tails, asymmetric losses not captured by two-Gaussian mixtures, or alternative lapse models.
- Connect to optimal experimental design: discuss maximizing expected information gain over stimulus distributions and feedback/loss parameters; cite Chaloner & Verdinelli (1995), Paninski (2005) and related adaptive design work in neuroscience.
- Clarify and justify the maximum-entropy procedure for fixing mixture parameters; provide algorithmic details and conditions for uniqueness/stability.
- Consider including parameter priors in Eq. 13 to reflect realistic constraints and avoid pathological regions in θ.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely methodological gap.
- Solid and interpretable theory with practical implications.
- Yields actionable, nontrivial design recommendations.
- Broad, extensible modeling framework with analytic components.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of model misspecification and sensitivity to parametrization.
- Sparse reporting of computational/diagnostic details.
- Some results are qualitative; quantitative effect sizes and uncertainty are needed.
- OED connection is underdeveloped despite being a natural next step.

Overall, a valuable contribution that will help the community assess and improve the interpretability of Bayesian observer models; with added robustness analyses and clearer methodological details, this would be a strong paper.
