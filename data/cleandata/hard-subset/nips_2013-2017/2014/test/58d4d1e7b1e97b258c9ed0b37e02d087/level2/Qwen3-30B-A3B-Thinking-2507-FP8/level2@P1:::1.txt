

This paper addresses a critical yet frequently overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to degenerate parameter combinations that yield identical behavioral outputs. The authors propose a systematic framework for *a priori* identifiability testing, enabling researchers to evaluate experimental designs before data collection. The core contribution is a novel observer model class (Section 2) and a method to quantify identifiability via expected log-likelihood differences (Section 3), demonstrated in two case studies (Section 4).

**Strengths:**  
1. **High significance:** The problem is fundamental to the validity of Bayesian models in perception research. Many papers (e.g., [9, 24]) claim to recover internal representations without addressing degeneracy, risking misinterpretation. The framework directly tackles this gap, with clear practical utility for experimental design.  
2. **Technical soundness:** The theoretical foundation (Eq. 12) is rigorous, using KL divergence as a proxy for model distinguishability. The case studies provide concrete evidence: (a) in time estimation, the framework reveals that forcing a MAP loss function *worsens* prior recovery (Fig. 2d), contrary to intuition; (b) in speed perception, it confirms robustness of prior recovery (Fig. 3), validating prior work [9, 29].  
3. **Originality:** While non-identifiability is noted in [17,18,19], this is the first *systematic* framework for testing it *a priori*. The authors correctly position their work against prior ad-hoc approaches (e.g., [12,19]) and emphasize the lack of theoretical analysis of experimental design choices.  
4. **Clarity and structure:** The paper is well-organized, with clear model specification (Section 2), concise methodology (Section 3), and compelling case studies (Section 4). Figures 2–3 effectively communicate results.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Model scope:** The framework assumes unimodal sensory noise (Eq. 3) and a specific parametric form for the sensory map (Eq. 2). While justified for their case studies, the authors acknowledge limitations in Section 5 (e.g., "arbitrary loss spaces"). A brief discussion of how non-unimodal noise would affect identifiability would strengthen the generality claim.  
2. **Computational cost:** MCMC sampling of the identifiability landscape (Eq. 13) is computationally intensive for high-dimensional parameter spaces. While feasible for their cases (M=15, 15+ parameters), the paper could better address scalability for more complex models (e.g., multi-cue tasks).  
3. **Case study breadth:** Only two tasks are analyzed. While sufficient for proof-of-concept, adding a third example (e.g., orientation perception [8]) would further demonstrate the framework’s versatility.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper is a valuable contribution to the field. It provides a much-needed methodological tool to ensure Bayesian models of perception are interpreted correctly, with direct implications for experimental design. The case studies are compelling, the theory is sound, and the framework is immediately applicable. The weaknesses are minor and addressable in revision (e.g., expanding the scope discussion).  

**Pro vs. Con for Acceptance:**  
- *Pro:* Solves a critical, underappreciated problem; framework is practical, theoretically grounded, and validated in two key domains.  
- *Con:* Limited to unimodal sensory noise; computational cost not fully quantified.  
**Verdict: Strong accept.** The significance outweighs the minor limitations, and the authors have clearly demonstrated the framework’s utility. With minor clarifications (e.g., addressing computational trade-offs), this work will become a reference for experimental design in perceptual modeling.
