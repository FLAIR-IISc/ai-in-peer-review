This paper addresses the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The core argument is that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavioral predictions, undermining the ability to reliably recover internal representations from data. The paper proposes a framework for systematically testing identifiability and applying it to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The work is well-motivated, tackling a significant and often overlooked problem in the application of Bayesian models to psychological data. The proposal of a framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis is a valuable contribution. The authors correctly highlight the degeneracy inherent in the Bayesian framework and provide a clear explanation of the issue. The extension of previous work to a continuous class of observer models is a positive step, and the effort to create a computationally tractable framework is commendable. The case studies, while focused, demonstrate the application of the framework and yield interesting insights – specifically, identifying the importance of motor noise parameters in time interval estimation and demonstrating robustness in the speed perception scenario. The connection to existing literature is strong, and references are appropriately cited.

However, the paper could be strengthened in several areas. While the framework is presented clearly, the details of the MCMC sampling method used to explore the approximate posterior density (Eq. 13) are somewhat lacking. More detail on the implementation and validation of this sampling procedure would increase confidence in the results. The choice of the parametric form for the sensory mapping (Eq. 2) and the loss function (Eq. 6) is justified, but the sensitivity of the results to these choices should be explored. A brief analysis of how different functional forms might affect identifiability would be beneficial. 

The interpretation of the results in the time interval estimation task is somewhat limited by the focus on a specific experimental setup. Exploring a wider range of experimental designs and parameter values could reveal more generalizable conclusions. The finding that a narrow loss function worsens reconstruction is surprising and warrants further investigation. The authors briefly mention potential explanations, but a more in-depth analysis would be valuable. The application to the speed perception task provides validation, but it feels somewhat less exploratory than the time interval estimation case study. It would be stronger if the analysis revealed less obvious insights or highlighted potential limitations of the framework in this context.

Furthermore, the paper’s scope is somewhat restricted by the focus on one-dimensional stimulus magnitudes. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, discussing the challenges of extending the framework to higher-dimensional spaces would be helpful. The impact of simplifying assumptions, such as assuming independence between parameters, is not thoroughly discussed. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical and often overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling.
*   Proposes a novel and potentially useful framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
*   Provides clear explanations and strong connections to relevant literature.
*   Demonstrates the application of the framework with illustrative case studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The methodological details of the MCMC sampling procedure are insufficient.
*   The sensitivity analysis of the parametric choices is limited.
*   The interpretation of results in the time interval estimation task could be more thorough.
*   The scope of the framework is limited to one-dimensional stimuli.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would significantly enhance its impact. The framework has the potential to become a useful tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception, and the insights gained from the case studies are thought-provoking. A revision addressing the suggested points is recommended.
