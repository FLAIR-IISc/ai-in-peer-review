This paper tackles a central and under-addressed issue in Bayesian observer modeling: the practical identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and losses from behavioral data. The authors propose a general framework that (i) constructs a flexible yet computationally tractable observer family (mixture-based likelihoods and losses, parametric sensory warping), and (ii) quantifies a priori identifiability via the expected log-likelihood under a reference observer, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimuli. They use MCMC to sample an “approximate posterior” over model parameters, then compare experimental designs by how well they recover target components. Case studies in interval timing (à la Jazayeri & Shadlen) and speed perception with a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli) illustrate, respectively, fragility and robustness of inference. This relates to prior work on distinguishability/model identifiability (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and speaks to ongoing debates on “Bayesian just-so stories.”

Quality: The derivation connecting expected evidence to KL is sound and appropriate for a priori design evaluation. The case studies are careful and produce actionable insights (e.g., motor noise confounds prior recovery; narrow losses can harm identifiability). However, support rests entirely on simulations from the assumed model class; no empirical re-analyses are provided, and sensitivity to misspecification is only qualitatively discussed.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized; the model is clearly specified. Some choices (two-component mixtures; entropy-based parameter tying; MCMC setup and convergence) are deferred to the supplement—summarizing key diagnostics and computational costs in the main text would improve reproducibility.

Originality: While using expected evidence/KL for identifiability is known, the tailored observer class and its deployment for ranking psychophysical designs appear novel and practically valuable. The link to experimental design could be better situated within Bayesian optimal design (e.g., expected information gain, Chaloner & Verdinelli).

Significance: High potential impact for perceptual modeling and experiment planning. The interval-timing findings could recalibrate interpretations of prior/likelihood/loss fits; the speed-perception results strengthen confidence in slow-speed prior inferences.

Limitations and suggestions:
- Validate on real datasets (e.g., re-fit classic interval timing and speed studies; test predicted design rankings prospectively).
- Probe model misspecification (e.g., alternative loss families, non-mixture likelihoods, nonstationary priors, decision noise).
- Report sensitivity to M, mixture components, and f(s); provide code and convergence diagnostics.
- Consider info-theoretic design criteria (EIG) and local identifiability (Fisher information) as complements.

Pros:
- Addresses an important, often overlooked problem
- Clear, general framework with actionable guidance
- Insightful case studies; practical design recommendations

Cons:
- No empirical validation
- Potential over-parameterization and model-class dependence
- Limited discussion of computational scaling and robustness
