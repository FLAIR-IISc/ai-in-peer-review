- Novel framework addresses model identifiability in Bayesian observer models, tackling a fundamental limitation in perceptual decision-making research.
- Uses a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient approach that leverages KL-divergence to quantify identifiability.
- Demonstrates practical utility through two case studies (time-interval estimation and speed perception) and can rank experimental designs by reliability.
- Results are clearly presented with useful visualizations and insightful discussion of effects from experimental constraints (e.g., motor noise, loss functions).
- Framework has broader implications beyond psychophysics, potentially impacting neuroscience and machine learning.

- Framework complexity may limit accessibility for researchers without strong computational backgrounds; clearer, more intuitive exposition is needed.
- Case studies are somewhat narrow; inclusion of more diverse paradigms (e.g., multi-cue integration, discrete-choice models) would strengthen generality.
- Relies on assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific loss functions) that may introduce biases; a deeper discussion of these limitations is warranted.
- Lacks empirical validation with real experimental data; simulations are convincing but real-world testing would bolster claims.

- Recommendation: Strong candidate for acceptance with minor revisions to improve accessibility and expand discussion of limitations.

Pro:
- Novel, rigorous approach addressing a core problem in Bayesian modeling with actionable experimental-design insights.
- Practical demonstrations via case studies show the framework’s utility in real experimental scenarios.

Con:
- Complexity may hinder broader adoption among non-specialists.
- No empirical validation using real experimental datasets.
