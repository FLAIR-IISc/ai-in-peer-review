- Addresses identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, losses) in Bayesian perception due to inverse-problem degeneracy.  
- Proposes a systematic a priori identifiability-testing framework using KL divergence and MCMC to evaluate experimental designs.  
- Directly relevant to current reliance on Bayesian observer models and responds to prior non-identifiability concerns.  
- Extends prior model-distinguishability work by offering a computationally efficient, experiment-focused method for sensory estimation tasks.  
- Strong theoretical foundation and well-justified model choices (mixture-of-Gaussians parametrization).  
- Two compelling case studies demonstrate practical utility and generate non-intuitive, actionable insights.  
- Time-interval estimation result: imposing a MAP-like loss worsens prior recovery, while measuring motor noise (MTR design) resolves degeneracy.  
- Speed perception case: robust recovery of slow-speed priors and noise characteristics, validating earlier empirical work.  
- Efficiency via analytical KL approximations enables exploration of complex parameter spaces beyond ad hoc approaches.  
- Technically rigorous with thorough derivations, empirical validation, clear writing, and reproducible supplementary material.  
- Minor weakness: limited discussion of scalability to high-dimensional stimulus spaces (e.g., orientation).  
- Minor weakness: assumption of no learning in the speed study—real observers may adapt priors, which could interact with identifiability.  
- Sensory mapping (Weber–Fechner/Stevens parametrization) is well-justified but may not capture all noise models; authors note parameter flexibility.  
- High significance: provides a practical tool for experimentalists and advances interpreting Bayesian models as process (not just descriptive) models.  
- Meets NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance); minor limitations are reasonable extensions rather than flaws.
