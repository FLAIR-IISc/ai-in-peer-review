- Paper tackles identifiability in Bayesian observer models and proposes a flexible 1D observer family plus an a priori identifiability analysis reducing to stimulus-weighted KL divergences and an “approximate expected posterior” explored via MCMC.  
- Two case studies: interval timing (prior recovery fragile unless constraints like known motor noise imposed) and visual speed perception (prior exponent and noise recovery relatively robust).  
- Connects to prior work on model distinguishability and Bayesian perception; novelty lies in packaging KL-based expected evidence with a flexible observer class as a practical identifiability tool.  
- The derivation (expected log-likelihood → weighted KL) is sound and appropriate for probing identifiability.  
- The observer class is well-designed (monotone sensory map, mixtures for asymmetry/kurtosis, tractable loss, reporting noise, lapses) and computationally tractable.  
- Case studies are carefully specified and yield plausible qualitative conclusions, highlighting degeneracies like sensory vs motor noise and weak identifiability of loss width.  
- Main caveat: conclusions depend on chosen parametric families (two-Gaussian mixtures, prior lattice, sensory map).  
- Missing computational details: MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, effective sample sizes, and runtimes are not reported.  
- Clarity is good overall, but the paper should more clearly separate fixed vs inferred variables and summarize sensitivity analyses in the main text.  
- The integration of components into a systematic, a priori identifiability framework is original and timely, with useful design-oriented insights.  
- The work is significant because it provides a practical tool to assess identifiability and inform experimental design, though currently limited to 1D estimation tasks.  
- Strength: principled identifiability metric tied to expected log-evidence with intuitive dependence on trial count and stimulus distribution.  
- Strength: flexible observer class allowing rich priors/likelihoods/losses while retaining tractability.  
- Strength: concrete, design-relevant insights and transparent assumptions with shared parameters across conditions.  
- Weakness/suggestion: assess sensitivity to model choices (number of mixture components, alternative families like log-normal or Student-t).  
- Weakness/suggestion: complement global KL mapping with local analyses (Fisher information, profile likelihoods) to diagnose parameter constraints.  
- Weakness/suggestion: demonstrate experimental-design optimization (e.g., D-/A-optimal designs) using the KL criterion.  
- Weakness/suggestion: report MCMC/computational details to assess practical applicability.  
- Weakness/suggestion: include or discuss parameter priors, since E(θ|θ*) currently omits them and priors could stabilize exploration.  
- Weakness/suggestion: validate interval timing claims via independent tasks or control analyses manipulating loss externally.  
- Weakness/suggestion: check robustness of speed-prior results to task-induced learning or discuss its effects on identifiability.  
- Weakness/suggestion: quantify regimes where reporting noise σreport(s) is separable from sensory noise in the KL landscape.  
- Questions for authors: how sensitive are conclusions to M=15 prior lattice and the two-component mixture assumption?  
- Questions for authors: can you quantify identifiability (credible region widths or Cramér–Rao bounds) for key parameters as a function of trial count?  
- Questions for authors: have you tried optimizing stimulus placements Pi via the KL criterion to propose improved designs?  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a principled, practical framework that yields actionable guidance and is well integrated with existing literature.  
- Cons for acceptance: simulation-based and model-class dependent results, limited to 1D estimation, and missing computational/practical details plus no design-optimization demo.  
- Overall: solid, useful contribution recommended for acceptance after minor revisions addressing sensitivity analyses, computational details, and (if feasible) a brief design-optimization example.
