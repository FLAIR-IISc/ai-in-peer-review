- Addresses non-identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss in Bayesian observer models and formalizes 1D estimation tasks.
- Proposes a flexible parametric observer model: monotonic sensory map, unimodal mixture-of-Gaussians likelihoods/loss via max-entropy, Gaussian prior basis, response noise and lapses.
- Derives an a priori identifiability metric: expected log-likelihood reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions (Eq. 12).
- Introduces an “expected posterior” proportional to exp(expected log-likelihood) to explore non-identifiability structure (Eq. 13).
- Case study 1 (interval timing): prior recovery is fragile unless motor noise is constrained; design choices (range, loss, motor noise) affect identifiability.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): robust recovery of prior exponent and noise properties across contrasts, even when internal and external likelihoods are decoupled.
- Situates work among Bayesian degeneracy concerns and distinguishability/identifiability literature (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian; Navarro et al.).
- Strength: Provides a principled, quantitative framework for a priori identifiability analysis useful for experimental design and interpretation.
- Strength: General and computationally efficient model class with analytic expected-loss expressions enabling large-scale exploration.
- Strength: Clear operational identifiability criterion (KL decomposition) that is interpretable and scales with trial number.
- Strength: Yields practical, non-obvious design insights (e.g., MAP-like losses can hurt recovery; measuring motor noise helps).
- Strength: Validation via re-analysis of speed-perception data aligns with prior empirical conclusions.
- Weakness: Modeling assumptions (monotone map, two-Gaussian mixtures, fixed prior basis M=15, max-entropy unimodality) may drive identifiability conclusions; no sensitivity analyses shown.
- Weakness: “Expected posterior” omits parameter priors and normalization; labeling it a posterior may mislead; MCMC convergence/mixing, runtime, and diagnostics are not reported.
- Weakness: Limited misspecification testing — assumes data generated by same model class; robustness to heavy tails, non-unimodal losses, and alternative transforms not assessed.
- Weakness: Does not explicitly optimize experimental design or connect to Bayesian OED criteria like expected information gain or Fisher information.
- Weakness: Some results are qualitative; missing quantitative summaries (confidence intervals, symmetric KL, recovery error vs Ntr) to assess effect sizes.
- Weakness: Critical methodological details (max-entropy mixture computation, weight constraints, numerical stability of presp(r|s;θ)) are relegated to supplement rather than main text.
- Quality: Technically sound derivations and coherent observer model, but empirical validation lacks diagnostics and sensitivity/misspecification analyses.
- Clarity: Generally clear and modular, but some notational ambiguities and insufficient summary of the max-entropy procedure and figure scales/credible intervals.
- Originality: Novel combination of mixture-based observer parameterization and a priori identifiability mapping applied to Bayesian perception; builds on but extends prior distinguishability/OED ideas.
- Significance: High practical relevance for researchers fitting Bayesian observer models; can prevent over-interpretation and guide auxiliary measurements and design choices.
- Suggestion: Provide sensitivity analyses varying M, number of Gaussian components, and sensory transform family; report how identifiability changes.
- Suggestion: Include MCMC settings, convergence metrics, runtimes, ablations, and credible intervals for recovery metrics.
- Suggestion: Test robustness under realistic misspecification (heavy tails, asymmetric/non-unimodal losses, different lapse models).
- Suggestion: Connect framework to optimal experimental design literature (expected information gain) and cite relevant works (Chaloner & Verdinelli; Paninski).
- Suggestion: Clarify and justify the maximum-entropy unimodality procedure with algorithmic details and stability/uniqueness conditions.
- Pros for acceptance: Addresses an important gap, provides solid interpretable theory, and offers actionable design recommendations within an extensible framework.
- Cons for acceptance: Lacks misspecification/sensitivity analyses, sparse computational diagnostics, and some qualitative results need quantitative support.
