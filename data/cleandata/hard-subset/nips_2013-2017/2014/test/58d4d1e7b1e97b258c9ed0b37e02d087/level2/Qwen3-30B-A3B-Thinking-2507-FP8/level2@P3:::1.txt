

The paper addresses a critical but underexplored issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to degeneracy in the inverse problem. It proposes a systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing, leveraging KL divergence and MCMC sampling to evaluate how experimental designs affect parameter recovery. This work directly responds to the growing reliance on Bayesian observer models in psychophysics (e.g., [7,9,12]) while acknowledging their vulnerability to non-identifiability concerns raised by [17,18,19]. The framework extends prior theoretical work on model distinguishability [23] by providing a computationally efficient method tailored to sensory estimation tasks, filling a gap in experimental design guidance for the field.

Strengths include a clear theoretical foundation, well-justified model choices (e.g., the mixture-of-Gaussians parametrization for sensory, perceptual, and loss functions), and two compelling case studies demonstrating practical utility. The time interval estimation analysis reveals non-intuitive findings: imposing a MAP-like loss function (via "MAP" design) *worsens* prior recovery, while measuring motor noise parameters ("MTR" design) resolves degeneracy. This challenges common experimental assumptions and highlights the framework’s value for design optimization. The speed perception case study shows robust recovery of slow-speed priors and noise characteristics, validating prior work [9,29] under theoretical scrutiny. The method’s efficiency (via analytical KL divergence approximations) enables exploration of complex parameter spaces, a significant improvement over prior ad hoc approaches.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous derivations (Supplementary Material) and thorough empirical validation. It clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior identifiability studies [23] by focusing on *systematic experimental design* rather than abstract model comparisons. The case studies are well-motivated and align with key NIPS themes in computational neuroscience (e.g., [4,6,29]). Clarity is strong, with logical flow from problem statement to framework to applications. The writing avoids excessive jargon, and the supplementary material adequately supports reproducibility.

Minor weaknesses include limited discussion of the framework’s scalability to high-dimensional stimulus spaces (e.g., orientation) and the assumption of no learning during experiments in the speed perception study (real observers adapt priors to task distributions [30]). While the paper acknowledges these as potential extensions, a brief mention of how learning effects might interact with identifiability would strengthen the discussion. The sensory mapping in Eq. 2 (Weber-Fechner/Steven’s law parametrization) is well-justified but might not cover all noise models; however, the authors note its flexibility via parameters *s₀* and *d*.

The significance is high: resolving identifiability concerns is foundational for interpreting Bayesian models as *process* models (not just descriptive tools) [5,12]. The framework provides a practical tool for experimentalists to avoid misinterpreting behavioral data, directly advancing the field’s ability to infer neural representations [4,6]. It addresses a pervasive but understudied challenge in computational psychology, with clear applicability beyond the presented cases (e.g., to multisensory integration [25]).

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: **Quality** (sound theory, thorough validation), **Clarity** (well-organized, reproducible), **Originality** (novel framework for *a priori* design testing), and **Significance** (resolves a core methodological barrier in perception modeling). While the speed perception case study assumes no learning, this is a reasonable simplification for the theoretical analysis, and the framework’s utility remains unchallenged. The time estimation results—particularly the counterintuitive negative effect of MAP loss—demonstrate the framework’s capacity to yield non-obvious, actionable insights.

In summary, the work provides a rigorous, efficient, and immediately applicable solution to a long-standing problem in Bayesian perception modeling. It is a strong contribution to NIPS, offering both theoretical depth and practical value for experimental design. The minor limitations noted are reasonable extensions rather than flaws, and the paper’s clarity and empirical grounding make it highly suitable for the conference.
