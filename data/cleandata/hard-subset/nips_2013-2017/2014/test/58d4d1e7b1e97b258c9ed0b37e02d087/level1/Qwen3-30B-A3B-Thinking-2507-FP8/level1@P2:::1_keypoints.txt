- Addresses the identifiability problem in Bayesian perceptual models where different component combinations can produce identical behavior.  
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework to test identifiability of Bayesian models before experiments, aiding experimental design.  
- Develops a flexible, computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models covering many common perceptual tasks.  
- Introduces an a priori identifiability assessment method using KL divergence and MCMC sampling that is theoretically sound and implementable.  
- Demonstrates framework utility with two case studies: time-interval estimation (prior recovery) and speed perception (slow-speed prior inference).  
- Theoretical framework is rigorous with careful mathematical derivations grounded in Bayesian decision theory.  
- Case studies are well-designed and provide compelling empirical evidence of the framework’s usefulness.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and discuss extensions (e.g., discrete-choice paradigms).  
- Clarity is generally good and the paper is logically organized, though some technical sections (sensory stage) need more intuitive explanation.  
- Originality is strong: offers a principled, a priori solution to degeneracy beyond ad hoc parameter constraints.  
- Reveals non-intuitive findings (e.g., narrow loss functions can worsen prior recovery) that would be hard to discover otherwise.  
- High significance: guides better experimental design (e.g., measuring motor parameters improves identifiability) and can prevent misinterpretation of behavioral data.  
- Supports and theoretically validates prior speed-perception work (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006).  
- Weaknesses: lacks concrete implementation guidance for paradigms beyond the two case studies and does not fully characterize computational costs for larger models.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — framework likely to become a standard tool in perceptual modeling.
