- Problem: Bayesian perceptual models are non-identifiable — multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior — and there is no systematic a priori test for this degeneracy.

- Contribution: Proposes a systematic framework to test identifiability a priori for a broad class of Bayesian sensory-estimation models.

- Model class: Introduces a flexible observer model with four stages (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) covering many 1D psychophysical tasks.

- Key method: Quantifies identifiability via the expected log likelihood of parameter configurations, derived analytically using KL divergence.

- Practical tool: Enables evaluation of experimental designs before data collection to assess their ability to resolve parameter degeneracies.

- Implementation: Uses analytic derivations and MCMC sampling (Eq. 13) to compute identifiability metrics for candidate designs.

- Case study — interval timing: Shows how design choices (single vs dual conditions, loss assumptions) affect prior recovery, with counterintuitive findings like narrow loss functions harming recovery.

- Case study — speed perception: Demonstrates robust reconstruction of slow-speed priors across designs, supporting prior empirical results.

- Strength — technical soundness: Framework is mathematically rigorous, grounded in KL divergence and Bayesian decision theory, with clear derivations and validation.

- Strength — practical relevance: Directly addresses a major methodological gap and can save resources by informing experimental design.

- Strength — clarity and organization: Paper is well-written, systematically presented, and supported by clear figures.

- Strength — novelty: First systematic a priori identifiability testing framework tailored to Bayesian observer models, distinct from generic model-distinguishability methods.

- Weakness — scope: Framework is limited to a specific class of one-dimensional estimation models and certain noise/loss assumptions; boundaries of applicability need clearer discussion.

- Weakness — computational complexity: Claims of efficiency are unquantified; MCMC and larger models/designs may be computationally intensive.

- Weakness — assumptions in case studies: Results depend on specific reference-observer choices (θ*); robustness to these assumptions is not fully explored.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a fundamental unaddressed methodological challenge with a sound, applicable, and novel framework that yields non-intuitive design insights.

- Arguments against acceptance: Restricted model class, unclear computational scalability, and limited empirical demonstrations across diverse perceptual tasks.

- Overall assessment: Significant, novel, and rigorous methodological contribution likely to improve experimental design in perception, with recommended acceptance despite scope and computational concerns.
