- Addresses the important and often-overlooked problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework to test identifiability a priori for observer models.
- Demonstrates the framework on time-interval estimation and speed-perception tasks.
- Well-motivated, builds on established Bayesian decision theory and psychophysics literature.
- Clear, accessible writing that explains complex concepts effectively.
- Practical case studies show how the framework can guide experimental design and interpretation.
- Solid theoretical foundation and extension of prior work to a more general model class.
- Generalized Bayesian observer model is versatile and flexible for exploring model space.
- Exploring the full parameter space is computationally demanding and may limit applicability.
- Framework relies on specific model assumptions (functional forms for priors, likelihoods, losses) whose sensitivity is not fully explored.
- Use of Gaussian mixtures for likelihoods/losses is practical but potentially limiting.
- Case study tasks are relatively simple; scalability to complex, ecologically valid tasks is unclear.
- Interpretation of KL divergence as support/identifiability can be ambiguous and nontrivial.
- Limited sensitivity analysis of parameters (e.g., beyond motor noise in the interval task).
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem, offers a novel framework, useful case studies, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: computational complexity, sensitivity to assumptions, and uncertain scalability.
- Overall assessment: reviewer recommends strong accept while urging further investigation of model-assumption sensitivity and scalability.
