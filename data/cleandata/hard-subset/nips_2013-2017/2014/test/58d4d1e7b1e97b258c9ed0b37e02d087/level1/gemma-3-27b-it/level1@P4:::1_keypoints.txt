- Proposes a framework to test identifiability of Bayesian perceptual models, addressing degeneracy in recovering priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavior.  
- Argues identifiability should be analyzed a priori to guide experimental design.  
- Uses a continuous class of observer models to explore model space.  
- Employs Kullback-Leibler divergence as a quantitative measure of identifiability.  
- Demonstrates the method with case studies in time-interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Strength: Directly tackles a fundamental and often-overlooked identifiability problem in Bayesian perception modeling.  
- Strength: Provides a systematic, structured methodology that can inform experiment planning.  
- Strength: Presents a well-defined and flexible observer-model framework applicable to psychophysical tasks.  
- Strength: Analytical use of KL divergence gives a quantifiable way to compare experimental setups.  
- Strength: Case studies concretely demonstrate practical utility and highlight important insights (e.g., narrow loss functions can be detrimental).  
- Strength: Mathematical derivations are rigorous and supported by supplementary material.  
- Weakness: Exploring the identifiability landscape can be computationally demanding, particularly for complex models.  
- Weakness: Framework depends on specific model assumptions (Gaussian mixtures, chosen functional forms); robustness to assumption changes is not fully tested.  
- Weakness: Case studies are relatively simple, leaving unclear applicability to more complex perceptual phenomena.  
- Weakness: Validation mainly reproduces known results rather than predicting novel experimental outcomes.  
- Weakness: KL divergence is a reasonable proxy but has limitations (e.g., asymmetry) that need consideration.  
- Weakness: Results show sensitivity to parameter choices (e.g., prior exponent), lacking thorough sensitivity analysis.  
- For acceptance: Novel, technically sound framework addressing an important issue with useful case studies.  
- Against acceptance: Computational cost, restrictive model assumptions, and limited validation may limit generality and applicability.  
- Overall: Strong contribution that merits acceptance despite limitations in complexity, assumptions, and validation.
