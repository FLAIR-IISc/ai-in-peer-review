This paper proposes a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing the inherent degeneracy in inferring internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors argue that systematic analysis of identifiability is crucial before running experiments, and present a method to explore this a priori, illustrated with case studies in time interval estimation and speed perception.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in Bayesian models of perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]), which posit that human behavior approximates optimal Bayesian inference. However, it directly addresses a critical limitation acknowledged in the field: the non-uniqueness of solutions when inverting these models to recover internal representations [17, 18, 19]. The work acknowledges previous attempts to constrain this degeneracy [12, 19], but argues for a more systematic, a priori analysis.  The proposed framework leverages a continuous class of observer models (Section 2), and employs a Kullback-Leibler divergence-based approach (Section 3) to assess identifiability. The application to time interval estimation builds upon existing work in that area [24], while the speed perception example connects to the classic findings of Stocker & Simoncelli [9].

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is a fundamental concern in Bayesian modeling of perception, and this paper directly tackles it with a novel framework.
*   **Systematic Approach:** The authors provide a structured methodology for analyzing identifiability a priori, potentially aiding experimental design.
*   **Well-Defined Framework:** The proposed observer model is clearly defined and appears flexible enough to accommodate a range of psychophysical tasks.
*   **Analytical Insights:** The use of KL divergence provides a quantifiable measure of identifiability, allowing for comparisons between different experimental setups.
*   **Case Studies:** Applying the framework to established tasks (time interval estimation, speed perception) provides concrete examples and demonstrates its practical utility.  The analysis of the time interval task is particularly insightful, highlighting the potential detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous, and the derivations are referenced to supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors aim for efficiency, exploring the identifiability landscape can still be computationally demanding, particularly for complex models. This is acknowledged but not fully addressed.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The framework relies on specific assumptions about the observer model (e.g., Gaussian mixture distributions, functional forms for loss and sensory mappings). The robustness of the conclusions to variations in these assumptions is not thoroughly explored.
*   **Simplified Tasks:** The case studies focus on relatively simple tasks. The applicability of the framework to more complex perceptual phenomena remains unclear.
*   **Limited Scope of Validation:**  The validation of the framework largely relies on reproducing known results (e.g., in speed perception). A more challenging test would be to use the framework to predict novel experimental outcomes.
*   **KL Divergence Interpretation:**  The use of KL divergence as a proxy for identifiability is reasonable, but it's important to acknowledge its limitations.  Asymmetric nature of KL divergence should be considered.
*   **Parameter Dependence:**  Results appear sensitive to parameter choices within the model (e.g., the exponent *kprior* in the speed perception task).  A sensitivity analysis of key parameters would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and often overlooked problem in Bayesian modeling.
*   The proposed framework is novel and provides a systematic approach to analyzing identifiability.
*   The technical contributions are sound and well-presented.
*   The case studies demonstrate the practical utility of the framework and provide insightful results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the framework may limit its applicability to complex models.
*   The reliance on specific model assumptions could restrict the generality of the findings.
*   The validation is primarily based on reproducing known results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. The proposed framework addresses a critical issue and provides a systematic approach to analyzing identifiability. While there are some limitations regarding computational complexity and model assumptions, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe this work is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, and could be particularly impactful for researchers developing and applying Bayesian models of perception.
