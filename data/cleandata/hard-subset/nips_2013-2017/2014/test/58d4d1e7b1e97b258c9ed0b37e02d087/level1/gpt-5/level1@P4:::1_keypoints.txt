- Summary: Formalizes degeneracy in Bayesian observer models and proposes a flexible parametric observer class plus an identifiability criterion based on expected log-likelihood and an “approximate expected posterior” sampled by MCMC; tested on interval-timing and speed-perception case studies.

- Strength: Sound formalization linking expected log-likelihood to KL divergences, providing an interpretable foundation for identifiability analysis.

- Strength: Flexible, computationally tractable observer class (mixture-of-Gaussians, basis priors, invertible loss mixtures) allowing skew/kurtosis and analytic or fast numerical computations.

- Strength: Provides practical design insights — e.g., fixing motor noise improves prior recovery in timing, MAP-like losses can worsen identifiability, and slow-speed priors are robustly recoverable.

- Strength: Framework naturally connects to experiment design via stimulus distribution weighting, enabling pre-experiment design optimization.

- Weakness: Conclusions depend on specific parametric choices (f(s), number of mixture components, basis size/spacing), with limited sensitivity quantification.

- Weakness: The “approximate expected posterior” is a proxy rather than a calibrated posterior; its relation to Bayes factors or Fisher information is not fully explored.

- Weakness: High-dimensional parameter space (e.g., M=15 prior weights plus noise/loss params) may be multi-modal; MCMC convergence diagnostics and computational cost are minimally reported.

- Weakness: Limited scope for discrete-choice paradigms (2AFC) and only an assumed equivalence to estimation formulations without strong empirical validation.

- Weakness: Loss identifiability is weak in examples (e.g., σℓ in timing); further quantification and guidance on constraining loss parameters are needed.

- Technical quality: Derivations (Eq.11→12) and use of mixture models are sound; case studies are internally consistent but would benefit from extra diagnostics (sensitivity to Ntr, priors, basis size).

- Clarity: Model and equations are well-described, but notation can be dense and constraints on prior weights should be made explicit; include complexity and MCMC diagnostics in main text.

- Originality: Novel combination of an efficient observer model class, KL-based identifiability criterion, and systematic a priori design comparisons for perception tasks.

- Significance: Addresses a central problem in interpreting inferred priors/likelihoods/losses and provides a useful tool for pre-experiment identifiability assessment.

- Suggested improvements: Report sensitivity analyses (basis M, spacing, mixture counts, f(s), Ntr) and provide MCMC details (proposals, runtimes, convergence metrics, cost).

- Suggested improvements: Explore design optimization (choose si, Pi to maximize discrimination) and quantify identifiability of lapse vs prior tails and motor noise.

- Suggested improvements: Clarify prior-weight parameterization/constraints (e.g., softmax) and ideally include a discrete-choice (2AFC) demonstration or stronger equivalence justification.

- Argument for acceptance: Principled, implementable framework addressing an important problem with actionable insights and technically sound derivations.

- Argument against acceptance: Heavy reliance on specific parameterizations, uncalibrated approximate posterior, and limited empirical/discrete-choice validation in the paper.

- Overall: Solid, generally useful framework for a priori identifiability analysis in Bayesian perception models, but needs broader sensitivity analyses and clearer MCMC/convergence reporting.
