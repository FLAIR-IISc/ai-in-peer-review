

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the identifiability problem. The authors rightly point out that while Bayesian observer models are widely used to infer internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions), these models suffer from intrinsic degeneracy where multiple combinations of model components can yield identical behavioral outputs. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability of Bayesian models before conducting experiments, with practical applications for experimental design.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it develops a flexible class of Bayesian observer models that encompasses many common perceptual tasks while maintaining computational efficiency. Second, it introduces a method to assess identifiability a priori using KL divergence and MCMC sampling, which is both theoretically sound and practically implementable. Third, it demonstrates the framework's utility through two well-chosen case studies: time interval estimation (with a focus on prior recovery) and speed perception (with emphasis on slow-speed prior inference).

The paper excels in quality. The theoretical framework is rigorous, with careful mathematical derivations that build upon established Bayesian decision theory. The case studies are well-designed and provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility. The authors correctly acknowledge limitations and discuss how the framework could be extended (e.g., for discrete-choice paradigms). The paper properly situates itself within the NIPS literature, citing relevant prior work such as Knill & Richards (1996), Maloney & Mamassian (2002), and recent NIPS papers on Bayesian models of perception.

The clarity is generally good, with logical organization and clear explanations of key concepts. The figures referenced in the paper (though not shown here) appear to effectively illustrate the results. Some technical sections (particularly the sensory stage) could benefit from more intuitive explanations to aid readers less familiar with psychophysical modeling, but this is a minor point given the paper's technical nature.

The originality is strong. While identifiability is a known issue in statistics, the authors provide a novel, systematic approach tailored specifically to Bayesian models of perception. Their framework goes beyond ad hoc solutions to degeneracy (e.g., constraining parameters based on theoretical considerations) by offering a principled, a priori method to assess identifiability. The case studies demonstrate how the framework reveals non-intuitive findings (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function can worsen prior recovery in time estimation), which would be difficult to discover without this systematic approach.

The significance is high. The framework directly addresses a practical problem in perception research that has led to potentially misleading conclusions in the literature. The case studies show how the framework can guide better experimental design (e.g., measuring motor parameters improves identifiability in time estimation), with clear implications for future research. The paper's findings provide theoretical validation for previous work on speed perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), demonstrating the framework's utility beyond just identifying problems.

The paper could be strengthened by providing more concrete guidance on implementing the framework for specific experimental paradigms beyond the two case studies. While the authors mention extensions (e.g., for discrete-choice paradigms), they don't provide implementation details. Additionally, the computational cost of the framework for larger models could be better characterized.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field. The paper identifies a genuine problem in Bayesian models of perception, provides a novel and systematic solution, and demonstrates its utility through well-executed case studies. The framework has clear practical implications for experimental design and could help prevent misinterpretations of behavioral data in the field. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the literature.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature with a rigorous, practical solution; the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility; the findings have clear implications for experimental design and interpretation of prior work.

Con arguments against acceptance: Minor improvements could be made to the paper's accessibility for less technical readers, and more concrete implementation guidance would be helpful.

Given the paper's strong quality, clarity, originality, and significance, I strongly recommend acceptance. The framework has the potential to become a standard tool in perceptual modeling, helping researchers avoid drawing incorrect conclusions about internal representations due to model degeneracy.
