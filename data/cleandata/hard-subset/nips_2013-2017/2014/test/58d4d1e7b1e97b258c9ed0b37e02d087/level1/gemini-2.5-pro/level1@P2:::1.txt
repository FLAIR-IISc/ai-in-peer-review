### NIPS 2014 Review

**Paper Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses a fundamental and widely recognized problem in cognitive science and computational neuroscience: the non-identifiability of Bayesian observer models. Researchers frequently use such models to infer latent mental representations—such as priors, likelihoods, and loss functions—from behavioral data (e.g., Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006). However, as the authors correctly point out, an infinite number of combinations of these components can produce identical behavior, a degeneracy that threatens the validity of any claims about the recovered representations. While this issue is known (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2006), there has been no systematic framework to quantify this non-identifiability *a priori* and use this information to improve experimental design.

The authors' core contribution is to provide such a framework. They propose a two-part solution. First, they develop a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models. This model class uses mixtures of Gaussians to represent priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, and includes parametric forms for sensory mapping and motor noise, encompassing a wide range of models used in the literature. Second, they propose a principled metric for assessing the practical non-identifiability between a "ground truth" reference model (θ*) and any other model (θ). This metric is based on the expected log-likelihood over hypothetical datasets, which they elegantly show is proportional to the KL-divergence between the response distributions predicted by the two models. By sampling from the resulting "approximate expected posterior" via MCMC, they can map out the "identifiability landscape" and see which parameters are easily confused with one another.

The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two well-chosen case studies: (1) recovering priors in a time interval estimation task, where they show that certain experimental designs are far better than others at resolving degeneracies, and (2) examining the robustness of the famous "slow-speed prior" from vision science, which they find to be theoretically well-identifiable.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   The derivation of the identifiability metric from the expected log-likelihood to the KL-divergence is sound and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the work.
*   The construction of the general observer model is very clever. Using mixtures of Gaussians for the core components allows for great flexibility (approximating arbitrary distributions) while retaining computational tractability, as the expected loss can be calculated efficiently. This is a crucial element that makes the entire framework feasible.
*   The claims are well-supported by the results of the two case studies. The analysis is thorough, and the authors are careful to state their assumptions about the "reference observer" in each case. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

A minor weakness is that the framework's conclusions are necessarily conditioned on the assumptions made about the reference observer (θ*). If these initial guesses (drawn from literature or pilot data) are inaccurate, the resulting advice on experimental design might be suboptimal. However, this is an inherent feature of any *a priori* analysis, and the framework itself allows one to test the robustness of conclusions to different assumptions about θ*.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction, making the problem accessible even to readers not deeply embedded in psychophysical modeling.
*   The breakdown of the observer model into four distinct stages (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) is logical and easy to follow.
*   The figures are informative and effectively communicate the main results. Figure 2, in particular, does an excellent job of showing not just which experimental design is best (MTR), but *why* the others fail (e.g., the degeneracy between sensory and motor noise shown in 2c).

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   While the general problem of model identifiability is not new (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), applying a formal, quantitative framework to the specific and popular class of Bayesian observer models in perception is a novel and significant step.
*   Previous approaches to handling degeneracy have been largely ad-hoc (e.g., fixing certain parameters, assuming a specific loss function). This paper provides the first general-purpose tool for systematically investigating these issues *before* collecting data.
*   The combination of the flexible model class with the KL-divergence-based exploration of the parameter space is a novel methodological contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this paper is substantial, with a high potential for impact on both research and practice.
*   This work provides a concrete tool that can directly improve the quality and efficiency of scientific inquiry in perception and motor control. Experimentalists can use this framework to design studies that are maximally informative about the specific parameters they wish to measure, potentially saving significant time and resources.
*   The results of the case studies are themselves important. The finding that recovering priors in time interval estimation is highly sensitive to the independent measurement of motor noise is a critical cautionary tale for researchers in that field. Conversely, the theoretical validation that the slow-speed prior is a robust inference lends further confidence to a cornerstone finding in vision science.
*   More broadly, this paper champions a more rigorous approach to computational modeling in cognitive science, pushing the field from a qualitative awareness of a problem (non-identifiability) towards a quantitative solution. Other researchers are likely to build on these ideas, potentially extending them to other modeling domains.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental, pervasive issue of model non-identifiability that undermines many claims made in the computational cognitive science literature.
2.  **Novel and Sound Methodology:** It introduces a novel, principled, and practical framework for assessing identifiability *a priori*.
3.  **High Potential Impact:** The work provides an immediately useful tool for experimentalists to design more powerful and robust experiments.
4.  **Insightful Demonstrations:** The case studies are well-chosen and provide significant insights into specific, active areas of research.
5.  **Excellent Presentation:** The paper is clear, well-written, and technically solid.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Dependence on Assumptions:** The conclusions about optimal experimental design are contingent on the choice of the "reference observer." While this is an unavoidable limitation, it means the framework is a tool for exploration rather than a provider of definitive answers. (This is a very minor counterargument, as the authors are transparent about this.)

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a significant methodological contribution to the fields of computational neuroscience and cognitive science. It is technically sound, highly original, clearly presented, and has a high potential for impact. It provides a much-needed tool for improving the rigor of modeling work in perception and should be of great interest to a substantial portion of the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
