This paper tackles the crucial, yet often overlooked, problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in the Bayesian framework – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce the same behavioral predictions – and propose a novel framework to systematically assess this issue. The strength of the paper lies in its attempt to move beyond simply acknowledging the problem and toward offering a practical methodology for *a priori* identifiability analysis, potentially guiding experimental design. The application to time interval estimation and speed perception is well-chosen and provides concrete illustrations of the framework's utility.

The core idea – leveraging the structure of a parameterized class of observer models to explore the parameter space and assess how easily different parameter combinations can generate similar behavior – is a sound one. The model itself (Section 2), while complex, is reasonably well-motivated and offers a good balance between generality and computational tractability. The use of maximum entropy principles to define the functional forms of likelihoods and loss functions is a sensible approach. The development of the expected support metric (Eq. 12) as a proxy for identifiability, and its connection to KL divergence, provides a mathematically grounded basis for the analysis. 

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. Firstly, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of assuming a particular functional form for the stimulus-response mapping (Eq. 2), the sensitivity of the results to this choice isn't thoroughly explored.  Different functional forms could drastically alter the identifiability landscape. This sensitivity analysis is crucial, as it directly affects the applicability of the framework. Secondly, the parameterization of the observer model, though comprehensive, introduces a high degree of complexity. While this allows for flexibility, it also makes interpretation of the identifiability analysis more challenging. Identifying which parameters are driving the degeneracy, and *why*, is not always clear. The discussion in Section 4.1 regarding the effects of fixing motor parameters (MTR) hints at this challenge.  A more systematic examination of parameter correlations and their influence on identifiability would be valuable.

Thirdly, the assumption that the reference observer (θ*) accurately reflects the true underlying perceptual process is critical. If the initial assumptions about θ* are significantly off, the identifiability analysis may be misleading. The authors briefly touch on this but could elaborate on techniques to assess the robustness of the results to variations in θ*.  The application to speed perception (Section 4.2) feels less impactful than the time interval estimation example.  The results largely confirm existing findings, and the analysis doesn't reveal any surprising or novel insights. The statement that the reconstruction of priors and noise characteristics is “relatively reliable” feels somewhat understated given the existing literature demonstrating strong priors in speed perception – more critical discussion is needed here.

Finally, the computational cost of exploring the parameter space, even with the efficient techniques described, is likely substantial. The authors don’t provide detailed information about the computational resources required for the analyses presented.  This is important for evaluating the practical feasibility of applying the framework to more complex models or datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis is a significant contribution, addressing a critical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and implementation appear robust and well-motivated.
*   **Potential Impact:** The framework has the potential to improve experimental design and lead to more reliable inference of internal representations.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity of the model, the paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Sensitivity to Model Assumptions:**  The results are potentially sensitive to the choice of functional forms and parameterizations, which aren’t fully explored.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational demands of the analysis may limit its practical application.
*   **Limited Novelty in Speed Perception Application:** The speed perception example doesn’t showcase the full power of the framework.
*   **Interpretation Challenges:** The high dimensionality of the parameter space can make it difficult to interpret the identifiability results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed framework is a significant step toward addressing the crucial problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. While there are weaknesses, particularly regarding the sensitivity to model assumptions and the interpretation of results, the potential benefits outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the concerns raised above by: (1) Conducting a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of different functional forms for the stimulus-response mapping. (2) Providing more detailed information about the computational resources required for the analyses. (3) Expanding the discussion of the assumptions underlying the reference observer (θ*) and exploring techniques to assess the robustness of the results. (4) Providing a more critical discussion of the speed perception results in the context of existing literature.
