This paper addresses the problem of model non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models, a popular framework in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The authors note that the core components of these models—prior, likelihood, and loss function—are degenerate, meaning different combinations can produce identical behavioral predictions. This ambiguity poses a significant challenge to the common practice of "inverting" these models to recover an observer's latent mental representations. The paper proposes a novel framework to systematically assess the a priori identifiability of model parameters for a given experimental design.

The proposed framework consists of two main components. First, the authors introduce a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. This model parameterizes the entire process from sensation to response, using mixtures of Gaussians to represent the prior, likelihood, and loss function. This choice allows for fast, often analytical, computation of the observer's predicted behavior. Second, they introduce a method for mapping the "identifiability landscape." This is achieved by quantifying the distinguishability between a ground-truth "reference" observer model and other candidate models. The measure is based on the expected log-likelihood over hypothetical datasets, which is shown to be proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions predicted by the two models. By sampling from an approximate expected posterior density derived from this measure, the framework can reveal which parameters are well-constrained and which exhibit trade-offs or degeneracies.

The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies based on well-known perceptual tasks. In the first, modeling a time interval estimation task (related to work by Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010), the authors compare several experimental designs to see which best facilitates the recovery of the observer's prior. In the second, they re-examine the inference of a "slow-speed prior" in visual perception (related to Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006) and test the robustness of the original conclusions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and critical issue in a widely used modeling approach. The problem of non-identifiability is often acknowledged but rarely addressed with such systematic rigor. The ability to evaluate and compare experimental designs *before* data collection is a highly significant contribution that could improve the quality and reliability of research in the field.
*   **Originality:** While the general problem of identifiability is not new, the proposed framework for performing a systematic, a priori analysis is novel. The combination of a flexible parametric model class with a principled, KL-divergence-based metric for identifiability provides a powerful and original tool for modelers and experimentalists.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical framework is well-derived, and the modeling choices are well-justified for achieving a balance between flexibility and computational tractability. The case studies are thoughtfully designed and effectively illustrate the framework's power. For instance, the analysis of the time interval task produces the non-trivial insight that encouraging a MAP-like loss function could, under the model's assumptions, worsen the recovery of the prior, highlighting a subtle but critical model degeneracy. The validation of the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference provides strong theoretical support for previous empirical work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is compelling, the model is described in a logical step-by-step fashion, and the results are presented with informative figures that are easy to interpret. The authors do a good job of explaining complex concepts in an accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dependence on the Reference Observer:** The entire identifiability analysis is conditioned on the choice of a "reference observer" (`θ*`). The conclusions drawn—for instance, regarding the best experimental design—are therefore dependent on the assumptions made about this ground-truth model. While the authors state that `θ*` can be based on prior literature or pilot data, the sensitivity of the conclusions to this choice is not extensively explored. It would strengthen the paper to discuss how robust the findings are to variations in the reference observer's parameters.
*   **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers a significant class of psychophysical experiments, its applicability to other common paradigms, such as discrete-choice tasks (e.g., 2AFC), is mentioned but not demonstrated. The conversion of the 2AFC speed perception task into an "equivalent estimation task" is asserted rather than detailed, leaving some ambiguity about how the framework would handle such cases more generally.
*   **Model Complexity:** The proposed observer model is comprehensive but also has a large number of parameters (Eq. 10). While this provides flexibility, it also raises the question of whether the framework itself introduces identifiability issues or relies heavily on the specific parameterization chosen.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a fundamental, important, and often-underappreciated problem in a major area of NIPS research.
2.  Provides a novel and principled framework that is a clear methodological advance for the field.
3.  The potential impact on guiding experimental design is significant and could lead to more robust scientific conclusions.
4.  The case studies are compelling and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach with non-obvious results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The conclusions are conditional on the assumed "ground truth" reference model, a potential limitation that could have been discussed more thoroughly.
2.  The framework's scope is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks, and its extension to other critical paradigms like 2AFC is not fully developed.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and original contribution to the methodology of computational modeling in cognitive science. It is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates its value with well-chosen examples. While there are limitations regarding the scope and the dependence on initial assumptions, these are typical of any new framework and do not detract from the core contribution. The work is likely to be highly influential for researchers using Bayesian models to understand perception and cognition.
