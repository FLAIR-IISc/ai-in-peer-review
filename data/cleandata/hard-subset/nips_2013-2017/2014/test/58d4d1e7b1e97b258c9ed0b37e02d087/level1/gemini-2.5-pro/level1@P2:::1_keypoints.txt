- Addresses the fundamental problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models used to infer priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavior.
- Provides a two-part framework: a flexible parametric class of observer models and a principled metric to quantify practical non-identifiability.
- Uses mixtures of Gaussians for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions to balance flexibility and computational tractability.
- Derives an identifiability metric based on expected log-likelihood, shown to be proportional to the KL-divergence between predicted response distributions.
- Maps the "identifiability landscape" by sampling the approximate expected posterior via MCMC to reveal parameter confusions.
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies: recovering priors in time-interval estimation and assessing the slow-speed prior in vision.
- Shows certain experimental designs greatly improve identifiability (e.g., MTR) and explains why others fail (e.g., sensory vs. motor noise degeneracy).
- Technical derivations and model construction are sound and enable efficient expected-loss calculations.
- Claims are well-supported by thorough analyses and transparent assumptions about the reference observer.
- Main weakness: conclusions depend on assumptions about the reference observer (θ*), so design advice is exploratory rather than definitive.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with informative figures that effectively communicate results.
- The work is highly original in applying a formal quantitative identifiability framework to Bayesian observer models.
- Provides a general-purpose tool to investigate degeneracy a priori, improving experimental design beyond ad-hoc fixes.
- Significant potential impact: helps experimentalists design more informative studies and strengthens modeling rigor in cognitive science.
- Case-study insights are important: time-interval prior recovery is sensitive to motor-noise measurement; slow-speed prior appears robust.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical problem, introduces a novel and practical methodology, has high impact potential, includes insightful demos, and is excellently presented.
- Argument against acceptance: reliance on reference-observer assumptions limits definitiveness of recommendations (minor). 
- Overall recommendation: strong endorsement for acceptance based on technical quality, originality, clarity, and significance.
