The paper "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception" proposes a novel framework for systematically testing the identifiability of a significant class of Bayesian observer models. The authors aim to address the issue of model non-identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavior. The paper provides a general framework for investigating identifiability, which can be used to improve experimental design.

The authors introduce a continuous class of Bayesian observer models parametrized by a vector θ, which extends previous work by encompassing any sensorimotor estimation task in which a one-dimensional stimulus magnitude variable is directly estimated by the observer. The model is constructed in four stages: sensation, perception, decision-making, and response. The authors use a maximum-entropy approach to determine the shape of the measurement distribution and the loss function. The model allows for a large class of sensory models in the psychophysics literature, including stimulus-dependent noise and 'robust' mixture models.

The authors define the support for an observer model θ as its log likelihood, given a dataset D, and compute the expected support for model θ as the expected log likelihood over all possible datasets. They use the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response probability densities of the true model θ∗ and the observer model θ to quantify the average support for model θ. The authors sample from the approximate posterior density using MCMC and use the results to assess model identifiability.

The paper applies the framework to two case studies: the inference of priors in a time interval estimation task and the reconstruction of prior and noise characteristics in speed perception. The results suggest that the reconstruction of internal representations in interval timing requires strong experimental constraints and validations, and that the inference of internal representations in speed perception is generally robust and not prone to major degeneracies.
