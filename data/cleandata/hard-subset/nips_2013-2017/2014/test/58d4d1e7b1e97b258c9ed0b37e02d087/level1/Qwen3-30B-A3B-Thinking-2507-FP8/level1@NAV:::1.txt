

This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations due to the inherent degeneracy in the Bayesian framework. The authors propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing, which allows researchers to evaluate whether their experimental designs can reliably recover model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The work builds on previous concerns about model degeneracy in the field (cited as [17, 18, 19]) and extends the theoretical analysis of identifiability beyond the limited case studies previously examined.

The paper's strengths are significant. The framework is technically sound, with clear mathematical derivation (Section 3) that efficiently quantifies identifiability using KL-divergence. The authors demonstrate practical utility through two well-chosen case studies: time interval estimation (Section 4.1) and speed perception (Section 4.2). These applications reveal non-intuitive insights, such as how imposing a narrow loss function can actually degrade prior recovery in interval timing tasks. The paper is well-organized, with clear exposition of the model structure (Section 2) and thorough experimental design comparisons. The case studies provide concrete evidence of the framework's utility in improving experimental design.

The main weakness is the limited scope of the model class, which primarily addresses one-dimensional sensory estimation tasks. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest future extensions (Section 5), the framework's applicability to more complex perceptual tasks (e.g., multi-cue integration) remains untested. The paper also could better connect to recent work on identifiability in machine learning (e.g., [23]) to strengthen the theoretical grounding.

The significance is high for the perceptual psychology community, as it provides a practical tool to validate Bayesian model inferences. The framework directly addresses a methodological gap that has undermined the reliability of previous studies claiming to recover internal representations. The paper advances the field by providing a systematic approach to experimental design that has been lacking.

Pro: Novel framework for identifiability testing, practical case studies with actionable insights, strong technical foundation. Con: Limited scope to one-dimensional tasks, minimal connection to broader identifiability literature.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that should be accepted. The framework provides a much-needed tool for the field, and the case studies effectively demonstrate its utility in improving experimental design.
