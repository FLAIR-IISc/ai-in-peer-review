- Addresses model non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models, where prior, likelihood, and loss can produce identical behavior.
- Proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of model parameters for a given experimental design.
- Introduces a flexible, computationally efficient class of 1D Bayesian observer models using Gaussian mixtures for prior, likelihood, and loss.
- Develops an "identifiability landscape" method based on expected log-likelihood / KL divergence between predicted response distributions.
- Uses sampling from an approximate expected posterior to reveal which parameters are well-constrained vs. degenerate.
- Demonstrates the framework on two case studies: time-interval estimation (Jazayeri & Shadlen) and slow-speed prior in visual perception (Stocker & Simoncelli).
- Strength — Significance: tackles a fundamental, under-addressed problem and enables pre-experiment design evaluation.
- Strength — Originality: novel combination of flexible parametric models with a KL-based identifiability metric.
- Strength — Quality: mathematically sound, well-justified modeling choices, and insightful case-study results.
- Strength — Clarity: clearly written, well-organized, with interpretable figures and explanations.
- Weakness — Dependence on reference observer: conclusions rely on the chosen ground-truth θ*, with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness — Scope/generalizability: framework limited to 1D estimation tasks and lacks detailed treatment of discrete-choice paradigms like 2AFC.
- Weakness — Model complexity: large parameterization may itself introduce identifiability concerns or dependence on parameter choices.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers a principled methodological advance, has significant impact potential for experimental design, and presents convincing case studies.
- Arguments against acceptance: conclusions conditional on chosen reference model and limited extension to other paradigms like 2AFC.
- Overall: high-quality, original, and influential work with some limitations in scope and sensitivity to reference-model assumptions.
