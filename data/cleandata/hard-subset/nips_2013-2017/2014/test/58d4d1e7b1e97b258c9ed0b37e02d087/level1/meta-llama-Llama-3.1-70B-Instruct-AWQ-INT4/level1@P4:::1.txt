This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors argue that despite the success of Bayesian observer models in describing human performance in perceptual tasks, the intrinsic degeneracy of the Bayesian framework may lead to model non-identifiability, making it challenging to recover the internal representations of priors, likelihoods, or loss functions from the data.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of model identifiability in Bayesian observer models. They also provide a comprehensive review of the relevant literature, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches to addressing this issue.

The proposed framework is based on a novel class of observer models that is both flexible and efficient. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two case studies: the inference of priors in a time interval estimation task and the reconstruction of prior and noise characteristics in speed perception.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of model identifiability in Bayesian observer models.
* The proposed framework is based on a novel class of observer models that is both flexible and efficient.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two case studies.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a comprehensive review of the relevant literature.

However, there are some weaknesses and limitations to the paper:

* The authors assume that the observer's internal representation of the likelihood is expressed in the same measurement space as the true likelihood, which may not always be the case.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their framework, which may be a limitation for large-scale applications.
* The authors do not discuss the potential limitations of their framework in terms of its ability to handle complex or non-linear relationships between the internal representations and the observed behavior.
