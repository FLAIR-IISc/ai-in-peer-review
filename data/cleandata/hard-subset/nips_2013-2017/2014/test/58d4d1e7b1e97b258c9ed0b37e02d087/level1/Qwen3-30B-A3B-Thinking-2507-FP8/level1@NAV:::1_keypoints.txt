- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception and proposes an a priori identifiability testing framework.  
- Framework lets researchers evaluate whether experimental designs can recover priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavioral data.  
- Extends theoretical analysis of identifiability beyond previous limited case studies and builds on prior concerns in the field.  
- Technically sound: clear mathematical derivation using KL-divergence to quantify identifiability (Section 3).  
- Demonstrates practical utility with two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception).  
- Case studies yield non-intuitive, actionable insights (e.g., narrow loss functions can hurt prior recovery).  
- Well-organized exposition of model structure (Section 2) and thorough experimental design comparisons.  
- Main limitation: model class scoped to one-dimensional sensory estimation tasks; applicability to complex tasks (e.g., multi-cue integration) untested.  
- Paper could better connect to recent machine learning identifiability literature (e.g., [23]) to strengthen theoretical grounding.  
- High significance for perceptual psychology: provides a practical tool to validate Bayesian model inferences and improve experimental design.  
- Summary judgment: novel, practical, and technically strong contribution—should be accepted.
