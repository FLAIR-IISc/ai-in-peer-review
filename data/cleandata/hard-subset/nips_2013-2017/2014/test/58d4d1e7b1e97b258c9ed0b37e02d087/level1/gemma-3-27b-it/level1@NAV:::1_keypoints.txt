- Addresses identifiability in Bayesian perception models and proposes a framework to test it by exploring parameter space and quantifying expected support for alternative models using KL-divergence.  
- Demonstrates the framework on two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian observer modeling work and extends previous approaches to a broader class of models.  

Strengths:
- Well-motivated and theoretically sound framework with a clear explanation of the identifiability problem and proposed solution.  
- Uses KL-divergence as a principled metric for model similarity.  
- Case studies provide practical insights for experimental design, notably that narrow loss functions harm identifiability in time estimation.  
- Code availability would improve reproducibility (suggested but not provided).  

Weaknesses:
- Paper complexity and dense equations may limit accessibility for non-expert readers.  
- Weak linkage between computed identifiability metrics and concrete guidance for experimenters.  
- Reliance on Gaussian-mixture parametric assumptions for priors and likelihoods limits generality.  
- Speed perception case study largely validates known findings rather than uncovering new issues.  
- Emphasis on parameter recovery neglects potential identifiability problems in model structure.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a timely, important problem with a novel, sound framework and relevant practical demonstrations.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Accessibility concerns, limited exploration of the framework’s potential, and restrictive parametric assumptions.  

Overall recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify experimenter guidance and acknowledge parametric limitations.
