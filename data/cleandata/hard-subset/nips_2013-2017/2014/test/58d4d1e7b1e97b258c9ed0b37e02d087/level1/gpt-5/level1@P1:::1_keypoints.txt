- Formalizes degeneracy in Bayesian decision theory and proposes a framework to assess a priori identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss.
- Introduces an analytically tractable observer class for 1D estimation with flexible stimulus-to-internal mapping, two-Gaussian internal noise mixture, lattice-Gaussian prior, inverted-Gaussian loss mixtures, lapses, and motor noise.
- Uses mixture-of-Gaussians algebra to compute expected loss and closed-form predictive response densities efficiently.
- Quantifies identifiability via expected log marginal likelihood, reducible to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predictive response densities.
- Maps practical non-identifiability regions by sampling E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(〈log Pr(D|θ)〉) with MCMC to support experimental design comparisons.
- Case study 1 (interval timing): priors are poorly recovered in a two-range design; identifiability improves if motor noise is independently measured; MAP-like loss reduces recovery precision.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): robust recovery of prior shape and noise across contrast conditions, even when decoupling internal and objective likelihoods.
- Connects and extends prior work on model degeneracy and distinguishability by offering a domain-tailored, computationally efficient identifiability tool.
- Derivation of expected log evidence as KL sum is technically sound and appropriate for a priori analysis.
- Observer class balances analytic tractability with expressiveness (captures non-Gaussian skewness/kurtosis).
- Simulations convincingly demonstrate parameter trade-offs (sensory vs motor noise; loss width vs prior) and design-dependent identifiability.
- Sensitivity analyses are limited; results likely depend on choice of θ*, mixture resolution M, and number of trials Ntr.
- Conclusions depend on parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures, lattice prior with M=15, specific lapse model sampling from prior).
- 2AFC-to-estimation conversion in the speed study is only briefly justified and needs more support.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, with explicit modeling pipeline and stated assumptions.
- Important reproducibility details are deferred: maximum-entropy mixture setup, MCMC settings and diagnostics, and runtime information.
- Original contributions: unified efficient observer family spanning priors/likelihoods/losses with non-Gaussianity, operationalizing a priori identifiability maps, and realistic experimental design comparisons.
- Practical impact: provides a principled tool to stress-test identifiability before data collection, reducing risk of over-interpretation in Bayesian psychophysics.
- Scientific value: validates classic findings and challenges assumptions in interval timing paradigms.
- Broader relevance: links to optimal experimental design and suggests pathways to adaptive design using expected KL.
- Strengths: clear formalization of degeneracy, flexible observer parameterization, interpretable KL-based identifiability metric, actionable design recommendations, and robust recovery in speed perception.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: run broader sensitivity sweeps over θ*, σℓ, lapse models, stimulus placement, and mixture size M to test stability.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: test prior representation on heavy-tailed, multimodal, or sharply peaked true priors to evaluate lattice basis coverage.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: compare alternative lapse models (e.g., uniform) and quantify their impact on identifiability.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: demonstrate stimulus optimization or link to expected information gain / Fisher information to strengthen the design claim.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: provide derivation or empirical check that 2AFC-to-estimation surrogate preserves identifiability properties.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: release code and fuller algorithmic detail to improve reproducibility.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important issue, methodologically sound, yields actionable experimental guidance, and bridges theory and practice.
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to modeling choices and θ*, limited to 1D estimation tasks, and some computational/methodological details deferred to supplement.
- Overall: solid, timely contribution that advances identifiability analysis in Bayesian observer modeling; leans toward acceptance pending additional sensitivity analyses and reproducibility improvements.
