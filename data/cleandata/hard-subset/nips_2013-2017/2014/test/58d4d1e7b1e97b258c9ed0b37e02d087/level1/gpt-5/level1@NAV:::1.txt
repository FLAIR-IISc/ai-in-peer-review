This paper tackles a central and under-examined issue in Bayesian observer modeling: practical identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions recovered from behavioral data. The authors propose a unified, parametric class of 1D estimation models spanning sensation (stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) and a moment-constrained, unimodal Gaussian-mixture likelihood), prior (Gaussian lattice mixture), loss (inverted Gaussian mixture), decision (expected-loss minimization with lapses), and response (motor noise). They then assess a priori identifiability by computing the expected log-likelihood of candidate parameters θ under data generated by θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimulus conditions (Eq. 12). Sampling an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) allows exploration of degeneracies and design choices before collecting data. Two case studies illustrate: (i) in interval timing, priors are poorly recovered unless motor noise is known; incentivizing a near-MAP loss worsens recovery; (ii) in speed perception, the slow-speed prior and noise parameters are comparatively robust, aligning with Stocker & Simoncelli (2006) and related work. The paper is well-situated relative to Bayesian observer models (e.g., Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.) and model distinguishability (Navarro, Pitt & Myung).

Quality: The derivation of the expected support (KL-based) is sound under i.i.d. trials and known stimulus frequencies. The modeling framework is coherent and computationally motivated. However, conclusions rely on parametric choices (two-component mixtures, unimodality, 1D tasks) and on the assumed mapping f(s); sensitivity analyses to misspecification are limited. The 2AFC-to-estimation reduction is plausible but not validated here.

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear and well-structured, though several critical details (maximum-entropy constructions, computational cost, MCMC settings) are deferred to the supplement. Some notation could be streamlined.

Originality: The combination of a general observer class with an a priori identifiability mapping via expected support is novel and practically useful, complementing prior recovery and Bayesian transfer work.

Significance: The framework directly informs experimental design and addresses recurring critiques about degeneracy in Bayesian cognition. If adopted, it could improve the reliability of inferred mental representations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap with a principled, efficient identifiability metric (KL-based).
- Unifies priors/likelihood/loss under a flexible yet tractable parameterization.
- Nontrivial, actionable design insights in two canonical domains.
- Well-connected to NeurIPS literature and broadly relevant.

Cons against acceptance:
- No empirical validation or model-recovery benchmarks against full simulated datasets.
- Heavy parametric assumptions (unimodality, mixtures, 1D) and reliance on accurate f(s).
- Limited comparison to alternative design criteria (e.g., expected information gain/Fisher information).
- The estimation casting of 2AFC is assumed rather than demonstrated.

Suggestions: Add recovery curves comparing the KL-based approximation to full simulation; report computational costs; probe robustness to misspecified loss/likelihood forms; compare with BOED objectives; clarify extension to discrete-choice tasks. Overall, a strong and timely contribution.
