

# Peer Review

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the problem of model non-identifiability. The authors correctly point out that while Bayesian observer models are widely used to recover hidden mental representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data, the inverse mapping from behavior to these components is inherently degenerate. Multiple combinations of model components can produce identical behavioral outputs, making it impossible to reliably recover the intended internal representations. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models, which they apply to two case studies in time perception and speed perception.

The paper builds upon previous NIPS work on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 2005; Maloney & Mamassian, 2009; Körding & Wolpert, 2006) but addresses a significant gap: the lack of systematic methods for assessing whether model components can be reliably recovered from data. While prior work has proposed various constraints to reduce degeneracy (e.g., Mamassian, 2009), the authors are the first to present a general framework for analyzing identifiability a priori before conducting experiments.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors develop a flexible Bayesian observer model class that covers a wide range of psychophysical tasks, with a clear mathematical foundation. Their framework for identifiability testing using expected support (via KL divergence) is theoretically rigorous and well-motivated. The two case studies provide strong empirical validation of their approach. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the framework being applicable to a specific class of models rather than all Bayesian models of perception. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define their model class, develop their identifiability framework, and demonstrate it through well-chosen case studies. The figures effectively illustrate key findings (e.g., Figure 2 showing reconstruction errors across experimental designs). The mathematical derivations are presented with appropriate detail, and the writing is accessible to researchers in computational neuroscience and psychology. The only minor improvement would be to provide more detail on the MCMC sampling procedure in the main text.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution by providing the first systematic framework for testing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. While related work has discussed model degeneracy (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012), this paper offers a concrete, practical method that can be applied to experimental design. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous approaches by focusing on a priori identifiability testing rather than ad-hoc solutions. The framework represents a novel combination of Bayesian modeling, information theory (KL divergence), and experimental design principles.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. By providing a tool to systematically assess identifiability, the authors help prevent misinterpretation of results in studies that rely on Bayesian model inversion. Their case studies demonstrate how experimental design choices critically affect the reliability of recovered representations, with implications for how researchers design experiments in perception. The finding that imposing a narrow loss function (closer to MAP) can worsen prior recovery is particularly insightful and challenges some common assumptions in the field. The framework could be broadly adopted across cognitive science and neuroscience.

**Strengths:**
- Identifies a critical gap in the literature that has been overlooked in many Bayesian perception studies
- Proposes a theoretically sound, practical framework for identifiability testing
- Provides two well-executed case studies demonstrating the framework's utility
- Offers concrete guidance for experimental design (e.g., measuring motor parameters improves identifiability)
- Well-written and well-organized with clear mathematical foundations

**Weaknesses:**
- The framework is limited to a specific class of models (one-dimensional stimulus estimation), though the authors acknowledge this
- The computational cost of exploring the identifiability landscape may be high for very complex models
- The paper could more explicitly discuss how their framework relates to existing model comparison metrics (e.g., Bayesian model selection)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant, previously overlooked problem in Bayesian perception modeling
- Provides a novel, practical framework with clear experimental applications
- Well-executed case studies demonstrate the framework's value
- High potential impact on experimental design in perception research
- Rigorous theoretical foundation and clear presentation

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Framework limited to a specific class of models (though this is acknowledged)
- May require substantial computational resources for complex models
- The paper's scope is somewhat narrow (focusing on perceptual tasks rather than broader cognitive modeling)

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by providing a much-needed tool for assessing model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The framework is both theoretically sound and practically useful, with clear implications for experimental design. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility and reveal non-intuitive findings that could prevent misinterpretation of previous results. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. This work deserves publication at NIPS as it provides a valuable methodological contribution that will benefit researchers across computational neuroscience and cognitive science.

The paper is technically excellent, addresses an important problem, and provides practical guidance that will improve experimental design in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
