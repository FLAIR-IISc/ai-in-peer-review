- Addresses identifiability in Bayesian observer models, noting degeneracy among priors, likelihoods, and loss functions.  
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework to test identifiability and guide experimental design.  
- Quantifies identifiability using information-theoretic tools (e.g., KL divergence) and claims computational efficiency and generalizability.  
- Validates the framework through two case studies: time-interval estimation (examining experimental effects on recovering priors) and speed perception (robustness under a slow-speed prior).  
- Strength: Novel and important contribution to a foundational, underexplored problem in psychophysics.  
- Strength: Theoretically rigorous, with a well-structured mathematical approach.  
- Strength: Practically relevant—links identifiability to experimental design and highlights surprising effects (e.g., narrow loss functions harming recovery).  
- Strength: Case studies demonstrate versatility and align with prior empirical findings.  
- Weakness: Mathematical presentation is dense; key derivations (e.g., identifiability metric) may be hard for non-experts.  
- Weakness: Limited range of experimental designs examined; discrete-choice paradigms like 2AFC are mentioned but not tested.  
- Weakness: Framework depends on assumptions (e.g., Gaussian priors, specific noise models) whose general validity is uncertain.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and broaden discussion of applicability.
