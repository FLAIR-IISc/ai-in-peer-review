This paper addresses the critical and well-known issue of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly note that identical behavioral data can often be explained by different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, posing a fundamental challenge to the goal of recovering an observer’s true internal representations. Building on previous work that has highlighted this degeneracy (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2010), the paper introduces a novel and systematic framework for testing model identifiability *a priori*, before an experiment is run. The core contribution is a computationally efficient method that maps the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected Kullback-Leibler divergence between a hypothetical "ground truth" observer and alternative models, given a specific experimental design. This framework is then demonstrated on two case studies prominent in the NIPS community: interval timing (related to Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010) and speed perception (related to Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006).

**Quality & Originality:** The paper is of high quality and technically sound. While the problem of identifiability is not new, the proposed solution of a formal, general-purpose *a priori* testing framework is highly original and constitutes a significant methodological advance over common post-hoc model recovery simulations. The flexible parametric observer model is well-conceived, and the derivation of the expected log-likelihood as a metric for distinguishability is elegant. The work is a complete contribution, moving from theoretical framework to practical application.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is laid out compellingly in the introduction, the framework is described logically, and the results from the case studies effectively illustrate its utility. The figures are informative and support the main conclusions.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a principled tool that can directly help researchers in computational cognitive science and neuroscience design more robust and informative experiments. By identifying potential parameter degeneracies beforehand, the framework can guide choices about stimulus distributions, feedback conditions, and the necessity of complementary measurements (as shown in the MTR condition of the timing study). The finding that a MAP-like loss function can paradoxically *worsen* prior identifiability is a non-obvious insight with practical implications. Furthermore, the framework’s validation of the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference provides important theoretical support for a landmark finding in the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental methodological problem in a popular modeling approach within the NIPS community.
*   The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and provides a practical tool for improving experimental design.
*   The case studies are well-chosen, insightful, and connect directly to influential prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The framework, as presented, is limited to one-dimensional estimation tasks. While the authors acknowledge this, its extension to more complex paradigms like discrete-choice tasks or multi-cue integration remains future work.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong methodological contribution. It should be of great interest to anyone using Bayesian models to understand perception and cognition.
