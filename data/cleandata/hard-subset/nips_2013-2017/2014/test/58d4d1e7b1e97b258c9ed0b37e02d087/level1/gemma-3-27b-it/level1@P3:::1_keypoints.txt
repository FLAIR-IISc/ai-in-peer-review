- Tackles identifiability in Bayesian perception models, highlighting degeneracy where different priors/likelihoods/losses yield the same behavior.  
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework for a priori identifiability analysis to guide experimental design.  
- Applies framework to time interval estimation and speed perception, providing concrete illustrations.  
- Core method: explore a parameterized class of observer models to assess how different parameter combinations produce similar behavior.  
- Model (Section 2) is complex but well-motivated, balancing generality and computational tractability.  
- Uses maximum entropy principles to define likelihoods and loss functions, which is sensible.  
- Introduces the expected support metric (Eq. 12) linked to KL divergence as a mathematically grounded proxy for identifiability.  
- Sensitivity to the chosen functional form for stimulus-response mapping (Eq. 2) is insufficiently explored and could alter identifiability results.  
- High-dimensional parameterization increases flexibility but complicates interpretation and obscures which parameters drive degeneracy.  
- Section 4.1 suggests motor parameter (MTR) effects, but a systematic analysis of parameter correlations is needed.  
- Reliance on a reference observer (θ*) is critical; if θ* is misspecified, the analysis may be misleading.  
- Speed perception application feels less novel and doesn’t reveal surprising insights compared to time interval example.  
- Claim that priors/noise reconstruction is “relatively reliable” needs stronger critical discussion given prior literature.  
- Computational cost of exploring the parameter space is likely high and resource requirements are not reported.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty of the framework, technical soundness, potential impact on experimental design, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to assumptions, computational demands, limited novelty in the speed example, and interpretation challenges due to dimensionality.  
- Recommended revisions: perform sensitivity analysis over functional forms, report computational resources, assess robustness to θ* variations, and strengthen discussion of speed perception results.  
- Overall recommendation: valuable contribution; accept with minor revisions to address sensitivity, interpretability, robustness, and computational reporting.
