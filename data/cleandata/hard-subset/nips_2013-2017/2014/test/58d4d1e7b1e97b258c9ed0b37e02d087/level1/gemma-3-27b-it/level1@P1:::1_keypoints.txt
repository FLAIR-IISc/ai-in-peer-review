- Addresses identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models and proposes a framework to systematically test it.  
- Framework demonstrated through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Core contribution: formal analysis of degeneracy where multiple priors, likelihoods, and loss functions yield identical behavior.  
- Uses KL divergence as an analytical tool to quantify identifiability; explanation is clear and justified.  
- Finds that measuring motor noise is important for reliable prior recovery in time interval estimation.  
- Validation on speed perception corroborates previous findings and supports the approach.  
- Underlying model (Eqs. 2–9) is specific (Gaussian mixtures, 1D stimuli), limiting generality and applicability.  
- Generalization to more complex tasks and higher-dimensional stimuli is not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Choice of reference observer (θ*) is somewhat arbitrary and sensitivity to this choice is insufficiently explored.  
- Paper focuses on a priori identifiability with limited analysis of how identifiability changes with dataset size (Ntr).  
- Claim that increasing trials in single-condition SRT doesn’t help much is asserted without quantitative scale of improvement.  
- Conclusion about the “bad impact” of a narrow loss function is strong relative to model assumptions and needs deeper justification.  
- Some sections are dense and could be clearer (e.g., derivation around Equation 12).  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel and solid framework, useful case studies, mathematically justified.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope from model assumptions, incomplete sensitivity analysis, potential lack of generalizability, and writing clarity issues.  
- Overall: significant contribution worthy of acceptance with minor revisions to address sensitivity to θ*, model limitations, and dataset-size effects.
