### Summary

This paper presents a novel computational framework to systematically test the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors correctly identify a critical weakness in the application of Bayesian Decision Theory as a process model: the "inverse problem," where multiple combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss function can produce identical behavioral predictions, making it difficult to uniquely recover an observer's internal representations from data.

To address this, the authors develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of observer models for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. This model uses parametric forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians) to represent a wide range of sensory mappings, priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. The core of their framework is a method to quantify the identifiability of a "reference" observer model (θ*). They propose using the expected log-likelihood of a competing model (θ) over all possible datasets generated by θ*. This quantity is shown to be proportional to the negative Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the response distributions predicted by the two models. By sampling from an "approximate expected posterior" derived from this metric, one can map out the "identifiability landscape" and see which parameters are well-constrained by a given experimental design and which are degenerate.

The authors apply this framework to two case studies. First, in a time interval estimation task, they compare different experimental designs and find that simply adding more trials is less effective at improving prior recovery than combining data from multiple stimulus conditions or, most effectively, independently measuring motor noise. Counter-intuitively, they find that a design encouraging a narrower, MAP-like loss function can actually worsen identifiability. Second, they re-examine the well-known "slow-speed prior" in visual perception and find that the inference of both the prior and the noise characteristics is theoretically robust and not prone to major degeneracies, lending support to the original findings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The non-identifiability of model components is a pervasive and often-overlooked issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling. This paper tackles the problem head-on, moving beyond acknowledging the issue to proposing a concrete, principled solution. This is a significant contribution to the field, promoting more rigorous modeling practices.
2.  **Novel and Practical Framework:** The proposed method for exploring the identifiability landscape using the KL-divergence as a metric for model similarity is elegant and powerful. The framework's key strength is its *a priori* nature, allowing researchers to evaluate and refine experimental designs *before* collecting costly data, potentially saving significant resources and leading to more informative experiments.
3.  **General and Flexible Observer Model:** The parametric model is thoughtfully constructed to be both flexible and computationally tractable. It can approximate key phenomena like Weber-Fechner/Stevens' laws for sensory mapping and can represent non-Gaussian and asymmetric priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. The use of mixture-of-Gaussians to maintain analytical tractability is a clever engineering choice.
4.  **Insightful and Non-Trivial Case Studies:** The applications are not mere toy examples but address real scientific questions from the literature. The results provide concrete, actionable insights. For example, the finding that a narrow loss function can harm identifiability by trading off with other parameters is an important and non-obvious result. Likewise, providing theoretical validation for the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference adds confidence to a key finding in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Observer (θ*):** The entire analysis is conditional on the assumed "ground truth" reference observer. The conclusions about which experimental design is best might change if a different θ* were chosen. While the authors define their reference observer based on the literature, the paper would be stronger if it included a sensitivity analysis demonstrating how the conclusions hold up under different plausible assumptions for θ*.
2.  **Limited Scope (Estimation Tasks):** The framework is currently developed for one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. While this covers an important class of experiments, a large portion of psychophysics relies on discrete-choice paradigms like two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC). The authors mention this as a direction for future work, but the current applicability is limited. The re-analysis of the speed perception study, originally a 2AFC task, requires its conversion to an "equivalent estimation task," a step that could be elaborated on further.
3.  **Model Complexity:** The flexibility of the observer model comes at the cost of a high-dimensional parameter space (~25 parameters). While this is necessary to capture a wide range of possibilities, it raises concerns about the feasibility and stability of the MCMC sampling used to explore the identifiability landscape. It also means that new degeneracies might arise from the sheer complexity of the model itself, not just the core prior-likelihood-loss tradeoff.
4.  **Theoretical Idealization:** The analysis is purely theoretical and assumes the true observer is perfectly described by some parameterization within the model class. It does not address the issue of model mismatch, where a real human observer may not conform to the model's structural assumptions. This is not a flaw of the framework itself, but a general limitation of this type of *a priori* analysis that is worth acknowledging.

### Qualitative Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is theoretically sound, and the claims are well-supported by the simulation results presented in the case studies. The work is complete and provides a self-contained contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is compelling, the model is built up in logical stages, and the results are explained well. Given the complexity of the model and method, the authors have done an excellent job of making it accessible.
*   **Originality:** This work is highly original. While model identifiability is a known problem, this is, to my knowledge, the first attempt to create a general computational framework for systematically and quantitatively assessing it *a priori* in the context of Bayesian perceptual models. It is a novel and important synthesis of statistical methods and cognitive modeling.
*   **Significance:** The significance of this work is potentially very high. It provides a tool that could, and should, be used by researchers designing experiments to probe internal representations. It can help make modeling efforts more reliable and conclusions more robust. By providing a method to diagnose and potentially resolve degeneracies, this work significantly advances the state of the art in applying Bayesian models to understand perception.

### Arguments for/against acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses a critical, foundational problem in a popular and influential area of research.
*   Provides a novel, principled, and practical framework that can be used to improve experimental design.
*   Demonstrates the utility of the framework with well-chosen case studies that yield non-obvious and important insights.
*   The work is of high quality, original, and could have a significant impact on future research in computational cognitive science.

**Arguments against:**
*   The conclusions are dependent on the specific choice of the "reference observer," and sensitivity to this choice is not explored.
*   The framework is currently limited to a specific class of psychophysical tasks (1D estimation).

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents an innovative and much-needed methodological advance for the field. It is well-executed, clearly written, and the potential impact is substantial.
