Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception, where behavior is used to infer latent components (prior, likelihood, loss). It formalizes the intrinsic degeneracy of Bayesian decision theory (Equation 1), showing that combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss can yield indistinguishable behavior. To study practical identifiability a priori, the authors propose:
- A flexible parametric class of observer models for 1D estimation tasks, covering sensation (stimulus-to-measurement transform f, mixture-Gaussian noise with controlled variance, skewness, kurtosis), prior (Gaussian basis mixture), loss (mixture of inverted Gaussians), lapse, and motor reporting noise.
- An identifiability criterion based on the expected log-likelihood of data generated by a reference observer θ∗ under a candidate θ, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions (Eq. 12). They define an “approximate expected posterior” E(θ|θ∗) ∝ exp〈log Pr(D|θ)〉 and sample it via MCMC to map identifiability.
- Two case studies: (1) interval timing (similar to Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010), comparing experimental designs for recovering priors; (2) speed perception (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), testing recovery of a slow-speed prior and noise parameters (including a variant decoupling internal and experimental likelihoods).

This work relates to discussions on Bayesian model degeneracy and “Bayesian just-so” critiques, and to model distinguishability work in cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung). It complements empirical inversion of observer models (e.g., Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.) by providing a priori identifiability analysis and design guidance.

Strengths
- Clear formalization: The expected log-likelihood under the true generator equates to negative KL divergences plus a constant; this is a sound and interpretable foundation for identifiability assessments.
- Flexible yet computationally tractable observer class: The mixture-of-Gaussians machinery yields analytic or fast numerical expected loss computations and allows skew/kurtosis in both likelihood and loss.
- Practical design insights: For interval timing, the framework diagnoses non-identifiability between sensory and motor noise and shows that fixing motor noise improves prior recovery; it also reveals that encouraging MAP-like losses can worsen identifiability. For speed, it shows robust recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent and noise characteristics across conditions.
- Potential for pre-experiment design optimization: The weighting by experimental stimulus distribution Pi naturally connects to optimizing stimulus sets/frequencies.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Dependence on parametric choices: Identifiability conclusions hinge on the chosen parameterizations (e.g., f(s) family, two-Gaussian mixtures, Gaussian basis for priors with fixed M). Sensitivity to these choices (e.g., M, basis spacing a, number of mixture components) is not fully quantified.
- “Approximate expected posterior”: E(θ|θ∗) is not a true posterior and is used as a proxy for identifiability. While appropriate for ranking designs, it does not directly yield calibrated uncertainty. Discussion of how this proxy relates to Bayes factors or Fisher information would strengthen the methodology.
- High dimensionality: With M=15 prior weights plus numerous noise and loss parameters, the landscape may be multi-modal. Details on MCMC convergence diagnostics, proposal schemes, and computational cost are minimal in the main text.
- Scope: Extensions to discrete-choice paradigms (e.g., 2AFC) are only suggested. The second case study assumes equivalence via an estimation reformulation; empirical validation of this equivalence or sensitivity analyses would increase confidence.
- Loss identifiability: The timing example shows σℓ is weakly identifiable; further quantification (e.g., profile likelihoods) and guidance on when losses can be constrained would be helpful.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The derivation of Eq. 12 from Eq. 11 is standard and correct (cross-entropy/KL identity). The use of mixtures for sensory and loss models, and the maximum-entropy selection under moment constraints are reasonable and referenced, though full details are deferred to the supplement.
- The case studies are internally consistent, and the analysis linking observed reconstruction biases to parameter confounds (e.g., σ vs motor noise) is coherent.
- Some claims would benefit from additional diagnostics (e.g., sensitivity to Ntr, alternative priors over θ, and basis size M).

Clarity
- The model is carefully described with explicit equations and assumptions for each stage. The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Some notation choices are dense (e.g., reuse of σ across different roles) and could be streamlined. Explicit constraints on prior weights (non-negativity, normalization) should be stated. A brief complexity analysis and MCMC diagnostic summary in the main text would improve readability.

Originality
- Novel combination of: a general, efficient observer model class; a KL-based expected-support criterion for identifiability; and systematic, a priori design comparison in perception tasks. While related to distinguishability methods in cognitive modeling, applying this to Bayesian observer models with practical design recommendations is original.

Significance
- Identifiability is central to interpreting inferred priors/likelihoods/losses in perception. Providing a tool to assess and improve identifiability before running experiments is valuable. The findings for interval timing and speed perception are practically informative and address standing concerns about degeneracy.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report sensitivity analyses to: basis size M and spacing; number of mixture components; alternative f(s) families; and Ntr.
- Provide MCMC details (proposal, runtime, convergence metrics) and summarize computational cost.
- Explore design optimization using the framework (optimize si, Pi to maximize expected discrimination among parameter sets of interest).
- Quantify identifiability of lapse vs prior tails and motor noise.
- Clarify constraints and parameterizations of the prior weights (e.g., softmax).
- Include a discrete-choice (2AFC) demonstration or stronger justification of the estimation equivalence.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a principled and implementable framework.
- Demonstrates nontrivial, actionable design insights in canonical perceptual tasks.
- Technically sound derivations and a flexible generative observer class with efficient inference.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on specific parameterizations with limited sensitivity analyses reported in the main text.
- The “approximate expected posterior” lacks calibration and its relation to standard model selection metrics is not deeply discussed.
- Limited empirical validation or discrete-choice application shown within the paper.

Overall, the work offers a solid, general framework for a priori identifiability analysis in Bayesian perception models, with clear practical utility and mostly sound methodology, albeit with some dependence on modeling choices and a need for broader sensitivity and convergence reporting.
