

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the fundamental non-identifiability problem where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs. While previous work has acknowledged this degeneracy (e.g., Mamassian & Landy, 2001; Jones & Love, 2011), the authors rightly note there has been no systematic framework for analyzing identifiability a priori. The paper proposes a theoretically grounded framework to test identifiability of Bayesian observer models before conducting experiments, with practical applications for experimental design.

The authors develop a flexible Bayesian observer model class that covers a broad range of sensory estimation tasks. Their key innovation is a method to quantify identifiability by computing the expected log-likelihood support for alternative models given the true model and experimental design. This approach leverages KL divergence to systematically evaluate whether specific experimental setups can reliably recover internal representations from behavioral data. The authors demonstrate their framework through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significance and Problem Addressed:** The paper tackles a genuinely important problem in the field. Many studies claim to recover "internal representations" from behavioral data without sufficient justification for identifiability, potentially leading to misleading conclusions. This work provides a much-needed methodological foundation.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The framework is theoretically well-founded, with careful derivations and clear mathematical formulation. The use of KL divergence as a measure of identifiability is appropriate and well-justified. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can reveal non-obvious insights about experimental design (e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function can actually worsen prior recovery).

3. **Practical Utility:** The paper moves beyond theoretical discussion to provide concrete guidance for experimental design. The authors successfully demonstrate how their framework can rank experimental designs by their ability to recover specific parameters of interest, which is highly valuable for experimental psychologists.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the case studies are well-explained with appropriate visualizations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Case Studies:** While the two case studies are well-executed, they cover only a narrow range of perceptual tasks. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently address how their framework would generalize to more complex scenarios (e.g., tasks with multiple sensory cues, categorical judgments, or more complex loss functions). The claim that the framework "covers a large class of psychophysical tasks" is somewhat overstated given the limited examples.

2. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational burden of applying this framework. Sampling from the approximate posterior density (Eq. 13) requires significant computation, especially for high-dimensional parameter spaces. For researchers with limited computational resources, this might be a significant barrier to adoption.

3. **Practical Implementation Guidance:** The paper provides a theoretical framework but lacks concrete guidance on how to implement it in practice. What software tools would facilitate this analysis? How would one handle real-world data with noise and outliers? The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but don't offer solutions.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The paper doesn't sufficiently contrast their approach with existing methods for addressing identifiability (e.g., Bayesian model averaging, regularization techniques, or constraints based on prior knowledge). This would strengthen the argument for why their specific framework is necessary.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic method for addressing a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception. The theoretical framework is sound, and the case studies effectively demonstrate its utility. While the scope is somewhat limited, the methodological approach is general enough to be applicable to a wide range of perceptual tasks.

The significance of the problem addressed is high, as many conclusions in the literature about "internal representations" may be questionable due to non-identifiability. The paper provides a clear path forward for improving experimental design in Bayesian modeling of perception, which could have substantial impact on the field.

The paper is well-written and technically rigorous, though it would benefit from more discussion of practical implementation and computational considerations. The framework's potential for improving experimental design in perception research is compelling, and the case studies provide convincing evidence of its utility.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper addresses an important methodological gap in a clear, theoretically sound manner with practical applications. While not without limitations, the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors have provided a valuable tool that will help improve the reliability of Bayesian modeling in perception research.
