This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue in the field of psychophysics. The authors introduce a general class of Bayesian observer models, which can be used to model a wide range of psychophysical tasks. They then develop an efficient method for exploring the identifiability of these models, which involves computing the expected support for a given model, given a dataset generated by a reference model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and methodology. The introduction provides a thorough review of the relevant literature, highlighting the importance of Bayesian models in psychophysics and the need for a systematic approach to testing their identifiability. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the degeneracy problem in Bayesian models, which is a key issue that their framework aims to address.

The technical details of the framework are presented in a clear and concise manner, making it easy to understand how the authors' method works. The use of a maximum-entropy approach to define the likelihood and loss functions is a nice touch, as it provides a principled way of specifying these functions. The authors also provide a detailed explanation of how they compute the expected support for a given model, which is a critical component of their framework.

The case studies are well-chosen and provide a nice demonstration of the effectiveness of the authors' framework. The time interval estimation task is a classic problem in psychophysics, and the authors' analysis provides some interesting insights into the identifiability of the model parameters. The speed perception task is also a well-studied problem, and the authors' analysis provides some useful validation of their framework.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it assumes a relatively simple form for the likelihood and loss functions. While the authors argue that this is a reasonable assumption, it is not clear how well their framework would generalize to more complex forms for these functions. Additionally, the authors' analysis is based on a relatively small number of parameters, and it is not clear how well their framework would scale to more complex models.

Overall, however, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of psychophysics. The authors' framework is a useful tool for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models, and their case studies provide some interesting insights into the behavior of these models.
