Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a central and timely problem for Bayesian observer models in perception: identifiability of latent components (priors, likelihoods, loss). The authors formalize the degeneracy inherent in Bayesian decision theory (Eq. 1) and propose a framework to assess a priori whether a given experimental design allows reliable recovery of targeted components. The technical core is a flexible yet computationally tractable observer class for one-dimensional estimation tasks: (i) a stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) with parameters allowing Weber-Fechner/Stevens-like laws; (ii) a measurement distribution in internal space modeled as a two-Gaussian mixture matched to variance, skewness, kurtosis; (iii) a prior represented as a lattice mixture of Gaussians; (iv) a loss function represented as an inverted-Gaussian mixture; (v) lapses; and (vi) motor noise on reporting. These choices allow the expected loss to be computed efficiently (mixture-of-Gaussians algebra) and yield a closed-form predictive response density.

Identifiability is quantified via the expected log marginal likelihood under the data-generating “reference observer” θ*, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predictive response densities (Eq. 12). Sampling the induced density E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(〈log Pr(D|θ)〉) via MCMC maps practical non-identifiability regions and supports experimental design comparisons.

Two case studies illustrate the approach. In interval timing (inspired by Jazayeri & Shadlen), priors are poorly recovered in a baseline two-range design; identifiability improves markedly when motor noise is independently measured, while pushing the loss toward MAP degrades recovery (σℓ is weakly identifiable). In speed perception with a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli), the framework recovers both prior shape and noise characteristics robustly across contrast conditions, even when decoupling internal and objective likelihoods.

This work connects to and extends prior discussions of degeneracy in Bayesian cognitive models and model distinguishability (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung) by delivering a domain-tailored, computationally efficient tool for a priori identifiability and design assessment in Bayesian observer modeling.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Derivation of the expected log evidence as a KL sum is correct and appropriate for a priori analysis. The observer class is thoughtfully engineered to admit analytic computations while remaining expressive (e.g., non-Gaussian skew/kurtosis). The case studies are well specified.
- Evidence: Simulations convincingly expose parameter trade-offs (e.g., sensory vs motor noise; loss width vs prior) and demonstrate design-dependent identifiability. However, sensitivity analyses are limited: results likely depend on the choice of θ*, on mixture resolution (M), and on Ntr.
- Limitations: Conclusions hinge on the parametric choices (two-Gaussian mixtures; lattice prior with M=15; specific lapse model sampling from the prior). The 2AFC-to-estimation conversion in the speed study is only briefly justified.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The modeling pipeline is explicit, and assumptions are stated. Some details are deferred to the supplement that are important for reproducibility and interpretation:
- The “maximum-entropy” procedure to set mixture parameters (sensation and loss) needs a concise in-text summary.
- Computational aspects (MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, runtime) are absent.
- The mapping from 2AFC to estimation warrants a brief derivation or citation.

Originality
- The identifiability objective (expected log evidence/KL) is related to established model distinguishability and information gain, but the contribution is original in (i) constructing a unified, efficient observer family that spans priors/likelihoods/losses with non-Gaussianity; (ii) operationalizing a priori identifiability maps for psychophysical estimation tasks; and (iii) using the tool to compare realistic experimental designs. This is a meaningful advance beyond prior conceptual critiques and ad hoc checks.

Significance
- Practical impact: A principled way to stress-test identifiability before collecting data could substantially improve experimental design and interpretation in Bayesian psychophysics—a community where over-interpretation of latent structure is a recognized risk.
- Scientific value: The contrasting case studies are informative: they both validate a well-known inference (slow-speed prior) and challenge design assumptions in interval timing (e.g., that MAP-like feedback helps).
- Broader relevance: The approach connects naturally to optimal experimental design (maximize expected KL), suggesting a pathway to adaptive design for psychophysics.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of degeneracy and practical non-identifiability.
- Flexible, efficient observer parameterization enabling analytic expected loss.
- Sensible, interpretable identifiability metric grounded in KL divergence.
- Concrete, insightful design recommendations (e.g., independently measuring motor noise; caution about MAP-like losses).
- Robust recovery in speed perception gives theoretical support to classic findings.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on θ*: Provide broader sensitivity sweeps over key dimensions (e.g., σℓ, lapse model, number and placement of stimuli, mixture size M), and report stability of conclusions.
- Prior representation: Discuss how the lattice Gaussian basis approximates heavy-tailed priors in task vs internal space; include an example where the true prior is multi-modal or sharply peaked to test coverage.
- Lapse model: Test alternatives (e.g., uniform over response range) and quantify impact on identifiability.
- Experimental design: Go beyond condition toggles to optimize the stimulus set and frequencies Pi (link to expected information gain / Fisher information). Even a small demonstration would strengthen the “design” claim.
- 2AFC mapping: Provide a derivation or empirical check that the estimation surrogate preserves identifiability properties of the original 2AFC task.
- Reproducibility: Release code and provide enough algorithmic detail for others to apply the framework.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-explored issue with clear relevance to the NeurIPS community.
- Methodologically sound and thoughtfully engineered for the target domain.
- Yields actionable guidance for experimenters; challenges assumptions in a high-profile paradigm.
- Bridges theory (KL-based identifiability) and practice (design comparisons) convincingly.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conclusions may be sensitive to modeling choices and to θ*; more extensive sensitivity analyses would inspire greater confidence.
- The scope is limited to 1D estimation tasks; discrete-choice tasks are left for future work, yet many studies use them.
- Some key computational and methodological details are deferred to the supplement.

Overall
A solid, timely contribution that advances the rigor of Bayesian observer modeling by providing a practical a priori identifiability tool and design insights. With additional sensitivity analyses, clearer links to optimal design, and improved reproducibility details, this work would be a strong fit for NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
