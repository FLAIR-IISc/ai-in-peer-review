- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models: multiple priors/likelihoods/losses can produce identical behavior, making inverse inference ill-posed.  
- Main contribution: a systematic, a priori framework for testing model identifiability to improve experimental design before data collection.  
- Constructs a flexible, tractable parametric class of 1D Bayesian observer models including stimulus-dependent noise, mixture-of-Gaussians priors/likelihoods/losses, and motor noise.  
- Key technical insight: expected log-likelihood of a model given data from a reference model equals KL divergence between their response distributions, avoiding costly data sims.  
- Uses MCMC sampling of an approximate expected posterior to map an “identifiability landscape” and reveal parameter confounds.  
- Case study 1 (time-interval estimation): standard two-context designs poorly recover priors due to degeneracy between sensory and motor noise.  
- Design recommendation from timing study: independently measuring motor noise greatly improves identifiability; encouraging near-MAP loss may worsen it.  
- Case study 2 (slow-speed visual prior): inference of the slow-speed prior appears theoretically robust, supporting prior empirical findings.  
- Situates work within prior concerns about identifiability in cognitive modeling and tailors a practical solution for Bayesian observer models.  
- Strength — significance/originality: offers a principled, novel method to evaluate and optimize experiments a priori rather than post-hoc.  
- Strength — technical quality: broad parametrization and use of KL divergence make the approach computationally feasible and expressive.  
- Strength — clarity/practical impact: well-written, actionable guidance for experimentalists; potential standard tool for psychophysics.  
- Weakness — dependence on reference model: conclusions are conditional on the chosen θ* and sensitivity to misspecification is not fully explored.  
- Weakness — model class assumptions: identifiability is assessed within a specific parametric class (e.g., Gaussian mixtures), so degeneracies outside this class may be missed.  
- Weakness — scope/generalizability: demonstrated only for 1D continuous estimation; extension to discrete-choice paradigms like 2AFC is non-trivial and unshown.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical methodological issue, presents a novel/usable framework, and validates insights through case studies.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on assumptions about the true observer model and limited task scope reduce generality.  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, significant contribution with limitations that suggest future work rather than fatal flaws; strong recommendation for acceptance.
