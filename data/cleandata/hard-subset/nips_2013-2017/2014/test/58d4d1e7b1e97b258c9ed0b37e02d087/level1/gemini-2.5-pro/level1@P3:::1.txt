This paper addresses the critical and often-understated problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that since different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, the "inverse problem" of recovering an observer's internal representations from data is ill-posed. This degeneracy threatens the validity of many studies that claim to have uncovered such representations. The central contribution is a novel, systematic framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of these models, with the practical goal of improving experimental design *before* data collection.

To achieve this, the authors first construct a highly flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks. This model encompasses stimulus-dependent sensory noise (via a nonlinear mapping), flexible representations of the prior, likelihood, and loss function (using mixtures of Gaussians), and motor noise. They then propose an elegant method to quantify the distinguishability between a hypothesized "true" reference observer model (θ*) and any other model (θ). Instead of relying on expensive data simulations, they show that the expected log-likelihood of a model θ, given data generated by θ*, is directly related to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the two models' response distributions. This allows them to efficiently map out the "identifiability landscape" by sampling from an approximate expected posterior via MCMC, revealing which parameters or combinations of parameters are likely to be confounded in a given experimental design.

The framework's utility is demonstrated through two compelling case studies. First, in a time interval estimation task, they show that a standard experimental design with two stimulus contexts does a poor job of recovering the observer's priors due to a degeneracy between sensory and motor noise parameters. They use the framework to compare alternative designs, yielding the non-obvious conclusion that encouraging a near-MAP loss function could worsen identifiability, while independently measuring motor noise would be highly effective. Second, they re-examine the well-known "slow-speed prior" in visual perception, finding that the inference of this prior is theoretically robust and not prone to major degeneracies, thus providing a new line of support for those original findings.

This work relates to a long history of concern about model identifiability in cognitive psychology (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), but it provides a specific, powerful, and tailored solution for the increasingly popular domain of Bayesian observer modeling, where the problem is particularly acute (Mamassian & Landy, 2013).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental methodological problem at the heart of computational cognitive science. The idea of using a formal framework to evaluate and optimize experimental designs *a priori* is a significant step forward from the common practice of post-hoc model comparison. The approach is novel and provides a principled way to assess what can and cannot be learned from a proposed experiment.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sophisticated and well-executed. The parametrization of the observer model is impressively broad, capturing many key features found in the literature (e.g., Weber-Fechner/Steven's Law, asymmetric losses, non-Gaussian noise). The use of the KL divergence to create an efficient proxy for the expected posterior is a key insight that makes the entire enterprise computationally feasible.

3.  **Clarity and Practical Impact:** The paper is well-written and the motivation is crystal clear. The case studies are excellent demonstrations of the framework's power. The analysis provides concrete, actionable advice for experimentalists (e.g., the high value of independently measuring motor noise in timing tasks). This work has the potential to become a standard tool for researchers designing psychophysical experiments aimed at uncovering internal representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on the Reference Model:** The entire analysis is conditional on the choice of the reference observer, θ*. If this "ground truth" model is misspecified, the conclusions about the relative merits of different experimental designs may not hold. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the sensitivity of their conclusions to this choice. For example, how different does the reference observer have to be before the ranking of experimental designs in the timing study changes?

2.  **Model Class Assumptions:** While the parametric model class is flexible, it is still a specific set of assumptions (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians for all distributions). The identifiability (or lack thereof) is assessed *within* this space. It's possible that degeneracies exist with models outside this class that would not be captured. The paper could be more explicit about this limitation—that it assesses identifiability within a specified, albeit large, hypothesis space.

3.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently demonstrated for 1D continuous estimation tasks. While the authors suggest it can be extended, generalization to other common paradigms, particularly discrete-choice tasks like 2-alternative forced choice (2AFC), is non-trivial. Given that many seminal findings (including the slow-speed prior) were originally based on 2AFC data, demonstrating how the framework would apply there would significantly broaden its impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, foundational problem in a major area of NIPS research.
*   Presents a novel and powerful computational framework with clear practical applications for improving scientific methodology.
*   The technical approach is sound, elegant, and well-demonstrated.
*   The findings from the case studies provide both new insights and validation of prior work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies heavily on assumptions about the form of the "true" observer model, and sensitivity to these assumptions is not fully explored.
*   The scope is currently limited to a specific, though important, class of experimental tasks.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the methodology of computational modeling in perception. It provides researchers with a principled tool to move beyond simply fitting models and to proactively design experiments that can effectively disentangle the components of Bayesian computation in the mind. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations that point toward future work, rather than fatal flaws. The paper is of high quality, clarity, and significance, and is exceptionally well-suited for the NIPS audience. I strongly recommend acceptance.
