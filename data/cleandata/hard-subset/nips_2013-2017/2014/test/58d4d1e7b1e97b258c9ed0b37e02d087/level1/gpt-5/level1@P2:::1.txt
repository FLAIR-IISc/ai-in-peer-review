Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses a central and under-discussed issue in the use of Bayesian observer models for perception: non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data due to degeneracies in the Bayesian decision pipeline. The authors propose a practical, a priori framework to assess identifiability for a broad class of estimation tasks. They build a parametric family of observer models with: (i) a monotonic sensory mapping f(s) and a flexible measurement distribution (two-Gaussian mixture determined by moments), (ii) a prior represented as a Gaussian lattice mixture, (iii) a flexible, maximum-entropy loss (two inverted Gaussians) and lapses, and (iv) additive report (motor) noise. A key analytical contribution is using the expected log-likelihood of an alternative model θ under data generated by the reference model θ* to obtain a tractable criterion equal (up to an additive constant) to negative, trial-weighted sums of KL divergences between the induced response distributions for each stimulus. They define an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(E[log p(D|θ)]) and sample it by MCMC to map identifiability landscapes and compare experimental designs.

Two case studies explore practical implications. In interval timing (following Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.), they show that standard designs poorly recover priors due to confusability between sensory and motor noise; increasing trials in a single condition helps little; enforcing near-MAP loss worsens identifiability; independently measuring motor noise substantially improves recovery. In speed perception (following Stocker & Simoncelli), they find that a slow-speed prior can be robustly recovered, and key noise parameters are fairly identifiable even when internal and measurement noise are decoupled, with some biases (e.g., underestimating lower-bound noise s0 and internal σ̃ at high contrast).

This work connects to prior concerns about degeneracy in Bayesian cognitive models (e.g., Anderson; Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love) and model distinguishability/data informativeness (Navarro, Pitt & Myung). It differs by delivering a domain-tailored, computationally efficient pipeline for observer models that supports a priori design comparison, complementing earlier empirical inversions of priors/likelihoods/loss (e.g., Girshick et al.; Körding & Wolpert; Houlsby et al.).

Quality:
- The derivation that the expected log-likelihood under θ* reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions is standard and sound, and appropriate for measuring distinguishability.
- The observer class is deliberately flexible yet structured to enable analytical computation of expected loss and efficient evaluation of response distributions. This is a thoughtful engineering choice for tractability.
- The identifiability analyses are compelling in both case studies and yield nontrivial, actionable conclusions (e.g., the counterintuitive harm of narrower loss).
- Limitations: conclusions depend on the chosen parameterization (two-Gaussian mixtures, monotonic f(s), Gaussian-lattice priors), and results are not validated with end-to-end simulated-fitting on finite datasets using standard Bayesian inference to confirm that the predicted identifiability translates to practical recovery. The “expected posterior” is a likelihood-only proxy; it omits model priors and finite-sample complexity penalties. MCMC details (mixing diagnostics, runtime) are not described in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly organized. The four-stage decomposition, parameterization, and assumptions are explicitly stated. The rationale for each modeling choice and its scope are discussed.
- The notation is mostly consistent, though the proliferation of parameters could be helped by a summary table. A brief pseudocode for the evaluation/sampling loop and more guidance on choosing M, the Gaussian lattice width/spacing, and Ntr would further aid reproducibility.
- Figures (as described) appear to communicate the key identifiability findings; referencing concrete effect sizes where possible would strengthen the narrative.

Originality:
- While expected log-likelihood/KL as a discriminability measure is known, the paper’s contribution is a domain-specific, computationally efficient framework tailored to Bayesian observer models, plus a systematic use of this metric to rank experimental designs a priori.
- The mixture-based parameterizations of priors and losses and the analytic handling of expected loss are well-integrated; this combination and its application to identifiability seem novel within the psychophysics modeling literature.

Significance:
- The ability to diagnose and mitigate non-identifiability before running experiments is impactful for a large body of work that infers internal representations from behavior. The case studies produce concrete design recommendations (e.g., independent motor noise measurement) and cautionary notes (e.g., about interpreting loss).
- The scope is currently limited to 1D estimation tasks; an extension to discrete-choice paradigms (e.g., 2AFC) and multi-cue scenarios would broaden impact. Nonetheless, even in its current form, this work is likely to influence how observer models are designed and interpreted.

Pros:
- Addresses an important, underappreciated problem with clear practical implications.
- Sound, tractable identifiability metric with an efficient observer model class.
- Non-obvious, actionable insights for experimental design in two well-studied domains.
- Careful discussion of degeneracy sources and constraints (priors, loss, motor noise).

Cons:
- Dependence on specific parametric choices may bias identifiability conclusions; sensitivity analyses (e.g., varying M, alternative loss families) are limited.
- Lacks confirmation that predicted identifiability translates to improved recovery on finite-sample simulated fits or real datasets.
- The “expected posterior” ignores model priors/complexity penalties; guidance on finite-N design optimization is limited.
- 2AFC and more general tasks are deferred to future work, whereas many classic studies use such paradigms.

Suggestions:
- Validate the framework by simulating datasets and performing full inference to show concordance between predicted and realized recoverability.
- Provide sensitivity analyses to the choice of mixture components, lattice resolution M, and alternative f(s) forms.
- Include or release code and explicit MCMC settings/diagnostics.
- Outline an optimization procedure (e.g., selecting stimuli/frequencies) to maximize identifiability under a trial budget.
- Discuss the mapping from 2AFC to estimation more explicitly, or provide a preliminary extension.

Overall recommendation:
A thoughtful and useful contribution that advances methodological rigor in Bayesian modeling of perception by offering a principled, practical approach to identifiability and design. Despite some limitations in validation and scope, the paper meets the NIPS criteria well enough to merit acceptance, particularly for its significance to the perception/modeling community and the clarity of its insights.
