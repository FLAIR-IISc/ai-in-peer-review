This paper addresses the crucial issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a topic increasingly relevant as these models are used to infer internal representations. The authors present a framework for systematically testing identifiability, with the potential to improve experimental design. They demonstrate the framework through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The core idea – formally analyzing the degeneracy inherent in Bayesian inference when applied to perceptual modeling – is timely and important. The authors correctly identify that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral results, and propose a method to assess the sensitivity of inference to this degeneracy. The presented framework, which leverages the structure of a specific class of observer models, is a valuable contribution. The analytical tools developed, particularly the use of KL divergence to quantify identifiability, are well-justified and clearly explained.  The two case studies offer practical demonstrations of the framework’s utility.  The analysis of time interval estimation reveals the importance of motor noise measurement for reliable prior recovery, a potentially important insight for experimental design. The validation on speed perception, corroborating previous findings, lends further credibility to the approach.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Firstly, while the authors state the framework is for a "large class" of psychophysical tasks, the underlying model (Eqs. 2-9) is quite specific. The reliance on Gaussian mixtures, while computationally convenient, may limit the applicability of the framework to more complex perceptual phenomena. The generalization to other tasks is not fully convincing.  The restriction to one-dimensional stimulus magnitudes also reduces the scope.  Secondly, the choice of the reference observer (θ*) is somewhat arbitrary. The sensitivity of the results to different choices of θ* isn't thoroughly explored.  While the authors justify their parameter settings, a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.  Thirdly, the paper largely focuses on *a priori* identifiability. The impact of dataset size (Ntr) is acknowledged, but a more detailed investigation of how identifiability changes with increasing data would be valuable. Specifically, the authors suggest that increasing trials in the single-condition SRT experiment doesn't significantly improve inference; however, the scale of the improvement (or lack thereof) isn’t quantitatively assessed. Fourthly, the analysis in the time interval estimation task reveals a “bad impact” of a narrow loss function. This conclusion seems strong given the model's assumptions, and could benefit from more detailed justification; the authors state this is only within “our model” but don’t fully unpack the implications. Finally, the writing could be improved in places. While generally clear, some sections are dense and require careful reading. For example, the derivation in Equation 12 could be expanded for clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** Addresses a critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.
*   **Novel Framework:** Presents a systematic approach for analyzing identifiability *a priori*.
*   **Analytical Tools:**  Provides a clear and justified use of KL divergence.
*   **Practical Demonstrations:**  Two case studies illustrate the framework's utility.
*   **Potentially impactful results:** The finding regarding the importance of motor noise measurement in time interval estimation is a valuable insight.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The underlying model's assumptions (Gaussian mixtures, 1D stimuli) restrict applicability.
*   **Arbitrary Reference Observer:** The choice of θ* and sensitivity to its parameters are not fully explored.
*   **Focus on A Priori Identifiability:** Limited investigation of the impact of dataset size.
*   **Strong Conclusion:** The claim about narrow loss functions needs further justification.
*   **Writing Clarity:** Some sections could benefit from more detailed explanation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamentally important issue in the field.
*   The proposed framework is novel and provides a valuable tool for researchers using Bayesian models.
*   The case studies demonstrate the potential of the framework to inform experimental design.
*   The mathematical framework is solid and well-justified.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scope of the framework is limited by its underlying assumptions.
*   The sensitivity analysis of the reference observer is incomplete.
*   The results might be specific to the chosen model and not generalize broadly.
*   The writing could be improved for clarity and accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field, despite its limitations. The proposed framework is a valuable tool for assessing identifiability in Bayesian models, and the case studies provide compelling demonstrations of its utility. While the scope is somewhat restricted, the core ideas are generalizable and have the potential to influence how researchers design and interpret experiments using Bayesian modeling.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the reference observer, and a more thorough discussion of the limitations imposed by the model’s assumptions. The authors should also consider expanding on the impact of dataset size on identifiability.
