- Addresses non-identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian observer models and proposes an a priori framework to assess identifiability for estimation tasks.

- Introduces a parametric observer family: monotonic sensory mapping f(s), flexible measurement distribution (two-Gaussian moment-based mixture), Gaussian-lattice mixture priors, flexible max-entropy loss (two inverted Gaussians) with lapses, and additive motor noise.

- Key analytical result: expected log-likelihood under the true model reduces (up to constant) to a trial-weighted sum of KL divergences between induced response distributions, providing a tractable distinguishability criterion.

- Defines an “expected posterior” ∝ exp(E[log p(D|θ)]) and samples it via MCMC to map identifiability landscapes and compare experimental designs.

- Interval-timing case study: standard designs poorly recover priors due to sensory/motor noise confusability; more trials in one condition help little; enforcing near-MAP loss harms identifiability; independent motor-noise measurement substantially improves recovery.

- Speed-perception case study: slow-speed prior is robustly recoverable; key noise parameters are fairly identifiable even with decoupled internal/measurement noise, though lower-bound noise s0 and internal σ̃ can be underestimated at high contrast.

- Situates work among prior concerns about degeneracy and model distinguishability, contributing a domain-tailored, computationally efficient pipeline for observer models.

- Strength: derivation is standard and sound; the observer class balances flexibility and tractability enabling analytic expected-loss computation.

- Strength: identifiability analyses are compelling and yield actionable, nontrivial experimental design insights.

- Limitation: conclusions depend on specific parameterizations (two-Gaussian mixtures, monotonic f(s), Gaussian-lattice priors).

- Limitation: no end-to-end simulated fitting on finite datasets to confirm that predicted identifiability leads to practical parameter recovery.

- Limitation: the “expected posterior” is a likelihood-only proxy that ignores model priors and finite-sample complexity penalties.

- Missing details: MCMC settings, mixing diagnostics, and runtimes are not reported in the main text.

- Clarity: paper is well-organized with explicit assumptions; notation is mostly consistent but could benefit from a parameter summary table and pseudocode for reproducibility.

- Originality: while KL-based discriminability is known, the integrated mixture parameterizations and efficient domain-specific pipeline for identifiability are novel within psychophysics modeling.

- Significance: impactful for pre-experiment diagnosis of non-identifiability and provides concrete design recommendations, though currently limited to 1D estimation tasks.

- Pros summary: tackles an important problem, offers a sound metric and efficient model, delivers actionable insights, and carefully analyzes degeneracy sources.

- Cons summary: potential bias from parametric choices, limited sensitivity analyses, lack of finite-sample validation, omission of priors/complexity in expected posterior, and deferred 2AFC/general task extensions.

- Suggestions: validate with simulated-data full inference, run sensitivity analyses over mixtures/M/f(s), release code and MCMC diagnostics, propose optimization for stimulus selection under trial budgets, and address 2AFC mapping or extensions.

- Overall recommendation: thoughtful, useful contribution that merits acceptance despite validation and scope limitations.
