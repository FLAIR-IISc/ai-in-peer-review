Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses a central open problem in the use of Bayesian observer models in perception: the practical identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) from behavioral data. The authors formalize an a priori identifiability analysis by (i) proposing a flexible but computationally tractable observer class for 1D estimation tasks (stimulus mapped via a parametric f(s) to an internal metric; measurement noise as a two-Gaussian mixture; priors represented as a Gaussian lattice; loss as an inverted Gaussian mixture; lapse; and response noise), and (ii) evaluating, for a given “reference” observer and experimental design, the expected log-evidence for alternative parameter settings, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions. Sampling this “expected posterior” via MCMC yields identifiability maps and design comparisons before running experiments. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (showing strong parameter tradeoffs and poor prior recovery unless motor noise is independently measured; MAP-like loss can worsen recovery) and speed perception (robust recovery of a slow-speed prior exponent and noise characteristics).

The work is well-situated within Bayesian perception (Geisler; Knill & Richards), Bayesian transfer (Maloney & Mamassian), priors in orientation and speed (Girshick et al.; Stocker & Simoncelli), and debates on “Bayesian just-so stories” (Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis). On identifiability/model distinguishability, it relates to Myung, Pitt & Navarro and to model evidence metrics (AIC/BIC/DIC), but adapts these ideas to the BDT observer modeling context with an explicit expected-evidence calculation and a practical sampling scheme. It also connects to recent “cognitive tomography” and parametric observer work at NIPS and elsewhere (Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.).

Quality:
- Methodology: The expected log-likelihood under the true data-generating model reduces to a constant minus Ntr times the stimulus-weighted KL divergence between response distributions. This is correct and provides a principled efficiency gain over brute-force simulation across many datasets. The MCMC sampling of the exponentiated expected log-likelihood is a pragmatic proxy for the identifiability landscape.
- Modeling: The observer class is expressive yet permits fast computation (closed-form mixtures for expected loss). The parameterization (mixtures for likelihood and loss; Gaussian lattice for priors; log-like sensory map) is reasonable and unifies many prior models. The lapse and motor noise stages are standard.
- Case studies: The interval timing example convincingly shows practical non-identifiability between sensory and motor noise (and knock-on effects for prior recovery) and a counter-intuitive sensitivity to the loss width. The speed example corroborates the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference and noise dependence on contrast.
- Caveats: Conclusions are conditional on the chosen parameterization and on the “reference” observer. The identifiability analysis is thus local to a neighborhood of θ*. The paper emphasizes this, but a more systematic sensitivity analysis (e.g., across different mixture orders M, different f(s), or mis-specified loss families) would strengthen claims. Details on MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, and computational costs are missing; without these, reproducibility and practical usability are harder to assess. The conversion of a 2AFC paradigm to an estimation setting is plausible but should be justified more explicitly (e.g., via an explicit mapping or simulation check). The assumption that loss depends only on t̂−t is common but could be violated; showing how violations affect identifiability would be valuable.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a logical progression from model to identifiability metric to case studies. Notation is heavy; a compact symbol table and an early statement of all shared vs. condition-specific parameters would help. Some choices deferred to the Supplement (maximum-entropy mixture parameter fixing; derivation of Eq. 12; computational details) are central and deserve a concise in-text summary. More explicit reporting of posterior credible intervals and effect sizes in the figures would improve interpretability.

Originality:
While the expected-evidence/KL decomposition is known, its application as an a priori identifiability map for Bayesian observer models, coupled with a flexible observer class admitting efficient computation, is novel and of clear methodological value. The design-comparison angle (e.g., showing the benefit of independent motor noise measurement, and the potential harm of narrow loss) provides new, actionable insights.

Significance:
This work directly addresses a pervasive criticism of Bayesian modeling in perception—non-identifiability—by giving researchers a practical tool to diagnose and mitigate it before data collection. The framework should influence experimental design (e.g., adding control conditions to pin down motor noise, choosing stimulus sets) and analysis (explicitly reporting identifiability). The case studies are well-chosen and relevant, with implications for interpreting prior and loss inferences in the literature.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide MCMC details (proposal, number of samples, chains, convergence metrics), runtime, and code availability.
- Sensitivity analyses: mixture order M; alternative f(s); non-Gaussian response noise; varying the prior basis; mis-specified loss family.
- Formalize the invariance/degeneracy group behind Eq. 1 (beyond the φ1φ2φ3=c example) to clarify structural vs. practical non-identifiability.
- Compare to Fisher information/optimal design or mutual-information criteria, and discuss adaptive stimulus selection.
- For the speed case, explicitly validate the 2AFC-to-estimation conversion by recovering published thresholds/psychometric curves.
- Report uncertainty (e.g., credible intervals) on recovered prior moments/exponents and key parameters in the figures.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Tackles an important and timely problem (identifiability of Bayesian observer models) with a principled framework.
- Provides a unified, efficient observer model enabling broad applicability.
- Delivers concrete, non-trivial design recommendations backed by analysis.
- Case studies connect to influential literature and will interest the NIPS audience.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Conclusions are conditional on modeling choices; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Missing computational details and reproducibility information.
- Some assumptions (loss form; 2AFC-to-estimation conversion) need stronger justification or validation.
- Theoretical characterization of structural identifiability is limited; framework is empirical/local around θ*.

Overall evaluation: Strong, useful contribution with clear relevance to NIPS. With added computational detail and a modest sensitivity analysis, this would be an easy accept. As is, I lean to accept.
