- Addresses identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian observer models via an a priori identifiability analysis.
- Proposes a tractable 1D observer class: parametric sensory map f(s), two-Gaussian-mixture measurement noise, Gaussian-lattice priors, inverted-Gaussian-mixture loss, lapse, and response noise.
- Shows expected log-evidence reduces to a stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions, enabling efficient analysis.
- Uses MCMC on the exponentiated expected log-likelihood to produce identifiability maps and compare experimental designs before data collection.
- Case study — interval timing: reveals strong tradeoffs, poor prior recovery without independent motor-noise measurement, and MAP-like loss can worsen recovery.
- Case study — speed perception: shows robust recovery of a slow-speed prior exponent and contrast-dependent noise characteristics.
- Well-situated in Bayesian perception and model-identifiability literature; applies known KL decomposition in a novel diagnostic way for observer models.
- Methodologically sound: analytic reduction gives efficiency over brute-force simulation; MCMC proxy is pragmatic for the identifiability landscape.
- Modeling choice is expressive yet computationally efficient and unifies many prior observer models.
- Main caveat: conclusions are conditional on the chosen parameterization and the reference observer—analysis is local to a neighborhood of θ*.
- Lacks systematic sensitivity analyses (mixture order M, alternative f(s), mis-specified loss families) to assess generality.
- Missing important reproducibility details: MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, runtimes, and code availability.
- Conversion of 2AFC paradigms to estimation should be justified or validated explicitly.
- The assumption that loss depends only on t̂−t could be violated; effects of such violations on identifiability are not shown.
- Clarity is generally good but notation is heavy; recommend a symbol table and concise in-text summaries of supplemental derivations and choices.
- Figures should report posterior credible intervals and effect sizes for interpretability.
- Originality: novel, practical tool for pre-experiment identifiability diagnosis and design comparison.
- Significance: addresses a central critique of Bayesian perceptual modeling and offers actionable experimental-design guidance.
- Suggested improvements: provide MCMC/code details, run sensitivity analyses, formalize structural invariances/degeneracies, compare to Fisher-information/MI criteria, discuss adaptive stimulus selection, and validate 2AFC-to-estimation mapping.
- Recommendation: strong contribution with relevance to NIPS; lean accept but requires added computational detail and modest sensitivity analyses.
