- Presents a novel computational framework to test a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science.  
- Identifies the inverse problem: priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can trade off to produce identical behavioral predictions.  
- Develops a flexible, computationally efficient parametric observer model for 1D continuous estimation tasks (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians).  
- Proposes using expected log-likelihood (proportional to negative KL divergence between response distributions) to quantify model similarity.  
- Maps the "identifiability landscape" by sampling an approximate expected posterior to reveal well-constrained versus degenerate parameters.  
- Case study 1 (time interval estimation): combining multiple stimulus conditions or measuring motor noise improves prior recovery more than increasing trial count.  
- Case study 1: encouraging a narrower, MAP-like loss function can counterintuitively worsen identifiability by trading off with other parameters.  
- Case study 2 (slow-speed prior): inference of prior and noise characteristics is theoretically robust, supporting prior literature.  
- Strength: Directly addresses a fundamental and often-overlooked non-identifiability problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling.  
- Strength: Provides a principled, a priori experimental-design tool that can save resources and improve inference quality.  
- Strength: The parametric model balances flexibility and tractability, capturing Weber-Fechner/Stevens laws and non-Gaussian/asymmetric components.  
- Strength: Case studies are realistic and yield actionable, non-trivial insights for experimental design and theory validation.  
- Weakness: Analysis is conditional on a chosen reference observer (θ*); results may change for different θ* without a reported sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Framework is limited to one-dimensional continuous estimation; many psychophysics tasks (e.g., 2AFC) are not directly covered.  
- Weakness: High model complexity (~25 parameters) raises concerns about MCMC feasibility, stability, and additional degeneracies from model dimensionality.  
- Weakness: Theoretical idealization assumes the true observer lies within the model class and does not address model mismatch with real human behavior.  
- Quality/clarity/originality/significance: Paper is technically strong, clearly written, highly original, and potentially of high impact for experimental design in perception.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical problem, offers a practical and novel framework, demonstrates utility with insightful case studies, and promises substantial impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on the specific reference observer and limited applicability to 1D estimation tasks are notable limitations.
