This paper addresses the important and often overlooked issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors correctly point out that multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can lead to the same observed behavior, hindering the reliable recovery of internal representations. They propose a framework for systematically testing identifiability and demonstrate its application to time interval estimation and speed perception tasks.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly explains the problem of degeneracy in Bayesian models. It builds upon established work in Bayesian decision theory and psychophysics (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]), and appropriately cites relevant literature. The proposed framework, which leverages the structure of observer models to explore identifiability, is a novel and valuable contribution. The two case studies are well-chosen to illustrate the application of the framework and provide practical insights. The extension of previous work [12, 14] to a more general class of models is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Identifiability is a critical issue in Bayesian modeling, and this paper brings it to the forefront.
*   **Novel framework:** The proposed framework offers a systematic way to assess identifiability *a priori*, which is a significant advance.
*   **Clear explanation:** The paper is well-written and explains complex concepts in an accessible manner.
*   **Practical applications:** The case studies demonstrate how the framework can be used to improve experimental design and interpret results.
*   **Solid theoretical foundation:** The work builds upon established principles of Bayesian decision theory and statistical inference.
*   **Comprehensive Model:** The generalized Bayesian observer model seems quite versatile and allows for a lot of flexibility in parameterization, necessary for exploring the model space.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational complexity:** Exploring the full parameter space, even with the proposed framework, is computationally demanding, potentially limiting its applicability to more complex models. While the authors make attempts to make the computation efficient, it remains a concern.
*   **Model assumptions:** The framework relies on several assumptions about the observer model (e.g., specific functional forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored. The choice of a Gaussian mixture for likelihoods and loss functions, while practical, could be limiting.
*   **Simplified Tasks:** The tasks chosen, while illustrative, are relatively simple. It's unclear how well the framework would scale to more ecologically valid or complex perceptual tasks.
*   **Interpretation of KL divergence:** While the use of KL divergence for quantifying support is reasonable, the interpretation of its magnitude is not always straightforward. A small KL divergence doesn't necessarily imply strong identifiability, but merely similarity of predictions.
*   **Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity:** In the time interval estimation case study, the authors identify motor noise as a source of degeneracy. However, a more detailed sensitivity analysis of all parameters could be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   The proposed framework is novel, well-motivated, and potentially impactful.
*   The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the framework may limit its broader applicability.
*   The results are sensitive to model assumptions, which are not fully investigated.
*   The framework's scalability to more complex perceptual tasks is uncertain.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them significantly. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic framework for addressing the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The framework is novel, well-motivated, and has the potential to improve the design and interpretation of psychophysical experiments. Addressing the identified weaknesses—particularly exploring the sensitivity to model assumptions and evaluating scalability—could further strengthen the work. The paper clearly meets the NIPS reviewing criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The work is technically sound, clearly written, novel, and has the potential to influence future research in Bayesian modeling of perception.
