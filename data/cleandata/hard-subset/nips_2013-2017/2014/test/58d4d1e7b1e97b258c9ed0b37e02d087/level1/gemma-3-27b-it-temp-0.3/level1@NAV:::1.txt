## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the inherent non-identifiability of these models, meaning multiple parameter combinations can produce the same observed behavior. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing the identifiability of a class of Bayesian observer models, with the ultimate goal of improving experimental design. The approach centers around quantifying the expected support for different parameter settings given a true model, using KL divergence as a metric. They demonstrate the framework’s utility through two case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception – highlighting how different experimental setups impact the ability to recover underlying representations.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in Bayesian modeling of perception (Knill & Richards, 2003; Körding & Wolpert, 2004), which has successfully explained a wide range of behavioral data. However, it directly tackles a long-standing concern regarding the validity of inferences drawn from these models (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012), specifically the potential for degenerate solutions. The proposed framework offers a valuable tool for addressing this issue, moving beyond simply acknowledging the problem to providing a method for *a priori* analysis. The extension to a continuous class of observer models (Section 2) is a significant contribution, allowing for a more flexible and comprehensive analysis.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound and presents a well-defined framework. The use of KL divergence provides a principled way to quantify identifiability. The case studies are relevant and demonstrate the practical application of the framework, yielding non-trivial insights into experimental design (e.g., the detrimental effect of a narrow loss function in time estimation). The clarity of the mathematical derivations and the detailed explanation of the model components are commendable.

**Weaknesses:** While the framework is theoretically appealing, its computational cost could be a limitation for more complex models. The assumption of Gaussian distributions throughout (likelihoods, priors, noise) might be restrictive, and the impact of deviating from this assumption isn’t fully explored. The analysis in the speed perception case study seems to suggest a relatively robust identifiability, which might be overly optimistic given known challenges in inferring priors (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006). The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the framework and potential avenues for future work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for assessing identifiability.
*   Demonstrates practical utility through insightful case studies.
*   Clear and well-written with detailed mathematical derivations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost could limit applicability to complex models.
*   Reliance on Gaussian assumptions may be restrictive.
*   Potential for overoptimistic results in certain scenarios (e.g., speed perception).



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a practical tool for addressing the crucial issue of model identifiability. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
