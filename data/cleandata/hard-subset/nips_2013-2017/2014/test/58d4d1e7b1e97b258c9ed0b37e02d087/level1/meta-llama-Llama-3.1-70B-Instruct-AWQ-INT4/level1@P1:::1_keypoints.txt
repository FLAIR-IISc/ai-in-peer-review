- Proposes a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability of a broad class of Bayesian observer models of perception.  
- Introduces a continuous class of Bayesian observer models parametrized by θ for one-dimensional stimulus estimation, composed of sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages.  
- Uses a maximum-entropy approach to determine measurement distribution shape and loss function, allowing many sensory models including stimulus-dependent noise and robust mixtures.  
- Defines model support as log likelihood given data, computes expected support via expected log likelihood, and quantifies average support using KL divergence between true and candidate response densities.  
- Samples from the approximate posterior with MCMC to assess identifiability across parameter space.  
- Applies the framework to two case studies: prior inference in interval timing and reconstruction of prior/noise in speed perception.  
- Finds interval-timing prior reconstruction requires strong experimental constraints and validations.  
- Finds speed-perception inference is generally robust and not prone to major degeneracies.  
- Strength: provides a clear, detailed, and accessible framework addressing a crucial identifiability problem in psychophysics.  
- Strength: offers a flexible continuous model class applicable to many sensorimotor estimation tasks.  
- Strength: demonstrates practical utility through two case studies that can inform experimental design.  
- Weakness: assumes the true model θ* is known, which may not hold in real experiments.  
- Weakness: relies on a maximum-entropy assumption for measurement and loss that may be unrealistic in some settings.  
- Weakness: lacks a clear discussion of the framework’s limitations and potential biases.  
- Weakness: does not provide a detailed comparison with existing identifiability assessment methods in psychophysics.  
- Recommendation: add explicit discussion of limitations and potential biases of the framework.  
- Recommendation: include a detailed comparison with existing identifiability methods.  
- Recommendation: apply the framework to additional case studies to demonstrate robustness.  
- Recommendation: clarify how the framework can be used when the true model θ* is unknown in real-world experiments.
