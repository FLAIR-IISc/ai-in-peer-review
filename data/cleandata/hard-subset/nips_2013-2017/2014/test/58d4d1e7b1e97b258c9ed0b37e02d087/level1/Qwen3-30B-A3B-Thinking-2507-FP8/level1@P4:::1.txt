

# Review: A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

This paper addresses a critical methodological issue in Bayesian models of perception: the identifiability problem. The authors recognize that the inverse mapping from observed behavior to elements of the decision process (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is inherently degenerate, as multiple combinations of these elements can yield identical behavioral outputs. Previous work has proposed various ad hoc approaches to suppress this degeneracy (e.g., [12, 19]), but the authors correctly identify the absence of a systematic framework for evaluating identifiability a priori before conducting experiments.

The authors propose a novel framework for systematic identifiability testing that leverages the common structure shared by many Bayesian models of sensory estimation. They introduce a flexible observer model class that encompasses a wide range of psychophysical tasks involving one-dimensional stimulus estimation. This model includes four stages: sensation, perception, decision-making, and response. The key contribution is their method for quantifying identifiability through the expected log likelihood of parameter configurations, which they derive analytically using KL divergence. This approach enables a priori evaluation of experimental designs for their ability to resolve parameter degeneracies.

The authors apply their framework to two well-established case studies in perception. In the time interval estimation task, they demonstrate how different experimental designs (e.g., single vs. dual conditions, assumptions about loss function) affect the ability to recover priors. Their analysis reveals non-intuitive findings, such as the detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function on prior recovery. In the speed perception task, they show that the reconstruction of slow-speed priors is relatively robust across experimental designs, providing theoretical validation for previous empirical findings.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a mathematically rigorous framework that builds upon well-established concepts (KL divergence, Bayesian decision theory). The derivation of the expected log likelihood (Eq. 12) is clear and well-supported. The case studies provide concrete empirical validation of the framework's utility.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The framework directly addresses a significant methodological gap in the field. It provides researchers with a tool to systematically evaluate experimental designs before collecting data, potentially saving time and resources. The case studies demonstrate how the framework can yield non-intuitive insights about experimental design (e.g., the negative impact of narrow loss functions in interval timing).

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is systematically introduced, and the framework for identifiability testing is explained with sufficient mathematical detail. The case studies are clearly presented with appropriate figures (Figures 2-3) that effectively illustrate the findings.

4. **Novelty**: While the problem of model degeneracy has been noted previously [17, 18, 19], the authors present the first systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing in Bayesian models of perception. Their approach is distinct from generic model distinguishability methods [23] because it specifically targets the degeneracy in Bayesian observer models.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Scope Limitations**: The framework is designed for a specific class of Bayesian models (one-dimensional stimulus estimation with certain assumptions about noise and loss functions). While the authors acknowledge this limitation and note potential extensions, the paper could more explicitly discuss the boundaries of applicability.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions that their approach is "efficient" but does not provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity. For larger models or more complex experimental designs, the MCMC sampling (Eq. 13) might become computationally intensive, which could limit practical adoption.

3. **Assumptions in Case Studies**: The case studies rely on specific assumptions about the reference observer (θ*). While the authors acknowledge this, they could more explicitly discuss how robust their conclusions are to variations in these assumptions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper addresses a fundamental and previously unaddressed methodological challenge in Bayesian modeling of perception.
- The framework is technically sound, well-justified, and directly applicable to improving experimental design in psychophysics.
- The case studies provide compelling evidence of the framework's utility, revealing non-intuitive insights about experimental design.
- The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience.
- The work advances the field by providing a systematic tool for addressing a known limitation in current modeling practices.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The framework is limited to a specific class of Bayesian models, which may restrict its applicability to some perceptual tasks.
- The computational complexity of the method is not thoroughly evaluated, which could be a barrier to adoption.
- The case studies are limited to two specific tasks; while illustrative, they don't demonstrate the framework's utility across a broader range of perceptual phenomena.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a significant contribution to the methodological toolkit for researchers using Bayesian models in perception. The framework is novel, technically sound, and addresses a critical gap in the field. The authors provide clear theoretical foundations, rigorous mathematical derivations, and compelling case studies that demonstrate the framework's practical utility. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a strong case for the importance of systematic identifiability testing in Bayesian modeling of perception.

The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on rigorous, methodologically sound contributions to machine learning and computational neuroscience. The framework has the potential to improve experimental design across a wide range of perceptual tasks, making it a valuable addition to the literature. While the scope is somewhat limited, the authors provide clear pathways for extension, and the core contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance. The paper demonstrates the kind of methodological rigor that advances the field beyond simply applying existing techniques to new data.
