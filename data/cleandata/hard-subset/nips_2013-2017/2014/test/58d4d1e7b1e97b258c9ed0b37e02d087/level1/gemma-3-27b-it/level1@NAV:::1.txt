## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper tackles the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing concern as these models are increasingly used to infer internal representations. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy in the Bayesian framework – multiple parameter combinations can yield the same observed behavior – and propose a novel framework for systematically testing this identifiability. The core idea is to explore the parameter space by quantifying the expected support for alternative models given data generated by a reference model, using KL-divergence as a key metric. They demonstrate the utility of their framework through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The paper builds upon established work in Bayesian modeling of perception (Knill & Richards, 2003; Körding & Wolpert, 2004) and recognizes the limitations of simply inverting models to recover internal representations (Jones & Love, 2011).  It addresses a gap in the field by moving beyond intuitive arguments about identifiability and providing a computational approach. The extension of previous work (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2014) to a more general class of observer models is a significant step.

**Strengths:** The framework presented is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The authors provide a clear explanation of the identifiability problem and their proposed solution. The use of KL-divergence offers a principled way to quantify model similarity. The case studies are relevant and demonstrate the practical application of the framework, revealing insights into experimental design. Specifically, the findings regarding the detrimental effect of narrow loss functions in the time estimation task are notable. The code availability, while not explicitly mentioned, would be a great addition to enhance reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's complexity and the number of equations could present a barrier to entry for readers unfamiliar with Bayesian modeling. While the theoretical justification is strong, the link between the calculated identifiability metrics and concrete guidance for experimenters could be strengthened. The assumption of a specific parametric form for the prior and likelihood (Gaussian mixtures) limits the generality of the framework.  The speed perception case study primarily *validates* existing findings rather than uncovering new issues, suggesting the framework may be more powerful for exploratory analyses.  Furthermore, the analysis focuses heavily on parameter recovery, neglecting the potential for identifiability issues in the *structure* of the model itself.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial and timely problem in the field.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound framework.
*   Provides practical insights into experimental design.
*   Demonstrates the framework's utility through relevant case studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Complexity may limit accessibility.
*   Relatively limited exploration of the framework’s full potential.
*   The parametric assumptions restrict generality.




**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by providing a formal framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian models. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above—specifically clarifying the link between identifiability metrics and experimental design, and acknowledging the limitations of the parametric assumptions—would further enhance the paper's impact.
