This manuscript addresses the important and challenging problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The paper is well-written and the arguments are presented clearly. However, the core contribution rests on a series of strong assumptions that limit the generality of the findings. While the methodology is interesting and innovative, the key results do not feel impactful enough to fully justify the extensive theoretical framework required to produce them. Despite these reservations, the work's novelty and its focus on a significant problem make it a valuable contribution, and I recommend its acceptance for presentation.

**Major Comments**

1.  The finding in Section 4.1, regarding the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise, is not particularly surprising, as these components often serve similar functional roles in such models. To strengthen this section, the authors should clarify which of the condition differences are statistically significant. The current box plots make it difficult to assess the reliability of these effects.

2.  The analysis in Section 4.2 would be more convincing if it included a crucial control condition. To demonstrate the model's specificity, the authors should show that it can also recover the *absence* of a slow-speed prior from data generated by an observer who does not possess one. This would confirm that the model is not simply biased towards finding such a prior.

**Specific Comments and Points for Clarification**

*   **line 106:** Could the authors clarify the choice to model the stimulus with a discrete distribution, given that it is a continuous variable? While this likely reflects the discrete stimulus levels used in typical experimental designs, an explicit statement would be helpful.

*   **line 134:** The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, appears to be a critical component for the model's success. This raises the question of whether the identifiability demonstrated is a general property of Bayesian models or, more narrowly, a consequence of imposing this specific and powerful constraint on the representational space. The paper could be interpreted as showing that Bayesian models are identifiable *given* strong, pre-existing assumptions about internal representations.

*   **line 143:** Regarding Equation 3, do the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee a unimodal distribution for the fit? It seems plausible that a bimodal distribution could, in principle, satisfy these constraints.

*   **line 158:** What is the justification for the observer's prior favoring a lattice representation over, for instance, a uniform one? Furthermore, what information allows the observer to form a prior that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range? The rationale behind these specific modeling choices is unclear.

*   **Figure 1:** This figure is difficult to interpret. Its clarity could be substantially improved by integrating panel (b) into panel (a) and removing the derived inference, p_est(s^*|x), to focus the reader on the model's core components.

*   **line 252:** The derivation of Equation 15 relies on several approximations that warrant further justification. Specifically, under what conditions on N are Stirling's approximation and the approximation E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x] considered valid in this context? By the time the prior on θ is abandoned and these approximations are made, the justification for Equation 15 seems less grounded in rigorous derivation and more in its ability to produce an intuitively appealing rule for assessing predictive similarity.

*   **line 262:** The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous. Please clarify which density is being referred to (e.g., the distribution over the dataset?).

*   **line 365:** The claim that the learning of priors does not matter in a 2AFC task is counterintuitive and requires a more detailed explanation.
