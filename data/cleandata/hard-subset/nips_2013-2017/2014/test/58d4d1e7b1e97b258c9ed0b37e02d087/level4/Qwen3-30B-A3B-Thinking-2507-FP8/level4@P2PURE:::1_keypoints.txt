- Proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data: summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory, identifies non-identifiability, outlines constraints to make terms identifiable, imposes assumptions, examines parameter differentiability, and applies to time-interval estimation and motion-perception studies.

- Notes that identifiability of internal representations and processes is a longstanding problem in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978).

- Points out that identifiability concerns have hindered broader adoption of Bayesian models in recent critiques (Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012).

- Major concern 1: Overlooks substantial prior model-comparison literature (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang 2002; Navarro et al. 2004), making it difficult to assess the paper’s novelty and contribution.

- Major concern 2: Authors make assumptions that appear arbitrary (e.g., form of p_meas and parameter definitions) and do not sufficiently justify how dependent results are on those assumptions.

- Recommendation: The manuscript should explicitly discuss and justify the constraints used to achieve identifiability in Bayesian models.

- Minor issues: explain the “trivial mathematical fact” on line 53, change the second “nor” to “or” on line 73, and clarify Figure 2 caption about which line corresponds to the true value.

- Overall assessment: Well written on an important topic, but lacking essential connections to prior work in model comparison.
