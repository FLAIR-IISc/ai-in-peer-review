This paper addresses the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The manuscript is well-written and clear. However, the work is premised on a long list of assumptions that the reader must accept. The number of assumptions seems substantial relative to the impact of the key results presented. Nevertheless, because the methodology is interesting and innovative, I judge the work to be above the acceptance threshold for NIPS.

Regarding the key results: The finding in Section 4.1, concerning the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise, is not particularly surprising; I was already questioning the need to incorporate both while reading the model description. It would be helpful if the authors indicated which differences between conditions are statistically significant, as this is not immediately apparent from the box plots. In Section 4.2, it seems essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior. A critical control would be to show that if the reference observer did not have such a prior, the model could successfully recover this fact.

**Specific comments:**

*   **Line 106:** Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is, in fact, a continuous variable? I assume this is due to typical experimental designs where discrete stimulus levels are used as independent variables, but the authors should clarify this choice.

*   **Line 134:** As the authors note, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. The success of the proposed method may be due in large part to the incorporation of this transform. In other words, the transform imposes a strong constraint on inference. Consequently, the work's central claim could be interpreted as: Bayesian models are identifiable provided one accepts strong, pre-specified assumptions about mental representations.

*   **Line 143:** Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that the resulting distribution in Eq. 3 is unimodal? It seems plausible that this procedure could, in principle, yield a bimodal distribution.

*   **Line 158:** What is the justification for the observer's prior favoring a lattice representation over a uniform one? Furthermore, what knowledge does the observer possess about the true stimulus distribution that leads them to adopt a prior that is 50% wider than the actual stimulus range?

*   **Figure 1:** This figure is difficult to interpret and, for me, was more confusing than illuminating. The clarity could be improved by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing the plot of p_est(s^*|x), as this is a derived quantity rather than a core component of the model.

*   **Line 252:** The authors should comment on the conditions on N under which the approximation in Equation 14 is valid. Specifically, what are the requirements for Stirling's approximation to hold, and under what circumstances is the approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] justified? After a series of strong approximations and the decision to abandon priors on θ, the justification for Equation 15 appears weak. It seems to be motivated primarily by its intuitive appeal for assessing the similarity of predictions, rather than by a rigorous derivation.

*   **Line 262:** The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous. Please specify which density is being referred to—is it the distribution over datasets?

*   **Line 365:** Could the authors elaborate on why the learning of priors is considered irrelevant in the 2AFC task?
