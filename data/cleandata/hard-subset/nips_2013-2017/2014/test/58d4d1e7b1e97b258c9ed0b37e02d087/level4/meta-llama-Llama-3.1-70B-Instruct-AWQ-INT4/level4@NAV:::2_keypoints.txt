- Addresses an important problem (model identifiability) in cognitive modeling with clear, engaging writing.  
- Methodology is innovative and suitable for a high-tier conference (NIPS), but rests on many strong assumptions that may overwhelm readers.  
- Section 4.1: finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising; statistical significance of condition differences should be reported and boxplots may be a poor choice.  
- Section 4.2: must show the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer to strengthen validity.  
- Clarify why a discrete stimulus distribution was chosen despite the stimulus being continuous (Line 106).  
- The log-stimulus transform (Eq.2) is crucial to the results, implying identifiability may depend on strong assumptions about internal representational transforms (Line 134).  
- Constraints in Eq.4 and maximizing differential entropy do not guarantee a unimodal fit; bimodality is possible and should be addressed (Line 143).  
- Justify the assumption that the observer’s prior favors the lattice representation and explain how the observer could have a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range (Line 158).  
- Figure 1 is confusing; consider integrating panels and removing derived inference p_est(s^*|x) to improve clarity.  
- Provide conditions and validity limits for Equation 14, including Stirling’s approximation and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] holds (Line 252).  
- Equation 15’s justification is weakened by earlier approximations and dropping priors on θ; clarify its validity beyond intuitive appeal.  
- Clarify which “unnormalized density” is meant when referring to sampling — is it the dataset distribution? (Line 262).  
- The claim that learning priors does not matter in 2AFC is not fully convincing and needs stronger evidence (Line 365).  
- Overall: tackles a significant issue with interesting results, but conclusions are not overwhelmingly convincing given the assumptions and gaps.
