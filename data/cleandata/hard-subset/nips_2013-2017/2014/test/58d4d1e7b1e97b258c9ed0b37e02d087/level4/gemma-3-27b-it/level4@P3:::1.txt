This article proposes a novel framework for assessing the identifiability of Bayesian models applied to perceptual inference. The authors correctly identify a core issue with the Bayesian approach – the degeneracy where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior – and aim to provide tools for systematically analyzing this problem and improving experimental design. They introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model and demonstrate its application to time interval estimation and speed perception tasks, showcasing how to evaluate the effectiveness of different experimental setups.

The use of Bayesian models to explain human perception has become increasingly prevalent (Knill & Richards, 1996; Pouget et al., 2013), with successes in explaining diverse behavioral data and suggesting the existence of internally represented probabilistic models (Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Vilares et al., 2014). However, the potential for non-identifiability (Anderson, 1978; Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) represents a significant challenge to interpreting these models as reflections of underlying cognitive processes. This work directly addresses this critical issue.

The paper is well-written and the proposed framework is conceptually sound. The authors clearly articulate the problem of non-identifiability and offer a practical approach for investigating it. The application to both time interval estimation and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used. However, the paper's impact is diminished by a limited engagement with the existing literature on model comparison and a reliance on potentially strong, and insufficiently justified, modeling assumptions. 

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is a fundamental concern for Bayesian modeling in cognitive science and the authors tackle it head-on.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed method for systematically exploring identifiability is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is well-organized and the concepts are explained clearly, making it accessible to a broad audience.
* **Practical Applications:** The case studies demonstrate the potential of the framework for improving experimental design and interpreting results.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical framework appears rigorous and the derivations seem logically consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Literature Review:** The paper overlooks a substantial body of work on model comparison and selection within cognitive science (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004; Myung et al., 2000). This omission makes it difficult to assess the novelty and incremental contribution of the proposed approach. Placing the work within this broader context would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **Arbitrary Assumptions:** Several modeling choices, such as the specific functional forms for the likelihood (Equation 3) and loss function (Equation 6), appear somewhat arbitrary. While the authors acknowledge the flexibility of these choices, the justification for these particular forms is lacking. A more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is needed. The assumption of shared parameters across conditions, while common, also warrants further justification.
* **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the robustness of its conclusions to variations in the reference observer (θ*). Investigating how the identifiability analysis changes with different plausible parameter values for the reference observer would increase confidence in the findings.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention computational efficiency, a more detailed discussion of the computational demands of the method, and its scalability to more complex models, would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of cognition.
* The proposed framework provides a novel and practical approach for investigating identifiability.
* The case studies demonstrate the potential of the framework for informing experimental design.
* The paper is technically sound and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited engagement with the existing literature on model comparison weakens the paper's impact.
* The reliance on potentially arbitrary modeling assumptions raises concerns about the robustness of the findings.
* The lack of a thorough sensitivity analysis limits the generalizability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a valuable contribution that addresses an important issue in the field. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive review of the relevant literature and a more thorough justification of the modeling assumptions. As it stands, it is a solid, but not groundbreaking, work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised above regarding the literature review, modeling assumptions, and sensitivity analysis.
