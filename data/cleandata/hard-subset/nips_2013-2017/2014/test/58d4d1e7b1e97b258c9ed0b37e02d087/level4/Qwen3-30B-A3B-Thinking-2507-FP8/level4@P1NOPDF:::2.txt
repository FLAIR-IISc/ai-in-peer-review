

The manuscript addresses a significant challenge in cognitive modeling: model identifiability. It is well-written and clear, yet relies on numerous assumptions whose necessity and interplay are not sufficiently justified. Given the methodology's innovation, I judge the work as above NIPS' threshold for presentation, though the assumption burden somewhat outweighs the impact of the core results.

Regarding key findings:  
Section 4.1 presents the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise as unsurprising. I questioned including both in the model while reading; the authors should clarify which observed condition differences were statistically reliable (I may have misinterpreted the box plots).  
Section 4.2 requires justification: if the reference observer lacked a slow-speed prior, could the model recover this absence? This is essential for validating the approach.

Specific comments:  
Line 106: The stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, yet it is a continuous variable. This likely stems from experimental design conventions (where stimulus level is an independent variable), but the authors should clarify the rationale.  
Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, appears critical to the method's success. The transform imposes strong constraints on inference, suggesting the paper’s core claim—that Bayesian models are identifiable—depends heavily on specific assumptions about representational transformations.  
Line 143: Do the constraints in Equation 4 and differential entropy maximization guarantee Equation 3 yields a unimodal distribution? The fit could theoretically produce bimodality.  
Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over uniform? What knowledge justifies a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range?  
Figure 1: Despite careful examination, I remain unclear about Figure 1b’s purpose and find Figure 1a more informative without the derived p_est(s^*|x). Integrating Figure 1b into 1a and omitting p_est(s^*|x) would improve clarity.  
Line 252: The authors should specify conditions on N for Equation 14’s validity, particularly regarding Stirling’s approximation and the approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]. By Equation 15, the abandonment of priors and questionable approximations render the derivation’s justification largely dependent on yielding an intuitive rule of thumb for prediction similarity.  
Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density”—which density is referenced? The dataset distribution?  
Line 365: The claim that prior learning does not matter in 2AFC is innovative and addresses an important problem, though the results do not feel fully compelling.  

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to identifiability in cognitive models through its methodological creativity. However, the reliance on strong, potentially restrictive assumptions requires stronger justification relative to the presented results. The work would benefit from deeper engagement with the necessity of these assumptions and clearer articulation of their implications for model interpretability.
