

The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing mathematical formulations for sensory noise, likelihood functions, prior probability distributions, loss functions, and response distributions. A significant strength of this model is its dual emphasis on robustness and computational tractability: it accommodates diverse scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while maintaining analytical convenience (e.g., tractable inference). The authors appropriately highlight the model’s mathematical identifiability, enabling straightforward distinction between parameter estimates.  

I find the paper compelling and would support its acceptance at NIPS. The proposed framework offers a mathematically elegant formalization of perceptual estimation applicable across numerous experimental paradigms. However, I have reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. The model aligns closely with recent Bayesian approaches in the literature—particularly those in *PLoS Computational Biology*—suggesting an incremental rather than transformative advance. For instance:  
- Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 8(11), e1002771.  
- Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 10(6), e1003661.  

While the current submission differs from these works, the conceptual and methodological overlap is substantial. I recommend that the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception, explicitly articulating how their model constitutes a significant advance over existing approaches.  

Additionally, the emphasis on mathematical identifiability requires clarification. The model’s identifiability stems solely from its mathematical assumptions (e.g., fixed noise structures), not from experimental design. Crucially, it cannot resolve whether perceptual errors arise from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise—addressing the core identifiability challenge faced by experimentalists. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation in the revision.  

In summary, the paper provides a mathematically elegant framework for perceptual estimation, but its novel contribution is incremental relative to established literature. Addressing these points will substantially strengthen the submission.
