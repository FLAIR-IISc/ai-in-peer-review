- Proposes a general framework for a priori identifiability assessment in Bayesian observer models using a flexible 1D estimation model spanning sensation, perception, decision, and response stages.  
- Model components: monotonic sensory mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians priors/likelihoods, max-entropy noise/loss parameterizations, BDT decision rule with lapses, and additive motor/report noise.  
- Defines an expected log-likelihood criterion based on KL divergences between predicted response distributions and estimates an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) via MCMC to map identifiability under designs.  
- Two case studies: interval timing (prior recovery hard unless motor noise is constrained) and speed perception with slow-speed prior (robust recovery of prior and noise).  
- Positions framework as a tool to rank experimental designs and inform choices like feedback, number of conditions, and external measurements.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian observer and parameter-recovery work (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Körding & Wolpert; Acerbi et al.) and connects to model distinguishability literature.  
- Main novelty: unified, computationally efficient parameterization plus KL-based expected support for design ranking.  
- Strength: well-specified, computationally tractable model class supporting stimulus-dependent noise, flexible priors/losses, lapses, and motor noise.  
- Strength: KL-based expected log-likelihood is a principled, scalable surrogate for simulation-heavy model comparison.  
- Strength: Case studies effectively illustrate both failure modes and successful recovery, yielding practical guidance.  
- Weakness: Identifiability assessed only within the assumed parametric model, relying on specific assumptions (mixture order, unimodality, f(s) family, near-quadratic loss).  
- Weakness: The approximate expected posterior is not a true posterior; sensitivity to this approximation, mixture order, and misspecification is underexplored.  
- Weakness: Conversion of 2AFC tasks to estimation models is only briefly justified and needs further validation.  
- Weakness: Limited empirical corroboration beyond simulations.  
- Clarity suggestions: add a dedicated related-work section and clearly state what is new versus adapted.  
- Clarity suggestions: clarify distinction between structural identifiability (within model class) and practical identifiability across plausible model families.  
- Clarity suggestions: summarize computational complexity, provide implementation details or code, and report sensitivity analyses (mixture components M, non-unimodal deviations, alternative f(s), loss misspecification).  
- Originality: useful but incremental—many building blocks exist; the practical KL-based design-ranking procedure is the main added value.  
- Significance: can help anticipate parameter confounds, choose informative designs, and avoid overconfident interpretations; likely to be adopted by practitioners.  
- Major concern: scope of “identifiability” must be clarified and guidance provided on disentangling sensory, decision, and response noise (e.g., motor-noise calibration, multiple sensory-noise conditions).  
- Major concern: include robustness/misspecification analyses for mixture order, loss family, and f(s), and evaluate effects of skewed or multimodal likelihoods.  
- Pros summary: flexible, efficient model; principled identifiability metric; insightful case studies; practical experimental guidance.  
- Cons summary: incremental contribution; model-dependent identifiability with persistent confounds without external constraints; limited sensitivity analyses and empirical tests; 2AFC mapping needs stronger justification.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance contingent on revisions to contextualize novelty, sharpen identifiability scope with design guidelines, and add robustness analyses and implementation details.
