The manuscript addresses the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. It proposes a general observer-model class, an a priori measure of model distinguishability via expected log-likelihood/KL divergence, and an MCMC-based exploration of identifiability under different experimental designs. The approach is interesting and innovative, and the paper is clearly written overall. However, the framework rests on a long list of assumptions, and the key empirical takeaways feel modest relative to the complexity of the setup. Still, I judge the contribution to be above the acceptance threshold for NIPS.

Comments on key results:
- Section 4.1: The partial interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising (I questioned including both in the model while reading). It would help to make explicit which condition differences are statistically reliable; the box plots were hard for me to interpret clearly.
- Section 4.2: It seems essential to demonstrate that if the reference observer does not have a slow-speed prior, the method can recover that fact. If this is only in the supplement, please consider showing it more prominently in the main text.

Specific comments:
- Line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is in fact continuous? I assume this mirrors typical experimental designs in which stimulus levels are discrete and controlled by the experimenter. Please clarify.

- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Eq. 2) is motivated by classic psychophysical laws. It also imposes a strong constraint on the inference problem. It seems much of the identifiability you obtain may hinge on this representational transformation. In other words, the results suggest that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about internal transformations. Please discuss the extent to which your conclusions depend on this transform.

- Line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 together with the maximum-entropy choice guarantee that Eq. 3 is unimodal? In principle a mixture fit could be bimodal; how is unimodality enforced in practice?

- Line 158: Why prefer the lattice representation for the prior over, say, a uniform basis? Also, what knowledge justifies assuming a prior support that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range from the observer’s perspective?

- Figure 1: I found the figure more confusing than illuminating. It might be clearer to integrate 1b into 1a and remove p_est(s*|x), since it is derived, to reduce clutter.

- Line 252: Please comment on the conditions on N under which Eq. 14 is a good approximation: when does Stirling’s approximation hold well, and under what circumstances is E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] acceptable? By the time Eq. 15 is introduced—after dropping priors on θ and making these approximations—the justification feels primarily that it yields an intuitive similarity metric. A brief sensitivity analysis or error bound would help.

- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density”—which density precisely? The unnormalized target for MCMC over θ given Eq. 13, or the dataset distribution?

- Line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC? Please clarify the assumptions that justify ignoring learning of the experimental stimulus distribution in this conversion to an estimation framework.

Overall assessment: Innovative approach and important problem; clear exposition; however, the substantive results are somewhat limited given the number of assumptions, and several clarifications would strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
