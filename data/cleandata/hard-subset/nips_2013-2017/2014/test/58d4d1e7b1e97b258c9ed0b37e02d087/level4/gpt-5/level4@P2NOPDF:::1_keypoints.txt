- Proposes a framework to compare Bayesian models using behavioral data, reviewing Bayesian decision theory and identifying sources of non-identifiability.  
- Describes constraints that can render each term in the framework identifiable and imposes specific assumptions to operationalize them.  
- Assesses discriminability of parameter values and demonstrates the approach on two case studies: interval timing and motion perception.  
- Treats an important, long-standing identifiability problem in cognitive science and is clearly written with a sensible strategy.  
- Major concern 1: Largely overlooks substantial prior literature on model comparison/distinguishability (e.g., Pitt, Myung, Navarro) and needs to clarify novelty relative to these works.  
- Major concern 2: Several assumptions used to achieve identifiability appear ad hoc; the paper must justify them, provide empirical/theoretical motivation, and analyze sensitivity to alternative constraints.  
- Specific methodological questions: why define p_meas as in Eq.3, and are the parameter choices in Eq.12 truly sufficient to capture the processes of interest?  
- Minor comments: explain the “trivial mathematical fact” at Line 53, change “nor” to “or” at Line 73, and clarify what the star and dashed lines denote in Figure 2.  
- Overall: A well-written paper on an important topic that requires stronger engagement with prior work and clearer justification of its identifiability assumptions.
