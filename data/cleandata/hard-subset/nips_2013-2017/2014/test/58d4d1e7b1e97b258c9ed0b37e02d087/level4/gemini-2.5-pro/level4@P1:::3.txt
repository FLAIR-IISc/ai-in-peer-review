This paper presents a formal framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors first develop a flexible and computationally efficient class of observer models. This model encompasses the entire perceptual and decision-making pipeline, including stages for sensation (with stimulus-dependent noise), perception (priors and likelihoods), decision-making (loss functions), and motor response. A key technical feature is the use of mixtures of Gaussians to parameterize these components, which allows for a wide range of functional forms while maintaining computational tractability. The main contribution is a method to use this model class to systematically explore the identifiability landscape of a given experimental design before data collection. This allows researchers to assess how well key parameters, such as the observer's prior, can be recovered and to compare the effectiveness of different experimental setups. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: inferring priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing the slow-speed prior in a speed perception task.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work is its direct and systematic approach to tackling the critical problem of model identifiability in computational studies of perception. The proposed framework, which allows researchers to assess the theoretical limits of parameter recovery for a given experimental design, is a valuable methodological contribution. This *a priori* analysis can guide more efficient and powerful experimental design, potentially saving resources and leading to more robust scientific conclusions.

The observer model itself is a significant technical achievement. It is both comprehensive and flexible, capable of capturing a wide variety of phenomena discussed in the literature (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise that approximates Weber's or Steven's law, asymmetric loss functions, non-Gaussian priors) within a single, unified parameterization. The fact that this is achieved while maintaining computational efficiency for calculating the expected log-likelihood is impressive and is the key enabler for the proposed identifiability analysis.

The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's practical value. The analysis of the time estimation task, for instance, yields non-obvious insights, such as how encouraging a MAP-like strategy (narrow loss function) could paradoxically worsen the recovery of the prior, or how independently measuring motor noise can substantially resolve model degeneracy.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While the paper's focus on identifiability is novel and important, the underlying modeling framework, in its spirit and components, feels like an incremental advance over several recent, highly sophisticated Bayesian observer models. For example, the work by Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) also presents comprehensive models that flexibly parameterize priors, likelihoods, and noise sources to account for behavior in perceptual tasks. While the current submission is not identical, it shares enough structural and conceptual similarities with this line of work that its contribution to the art of modeling itself feels evolutionary rather than revolutionary. I suggest that the authors revise the manuscript to include a more thorough discussion of these related models, clearly articulating how their proposed framework moves beyond them in ways other than its direct application to identifiability analysis.

A second point concerns the paper's claims about identifiability. The framework excellently addresses *parameter identifiability* within the assumed structure of the model. However, the term "identifiability" can be interpreted more broadly, and the paper does not solve the deeper challenge that concerns many experimentalists: *model class identifiability*. For instance, the framework would likely struggle to definitively distinguish whether an observer's sub-optimal behavior stems from sensory noise, an internal mismatch between the true and assumed likelihood, or motor response noise. These different sources of variability can often trade off against one another to produce nearly identical behavioral data. It would strengthen the paper to explicitly acknowledge this distinction. The authors could clarify that their method is designed to optimize parameter recovery within a given, well-motivated model class, but that it does not resolve the more fundamental ambiguity between different structural accounts of behavioral variability.

In summary, this is a strong paper that I would be happy to see accepted at the conference. It presents a mathematically elegant and practically useful framework for formalizing and testing perceptual estimation models. My reservations are minor and can be addressed through revision.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and valuable framework for conducting *a priori* identifiability analysis to improve experimental design.
*   The underlying observer model is technically sophisticated, flexible, and computationally tractable, representing a solid piece of engineering.
*   The case studies provide compelling demonstrations of the framework's ability to yield practical and sometimes non-intuitive insights.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The modeling approach, while powerful, represents an incremental advance over existing work in the literature.
*   The claims regarding identifiability should be carefully qualified to distinguish between the solvable problem of parameter identifiability and the more challenging problem of model-class identifiability.
