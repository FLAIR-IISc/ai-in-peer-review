This article proposes a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a crucial issue that has hindered the widespread adoption of the Bayesian framework in cognitive science and psychology. The authors provide a thorough summary of the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, highlighting areas of non-identifiability and outlining the types of constraints that can be used to make each term in the framework identifiable.

The authors' approach is well-motivated, as issues of identifiability of internal representations and processes have been prominent in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when representational formats can mimic each other, proving that they are indistinguishable without some constraints on processes. More recently, Jones and Love (2011) and Bowers and Davis (2012) have discussed the limitations of the Bayesian framework in addressing these issues.

The article is well-written and effectively conveys the authors' ideas. However, I have two major reservations. First, the authors fail to adequately acknowledge and engage with the large previous literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004) have proposed general frameworks for model comparison that share similarities with the authors' approach. A more thorough discussion of how the authors' framework contributes to and differs from this existing work is necessary to evaluate its significance.

Second, the authors make several assumptions that seem arbitrary and are not thoroughly justified. For example, the choice of Equation 3 to define $p_{meas}$ is not clearly motivated, and it is unclear how this definition captures the essential characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process. While the authors argue that this definition is robust and can express a large class of sensory models, this justification is insufficient. A more detailed discussion of the rationale behind this assumption and its implications for the framework is necessary.

Minor comments:

* Line 53: Instead of stating that it is a "trivial mathematical fact," the authors could provide a brief explanation to clarify the point for readers who may not find it obvious.
* Line 73: The second "nor" should be replaced with "or" for correct grammar.
* Figure 2: The caption should clearly explain the meaning of the star and dashed lines, which appear to denote the true value.

Overall, the article addresses an important issue in cognitive science and psychology, but its contribution would be strengthened by a more thorough engagement with existing literature and clearer justification of the authors' assumptions.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The article is well-written and effectively conveys the authors' ideas.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The authors fail to adequately acknowledge and engage with existing literature on model comparison.
* The authors make assumptions that seem arbitrary and are not thoroughly justified.
* The article could benefit from clearer explanations and justifications of the authors' assumptions and methods.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the authors revise the article to address the concerns mentioned above. Specifically, they should provide a more thorough discussion of the existing literature on model comparison and clearly justify their assumptions and methods. With these revisions, the article has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
