Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a central challenge in Bayesian observer modeling: non-identifiability of internal components (prior, likelihood, loss) when inferring them from behavioral data. The authors (i) formalize how degeneracies arise within Bayesian Decision Theory, (ii) propose constraints that can be used to make inference tractable, and (iii) introduce a computational framework to assess a priori whether particular model parameters are practically identifiable under a given experimental design. Their key technical device is an expected log-likelihood criterion, which reduces to a sum of KL divergences between response distributions under a reference observer and a candidate observer across stimuli; they then sample an “approximate expected posterior” over parameters using MCMC to map identifiability. Two case studies illustrate the approach: estimation of interval duration (where identifiability of priors depends critically on motor-noise calibration and loss-function assumptions) and speed perception under a slow-speed prior (where prior and noise parameters appear recoverable and robust).

The topic is timely and important. There is a long tradition in psychology and cognitive science of studying model comparison, identifiability, and parameter recovery (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), as well as critiques of over-interpretation of Bayesian models (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). The paper’s framing resonates with earlier concerns about representational indistinguishability (e.g., Anderson, 1978). Relative to this literature, the contribution here is to tailor a systematic, simulation-based identifiability analysis to a broad but specific class of Bayesian observer models, with an emphasis on experimental design choices.

Evaluation

Quality
- The derivation connecting expected log-likelihood to a stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences is sound and appropriate for measuring practical identifiability under a specified Ntr. The use of a reference observer aligns with established “model/parameter recovery” practices.
- The observer class is fairly rich (mixtures of Gaussians for measurement noise and loss; flexible priors; monotone sensory mapping), enabling efficient computation while covering many psychophysical tasks.
- The case studies are carefully executed and yield actionable insights (e.g., fixing motor noise greatly improves prior recovery in interval timing; narrowing the loss function can be detrimental).
- That said, several assumptions are strong and deserve clearer justification and sensitivity analyses: (a) the specific mixture-of-Gaussians form and moment constraints for measurement and loss; (b) the monotonic mapping in Eq. 2; (c) drawing lapse responses from the prior; and (d) discretization of stimulus sets. It would strengthen the paper to probe robustness to these choices and to demonstrate recovery when the generative observer lies outside the model class.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The modeling pipeline (sensation, perception, decision, response) is clear, and equations are well motivated.
- A few clarifications would help: explicitly define all symbols when first introduced; state how Ntr is chosen and how results scale with Ntr; discuss computational cost; and more fully describe how 2AFC is mapped to estimation in the speed task.
- Figures convey the main points, but some legends need more detail (see minor comments).

Originality
- The core idea—using expected log-likelihood/KL-based comparisons to map identifiability under a reference observer—is not entirely new in spirit (cf. Myung/Pitt/Navarro on model distinguishability and parameter recoverability). The novel aspects are the specialized, efficient observer class; the practical MCMC-based exploration of identifiability “landscapes”; and the explicit use of this analysis to rank experimental designs in Bayesian perception tasks.

Significance
- The framework can help practitioners avoid common pitfalls in interpreting inferred priors/likelihoods/losses, and guide experiment design (e.g., importance of independently measuring motor noise). The speed perception case provides theoretical support for prior empirical claims about slow-speed priors.
- Broader impact will depend on demonstrating robustness beyond the chosen parametric forms and extending to common paradigms (e.g., discrete choice, cue integration).

Pros
- Tackles an important, under-addressed problem in Bayesian observer modeling.
- Provides a coherent, computationally efficient framework and clear recipe for a priori design evaluation.
- Insightful case studies with nontrivial recommendations (e.g., narrow loss can harm identifiability).
- Bridges theoretical identifiability with practical experimental design.

Cons
- Limited engagement with the substantial literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive modeling; positioning relative to Pitt & Myung’s framework, Navarro et al.’s distinguishability analyses, and related NIPS work would clarify novelty.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions (mixture-of-Gaussians likelihoods/loss, logarithmic mapping, lapse model) without sufficient sensitivity analyses; unclear robustness to misspecification or to observers outside the model class.
- Some methodological choices need clearer motivation (e.g., “maximum-entropy” claims for mixtures tied to first four moments; drawing lapse responses from the prior).
- Scope currently limited to 1D estimation; mapping from 2AFC to estimation warrants more detail.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand related-work discussion and explicitly contrast the proposed approach with prior identifiability/model recovery methods (e.g., simulation-based model selection, information criteria, profile likelihood, Fisher information).
- Add sensitivity analyses: vary number of mixture components; test alternative loss families; consider nonmonotonic or different sensory mappings; assess recovery when the generative observer violates model assumptions.
- Report computational costs and convergence diagnostics for MCMC; discuss scalability with dimensionality and Ntr.
- In the interval-timing case, include a design where the loss is empirically measured or manipulated with independent validation, to test the conclusion about narrow losses.
- Clarify the estimation-equivalent setup for the speed task and discuss when the mapping holds.

Minor comments
- Please replace “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” with a brief explanation or citation clarifying the degeneracy; not all readers will find it trivial.
- Line 73: the second “nor” should be “or.”
- Figure 2: clarify what the star and dashed lines denote; if both indicate the true value, distinguish them or remove redundancy.
- Unify notation (p vs q) and ensure all variables (e.g., Ntr, a, t) are defined on first use.
- Explain and justify the “maximum entropy” rationale for choosing mixture parameters based on first four moments.
- Consider noting explicitly that E(θ|θ*) is not a true posterior, but a convenient device for exploring identifiability under datasets generated by θ*.

Overall assessment
A well-written and timely paper that proposes a practical framework for probing identifiability in Bayesian observer models, with informative case studies. Its impact would be strengthened by deeper engagement with prior identifiability/model-comparison work and by robustness analyses that relax key parametric assumptions.
