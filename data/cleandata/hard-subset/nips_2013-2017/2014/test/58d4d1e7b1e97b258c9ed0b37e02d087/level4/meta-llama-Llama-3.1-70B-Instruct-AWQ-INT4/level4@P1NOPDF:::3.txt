The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, encompassing mathematical equations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A notable strength of this model lies in its ability to balance robustness and mathematical convenience, allowing it to accommodate various situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining computational tractability.

The paper highlights the model's identifiability, enabling straightforward evaluation and distinction between two parameter estimates. Overall, I find this paper appealing and believe it deserves consideration for acceptance at the NIPS conference. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in numerous experiments.

However, I have some reservations regarding the paper's novelty. While the proposed model is not identical to existing literature, it shares a similar spirit and style with other recently published models, such as those found in PLoS Computational Biology. For instance, the reader may consider the following papers:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.

Given the similarities with existing literature, I believe it is fair to state that the contribution of the current submission is incremental.

To strengthen the manuscript, I suggest the authors include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly articulate why their proposed model represents a significant advancement over previously published models.

Additionally, I would like to comment on the emphasis placed on the model's mathematical identifiability. While it is true that the model's parameters are identifiable, this is largely due to the mathematical assumptions underlying the model. The term "identifiable" can be interpreted in other ways, such as the model's ability to distinguish between different sources of error (e.g., sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise) in experimental subjects. In this context, it could be argued that the model does not contribute significantly to addressing the identifiability concerns that are most relevant to perceptual scientists. I encourage the authors to address this point in the revised manuscript.

In summary, the paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novel contribution is incremental, building upon closely related work already existing in the literature.
