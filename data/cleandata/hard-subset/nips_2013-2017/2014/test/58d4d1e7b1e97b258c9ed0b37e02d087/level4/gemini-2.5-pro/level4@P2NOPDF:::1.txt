This paper presents a framework for model comparison of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors address the critical and long-standing issue of model identifiability, a problem that has been a significant focus in cognitive science and psychology for decades (e.g., Anderson, 1978). Specifically, they tackle the non-identifiability challenges within Bayesian Decision Theory, which have been cited as a major hindrance to its broader adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). The proposed approach involves outlining the sources of non-identifiability within the Bayesian framework and then introducing specific constraints on each component to achieve identifiability. The authors demonstrate their method by exploring parameter differentiability and applying it to two case studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The manuscript is well-written and addresses a topic of significant theoretical importance. The authors' approach to formalizing the problem and proposing a solution is generally reasonable and effective. However, I have two major reservations that temper my enthusiasm for the work in its current form.

First, the paper overlooks a substantial body of prior work on model comparison and selection in cognitive science and psychology. This omission makes it difficult to situate the current work and evaluate its novel contributions. The general framework proposed here shares significant overlap with established methods for assessing model mimicry and identifiability (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). A discussion comparing and contrasting the proposed framework with these influential approaches is necessary to clarify what advantages the current method offers.

Second, the framework relies on a series of assumptions to enforce identifiability, but the justification for these choices is often unclear, and some appear arbitrary. The validity and generality of the entire approach hinge on these assumptions, yet their impact is not fully explored. For instance, the paper should provide a stronger rationale for defining the measurement probability, $p_{meas}$, specifically as shown in Equation 3. Similarly, while the parameterization in Equation 12 is described as robust and capable of expressing a large class of sensory models, this flexibility alone is not a sufficient justification for its specific form. Is this parameterization truly sufficient to capture the essential characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? The paper would be substantially improved by a dedicated section that thoroughly discusses and defends the key assumptions made to constrain the model, explaining why they are plausible and how sensitive the results are to these specific choices.
