- Proposes a framework for systematically testing identifiability in Bayesian perception models.
- Demonstrates degeneracy: different priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs.
- Provides an a priori method to evaluate experimental designs before data collection.
- Develops a flexible, computationally tractable Bayesian observer model that handles stimulus-dependent noise.
- Uses a four-stage model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) to explore parameter space efficiently.
- Case study (time interval estimation): baseline design fails to recover priors; measuring motor parameters independently (MTR) improves reconstruction.
- Time estimation finding: encouraging MAP-like loss actually worsens inference quality.
- Case study (speed perception): reconstruction of slow-speed priors is robust, supporting prior empirical results.
- Main concern: novelty is incremental and closely related to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014); needs clearer differentiation from prior work.
- Identifiability claims need qualification: framework shows identifiability within the model but doesn’t resolve broader separability of sensory, decision, and response noise.
- Mathematical treatment, derivations, and visualizations are sound and results are well supported.
- Recommended revisions: add a review of recent Bayesian perception models, clarify identifiability scope/limitations, and precisely define contributions relative to prior work.
- Overall judgment: useful incremental methodological contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
