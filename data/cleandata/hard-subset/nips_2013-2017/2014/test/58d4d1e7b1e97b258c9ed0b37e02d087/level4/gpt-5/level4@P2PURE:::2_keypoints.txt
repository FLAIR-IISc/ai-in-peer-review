- Addresses important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling; well written and judged above acceptance threshold.  
- Approach depends on a long, extensive sequence of assumptions that may be excessive relative to the impact of results.  
- Section 4.1: Claim that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising; require statistical tests to show which condition differences are reliable because box plots are hard to interpret.  
- Section 4.2: Must demonstrate that the model would correctly recover the absence of a slow-speed prior if the reference observer lacked it.  
- line 106: Clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution (likely experimental design mimicry).  
- line 134: The log-stimulus transform strongly constrains inference; identifiability may largely stem from this representational assumption.  
- line 143: Ask whether constraints and differential-entropy maximization guarantee the fitted distribution in Eq.3 is unimodal, since bimodality is possible.  
- line 158: Question why the observer’s prior should favor a lattice over a uniform representation and how a prior 50% wider than the true range is justified.  
- Figure 1: Figure is confusing; suggest integrating panel b into a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x) to improve clarity.  
- line 252: Request clarification on conditions for Stirling’s approximation (N) and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]; note Eq.15 follows several approximations and seems justified mainly for intuitive interpretation.  
- line 262: Clarify what “sample from this unnormalized density” refers to (which density is meant).  
- line 365: Explain why learning of priors does not matter in the 2AFC task.  
- Overall verdict: Innovative and interesting methodology on an important problem, but results are modest and rely on strong assumptions.
