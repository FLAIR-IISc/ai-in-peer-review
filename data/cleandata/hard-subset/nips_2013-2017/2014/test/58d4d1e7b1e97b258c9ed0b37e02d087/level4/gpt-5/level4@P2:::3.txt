Summary
This paper presents a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks. The authors introduce a flexible, computationally efficient class of observer models that covers one-dimensional estimation (e.g., time, distance, speed), with extensions to angular and symmetric multidimensional cases. The model includes: a sensory mapping from stimulus to internal measurement space (with stimulus-dependent noise captured by a parametric transform), a prior represented as a Gaussian mixture on a lattice, an internal likelihood (also a Gaussian mixture) that may differ from the true measurement distribution, a loss function modeled as a mixture of inverted Gaussians, lapses, and motor/report noise. The behavioral response distribution is obtained by marginalizing over unobserved variables.

To study identifiability, the authors define the expected log-evidence for a candidate parameter vector relative to a reference observer as the negative, stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions, scaled by trial count. They then sample an approximate expected posterior over parameters via MCMC to map practical identifiability under different experimental designs.

Two case studies illustrate the approach. In interval timing (short/long duration conditions), priors are difficult to recover reliably unless motor parameters are known; narrowing the loss toward MAP degrades prior recovery; using one condition with more trials only marginally helps. In speed perception with a slow-speed prior, priors and noise characteristics are generally well recovered, even when the internal likelihood is decoupled from the objective noise, lending theoretical support to existing empirical claims.

Relation to prior work
The modeling approach and case studies build on a substantial literature on Bayesian observer models and inference of priors/likelihoods/losses (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, Jazayeri & Shadlen). The model class and computational strategy are closely related in spirit to recent PLoS Computational Biology work on internal representations and suboptimality (Acerbi et al., 2012; 2014), as well as general treatments of model distinguishability (Navarro, Pitt, & Myung). The primary contribution here is to systematize a priori practical identifiability within a unified observer parametrization and to use it for design evaluation, rather than proposing a qualitatively new observer or inference paradigm.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent framework that integrates a broad, expressive observer class with an efficient identifiability metric (expected log-evidence via KL sums).
- Practical value: enables a priori comparisons of experimental designs and highlights which parameters most limit identifiability (e.g., motor noise, loss width).
- Computational tractability: mixture-based forms yield fast evaluation of expected loss and response distributions; the approach scales to realistic designs.
- Case studies are well chosen and tie results to active debates (interval timing priors; slow-speed prior).

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: the observer formulation, inferential perspective, and case analyses are closely aligned with prior Bayesian observer work (including by the same authors). The distinct advance is the specific identifiability mapping and design-ranking exercise, which could be emphasized and benchmarked more clearly.
- Scope of “identifiability”: the paper focuses on identifiability within the assumed parametric family. However, without additional constraints or measures, the framework cannot, in general, disentangle sensory vs decision vs response noise contributions, nor guarantee recovery under model mis-specification (e.g., incorrect loss family or prior basis).
- Limited comparison to existing identifiability tools: there is little contrast with alternatives such as Fisher information, profile likelihoods, or formal design-of-experiments criteria, and no empirical runtime/accuracy comparisons.
- Some assumptions merit sensitivity analysis: e.g., the choice of mixture order and lattice spacing for priors, the specific transform f(s), the mixture form of the loss, and the mapping from 2AFC to estimation in the speed case.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a dedicated related-work section situating the framework relative to recent Bayesian observer models and prior identifiability/design analyses (e.g., Acerbi et al., Navarro et al.). Clarify the key novel elements and why they matter in practice.
- Clarify terminology and scope: explicitly frame the results as a priori practical identifiability within a specified model family and design, and discuss what cannot be identified without additional constraints or auxiliary measurements (e.g., separate motor-noise calibration).
- Provide sensitivity analyses: assess robustness to mis-specified priors (different bases), loss function forms, transform f(s), and mixture order. Report how identifiability changes with these choices.
- Compare to alternative identifiability/design metrics (e.g., Fisher information, D-optimality) on at least one case study, to contextualize the KL-based expected evidence approach.
- In the interval timing case, discuss the implications of the low identifiability of the loss width parameter and reconcile with empirical claims about MAP-like behavior; recommend concrete design modifications (e.g., independent motor-noise calibration, manipulations that selectively alter loss vs noise).
- In the speed-perception case, justify the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping and examine robustness to lapse-rate mis-specification and contrast-dependent internal noise decoupling.

Pros for acceptance
- Provides a useful, general framework for probing identifiability and informing experimental design in Bayesian observer studies.
- Methodologically sound, with clear derivations and efficient computation.
- Case studies deliver actionable insights (e.g., value of independently measuring motor noise; limited gains from MAP-like losses).

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is incremental relative to existing Bayesian observer literature.
- Scope and limits of identifiability are not always clearly separated from standard concerns about model equivalence and noise-source confounds.
- Limited benchmarking against alternative identifiability/design methodologies.

Overall evaluation
A mathematically elegant and practically useful framework that can help researchers anticipate and mitigate identifiability issues before running experiments. While the contribution is incremental, the paper is well executed and valuable to the community. With stronger positioning relative to prior work and clearer discussion of the scope and limits of “identifiability,” this would be a solid addition to NIPS.
