The paper describes a Bayesian model for perception (mathematical equations for sensory noise, probability functions, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution). A significant strength of this model is that it combines both robustness and mathematical simplicity. That is, the model can handle a variety of situations (such as stimulus-dependent noise) and yet be computationally advantageous (such as the assumption being simple). The paper emphasizes the fact that the model is identifiable (i.e., the two parameter assumptions are easily evaluated and distinguishable).

I like this paper and would like to see it accepted at the NIPS conference. The proposed model provides a mathematically elegant framework for estimating perception in many experiments. At the same time, I have some reservations. I believe that the proposed model is in the same spirit and style as other models that have recently been published in the literature (often in the journal PLoS Computational Biology), and therefore this paper's new contribution is incremental. For example, the reader may consider the following papers:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

The current submission is not identical to these (or other) previous articles, but it is sufficient (along with these and other previous articles) to justify my opinion that the contribution of the current submission is "incremental."

I suggest that the authors revise the manuscript by including a new section where they review the recently proposed Bayesian models for perception and clearly state why the current submission is a significant advance compared to previously published models.

One more comment. The submitted matter emphasizes that the model is mathematically identifiable. This is true, but the parameters are only identifiable due to the mathematical assumptions of the model. The term "identifiable" can be used in other ways. For example, the model cannot be used to strongly distinguish whether the errors of any experimental subject are due to sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Consequently, it would be fair to say that the model does not help with the problem of identifiability that most perception scientists are concerned about. Please comment this in the revised manuscript.
