This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, specifying mathematical formulations for sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A key strength of the model lies in its combination of robustness—its ability to accommodate diverse scenarios such as stimulus-dependent noise—and computational tractability, allowing for efficient inference. The authors rightly emphasize the model’s identifiability, facilitating clear evaluation and distinction between parameter estimates.

I find this paper promising and recommend it for acceptance to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation within numerous experimental contexts. However, I do have some reservations. The model appears to align closely with a growing body of recent work, often published in *PLoS Computational Biology*, and I believe the contribution, while valuable, is incremental. For instance, readers might find similarities with the following publications:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *8*(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *10*(6), e1003661. 

While not identical, the current submission shares sufficient similarities with these and other related works that characterizing the contribution as incremental feels appropriate. 

To address this, I suggest the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception and explicitly articulating how the presented model represents a significant advancement over existing approaches.

Finally, while the authors correctly highlight the mathematical identifiability of the model, it is important to acknowledge that this identifiability is predicated on the model’s underlying assumptions. The model does not, for example, allow one to definitively differentiate between errors arising from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Consequently, the model does not resolve the core identifiability challenges typically encountered by perceptual scientists. The authors should address this nuance in the revised manuscript.

In summary, the paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalising perceptual estimation. However, the novel contribution is incremental given closely related work already present in the literature.
