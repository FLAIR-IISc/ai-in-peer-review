The manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and well-organized, making it a pleasure to read. However, the work relies on a long list of assumptions that the reader must accept and understand, which may detract from the impact of the key results. Despite this, the methodology is innovative and interesting, and I believe the work meets the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results, the finding in section 4.1 that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not particularly surprising, and it would be helpful to indicate which condition differences are reliable. The use of box plots may not be the most effective way to present this information, and the reader may benefit from additional clarification.

In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the fact that the reference observer does not have a slow-speed prior. This would strengthen the results and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's capabilities.

Several specific comments are also worth noting:

* Line 106: The authors should clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution when it is, in fact, a continuous variable. This may be due to the typical design of experiments, but a brief explanation would be helpful.
* Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws, and its incorporation may be a key factor in the success of the work. This suggests that the results may be more accurately interpreted as demonstrating the identifiability of Bayesian models under strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.
* Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy do not necessarily ensure that Equation 3 is fit to be a unimodal distribution. The authors should address the possibility that the fit could yield a bimodal distribution.
* Line 158: The authors should justify why the observer's prior favors the lattice representation over a uniform representation. What information does the observer have about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?
* Figure 1: The figure is confusing and could be improved by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x), as it is a derived inference.
* Line 252: The authors should comment on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, including the accuracy of Stirling's approximation of the factorial and the circumstances under which E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x].
* Line 262: The authors should specify which density is being referred to when they mention "sample from this unnormalized density."
* Line 365: The authors should explain why the learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC. While the approach is innovative and the problem is important, the results are not overwhelmingly convincing.

Overall, the manuscript presents an interesting and innovative approach to a significant problem in cognitive modeling. While the results are not entirely convincing, the work is well-written and clear, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
