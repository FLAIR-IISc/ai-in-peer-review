The manuscript tackles the important issue in cognitive modeling of model identifiability. The manuscript is nicely written and quite clear. However, the work is premised on a long list of assumptions that one must buy into and make sense of, and this list is quite long relative to the impact of the key results presented. Because the methodology is interesting and innovative, I judge the work to be above threshold for presentation at NIPS.

The paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, specifically addressing the issue of model non-identifiability due to the degeneracy of the Bayesian framework. The authors propose a systematic approach to investigate identifiability, which involves analyzing the expected support for a model given a dataset. They apply this framework to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The strengths of the paper include its clear and concise writing style, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and methodology. The framework proposed by the authors is innovative and has the potential to be widely applicable in the field of cognitive modeling. The case studies provide a good demonstration of the framework's capabilities and limitations.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The long list of assumptions required to understand the work may be overwhelming for some readers. Additionally, the results presented in the case studies are not overwhelmingly strong, and some of the findings may be expected or intuitive.

Specifically, in section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was little surprise, and it would be helpful to indicate which condition differences were reliable. In section 4.2, it is essential to show that if the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, the model could recover this fact as well.

Some minor comments on the paper include:

* Line 106: The authors may wish to clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution when it is in fact a continuous variable.
* Line 134: The success of the work may be due in large part to the incorporation of the log-stimulus transform, which imposes a fairly strong constraint on inference.
* Line 143: The constraints in Eq 4 and the maximization of differential entropy may not ensure that Eqn 3 is fit to be a unimodal distribution.
* Line 158: The authors may wish to explain why the observer's prior favors the lattice representation over a uniform representation.
* Figure 1: The figure could be improved by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) from the figure.
* Line 252: The authors may wish to comment on the conditions on N that make Equation 14 approximately correct.
* Line 262: The authors may wish to clarify which density they are sampling from.
* Line 365: The authors may wish to explain why learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC.

Overall, the paper presents an innovative framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. While the results are not overwhelmingly strong, the methodology is interesting and has the potential to be widely applicable in the field of cognitive modeling.
