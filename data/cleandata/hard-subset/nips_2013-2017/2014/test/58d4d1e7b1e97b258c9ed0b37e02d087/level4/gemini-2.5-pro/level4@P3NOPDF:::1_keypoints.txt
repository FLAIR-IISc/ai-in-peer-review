- Proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian behavioral models by identifying non-identifiable components and imposing constraints to achieve identifiability.  
- Summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory and instantiates the approach with simplifying assumptions, analyzing parameter differentiability and applying it to time-interval estimation and motion perception case studies.  
- Tackles an important, long-standing problem in cognitive science: model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling.  
- Major concern: insufficient engagement with prior model-comparison literature (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang; Navarro, Pitt & Myung), leaving the novelty and advantages unclear.  
- Major concern: key model-constraining assumptions (e.g., measurement-noise form in Eq.3, parameterization in Eq.12) lack justification and may be arbitrary.  
- Suggests the authors should discuss the importance of their choices and test sensitivity of results to alternative assumptions.
