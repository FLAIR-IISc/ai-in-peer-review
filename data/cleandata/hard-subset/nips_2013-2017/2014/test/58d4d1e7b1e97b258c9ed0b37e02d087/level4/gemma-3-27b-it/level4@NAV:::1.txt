This article presents a framework for comparing Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors provide a summary of Bayesian Decision Theory, identify challenges related to model identifiability, and describe constraints that can be applied to achieve identifiability for each component of the Bayesian framework. They then implement these constraints, explore parameter differentiability, and demonstrate the approach using two established applications of Bayesian decision theory – time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issue of identifying internal representations and processes has been a longstanding concern in cognitive science and psychology. Classic work, such as Anderson (1978), highlighted the indistinguishability of representational formats without process-level constraints. More recently, limitations in identifiability within the Bayesian framework itself have been recognized as a major impediment to its broader adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). 

While the paper addresses a theoretically significant problem and is generally well-executed, I have two primary concerns. First, the work lacks sufficient engagement with the existing literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, hindering a complete assessment of its novelty. The proposed framework shares similarities with previous approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, certain assumptions made by the authors appear arbitrary, and the degree to which the framework's results depend on these assumptions remains unclear. Specifically, the rationale for defining $p_{meas}$ with equation 3 is not fully explained. Furthermore, the justification for assuming that parameter values in Equation 12 adequately capture the characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process is insufficient, despite the framework’s robustness and ability to represent a broad class of sensory models. A more thorough discussion and justification of the identifiability-enforcing assumptions are needed.
