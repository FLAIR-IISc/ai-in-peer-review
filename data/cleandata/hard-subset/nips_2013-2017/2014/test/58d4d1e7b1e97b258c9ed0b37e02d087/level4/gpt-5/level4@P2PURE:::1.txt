This paper proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data. The authors review Bayesian Decision Theory, identify sources of non-identifiability, and specify the types of constraints needed to render each component of the Bayesian framework identifiable. They then introduce particular assumptions to constrain each term, assess how well parameter values can be differentiated in their model, and apply the method to two domains—time-interval estimation and motion perception—where Bayesian decision theory has been used to explain behavioral responses.

Questions about the identifiability of internal representations and processes have been central in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For instance, Anderson (1978) analyzed when different representational formats can mimic one another, showing they are behaviorally indistinguishable without constraints on processes whenever the representations carve inputs into equivalent distinctions. More recently, this has been cited as a major drawback of the Bayesian framework that has impeded its broader adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012—the latter directly discusses speed perception).

Although the paper addresses a topic of considerable theoretical importance, is clearly written, and generally proceeds in a sensible and effective way, I have two major reservations:
1) The paper largely overlooks the substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, making it hard to evaluate its incremental contribution. The proposed general framework for model comparison is not so different from some earlier work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004).
2) Several of the assumptions used to constrain the framework appear arbitrary, and it is unclear how strongly the overall approach depends on them. For example, why should p_meas be defined by Equation 3? Is specifying the parameters in Equation 12 truly sufficient to define and capture all aspects of a cognitive or perceptual process? I appreciate that the parameterization is robust and can express a wide class of sensory models, but that alone does not seem to justify the assumption. At a minimum, the paper should explicitly discuss and defend the particular assumptions used to make the Bayesian models identifiable.

Minor comments:
- Line 53: “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” Rather than labeling this as trivial, it would be more helpful to briefly explain why it is true; it may not be obvious to all readers.
- Line 73: The second “nor” should be “or.”
- Figure 2: What do the star and dashed lines denote? As written, the caption suggests both indicate the true value.
