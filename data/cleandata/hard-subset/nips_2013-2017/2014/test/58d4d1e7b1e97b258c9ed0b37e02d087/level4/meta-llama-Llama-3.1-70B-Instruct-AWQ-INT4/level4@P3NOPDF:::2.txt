The manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling, presenting a well-written and clear discussion of the topic. However, the work relies on a long list of assumptions that the reader must accept and understand, which may detract from the impact of the key results. Despite this, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and I believe the work meets the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results, the finding in section 4.1 that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not particularly surprising, and it would be helpful to indicate which condition differences were reliable. This could be achieved by providing additional statistical analysis or visualizations to supplement the box plots.

In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the fact that the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior. This would strengthen the results and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's capabilities.

Several specific comments and suggestions are provided below to further improve the manuscript:

* Line 106: The authors may wish to clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, despite being a continuous variable. This may be due to the typical design of experiments, where the stimulus level is the independent variable.
* Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws, and its incorporation appears to be a crucial factor in the success of the work. This suggests that the results may be heavily dependent on the assumption of strong representational transformations in the mind, and that Bayesian models are identifiable only under these conditions.
* Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy do not necessarily ensure that Equation 3 will be a unimodal distribution. In principle, the fit could yield a bimodal distribution, and the authors should address this possibility.
* Line 158: The authors should justify why the observer's prior favors the lattice representation over a uniform representation. What information does the observer have about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?
* Figure 1: The figure is confusing and could be improved by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x), as it is a derived inference.
* Line 252: The authors should provide more information on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, including the circumstances under which Stirling's approximation of the factorial holds and when E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x].
* Line 262: The authors should specify which unnormalized density is being sampled from.
* Line 365: The authors should explain why the learning of priors does not matter in the 2AFC task. This approach is innovative, but the results are not overwhelming.

Overall, the manuscript presents an important problem and an innovative approach, but the results could be strengthened with additional analysis and clarification of the assumptions and methods used.
