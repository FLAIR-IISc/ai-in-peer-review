

This paper addresses a critical issue in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors propose a systematic framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian models, which is essential because the inverse mapping from observed behavior to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is inherently degenerate. As noted by previous work [19], multiple combinations of model components can yield identical behavior, making reliable recovery of mental representations problematic. The paper builds on established NIPS contributions in Bayesian modeling of perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 2003; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Maloney & Mamassian, 2002), but makes a significant contribution by providing a practical method for assessing identifiability a priori.

The authors introduce a general class of Bayesian observer models that can efficiently represent many psychophysical tasks. Their framework allows researchers to systematically explore which experimental designs best resolve degeneracies in parameter recovery. The key innovation lies in their efficient method for computing expected posterior densities, which avoids the intractability of sampling from all possible datasets. The paper demonstrates this approach through two compelling case studies: time interval estimation (Section 4.1) and speed perception (Section 4.2).

The strengths of this work are substantial. First, the framework directly addresses a well-known but previously unaddressed problem in the field, providing a practical tool for experimental design. Second, the theoretical development is sound, with clear mathematical derivations that connect to established concepts in information theory (KL divergence) and Bayesian inference. Third, the empirical validation through two case studies demonstrates the framework's utility in identifying problematic experimental designs and revealing unexpected interactions (e.g., the counterintuitive negative impact of imposing a narrow loss function in interval timing). The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-structured explanations and sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

The main weaknesses relate to the paper's assumptions and the need for greater experimental validation. The framework's effectiveness relies on several key assumptions, particularly the sensory transformation in Equation 2, which imposes strong constraints on the model. While the authors justify this transformation through psychophysical laws, it's worth noting that the success of their approach may be partly due to this specific constraint. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how the framework would perform under alternative transformations.

Another limitation concerns the case study on speed perception (Section 4.2), where the authors assume observers don't learn prior expectations during the experiment. This contradicts established literature showing that observers rapidly adapt their priors to experimental distributions [30]. While the authors acknowledge this as a simplification, the paper would be stronger with a more thorough discussion of how this assumption affects their conclusions.

The paper also lacks a crucial validation: demonstrating that the framework can correctly identify the absence of a slow-speed prior when one doesn't exist. While the authors mention this in their draft review, it's not addressed in the manuscript, which is a significant oversight given the paper's focus on identifiability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding model identifiability in Bayesian perception models
- Provides a theoretically sound, practical framework for experimental design
- Demonstrates clear utility through two well-chosen case studies
- Well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility
- Builds logically on previous NIPS work in the field

Arguments against acceptance:
- Relies on several strong assumptions that may limit generalizability
- Lacks validation for the case where no prior exists (critical for identifiability claims)
- The sensory transformation in Equation 2 may be unduly constraining the framework's applicability
- Some aspects of the methodology could benefit from further clarification (e.g., the justification for Equation 15)

The paper's significance is substantial. It provides a tool that directly addresses a fundamental limitation in Bayesian modeling of perception, potentially improving the reliability of conclusions drawn from such models. The framework could become standard practice for designing experiments in the field, particularly given the increasing reliance on Bayesian models to infer internal representations from behavioral data.

This work represents a significant contribution to the field of computational cognition and perception. While it has some limitations related to assumptions, the framework is innovative, well-executed, and addresses a genuine problem in the literature. The authors have provided a valuable methodological tool that should be of interest to a wide range of researchers in perception, cognition, and computational neuroscience.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to address the validation for the case where no prior exists and to clarify the assumptions regarding the sensory transformation. These revisions would strengthen the paper's claims about the framework's identifiability testing capabilities. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a critical need in the field, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
