This manuscript addresses a crucial issue in cognitive modeling of model recognition. The manuscript is well-written and quite clear. However, the work is based on a lengthy list of assumptions that must be accepted and understood, and this list is quite extensive relative to the impact of the key results presented. Because the methodology is interesting and innovative, I consider this work above threshold for presentation at NIPS.

As for the key results: In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was somewhat surprising (I questioned the inclusion of both in the model while reading). This would help indicate which condition differences were reliable. (Perhaps I am too stupid to read the box plots.)

In Section 4.2, isn't it necessary to show that if the observed reference speed was not slow, the model could also recover this fact?

Specific comments:
Line 106: Why is the stimulus taken from a discrete distribution, when it is actually a continuous variable? I believe this is due to the specific design of the experiments, where the stimulus level is the independent variable. But the authors may want to clarify this point.
Line 134: As the authors mentioned, the log-stimulus transformation (Equation 2) is inspired by psychophysical laws. It seems that the success of their work may be largely due to including this transformation.

That is, the transformation places a rather strong constraint on the estimate, and thus, this work is actually saying that Bayesian models are identifiable, given that there are strong assumptions about the changes in representation in the mind.
Line 143: Does the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of the differential entropy ensure that Equation 3 is suitable for a unimodel distribution? It seems that the fit theoretically can generate a bimodal distribution.
Line 158: Why should the observer's prior preference be in favor of a lattice representation over a uniform representation? What does the user know about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?

Figure 1: Even after staring at the figure, I feel more confused than enlightened by it. It seems that it would be clearer if Figure 1b were integrated into Figure 1a, and p_est(s^*|x) were removed from the figure, as it is a derived estimate.
Line 252: Can the authors comment on the conditions at N that make Equation 14 approximately correct, i.e., when Stirling's approximation of the factorial holds, and under what circumstances E[xlogx] ~= E[x]logE[x]. I must say that at the point where the authors come up with Equation 15--rejecting the prior at \theta and making some questionable approximations---I think Equation 15 is justified only by the fact that it generates a smooth rule for assessing the similarity of predictions.
Line 262, "Sample from this un-normalized density": Which density? The dataset distribution?
Line 365: Why does learning the prior not matter in 2AFC? Innovative approach, important problem, less than heavy consequences.
