The authors propose a framework for comparing Bayesian models of behavioral data. They summarize Bayesian decision theory, identify sources of non-identifiability, and outline the constraints required to render each term in the Bayesian framework identifiable. They then impose specific assumptions to constrain each term, examine how distinguishable parameter values are within their models, and apply the approach to two domains that use Bayesian decision theory to explain behavior: time-interval estimation and motion perception.

Issues of identifiability of internal representations and processes have been prominent in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when representational formats can mimic each other, showing they are indistinguishable without constraints on processes whenever the representations make equivalent distinctions among inputs. In recent years, non-identifiability has been a major drawback of the Bayesian framework that has hindered its widespread adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012—the latter directly addresses speed perception as an example).

Although the paper addresses a theoretically important issue, is clearly written, and generally proceeds in a reasonable and effective manner, I have two major reservations:

1) Limited engagement with prior work on model comparison. The paper largely overlooks a substantial literature in cognitive science and psychology on model comparison, making it hard to place the contribution in context. The general framework the authors propose for model comparison is not so different from some earlier work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004; also see Myung, Pitt, Zhang, & Balasubramanian, 2000, NIPS 13). A fuller discussion of similarities and differences would clarify what is genuinely new here.

2) Reliance on assumptions that sometimes seem arbitrary, with unclear dependence of the framework on them. For example, why should p_meas be defined by Equation 3? Is specifying the parameter values in Equation 12 really sufficient to define and capture all characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? While I understand the parameterization is robust and can express a large class of sensory models, this alone does not obviously justify the assumption. At minimum, the paper should discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models so that they become identifiable, and clarify how conclusions might change under alternative, plausible constraints.

Minor comments:
- Line 53: “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” Rather than declaring it trivial, it would be more helpful to explain why it is true; it may not be trivial for all readers.
- Line 73: The second “nor” should be “or.”
- Figure 2: What do the star and dashed lines denote? The caption suggests both indicate the true value; please clarify the distinction.
