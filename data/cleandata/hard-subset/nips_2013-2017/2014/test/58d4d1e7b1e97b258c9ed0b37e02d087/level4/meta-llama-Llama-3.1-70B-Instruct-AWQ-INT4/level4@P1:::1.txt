This article proposes a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a crucial issue in cognitive science and psychology. The authors provide a thorough summary of the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, highlighting areas of non-identifiability and outlining the constraints necessary to make each term identifiable. They then present a novel class of Bayesian observer models, which they apply to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The article is well-written and effectively conveys the importance of addressing identifiability issues in Bayesian models. However, I have two major reservations. Firstly, the authors fail to adequately acknowledge and engage with the extensive previous literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004) proposed general frameworks for model comparison that share similarities with the authors' approach. A more comprehensive review of existing work would facilitate a better understanding of the article's contributions.

Secondly, some of the assumptions made by the authors seem arbitrary and lack clear justification. For example, the definition of pmeas(x|s) in Equation 3 appears to be based on computational convenience rather than empirical evidence. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation for their choices and discuss the implications of these assumptions on the framework's validity. Furthermore, it is unclear whether the parameter values in Equation 12 are sufficient to capture the complexities of cognitive and perceptual processes.

Minor comments:

* Line 53: Instead of stating that the mathematical fact is trivial, the authors could provide a brief explanation to facilitate understanding.
* Line 73: The second "nor" should be replaced with "or" for clarity.
* Figure 2: The legend should clearly indicate what the star and dashed lines represent, as it is not immediately apparent from the caption.

Overall, the article addresses a critical issue in Bayesian modeling, but its impact would be enhanced by a more thorough engagement with existing literature and a clearer justification of the assumptions underlying the proposed framework.
