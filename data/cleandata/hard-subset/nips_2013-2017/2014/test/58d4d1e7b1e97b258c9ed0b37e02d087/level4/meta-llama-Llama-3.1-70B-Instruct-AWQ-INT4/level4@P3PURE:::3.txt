The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, encompassing mathematical equations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A significant strength of this model lies in its ability to balance robustness and mathematical convenience, effectively handling various situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining computational tractability.

The paper highlights the model's identifiability, enabling easy evaluation and distinction of parameter estimates. I appreciate the proposed model's provision of a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in numerous experiments, and I believe it has the potential to be accepted to the NIPS conference.

However, I have some reservations regarding the paper's novelty. The proposed model appears to be in the same spirit and style as other recently published models, such as those found in PLoS Computational Biology. For instance, the reader may consider the following papers:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

While the current submission is distinct from these earlier articles, it bears sufficient similarities to suggest that its contribution is incremental. To address this concern, I recommend that the authors revise the manuscript by incorporating a new section that reviews recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly articulates why the model proposed in this submission represents a significant advancement over previously published models.

Furthermore, I would like to comment on the model's identifiability. Although the model is mathematically identifiable due to its assumptions, the term "identifiable" can be interpreted in different ways. For instance, the model may not be able to definitively distinguish whether an experimental subject's errors are due to sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Therefore, it can be argued that the model does not effectively address the identifiability issue that is most relevant to perceptual scientists. I suggest that the authors discuss this point in the revised manuscript.

In summary, the paper provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novel contribution is incremental, building upon closely related work already existing in the literature.
