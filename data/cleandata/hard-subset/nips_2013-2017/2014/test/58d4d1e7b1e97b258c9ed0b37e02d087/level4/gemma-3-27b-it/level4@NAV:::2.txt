The manuscript addresses the crucial problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. It is well-written and generally clear. However, the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions, and the extent of these assumptions feels disproportionate to the impact of the presented results. Despite this, the methodology is both interesting and innovative, leading me to recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS, albeit above the threshold.

Regarding the key results: The finding in Section 4.1 regarding the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising, and I questioned the need to incorporate both noise types into the model during my initial reading. Clarifying which observed condition differences are statistically reliable would be beneficial. (I admit to struggling with the interpretation of the box plots.)

In Section 4.2, it seems essential to demonstrate the model's ability to recover information about the stimulus when the reference observer *does not* possess a slow-speed prior.

Specific comments:

Line 106: The authors should clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, given that it represents a continuous variable. While I suspect this is a consequence of typical experimental design, where stimulus level serves as an independent variable, explicit acknowledgement would be helpful.

Line 134: The authors appropriately note that the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical principles. The success of this work may be significantly attributable to this transform, suggesting that it imposes a strong constraint on inference. Thus, the findings may demonstrate that Bayesian models are identifiable primarily *given* strong assumptions regarding representational transformations.

Line 143: Do the constraints in Eq 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that Eqn 3 will fit to a unimodal distribution? It appears that a bimodal distribution remains a theoretical possibility.

Line 158: What justification exists for the observer’s prior favoring the lattice representation over a uniform representation? What prior knowledge regarding the true stimulus distribution informs a prior that is 50% wider than the actual range?

Figure 1: The figure currently creates more confusion than clarity. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing *p_est(s^*|x)*, as a derived inference, would likely improve comprehension. 

Line 252: Could the authors elaborate on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, specifically regarding the applicability of Stirling's approximation of the factorial and the validity of E[xlogx] ~= E[x]logE[x]? By the time Equation 15 is reached—following the abandonment of priors on θ and the implementation of several approximations—its justification appears to reside primarily in its ability to generate an intuitively appealing rule for assessing prediction similarity.

Line 262: The phrase “sample from this unnormalized density” is unclear. Which density is being referenced – the dataset distribution?

Line 365: The lack of influence of prior learning in the 2AFC task is noteworthy. Overall, this is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are not overwhelmingly impactful.
