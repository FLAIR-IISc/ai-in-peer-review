- Tackles an important identifiability problem in cognitive modeling and is clearly written.  
- Methodology is interesting and innovative, warranting acceptance for presentation.  
- Work relies on a long list of strong assumptions that reduce its impact.  
- Many results (e.g., interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in Sec 4.1) are expected rather than surprising.  
- Report which condition differences are statistically reliable to clarify results.  
- Clarify interpretation and labeling of box plots for reader accessibility.  
- In Sec 4.2, show that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior when the reference observer lacks one.  
- Explain why a continuous stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution (line 106).  
- Acknowledge that the log-stimulus transform may drive much of the success, implying identifiability depends on representational assumptions (line 134).  
- Clarify whether constraints and entropy maximization force Eqn 3 to be unimodal or could yield bimodality (line 143).  
- Justify why the observer’s prior favors a lattice over uniform representation and the rationale for a prior 50% wider than the true range (line 158).  
- Simplify Figure 1: integrate panel b into a, and remove derived p_est(s^*|x) from the figure.  
- State conditions on N and justify approximations used for Eq 14 (Stirling’s approximation, when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) and consequent validity of Eq 15 (line 252).  
- Clarify which density is meant by “sample from this unnormalized density” (dataset distribution or other) (line 262).  
- Explain why learning priors does not affect 2AFC performance (line 365).  
- Overall: innovative approach to an important problem, but results are limited by assumptions and need clearer justification and exposition.
