This paper proposes a Bayesian model of perception that specifies the mathematical form of the sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A key strength of the proposed model is its combination of robustness and mathematical convenience. The framework is flexible enough to accommodate various experimental conditions, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while remaining computationally tractable for inference. The authors also rightly emphasize the model's mathematical identifiability, meaning its parameters can be reliably estimated and distinguished.

Overall, this paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, and I am supportive of its acceptance at NIPS. However, I have some reservations, primarily concerning the novelty of the contribution. The proposed model is very similar in spirit and style to other Bayesian models that have recently appeared in the literature. For instance, readers may consider the following related work:

*   Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 8(11), e1002771.
*   Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 10(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these prior works, the similarities are significant enough to characterize its contribution as incremental. To address this, I recommend that the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing these and other related models. In this section, they should explicitly articulate what makes their proposed model a significant advance over previously published work.

A second point concerns the claim of model identifiability. While the model is mathematically identifiable as claimed, this property stems directly from its core assumptions. It is important to note that the term "identifiability" can be interpreted differently in the field of perceptual science. Specifically, the model cannot disentangle distinct sources of an experimental subject's errors—such as sensory noise, decision noise, and response noise—which is a primary identifiability challenge that perceptual scientists face. Consequently, one could argue that the model does not resolve the identifiability problem as it is commonly understood in the field. I suggest the authors acknowledge and discuss this limitation in the revised manuscript.
