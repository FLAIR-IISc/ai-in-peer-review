- Proposes a framework to assess identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions in Bayesian observer models using behavioral data.  
- Summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory and pinpoints sources of non-identifiability (degeneracies among priors, likelihoods, and loss).  
- Describes constraints that can restore identifiability: theoretical constraints, task design, and additional measurements.  
- Instantiates a flexible parametric observer model (parametrized pmeas, priors, and loss functions) for analysis.  
- Uses an expected log-likelihood / KL-based criterion to map how distinguishable different parameterizations are.  
- Presents two case studies: interval timing and speed perception to demonstrate the approach.  
- Timing case: recovering priors is difficult without extra constraints (e.g., known motor noise, multiple stimulus ranges); narrow loss functions cause pitfalls.  
- Speed perception case: reconstructing a slow-speed prior and noise characteristics is relatively robust, even when separating internal and external noise.  
- Relation to prior work: overlaps conceptually with long-standing literature on model distinguishability and identifiability (e.g., Pitt et al., Navarro et al., Anderson, critiques of Bayesian modeling).  
- Major reservation 1: insufficient engagement with prior literature makes it hard to judge novelty; need to clarify what is genuinely new.  
- Major reservation 2: key modeling assumptions (e.g., mixture-of-Gaussians pmeas, moment/max-entropy choices, Eq. 12 parametrization) are under-justified and require sensitivity analyses or discussion.  
- Evaluation (NIPS criteria): derivations and case studies are solid, clarity is good, originality is partial (tailored model and computational efficiency), and significance is potentially high.  
- Minor comments: avoid calling facts “trivial” without explanation, fix a wording typo (“nor” → “or”), and clarify Figure 2 symbols.  
- Pros for acceptance: tackles an important problem, offers an efficient implementable framework, and gives actionable experimental design guidance.  
- Cons for acceptance: weak literature situating and under-justified parametrization choices limit confidence in conclusions.  
- Overall: useful and well-written contribution that would be stronger with clearer positioning relative to prior work and fuller justification/sensitivity checks for modeling choices.
