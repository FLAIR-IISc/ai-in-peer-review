## Review of Manuscript NIPS_2013-2017/2014

This manuscript addresses the important and challenging problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and the presentation generally well-organized. While the methodology is innovative and interesting, the work rests on a substantial set of assumptions, and the impact of the key results feels somewhat limited given their dependence on these assumptions. Nevertheless, I believe the work is above the NIPS threshold for presentation.

Regarding the key results, the finding in Section 4.1 concerning the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise wasn’t particularly surprising, and I questioned the necessity of including both noise types in the model from the outset. It would be beneficial to explicitly indicate which condition differences were statistically reliable, perhaps with clearer labeling or significance markers on the box plots. 

In Section 4.2, a critical analysis would involve demonstrating the model's ability to recover the stimulus distribution even when the reference observer *does not* possess a slow-speed prior. Establishing this capability is crucial to bolstering the claim of identifiability.

Here are several specific comments and suggestions for improvement:

* **Line 106:** The authors draw stimuli from a discrete distribution despite representing the underlying variable as continuous. While this likely reflects a common experimental design constraint – where stimulus levels function as discrete independent variables – clarification on this point would be helpful.

* **Line 134:** The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical principles, appears to play a significant role in the success of the model. It's worth acknowledging that the identifiability demonstrated may largely hinge on this strong prior constraint imposed by the representational transformation. The work essentially demonstrates that Bayesian models *can* be identifiable under substantial, biologically-motivated assumptions about the internal representation of stimuli.

* **Line 143:** The constraints in Equation 4 and the differential entropy maximization are intended to enforce a unimodal distribution for Equation 3. However, it's not immediately clear why these constraints *guarantee* unimodality. A bimodal solution remains theoretically possible, and the authors should address this potential.

* **Line 158:** The justification for the observer’s prior favoring the lattice representation over a uniform prior requires further explanation. What information does the observer possess about the true stimulus distribution that justifies a prior distribution 50% wider than the actual stimulus range?

* **Figure 1:** The figure currently feels more confusing than informative. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and removing *p<sub>est</sub>(s*<sup>*</sup>|x)*, which appears to be a derived quantity, would likely improve clarity.

* **Line 252:** When discussing Equation 14, please comment on the conditions under which the approximations – Stirling’s approximation for the factorial and *E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]* – are valid. By the time Equation 15 is reached, and given the abandonment of priors on θ and the use of these approximations, the justification for the equation feels primarily empirical; it yields an intuitive rule for assessing prediction similarity, but its theoretical foundation appears weakened.

* **Line 262:** The phrase “sample from this unnormalized density” is ambiguous. Please specify *which* density is being referred to – is it the dataset distribution, or another density defined previously?

* **Line 365:** The authors note that learning of priors does not appear to matter in a 2AFC task. This observation, combined with the innovative approach and important problem being tackled, leads me to conclude that the results, while technically sound, are less impactful than one might hope.



In summary, this manuscript presents a valuable contribution to the understanding of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. While the extensive assumptions underlying the work and the somewhat limited scope of the core results temper its overall impact, the innovative methodology and clear writing justify acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the specific comments above will further strengthen the manuscript.
