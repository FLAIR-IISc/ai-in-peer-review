- Tackles non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models by proposing a general, parametric class for 1D estimation tasks.  
- Introduces an a priori identifiability framework using an expected log-likelihood that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences, producing an approximate expected posterior for pre-data MCMC exploration.  
- Demonstrates the framework via two case studies: experimental design comparison for interval timing and reconstructability of a slow-speed prior in speed perception.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian decision-theory work; novelty lies in explicit pre-experimental identifiability analysis and a unified, tractable observer class.  
- Modeling pipeline (internal mapping; mixture-of-Gaussians likelihood/loss; lattice-mixture prior) is coherent, computationally efficient, and the KL-based expected log-likelihood is sound.  
- Findings are plausible (e.g., sensory vs motor noise degeneracy in timing; robustness of slow-speed prior inference) but based on simulations rather than empirical data.  
- Main limitations: reliance on specific parametric choices, limited sensitivity analyses, theoretical/simulated validation only, and focus restricted to 1D estimation (no implemented discrete-choice extension).  
- Clarity is good overall but the paper should more explicitly define “identifiability,” better situate related work, and report computational costs and hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Originality is incremental in components but novel in synthesizing a general, pre-experimental identifiability/design-comparison framework.  
- Significance: provides practical, sometimes counterintuitive design guidance useful for experimenters planning to recover internal representations.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled treatment of practical identifiability, unified observer class enabling broad exploration, concrete design insights, and clear exposition.  
- Cons against acceptance: incremental methodological novelty, strong parametric assumptions, no real-data validation, and limited scope to 1D tasks.  
- Recommended improvements: add a focused related-work section, clarify identifiability limits and degeneracies, include sensitivity analyses and compute-cost reporting, add empirical/benchmark validation, and discuss extensions to 2AFC/multi-cue settings.  
- Overall evaluation: mathematically elegant and practically motivated framework worth accepting if authors better clarify novelty, literature placement, and limits of identifiability (especially sensory vs decision vs motor noise).
