- Paper addresses identifiability in Bayesian observer models and proposes an a priori framework to test parameter recoverability before experiments.  
- Framework comprises a flexible parametric class of observer models and a KL-divergence–based metric using expected log-likelihood to quantify model distinguishability.  
- Demonstrated on two case studies: time-interval estimation (prior recovery sensitive to sensory vs. motor noise; recommend measuring motor noise independently).  
- Second case study re-evaluates the slow-speed prior in vision and finds inference of that prior and key noise parameters is theoretically robust.  
- Fails to engage with extensive prior literature on model comparison and identifiability (e.g., Myung, Pitt, Navarro), leaving unclear comparisons in power and scope.  
- Strength: tackles a fundamental, timely problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling that grows with model complexity.  
- Strength: novel emphasis on a priori analysis to guide experimental design and save resources.  
- Strength: technically sound model family and KL-based identifiability metric that avoids costly full-data simulations.  
- Strength: clear writing and illustrative case studies that yield concrete experimental recommendations.  
- Weakness: reliance on specific parametric assumptions (e.g., mixture Gaussians) raises concerns about robustness if true processes lie outside the model class.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification and motivation for chosen functional forms (e.g., the form of p_meas in Eq.3).  
- Minor: explain why the degeneracy is a “trivial mathematical fact” for broader audience.  
- Minor: correct phrasing at Line 73 ("nor" → "or") and clarify Figure 2 caption about star vs. dashed line denoting “true value.”  
- Conclusion: valuable, well-developed a priori identifiability framework but needs to situate itself in existing literature and analyze sensitivity to parametric assumptions before acceptance.
