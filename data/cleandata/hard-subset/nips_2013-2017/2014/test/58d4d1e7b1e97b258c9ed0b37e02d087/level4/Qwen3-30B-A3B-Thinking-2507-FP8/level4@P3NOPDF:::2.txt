

The manuscript addresses a critical challenge in cognitive modeling—model identifiability—with a theoretically grounded approach. The writing is clear and the methodology demonstrates genuine innovation, placing the work above the threshold for NeurIPS. However, the core claims rely on an extensive set of assumptions whose necessity and justification require deeper scrutiny, particularly given the incremental nature of the empirical results. Below, I detail key concerns that must be addressed to strengthen the contribution.

The most significant issue is the reliance on strong, often implicit, assumptions. The success of the Bayesian framework hinges critically on the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), which imposes a stringent constraint on representational structure. As noted in line 134, this transform is motivated by psychophysical laws, but the work effectively posits that identifiability is achieved *only* under this specific transformation. The authors should explicitly clarify whether identifiability fails without it, and whether the transform is justified by empirical evidence or theoretical preference. This is not merely a technical detail; it fundamentally shapes the paper’s broader claim that Bayesian models can be identifiable with sufficient constraints.

The assumptions extend to the stimulus representation. Line 106 states the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, yet it is treated as a continuous variable in the model. The authors should clarify this discrepancy—whether it reflects experimental design constraints (e.g., discrete stimulus levels in psychophysical tasks) or a modeling choice—and justify why the discrete sampling does not compromise the continuous inference framework. Similarly, line 158 raises a critical point: why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over uniform, given that the prior is 50% wider than the true stimulus range? The justification for this prior must be grounded in the experimental context or theoretical principles, not assumed.

Methodologically, several points require clarification. In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is presented as a result, yet this follows directly from the model structure (with both noise sources included). The authors should either justify the inclusion of both noise terms or clarify why their interchangeability is non-trivial. More importantly, they should report which observed condition differences are statistically reliable—box plots alone are insufficient for assessing significance. In Section 4.2, the claim that the model recovers a slow-speed prior depends critically on the reference observer *having* that prior. The authors must demonstrate that the model can *detect* the absence of such a prior (e.g., by testing against an observer without a slow-speed bias), as this is essential for validating the method’s utility in real-world scenarios.

Technical concerns also warrant attention. Line 143 questions whether the constraints in Equation 4 and differential entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal distribution for Equation 3. The authors should explicitly address whether bimodality is possible under their framework and how it would impact inference. Line 252 notes that Equation 14’s validity depends on specific conditions for Stirling’s approximation and the approximation \(E[x \log x] \approx E[x] \log E[x]\). The authors should quantify these conditions (e.g., minimum \(N\), required signal-to-noise ratios) and clarify when the approximations hold. Equation 15, derived after abandoning priors on \(\theta\) and making these approximations, appears justified largely by its intuitive appeal rather than rigorous derivation. The authors should either strengthen the theoretical basis or temper claims about its generality.

Figure 1 is confusingly structured. The separation of Figure 1a and 1b obscures relationships, and the inclusion of \(p_{\text{est}}(s^*|x)\) as a derived inference is redundant with the core visualizations. Integrating Figure 1b into 1a and omitting \(p_{\text{est}}(s^*|x)\) would significantly improve clarity. Line 262’s phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous—does it refer to the dataset distribution or a posterior? This must be specified.

Finally, while the 2AFC analysis (line 365) is innovative, the claim that "learning of priors does not matter" seems overstated. The model assumes priors are fixed, but in realistic 2AFC tasks, prior learning could influence behavior. The authors should either justify this simplification or acknowledge its limitations.

In summary, the paper’s core innovation—leveraging strong representational constraints to achieve identifiability—is valuable but underexplored. The current presentation overstates the generality of results by conflating methodological assumptions with theoretical claims. Addressing the points above would clarify the scope of the contribution, strengthen the empirical validation, and better align the results with the paper’s ambitious claims. The work is promising but requires deeper justification of its foundational assumptions to fully realize its potential. With revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the field.
