- Introduces a systematic framework for testing identifiability in Bayesian observer models, addressing experimental non-identifiability.  
- Formalizes degeneracies in the inverse mapping from behavior to priors, likelihoods, and loss functions, and proposes a priori identifiability evaluation.  
- Leverages a flexible Bayesian observer model class spanning sensory, perceptual, decision, and response stages with tractable computations.  
- Enables efficient exploration of parameter identifiability using KL-divergence-based metrics and expected log-likelihood as a proxy for model discrimination.  
- Case study 1 (interval timing): shows motor parameter measurement and avoiding overly narrow (MAP-like) loss functions improve prior recovery.  
- Case study 2 (speed perception): validates robust inference of slow-speed priors.  
- Strength — significance: directly addresses a known, under-addressed problem and provides a practical tool for reliable recovery of internal representations.  
- Strength — clarity & rigor: precise mathematical formulations and logically sound identifiability methodology, with effective illustrative results.  
- Strength — originality: novel systematic, a priori identifiability framework and design-oriented approach distinct from prior model-fitting work.  
- Weakness — terminology: conflates mathematical identifiability with experimental identifiability; needs explicit distinction.  
- Recommendation: add a brief paragraph clarifying the difference between mathematical and experimental identifiability.  
- Weakness — novelty nuance: perceived as incremental relative to prior architectures; needs clearer comparison to prior work (e.g., Acerbi et al., Stocker & Simoncelli).  
- Recommendation: add a "Comparison to Prior Approaches" subsection and a table summarizing key differences (identifiability analysis vs. model fitting).  
- Minor clarity: better contextualize the MAP experimental variant’s degradation and link loss-function non-identifiability to prior recovery errors.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, technically sound, generalizable, and practically valuable; recommendation to accept with minor clarifications.
