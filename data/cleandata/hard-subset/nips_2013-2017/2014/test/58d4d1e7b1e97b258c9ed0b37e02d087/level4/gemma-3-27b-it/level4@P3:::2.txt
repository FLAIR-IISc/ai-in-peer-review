## Review of “A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception”

This manuscript addresses the critical issue of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing concern as these models are increasingly used to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability, with the potential to improve experimental design. The paper is generally well-written and clear, presenting a complex methodology in a reasonably accessible manner. While the framework relies on a substantial number of assumptions – which limits the generalizability of the conclusions – the innovative methodology and potential impact justify acceptance to NIPS, albeit with minor revisions.

The core idea is to assess how easily different parameter settings can generate similar behavioral predictions. The authors develop a continuous class of Bayesian observer models and then utilize a Kullback-Leibler divergence-based approach to quantify the average support for alternative models given data generated by a reference observer. This allows for a principled exploration of the identifiability landscape. The application to time interval estimation and speed perception provides concrete examples of the framework’s utility. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is fundamental to the validity of inferences drawn from Bayesian models of perception. Addressing this is a significant contribution.
*   **Novel Framework:** The proposed method provides a structured way to analyze identifiability *a priori*, potentially guiding experimental design and interpretation of results.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical development appears rigorous, building upon existing Bayesian machinery. The use of KL divergence as a measure of model distinguishability is appropriate.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity of the topic, the paper is generally well-written and organized, though some sections could benefit from streamlining (see Clarity comments below).
*   **Demonstration with Case Studies:** Applying the framework to established paradigms (time and speed perception) strengthens the paper and demonstrates its potential utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption Dependence:** The framework’s results are heavily reliant on the specific assumptions embedded within the observer model (e.g., the functional form of the prior, the sensory mapping, the loss function). The authors acknowledge this, but the strength of the conclusions is directly tied to the plausibility of these assumptions.
*   **Limited Impact of Results:** While the methodology is valuable, the specific findings of the case studies are somewhat limited. For example, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in the time interval estimation task is not entirely surprising. 
*   **Lack of Ground Truth Recovery Demonstration:** In the speed perception case study, it's crucial to demonstrate the framework’s ability to correctly identify the absence of a slow-speed prior when the true model does *not* have one.  Without this, the positive result regarding prior recovery feels incomplete.
*   **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1, in particular, is dense and difficult to interpret, hindering understanding of the model architecture.



**Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

*   **Line 106:** Clarify why a discrete stimulus distribution is chosen when the underlying variable is continuous. Addressing whether this is solely a reflection of typical experimental designs would be helpful.
*   **Line 134:** Emphasize the strong constraint imposed by the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2). Acknowledge that the success of the work may be significantly influenced by this assumption and frame it as a limitation/area for future exploration. The manuscript currently implies that this is just a convenience for the psychophysical laws it can approximate.
*   **Line 143:**  Clarify whether the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy *guarantee* a unimodal distribution, or simply encourage it. A more definitive statement would strengthen the argument.
*   **Line 158:** Provide a stronger justification for the assumption of a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range. What theoretical or empirical basis supports this choice?
*   **Figure 1:** Reconsider the presentation of Figure 1. Integrating 1b into 1a would improve clarity. Removing `p_est(s^*|x)` might streamline the diagram, as it’s a derived inference.
*   **Lines 252-262:** Address the conditions under which the approximations used to derive Equation 15 are valid (e.g., applicability of Stirling’s approximation). While the resulting rule for assessing prediction similarity is intuitive, a more rigorous justification would be appreciated. Clarify which density is being sampled from on line 262.
*   **Line 365:**  Expand on why prior learning might be less relevant in a 2AFC task. Is it due to the task structure or the specific implementation in the models?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The manuscript addresses a significant and increasingly relevant problem in computational cognitive science.
*   The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and provides a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models.
*   The case studies demonstrate the applicability of the framework, even if the results are not groundbreaking.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The framework’s results are heavily dependent on a substantial number of assumptions.
*   The impact of the specific findings presented is limited.
*   Some aspects of the presentation (e.g., Figure 1) could be improved.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The methodological contribution is substantial, and the paper addresses an important issue. Addressing the comments regarding the assumptions, providing a more complete demonstration in the speed perception case study (showing recovery of a flat prior), and improving figure clarity would further strengthen the manuscript.  I believe that the potential benefit to the NIPS community outweighs the current limitations.
