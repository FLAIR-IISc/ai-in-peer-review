

The paper introduces a systematic framework for testing identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception, addressing a critical gap in the field. It formalizes how degeneracies in the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can undermine model recovery, and proposes a method to evaluate identifiability *a priori* for experimental design. The framework leverages a flexible Bayesian observer model class (covering sensory, perceptual, decision, and response stages) with tractable computations, enabling efficient exploration of parameter identifiability via KL-divergence-based metrics. Two case studies demonstrate its utility: (1) optimizing experimental designs for interval timing tasks (showing that motor parameter measurement and avoiding overly narrow loss functions improve prior recovery), and (2) validating robust inference of slow-speed priors in speed perception.

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality & Significance (Core Contribution):** The work directly addresses a well-known but unaddressed problem in perceptual modeling: the *experimental* non-identifiability of model components (e.g., prior vs. likelihood vs. loss function). The framework provides a practical tool for designing experiments that reliably recover internal representations, advancing the field beyond "black-box" model fitting. The case studies are well-executed, demonstrating non-trivial insights (e.g., the counterintuitive detriment of MAP-like loss functions in interval timing).  
- **Clarity & Rigor:** The model is clearly defined with precise mathematical formulations (Eqs. 2–10), and the identifiability methodology (Section 3) is logically sound, using expected log-likelihood as a proxy for model discrimination. The results (Fig. 2–3) effectively illustrate how experimental design choices impact parameter recovery.  
- **Originality (Contextualized):** While Bayesian models of perception are established (e.g., [5, 7, 9, 12, 24]), prior work *lacks systematic identifiability analysis* for experimental design. The authors correctly note that approaches like Bayesian transfer [5] or constraints on likelihood [21] are ad hoc. This paper’s *systematic framework*—enabling *a priori* comparison of experimental designs—is novel. It builds on Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) but extends beyond their focus on *model fitting* to *design validation* for identifiability, a distinct contribution.  

**Weaknesses & Recommendations:**  
- **Clarity on Identifiability Terminology:** The draft review conflates *mathematical identifiability* (the paper’s focus) with *experimental identifiability* (e.g., distinguishing sensory vs. response noise). The paper correctly frames its contribution as solving *mathematical identifiability* under the model’s constraints (e.g., via motor parameter measurement in Section 4.1). However, the manuscript should explicitly clarify this distinction to avoid confusion. *Recommendation:* Add a brief paragraph in Section 1 or 5 distinguishing:  
  > "Our framework addresses *mathematical identifiability*—whether model parameters can be uniquely recovered *given the model structure and experimental constraints*. It does not resolve *experimental identifiability* (e.g., separating sensory from response noise), which requires additional empirical manipulations."  
- **Originality Nuance:** While the model architecture shares elements with prior work (e.g., Acerbi et al. [12, 14]), the *identifiability framework* is the novel core. The draft review’s "incremental" claim overlooks that prior papers *did not provide a method for *a priori* identifiability assessment*. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should:  
  *   Add a new subsection (e.g., "Comparison to Prior Approaches") contrasting their framework with existing models (e.g., Acerbi et al. [12, 14], Stocker & Simoncelli [9]). A table summarizing key differences (e.g., "Focus: Identifiability analysis vs. Model fitting") would clarify novelty.  
  *   Emphasize that their method is *general* (applies to any Bayesian observer model in the class) and *design-oriented* (enables ranking of experimental setups), unlike ad hoc solutions in the literature.  
- **Minor Clarity:** In Section 4.1, the "MAP" experimental variant’s degradation (Fig. 2d) could be better contextualized. *Suggestion:* Explicitly state: "The loss function’s non-identifiability (σ` weakly constrained) directly propagates to prior recovery errors, demonstrating why *a priori* identifiability testing is essential."  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, technically sound paper with significant practical value for the perceptual modeling community. It advances the field by providing a *methodological tool* to avoid unreliable inferences from Bayesian models—a persistent issue highlighted by critiques like [17, 18]. The contribution is **not merely incremental**; it solves a specific, well-documented problem (lack of identifiability analysis) with a generalizable framework. The case studies demonstrate clear utility, and the model’s efficiency enables broader application.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept.** The paper meets all NIPS criteria:  
- *Quality:* Rigorous theoretical foundation, validated via two case studies.  
- *Clarity:* Well-structured, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- *Originality:* Novel framework for identifiability testing (distinct from prior modeling work).  
- *Significance:* Directly addresses a barrier to trustworthy Bayesian modeling in perception.  

**Summary of Pro/Con:**  
- **Pro:** Solves a critical, unsolved problem (identifiability in experimental design); provides actionable tool for researchers; technically robust; case studies demonstrate practical impact.  
- **Con:** Terminology on identifiability needs tightening; minor clarification needed on novelty relative to prior models (easily addressed in revision).  

The authors should revise to explicitly distinguish identifiability types and clarify the framework’s novelty versus existing Bayesian models. With these minor clarifications, the paper will be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
