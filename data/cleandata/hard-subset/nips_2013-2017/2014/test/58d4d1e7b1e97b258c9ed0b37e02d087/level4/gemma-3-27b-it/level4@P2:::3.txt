This paper presents a Bayesian observer model for perception, aiming for a balance between robustness and computational tractability. The model integrates sensory noise, likelihoods, priors, loss functions, and response distributions within a mathematically convenient framework. A key focus is on the identifiability of model parameters – the ability to reliably estimate them from data.

The paper’s strength lies in its elegant mathematical formulation and its potential applicability to a wide range of psychophysical tasks. The authors successfully demonstrate the framework’s utility with case studies in time interval estimation and speed perception. The emphasis on *a priori* identifiability analysis is valuable, offering a method to improve experimental design and assess the reliability of recovered internal representations. 

However, the contribution, while solid, appears incremental. The work builds upon a substantial body of existing research applying Bayesian methods to perception, particularly those appearing in journals like *PLoS Computational Biology*. Several previous models share a similar spirit and methodological approach. For instance, Acerbi et al. (2012, *PLoS Computational Biology*) investigated internal representations of temporal statistics, and Acerbi et al. (2014, *PLoS Computational Biology*) explored the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. While the current submission is not a direct replication, its similarities to these and other prior works necessitate a clearer articulation of its novel advancements. 

**I recommend the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section thoroughly reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception.** This section should explicitly position the current work within the existing literature, highlighting the specific ways in which it extends, improves upon, or diverges from prior approaches. A detailed comparison will strengthen the paper’s perceived originality and impact.

Furthermore, the paper’s emphasis on mathematical identifiability warrants further clarification. While the model’s parameters are identifiable *given its assumptions*, this does not necessarily resolve broader identifiability challenges in perceptual science. For example, the model, despite its sophistication, might not be able to definitively distinguish between errors originating from sensory noise, decision processes, or motor responses.  The authors should address this point directly, acknowledging the limitations of their framework and discussing how it relates to the more general problem of inferring underlying cognitive mechanisms. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a mathematically elegant and potentially broadly applicable framework for modeling perceptual estimation.
*   Offers a novel method for *a priori* identifiability analysis, allowing for informed experimental design.
*   Demonstrates the framework's utility through well-chosen case studies.
*   Addresses an important issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the potential for non-identifiability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core contribution feels incremental, building upon a substantial existing literature.
*   The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recently published Bayesian models of perception.
*   The concept of “identifiability” requires nuanced discussion; mathematical identifiability does not solve all inference problems in perceptual science.



**Overall:** This is a solid and well-executed paper. Addressing the concerns regarding novelty and clarifying the limitations of the framework’s identifiability analysis would substantially strengthen the manuscript and increase its potential impact. I lean towards acceptance, contingent upon addressing these points in a revision.
