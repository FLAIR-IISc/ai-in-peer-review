In this article, the authors propose a framework for comparing Bayesian models based on behavioral data. To do this, they outline a summary of the Bayesian decision theory framework, identify non-identifiable fields, and highlight the types of constraints that can be used to make each term in the Bayesian framework identifiable. They then make some assumptions to constrain each term in the Bayesian framework, investigate how differentiable the parameter values are in their model, and apply this technique in two studies to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issues of internal representation and the identifiability of processes have been a significant problem in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when representation patterns can imitate each other, proving that they cannot be distinguished without some constraints on the processes (when the representations create equivalent differences to their inputs).

In recent years, this has been one of the main flaws of the Bayesian framework that has hindered its widespread adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 - the latter directly mentions motion perception as an example).

Although the article touches on a theoretically very important topic, is well-written, and generally does so reasonably and effectively, I have two main objections to it. Firstly, it ignores the previous large literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, which makes it difficult to properly evaluate its contribution. The general framework for comparing models proposed by the authors is not very different from some previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myong, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myong, 2004). Secondly, the authors make some assumptions that sometimes seem arbitrary, and how much of their framework depends on them is unclear. For example, why should $p_{meas}$ be defined by equation 3? Is defining the values of the parameters in equation 12 really enough to define and capture all the characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? I understand that this can express a large class of powerful and sensitive models. However, it is not clear that this is sufficient justification for the assumptions, and at least, the article should discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models so that they are identifiable.

Small comments:
Line 53: "This is a trivial mathematical truth..." It might be more helpful to explain why it is true than to declare it trivial. It may not be trivial to all readers.
Line 73: I think "or" should be used instead of the second "nor".
Figure 2: What do the stars and dashed lines mean? From the caption, it seems they both mean real values.
