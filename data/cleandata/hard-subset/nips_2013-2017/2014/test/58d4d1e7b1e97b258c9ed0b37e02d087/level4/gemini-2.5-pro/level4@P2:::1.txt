This paper addresses the critical and often overlooked problem of model identifiability within the Bayesian framework for modeling perception. The authors argue that because multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, claims about recovering an observer's internal representations from data are potentially ill-posed. To address this, they propose a systematic framework to test the identifiability of Bayesian observer models *a priori*. The core of their contribution is a flexible, parametric class of observer models and an efficient method based on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence to explore the "identifiability landscape" via MCMC sampling. They apply this framework to two case studies: (1) a time interval estimation task, where they compare different experimental designs to see which best constrains the inference of the observer's prior, and (2) a speed perception task, where they re-examine the robustness of the well-known "slow-speed prior" finding.

This work connects to a long-standing and fundamental issue in cognitive science regarding the non-identifiability of internal representations and processes (e.g., Anderson, 1978). More recently, the problem has been highlighted as a major challenge for the "Bayesian brain" hypothesis, with critics arguing that the flexibility of the framework can lead to "just-so stories" that lack explanatory power (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). This paper provides a concrete, computational approach to begin addressing these important critiques.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance. The issue of non-identifiability is a major hurdle for making meaningful scientific claims based on fitting Bayesian models to behavioral data. Providing a tool to assess and mitigate this issue is a significant contribution to the fields of computational neuroscience and cognitive science.

2.  **Practical Utility:** The framework is not merely a theoretical exercise. The first case study on time interval estimation demonstrates how the method can be used as a practical tool to improve experimental design. By comparing different setups (e.g., number of conditions, assumed loss function), the authors show how one might prospectively choose a design that is more likely to yield identifiable parameters. This could be very valuable for researchers in the field.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the motivation for the work is laid out very clearly. The four-stage observer model is explained methodically, and the logic behind using the expected log-likelihood (and thus the KL divergence) as a measure of identifiability is sound.

4.  **Quality of Case Studies:** The two case studies are well-chosen. The first illustrates how the framework can reveal potential weaknesses in an experimental design and identify specific parameter degeneracies (e.g., between sensory and motor noise). The second provides a valuable theoretical validation for a classic result in the literature, showing that the inference of a slow-speed prior is relatively robust.

**Weaknesses / Arguments for Rejection:**

1.  **Limited Engagement with Prior Literature on Model Identifiability:** The primary weakness is the paper's limited engagement with the extensive prior literature on model comparison, parameter recovery, and identifiability from mathematical psychology and cognitive science. The general problem of assessing model distinguishability has been formally studied for decades, leading to a variety of methods (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Without situating their work in this context, it is difficult to evaluate the novelty of the proposed method (Eq. 12-13) relative to existing techniques for assessing parameter identifiability. A thorough discussion of how their approach relates to, and potentially improves upon, these prior methods is necessary.

2.  **Justification of Modeling Assumptions:** A second concern relates to the justification for several key modeling assumptions. For example, the likelihood (`p_meas`) and loss function are both parameterized as a mixture of two Gaussians (Eq. 3 and 6). The authors state this is for computational convenience and flexibility, which is reasonable. However, it is unclear how sensitive the conclusions of the identifiability analysis are to these specific parametric forms. The framework is presented as a general tool, but its conclusions might be heavily dependent on these specific, and somewhat arbitrary, choices. The paper would be much stronger if it included a discussion of this limitation or a sensitivity analysis showing that the main conclusions are robust to alternative functional forms.

**Minor Comments & Suggestions:**

*   The abstract states the framework is for "a significant class of Bayesian observer models," and the paper similarly focuses on one-dimensional continuous estimation tasks. It would be helpful to briefly discuss the challenges in extending this framework to other common paradigms, such as discrete-choice tasks (e.g., 2AFC), to better frame the scope of the contribution.
*   Figure 2: The caption and text should clarify the meaning of the symbols. It appears both the star and the dashed lines denote the "true value," which is confusing. Please specify what each represents.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This is a well-written paper that proposes a valuable and timely framework for addressing the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The method is clearly explained and its practical utility is convincingly demonstrated through two relevant case studies. However, the paper's contribution is currently weakened by its failure to connect with the substantial body of existing work on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science, making it difficult to assess its originality. Furthermore, the reliance on specific parametric forms without sufficient justification or sensitivity analysis raises questions about the generality of the findings.

I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to NIPS. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address these major points, particularly by situating their framework within the broader literature on model identifiability and by better justifying their core modeling assumptions.
