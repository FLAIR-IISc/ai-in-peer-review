The manuscript addresses the crucial issue of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing concern as these models are increasingly used to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The work is clearly written and well-motivated, presenting a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability. While the approach relies on a substantial set of assumptions, the methodology is innovative and the results, though not groundbreaking, are above the threshold for NIPS presentation.

The core idea is to develop a framework to *a priori* assess whether a given Bayesian observer model, and its parameters, can be reliably recovered from behavioral data given a specific experimental design. This is achieved through a sophisticated analysis of the likelihood surface, approximating expected posterior densities using KL divergence. The paper demonstrates this framework with two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. 

In the time interval estimation case (Section 4.1), the finding that sensory and motor noise are difficult to disentangle is not entirely surprising, though the formalization within the proposed framework is valuable. The presentation of results would be strengthened by explicitly highlighting statistically reliable differences between conditions. The speed perception analysis (Section 4.2) demonstrates the framework's ability to validate existing findings – namely, the robustness of inferences about a slow-speed prior – but importantly, doesn’t demonstrate the ability to *disprove* a prior when one doesn’t exist. Establishing this negative control would significantly strengthen the conclusions.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The issue of model identifiability is a fundamental concern for Bayesian cognitive modeling, and this work tackles it head-on.
* **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework provides a systematic, principled approach to assess identifiability *a priori*, offering a valuable tool for experimental design.
* **Clear Writing:** The manuscript is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the complexity of the topic.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical formulation and analysis appear sound, demonstrating a strong technical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Strong Assumptions:** The framework relies on numerous assumptions about the form of priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and noise distributions. While necessary for tractability, the reliance on these assumptions limits the generality of the results and raises the question of how sensitive the conclusions are to violations of these assumptions. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Results:** The findings, while insightful, are not transformative. The identification of the interplay between sensory and motor noise in time interval estimation, and the validation of the slow-speed prior in speed perception, are interesting, but do not represent a paradigm shift.
* **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1 is somewhat convoluted and could be simplified. Integrating 1b into 1a and removing unnecessary elements (like *p<sub>est</sub>(s*|x)*) would improve comprehension.
* **Approximations & Justification:** Some of the mathematical approximations (e.g., around lines 252 and 262) lack sufficient justification and could benefit from further explanation. The reliance on intuition to justify Equation 15 feels somewhat weak.



**Specific Comments:**

* **Line 106:** Clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, given its continuous nature. Is this purely a consequence of experimental design?
* **Line 134:** Emphasize that the logarithmic stimulus transform is a strong constraint, and that the success of the framework may largely depend on its inclusion. This frames the work as demonstrating identifiability *given* these strong representational assumptions.
* **Line 143:** Explain how the constraints in Eq. 4 and differential entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal distribution for Eqn. 3.
* **Line 158:** Justify the assumption of a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range. What prior knowledge supports this choice?
* **Line 252:** Provide conditions under which the approximations used in Equation 14 are valid (e.g., conditions for Stirling’s approximation).
* **Line 262:** Specify which density is being sampled from.
* **Line 365:** Address why prior learning is assumed to be irrelevant in the 2AFC task.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The work addresses a significant and timely problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
* The proposed framework is novel, theoretically sound, and provides a valuable tool for researchers.
* The writing is clear and the manuscript is well-organized.
* The technical contribution is sufficient to warrant presentation at NIPS.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The results are incremental and do not represent a major advance in the field.
* The strong assumptions underlying the framework limit its generality and applicability.
* The sensitivity analysis is limited, leaving open the question of how robust the conclusions are.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Addressing the concerns regarding the justification of approximations, the clarification of assumptions, and the inclusion of a negative control in the speed perception experiment would significantly strengthen the manuscript. The work is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a much-needed framework for tackling the challenging problem of model identifiability.
