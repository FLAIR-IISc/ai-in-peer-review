The manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The work is well-written and generally clear. However, the methodology relies on a substantial list of assumptions, and the impact of the key results feels somewhat limited given the complexity of the framework. Despite this, the innovative nature of the methodology leads me to judge the work as above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding key results: In section 4.1, the observed interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising, and I questioned the need to incorporate both into the model as I was reading. It would be helpful to indicate which differences between conditions were statistically reliable. (Perhaps the presentation of results could be clarified with more explicit statistical comparisons.) In section 4.2, it's essential to demonstrate that the model can correctly identify the absence of a slow-speed prior when the true observer does not possess one. 

Specific comments:

Line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is inherently a continuous variable? I assume this reflects the typical design of psychophysical experiments, where stimulus level serves as the independent variable. The authors might clarify this point.

Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. It appears the success of this work may largely stem from incorporating this transform. This suggests that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations within the mind.

Line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that Eq. 3 will fit a unimodal distribution? It seems a bimodal distribution could, in principle, result from the fit.

Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over a uniform representation? What prior knowledge about the true stimulus distribution justifies a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?

Figure 1: Even after careful review, I find Figure 1 more confusing than illuminating. It might be clearer to integrate Figure 1b into Figure 1a and remove *p<sub>est</sub>(s<sup>\*</sup>|x)* from the figure, as it’s a derived quantity.

Line 252: Can the authors comment on the conditions on *N* that make Equation 14 approximately correct – specifically, when Stirling’s approximation of the factorial holds and under what circumstances *E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]*? By the point where the authors arrive at Equation 15—having abandoned priors on *θ* and made several approximations—I believe Equation 15 is justified primarily by its intuitive nature in assessing the similarity of predictions.

Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density are you referring to? The dataset distribution?

Line 365: Why doesn't prior learning matter in the 2AFC task? 

Overall, this is an innovative approach to an important problem, although the results are less than transformative.
