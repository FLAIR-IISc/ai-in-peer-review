This paper presents a Bayesian framework for modeling perception, specifying the mathematical formulation for sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and the response distribution. A key merit of this model is that it successfully combines robustness with mathematical tractability. That is, the framework is flexible enough to handle diverse conditions (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while remaining computationally efficient for inference. The authors also emphasize the model's identifiability, noting that parameter estimates can be evaluated and distinguished.

I am supportive of this paper's acceptance to the conference. The model provides a theoretically principled and elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation across a range of experimental paradigms. Nevertheless, I have some concerns. My primary reservation is that the proposed model aligns closely in spirit and methodology with several other models recently published in the literature (e.g., in PLoS Computational Biology), which suggests the novel contribution here is incremental. As examples, readers might consider:

*   Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology, 8*(11), e1002771.
*   Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology, 10*(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is distinct from these and other prior articles, the conceptual overlap is significant enough to warrant characterizing the contribution as an incremental advance. I suggest the authors revise the manuscript to include a new section that reviews recently proposed Bayesian perceptual models. This section should clearly articulate how the currently proposed model constitutes a significant advance over these previously published approaches.

A second point concerns the emphasis on model identifiability. While the model is indeed mathematically identifiable, this property arises from its specific assumptions. The term "identifiable" can be interpreted in other, more practical ways. For instance, the model in its current form cannot be used to unambiguously determine whether an experimental subject's behavioral errors stem from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Consequently, one could argue that the model does not address the core identifiability challenge that is a central concern for most perceptual scientists. A discussion of this point should be included in the revised manuscript.
