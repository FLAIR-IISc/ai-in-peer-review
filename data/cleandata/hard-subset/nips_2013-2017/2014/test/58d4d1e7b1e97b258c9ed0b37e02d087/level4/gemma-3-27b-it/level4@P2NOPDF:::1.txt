This paper proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors effectively summarize Bayesian Decision Theory, identify potential issues of non-identifiability, and outline constraints to achieve identifiability for each component of the Bayesian framework. They then explore the differentiability of their model’s parameters and demonstrate its application through re-analysis of two established examples: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The problem of identifying internal representations and processes has been a longstanding challenge in cognitive science and psychology. Landmark work, such as Anderson (1978), demonstrated that different representational formats can produce indistinguishable behavior without process-level constraints. More recently, limitations in identifiability have been recognized as a significant barrier to the wider adoption of Bayesian models in the field (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). 

While the paper addresses a theoretically important issue and presents its arguments clearly and logically, I have two primary concerns. First, the work largely overlooks existing literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. This omission hinders a proper assessment of the contribution’s novelty. The proposed framework shares considerable overlap with previous approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). A more thorough engagement with this literature is needed. Second, several of the authors’ assumptions appear somewhat arbitrary, and the extent to which the framework’s validity hinges on these assumptions remains unclear. For instance, the justification for defining $p_{meas}$ using equation 3 is not fully explained. Similarly, it’s unclear whether defining parameter values in Equation 12 is sufficient to fully capture the complexity inherent in cognitive and perceptual processes. While the authors acknowledge the model’s ability to represent a broad class of sensory models, this does not inherently justify these specific assumptions. A more detailed discussion and rigorous justification of these constraints are crucial.

Regarding minor points:

* Line 53: The statement "It is a trivial mathematical fact…" would be more helpful if expanded to *explain* why the fact holds, rather than simply asserting its triviality. Not all readers may share this assessment.
* Line 73: The second instance of "nor" likely should be "or."
* Figure 2: The figure legend should clarify the distinction, if any, between the star and dashed lines, as they both currently appear to represent the true value.
