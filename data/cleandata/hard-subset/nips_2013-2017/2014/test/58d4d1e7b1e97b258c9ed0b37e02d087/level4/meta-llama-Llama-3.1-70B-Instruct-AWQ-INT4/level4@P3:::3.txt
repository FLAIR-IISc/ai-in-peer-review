This paper proposes a Bayesian model of perception, which combines robustness and mathematical convenience. The model can handle various types of situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining computational tractability. The authors emphasize the model's identifiability, allowing for easy evaluation and distinction between parameter estimates.

The paper's strengths include its mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in many experiments. However, the novel contribution of this paper is incremental, building upon similar models that have recently appeared in the literature, such as those published in PLoS Computational Biology (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014). While the current submission is not identical to these earlier articles, it is similar enough to be considered an incremental contribution.

To strengthen the manuscript, the authors should consider adding a new section that reviews recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly explains why their model represents a significant advance over previously published models. This would help to contextualize the contribution of the paper and address potential concerns about its novelty.

Furthermore, the authors should clarify the concept of identifiability in their model. While the model is mathematically identifiable, this identifiability is based on the mathematical assumptions of the model. However, the model may not be able to distinguish between different sources of experimental errors, such as sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. The authors should comment on this aspect of identifiability and its implications for perceptual scientists.

Overall, the paper provides a solid foundation for a Bayesian model of perception, but its contribution is incremental, and the authors should address the concerns mentioned above to strengthen their manuscript.
