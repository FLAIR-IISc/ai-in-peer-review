The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, specifying mathematical formulations for sensory noise, the likelihood function, the prior distribution, the loss function, and the response distribution. A key strength is that it combines robustness with mathematical convenience: the model accommodates a range of situations (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while keeping inference tractable. The paper also emphasizes that the model is identifiable, in the sense that competing parameter estimates can be readily evaluated and distinguished.

I like this paper and would like to see it accepted to the NIPS conference. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation across many experimental settings. That said, I have some reservations. The model is very much in the spirit of several recent contributions (often in PLoS Computational Biology), making the novelty primarily incremental. For example:
Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.
The current submission is not identical to these (or other) prior articles, but it is sufficiently similar that it is fair to characterize the contribution as incremental.

I recommend revising the manuscript to add a section that reviews recent Bayesian models of perception and clearly articulates why the present model constitutes a significant advance over previously published work.

One additional comment concerns identifiability. While the model is mathematically identifiable, this identifiability follows from its assumptions. The term “identifiable” is also used in a broader sense. For instance, the model cannot decisively determine whether a subject’s errors arise from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Thus, it does not resolve the identifiability issue that most perceptual scientists are concerned with. Please address this point in the revision.

In sum: a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but the contribution is incremental given closely related prior work.
