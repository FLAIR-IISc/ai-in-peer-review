- Paper tackles an important problem (model identifiability in cognitive modeling) and is well written.  
- Work rests on many strong assumptions, which seem large relative to the impact of the main results.  
- Methodology is interesting and innovative, and reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Section 4.1 result (sensory vs motor noise interchangeability) is unsurprising; authors should mark statistical significance in comparisons.  
- Section 4.2 needs a control showing the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior when the reference observer lacks it.  
- Line 106: clarify why a discrete stimulus distribution is used when the stimulus is continuous.  
- Line 134: the log-stimulus transform may strongly constrain inference, implying identifiability hinges on strong, pre-specified representational assumptions.  
- Line 143: question whether the constraints and entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal distribution or could produce bimodality.  
- Line 158: justify why the observer’s prior favors a lattice over uniform and why it is set 50% wider than the true stimulus range.  
- Figure 1 is confusing; consider integrating panels and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) to improve clarity.  
- Line 252: request conditions for validity of approximations (Stirling’s approximation, when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] holds) and stronger justification for Equation 15 after dropping priors.  
- Line 262: clarify what is meant by “sample from this unnormalized density” — specify which density (e.g., distribution over datasets).  
- Line 365: explain why learning of priors is deemed irrelevant in the 2AFC task.
