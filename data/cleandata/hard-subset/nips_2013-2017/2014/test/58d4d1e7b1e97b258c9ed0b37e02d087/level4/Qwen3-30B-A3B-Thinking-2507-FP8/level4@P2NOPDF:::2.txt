

The manuscript addresses a significant challenge in cognitive modeling: model identifiability. It is well-written and clear, and the methodology is innovative. However, the work relies on an extensive set of assumptions that must be accepted for the results to hold, and the scope of these assumptions appears disproportionate to the impact of the key findings. Despite this, the technical contribution merits consideration for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding key results:  
Section 4.1 presents the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise, which aligns with established intuition (the inclusion of both noise sources appears unnecessary and warrants justification). The authors should clarify which condition differences are statistically reliable, as the box plots do not sufficiently convey this.  
Section 4.2 raises a critical concern: the model’s ability to recover a slow-speed prior hinges on the reference observer possessing one. The authors should demonstrate that the model fails to recover this prior when the reference observer lacks it, as this is essential for validating the approach.  

Specific technical comments:  
- **Line 106**: The stimulus is modeled as discrete despite being a continuous variable. This likely reflects experimental design choices (where stimulus levels are discrete independent variables), but the authors should clarify this distinction to avoid confusion about the model’s assumptions.  
- **Line 134**: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. The success of the method appears heavily dependent on this transform, which imposes strong constraints on inference. Consequently, the core claim—that Bayesian models are identifiable—effectively reduces to "Bayesian models are identifiable *given specific representational transformations*." The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation.  
- **Line 143**: The constraints in Equation 4 and differential entropy maximization do not guarantee that Equation 3 yields a unimodal distribution. The authors should address whether bimodal solutions are possible under these constraints and how they are excluded.  
- **Line 158**: The observer’s prior favoring a lattice representation over uniform is unexplained. The justification for a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range requires clarification—what prior knowledge justifies this choice?  
- **Figure 1**: The figure is confusing. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a would improve clarity, and removing *p_est(s*|x) from the figure is advisable, as it is a derived inference rather than a primary observation.  
- **Line 252**: The validity of Equation 14 depends on specific conditions for Stirling’s approximation and the approximation *E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]*. The authors should specify the required sample size *N* and contextual conditions under which these approximations hold. By Equation 15, the model abandons priors on *θ* and employs questionable approximations; the derivation appears justified primarily by its intuitive outcome, not rigorous support.  
- **Line 262**: The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" lacks specificity. Which density is referenced (e.g., the dataset distribution or a posterior)? This should be clarified.  
- **Line 365**: The claim that prior learning "does not matter" in 2AFC tasks requires stronger justification. The authors should explain why prior learning is irrelevant in this context, as it contradicts standard assumptions in perceptual learning.  

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem with a novel approach. However, the results are modest relative to the extensive assumptions required. The methodology is interesting and technically sound, but the conclusions depend critically on the log-stimulus transform and the lattice prior assumption. Without stronger evidence for these constraints (e.g., Section 4.2’s missing validation), the impact is limited. The work meets the threshold for NIPS but would benefit from addressing these points to strengthen the argument. The authors should clarify the scope of their identifiability claim—specifically, that it applies only under their specific representational assumptions.  

In summary: **Innovative methodology addressing a key problem, but results are constrained by heavy assumptions. Acceptable for NIPS with minor revisions to clarify assumptions and strengthen validation.**
