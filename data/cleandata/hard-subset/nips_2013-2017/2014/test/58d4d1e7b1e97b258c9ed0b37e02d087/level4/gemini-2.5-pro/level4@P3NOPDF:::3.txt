This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception that specifies the mathematical forms for sensory noise, the likelihood function, the prior probability distribution, a loss function, and the resulting response distribution. A significant strength of this framework is its successful combination of robustness and mathematical convenience. The model is flexible enough to handle various experimental situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while remaining computationally tractable, allowing for efficient inference. The authors also place considerable emphasis on the mathematical identifiability of the model, noting that parameter estimates can be readily evaluated and distinguished.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that proposes a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in a wide range of experiments. I am supportive of its acceptance to the conference. However, I have two primary reservations that, if addressed, would significantly strengthen the manuscript and clarify its contribution to the field.

My main concern pertains to the novelty of the contribution. While the proposed model is elegant, it appears to be closely related in spirit and style to several recent Bayesian models of perception and decision-making. The framework and its constituent components bear a strong resemblance to other models that have recently appeared in the literature, for example:

*   Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 8(11), e1002771.
*   Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 10(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other related works, the conceptual and methodological overlap is substantial. Consequently, the contribution, as currently framed, could be perceived as incremental. To better situate the work and highlight its unique merits, I strongly suggest that the authors revise the manuscript to include a new section that reviews recently proposed Bayesian models of perception. In this section, they should explicitly delineate the unique contributions of their proposed framework and articulate precisely how it represents a significant advance over previously published models.

My second point concerns the paper's emphasis on model identifiability. The authors correctly state that the model's parameters are mathematically identifiable within the specific set of assumptions made. While this is true, the claim requires further qualification to avoid potential misunderstanding. The term "identifiability" carries different meanings in different contexts. From a practical, experimental perspective, a central challenge in perceptual science is dissociating various sources of behavioral variability—for instance, distinguishing errors that arise from sensory noise, suboptimal inference (or "decision noise"), and motor response noise. The proposed model, despite its mathematical identifiability, does not appear to resolve this more general and pressing identifiability problem with which most perceptual scientists are concerned. It would therefore be fair to say that the model does not fully address the core identifiability issues faced by the experimental community.

To clarify this, I recommend the authors add a discussion that acknowledges this limitation. A comment in the revised manuscript that clarifies the scope of their identifiability claim, and contrasts it with the broader challenge of identifying distinct noise sources in perceptual tasks, would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
