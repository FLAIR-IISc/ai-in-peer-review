- Addresses important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling; writing is clear and organized.  
- Relies on a large number of strong assumptions; list feels disproportionate to the impact of results.  
- Methodology is interesting and innovative; reviewer rates it above acceptance threshold.  
- Finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable (Section 4.1) is unsurprising; question inclusion of both noise types.  
- Need to indicate which condition differences are statistically reliable (box plots unclear).  
- Must show the model can recover the stimulus distribution when the reference observer lacks a slow-speed prior (Section 4.2).  
- Clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution when it is actually continuous (line 106).  
- The log-stimulus transform (Eq. 2) strongly constrains inference; identifiability may hinge on this representational assumption (line 134).  
- Do the constraints (Eq. 4) and differential entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fit for Eq. 3, or could a bimodal distribution arise? (line 143).  
- Justify why the observer’s prior should favor a lattice representation over a uniform one and the rationale for a prior 50% wider than the stimulus range (line 158).  
- Figure 1 is confusing; suggest integrating 1b into 1a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x) for clarity.  
- Request clarification on conditions for N that make Eq. 14 valid, and when Stirling’s approximation and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] hold (line 252).  
- Specify which “unnormalized density” is being sampled from in line 262 (e.g., dataset distribution?).  
- Explain why learning of priors appears unimportant in the 2AFC task; results feel less compelling than expected (line 365).
