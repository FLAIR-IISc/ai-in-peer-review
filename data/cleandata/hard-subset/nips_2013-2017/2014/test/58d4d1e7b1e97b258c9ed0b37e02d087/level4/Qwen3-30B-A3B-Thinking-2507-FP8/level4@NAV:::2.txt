

The manuscript addresses a significant challenge in cognitive modeling: model identifiability. It is well-written and clearly presented. However, the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions that require acceptance to interpret the results, and this list is lengthy relative to the impact of the key findings. Given the methodological innovation and interest, I judge the work to be above threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding key results:  
Section 4.1: The interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is expected (the inclusion of both in the model was questionable during reading). The authors should explicitly state which condition differences are statistically reliable.  
Section 4.2: It is essential to demonstrate whether the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer.  

Specific comments:  
Line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is a continuous variable? This likely stems from typical experimental design (where stimulus level is the independent variable), but clarification is warranted.  
Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, appears critical to the method's success. The results effectively demonstrate that Bayesian models are identifiable *only* under strong constraints imposed by such representational transformations.  
Line 143: Do the constraints in Equation 4 and differential entropy maximization guarantee that Equation 3 fits a unimodal distribution? A bimodal solution appears theoretically possible.  
Line 158: Why does the observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over uniform? What prior knowledge justifies a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range?  
Figure 1: The figure is confusing rather than illuminating. Integrating Figure 1b into 1a and removing *p_est(s^*|x)* (a derived inference) would improve clarity.  
Line 252: The authors should specify conditions on *N* for Equation 14’s validity (e.g., Stirling’s approximation and *E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]*). By the time Equation 15 is derived (after abandoning priors and making questionable approximations), its justification appears driven by yielding an intuitive similarity rule rather than theoretical rigor.  
Line 262: "Sample from this unnormalized density" – which density is referenced (e.g., dataset distribution)?  
Line 365: Why is prior learning irrelevant in 2AFC?  

In summary: Innovative approach, important problem, but results are less than overwhelming.
