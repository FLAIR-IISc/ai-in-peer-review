This paper proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models of behavioral data. The authors summarize the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identify key areas of non-identifiability, and outline the types of constraints needed to make each component of the framework identifiable. To demonstrate their approach, they introduce a set of specific assumptions to constrain the model, explore the distinguishability of parameter values under these constraints, and apply the resulting technique to two case studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The problem of identifiability for internal representations and processes has been a prominent issue in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed the conditions under which different representational formats can mimic each other, proving they are indistinguishable without constraints on the processes that operate on them (specifically, when the representations make equivalent distinctions among their inputs). More recently, the non-identifiability of model components has been highlighted as a major drawback of the Bayesian framework, hindering its widespread adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). The work by Bowers & Davis is particularly relevant, as it directly addresses speed perception, one of the examples used in the current paper.

While this article addresses a topic of great theoretical importance and is generally well-written and effective, I have two major reservations.

First, the paper largely ignores the extensive prior literature on model comparison and identifiability within cognitive science and psychology. This omission makes it difficult to properly evaluate the novelty and contribution of the proposed framework. The general approach presented here is quite similar to previous work in the field (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), and the authors should situate their contribution within this existing body of research.

Second, some of the specific assumptions made to constrain the model seem arbitrary, and it is unclear how crucial these choices are to the overall framework. For instance, why should the measurement probability, $p_{meas}$, be defined by Equation 3 specifically? Furthermore, is specifying the parameter values in Equation 12 truly sufficient to define and capture all relevant characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? The authors state that this formulation is robust and can express a large class of sensory models, but it is not clear that this is a sufficient justification on its own. At a minimum, the paper should include a more thorough discussion and justification for the specific assumptions used to make the Bayesian models identifiable.

In summary, this is a well-written paper that tackles an important and timely problem. However, its contribution is undermined by a failure to connect with the substantial body of existing work on model comparison and by the use of seemingly arbitrary assumptions that lack sufficient justification. Addressing these two points would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify its place in the literature.
