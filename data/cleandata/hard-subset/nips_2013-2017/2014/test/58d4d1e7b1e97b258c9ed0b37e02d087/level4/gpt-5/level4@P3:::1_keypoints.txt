- Paper tackles non-identifiability of prior, likelihood, and loss in Bayesian observer models and proposes a framework to assess practical identifiability under specific experimental designs.  
- Authors formalize degeneracies in Bayesian Decision Theory and propose constraints to make inference tractable.  
- Introduce an expected log-likelihood criterion that reduces to stimulus-weighted sums of KL divergences between reference and candidate observers.  
- Use MCMC to sample an “approximate expected posterior” over parameters and map identifiability landscapes.  
- Case study 1 (interval timing): prior recoverability depends strongly on motor-noise calibration and loss-function assumptions.  
- Case study 2 (speed perception): prior and noise parameters appear recoverable and robust under the slow-speed prior.  
- Derivation linking expected log-likelihood to KL sums is sound and aligns with model/parameter recovery practices.  
- Observer class is flexible (mixtures of Gaussians, flexible priors, monotone sensory mapping) and computationally efficient for many psychophysical tasks.  
- Case studies are well executed and provide actionable experimental recommendations (e.g., fix motor noise; beware narrow loss functions).  
- Several strong modeling assumptions need clearer justification and sensitivity analyses (mixture-of-Gaussians form, monotonic mapping, lapse model, stimulus discretization).  
- Robustness to model misspecification and recovery when the generative observer lies outside the model class is not demonstrated.  
- Paper is generally clear and organized, but needs explicit symbol definitions, explanation of Ntr choice and scaling, computational cost, and 2AFC-to-estimation mapping.  
- Originality is moderate: conceptually related to existing identifiability/recovery literature, but novel in the specialized observer class and MCMC-based identifiability landscapes.  
- Framework is significant for guiding experimental design and avoiding misinterpretation of inferred priors/likelihoods/losses.  
- Broader impact depends on showing robustness beyond the chosen parametric forms and extension to other paradigms (discrete choices, cue integration).  
- Pros: addresses an important problem, provides a coherent computational recipe, and yields nontrivial experimental recommendations.  
- Cons: limited engagement with prior identifiability literature, heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions, unclear motivation for some methodological choices, and limited scope to 1D estimation.  
- Suggestions: expand related-work comparison, perform sensitivity analyses (mixture components, loss families, sensory mappings), test out-of-class recovery, report MCMC diagnostics and computational costs, and include empirical loss-manipulation designs.  
- Minor comments: clarify the “trivial” degeneracy comment or cite, fix typo (“nor”→“or”), clarify Figure 2 legend, unify notation, justify maximum-entropy mixture choice, and note that E(θ|θ*) is not a true posterior.  
- Overall: well-written, timely, and practical framework whose impact would increase with deeper literature engagement and robustness analyses.
