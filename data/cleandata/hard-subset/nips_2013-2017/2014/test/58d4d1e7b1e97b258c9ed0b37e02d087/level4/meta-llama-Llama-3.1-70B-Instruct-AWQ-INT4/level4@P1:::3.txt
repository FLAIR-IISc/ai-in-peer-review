The paper describes a Bayesian model of perception, providing mathematical equations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A significant strength of this model is its ability to balance robustness and mathematical convenience, allowing it to handle various situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining computational tractability.

The model's identifiability is a notable aspect, as it enables the evaluation and distinction of two parameter estimates. This is a crucial feature, as it allows researchers to assess the reliability of the model's parameters. However, it is essential to acknowledge that this identifiability is a result of the mathematical assumptions underlying the model.

The proposed model provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in many experiments. Nevertheless, I have some reservations regarding its novelty. The model appears to be in the same spirit and style as other models that have recently appeared in the literature, such as those published in PLoS Computational Biology. For instance, the reader may consider the following papers:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these earlier articles, it shares similarities with them, leading me to conclude that its contribution is incremental.

To strengthen the manuscript, I suggest that the authors include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly stating why the model proposed in the current submission represents a significant advance over previously published models.

Additionally, I would like the authors to address the issue of identifiability in a broader sense. While the model is mathematically identifiable, it may not necessarily help distinguish whether an experimental subject's errors are due to sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. This limitation is essential to acknowledge, as it affects the model's ability to address the identifiability concerns that are most relevant to perceptual scientists.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but its impact would be enhanced by addressing these concerns and providing a clearer explanation of its novelty and significance.
