This paper proposes a Bayesian model of perception that combines robustness and mathematical convenience, making it applicable to a wide range of situations while still allowing for tractable inference. The model's identifiability is a significant strength, as it enables the evaluation and distinction of different parameter estimates. The authors provide a comprehensive framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in various experiments, which is a notable contribution to the field.

The paper's strengths lie in its ability to handle complex situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, and its computational convenience. The model's flexibility and generality make it a valuable tool for researchers in perception and related fields. The authors' emphasis on the model's identifiability is well-justified, as it is a crucial aspect of any statistical model.

However, I have some reservations regarding the paper's novelty and contribution. While the proposed model is not identical to existing models, it is similar in spirit and style to other recently published Bayesian models of perception. For example, the work by Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) shares similarities with the current submission. Therefore, I consider the contribution of this paper to be incremental, building upon existing research rather than presenting a groundbreaking new approach.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest that the authors include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly explaining how their model advances the state-of-the-art. This would help to contextualize their contribution and demonstrate its significance.

Additionally, the authors should address the issue of identifiability in a broader sense. While the model is mathematically identifiable, it may not be able to distinguish between different sources of noise (sensory, decision, or response) in experimental data. This limitation is important to acknowledge, as it affects the model's ability to provide insights into the underlying perceptual processes.

Overall, the paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novelty and contribution are incremental. With revisions addressing these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a comprehensive and flexible Bayesian model of perception.
* The model's identifiability is a significant strength, enabling the evaluation and distinction of different parameter estimates.
* The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their framework.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper's novelty and contribution are incremental, building upon existing research rather than presenting a groundbreaking new approach.
* The authors should address the issue of identifiability in a broader sense, acknowledging the model's limitations in distinguishing between different sources of noise.

Recommendations:

* Include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly explaining how the proposed model advances the state-of-the-art.
* Address the issue of identifiability in a broader sense, acknowledging the model's limitations in distinguishing between different sources of noise.
* Provide more context and discussion on the implications of the model's identifiability and its limitations.
