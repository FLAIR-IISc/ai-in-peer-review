## Review of “A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception”

This paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for Bayesian modeling of perception, focusing on the identifiability of model parameters. The authors develop a flexible observer model encompassing a range of psychophysical tasks, allowing for a systematic investigation of how experimental design impacts the recovery of internal representations. A key strength is the model’s combination of robustness – accommodating various noise types and prior structures – with computational tractability, enabling efficient exploration of the model space. The application to time interval estimation and speed perception demonstrates the potential of the framework for improving experimental design and validating inferences about internal representations.

The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the inherent degeneracy in Bayesian models and the critical need for assessing identifiability. The detailed mathematical formulation is a substantial undertaking and clearly presented. The choice to leverage a maximum-entropy approach is sensible and contributes to the model’s generality. The exploration of identifiability through the proposed MCMC-based approach (Eqs. 12-13) offers a novel method for evaluating experimental designs *a priori*.  The case studies provide concrete examples of how the framework can be applied and yield potentially valuable insights, such as highlighting the potential pitfalls of imposing strong constraints on the loss function in time interval estimation.

However, while a strong contribution, the paper's novelty is somewhat incremental. The approach builds upon a well-established line of research applying Bayesian modeling to perception, and shares similarities with existing work, particularly within the computational biology literature. Related work such as Acerbi et al. (2012, *PLoS Computational Biology*) and Acerbi et al. (2014, *PLoS Computational Biology*) also explore Bayesian models with a focus on inferring internal representations. Although the current submission is not identical to these works, the conceptual overlap is substantial.  

To strengthen the paper, the authors should dedicate a section to a more comprehensive review of recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should explicitly articulate how the proposed framework distinguishes itself from existing approaches, outlining the unique contributions in terms of generality, computational efficiency, or methodological innovation.  Specifically, detailing how the identifiability analysis presented here extends or differs from previous attempts to address model degeneracy would be highly beneficial. 

Furthermore, the paper's emphasis on mathematical identifiability warrants further discussion. While the model’s parameters are identifiable *within the model’s assumptions*, this does not necessarily resolve the broader challenges of inferring the underlying causes of perceptual errors. As the authors acknowledge, the model may not be able to definitively separate sensory, decision, or motor noise. This limitation should be explicitly addressed, acknowledging that the “identifiability” achieved here is specific to the model’s structure and may not translate directly to resolving real-world perceptual ambiguities.  A clarification of this point would prevent potential misinterpretations of the framework's capabilities.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Presents a mathematically sound and flexible framework for Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Offers a novel method for *a priori* evaluation of experimental designs.
*   Demonstrates the potential of the framework through concrete case studies.
*   Addresses a crucial issue in Bayesian modeling: the problem of identifiability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the contribution is somewhat incremental, building upon a well-established literature.
*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to recent related work.
*   The concept of “identifiability” should be clarified to emphasize its dependence on model assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has merit and could be a valuable contribution to NIPS, but would benefit from revisions addressing the concerns outlined above.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors strengthening the discussion of related work and clarifying the scope of the identifiability claims.
