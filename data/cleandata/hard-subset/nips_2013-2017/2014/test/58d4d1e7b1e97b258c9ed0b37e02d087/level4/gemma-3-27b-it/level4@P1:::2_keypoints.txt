- Addresses an important model-identifiability problem in cognitive modeling and is well written and clear.  
- Methodology depends on many strong assumptions, which limit the impact given the framework’s complexity.  
- Innovative approach merits acceptance for presentation despite limited transformative results.  
- Section 4.1: observed interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; question necessity of modeling both.  
- Recommend reporting which condition differences are statistically reliable and adding explicit statistical comparisons.  
- Section 4.2: must demonstrate the model can correctly detect the absence of a slow-speed prior when it truly isn’t present.  
- Line 106: why model an inherently continuous stimulus as drawn from a discrete distribution—clarify rationale.  
- Line 134: the log-stimulus transform may drive much of the success, implying identifiability follows from strong representational assumptions.  
- Line 143: question whether constraints and entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fit, since bimodality seems possible.  
- Line 158: challenge the justification for a prior favoring a lattice over uniform representation and for a prior 50% wider than the true range.  
- Figure 1 is confusing; suggest integrating panel b into a and removing the derived p_est(s*|x) to improve clarity.  
- Line 252: request clarification on conditions for Equation 14 approximations (Stirling’s approximation validity and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]).  
- Line 262: specify which “unnormalized density” is being sampled—ambiguity whether this is the dataset distribution.  
- Line 365: question why prior learning is claimed not to matter in the 2AFC task.  
- Overall summary: novel and important approach but results and assumptions need clearer justification and stronger empirical/statistical support.
