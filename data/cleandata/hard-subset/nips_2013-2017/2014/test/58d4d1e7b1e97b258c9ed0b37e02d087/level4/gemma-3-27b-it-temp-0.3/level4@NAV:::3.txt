This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, comprehensively defined through mathematical formulations of sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and resulting response distribution. A key strength lies in the model’s combination of robustness – its ability to accommodate diverse scenarios like stimulus-dependent noise – and computational tractability, allowing for efficient inference. The authors rightly emphasize the model’s identifiability, demonstrating clear distinction between parameter estimates.

Overall, I recommend acceptance to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation applicable to a wide range of experimental contexts. However, I do have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. The model aligns closely with a growing body of recent work in the field, particularly publications appearing in *PLoS Computational Biology*, and I believe the advance presented here is incremental. For instance, readers might find parallels with the approaches taken in:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *8*(11), e1002771.

Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *10*(6), e1003661.

While not identical, the current submission shares sufficient similarities with these and other existing models that characterizing the contribution as “incremental” feels appropriate. 

To address this, I suggest the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should explicitly articulate how the proposed model represents a significant advancement over existing literature.

Finally, while the authors correctly highlight the mathematical identifiability of the model’s parameters, it’s important to acknowledge that this identifiability stems from the model’s inherent mathematical assumptions. The model does not resolve the broader identifiability problem frequently encountered in perceptual science – specifically, the difficulty in disentangling sensory, decision, and response noise sources. I recommend the authors address this nuance in the revised manuscript, discussing the limitations of the model in this regard.



In summary, a mathematically elegant framework for perceptual estimation, but with an incremental novel contribution given related work.
