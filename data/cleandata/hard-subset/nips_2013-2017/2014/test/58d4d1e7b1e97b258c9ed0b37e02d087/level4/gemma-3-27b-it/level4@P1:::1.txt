In this article, the authors propose a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. They summarize the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, pinpoint areas of non-identifiability, and outline constraints to make each component of the Bayesian model identifiable. They then make specific assumptions to constrain their model, explore the sensitivity of parameter values, and apply the technique to two tasks commonly analyzed with Bayesian models: time interval estimation and speed perception.

Issues of identifiability of internal representations and processes have been a long-standing concern within cognitive science and psychology. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed representational formats, demonstrating that indistinguishable representations require constraints on underlying processes. 

More recently, the problem of identifiability has emerged as a major limitation hindering the widespread adoption of Bayesian models (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter directly addresses speed perception as an example).

Although the article addresses an important theoretical issue and is generally well-written and effective, I have two main reservations. First, it overlooks a substantial body of previous work on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making a comprehensive evaluation of its contribution difficult. The proposed framework shares similarities with existing approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, some assumptions appear arbitrary, and the degree to which the framework relies on these assumptions is unclear. For example, the justification for defining $p_{meas}$ with equation 3 is not fully developed. While the authors note it can express a wide range of sensory models, this does not fully justify the choice, and a discussion of the impact of these assumptions on identifiability would strengthen the paper. Similarly, is defining the parameters in Equation 12 sufficient to fully characterize a cognitive or perceptual process?
