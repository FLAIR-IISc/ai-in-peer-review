The paper introduces a Bayesian observer framework for perception, specifying mathematical components for sensory noise, likelihoods, priors, loss functions, and response generation. A key advantage is the combination of flexibility and computational efficiency: the model accommodates diverse settings (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while maintaining tractable inference. The manuscript places particular emphasis on identifiability, i.e., the ability to compare and distinguish competing parameterizations.

I am favorably inclined toward this work and would support acceptance to NIPS. The proposed framework is mathematically elegant and provides a unified formalism for modeling perceptual estimation across a range of experiments. That said, I have reservations about novelty. The approach is very much in line with recent literature (often in PLoS Computational Biology), and the incremental nature of the contribution should be acknowledged. For instance, see:
Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.
The present submission is not a duplicate of these or other prior works, but it is sufficiently close in spirit and methodology that its contribution feels incremental.

I recommend adding a dedicated section that reviews recent Bayesian observer models and clearly articulates how the present framework substantively advances beyond prior art.

A further point about identifiability: while the paper convincingly demonstrates mathematical identifiability within the assumed model class, this identifiability hinges on the modeling assumptions. In common usage, “identifiability” may refer to the ability to disentangle distinct latent sources of variability. In particular, as currently formulated, the framework cannot unambiguously attribute errors to sensory noise versus decision (policy/loss) noise versus response (motor/report) noise. Thus, it does not resolve the kind of identifiability that many perceptual scientists prioritize. Please address this nuance explicitly in a revision.

Mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but the contribution is incremental given closely related prior work.
