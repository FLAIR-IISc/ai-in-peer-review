This manuscript addresses the critical problem of model identifiability within the field of cognitive modeling. The paper is well-written and presented with clarity. However, my primary concern is that the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions that the reader must accept, and the extent of these assumptions seems disproportionate to the impact of the core findings. Nevertheless, because the methodology is novel and interesting, I find the work to be above the threshold for acceptance at this conference.

Regarding the primary results:
In Section 4.1, the finding of interchangeability between sensory and motor noise is not particularly surprising; this possibility became apparent while reading the model's description. It would strengthen the analysis to explicitly state which of the reported condition differences are statistically significant.
In Section 4.2, a crucial control experiment appears to be missing. It is essential to demonstrate that if a reference observer did *not* utilize a slow-speed prior, the model would be capable of recovering this fact.

Specific Comments:
*   **line 106:** The stimulus, which is an inherently continuous variable, is modeled as being drawn from a discrete distribution. The authors should clarify this choice. I suspect this is a consequence of typical experimental designs where discrete stimulus levels are used as independent variables, but this should be stated.
*   **line 134:** As the authors note, the log-stimulus transformation in Equation 2 is motivated by psychophysical principles. It seems plausible that the method's success is largely attributable to this transformation. If so, the paper's central claim is more nuanced: that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong, psychologically-plausible constraints on the nature of mental representations.
*   **line 143:** Do the constraints in Equation 4, in conjunction with the maximization of differential entropy, guarantee that the fit for Equation 3 will be a unimodal distribution? It seems that, in principle, a bimodal distribution could also satisfy these conditions.
*   **line 158:** What is the justification for an observer's prior to favor a lattice representation over a uniform one? Furthermore, what knowledge does the observer possess that would lead them to adopt a prior that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range?
*   **Figure 1:** In its current form, the figure is more confusing than illustrative. Its clarity could be improved by integrating panel (b) into panel (a) and by removing the plot of p_est(s^*|x), as this is a derived inference rather than a core component of the model.
*   **line 252:** The derivation leading to Equation 15 relies on several approximations. Could the authors comment on the conditions on N under which the approximation in Equation 14 is valid? Specifically, regarding the use of Stirling's approximation and the assumption that E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]. By the point the authors introduce Equation 15—having disregarded priors on θ and made these approximations—its justification seems to rest more on its ability to provide an intuitive rule for assessing predictive similarity than on a rigorous derivation.
*   **line 262:** The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous. Please clarify which density is being referred to—is it the distribution of the dataset?
*   **line 365:** Could the authors elaborate on why the learning of priors is considered irrelevant in the context of a 2AFC task?

In summary, the work presents an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less impactful than the methodology itself.
