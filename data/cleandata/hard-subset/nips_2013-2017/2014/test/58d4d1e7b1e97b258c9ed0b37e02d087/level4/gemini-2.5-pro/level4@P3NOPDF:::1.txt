### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a framework for model comparison between Bayesian models of behavioral data. The authors begin by summarizing the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identifying key areas where components are non-identifiable. They then outline general constraints that can render these components identifiable. Finally, they instantiate this approach by making a specific set of simplifying assumptions, exploring the differentiability of parameter values within their constrained model, and applying the resulting technique to two case studies in perception: time interval estimation and motion perception.

**Evaluation and Major Comments**

The problem of model identifiability, which this paper addresses, is a central and long-standing challenge in cognitive science and psychology. As Anderson (1978) famously argued, different internal representations can be mathematically indistinguishable without imposing constraints on the processes that operate on them. This issue has become particularly salient in recent years with the rise of Bayesian cognitive modeling, where the flexibility of the framework can make it difficult to falsify models or definitively compare competing accounts (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). The authors are therefore tackling a problem of significant theoretical importance, and the manuscript is well-written and presents its case effectively.

Despite these strengths, I have two major reservations that prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance.

1.  **Lack of Engagement with Prior Literature on Model Comparison:** My first major reservation is that the paper does not adequately engage with the extensive prior literature on model comparison and selection within cognitive science and psychology. The general problem of selecting among complex computational models has been thoroughly investigated, leading to principled methods for balancing model fit and complexity. The framework proposed here bears a strong resemblance to foundational work in this area (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), which also deals with issues of parameter identifiability and model distinguishability. Without situating their contribution relative to these established methods, it is difficult for a reader to evaluate the novelty and specific advantages of the proposed framework.

2.  **Justification of Model-Constraining Assumptions:** My second major concern relates to the specific assumptions made to render the models identifiable. While some constraints are necessary, several of the choices made by the authors appear arbitrary and lack sufficient justification. The reader is left wondering how critical these specific assumptions are to the framework's success. For instance, why is the measurement noise ($p_{meas}$) constrained to the specific functional form in Equation 3? Similarly, while the parameterization in Equation 12 is described as robust and capable of expressing a large class of sensory models, this does not in itself justify its use over other potential representations of the cognitive process. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a more thorough discussion and justification for these crucial modeling choices, and ideally, explore the sensitivity of their results to these assumptions.

**Minor Comments**

*   **Line 53:** "It is a trivial mathematical fact…" – The authors might consider briefly explaining *why* this is true rather than labeling it as trivial, as it may not be obvious to all readers.
*   **Line 73:** The second "nor" appears to be a typo and should likely be "or".
*   **Figure 2:** The caption is ambiguous. It states that both the star and the dashed lines denote the "true value". Please clarify what each symbol represents.
