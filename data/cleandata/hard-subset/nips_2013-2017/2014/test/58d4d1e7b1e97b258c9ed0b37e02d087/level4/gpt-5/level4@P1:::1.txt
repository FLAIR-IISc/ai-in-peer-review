This paper proposes a framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian observer models using behavioral data. The authors summarize Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT), articulate where non-identifiability arises (e.g., degeneracies among priors, likelihoods, and loss functions), and discuss constraints that may render model components identifiable (theoretical constraints, task design, and additional measurements). They then instantiate a flexible observer model with parametric assumptions for pmeas, priors, and loss functions, and use an expected log-likelihood/KL-based criterion to explore how well different parameterizations can be distinguished. Two case studies illustrate the approach: interval timing and speed perception. For timing, they compare experimental designs (e.g., single vs. multiple stimulus ranges, MAP-like loss vs. broader loss, known motor noise) and show that recovering priors can be difficult unless additional constraints are imposed (e.g., measured motor noise). For speed perception, they find that reconstructing a slow-speed prior and noise characteristics is relatively robust, even when decoupling internal from external noise.

Relation to prior work: Identifiability of internal representations has a long history in cognitive science and psychology. Anderson (1978) analyzed when different representational formats are indistinguishable without process constraints; more recently, critiques of Bayesian modeling (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) have highlighted non-identifiability as a key limitation. In cognitive modeling, there is substantial prior work on model distinguishability and data informativeness (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004; and related NIPS work by Myung, Pitt, Zhang, & Balasubramanian, 2000). The present paper addresses similar concerns in the specific context of Bayesian observer models and experimental design.

Strengths:
- Important problem: Identifiability is central to interpreting inferred priors, likelihoods, and losses from behavior.
- Clear exposition of BDT-related degeneracies and how constraints can help.
- A concrete, computationally efficient observer model and a principled expected log-likelihood/KL criterion to map identifiability.
- Useful case studies demonstrating how the framework can inform experimental design (e.g., the impact of known motor noise; pitfalls of a narrow loss function).

Major reservations:
1) Limited engagement with relevant prior literature on model distinguishability and model comparison in cognitive science makes it hard to assess the methodological contribution. The proposed approach—defining expected support via KL divergence and exploring identifiability landscapes—is conceptually close to existing work (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004). The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning: what is genuinely new (e.g., the specific observer model class, the efficient computation for BDT-based estimation tasks, the experimental design comparisons), and how it extends or differs from earlier frameworks.

2) Some modeling assumptions seem arbitrary or are insufficiently justified, and it is unclear how sensitive the conclusions are to them. For example, why must pmeas be the mixture-of-Gaussians form in Equation 3? Is specifying moments (and the remaining degree of freedom via a maximum-entropy choice) adequate to capture a broad class of sensory processes? Similarly, is defining parameters in Equation 12 sufficient to characterize the relevant aspects of cognitive/perceptual processes for identifiability analysis? While the authors note that their parameterization can express a wide range of models, the paper should explicitly justify these choices and provide sensitivity analyses (or at least discussion) to show that the main conclusions do not hinge on them. At a minimum, please discuss alternative forms (e.g., different noise models or loss families), why they were not chosen, and how they might affect identifiability.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The derivations and use of expected log-likelihood/KL are sound, and the case studies are well executed. However, the framework’s reliance on specific parametric forms, without sufficient justification or sensitivity checks, weakens the evidential basis for some conclusions.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Some statements could be clarified (see minor comments). More explicit discussion of assumptions and their implications would improve transparency.
- Originality: The specific application to BDT observer models and experimental design is useful, but the general approach overlaps conceptually with prior work on model distinguishability. The novelty lies primarily in the tailored observer model class, computational efficiency, and case-study insights.
- Significance: Potentially high. A practical framework to assess identifiability and guide experimental design is valuable for the community. Strengthening the connection to prior literature and clarifying assumptions would increase its impact.

Minor comments:
- Line 53: “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” Consider briefly explaining why, rather than labeling it trivial; not all readers will find it obvious.
- Line 73: The second “nor” should be “or.”
- Figure 2: Please clarify the meaning of the star and dashed lines. The caption suggests both denote the true value.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and timely problem with clear practical implications.
- Provides an efficient, implementable framework and informative case studies.
- Offers actionable guidance for experimental design.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficiently situated within the existing literature on model distinguishability and identifiability in cognitive science.
- Key modeling assumptions (e.g., pmeas in Eq. 3; parametrization choices vis-à-vis Eq. 12) are under-justified; limited discussion of robustness.
