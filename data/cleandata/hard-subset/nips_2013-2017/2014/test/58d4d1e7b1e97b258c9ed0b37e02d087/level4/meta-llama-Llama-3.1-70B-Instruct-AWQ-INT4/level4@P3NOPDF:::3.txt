The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, providing a comprehensive framework that incorporates mathematical equations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A significant strength of this model lies in its ability to balance robustness and mathematical convenience, allowing it to handle diverse situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining tractable inference.

The model's identifiability is also highlighted as a key feature, enabling easy evaluation and distinction of parameter estimates. I appreciate the paper's contribution to formalizing perceptual estimation in various experiments and believe it has the potential to be accepted to the NIPS conference.

However, I have some reservations regarding the model's novelty. While the proposed model is not identical to existing work, it bears similarities to other models recently published in the literature, such as those in PLoS Computational Biology. For instance, the reader may consider the following papers:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.

Given these similarities, I believe it is fair to state that the contribution of the current submission is incremental. To strengthen the manuscript, I suggest the authors include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly articulate why their model represents a significant advance over previously published work.

Additionally, while the submission emphasizes the model's mathematical identifiability, it is essential to acknowledge that this identifiability is contingent upon the model's mathematical assumptions. Furthermore, the model may not provide clear insights into distinguishing between experimental subjects' errors due to sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Therefore, it would be justified to argue that the model does not address the identifiability concerns most relevant to perceptual scientists. I recommend the authors address this point in the revised manuscript.

In summary, the paper provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novel contribution is incremental, building upon existing work in the field.
