Summary
This paper addresses a central question for Bayesian observer models in perception: to what extent can priors, likelihoods, and loss functions be uniquely recovered from behavior, given the well-known degeneracies of Bayesian decision theory. The authors propose a general, parametric observer model and an a priori identifiability analysis that computes the expected log-likelihood of competing parameterizations θ under data generated by a reference observer θ*, yielding an MCMC-samplable “expected posterior” proportional to the exponential of a sum of KL divergences across stimuli. They demonstrate the framework on two case studies. For interval timing, they show that (i) sensory and motor noise can be hard to distinguish, degrading prior recovery, (ii) using a narrow, MAP-like loss can worsen prior identifiability, and (iii) independent measurement of motor noise substantially improves inference. For speed perception, they find that the slow-speed prior and noise characteristics can be reliably recovered, even when the internal likelihood is decoupled from the generative measurement model.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-motivated by debates on the explanatory status of Bayesian models in cognition and perception (e.g., Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love), practical successes in inverse inference of priors/likelihoods/losses (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Maloney & Mamassian; Girshick et al.; Acerbi et al.), and prior work on model distinguishability (Navarro, Pitt & Myung). The contribution here is a systematic, efficient, and pre-experimental identifiability analysis that can inform experimental design.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem: practical non-identifiability undermines many inferences from Bayesian observer models.
- Methodological contribution: an efficient, generalizable framework that recasts expected model support as a sum of KL divergences, enabling MCMC exploration of identifiability landscapes.
- Useful design insights: concrete guidance for interval timing (measure motor noise; avoid overly narrow loss assumptions) and theoretical support for robustness in speed-perception prior inference.
- Clarity of the modeling pipeline: well-structured observer model from measurement to response, with interpretable parameters and computationally tractable mixture forms.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on strong modeling assumptions (e.g., specific sensory transforms, mixture-of-Gaussians forms, lattice priors), which themselves impose constraints that may drive identifiability.
- Some case-study results are unsurprising (e.g., confounding between sensory and motor noise), limiting the “wow” factor.
- Key approximations leading to the expected log-likelihood expression and the unnormalized “expected posterior” are under-explained; no quantitative error analysis of these approximations is provided.
- Reporting could be clearer: it is not always evident which condition differences are statistically reliable; Figure 1 is confusing.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The reduction to expected log-likelihood via KL divergence is reasonable and aligns with widespread model-comparison practice. However:
- Please justify the approximations leading to Eq. 14–15 (as numbered in the paper) and characterize when they hold (e.g., trial counts Ntr for Stirling’s approximation; conditions for E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]). A small simulation demonstrating approximation accuracy across Ntr and distributional shapes would strengthen the paper.
- Because identifiability conclusions can be driven by parametric choices (e.g., log transform; mixture families), include sensitivity analyses showing how conclusions change if these elements are misspecified.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written, but several clarifications would help:
- Clearly report which differences across experimental designs are reliable (e.g., credible intervals/SDs on KL-based error metrics; test whether posterior summaries differ across setups).
- Figure 1 is difficult to parse. Consider integrating 1b into 1a and removing p_est(s*|x) from the graphic (it is a derived quantity), or explicitly annotating the flow of random variables and deterministic transforms.
- Specify precisely which “unnormalized density” is being sampled in Section 3 (Eq. 13), and state the proposal and diagnostics used in MCMC.

Originality
The combination of a flexible observer family with an expected-KL-based identifiability map and its use for a priori design optimization is novel and valuable. The case-study findings for interval timing are informative despite being partly expected; the speed-perception analysis adds theoretical support to influential prior work.

Significance
The framework is likely to be useful to researchers designing experiments to infer internal representations, offering a way to prioritize designs and identify parameters that require independent constraints. The broader impact is in improving the credibility of inverse-Bayesian claims through pre-registered identifiability checks.

Requests for revision and questions
- Section 4.2: Include the “flat prior” control explicitly. If the true observer lacks a slow-speed prior, can the method reliably recover that fact?
- Report which differences across designs in Section 4.1 are statistically reliable and how they scale with Ntr.
- Provide a brief quantitative assessment of the accuracy of the approximations used to derive Eq. 14–15, and state conditions on Ntr for them to be acceptable.
- Sensory vs. motor noise confound: show 2D posterior slices and, if possible, how independent motor measurements (MTR) break the degeneracy in practice.
- Discuss sensitivity to misspecification of the sensory transform f(s). To what extent do your identifiability conclusions depend on assuming the log-like form in Eq. 2?

Specific comments (by line/figure, as labeled in the manuscript)
- Line 106: Please justify modeling stimuli as drawn from a discrete distribution. If this reflects common psychophysical designs, say so and discuss approximation error relative to continuous designs.
- Line 134: The log-like transform is a strong assumption that constrains inference. Please discuss how identifiability depends on this choice and whether alternative transforms change conclusions.
- Line 143: Do the constraints and max-entropy choice guarantee unimodality for Eq. 3? If not, specify parameter ranges that ensure unimodality or describe checks/enforcements used.
- Line 158: Why a lattice prior rather than, say, a smooth parametric prior? What motivates defining the prior over a range 50% wider than the stimulus range, and is this knowledge realistic for observers?
- Figure 1: Consider integrating panels, simplifying the depicted variables, and clarifying which quantities are latent vs. observed vs. deterministic transforms.
- Line 252: Clarify conditions under which Stirling’s approximation and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] are accurate in your setting; provide an error bound or empirical check.
- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density” — specify this refers to E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp⟨log P(D|θ)⟩, and detail the MCMC scheme and convergence diagnostics.
- Line 365: Please justify the statement that prior learning does not matter in the 2AFC formulation, or clarify the assumptions when translating 2AFC to an equivalent estimation task.

Pros for acceptance
- Important, under-addressed problem with practical implications.
- Methodologically sound framework with clear potential to improve experimental design.
- Thoughtful case studies that illustrate actionable design recommendations.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Some results are unsurprising; impact would be greater with additional controls (e.g., flat prior recovery) and quantified approximation accuracy.
- Clarity issues in figures and reporting of reliability.

Overall assessment
Innovative approach to an important problem with useful, if not yet definitive, results. With the requested clarifications, sensitivity checks, and added controls, this paper would merit acceptance.
