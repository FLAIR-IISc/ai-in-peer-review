- Proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models on behavioral data to address identifiability of internal representations and processes.
- Summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory, identifies areas of non-identifiability, and outlines constraints to make terms identifiable.
- Applies the framework to two studies, testing parameter differentiability and using time-interval estimation and motion-perception examples.
- Notes that identifiability of internal representations/processes is a longstanding problem in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978).
- Argues that identifiability concerns have hindered widespread adoption of Bayesian models (cites Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis).
- Major concern 1: The manuscript largely ignores extensive prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang; Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and appears similar to earlier work.
- Major concern 2: Several key assumptions seem arbitrary or unjustified (e.g., definition of p_meas in Eq.3 and parameter definition in Eq.12), raising doubts about the framework’s dependence on them.
- The claim that the framework covers a large class of sensory models does not by itself justify the assumptions; the paper should discuss and justify these assumptions to ensure identifiability.
- Minor: Line 53—don’t label a mathematical fact “trivial”; provide the reasoning.
- Minor: Line 73—replace the second “nor” with “or” for clarity.
- Minor: Figure 2 caption—clarify the meaning of the star and dashed lines and explicitly state which denotes the true value.
- Overall: well-written and addresses an important issue but needs stronger engagement with previous work and clearer justification of its assumptions.
