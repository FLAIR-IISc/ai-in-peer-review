This paper addresses the important question of identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The manuscript is clearly written and generally easy to follow. That said, the main results rest on a substantial set of assumptions that readers must accept, and the number and strength of these assumptions feel large relative to the empirical punch of the key findings. Nonetheless, the methodology is novel and interesting, and I consider the contribution above the bar for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the central results: In Section 4.1, the near-exchangeability between sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising (I found myself wondering whether both needed to be modeled as separate contributors as I read). It would be helpful to indicate which differences across experimental conditions are statistically reliable; I found the box plots difficult to interpret on that point. In Section 4.2, it seems essential to also demonstrate that if the reference observer lacks a slow-speed prior, the framework recovers this fact (i.e., correctly infers a flat prior).

Specific comments:
- line 106: Why is the stimulus sampled from a discrete distribution when the underlying variable is continuous? I assume this reflects common psychophysical practice (fixed stimulus levels as the manipulated variable), but it would help to make this explicit.
- line 134: The log-like transform in Eq. 2 is motivated by classic psychophysical laws. It appears that much of the success here hinges on this transform, which imposes a strong constraint on inference. In that sense, the work shows that Bayesian models can be identifiable given strong assumptions about representational mappings.
- line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 together with the maximum-entropy choice guarantee that Eq. 3 yields a unimodal measurement distribution? In principle, fitting a mixture could produce bimodality.
- line 158: Why would an observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over a uniform one? And what justifies assuming a prior that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range—what knowledge about the stimulus space would support that?
- Figure 1: Even after careful inspection, the figure was more confusing than clarifying. It might be clearer to merge panel b into panel a and omit p_est(s^*|x), which is derived rather than fundamental to the generative flow.
- line 252: Please comment on the conditions on N under which Eq. 14 is a good approximation—i.e., when Stirling’s approximation applies—and when the approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] is justified. By the time Eq. 15 appears—after dropping priors on θ and introducing these approximations—it feels chiefly motivated by yielding an intuitive similarity metric rather than by a fully rigorous derivation.
- line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: which density is meant here exactly—the distribution over datasets or over parameters?
- line 365: Why does prior learning not matter in 2AFC? A brief justification would help.

Innovative approach, important problem, results not especially striking.
