- Presents a formal framework for a priori identifiability analysis of Bayesian observer models of perception.
- Builds a flexible, computationally efficient observer model covering sensation (stimulus-dependent noise), perception (priors/likelihoods), decision-making (loss), and motor response.
- Uses mixtures of Gaussians to parameterize components, enabling wide functional expressivity with computational tractability.
- Main contribution: method to systematically map the identifiability landscape of an experimental design before data collection to assess parameter recoverability and compare designs.
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies: time-interval estimation (inferring priors) and slow-speed prior reconstruction in speed perception.
- Strength: provides a direct, systematic tool to guide experimental design and improve efficiency and robustness of parameter recovery.
- Strength: the observer model is comprehensive and flexible, capturing stimulus-dependent noise (Weber/Stevens), asymmetric loss, and non-Gaussian priors.
- Strength: computational efficiency for expected log-likelihood calculations is a key enabler of the identifiability analysis.
- Case studies deliver practical, sometimes non-intuitive insights (e.g., narrow loss can worsen prior recovery; measuring motor noise can resolve degeneracies).
- Weakness: modeling framework is somewhat incremental relative to prior comprehensive Bayesian observer models (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014); needs fuller comparison.
- Weakness: claims about "identifiability" should be qualified—framework addresses parameter identifiability within a model class but does not resolve model-class identifiability (distinguishing sensory vs likelihood mismatch vs motor noise).
- Overall recommendation: strong paper warranting acceptance with minor revisions to better situate against related work and clarify identifiability scope.
