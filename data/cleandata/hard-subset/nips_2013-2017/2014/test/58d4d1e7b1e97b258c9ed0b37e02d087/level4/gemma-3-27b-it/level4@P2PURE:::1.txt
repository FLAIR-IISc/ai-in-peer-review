This article presents a framework for model comparison of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors begin by summarizing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying potential issues of non-identifiability, and outlining constraints to ensure each component of the Bayesian framework is identifiable. They then propose specific assumptions to constrain their framework, investigate the differentiability of parameters within their model, and demonstrate its application to two established areas leveraging Bayesian decision theory: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The problem of identifying internal representations and processes has long been a central challenge in cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that different representational formats can produce indistinguishable behavior without constraints on processing mechanisms. 

Recent critiques have highlighted identifiability as a key limitation hindering broader adoption of the Bayesian framework in these fields (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter using speed perception as a specific example). 

While the article addresses a theoretically significant issue and is generally well-written and clearly presented, I have two primary concerns. First, the work largely overlooks the existing literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making it difficult to fully assess its novelty and contribution. The proposed framework shares similarities with previous approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, several assumptions appear arbitrary, and the extent to which the framework’s validity depends on them is unclear. Specifically, the rationale for defining $p_{meas}$ using equation 3 is not fully explained. Furthermore, it is unclear whether specifying parameter values in Equation 12 sufficiently captures the full complexity of a cognitive or perceptual process. While the authors note its robustness and ability to represent a broad class of sensory models, this does not fully justify the assumption; a more thorough discussion and justification of the constraints imposed for identifiability would strengthen the work.
