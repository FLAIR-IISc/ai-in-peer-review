This paper proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data. The authors review Bayesian decision theory, identify sources of non-identifiability, and describe constraints that can render each term in the framework identifiable. They then impose specific assumptions to constrain these terms, assess the discriminability of parameter values in their models, and demonstrate the approach on two case studies in which Bayesian decision theory is used to explain behavior: interval timing and motion perception.

Questions of identifiability—of both internal representations and processes—have been central in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For instance, Anderson (1978) analyzed conditions under which different representational formats can mimic each other, showing that they are indistinguishable without constraints on processing whenever they make equivalent distinctions over inputs. More recently, concerns about identifiability have been highlighted as a key limitation of Bayesian approaches that has curtailed their broader uptake (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012, which discusses speed perception specifically).

The manuscript addresses an issue of substantial theoretical significance, is clearly written, and generally pursues a sensible strategy. However, I have two major reservations.

First, the paper largely overlooks a substantial prior literature on model comparison and model distinguishability in cognitive science and psychology, which complicates the evaluation of its novel contribution. The proposed framework does not appear fundamentally different from earlier approaches to model selection and model identifiability (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004; see also Myung, Pitt, Zhang, & Balasubramanian, 2000, NIPS 13). A more thorough engagement with this body of work is needed to clarify what is new here, how the proposed constraints relate to prior methods, and under what conditions the framework offers advantages.

Second, several assumptions used to render the Bayesian terms identifiable seem ad hoc, and it is unclear how strongly the conclusions depend on them. For example, why should p_meas be defined as in Equation 3? Is specifying the parameter values in Equation 12 truly sufficient to define and capture the relevant features of a cognitive or perceptual process? I acknowledge that the parameterization is flexible and can represent a broad class of sensory models, but this by itself does not justify the assumption. At minimum, the paper should articulate and defend the choices made to constrain the models, explain their empirical and theoretical motivation, and discuss how alternative, equally plausible constraints would affect identifiability and model comparison outcomes.
