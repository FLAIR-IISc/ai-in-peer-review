This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception that explicitly specifies sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior distributions, a loss function, and the response distribution. A key strength is that it combines robustness with mathematical convenience: it can accommodate a range of experimental situations (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while keeping inference tractable. The authors also emphasize that the model is identifiable in the sense that different parameter settings can be evaluated and distinguished.

I like this paper and would be happy to see it accepted to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation across many experimental paradigms. However, I have reservations about the degree of novelty. The work is very much in the spirit of several recent Bayesian models of perception and decision-making, many of which have appeared in PLoS Computational Biology. While the current submission is not identical to these papers, it is similar enough that the contribution seems incremental rather than a substantial departure. For example:
- Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
- Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.

I recommend revising the manuscript to include a dedicated section that reviews these and other recent Bayesian models of perception, and to state clearly and concretely how the present model constitutes a significant advance over prior work (methodologically, theoretically, or empirically).

A further point concerns the emphasis on identifiability. While the parameters may be identifiable under the model’s mathematical assumptions, “identifiability” is used more broadly in this field. In particular, the model as presented does not allow one to unambiguously attribute an experimental subject’s errors to sensory noise versus decision noise versus response noise. In this practical sense—arguably the sense many perceptual scientists care about—the model does not resolve the identifiability problem. Please address this nuance in the revision.

In summary: this is a mathematically elegant and tractable framework for perceptual estimation, but the novelty appears incremental given closely related prior work.
