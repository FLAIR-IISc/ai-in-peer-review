This paper introduces a Bayesian model of perception that incorporates a sensory noise model, a likelihood function, a prior distribution, a loss function, and a response distribution. A significant strength of this framework is its combination of modeling flexibility with computational tractability. Specifically, the model can accommodate various complexities, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining tractable inference. The authors also emphasize that the model is identifiable, meaning its parameter estimates can be reliably evaluated and distinguished.

The proposed model provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, which could be valuable for analyzing data from a wide range of experiments. For this reason, I am supportive of the paper's acceptance. However, I have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. The model is conceptually similar to several other Bayesian perceptual models recently published in the literature, particularly in journals such as PLoS Computational Biology. For example, readers may consider the following related works:

*   Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 8(11), e1002771.
*   Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 10(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other prior articles, the parallels are strong enough to characterize the contribution as incremental. To address this, I suggest that the authors revise the manuscript to include a new section that reviews recently proposed Bayesian models of perception. This section should clearly articulate why the model proposed here constitutes a significant advance over previously published approaches.

A second point concerns the paper's emphasis on model identifiability. While it is true that the model's parameters are identifiable within its specific mathematical formulation, this property is a direct consequence of its underlying assumptions. The term "identifiability" also refers to a more fundamental challenge in perceptual science: disentangling the distinct contributions of sensory noise, decision noise, and response noise to an observer's behavior. The proposed model does not appear to resolve this more general identifiability problem, which is a primary concern for most perceptual scientists. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion of this limitation and clarify the scope of the identifiability claim in the revised manuscript.
