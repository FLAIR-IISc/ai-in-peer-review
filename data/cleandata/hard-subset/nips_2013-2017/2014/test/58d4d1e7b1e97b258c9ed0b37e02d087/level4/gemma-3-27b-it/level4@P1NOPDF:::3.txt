The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing the mathematical formulations for sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A key strength of this model is its combination of robustness and computational tractability. It can accommodate a variety of scenarios, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while remaining analytically convenient for inference. The authors rightly emphasize the model’s identifiability – the ability to readily evaluate and distinguish between different parameter estimates.

I find this paper promising and would support its acceptance to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in a wide range of experimental contexts. However, I do have some reservations. The model feels very much aligned with, and in the style of, recent work appearing in venues like *PLoS Computational Biology*, and therefore, I believe the contribution is incremental. Readers might find parallels with papers such as:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology, 8*(11), e1002771.

Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology, 10*(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other existing articles, it shares enough similarities that I believe characterizing the contribution as “incremental” is fair. 

To address this, I suggest the authors revise the manuscript to include a new section thoroughly reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception. In this section, they should explicitly articulate how the model presented here represents a significant advancement over previously published work.

Another point concerns the emphasis on the model’s mathematical identifiability. While true, this identifiability stems directly from the model’s underlying mathematical assumptions. The term “identifiable” has broader applications. For instance, the model doesn’t allow for a firm distinction between sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise as the source of experimental errors. Consequently, one could argue that the model doesn’t resolve the identifiability problems that perceptual scientists commonly grapple with. The authors should address this nuance in the revised manuscript.

In summary, the paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalising perceptual estimation. However, the novel contribution is incremental given closely related work already present in the literature.
