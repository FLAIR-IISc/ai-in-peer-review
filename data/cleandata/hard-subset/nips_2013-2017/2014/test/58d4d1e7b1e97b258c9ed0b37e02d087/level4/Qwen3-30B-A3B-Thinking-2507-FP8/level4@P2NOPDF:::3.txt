

This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception formalizing sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions. A key strength is its dual merit: the model accommodates diverse scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while maintaining computational tractability, enabling efficient inference. The authors emphasize the model's mathematical identifiability, where distinct parameter estimates can be readily evaluated and distinguished. The framework offers a mathematically elegant approach for formalizing perceptual estimation across experimental contexts.

The work is well-motivated and technically sound, and I recommend acceptance. However, the novelty requires careful assessment. The proposed model shares core methodological approaches with recent Bayesian perceptual models, particularly those published in *PLoS Computational Biology* (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014). While not identical, the current submission builds incrementally on this established line of work. For instance:  
- Acerbi et al. (2012) address temporal statistics and motor-sensory timing using similar Bayesian frameworks.  
- Acerbi et al. (2014) analyze suboptimality in human probabilistic inference through comparable model structures.  

The contribution thus appears incremental rather than transformative. To strengthen the paper, the authors should add a dedicated section explicitly reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models, clearly articulating how their approach advances beyond prior work—e.g., by addressing specific limitations or extending applicability.  

A critical clarification is needed regarding "identifiability." The model’s parameters are mathematically identifiable *under its specific assumptions*, but this does not resolve the practical identifiability challenge faced by perceptual scientists. Specifically, the model cannot experimentally distinguish between sensory noise, decision noise, and response noise, as these are conflated within its structure. Consequently, the claim that the model "solves" identifiability issues is misleading. The authors must address this limitation in the revision, clarifying that identifiability here refers only to parameter estimation within the model’s formalism—not to experimental disambiguation of noise sources.  

In summary, the paper provides a technically solid and elegant framework for perceptual estimation. Its primary weakness is the incremental nature of the contribution relative to existing literature, compounded by an imprecise discussion of identifiability. With the suggested revisions—particularly a focused comparison to prior work and a nuanced clarification of identifiability—the manuscript would make a stronger case for publication. I strongly recommend acceptance upon these revisions.
