

This paper presents a framework for systematically testing identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, addressing a critical issue in the field. The authors demonstrate that Bayesian observer models can suffer from degeneracy, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions produce identical behavioral outputs. This non-identifiability undermines the validity of recovering internal representations from data. The authors propose a general framework to assess identifiability a priori, allowing researchers to evaluate experimental designs before conducting costly experiments.

The paper's main strength lies in its mathematical elegance and practical utility. The authors develop a flexible Bayesian observer model that accommodates various sensory noise structures (including stimulus-dependent noise) while maintaining computational tractability. This is achieved through a well-structured four-stage model (sensation, perception, decision-making, response) that allows efficient exploration of the parameter space. The framework's ability to systematically evaluate identifiability across different experimental designs represents a significant contribution to the field.

The authors demonstrate their approach through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. In the time estimation task, they show that the baseline experimental design (testing both Short and Long conditions) performs poorly at recovering priors, while measuring motor parameters independently (MTR) greatly improves reconstruction accuracy. Surprisingly, they find that encouraging a MAP-like loss function (MAP condition) actually worsens inference quality. In the speed perception task, they confirm that the reconstruction of slow-speed priors is robust and reliable, providing theoretical validation for previous empirical findings.

While the paper makes a valuable contribution, I have concerns about its novelty relative to existing work. The framework and model architecture are closely related to recent publications, particularly Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) in PLoS Computational Biology, which also developed Bayesian models for perceptual tasks with similar mathematical structures. The current work extends these approaches but doesn't present a fundamentally new modeling paradigm. The authors should more explicitly differentiate their contribution from these prior works and clarify the specific advances over existing frameworks.

The paper's claim about "identifiability" requires careful interpretation. While the authors correctly demonstrate that their framework can distinguish between parameter values within their specific model structure, this does not address the broader identifiability concerns in perception research. Many perceptual scientists are concerned about distinguishing between sensory noise, decision noise, and response noise in experimental data. The authors' framework, like most Bayesian models, assumes these components are separable through the model structure. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently address whether this assumption is justified or how the framework might help resolve these more fundamental identifiability issues. This is a critical distinction that should be clarified in the manuscript.

The paper's mathematical treatment is sound, and the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility. The authors provide clear explanations of the model components and their relationships, and the technical derivations appear correct. The results are well-presented with appropriate visualizations, and the conclusions are supported by the analysis.

I recommend the paper for acceptance with the following revisions:
1. Add a new section that thoroughly reviews recent Bayesian models of perception, clearly articulating how this work advances beyond previous approaches.
2. Clarify the scope and limitations of the identifiability claims, explicitly addressing how their framework relates to the broader identifiability challenges in perceptual science.
3. More precisely define the contribution relative to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) and other relevant work, avoiding vague statements about "novelty."

The paper presents a useful methodological contribution to the field, particularly for researchers designing perceptual experiments. The framework provides a principled approach to evaluating experimental designs before data collection, potentially saving significant time and resources. The mathematical treatment is rigorous, and the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's practical value.

While the contribution is not revolutionary, it represents a valuable incremental advance that addresses a genuine methodological gap. The authors have developed a practical tool that will help researchers avoid pitfalls in experimental design and interpretation. With the suggested revisions to clarify the novelty and scope of the work, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the technical content. The mathematical formalism is appropriately detailed, and the connections between the model components are well-explained. The results section effectively demonstrates the framework's utility through concrete examples.

In summary, this paper offers a valuable methodological contribution to the field of Bayesian models of perception. While the novelty is incremental, the practical utility of the framework is significant. With the suggested revisions to clarify the contribution relative to prior work and address the nuances of identifiability claims, this paper would be a strong fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
