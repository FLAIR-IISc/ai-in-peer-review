Summary
The paper proposes a general framework for comparing Bayesian models of cognition using behavioral data. It summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT), highlights sources of non-identifiability, and specifies constraints intended to render each component of the Bayesian pipeline identifiable. The authors instantiate these constraints, analyze parameter identifiability and differentiability, and apply the framework to two domains—time-interval estimation and motion perception—where Bayesian accounts are common.

Strengths
- The paper addresses a question of central importance: how to make Bayesian cognitive models empirically distinguishable and testable.
- The writing is generally clear, and the structure—from theoretical setup to applied demonstrations—is logical and accessible.
- Emphasizing identifiability explicitly is valuable and timely, given persistent concerns about the flexibility of Bayesian explanations in psychology and neuroscience.

Major concerns

1) Limited engagement with prior literature on model comparison and distinguishability
The paper’s contribution is difficult to evaluate without a fuller accounting of existing frameworks in cognitive science and psychology. Much of what is presented resembles established approaches to model selection, distinguishability, and model recovery. For instance:
- Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004) offer general treatments of model selection and the distinguishability/informativeness of data, including practical tools such as model recovery studies and analyses of parameter mimicry.
- Anderson (1978) formalized when different representational schemes are empirically indistinguishable, anticipating many current debates about identifiability in BDT.
- Broader critiques of Bayesian “just-so” flexibility (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) directly raise the identifiability problems the paper seeks to remedy, including in speed/motion perception.

The manuscript should position its contribution relative to this prior work. What is novel here beyond (i) restating identifiability within BDT; and (ii) applying a particular set of constraints in two case studies? Concrete suggestions:
- Provide a comparative mapping: how do the proposed constraints and diagnostics relate to established tools (e.g., Bayes factors, marginal likelihoods, MDL, posterior predictive checks, model recovery, distinguishability metrics)?
- Explicitly connect the framework to model mimicry results (e.g., when different priors/likelihoods/decision rules yield observational equivalence).
- Discuss how this approach remedies specific criticisms in Jones & Love (2011) and Bowers & Davis (2012), especially regarding flexibility in perception models.

2) Dependence on strong, sometimes unmotivated assumptions
Several modeling choices appear ad hoc, and it is unclear how robust the conclusions are to these choices.
- Why must the measurement model p_meas take the particular form in Eq. 3? Is there theoretical or empirical justification (e.g., derivation from sensory encoding constraints, known neural noise properties), or is it chosen for convenience?
- Is the parameterization in Eq. 12 sufficiently rich to capture the relevant cognitive/perceptual processes? While it may be “robust” in a broad sense, its sufficiency should be justified, at least by demonstrating coverage of competing functional forms used in the literature.

Recommended analyses and clarifications:
- Sensitivity analyses: Vary key assumptions (p_meas functional form, noise distributions, priors, decision rules) and quantify impact on model rankings and fitted parameters. Summarize robustness of comparisons.
- Model recovery: Simulate data from known generative models (including alternatives not conforming to Eq. 3/Eq. 12) and test whether the framework correctly identifies the true model. Report confusion matrices and recovery rates.
- Identifiability vs. differentiability: Demonstrate practical identifiability (e.g., via profile likelihoods, posterior curvature, simulation-based calibration), not only differentiability. Highlight parameter trade-offs and near-non-identifiabilities.
- Prior sensitivity and misspecification: Show how different prior choices influence conclusions; assess behavior under mild model misspecification.
- Distinguishability metrics: Where possible, quantify separability among models (e.g., through KL divergence, Fisher information, or predictive cross-validation).

Clarity and presentation
- Please define clearly the visual annotations in Figure 2. The caption suggests both the star and dashed lines indicate the true value; reconcile or clarify their distinct roles.
- Line 53: Rather than “It is a trivial mathematical fact…,” briefly explain why the fact holds or provide an intuition or reference. This will help readers unfamiliar with the underlying argument.
- Line 73: The second “nor” should be “or.”
- Consider consolidating notation early (e.g., mapping between stimulus, internal variable, measurement, decision) and maintain consistency across sections and figures. This will aid readers in following which component of BDT is being constrained at each step.

Assessment of applications
The two case studies are welcome, but it would help to:
- Include quantitative measures of predictive performance (e.g., cross-validated log likelihood, WAIC/LOO) alongside fit quality.
- Show posterior predictive checks that target the specific behaviors the models aim to explain (bias, variability, lapse behavior).
- Report uncertainty in parameter estimates and discuss practical identifiability in these datasets.
- Release code and data to facilitate reproduction and independent model recovery tests.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-written paper on a topic of high theoretical and practical importance. However, its contribution is undermined by limited engagement with the substantial prior literature on model distinguishability and by heavy reliance on assumptions that are currently under-justified. With a clearer positioning relative to prior work, explicit justification (or at least sensitivity analysis) of key modeling choices, and stronger empirical diagnostics of identifiability and recovery, the framework could make a valuable contribution to Bayesian modeling in cognition.
