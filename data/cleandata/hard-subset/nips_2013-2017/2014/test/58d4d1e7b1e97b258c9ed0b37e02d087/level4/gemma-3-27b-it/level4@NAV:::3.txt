This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing the mathematical formulation of sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions. A key strength lies in its combination of robustness – accommodating diverse scenarios like stimulus-dependent noise – and computational tractability, enabling efficient inference. The authors rightly highlight the model’s identifiability, demonstrating clear differentiation between parameter estimates. 

Overall, I favorably assess this work and recommend it for acceptance to NIPS. The model offers a mathematically sound framework for formalizing perceptual estimation applicable to a wide range of experimental settings. However, I do have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. The approach closely aligns with a growing body of recent work in Bayesian models of perception, notably appearing in publications such as *PLoS Computational Biology*. While not a direct replication, the similarities to existing models – for instance, those presented in Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) – lead me to characterize the advance as incremental.

To address this, I recommend the authors expand the manuscript with a dedicated section providing a thorough review of recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should clearly articulate how the proposed model surpasses existing approaches and represents a substantial advancement.

Furthermore, while the mathematical identifiability of the model is indeed a valid point, it is crucial to acknowledge that this identifiability stems from the model’s inherent assumptions. The model doesn’t resolve the broader identifiability challenges common in perceptual science—specifically, disentangling contributions from sensory, decision, and response noise. I suggest the authors address this nuance in the revised manuscript, commenting on the limitations of the model with respect to these broader perceptual inference questions.
