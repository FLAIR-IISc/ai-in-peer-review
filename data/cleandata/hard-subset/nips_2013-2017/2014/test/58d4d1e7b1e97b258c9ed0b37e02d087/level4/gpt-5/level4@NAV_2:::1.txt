This paper presents a framework for comparing Bayesian observer models on behavioral data with a focus on testing identifiability. The authors recap Bayesian Decision Theory, highlight sources of non-identifiability, and enumerate constraint types that can render the components of a Bayesian model identifiable. They then instantiate specific assumptions for each component of the framework, investigate how well different parameter settings can be distinguished, and apply the approach to two exemplar domains that have leveraged Bayesian decision theory to account for behavior: interval timing and visual motion perception.

Questions about the identifiability of internal representations and mechanisms have a long history in cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Anderson (1978) analyzed when different representational schemes can emulate one another, showing they are empirically indistinguishable absent process-level constraints if they partition inputs equivalently. More recently, limitations of the Bayesian approach—especially non-identifiability—have been a central point of critique (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012, the latter specifically discussing speed perception).

While the paper addresses a theoretically important issue, is clearly written, and generally executes the program effectively, I have two principal concerns. First, it largely overlooks an extensive prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, making it hard to situate the contribution. The proposed model-comparison framework is closely related to earlier work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, several assumptions appear ad hoc, and it is unclear how dependent the framework is on them. For example, why adopt the specific form for p_meas in Eq. 3? Is fixing the parameters referenced in Eq. 12 truly sufficient to specify and capture the relevant characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? Although the chosen forms are expressive and can represent a broad class of sensory models, this alone may not justify the assumptions; at minimum, the paper should explicitly motivate and defend the constraints used to render the Bayesian models identifiable.

Minor comments:
- Line 53: Rather than describing the result as “trivial,” it would be more helpful to briefly explain why it holds—this may not be obvious to all readers.
- Line 73: The second “nor” likely should be “or.”
- Figure 2: Please clarify what the star and dashed lines denote; the caption suggests both correspond to the true value.

Overall: A strong manuscript on a highly significant topic, but it needs stronger integration with prior related work.
