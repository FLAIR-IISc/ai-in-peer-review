This paper introduces a novel computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a critical issue in computational cognitive science. The authors correctly identify that the inherent degeneracy of the Bayesian framework—where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield empirically indistinguishable behavior—poses a significant challenge to the goal of recovering an observer's internal representations. To address this, they propose a flexible and computationally efficient parametric class of observer models. They then introduce a method to map the identifiability landscape by sampling from an approximate expected posterior density, which is a function of the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of a "true" reference model and other candidate models. This framework is applied to two case studies from the literature: inferring priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing the well-known "slow-speed prior" in visual perception. This work addresses a fundamental, long-standing problem in the field by providing a practical tool to aid in experimental design.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the crucial and notoriously difficult problem of model identifiability. Providing a systematic, computational method to evaluate the feasibility of recovering model parameters *before* conducting an experiment is a significant and original contribution. This approach has the potential to improve the rigor of experimental design in studies using Bayesian observer models.
*   **Methodological Innovation:** The development of a general yet computationally tractable class of observer models is a key strength. The use of an expected log-likelihood surface, approximated via KL-divergence, to explore the identifiability landscape is an elegant and powerful approach to the problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the motivation for the work is articulated clearly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary weakness of the work is that the proposed framework is built upon a large number of specific parametric assumptions. While necessary for tractability, the impact of these choices on the final conclusions could be discussed more thoroughly.

*   **Reliance on a Constrained Model Space:** The framework's success appears to depend significantly on strong built-in assumptions, such as the specific functional form of the sensory map (Eq. 2) and the mixture-of-Gaussians representations for the prior, likelihood, and loss. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the sensitivity of the identifiability analysis to these specific modeling choices. The current work could be interpreted as demonstrating that Bayesian models are identifiable *given a set of strong, simplifying assumptions*, which is a more constrained claim.
*   **Interpretation of Case Study Results:**
    1.  In the time-interval estimation study (Sec 4.1), the finding of a trade-off between sensory and motor noise is not surprising, given that both are modeled as approximately Gaussian in the internal (log-transformed) space. To improve clarity, it would be helpful if the authors could explicitly state which differences between the experimental designs (e.g., BSL vs. SRT) are reliable, as this is difficult to ascertain from the box plots alone.
    2.  In the speed perception study (Sec 4.2), the demonstration that the framework can recover a known slow-speed prior is compelling. However, a critical control experiment is needed to bolster this claim: showing that the framework can also correctly infer the *absence* of such a prior if the reference observer had a uniform prior. While the authors note this is covered in the supplement (the FLA condition), this point is central to validating the method's utility and would be worth briefly discussing in the main text.

**Specific Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Stimulus Distribution (line 106):** The model assumes a discrete stimulus distribution, though the underlying variable is continuous. While this often reflects experimental paradigms, a brief justification for this modeling choice would be helpful for the reader.
2.  **Unimodality of Likelihood (line 143):** The likelihood (Eq. 3) is represented as a mixture of two Gaussians, with parameters constrained by the first four moments and a maximum entropy principle. Is this sufficient to guarantee that the resulting distribution is always unimodal? It seems plausible that this parametrization could, in principle, yield a bimodal distribution.
3.  **Prior Representation (line 158):** Could the authors provide more motivation for using a lattice of Gaussians (Eq. 4) to represent the prior? Furthermore, what is the justification for setting the range of the observer's prior to be 50% wider than the true stimulus range?
4.  **Figure 1:** The figure is somewhat difficult to follow. The clarity could be improved by integrating Figure 1b more directly into the workflow of Figure 1a and perhaps removing `p_est(s*|x)`, as it is a derived quantity rather than a core component of the model.
5.  **Derivation of Identifiability Metric (line 252, Eq. 12):** The derivation of the expected log-likelihood in terms of KL-divergence relies on several approximations. A brief discussion of the conditions under which these approximations hold would increase confidence in the method. As written, the final metric feels justified primarily by its intuitive appeal rather than the rigor of its derivation.
6.  **Sampling Method (line 262):** The text states, "we sample from Eq. 13 via MCMC." Could the authors clarify precisely which density is being sampled from? Is it the posterior over the model parameters `θ`, conditioned on the reference model `θ*`?
7.  **Prior Learning in 2AFC (line 365):** The assumption that observers do not learn the experimental distribution of speeds when mapping the task from a 2AFC paradigm is a strong one. Could the authors comment on the validity of this assumption, especially since estimation tasks are known to induce prior learning?

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and innovative methodology to address the important problem of model identifiability. The approach is novel and provides a potentially valuable tool for experimental design. While the conclusions rely on a fairly constrained set of assumptions and the results could be strengthened with additional controls, the work is a strong contribution that is above the threshold for acceptance.
