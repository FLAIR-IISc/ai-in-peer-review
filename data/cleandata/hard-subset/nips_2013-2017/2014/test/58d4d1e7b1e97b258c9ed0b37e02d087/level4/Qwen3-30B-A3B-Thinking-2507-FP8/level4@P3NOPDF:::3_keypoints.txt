- Presents a mathematically rigorous Bayesian perceptual model specifying sensory noise, likelihoods, priors, loss, and response distributions.  
- Strength: robust across diverse perceptual scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while remaining computationally tractable for efficient inference.  
- Claims model identifiability (parameters are mathematically distinguishable) and positions this as important for empirical validation.  
- Contribution is incremental and substantially overlaps with recent Bayesian perception work (notably Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014), limiting novelty.  
- The manuscript lacks an explicit comparison to prior models; should add a "Related Work and Novelty" section to justify distinct value.  
- The identifiability claim is overstated: identifiability follows from model assumptions and does not solve the empirical problem of separating sensory, decision, and response noise.  
- Authors should clarify mathematical versus empirical identifiability and acknowledge limitations for real behavioral data (cite Kersten et al. 2004).  
- Recommended revisions: (1) explicitly position the model relative to prior work (comparative table suggested), (2) qualify identifiability claims and discuss implications for experiments, (3) strengthen motivation by identifying concrete experimental contexts where the model adds tangible advantages.  
- Overall: elegant and well-explained model useful for controlled experiments but requires major revisions on novelty and identifiability to merit acceptance; recommend conditional acceptance pending those changes.
