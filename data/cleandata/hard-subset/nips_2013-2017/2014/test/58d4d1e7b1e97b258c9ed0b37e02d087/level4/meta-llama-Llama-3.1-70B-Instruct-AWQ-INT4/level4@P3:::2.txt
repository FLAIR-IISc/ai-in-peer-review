This manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The work is nicely written and quite clear, presenting a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The proposed method is innovative and interesting, making the manuscript above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

The manuscript provides a thorough introduction to the problem of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors clearly explain the concept of identifiability and its importance in cognitive modeling. They also provide a comprehensive review of previous work on the topic, highlighting the need for a systematic approach to testing identifiability.

The proposed framework is based on a continuous class of Bayesian observer models, which allows for the efficient exploration of the identifiability landscape. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task. The results show that the framework can be used to rank experimental designs by their ability to resolve the underlying degeneracy of parameters of interest.

One of the strengths of the manuscript is its clarity and organization. The authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its application to the case studies. The text is well-structured, making it easy to follow the authors' argument.

However, there are some areas that require further clarification. For example, the authors assume that the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, which may not be immediately clear to readers. Additionally, the success of the work may be due in large part to the incorporation of the log-stimulus transform, which imposes a strong constraint on inference.

The manuscript also raises some questions about the assumptions underlying the model. For instance, the constraints in Eq 4 and the maximization of differential entropy do not necessarily ensure that Eqn 3 is a unimodal distribution. Furthermore, the observer's prior favors the lattice representation over a uniform representation, but it is not clear what the user knows about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior which is 50% wider than the true range.

In terms of the results, it would be helpful to indicate which condition differences were reliable in section 4.1. Additionally, in section 4.2, it is essential to show that if the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, the model could recover this fact as well.

Overall, the manuscript presents an innovative and important contribution to the field of cognitive modeling. While there are some areas that require further clarification, the work is well-written and clear, making it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
