

This paper proposes a framework for Bayesian model comparison in cognitive science, focusing on behavioral data. The authors summarize Bayesian decision theory, identify key non-identifiability issues, and outline constraint types to render terms identifiable. They then impose specific constraints, analyze differentiability of parameters, and apply the approach to two behavioral domains: time interval estimation and motion perception.  

Identifiability of internal representations and processes has been a longstanding challenge in cognitive science and psychology. Anderson (1978) demonstrated that representational formats can be indistinguishable without process constraints, as they make equivalent input distinctions. Despite the framework’s theoretical importance and clear exposition, recent literature (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) identifies identifiability as a major barrier to broader adoption of Bayesian models in cognitive science—Bowers & Davis (2012) explicitly cite speed perception as a case study.  

While the paper addresses a critical issue, I have two significant reservations. First, it overlooks substantial prior work on model comparison in cognitive science. The proposed framework largely overlaps with established approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), making it difficult to assess the novelty or contribution. Second, the assumptions used to enforce identifiability appear arbitrary without justification. For instance:  
- Why is $p_{meas}$ defined by Equation 3?  
- Is defining parameters in Equation 12 sufficient to capture all characteristics of a cognitive/perceptual process?  
While the framework is robust and expressive, the paper does not justify why these specific assumptions are necessary or adequate. A thorough discussion of the assumptions’ justifications is essential.
