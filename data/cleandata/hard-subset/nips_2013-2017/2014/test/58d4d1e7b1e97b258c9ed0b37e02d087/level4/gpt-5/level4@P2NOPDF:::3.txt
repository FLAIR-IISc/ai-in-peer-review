This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception that specifies sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions. A key strength is that it combines robustness with computational convenience: the framework accommodates a range of settings (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while keeping inference tractable. The authors also emphasize parameter identifiability, allowing competing parameterizations to be evaluated and distinguished within the model.

Overall, I like this work and would be happy to see it accepted to NIPS. The proposed framework is mathematically elegant and broadly applicable for formalizing perceptual estimation across many experimental paradigms. That said, I have reservations about the degree of novelty. The model is closely aligned in spirit and methodology with recent Bayesian approaches in the literature—particularly several papers in PLoS Computational Biology—and thus the contribution feels incremental rather than a substantial departure from prior work. For example:
- Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
- Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.

The current submission is not identical to these or other related articles, but the overlap in assumptions, modeling choices, and goals is substantial enough that the advance appears incremental.

I recommend adding a dedicated section that systematically positions this work within the landscape of recent Bayesian models of perception. Please make explicit what is genuinely new here—e.g., broader generality (which settings were previously unsupported), specific tractability results (proofs or algorithms enabling efficient inference where prior models struggled), formal identifiability results (and how they differ from earlier treatments), or distinct empirical predictions and better fits across tasks. A clear comparative analysis would strengthen the case for novelty.

A further point concerns identifiability. While the parameters are identifiable under the model’s assumptions (statistical identifiability within the specified parametric family), this does not resolve the kind of identifiability that many perceptual scientists prioritize: distinguishing the sources of behavioral error. In practice, the model, as presented, cannot decisively separate contributions from sensory noise, decision noise, and response/motor noise. Please discuss this limitation explicitly. If possible, include simulations or theoretical arguments illustrating parameter trade-offs among these components, outline conditions or experimental manipulations that could improve separability, and clarify which conclusions remain robust given these confounds.

In sum: this is a mathematically polished and useful framework for perceptual estimation, but its novelty is modest given closely related prior work. Strengthening the related-work comparison and clarifying the scope and limits of identifiability would significantly improve the manuscript.
