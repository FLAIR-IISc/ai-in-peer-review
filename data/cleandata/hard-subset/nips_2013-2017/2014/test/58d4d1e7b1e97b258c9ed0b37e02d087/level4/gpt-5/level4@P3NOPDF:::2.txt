This paper tackles the important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The manuscript is clearly written and the methodology is innovative. However, the main results rely on a long list of assumptions, some of which feel stronger than the evidence provided. Despite these concerns, I judge the contribution to be above the threshold for NIPS and recommend acceptance.

Major comments
- Scope and assumptions: The approach appears to achieve identifiability in large part due to strong structural assumptions (e.g., the log-stimulus transform and constraints on the prior). This is intellectually interesting, but it tempers the generality of the conclusions. It would help to more explicitly separate what is identifiable due to data and task design versus what is “built in” by representational assumptions.
- Section 4.1: The apparent interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is not surprising. Please report which between-condition differences are statistically reliable rather than relying on visual inspection of box plots, which are hard to interpret without explicit tests.
- Section 4.2: A crucial validation is missing. If the reference observer does not have a slow-speed prior, can your method recover this fact? Demonstrating recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior would directly support identifiability of prior structure rather than only confirming a favored hypothesis.

Specific comments by location
- Line 106: Why is the stimulus modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution when the underlying variable is continuous? If this is driven by typical experimental designs (discrete stimulus levels), please clarify the motivation and discuss any implications for inference or identifiability.
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform in Equation 2 is motivated by psychophysical laws, but it also imposes a strong constraint that may underwrite identifiability. To what extent do the results depend on this transform? Could the method still distinguish models if a different, plausible monotonic transform were used, or if the transform were unknown and estimated?
- Line 143: Do the constraints in Equation 4, together with the maximization of differential entropy, guarantee that the fitted distribution in Equation 3 is unimodal? It seems possible in principle to obtain a bimodal solution. If unimodality is intended, please state and justify the constraint; if not, discuss the implications for identifiability and interpretation.
- Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over a uniform one? More generally, what information would the observer plausibly have about the stimulus distribution that would lead to a prior that is 50% wider than the true range? Please justify these choices and assess sensitivity to them.
- Figure 1: The figure is difficult to parse. Consider integrating panel b into panel a and removing p_est(s*|x), which is derived, to focus the reader on the core generative and inference components. A simplified, single-panel schematic may improve clarity.
- Line 252: Please specify the conditions on N under which Equation 14 is a good approximation. In particular, (i) when is Stirling’s approximation adequate in your regime, and (ii) under what conditions is E[x log x] approximately E[x] log E[x]? The latter generally introduces a Jensen gap; quantifying or bounding this error would be helpful. Relatedly, by the time you reach Equation 15, you have dropped priors on θ and accumulated several approximations. As presented, Equation 15 reads as a heuristic justified primarily by yielding an intuitive similarity rule. Either provide a clearer derivation with error bounds or more explicitly label it as a practical heuristic.
- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density” is ambiguous. Which density do you mean—the dataset distribution, a posterior over parameters, or something else? Please specify exactly what is sampled and how normalization is handled.
- Line 365: Please clarify why learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC. Is this due to symmetry in the design, decision rules that cancel prior terms, or a specific property of the estimators used? A short derivation or intuitive argument would help.

Experimental and reporting suggestions
- Clearly report which effects are statistically significant, with effect sizes and confidence intervals where appropriate.
- Where possible, include recovery analyses: simulate from known ground-truth observers that violate your key assumptions (e.g., no slow-speed prior, different transform) and show whether the method recovers the correct structure versus “hallucinating” the assumed form.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for key assumptions: the choice of stimulus discretization, the width of the assumed prior, and the form of the transform.

Overall assessment
- Strengths: Well-written; tackles an important and underexplored problem; methodology is creative and potentially broadly useful; relevance to the community is high.
- Weaknesses: Heavy reliance on strong representational and distributional assumptions; limited validation that these assumptions are necessary rather than sufficient; some approximations are insufficiently justified; figure clarity and some notation/phrasing need work.

Recommendation: Weak accept. Innovative approach to an important problem, but the empirical results and theoretical guarantees are not yet overwhelming. Addressing the validation and clarity issues above would substantially strengthen the paper.
