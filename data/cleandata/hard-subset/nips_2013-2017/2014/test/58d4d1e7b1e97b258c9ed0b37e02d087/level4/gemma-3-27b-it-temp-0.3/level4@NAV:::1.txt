This article presents a framework for comparing Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors begin by reviewing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying potential issues with identifiability, and outlining constraints to achieve identifiability for each component of the Bayesian framework. They then apply this approach – involving assumptions and differentiable parameter exploration – to two established applications of Bayesian decision theory: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The challenge of identifying internal representations and processes has long been a central concern in cognitive science and psychology. Classic work, such as Anderson (1978), demonstrated how different representational formats can produce indistinguishable behavior without process constraints. More recently, limitations in identifiability have been recognized as a significant obstacle to the broader adoption of the Bayesian framework itself (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

While the paper addresses a theoretically important problem and does so clearly and effectively, I have two primary concerns. First, the work largely overlooks the existing literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, hindering a full assessment of its novelty. The proposed framework shares similarities with previous approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, some of the authors’ assumptions appear somewhat arbitrary, and the degree to which the framework’s validity relies on these assumptions is unclear. Specifically, the justification for defining $p_{meas}$ as in equation 3 is not fully explained. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the model’s ability to represent a broad class of sensory models, simply demonstrating robustness doesn’t fully justify the assumptions made to ensure identifiability; a more thorough discussion and justification of these constraints is needed.
