This manuscript addresses the critical issue of model identifiability within cognitive modeling, and is generally well-written and clear. While the methodology is interesting and represents a valuable innovation, the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions, and the impact of the key results feels somewhat limited given the extent of these assumptions. Nevertheless, I believe the work meets the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results: The finding in section 4.1 concerning the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising, and I questioned the necessity of including both noise types in the model during my initial reading. Clarifying which condition differences were statistically reliable would be beneficial. (I admit I struggled to interpret the box plots.) In section 4.2, it seems crucial to demonstrate that the model can also recover the scenario where the reference observer *does not* possess a slow-speed prior.

Specific comments:

line 106: The use of a discrete stimulus distribution, despite the underlying continuous nature of the variable, warrants clarification. While likely a consequence of typical experimental design, explicitly acknowledging this would be helpful.

line 134: The authors rightly point out the psychophysical basis for the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2). However, the success of this work may be heavily dependent on this transform, effectively imposing a strong constraint on inference. This suggests the work demonstrates identifiability of Bayesian models primarily *given* strong assumptions about representational transformations.

line 143: Do the constraints in Eq 4 and the differential entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fit for Eqn 3? It appears a bimodal distribution remains a theoretical possibility.

line 158: What justification exists for the observer’s prior favoring the lattice representation over a uniform one? What prior knowledge about the true stimulus distribution informs a prior that is 50% wider than the actual range?

Figure 1: The figure currently induces more confusion than clarity. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and removing p_est(s^*|x) – as a derived inference – might improve comprehension.

line 252: Could the authors elaborate on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, specifically regarding the applicability of Stirling’s approximation and the validity of E[xlogx] ~= E[x]logE[x]? By the time Equation 15 is reached – following the abandonment of priors on \theta and several approximations – its justification appears to rest primarily on its intuitive appeal for assessing prediction similarity.

line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: Could the authors specify which density is being referenced – the dataset distribution?

line 365: The lack of impact from prior learning in the 2AFC task is noted. Overall, this is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are not overwhelmingly impactful.
