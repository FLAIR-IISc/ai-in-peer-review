

This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception featuring well-defined mathematical components (sensory noise, likelihood function, prior distribution, loss function, response distribution). A key strength is its ability to handle diverse scenarios—such as stimulus-dependent noise—while maintaining computational tractability, offering both robustness and mathematical convenience. The model also demonstrates clear identifiability, meaning parameters are uniquely determined from the data under the specified assumptions. I find this a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation across experimental settings and recommend acceptance.

However, I have concerns about the novelty contribution. The model shares significant methodological overlap with recent work, particularly in PLoS Computational Biology (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014), and represents an incremental advance rather than a substantial departure. While the current submission is not identical to these prior works, the core approach—integrating probabilistic inference with experimental paradigms—aligns closely with established frameworks. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should add a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception and explicitly articulate how their model advances beyond existing literature. This would clarify the paper’s distinct value.

Additionally, the claim regarding identifiability warrants refinement. The model’s parameters are identifiable *mathematically* under its specific assumptions, but this does not resolve the common experimental challenge of distinguishing between sensory, decision, and response noise sources. As such, it does not address the primary identifiability concerns in perceptual science. The authors should clarify this distinction in the revision, acknowledging both the mathematical property and its limitations for experimental interpretation. 

In summary, the paper offers a technically sound and elegant model. Its primary weakness is insufficient differentiation from prior work. Addressing the novelty gap through a focused literature comparison and clarifying the scope of identifiability would significantly strengthen the submission. I recommend acceptance pending these revisions.
