- Summary: Presents a Bayesian perceptual-estimation model that specifies noise, likelihood, priors, loss, and response distributions, claiming robustness, tractability, and attention to identifiability.

- Strength: Clear, coherent Bayesian formulation unifying likelihood, priors, loss, and response.

- Strength: Flexible framework that can handle stimulus-dependent and non-Gaussian noise.

- Strength: Tractable inference suitable for data analysis and experimental design.

- Strength: Careful attention to model identifiability within the assumed generative structure.

- Strength: Potentially broadly applicable across perceptual tasks and datasets.

- Weakness: Novelty seems incremental relative to recent Bayesian perception literature (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014) and needs clearer positioning.

- Weakness: “Identifiability” claim is imprecise — structural identifiability under assumptions vs practical separability of sensory, decision, and response noise from behavioral data.

- Weakness: Unclear robustness to model misspecification (e.g., wrong loss or noise family); sensitivity analyses needed.

- Weakness: Unclear exactly what is novel (new likelihood/prior class, unifying parameterization, identifiability proofs, or algorithmic/inference advances).

- Revision request: Add a dedicated related-work section systematically comparing to recent Bayesian models (including Acerbi et al.) and clearly articulate specific advances.

- Revision request: Clarify identifiability claims by distinguishing structural vs practical identifiability, discuss limits of disentangling noise sources, and include simulations showing when separation is/ isn’t possible and how to improve it experimentally.

- Revision request: Provide parameter-recovery and model-recovery analyses under realistic sample sizes and noise, and test robustness to modest misspecification.

- Revision request: If claiming computational advantages, quantify them (runtime, convergence, scalability) against representative baselines.

- Revision request: Improve clarity and psychological interpretation of the loss function and show how inferences change across plausible alternative losses.

- Revision request: If feasible, include empirical demonstrations across multiple datasets/tasks and report goodness-of-fit and predictive performance.

- Minor: Define “identifiable” precisely at first use and use consistent structural vs practical terminology.

- Minor: Ensure consistent notation across the manuscript and consider a concise notation table.

- Minor: Consider releasing code to facilitate adoption and reproducibility.

- Recommendation: Positive overall — recommend acceptance conditional on revisions that (i) better position the contribution relative to recent work, (ii) temper and clarify identifiability claims, and (iii) include parameter/model-recovery and robustness analyses.
