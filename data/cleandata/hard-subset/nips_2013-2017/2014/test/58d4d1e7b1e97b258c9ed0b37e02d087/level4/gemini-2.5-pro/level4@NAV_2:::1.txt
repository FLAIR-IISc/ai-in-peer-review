This paper presents a framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks. The authors begin by summarizing the Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) framework and highlighting the inherent degeneracy problem, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior. They then propose a specific, yet flexible, class of observer models with parametric forms for each component of the BDT framework. This allows them to systematically explore the identifiability landscape and evaluate how different experimental designs might resolve parameter degeneracies. The utility of this approach is demonstrated through two case studies: recovering priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing both prior and noise characteristics in a speed perception task.

The problem of model and representational identifiability is a long-standing and fundamental issue in cognitive science. Foundational work by Anderson (1978), for instance, explored the conditions under which different representational formats are behaviorally indistinguishable. More recently, the non-identifiability of components in the BDT framework has been a central criticism leveled against the "Bayesian brain" hypothesis, hindering its broader acceptance (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). This paper is therefore timely and addresses a topic of significant theoretical importance.

While the paper is well-written and the proposed approach is generally sound and effective, I have two major reservations that temper my enthusiasm.

First, the paper's originality and contribution are difficult to assess because it does not engage with the extensive literature on model comparison and identifiability analysis within cognitive psychology and computational modeling. The general method of assessing how well model parameters can be recovered from data is conceptually similar to established frameworks (e.g., Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004; Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002). By not situating their work within this context, the authors miss an opportunity to clarify what is novel about their specific approach compared to these more general-purpose techniques.

Second, the framework's conclusions appear to rest on a series of specific parametric assumptions that are not always sufficiently justified. For example, the measurement distribution ($p_{meas}$) is defined as a two-component Gaussian mixture (Eq. 3), and similar choices are made for the prior and loss function. While the authors argue these forms are flexible, it is unclear how sensitive the reported identifiability results are to these particular modeling choices. The validity of the conclusions (e.g., that a narrow loss function is detrimental to recovering the prior in the time estimation task) may be contingent on these assumptions. A more thorough discussion and justification of these choices, perhaps including a sensitivity analysis, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of generality.

In summary, this paper addresses a crucial problem with a thoughtful and clearly presented framework. However, its significance would be substantially enhanced by explicitly connecting it to the rich prior literature on model selection/identifiability and by providing stronger justification for the specific modeling assumptions upon which the analyses rely.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The text describes a degeneracy as a "trivial mathematical fact." It would be more constructive for the reader to briefly explain *why* this degeneracy holds rather than simply stating it is trivial.
*   In the motivation section, there appears to be a typo in the sentence "there has been no systematic analysis... nor a framework...". The second "nor" should likely be "or".
*   In the caption for Figure 2, the meaning of the star and the dashed lines is ambiguous. Both seem to refer to the true value of the reference model, which is confusing. Please clarify.
