- Tackles the important problem of model identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models.  
- Proposes a novel framework to assess identifiability a priori using likelihood-surface analysis and KL divergence.  
- Demonstrates the approach on two case studies: time-interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Finds sensory and motor noise are difficult to disentangle in time-interval estimation.  
- Shows robustness of inferences about a slow-speed prior in speed perception but lacks a negative control to test disproving a prior.  
- Writing is clear and the manuscript is well-organized.  
- Mathematical formulation is theoretically rigorous and technically sound.  
- Framework depends on strong assumptions about priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and noise distributions.  
- Limited sensitivity analysis leaves open how conclusions change when assumptions are violated.  
- Results are insightful but incremental rather than transformative.  
- Figure 1 is convoluted and could be simplified (e.g., merge panels and remove unnecessary elements).  
- Some mathematical approximations (e.g., around lines 252, 262, and Eq. 15) lack sufficient justification and need clarification.  
- Specific methodological clarifications requested: why discrete stimulus sampling (Line 106), justification of the logarithmic stimulus transform (Line 134), how constraints ensure unimodality (Line 143), and why prior is set 50% wider (Line 158).  
- Request to state conditions under which approximations (e.g., Stirling’s) are valid (Line 252) and to specify which density is sampled (Line 262).  
- Ask authors to justify why prior learning is assumed irrelevant in the 2AFC task (Line 365).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a timely problem, offers a principled tool for experimental design, and has sufficient technical contribution for NIPS.  
- Arguments for rejection: incremental advance, strong limiting assumptions, and insufficient robustness/sensitivity checks.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to justify approximations, clarify assumptions, improve figures, and include a negative control for speed perception.
