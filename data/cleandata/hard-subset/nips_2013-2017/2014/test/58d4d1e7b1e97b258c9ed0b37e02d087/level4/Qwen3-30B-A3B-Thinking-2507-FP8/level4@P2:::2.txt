

This paper addresses a critical issue in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks. The authors propose a systematic framework for testing identifiability of a significant class of Bayesian models, with practical applications for experimental design. This work builds on previous efforts in Bayesian modeling of perception [12, 19] but moves beyond ad hoc solutions by providing a general theoretical framework for identifiability analysis. The paper's approach is particularly relevant given the growing use of Bayesian models to infer internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data in cognitive science.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of identifiability, demonstrating that multiple combinations of model components can produce identical behavior, making recovery of internal representations problematic without proper constraints. The authors develop a comprehensive observer model class that enables efficient exploration of identifiability, and apply it to two well-studied perceptual tasks: time interval estimation and speed perception.

**Strengths:**
- The paper's core contribution is theoretically sound and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The framework provides a systematic way to evaluate identifiability a priori, which is valuable for experimental design.
- The writing is clear and well-organized, with thorough mathematical development and careful attention to detail.
- The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility. The results for speed perception (Section 4.2) are particularly convincing, showing robust recovery of internal representations with minimal bias.
- The paper provides specific, actionable recommendations for experimental design (e.g., measuring motor parameters to reduce degeneracy).

**Weaknesses:**
- The framework relies on several strong assumptions that may limit its applicability. The sensory transformation (Equation 2) is particularly crucial, as it imposes a strong constraint on the model that may not generalize to all perceptual tasks.
- The interpretation of the time interval estimation results (Section 4.1) is less compelling. The finding that a narrower loss function (MAP) worsens inference quality is counterintuitive and requires stronger justification. The authors should clarify why this occurs within their model framework.
- The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the framework's ability to recover the absence of a prior (as suggested in the draft review's specific comment about Section 4.2). This would strengthen the claim that the framework reliably identifies internal representations.
- The manuscript could benefit from more explicit discussion of the framework's limitations and potential extensions to other perceptual domains.

**Specific Comments:**
- Line 106: The use of discrete stimulus distributions appears to be a modeling convenience rather than a reflection of experimental practice. The authors should clarify this choice, as perceptual tasks often use continuous stimuli.
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is central to the model's success. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge that this transform imposes a strong constraint on inference, making the identifiability results dependent on this assumption.
- Line 143: The unimodality constraint for the sensory model (Equation 3) requires more justification. The authors should clarify how the entropy maximization procedure ensures unimodality.
- Line 158: The prior representation (Equation 4) uses a lattice spacing 50% wider than the true stimulus range. The authors should explain why this specific choice is optimal rather than arbitrary.
- Figure 1: The current figure is confusing. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) would improve clarity.
- Line 252: The approximations leading to Equation 15 require more careful justification. The authors should specify the conditions under which these approximations hold.
- Line 262: The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous. The authors should clarify which density they refer to.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and technically sound approach. While the results are not groundbreaking in terms of new perceptual insights, the framework itself represents a valuable methodological contribution to the field. The significance lies in providing researchers with a tool to design better experiments for inferring internal representations. The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making a strong case for its contribution.

Given the theoretical importance of the problem, the paper's methodological contribution, and the clear presentation, I recommend acceptance. The framework has potential to influence experimental design across multiple domains in cognitive science and neuroscience. The weaknesses noted are important but do not undermine the paper's core contribution.

The work meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. It is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The originality lies in the systematic framework for identifiability analysis, which is a novel contribution to the field.
