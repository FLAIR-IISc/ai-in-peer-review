This paper proposes a framework for performing model comparison of Bayesian models on behavioral data. The authors begin by summarizing the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, pinpointing sources of non-identifiability, and outlining the types of constraints that can be used to make each term in the framework identifiable. They then introduce a specific set of assumptions to constrain each term, explore the differentiability of the model's parameters, and apply their technique to two case studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The problem of identifiability of internal representations and processes is a long-standing and prominent issue in cognitive science and psychology. For example, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that different representational formats can be indistinguishable without constraints on their associated processes. More recently, the non-identifiability problem has been highlighted as a major drawback of the Bayesian framework, hindering its widespread adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter of which directly addresses speed perception as an example).

While this paper addresses a topic of significant theoretical importance, and is generally well-written and effective, I have two major reservations.

First, the paper largely ignores the extensive previous literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. This omission makes it difficult to properly evaluate the novelty and contribution of the proposed framework. The general approach for model comparison presented here appears conceptually similar to previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), and the authors should situate their contribution within the context of this existing literature.

Second, the authors rely on several assumptions that appear arbitrary, and it is unclear to what extent the framework's success depends on these specific choices. For example, what is the justification for defining $p_{meas}$ according to Equation 3? Similarly, is the parameterization in Equation 12 truly sufficient to define and capture the full characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? The authors argue that this parameterization is robust and can express a large class of sensory models, but this does not seem to be a sufficient justification on its own. At a minimum, the paper should provide a more thorough discussion and justification for the specific assumptions used to render the Bayesian models identifiable.
