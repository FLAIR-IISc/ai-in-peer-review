This manuscript addresses the important and challenging problem of model identifiability within cognitive modeling. The paper is well-written and generally clear. However, the work relies on a substantial list of assumptions, and the strength and number of these assumptions seem disproportionate to the impact of the key results presented. Nevertheless, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and on this basis, I recommend the paper for acceptance.

Regarding the key results:
*   In Section 4.1, the finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not particularly surprising; one might question the initial decision to include both as distinct components in the model. It would be helpful if the authors explicitly stated which differences between conditions are statistically significant.
*   In Section 4.2, a crucial validation is missing. To confirm the model's recovery capabilities, it seems essential to demonstrate that it can also recover the *absence* of a slow-speed prior in a reference observer who does not possess one.

Specific comments:
*   **line 106:** The stimulus, which is inherently a continuous variable, is modeled as being drawn from a discrete distribution. While this likely reflects the common experimental design where specific stimulus levels are chosen as independent variables, the authors should clarify this modeling choice.
*   **line 134:** The authors motivate the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) by citing established psychophysical laws. It appears that the success of the proposed method might hinge heavily on this specific transform, which imposes a strong constraint on the inference process. Consequently, the paper's central claim could be interpreted as: Bayesian models are identifiable, provided one accepts strong, pre-specified assumptions about the nature of mental representations.
*   **line 143:** Regarding Equation 3, do the constraints in Equation 4, combined with the maximization of differential entropy, guarantee that the resulting distribution will be unimodal? It seems plausible that this procedure could, in principle, yield a multimodal distribution.
*   **line 158:** The choice of the observer's prior warrants further justification. Why should it favor a lattice representation over a simpler uniform one? Furthermore, what knowledge does the observer possess that would justify a prior that is 50% wider than the actual stimulus range?
*   **Figure 1:** This figure is more confusing than illuminating. Its clarity could be improved by integrating panel (b) into panel (a) and removing the plot of p_est(s^*|x), as this term represents a derived inference rather than a core model component.
*   **line 252:** The authors should comment on the conditions on N under which the approximation in Equation 14 is valid. Specifically, what are the requirements for Stirling's approximation to hold, and under what circumstances is it justified to approximate E[x log x] with E[x] log E[x]? By the time Equation 15 is introduced—following the decision to abandon priors on θ and the use of several questionable approximations—its primary justification seems to be its intuitive appeal for assessing predictive similarity, rather than a rigorous derivation.
*   **line 262:** The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous. Please clarify which density is being referred to—is it the distribution of the dataset?
*   **line 365:** The claim that the learning of priors does not matter in a two-alternative forced choice (2AFC) task is counterintuitive and requires explanation.

In summary, the paper presents an innovative approach to an important problem, but the impact of the results is less compelling than the methodology itself.
