Summary
This paper tackles a central challenge in Bayesian observer modeling: the non-identifiability of internal components (priors, likelihoods, and loss functions) when inferring them from behavioral data. The authors propose a general, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional estimation tasks, designed to cover a wide family of priors, likelihoods, and losses while retaining computational tractability. They introduce an a priori identifiability framework that quantifies how well different parameter settings can be distinguished under a fixed experimental design. Their key device is an expected log-likelihood (under a reference “true” observer) that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions; this yields an “approximate expected posterior” over parameters that can be explored via MCMC before collecting data. Two case studies demonstrate how the framework can be used to (i) compare experimental designs for interval timing and (ii) assess the reconstructability of a slow-speed prior in speed perception.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on extensive literature using Bayesian decision theory to model perception and infer internal representations (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Maloney & Mamassian; Houlsby et al., 2013; Acerbi et al., 2012; 2014). The novelty here is the explicit, pre-experimental analysis of practical identifiability via an analytically tractable expected support measure and a unified parametric observer class that enables systematic exploration across priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. This complements general distinguishability/identifiability methods in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004) by tailoring them to Bayesian observer models and by delivering design guidance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling pipeline (sensory mapping to internal space; mixture-of-Gaussians measurement and loss; lattice-mixture prior) is coherent and computationally efficient. The expected log-likelihood expression as a KL sum is correct and appropriate for a priori design comparison under the assumed generative model. The case studies are carefully specified and the findings (e.g., degeneracy between sensory and motor noise in interval timing; robustness of slow-speed prior inference) are plausible.
- Limitations: Conclusions depend on parametric choices (two-component mixtures; prior lattice resolution; specific f(s) mapping). The analysis is theoretical/simulated; no empirical validation of the design rankings is presented. The framework currently targets 1D estimation; extensions to discrete-choice tasks (e.g., 2AFC) are discussed but not implemented. Some potentially important confounds (e.g., decision noise vs sensory noise vs motor noise) are acknowledged but not systematically disentangled.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The modeling assumptions and parameterization are described clearly, and the rationale for the internal-space mapping and mixture parameterizations is well-motivated. It would help to:
- More explicitly define “identifiability” in this context (practical a priori distinguishability under a specified generative model), and contrast it with other uses in psychophysics (e.g., dissociating sensory, decision, and response noise).
- Add a focused related-work section that situates the contributions relative to recent Bayesian observer inversion and recovery papers (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012; 2014; Houlsby et al., 2013; Navarro et al., 2004; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), highlighting what is genuinely new (KL-based design comparison; unified tractable observer class; explicit design ranking).
- Report computational costs, sensitivity to key hyperparameters (number of prior components M; mixture components; prior range), and provide practical guidance for users.

Originality
The individual modeling components echo prior work, and the paper’s contributions are incremental in that sense. The more novel aspect is the explicit, general framework for a priori identifiability analysis tailored to Bayesian observer models, coupled with design comparisons. This fills a gap between model fitting papers and generic model distinguishability methods, although parts of the approach (e.g., KL-based expected evidence) are standard.

Significance
The framework is useful for experimenters planning studies that aim to recover internal representations. The interval timing case study yields actionable recommendations (e.g., independent measurement of motor parameters can break key degeneracies; encouraging MAP-like losses may harm prior recovery). The speed perception analysis provides theoretical reassurance about robustness of slow-speed prior inference. With broader validation and clearer guidance, this framework could see practical adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-discussed issue (practical identifiability) with a principled, tractable method.
- Provides a unified observer class that facilitates broad exploration across priors/likelihoods/losses.
- Delivers concrete, sometimes counterintuitive design insights for interval timing; corroborates robustness in speed perception.
- Clear exposition and thoughtful case studies.

Cons against acceptance
- Incremental relative to recent Bayesian observer inference literature; innovation lies more in synthesis and framing than in fundamentally new methodology.
- Strong reliance on specific parametric forms; limited sensitivity analyses.
- No application to real data or empirical validation of design rankings.
- Scope currently limited to 1D estimation; discrete-choice tasks are not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
1) Add a dedicated related-work section contrasting the framework with prior Bayesian recovery approaches (Acerbi et al., 2012; 2014; Houlsby et al., 2013; Sanborn & Griffiths, 2008; Navarro et al., 2004; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), and articulate the specific advances here.
2) Clarify the definition and scope of “identifiability,” and explicitly discuss limits to dissociating sensory, decision, and response noise within the proposed framework. Indicate which degeneracies are resolvable under what design constraints.
3) Provide sensitivity analyses (e.g., number of prior components, mixture components for likelihood/loss, prior range, mapping f(s)), and report computational costs. Offer practical recommendations for choosing parameterizations.
4) If possible, include an application to real behavioral data or a benchmark recovery study comparing alternative designs, to validate the predicted design rankings.
5) Elaborate on extensions to 2AFC and multi-cue settings and outline any expected changes in identifiability.

Overall evaluation
This is a mathematically elegant and practically motivated framework that can help design more informative experiments and interpret model recovery results. While the contribution is somewhat incremental relative to recent work, the focus on a priori identifiability and design guidance is valuable. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying novelty, better situating the work in the literature, and explicitly discussing the limits of identifiability (especially the separation of sensory, decision, and motor contributions).
