- Paper presents a framework to assess a priori practical identifiability of Bayesian observer models in 1D perceptual estimation, with extensions to angular and symmetric multidimensional cases.
- Introduces a flexible observer class: stimulus→internal mapping with stimulus-dependent noise, Gaussian-mixture priors on a lattice, internal likelihoods, mixture loss functions, lapses, and motor/report noise.
- Behavioral response distributions are computed by marginalizing unobserved variables; mixture forms enable computational efficiency.
- Defines expected log-evidence (stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences scaled by trial count) as an identifiability metric.
- Uses MCMC to approximate the expected posterior over parameters and map identifiability under different experimental designs.
- Two case studies: interval timing (short/long conditions) and speed perception (slow-speed prior) illustrate method and implications for design.
- Interval timing result: priors are hard to recover unless motor parameters are known; narrowing loss toward MAP reduces prior recovery; adding trials in one condition gives limited benefit.
- Speed perception result: priors and noise are generally well recovered, even with decoupled internal likelihood, supporting empirical slow-speed prior claims.
- Builds on extensive prior Bayesian observer work (Acerbi et al., Stocker & Simoncelli, Jazayeri & Shadlen) and model distinguishability literature (Navarro et al.).
- Main contribution is systematizing a priori practical identifiability and enabling design-ranking, rather than proposing a novel observer model.
- Strength: coherent, expressive framework with an efficient identifiability metric useful for comparing experimental designs and pinpointing limiting parameters.
- Strength: computational tractability via mixture-based forms makes approach scalable to realistic designs.
- Strength: case studies are relevant and tie results to active empirical debates.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental—methodology and cases closely align with prior work, including by the authors.
- Weakness: identifiability is limited to the assumed parametric family and cannot generally disentangle sensory, decision, and response noise or guarantee recovery under model misspecification.
- Weakness: limited comparison to alternative identifiability tools (Fisher information, profile likelihoods, design-of-experiments) and no runtime/accuracy benchmarks.
- Weakness: some modeling choices need sensitivity analysis (mixture order, lattice spacing, transform f(s), loss form, 2AFC→estimation mapping).
- Suggestions: add a focused related-work section, clarify terminology/scope as a priori identifiability within a model family, and discuss unidentifiable aspects without extra constraints.
- Suggestions: include sensitivity analyses to mis-specified priors/loss/transform and compare KL-based metric to Fisher/D-optimality on at least one case study.
- Suggestions (interval timing): discuss low identifiability of loss width, reconcile with MAP claims, and recommend concrete designs (e.g., motor-noise calibration).
- Suggestions (speed perception): justify 2AFC→estimation mapping and test robustness to lapse-rate mis-specification and contrast-dependent internal noise decoupling.
- Pros for acceptance: useful, general framework that helps anticipate identifiability issues, methodologically sound, and provides actionable insights for experiment design.
- Cons for acceptance: incremental conceptual novelty, unclear separation of identifiability scope from standard model-equivalence/noise confounds, and limited benchmarking against alternative methods.
- Overall: mathematically elegant and practically useful; with stronger positioning relative to prior work and clearer limits of identifiability, it would be a solid NIPS contribution.
