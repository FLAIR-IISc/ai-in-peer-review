In this article, the authors propose a framework for systematically assessing the identifiability of Bayesian models applied to perceptual behavior. They begin by outlining the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identifying inherent issues of non-identifiability, and detailing constraints that can be applied to achieve identifiability of model components. They then introduce assumptions to constrain their model, explore the parameter space to assess differentiability, and apply the framework to two established case studies utilizing Bayesian models: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The challenge of identifying underlying representations and processes has been a longstanding concern in cognitive science and psychology. Early work, such as Anderson (1978), demonstrated how different representational formats can produce indistinguishable behavior without sufficient process constraints. More recently, concerns regarding identifiability have emerged as a key limitation hindering wider adoption of the Bayesian framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter specifically addresses speed perception). 

This article addresses a critically important theoretical issue and is generally well-written and effectively executed. However, I have two primary reservations. First, the work overlooks a substantial body of previous research on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making a comprehensive evaluation of its novel contribution challenging. The proposed framework shares similarities with existing approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, some of the authors’ assumptions appear arbitrary, and the sensitivity of the framework to these assumptions remains unclear. For instance, the justification for defining $p_{meas}$ with equation 3 is not fully developed. While the authors note its flexibility in representing various sensory models, this does not fully address the rationale for this specific functional form. Similarly, the sufficiency of the constraints imposed in Equation 12 for fully capturing the complexity of cognitive and perceptual processes requires further discussion and justification.
