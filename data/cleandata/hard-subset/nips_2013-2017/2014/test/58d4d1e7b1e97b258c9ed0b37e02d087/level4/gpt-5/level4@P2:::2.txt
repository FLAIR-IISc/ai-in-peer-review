Summary
This paper addresses a central issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling: whether priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can be uniquely inferred from behavioral data. The authors develop a general framework for a priori identifiability analysis of a broad class of Bayesian observer models. The key idea is to define an approximate expected posterior over model parameters by computing the expected log likelihood of data generated by a reference observer, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions. The framework relies on a flexible, computationally efficient observer model (mixtures of Gaussians for likelihood and loss, a parametric sensory transform, and a lattice prior representation), and is used to compare experimental designs before data collection. Two case studies are presented: interval timing (where identifiability of priors is poor unless motor noise is independently known and MAP-like loss worsens recovery) and speed perception (where slow-speed priors and noise parameters are recovered robustly, even when decoupling internal from external noise).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Bayesian observer models in perception and action (e.g., Knill & Richards; Geisler; Körding & Wolpert; Stocker & Simoncelli) and engages with concerns about “just-so” explanations by focusing on identifiability (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung). The case studies connect to temporal context effects (Jazayeri & Shadlen; Acerbi et al.) and speed perception under a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli; Kwon & Knill).

Strengths
- Important problem with practical implications for experimental design.
- Methodologically novel and potentially general: expected log likelihood/KL-based identifiability mapping with MCMC exploration.
- Computationally efficient observer class; analytical expected loss for mixtures facilitates exploration.
- Clear, relevant case studies; the speed-perception results provide theoretical support for prior findings.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on specific modeling assumptions (discrete stimuli, the particular sensory transform, Gaussian mixture forms for likelihood and loss, lattice prior representation, shared parameters across conditions). The results’ robustness to these choices needs more systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Some findings are expected (e.g., trade-offs between sensory and motor noise in timing).
- Several clarity issues (notably Figure 1 and some derivational approximations).
- The paper would benefit from more explicit statements of which differences in the results are reliable and from a stronger negative-control demonstration in the main text.

Quality
The approach is technically sound overall, but hinges on approximations whose conditions of validity should be spelled out (see detailed comments). The empirical demonstrations are well-constructed, though some conclusions depend strongly on modeling choices (e.g., loss-function form and width).

Clarity
Generally well written. However, parts of the model specification and figures could be clearer (e.g., justification for discrete stimuli; Figure 1 layout; explicit identification of the “unnormalized density” used for sampling). Reporting which effects are statistically reliable would improve interpretability.

Originality
High. The a priori, design-guiding identifiability analysis for Bayesian observer models is novel and valuable.

Significance
Moderate to high. If adopted, the framework could improve the design and interpretation of psychophysical studies using Bayesian models.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a foundational problem with a practical, general tool.
- Well-motivated, computationally efficient framework.
- Concrete design guidance demonstrated in two domains.

Cons:
- Many assumptions; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Some results unsurprising; clarity gaps (figures, approximations).
- Negative control (no slow-speed prior) relegated to supplementary materials.

Detailed comments and questions
- Section 4.1: The interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is not surprising. Please quantify which condition differences are reliable (e.g., credible intervals or statistical tests), as the box plots are hard to interpret.
- Section 4.2: It is essential to demonstrate that, if the reference observer lacks a slow-speed prior, the method recovers that fact. If this is shown only in the supplement (FLA), consider presenting a concise version in the main text.
- Line 106: Clarify why stimuli are modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution when the underlying variable is continuous. If this mirrors typical experimental designs (fixed stimulus levels), state this explicitly and comment on the approximation quality as Nexp increases.
- Line 134: The log-like sensory transform (Eq. 2) is a strong constraint. Please discuss how much identifiability depends on this choice, and provide a sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying d and s0, or using alternative transforms).
- Line 143: Do the constraints and maximum-entropy choice guarantee that the mixture in Eq. 3 remains unimodal? Under what circumstances could the fit become bimodal, and how is this prevented in practice?
- Line 158: Why favor a lattice mixture prior over, say, a smooth basis or uniform representation? Also, on what basis does the observer’s prior span a range 50% wider than the true stimulus range? Please justify and assess sensitivity to this design choice.
- Figure 1: The figure is harder to follow than necessary. Consider integrating panel b into panel a, and possibly removing p_est(s*|x) from the graphic (it is derived), or make its derivation explicit to avoid confusion.
- Line 252: Please state the conditions on N under which the approximations used (e.g., Stirling’s approximation, and replacing E[x log x] with E[x] log E[x]) are accurate, and quantify the expected error. The leap to Eq. 15 is plausible but would benefit from a tighter justification or bounds.
- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density” — specify which density (presumably the approximate expected posterior E(θ|θ*) from Eq. 13) and describe the MCMC details (proposal, adaptation, convergence checks).
- Line 365: Provide a clearer justification for why learning of priors does not matter in the 2AFC formulation you analyze, given evidence that priors can adapt to experimental distributions.

Recommendation
Accept for presentation. The methodological contribution is innovative and useful, the paper is generally clear, and the applications are informative, though some results are unsurprising and several assumptions warrant additional justification and sensitivity analysis.
