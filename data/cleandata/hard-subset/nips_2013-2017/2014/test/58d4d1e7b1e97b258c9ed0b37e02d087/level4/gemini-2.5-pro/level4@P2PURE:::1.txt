This paper presents a framework for model comparison of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors summarize Bayesian Decision Theory, identify key areas of non-identifiability, and propose a set of constraints to render each component of the Bayesian framework identifiable. To demonstrate their approach, the authors introduce specific assumptions to constrain the model's terms, analyze the differentiability of the model's parameters, and apply their method to two case studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The problem of identifiability for internal representations and processes is a long-standing and prominent issue in cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that different representational formats can be computationally indistinguishable without imposing constraints on their associated processes. More recently, this issue has been highlighted as a significant drawback hindering the widespread adoption of the Bayesian cognitive modeling framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012), making the topic of this paper both timely and important.

Although this paper addresses a topic of significant theoretical importance and is generally well-written, I have two major reservations.

First, the manuscript fails to engage with the extensive prior literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. This omission makes it difficult to properly evaluate the novel contributions of the proposed framework, which bears a strong resemblance to previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004).

Second, the framework relies on several assumptions whose justifications are either unclear or appear arbitrary, and the extent of the framework's dependence on these specific choices is not explored. For instance, the motivation for defining the measurement probability $p_{meas}$ according to Equation 3 is not provided. Similarly, it is questionable whether the parameterization in Equation 12 is sufficient to capture the full characteristics of a given cognitive or perceptual process. While the authors note this parameterization is robust and can express a large class of sensory models, this does not serve as a complete justification. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a thorough discussion and justification of the specific assumptions used to achieve model identifiability.
