The authors propose a framework for comparing Bayesian models on behavioral data, addressing a crucial issue in cognitive science and psychology. They summarize the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identify areas of non-identifiability, and outline constraints to make each term identifiable. The authors then apply this framework to two studies, exploring the differentiability of parameter values and applying the technique to time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issue of identifiability of internal representations and processes has been a longstanding problem in cognitive science and psychology. For example, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that representational formats can be indistinguishable without constraints on processes. Recently, this issue has hindered the widespread adoption of the Bayesian framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

Although the article addresses an issue of great theoretical importance and is well-written, I have two major reservations. First, the authors largely ignore the extensive literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making it challenging to evaluate the contribution of this work. The proposed framework bears similarities to previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), and the authors should acknowledge and build upon these existing efforts.

Second, the authors make assumptions that seem arbitrary at times, and it is unclear how much of the framework depends on these assumptions. For instance, the definition of $p_{meas}$ by equation 3 appears unjustified. Similarly, the definition of parameter values in Equation 12 may not be sufficient to capture the characteristics of cognitive or perceptual processes. Although the authors argue that their framework can express a large class of sensory models, this does not necessarily justify the assumptions made. The article should discuss and justify these assumptions to ensure that the constrained Bayesian models are identifiable.

Minor comments:

* Line 53: Instead of stating that a mathematical fact is trivial, it would be more helpful to explain the underlying reasoning.
* Line 73: The second "nor" should be replaced with "or" for clarity.
* Figure 2: The caption does not clearly explain the meaning of the star and dashed lines. It appears that both lines denote the true value, but this should be explicitly stated.

Overall, the article is well-written and addresses a critical issue in cognitive science and psychology. However, it would benefit from a more thorough discussion of previous related work and a clearer justification of the assumptions made in the proposed framework.
