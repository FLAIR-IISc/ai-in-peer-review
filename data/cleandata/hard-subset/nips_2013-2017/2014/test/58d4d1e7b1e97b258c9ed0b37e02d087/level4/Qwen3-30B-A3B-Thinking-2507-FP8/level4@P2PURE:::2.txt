

The manuscript addresses a significant challenge in cognitive modeling: model identifiability. It is well-written and clear. However, the work rests on an extensive set of assumptions that must be accepted, and this list is disproportionately long relative to the significance of the key results. Despite this, the methodology is interesting and innovative, placing the work above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding key results:  
In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising (I questioned including both in the model while reading). It would be helpful to specify which condition differences are reliable (perhaps I simply lack experience interpreting box plots).  
In Section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that if the reference observer lacked a slow-speed prior, the model could still recover this fact.

Specific comments:  
Line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is actually a continuous variable? This likely stems from typical experimental design (where stimulus level is the independent variable), but the authors should clarify.  
Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, appears central to the work’s success. The transform imposes strong constraints on inference, suggesting the paper’s core claim—that Bayesian models are identifiable under strong assumptions about mental representational transformations—may be heavily dependent on this choice.  
Line 143: Do the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy ensure Equation 3 is fit to a unimodal distribution? The fit could theoretically yield a bimodal distribution.  
Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over a uniform one? What knowledge justifies a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range?  
Figure 1: After careful examination, I remain more confused than enlightened by this figure. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) (a derived inference) would improve clarity.  
Line 252: The authors should comment on the conditions on N for Equation 14’s approximation to hold (e.g., validity of Stirling’s approximation for factorials, and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]). By the time Equation 15 is introduced—after abandoning priors on θ and making questionable approximations—I feel Equation 15 is justified solely by its yielding an intuitive rule for assessing prediction similarity.  
Line 262: "Sample from this unnormalized density"—which density? The dataset distribution?  
Line 365: Why does learning priors not matter in 2AFC?  

Innovative approach, important problem, less than overwhelming results.
