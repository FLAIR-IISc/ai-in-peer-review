This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception that provides a comprehensive mathematical framework including equations for sensory noise, the likelihood function, a prior probability distribution, a loss function, and the response distribution. A significant strength of this model is its successful combination of robustness and mathematical convenience. Specifically, the model can accommodate a wide range of experimental conditions, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while remaining computationally tractable for inference. The authors also place considerable emphasis on the model's mathematical identifiability, meaning its parameters can be uniquely recovered from data.

Overall, I am supportive of this paper and would recommend its acceptance to the NIPS conference. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation that could be valuable in many experimental contexts.

However, I have some reservations that should be addressed in a revision. My primary concern is that the model is conceptually very similar to other Bayesian perceptual models that have recently appeared in the literature, particularly in journals like *PLoS Computational Biology*. Consequently, the novel contribution of this work feels more incremental than transformative. For example, the approach is reminiscent of the work by:

*   Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology, 8*(11), e1002771.
*   Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology, 10*(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other prior articles, the parallels are strong enough to characterize its contribution as incremental. To address this, I strongly suggest that the authors add a new section to the manuscript. This section should review recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and explicitly articulate how the proposed model constitutes a significant advance over this existing body of work.

My second comment relates to the authors' emphasis on identifiability. While it is true that the model is mathematically identifiable, this property is a direct consequence of its specific mathematical assumptions. This formal definition of identifiability, however, does not address the more practical identifiability challenge that concerns most perceptual scientists. For instance, the proposed model cannot be used to disambiguate whether an experimental subject's behavioral variability stems from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. As a result, the model does not resolve the core identifiability problem that is a central focus in the field. I ask that the authors comment on this limitation and clarify the scope of their identifiability claims in the revised manuscript.
