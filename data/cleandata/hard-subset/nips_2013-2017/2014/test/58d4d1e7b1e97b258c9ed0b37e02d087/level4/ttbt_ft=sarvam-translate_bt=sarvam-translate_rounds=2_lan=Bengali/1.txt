In this article, the authors propose a framework for comparing Bayesian models based on behavioral data. To do this, they outline a summary of the framework of Bayesian decision theory, identify non-identifiable fields, and highlight the types of limitations that can be used to make each term identifiable in the Bayesian framework. They then make some assumptions to limit each term in the Bayesian framework, investigate how separable the parameter values are in their model, and apply this technique to two studies to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issues of internal representation and process identifiability have been a significant problem in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when representation patterns can imitate each other, proving that processes cannot be separated without some limitations (when representations create equivalent differences to their inputs).

In recent years, this has been one of the main drawbacks of the Bayesian framework that has hindered its widespread adoption (e.g., Jones and Love, 2011; Bowers and Davis, 2012 - the latter directly mentions motion perception as an example).

Although the article touches on a theoretically very important topic, is well-written, and generally does this reasonably and effectively, I have two main objections to it. Firstly, it ignores the previous large literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, which makes it difficult to properly evaluate its contribution. The general framework for comparing models proposed by the authors is not very different from some previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myong, and Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, and Myong, 2004). Secondly, the authors make some assumptions that sometimes seem arbitrary, and how much of their framework depends on them is unclear. For example, why should $p_{meas}$ be defined by equation 3? Is defining the values of the parameters in equation 12 really enough to define and capture all the characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? I understand that this can reveal a large class of powerful and sensitive models. However, it is not clear that this is sufficient justification for the assumptions, and at least, the article should discuss and justify the assumptions used to limit Bayesian models so that they are identifiable.
