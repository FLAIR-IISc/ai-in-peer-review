

The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, specifying mathematical formulations for sensory noise, likelihood functions, prior probability distributions, loss functions, and response distributions. A significant strength of this model is its combination of robustness and computational tractability: it accommodates diverse scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while maintaining mathematical convenience (e.g., tractable inference). The authors emphasize the model's identifiability, enabling straightforward evaluation and distinction of parameter estimates.  

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation across various experimental contexts. However, I have reservations regarding novelty. The model is closely aligned with recent Bayesian approaches in the literature, particularly those published in journals like *PLoS Computational Biology* (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014), suggesting an incremental contribution rather than a substantial advance. Specifically:  
- Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *8*(11), e1002771.  
- Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *10*(6), e1003661.  

While the current submission differs from these works, its conceptual similarity warrants the assessment that its contribution is incremental. I suggest the authors revise the manuscript by adding a new section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception, explicitly articulating how their model constitutes a significant advance over prior work.  

Additionally, while the model is mathematically identifiable under its assumptions, this identifiability is contingent on those specific mathematical choices. The model does not resolve the core identifiability challenges faced by perceptual scientists—namely, distinguishing between sensory noise, decision noise, and response noise in experimental data. The authors should address this limitation in the revised manuscript.  

Mathematically elegant framework for perceptual estimation, but novel contribution is incremental (closely related work exists in the literature).
