

The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing mathematical formulations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A key strength lies in the model's dual capacity for robustness across diverse scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) and computational tractability (e.g., analytically tractable inference). The authors emphasize the model's identifiability—enabling straightforward evaluation and distinction of parameter estimates.  

I commend this work and support its acceptance at NIPS, as it offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in experimental contexts. However, I have reservations regarding novelty. The model closely aligns with recent Bayesian approaches in the literature (e.g., in *PLoS Computational Biology*), suggesting an incremental contribution rather than a substantial advance. For instance:  
Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 8(11), e1002771.  
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 10(6), e1003661.  
While the submission is not identical to these works, its conceptual and methodological proximity warrants classifying the contribution as incremental.  

I recommend the authors revise the manuscript by adding a new section that critically reviews recent Bayesian models of perception, explicitly articulating how their model constitutes a significant advancement over prior work.  

Additionally, while the model is mathematically identifiable under its assumptions, this does not resolve the practical identifiability challenge faced by perceptual scientists—namely, distinguishing between sensory, decision, and response noise sources. The claim of "identifiability" should be clarified to reflect that parameters are identifiable only within the model’s specific mathematical framework, not in experimental contexts where noise sources may be confounded. This nuance should be addressed in the revision.  

Mathematically elegant framework for perceptual estimation, but novel contribution is incremental (closely aligned with existing literature).
