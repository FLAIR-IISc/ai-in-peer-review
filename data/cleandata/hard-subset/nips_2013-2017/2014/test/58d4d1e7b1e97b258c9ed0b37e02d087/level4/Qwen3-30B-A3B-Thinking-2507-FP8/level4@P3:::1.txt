

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations. The authors propose a systematic framework for assessing the identifiability of Bayesian observer models, demonstrating its application to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. The paper begins by establishing that the inverse mapping from behavioral data to model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) is inherently degenerate, as multiple combinations of these elements can yield identical behavior. The authors then introduce a flexible observer model class that can represent a wide range of perceptual tasks while enabling efficient identifiability analysis. Their key contribution is a method to quantify the expected support for alternative models given data generated by a reference observer, using the expected log likelihood as a proxy for identifiability.

The paper relates well to previous work in the field. The identifiability problem has long been recognized in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, 1978), and more recently in Bayesian cognitive modeling (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). However, the authors provide a novel systematic approach to addressing this issue, distinct from previous model comparison techniques (Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004). Their framework specifically targets the unique challenges of Bayesian models of perception, where the degeneracy problem is particularly acute due to the interplay between sensory, perceptual, and decision-making components.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. Clear articulation of the identifiability problem and its significance for interpreting Bayesian models of perception.
2. Development of a flexible yet computationally tractable observer model class that covers a broad range of psychophysical tasks.
3. Demonstration of practical utility through two well-chosen case studies, showing how the framework can inform experimental design.
4. Thorough exploration of parameter identifiability and its impact on model recovery, with insightful findings (e.g., the surprising negative impact of imposing a narrow loss function on prior recovery).
5. Well-organized presentation with clear mathematical formulations and effective use of figures.

The paper's main weaknesses are:
1. Insufficient engagement with the broader literature on model comparison in cognitive science. While the authors mention some relevant work (Jones & Love, 2011), they fail to properly situate their framework within the extensive literature on model distinguishability (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004; Myung et al., 2000). This makes it difficult to assess the novelty of their contribution.
2. The assumptions underlying their model (particularly the parametric forms for sensory mapping and likelihood) are not sufficiently justified. While the authors note that their formulation "can approximate both Weber-Fechner's law and Stevens' law," they do not adequately explain why these specific parametric forms are preferable to alternatives or how sensitive their results are to these choices.
3. The theoretical analysis of identifiability (Equation 12) relies on a specific choice of the KL divergence as a measure of model difference, but the authors do not discuss potential limitations of this approach or alternative measures that might be more appropriate for their specific application.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to framework to applications. The technical content is sound, and the case studies provide convincing evidence of the framework's utility. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic approach to addressing a fundamental limitation of Bayesian models of perception.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant and underappreciated problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling
- Provides a novel, systematic framework with practical applications for experimental design
- Demonstrates clear utility through well-chosen case studies
- The framework is generalizable to a wide range of psychophysical tasks
- The paper is technically sound and well-written

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient engagement with the existing literature on model comparison
- Some key assumptions are not properly justified
- The connection to broader cognitive science literature is underdeveloped

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and has clear practical utility. While the authors could strengthen their paper by more thoroughly engaging with the existing literature on model comparison and better justifying their key assumptions, these are relatively minor issues that could be addressed in a revision. The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental methodological issue in computational modeling of perception.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better contextualize the work within the existing literature on model comparison and to provide more thorough justification for the model assumptions. The authors should also clarify the interpretation of the KL divergence in their identifiability metric (Equation 12) and discuss potential limitations of this approach. These revisions would strengthen the paper's contribution and make it more accessible to readers familiar with the broader cognitive science literature.
