- Paper proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models of behavioral data, summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory, addresses non-identifiability, and demonstrates with two behavioral studies (time estimation, motion perception).
- Authors identify non-identifiability problems and outline constraints to make terms in the framework identifiable.
- They present assumptions to constrain terms, analyze parameter differentiability, and show practical application in two example studies.
- Identifiability of internal representations/processes is a long-standing concern in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978).
- Difficulty of model identifiability has hindered wider adoption of Bayesian approaches (e.g., Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012).
- Major concern 1: The paper overlooks substantial prior work on model comparison in cognitive science/psychology (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang 2002; Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004), making its novelty hard to assess.
- Major concern 2: Key assumptions (e.g., definition of p_meas in Eq.3, parameterization in Eq.12) seem arbitrary or insufficiently justified; the framework’s reliance on these assumptions is unclear.
- Recommendation: Authors should more thoroughly discuss and justify assumptions used to enforce identifiability.
- Minor comments: explain why “trivial” mathematical facts hold (Line 53); fix wording error “nor”→“or” (Line 73); clarify star and dashed-line meanings in Figure 2 caption.
- Overall: Well-written and theoretically important but insufficiently connected to prior related work.
