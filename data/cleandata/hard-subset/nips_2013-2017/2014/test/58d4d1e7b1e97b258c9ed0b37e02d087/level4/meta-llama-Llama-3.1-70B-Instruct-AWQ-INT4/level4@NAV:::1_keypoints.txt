- Presents a Bayesian Decision Theory–based framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data, identifying non-identifiability and proposing constraints/assumptions to make terms identifiable.
- Applies the framework to two empirical domains: time interval estimation and motion perception.
- Notes long-standing identifiability concerns in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978) and recent critiques of Bayesian approaches (Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012).
- Major concern 1: Overlooks substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science/psychology, making the novel contribution unclear.
- The proposed framework appears similar to earlier work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung 2004).
- Major concern 2: Several key assumptions seem arbitrary and it is unclear how they affect identifiability and conclusions.
- Example: Definition of p_meas in Equation 3 is unjustified.
- Example: Parameter definitions in Equation 12 may not adequately capture cognitive or perceptual process characteristics.
- While the framework can express many sensory models, expressiveness alone does not justify the assumptions.
- Minor comments: clarify the “trivial” mathematical fact at Line 53, fix typo “nor”→“or” at Line 73, and explain the star/dashed lines in Figure 2 caption.
- Overall: well-written and addresses an important issue but lacks adequate connection to relevant previous work.
