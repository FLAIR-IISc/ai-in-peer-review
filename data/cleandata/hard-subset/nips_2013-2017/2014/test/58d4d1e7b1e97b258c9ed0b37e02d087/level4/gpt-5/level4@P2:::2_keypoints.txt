- Addresses identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss in Bayesian observer models via an expected log-likelihood / KL-based framework.
- Introduces a computationally efficient observer class (Gaussian-mixture likelihood/loss, parametric sensory transform, lattice prior) to evaluate identifiability a priori.
- Defines an approximate expected posterior by computing expected log likelihood of data from a reference observer, reducing to weighted KL sums.
- Uses MCMC to explore the approximate expected posterior; analytical expected loss for mixtures facilitates design exploration.
- Case study — interval timing: priors are poorly identifiable unless motor noise is independently known; MAP-like loss worsens recovery.
- Case study — speed perception: slow-speed priors and noise parameters are robustly recovered, even when internal/external noise are decoupled.
- Builds on existing Bayesian observer and identifiability literature and connects to prior empirical work on temporal context and speed perception.
- Strength: tackles an important, practical problem with a novel, potentially general methodological contribution.
- Strength: computational efficiency and analytical tractability enable pre-data experimental design comparisons.
- Strength: clear, relevant case studies that provide theoretical support for empirical findings.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on specific modeling assumptions (discrete stimuli, log sensory transform, Gaussian mixtures, lattice prior, shared parameters).
- Weakness: limited systematic sensitivity analyses to assess robustness to those modeling choices.
- Weakness: some findings are predictable (e.g., sensory vs motor noise trade-offs).
- Clarity issues: Figure 1, some derivational approximations, and model specification need clearer presentation.
- Recommendation to report statistical reliability (credible intervals or tests) because box plots are hard to interpret.
- Suggestion to move the negative-control (no slow-speed prior) from supplement into main text for clarity.
- Request clarification/justification for modeling discrete stimuli and approximation quality as Nexp increases.
- Request sensitivity analysis for the log sensory transform (parameters d and s0) and alternative transforms.
- Ask for justification that the mixture fits remain unimodal and how bimodality is prevented in practice.
- Ask why a lattice mixture prior was chosen, why the prior range is 150% of true range, and sensitivity to these choices.
- Request explicit conditions and error quantification for approximations used (e.g., Stirling, replacing E[x log x] with E[x] log E[x]).
- Request specification of the “unnormalized density” sampled, and full MCMC details (proposals, adaptation, convergence checks).
- Question the claim that learning priors doesn’t matter in 2AFC, given evidence that priors can adapt to experimental distributions.
- Overall recommendation: accept — innovative and useful methodological contribution despite assumptions and clarity/sensitivity gaps.
