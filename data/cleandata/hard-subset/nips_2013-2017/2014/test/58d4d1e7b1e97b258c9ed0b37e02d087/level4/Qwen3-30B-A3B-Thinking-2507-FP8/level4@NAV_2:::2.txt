

This paper addresses a crucial challenge in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian models. The writing is clear and well-structured. While the work relies on numerous assumptions that require careful consideration, and the number of these assumptions seems somewhat disproportionate to the significance of the primary findings, the methodological novelty and innovation are compelling. I therefore consider this work to meet the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key findings: In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise parameters was somewhat expected (I had questioned the inclusion of both in the model while reading). It would be helpful to clearly indicate which condition differences were statistically reliable (perhaps I'm misinterpreting the box plots). In Section 4.2, it would be important to demonstrate that the model can correctly infer the absence of a slow-speed prior when the reference observer does not possess one.

Line 106: The manuscript uses a discrete stimulus distribution, though the stimulus is inherently continuous. This choice likely reflects experimental design conventions where discrete stimulus levels serve as independent variables, but the authors should clarify this rationale.

Line 134: The log-stimulus transformation (Equation 2) is grounded in psychophysical principles. The effectiveness of the approach appears significantly dependent on this transformation, which introduces strong constraints on the inference process. Consequently, the work effectively demonstrates that Bayesian models of perception can be identifiable under specific, strong assumptions about the nature of internal representations.

Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and the differential entropy maximization procedure—do they guarantee that Equation 3 will yield a unimodal distribution? There's a possibility that the fitting process could produce a bimodal distribution.

Line 158: The paper assumes a lattice-based prior representation rather than a uniform one. Could the authors clarify why the lattice representation is preferred, and what prior knowledge about the stimulus distribution justifies using a prior range that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range?

Figure 1: Despite careful consideration, the figure remains confusing rather than illuminating. It would be clearer if Figure 1b were integrated into Figure 1a, and the derived quantity p_est(s^*|x) were omitted from the figure.

Line 252: The authors should elaborate on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately valid, particularly regarding the applicability of Stirling's approximation for factorials and the circumstances where E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]. By the time Equation 15 is derived (after abandoning priors on θ and making certain approximations), the justification for this equation appears to rely primarily on its intuitive appeal for assessing prediction similarity.

Line 262: "Sample from this unnormalized density"—could the authors clarify which density is being referred to, specifically whether it is the dataset distribution?

Line 365: The manuscript does not address how learning of priors might affect results in 2AFC paradigms. While the approach is innovative and the problem is important, the results do not feel particularly compelling.
