In this article, the authors propose a framework for model comparison of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. They begin by summarizing the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identifying areas of non-identifiability, and outlining constraints that can be used to render each term in the Bayesian framework identifiable. They then introduce assumptions to constrain their framework, explore the differentiability of parameters within their model, and apply the technique to two studies employing Bayesian decision theory to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception. 

Issues of identifiability in internal representations and processes have been longstanding concerns within cognitive science and psychology. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed the conditions under which representational formats can mimic each other, demonstrating that they are indistinguishable without constraints on processing mechanisms (when representations make equivalent distinctions of their inputs). 

More recently, a major drawback hindering wider adoption of the Bayesian framework has been the challenge of identifiability (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter directly addresses speed perception as an illustration). 

Although the article addresses a theoretically important issue and is generally well-written and effectively presented, I have two major reservations. First, it overlooks a substantial body of existing literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution of this work. The general framework for model comparison proposed by the authors is not substantially different from previous approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, the authors’ assumptions sometimes appear arbitrary, and the degree to which their framework relies on these assumptions remains unclear. For example, what is the rationale for defining $p_{meas}$ as in equation 3? Is defining the parameters in Equation 12 truly sufficient to define and capture all the characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? While the authors claim robustness and the ability to express a large class of sensory models, this does not necessarily justify the assumptions made to achieve identifiability. At a minimum, the article should more thoroughly discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models.
