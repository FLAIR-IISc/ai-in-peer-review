The paper proposes a Bayesian model of perception, providing a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in various experiments. The model's strength lies in its ability to handle different types of situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining computational convenience, allowing for tractable inference.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this framework through two case studies, showing how it can be used to improve experimental design and evaluate the robustness of internal representation inference.

The model is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying assumptions and mathematical derivations. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

One of the paper's strengths is its ability to provide a systematic and a priori investigation of identifiability, allowing researchers to evaluate the effectiveness of different experimental designs before running an experiment. The authors demonstrate the flexibility of their framework by applying it to two distinct case studies, showcasing its potential for evaluating the identifiability of various Bayesian models of perception.

However, the paper's contribution is incremental, as similar models have been proposed in the literature. For instance, Acerbi et al. (2012) and Acerbi et al. (2014) have developed Bayesian models of perception that share similarities with the proposed model. While the current submission is not identical to these earlier articles, it is similar enough to warrant a discussion of how it advances the state-of-the-art.

To strengthen the paper, the authors could include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly stating why their model is a significant advance over previously published models. This would help to contextualize their contribution and demonstrate its novelty.

Additionally, the authors emphasize the mathematical identifiability of their model, but it is essential to acknowledge that parameters are only identifiable due to the mathematical assumptions of the model. The term "identifiable" can be used in other ways, such as the ability to distinguish between different sources of noise (sensory, decision, or response noise). The authors could comment on this aspect and discuss how their model addresses the identifiability issue in the context of perceptual science.

In conclusion, the paper proposes a valuable framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. While its contribution is incremental, the authors' work has the potential to make a significant impact in the field. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper could be even stronger.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework through two case studies.
* The model is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying assumptions and mathematical derivations.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's contribution is incremental, as similar models have been proposed in the literature.
* The authors could strengthen their paper by including a review of recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly stating why their model is a significant advance.
* The authors should acknowledge the limitations of their model's identifiability and discuss how it addresses the identifiability issue in the context of perceptual science.
