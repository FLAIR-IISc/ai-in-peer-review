The manuscript addresses an important issue in cognitive modeling: model identifiability. It is clearly written and generally easy to follow. However, the results depend on a long list of assumptions, which feels disproportionate to the impact of the key findings. That said, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and I judge the work to be above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding key results:
- Section 4.1: The interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising (I questioned the need to include both while reading). Please indicate which condition differences are statistically reliable; the box plots alone were not sufficiently informative.
- Section 4.2: Isn’t it essential to demonstrate that if the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, the model would recover this as well? A recovery analysis without the slow-speed prior would substantially strengthen the identifiability claim.

Specific comments:
- Line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is in fact a continuous variable? I assume this reflects typical experimental designs where stimulus level is the independent variable, but please clarify.
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Eq. 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, appears to play a large role in your success. The transform imposes a strong constraint on inference; thus, the results suggest that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind. Please discuss this dependency explicitly.
- Line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4, together with maximization of differential entropy, ensure that Eq. 3 yields a unimodal distribution? It seems the fit could, in principle, be bimodal. Please clarify the conditions under which unimodality is guaranteed.
- Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over a uniform representation? What knowledge about the true stimulus distribution would justify a prior that is 50% wider than the true range? Please justify this assumption or examine sensitivity to it.
- Figure 1: The figure is more confusing than clarifying. It would be clearer if panel 1b were integrated into 1a, and if p_est(s^* | x) were removed, since it is a derived quantity.
- Line 252: Please comment on the conditions on N under which Eq. 14 is a good approximation—i.e., when Stirling’s approximation is valid and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]. By the time you introduce Eq. 15—after abandoning priors on θ and making several questionable approximations—the result seems justified mainly because it yields an intuitive rule for assessing predictive similarity. Either tighten the derivation or provide empirical validation of its adequacy.
- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density specifically? The dataset distribution?
- Line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC? Please provide an argument or simulation demonstrating this claim.

In summary: this is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less than overwhelming given the reliance on strong assumptions. Clearer assessment of statistical reliability, model-recovery demonstrations (especially without the slow-speed prior), and justification/sensitivity analyses for key assumptions would substantially strengthen the paper. Nonetheless, I recommend acceptance.
