- Proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models of behavioral data, summarizing Bayesian decision theory and identifying sources of non-identifiability.  
- Outlines constraints required to render each term in the Bayesian framework identifiable and imposes specific assumptions to do so.  
- Examines distinguishability of parameter values within their models and applies the approach to time-interval estimation and motion perception.  
- Notes that identifiability of internal representations/processes is a longstanding issue in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978) and has driven recent critiques of Bayesian approaches.  
- Major reservation 1: Limited engagement with prior model-comparison literature (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang; Navarro, Pitt & Myung), leaving the paper’s novelty unclear.  
- Major reservation 2: Reliance on assumptions that seem arbitrary (e.g., form of p_meas in Eq.3, parameter choices in Eq.12) without sufficient justification or exploration of alternatives.  
- Requests the authors justify the assumptions used to achieve identifiability and clarify how conclusions might change under alternative plausible constraints.  
- Minor comments: explain the “trivial” claim at line 53, change the second “nor” to “or” at line 73, and clarify the meanings of the star and dashed lines in Figure 2.  
- Overall assessment: well-written and addressing an important issue, but missing links to previous related work.
