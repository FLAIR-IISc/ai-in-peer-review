This paper presents a computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception. The authors' main contribution is a method to systematically evaluate how well a given experimental design can constrain a model's parameters before any data is collected. The framework consists of two core components: 1) a flexible and computationally efficient class of Bayesian observer models that can capture a wide range of perceptual tasks, including stimulus-dependent noise and various priors and loss functions; and 2) a technique based on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence to approximate the expected posterior over model parameters, thereby mapping out the "identifiability landscape." This landscape reveals which parameters are likely to be well-constrained and which may trade off against each other. The utility of this approach is demonstrated through two case studies: one on time interval estimation, showing how the framework can be used to compare and improve experimental designs, and another on speed perception, which provides theoretical validation for the robustness of previous findings concerning a slow-speed prior.

This work addresses a critical and often overlooked problem in computational modeling of perception. The issue of non-identifiability, where different combinations of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can produce identical behavior, poses a fundamental challenge to interpreting the results of model fitting. This paper's attempt to provide a principled, *a priori* tool for experimenters is both timely and valuable.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a major concern in the field. A framework that allows researchers to assess the power of their experimental design to identify parameters of interest *before* running costly experiments is a significant contribution. It has the potential to improve the rigor and efficiency of research in this domain.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound. The observer model is comprehensive, building on established principles while introducing flexible parameterizations (e.g., using mixtures of Gaussians for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions) that remain computationally tractable. The use of the expected log-likelihood, expressed via KL divergence, is a principled way to quantify the expected distinguishability between models.
3.  **Clarity and Application:** The paper is well-written and the framework is clearly explained. The two case studies are effective in demonstrating its practical utility. For instance, the analysis of the time perception task provides non-obvious insights, such as the potentially detrimental effect of a MAP-like loss function on prior recovery, and highlights the importance of independently measuring motor noise.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Originality in Context:** While the overarching framework is novel, the specific components of the observer model are heavily inspired by, and represent extensions of, previous work. In particular, the modeling approach is very similar in style and substance to recent models developed for sensorimotor tasks, such as those by Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), which are cited in the paper. The contribution could therefore be viewed as incremental, combining and generalizing existing modeling techniques into a new analytical framework. To strengthen the paper, the authors should more explicitly delineate their contribution. I suggest adding a more detailed comparison to this prior work, clarifying precisely how the proposed model class is more general and, more importantly, how the *a priori* identifiability analysis itself is a conceptual advance beyond simply building and fitting these complex models.

2.  **Scope of "Identifiability":** The paper makes strong claims about tackling the identifiability problem. However, it primarily addresses *parameter identifiability* within a specific, highly structured model class. This is a valid and important goal, but it does not resolve the deeper, more conceptual identifiability problem that many perceptual scientists face: distinguishing between different sources of behavioral variability. For example, the framework can show a trade-off between a sensory noise parameter (σ) and a motor noise parameter (ρ1), but it cannot fundamentally distinguish whether an observer's error originates from noisy sensation, suboptimal inference, or imprecise motor execution. The model assumes a particular causal structure, and the identifiability analysis operates within those assumptions. It would be beneficial for the authors to acknowledge this limitation explicitly. A brief discussion clarifying that their method addresses parameter degeneracy within a given model architecture, rather than degeneracy between fundamentally different cognitive architectures, would add nuance and honesty to their claims.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and thoughtfully constructed framework for a significant problem in computational cognitive science. The primary concerns relate to the degree of novelty of the modeling components and the scope of the identifiability claims. Addressing these points by better situating the work within the literature and clarifying the precise nature of the identifiability being tested would substantially improve the manuscript.
