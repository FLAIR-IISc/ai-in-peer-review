- Addresses non-identifiability in Bayesian perception models: degeneracy of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions in inverse problems.  
- Proposes a systematic a priori identifiability testing framework to assess whether experimental designs can recover model components.  
- Employs a flexible Bayesian observer model (sensory mapping, prior mixtures, loss functions, response noise) and measures identifiability via expected log-likelihood differences against a reference model.  
- Validated on two case studies: time-interval estimation (motor-noise measurement resolves prior degeneracy; MAP loss worsens recovery) and speed perception (slow-speed priors and noise are recoverable).  
- Technically sound: unified model of sensation→perception→decision→response with rigorous KL-divergence/expected log-likelihood derivation and synthetic-data validation.  
- Computationally efficient and flexible: handles stimulus-dependent noise and uses analytical posterior approximations.  
- Clear exposition and thorough mathematical supplement; four-stage model construction and case studies effectively link theory to empirical implications.  
- Minor clarity improvements suggested: include a parameter-role table or schematic of the identifiability workflow.  
- Originality: the identifiability-testing framework is novel and distinct from prior Bayesian perception models, which focused on model construction rather than identifiability diagnosis.  
- Corrects misconceptions in prior ad-hoc approaches and provides a unified tool for diagnosing and resolving degeneracy.  
- Significance: enables researchers to design experiments that avoid flawed inferences about internal representations; applicable beyond perception (e.g., neural decoding).  
- Key strengths: general, efficient framework; actionable experimental guidance; non-intuitive insights from case studies; rigorous theoretical basis.  
- Key weakness: needs to explicitly distinguish model identifiability (paper’s focus) from experimental confounds like sensory vs. response noise.  
- The review’s "incremental" critique mischaracterizes the work; authors should clarify contrast with prior modeling studies.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant, well-executed contribution with broad applicability.  
- Revision suggestion: add a short Related Work subsection contrasting the identifiability framework with prior Bayesian modeling papers and clarify the framework’s scope.
