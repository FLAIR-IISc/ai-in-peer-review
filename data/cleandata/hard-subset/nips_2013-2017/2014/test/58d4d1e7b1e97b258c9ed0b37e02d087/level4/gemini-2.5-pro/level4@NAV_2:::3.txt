This paper proposes a framework to systematically test the *a priori* identifiability of a significant class of Bayesian observer models of perception. To achieve this, the authors first construct a flexible and computationally tractable observer model that parameterizes the key components of a perceptual task: the sensory stage (including stimulus-dependent noise), the perceptual stage (internal prior and likelihood), the decision-making stage (loss function and lapses), and the response stage (motor noise). The core of the contribution is a method to explore the "identifiability landscape" by computing the expected log-likelihood for a candidate model, given data generated by a known reference model. This quantity, related to the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the models' response distributions, allows the authors to assess which model parameters can be reliably recovered. The framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one examining experimental designs for a time interval estimation task, and another re-examining the inference of a slow-speed prior in vision. This work addresses the well-known degeneracy problem in Bayesian cognitive science, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce indistinguishable behavior. While previous work, such as Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), has developed sophisticated Bayesian models for specific tasks, this paper's aim is to provide a more general, meta-level tool for assessing the reliability of parameter recovery before an experiment is conducted.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a major challenge for the field of computational cognitive science and a frequent criticism of the Bayesian approach. This paper provides a principled, systematic, and practical approach to address it, which is of great value to researchers designing psychophysical experiments.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed observer model is a notable strength. It is mathematically elegant, unifying a wide range of plausible components (e.g., Weber-Fechner and Steven's laws for sensory mapping, non-Gaussian likelihoods/priors, asymmetric loss functions) into a single, computationally convenient framework. The ability to derive an analytical expression for the expected loss is key to the framework's efficiency.
*   **Practical Utility:** The framework's direct application to improving experimental design is a significant contribution. The case studies are effective in demonstrating how the method can be used to quantitatively compare different experimental setups (e.g., showing that measuring motor noise directly is more effective than simply adding trials) and to identify potential parameter degeneracies that might otherwise go unnoticed.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** The primary weakness is that the contribution, while valuable, may be viewed as incremental. The architectural components of the observer model—such as using mixtures of Gaussians for priors, parameterizing sensory noise, and modeling lapses—are built upon concepts and techniques that are well-established in the literature, particularly in work from related research groups (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014). The manuscript would be stronger if it included a section that more directly and thoroughly contrasts its framework with these prior modeling efforts, articulating precisely what makes the current *identifiability analysis framework* a significant conceptual advance over the construction of those earlier, task-specific models.
*   **Scope of Identifiability:** The paper focuses on *parameter* identifiability within the defined model structure. This is valid, but it sidesteps a broader identifiability issue that concerns many perceptual scientists: firmly distinguishing the contributions of different sources of behavioral variability (e.g., sensory noise, decision noise, or motor noise). The model itself does not resolve this fundamental ambiguity, as the analysis in Figure 2c demonstrates a degeneracy between sensory and motor noise. It would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly discuss this limitation, clarifying that while their framework can diagnose these degeneracies under a given model architecture, it cannot resolve them without strong assumptions or external measurements (as demonstrated in their 'MTR' condition).

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and pervasive problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
*   Provides a novel and general framework with clear practical utility for improving experimental design.
*   The work is technically sound, and the proposed observer model is both flexible and computationally efficient.
*   The case studies are well-chosen and provide non-trivial insights.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The contribution could be perceived as incremental, as it heavily leverages modeling techniques from closely related prior work.
*   The paper could do more to situate its conceptual contribution with respect to this existing literature.
