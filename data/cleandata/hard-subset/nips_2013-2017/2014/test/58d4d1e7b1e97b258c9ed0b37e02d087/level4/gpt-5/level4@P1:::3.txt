Summary
This paper proposes a general framework for assessing a priori identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors construct a flexible class of 1D estimation models that spans sensation, perception, decision, and response stages: a monotonic mapping to internal space, mixture-of-Gaussians likelihoods and priors, maximum-entropy parameterizations of noise and loss, a BDT decision rule with optional lapses, and additive motor/reporting noise. They then define an expected log-likelihood criterion based on KL divergences between model-predicted response distributions, and sample an approximate “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) via MCMC to map identifiability under a chosen experimental design. Two case studies illustrate the approach: interval timing (where accurate recovery of the prior is difficult unless motor noise is independently constrained) and speed perception under a slow-speed prior (where both the prior and noise characteristics are recovered robustly). The framework is positioned as a tool to rank candidate designs by their ability to resolve parameter degeneracies and to inform experimental choices such as feedback (loss), number of conditions, and external measurements (e.g., motor noise).

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on a line of Bayesian observer modeling and parameter recovery in perception and sensorimotor control (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, Körding & Wolpert), and on previous work by the authors and others on inferring priors/likelihoods/loss functions (Acerbi et al., 2012; Acerbi et al., 2014). Methodologically, it connects to model distinguishability and identifiability analyses in cognitive modeling (Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004). The main novelty here is a unified, computationally efficient parameterization that supports systematic a priori identifiability assessment and design comparison via a KL-based expected support criterion.

Quality
- Strengths: The model class is well-specified, computationally tractable (mixture-of-Gaussians structure admits efficient evaluation), and wide-ranging (supports stimulus-dependent noise, flexible priors and losses, lapses, and motor/report noise). The KL-based expected log-likelihood provides a principled, scalable surrogate for full simulation-based model comparison. The case studies are appropriate and show both failure modes (interval timing) and robust recovery (speed).
- Weaknesses: Identifiability is assessed “within the model,” relying on specific parametric assumptions (e.g., two-component Gaussian mixtures, smooth unimodality, particular f(s) family, almost-quadratic loss). The approximate posterior E(θ|θ*) is not a true posterior; the degree to which conclusions depend on this approximation, on the choice of mixture order, and on misspecification is not fully explored. The conversion of a 2AFC task to an estimation model is briefly justified but could be validated more thoroughly. Empirical corroboration (beyond simulations) is limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a careful presentation of the model components and two concrete applications. A few suggestions:
- Add a dedicated related-work section situating this framework relative to recent Bayesian observer models and prior parameter-recovery efforts (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012; Acerbi et al., 2014; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Navarro et al., 2004). Clearly articulate what is new (unified parameterization + KL-based design ranking) versus what is adapted.
- Clarify the distinction between structural/parameter identifiability within the model and practical identifiability of psychological constructs across plausible model families (see below).
- Summarize computational complexity and provide implementation details or code for reproducibility.
- Report sensitivity analyses: number of mixture components M, deviations from unimodality, alternative f(s) forms, and robustness to loss mis-specification.

Originality
The contribution is useful but incremental. Many building blocks (Bayesian observer structure, mixture parameterizations, lapse modeling, and inference via MCMC) are in the literature. The main addition is an explicit, efficient procedure to quantify and visualize a priori identifiability and to rank experimental designs. Framing identifiability via expected KL support across designs is a valuable practical advance, even if conceptually close to established ideas in model distinguishability.

Significance
The framework can help researchers anticipate and mitigate parameter confounds, choose informative designs, and avoid overconfident interpretations of recovered priors/likelihoods/losses. The interval-timing analysis provides actionable guidance (e.g., independent measurement of motor noise) and cautions against overly narrow feedback-induced losses. The speed example offers theoretical validation of prior slow-speed inferences. Despite the incremental nature, the tool is likely to be adopted by practitioners designing perception experiments.

Major concerns and suggestions
- Scope of “identifiability”: The paper emphasizes identifiability but, as demonstrated in the interval-timing case, sensory, decision, and response noise can remain conflated unless externally constrained. Please clarify the sense in which parameters are identifiable: structural identifiability given the assumed model class versus practical identifiability of latent psychological sources. It would help to:
  - Explicitly define and contrast these notions.
  - Provide design guidelines (e.g., separate motor-noise calibration, multiple conditions manipulating sensory noise, task feedback manipulations) and show how they change E(θ|θ*).
  - Discuss to what extent the framework can (or cannot) disentangle sensory vs decision vs response noise without independent measurements.
- Novelty positioning: Include a section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception and parameter-recovery methods (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012; Acerbi et al., 2014; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Hedges et al., 2011; Natarajan et al., 2009), and clearly state the specific methodological advance here (KL-based expected support for design ranking and the unified parametric model enabling it).
- Robustness/misspecification: Add sensitivity analyses for mixture order, loss family, and f(s), and assess how violations (e.g., skewed or multimodal likelihoods) affect identifiability conclusions.

Pros
- Flexible, computationally efficient observer model spanning key components (prior, likelihood, loss, lapses, motor noise).
- Principled, scalable identifiability metric (expected KL support) enabling design comparison.
- Insightful case studies with practical guidance for experimental design.
- Clear demonstration that some popular intuitions (e.g., narrow loss/MAP) can harm recovery.

Cons
- Incremental relative to existing Bayesian observer modeling and parameter-recovery literature.
- Identifiability claims are model-dependent; core confounds (sensory vs decision vs response noise) persist without external constraints.
- Limited sensitivity analyses and empirical validation.
- 2AFC-to-estimation mapping could be justified and tested more thoroughly.

Recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance, contingent on revisions that (i) situate the contribution relative to closely related work and clarify novelty, (ii) sharpen the definition and scope of identifiability, including explicit guidance on disentangling noise sources, and (iii) provide robustness analyses and implementation details. The framework is mathematically elegant and practically useful for planning perceptual experiments, even if the contribution is incremental.
