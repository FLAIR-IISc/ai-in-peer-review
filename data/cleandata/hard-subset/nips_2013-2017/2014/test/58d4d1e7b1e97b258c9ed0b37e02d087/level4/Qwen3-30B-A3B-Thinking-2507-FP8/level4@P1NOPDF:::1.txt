

This paper addresses a critical issue in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian models when applied to behavioral data. The authors propose a framework for model comparison by synthesizing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying non-identifiability challenges, and outlining constraints to render model components identifiable. They then apply these constraints to examine parameter differentiability and demonstrate the approach using two case studies—time interval estimation and motion perception.  

The problem of identifiability in cognitive processes has long been central to psychology and cognitive science. Anderson (1978) famously showed that representational formats can be indistinguishable without process constraints, as they may make equivalent distinctions of inputs. Despite this foundational work, the Bayesian framework’s limited adoption in cognitive science has been hindered by persistent identifiability concerns (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012, which explicitly discusses speed perception as a case in point).  

While the paper tackles a theoretically significant problem and is well-written, I have two substantial reservations. First, it largely overlooks the extensive prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science. The authors’ general framework bears significant resemblance to established approaches, such as those by Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004), which provide rigorous methods for distinguishing cognitive models. Without engaging with this work, the paper’s contribution remains unclear and difficult to contextualize.  

Second, several assumptions appear unmotivated, raising questions about their necessity and impact. For instance, why is $p_{meas}$ constrained by Equation 3? Similarly, the claim that defining parameters via Equation 12 fully captures cognitive/perceptual processes requires stronger justification. The authors note the framework’s robustness and capacity to express diverse sensory models, but this does not suffice to validate the assumptions. The paper should explicitly justify each constraint and discuss how results might change under alternative assumptions.

This is a well-written paper on a vital topic, but it fails to adequately situate itself within existing model comparison frameworks. Addressing these gaps would significantly strengthen its contribution.
