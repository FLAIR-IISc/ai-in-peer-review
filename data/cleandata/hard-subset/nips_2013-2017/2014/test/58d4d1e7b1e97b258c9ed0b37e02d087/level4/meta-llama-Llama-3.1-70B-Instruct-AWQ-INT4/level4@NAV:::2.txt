Here's a paraphrased version of the review, maintaining the original structure, meaning, and technical content:

This manuscript addresses a crucial issue in cognitive modeling, specifically model identifiability. The writing is clear and engaging, making it a pleasure to read. However, the work relies on a substantial list of assumptions, which, although necessary, may be overwhelming for some readers. Despite this, the methodology is innovative and interesting, warranting presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results, the finding in section 4.1 that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not entirely unexpected. To strengthen this section, it would be helpful to indicate which condition differences are statistically significant. The use of box plots may not be the most effective way to convey this information.

In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer. This would significantly enhance the validity of the results.

Specific comments:

* Line 106: The choice of a discrete distribution for the stimulus, despite being a continuous variable, may be due to typical experimental design conventions. However, clarification on this point would be beneficial.
* Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws and appears to be a crucial factor in the success of the work. This suggests that the identifiability of Bayesian models may be contingent upon strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.
* Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy may not guarantee that Equation 3 yields a unimodal distribution. In principle, the fit could result in a bimodal distribution.
* Line 158: The assumption that the observer's prior favors the lattice representation over a uniform representation requires justification. What information does the observer possess about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior 50% wider than the true range?
* Figure 1: The figure is somewhat confusing. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) might improve clarity, as it is a derived inference.
* Line 252: The authors should provide comments on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, including the validity of Stirling's approximation and the circumstances under which E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]. By the time Equation 15 is introduced, the approximations and abandonment of priors on θ may undermine the justification for Equation 15, except for its intuitive appeal.
* Line 262: The text refers to "sample from this unnormalized density," but it is unclear which density is being referred to. Is it the dataset distribution?
* Line 365: The manuscript claims that learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC. While this approach is innovative and addresses an important problem, the results are not entirely convincing.

Overall, the manuscript tackles a significant issue in cognitive modeling, but the results, although interesting, are not overwhelming.
