The manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and well-organized, making it easy to follow. However, the work relies on a lengthy list of assumptions that the reader must accept and understand, which may detract from the impact of the key results presented. Despite this, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and I believe the work is above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in section 4.1 is not surprising, and it would be helpful to indicate which condition differences were reliable. This could be clarified by providing additional information or analysis. In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the fact that the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, if that is indeed the case.

Specific comments:

Line 106: The stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, despite being a continuous variable. This may be due to the typical design of experiments, where the stimulus level is the independent variable. However, the authors should clarify this point to avoid confusion.

Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws, and its incorporation may be a crucial factor in the success of this work. This suggests that the results may be dependent on strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind, rather than a general property of Bayesian models.

Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy do not guarantee that Equation 3 will be fit to a unimodal distribution. In principle, the fit could yield a bimodal distribution, and the authors should address this possibility.

Line 158: It is unclear why the observer's prior should favor the lattice representation over a uniform representation. What information does the observer have about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?

Figure 1: The figure is confusing, and integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) may improve clarity.

Line 252: The authors should comment on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, including when Stirling's approximation of the factorial holds and when E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]. By the time Equation 15 is introduced, the approximations and abandonment of priors on θ make it seem justified primarily by its intuitive result.

Line 262: It is unclear which unnormalized density is being sampled from. Is it the dataset distribution?

Line 365: The manuscript presents an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are not overwhelming. The claim that learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC is particularly noteworthy, but it would be beneficial to provide more context or justification for this assertion.
