- Manuscript addresses model identifiability in cognitive modeling, well-written but relies on many assumptions that may outweigh its impact.  
- Methodology is novel and interesting; paper is above threshold for acceptance.  
- Section 4.1: Interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising from the model description; report which condition differences are statistically significant.  
- Section 4.2: Missing control experiment to show the model can recover when a reference observer does not use a slow-speed prior.  
- line 106: Clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled with a discrete distribution (likely due to experimental discrete stimulus levels).  
- line 134: The log-stimulus transformation may drive the method’s success, implying identifiability may depend on strong psychophysical constraints.  
- line 143: Clarify whether the constraints plus differential entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fit for Equation 3, or if bimodal solutions are possible.  
- line 158: Justify why an observer’s prior would favor a lattice over uniform representation and why the prior would be 50% wider than the true stimulus range.  
- Figure 1: Current figure is confusing; consider integrating panels and removing p_est(s^*|x) to improve clarity.  
- line 252: State conditions on N for the approximations (Stirling, E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]) used to derive Equation 15 and acknowledge Eq.15 is more intuitive than rigorously derived.  
- line 262: Clarify what is meant by “sample from this unnormalized density” (specify which density—e.g., dataset distribution).  
- line 365: Explain why learning of priors is deemed irrelevant for a 2AFC task.  
- Overall: Innovative approach to an important problem, but results are less impactful than the methodological contribution.
