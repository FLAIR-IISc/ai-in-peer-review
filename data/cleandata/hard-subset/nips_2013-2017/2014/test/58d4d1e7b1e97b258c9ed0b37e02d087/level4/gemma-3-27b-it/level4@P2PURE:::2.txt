The manuscript addresses the important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and well-organized. However, the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions, and the length of this list feels disproportionate to the impact of the presented results. Despite this, the methodology is interesting and innovative, leading me to judge the work as above the presentation threshold for NIPS.

Regarding the key results: the finding in section 4.1 concerning the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising, and I questioned the inclusion of both noise types during my initial reading. It would be beneficial to indicate which condition differences were statistically reliable; the box plots were difficult to interpret. In section 4.2, it is crucial to demonstrate that the model can also recover the stimulus distribution when the reference observer does *not* possess a slow-speed prior.

Specific comments:

line 106: The stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution despite being a fundamentally continuous variable. This likely reflects standard experimental design where stimulus levels are discrete independent variables, but the authors should clarify this choice.

line 134: The authors rightly note that the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical principles. The success of this work appears heavily dependent on this transform, suggesting that the demonstrated identifiability of Bayesian models holds primarily given strong assumptions about representational transformations within the mind. 

line 143: Do the constraints in Equation 4, combined with the maximization of differential entropy, guarantee that Equation 3 will fit to a unimodal distribution? The fit could, in principle, yield a bimodal distribution.

line 158: What justification exists for the observer's prior favoring the lattice representation over a uniform representation? What prior knowledge about the true stimulus distribution allows for a prior that is 50% wider than the actual range?

Figure 1: The figure remains unclear even after careful review. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and removing p_est(s^*|x) as it’s a derived inference, would likely improve clarity.

line 252: Could the authors comment on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct – specifically, when Stirling’s approximation of the factorial holds, and under what circumstances E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]? By the time the authors arrive at Equation 15 – having dispensed with priors on θ and employed several approximations – the equation feels justified primarily by its intuitive appeal as a rule for assessing prediction similarity.

line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density is being referenced – the dataset distribution?

line 365: The lack of impact from learning of priors in the 2AFC task is notable. This is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less compelling than anticipated.
