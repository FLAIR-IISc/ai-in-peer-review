The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception that explicitly specifies the key components of perceptual decision-making: sensory noise, the likelihood function, the prior distribution, the loss function, and the response distribution. A major strength is that it combines robustness with computational convenience: it can accommodate a variety of scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while keeping inference tractable. The authors also emphasize identifiability, i.e., that distinct parameter settings are distinguishable given the model.

I like this paper and would like to see it accepted to the NIPS conference. The proposed framework is mathematically elegant and offers a clear formalization of perceptual estimation across many experimental paradigms. That said, I have reservations about the novelty. The approach is very much in the spirit of several recent Bayesian models in the literature (often in PLoS Computational Biology), and the contribution here feels incremental rather than a substantial advance. For example:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

The current submission is not identical to these (or other) prior papers, but it is sufficiently similar in style and goals that its novelty appears incremental.

I recommend revising the manuscript to include a dedicated section that reviews recent Bayesian models of perception and clearly articulates how the present model advances prior work. Please be explicit about the dimensions of improvement (e.g., the scope of noise models handled, generality of priors or loss functions, theoretical guarantees, tractability, or new empirical predictions) and position your contribution relative to the most relevant prior models.

A further comment on identifiability: while the model is mathematically identifiable under its assumptions, this notion differs from the broader identifiability problem that concerns many perceptual scientists. In particular, the model, as described, does not allow one to unambiguously determine whether an observer’s errors are due to sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. In that sense, it does not resolve the identifiability challenge that arises when multiple noise sources can produce similar behavioral outcomes. Please clarify this distinction in the revision—what is identifiable under your modeling assumptions, what is not, and what experimental manipulations (if any) would be required to separate these noise sources.

In sum: a mathematically elegant and practically useful framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but with an incremental contribution given closely related prior work.
