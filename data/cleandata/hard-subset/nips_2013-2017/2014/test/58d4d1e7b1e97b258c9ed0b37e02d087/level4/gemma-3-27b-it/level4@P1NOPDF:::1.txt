In this article, the authors propose a framework for model comparison of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. They begin by summarizing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying potential issues of non-identifiability, and outlining constraints that can be applied to make each term within the framework identifiable. They then present assumptions to constrain these terms, explore the differentiability of parameters in their model, and demonstrate the technique using two studies employing Bayesian decision theory to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception.

Issues of identifiability of internal representations and processes have been a long-standing concern within cognitive science and psychology. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed representational formats, demonstrating that they can be indistinguishable without constraints on underlying processes – specifically, when representations make equivalent distinctions of their inputs.

Recently, a significant drawback hindering the wider adoption of the Bayesian framework has been the difficulty of model identifiability (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter directly addresses speed perception as an illustrative example). 

While this article addresses a theoretically important issue and is generally well-written and presented, I have two major reservations. First, it overlooks a substantial body of previous work on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making it challenging to fully assess the contribution of this framework. The general approach to model comparison proposed by the authors bears similarities to existing work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, certain assumptions made by the authors appear somewhat arbitrary, and the degree to which their framework relies on these assumptions is unclear. For instance, what justification is there for defining $p_{meas}$ using equation 3? Furthermore, is defining the parameter values in Equation 12 truly sufficient to fully characterize a cognitive or perceptual process? While the authors note its robustness and ability to express a broad class of sensory models, this does not fully justify the assumption. At the very least, the article should more thoroughly discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models to ensure identifiability.
