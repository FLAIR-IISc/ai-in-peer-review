The paper presents a Bayesian observer model for perceptual estimation, offering a mathematically convenient and robust framework capable of handling diverse scenarios like stimulus-dependent noise. A key focus is on the identifiability of model parameters, crucial for reliable inference of internal representations. This is a valuable contribution, providing a formal tool for analyzing and potentially improving experimental design in perception research. The authors demonstrate its application through two well-chosen case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception – showcasing its utility in evaluating experimental setups and validating existing findings.

However, while the work is strong, its novelty appears incremental within the broader landscape of Bayesian modeling in perception. The model builds upon established principles and extends existing approaches, but doesn’t represent a radical departure. Several related models, often appearing in journals like *PLoS Computational Biology*, explore similar ground. For instance, Acerbi et al. (2012, *PLoS Computational Biology*) investigates the role of temporal statistics and feedback in interval timing using a Bayesian framework, and Acerbi et al. (2014, *PLoS Computational Biology*) addresses suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. The current submission, while not identical, shares significant stylistic and conceptual similarities with these and other prior works. 

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the authors expand the related work section. A more thorough discussion comparing and contrasting the proposed model with recent Bayesian models of perception – explicitly detailing its advantages and novel aspects – would significantly enhance its impact. This discussion should clearly articulate *how* this model moves beyond existing contributions.

Furthermore, the paper emphasizes mathematical identifiability, stemming from the model’s inherent assumptions. It’s important to acknowledge that this type of identifiability differs from the practical challenge of disentangling underlying sources of variability in perceptual data (e.g., sensory noise vs. decision noise vs. motor noise). The model, by its design, may not resolve *that* specific type of identifiability problem, a limitation worth explicitly addressing. A discussion acknowledging this distinction and its implications for interpreting results would improve the paper’s nuance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a mathematically rigorous and computationally tractable framework for Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   Offers a valuable tool for a priori analysis of experimental design and parameter identifiability.
*   Demonstrated through compelling case studies in time and speed perception.
*   Addresses a crucial issue in Bayesian modeling: the potential for non-identifiable solutions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the model is incremental, building heavily on existing Bayesian approaches in perception.
*   The discussion of “identifiability” focuses primarily on mathematical properties and may not fully address the broader challenges of inferring underlying perceptual mechanisms.
