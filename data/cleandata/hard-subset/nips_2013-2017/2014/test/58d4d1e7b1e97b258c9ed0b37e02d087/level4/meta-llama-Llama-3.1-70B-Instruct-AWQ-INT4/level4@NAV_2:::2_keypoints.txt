- Tackles identifiability of Bayesian cognitive models; proposes a novel framework for testing identifiability.  
- Manuscript is nicely written and quite clear.  
- Framework analyzes expected support for a model given a dataset and is applied to two case studies (time-interval estimation, speed perception).  
- Methodology is interesting, innovative, and likely broadly applicable in cognitive modeling.  
- Reviewer judges the work above threshold for presentation at NIPS.  
- Main concern: the approach rests on a long list of assumptions that readers must accept, which may outweigh the impact of the results.  
- Results in the case studies are not very strong and some findings are expected or intuitive.  
- Section 4.1: interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; need to report which condition differences are reliable.  
- Section 4.2: must demonstrate the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior if the reference observer lacks it.  
- Line 106: clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution.  
- Line 134: success may be driven by the log-stimulus transform imposing a strong constraint on inference.  
- Line 143: constraints in Eq.4 and maximizing differential entropy may not guarantee Eq.3 is unimodal.  
- Line 158: explain why the observer's prior favors a lattice representation over a uniform one.  
- Figure 1: improve by integrating panel b into a and removing p_est(s^*|x) from the figure.  
- Line 252: comment on conditions on N that make Equation 14 approximately correct.  
- Line 262: clarify which density is being sampled from.  
- Line 365: explain why learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC.  
- Overall: innovative and useful methodology despite modest/expected empirical findings.
