This article proposes a framework for assessing the identifiability of parameters in Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue given the increasing reliance on these models to infer internal representations from behavior. The authors highlight the inherent degeneracy in the Bayesian framework – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield the same observed behavior – and present a method for systematically investigating this issue. They instantiate the framework with applications to time interval estimation and speed perception, demonstrating its potential for improving experimental design and validating model inferences.

The issue of identifiability is longstanding in cognitive science and psychology. Early work, such as Anderson (1978), recognized that different representational formats can produce equivalent behavior, necessitating constraints to achieve unique solutions. More recently, concerns regarding identifiability have become a significant critique of the Bayesian approach (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012), particularly regarding the interpretation of recovered internal representations. 

The paper is clearly written and tackles a theoretically important problem. However, I have two main concerns. First, the work lacks sufficient engagement with the existing literature on model comparison and identifiability *within* cognitive science. While the proposed framework offers a specific approach, it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its relationship to established techniques (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004; and Myung et al., NIPS 2000). This would allow for a more nuanced evaluation of the contribution. Second, some of the assumptions made to constrain the Bayesian models appear arbitrary, and the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored. For instance, the choice of equation 3 to define $p_{meas}$ – while acknowledged as flexible – lacks strong justification. Similarly, the authors should further discuss the implications of imposing specific functional forms on priors and likelihoods and how these choices impact identifiability. A more thorough exploration of these assumptions would strengthen the paper’s conclusions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Identifiability is a fundamental challenge for Bayesian modeling in perception.
* **Novel framework:** The authors present a systematic approach to assessing identifiability that could be valuable for researchers in the field.
* **Clear and well-written:** The paper is easy to understand and logically organized.
* **Illustrative examples:** The applications to time interval estimation and speed perception demonstrate the potential utility of the framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited connection to related work:** The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough engagement with the literature on model comparison and identifiability.
* **Arbitrary assumptions:** The assumptions made to constrain the Bayesian models lack sufficient justification and their impact on the results is not fully explored.
* **Potential for overconfidence:** The framework provides a tool for *assessing* identifiability, but does not guarantee that recovered parameters are uniquely determined or meaningful.
