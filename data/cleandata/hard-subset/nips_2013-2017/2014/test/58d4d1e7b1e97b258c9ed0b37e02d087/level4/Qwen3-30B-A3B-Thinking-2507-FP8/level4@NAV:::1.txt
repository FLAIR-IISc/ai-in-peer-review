

This article introduces a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data. The authors summarize Bayesian Decision Theory, identify challenges of non-identifiability, and propose constraints to render model components identifiable. They then impose specific assumptions to constrain each Bayesian term, examine differentiability of parameters, and apply the method to two behavioral studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.  

The issue of identifiability in cognitive processes has been central to cognitive science and psychology for decades, as exemplified by Anderson (1978), who demonstrated that representational formats become indistinguishable without process constraints. In recent years, this has emerged as a significant barrier to broader adoption of Bayesian frameworks (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012—addressing speed perception as a case study).  

While the article addresses a theoretically important issue with clear writing and effective presentation, two major concerns arise. First, it fails to engage with extensive prior work on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, obscuring its novelty. The proposed framework bears substantial similarity to established approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, key assumptions lack justification. For instance, why is $p_{meas}$ defined by Equation 3? Does specifying parameters in Equation 12 fully capture cognitive/perceptual processes, or is this assumption overly restrictive despite its flexibility? The article should explicitly defend these constraints to establish their necessity and validity.
