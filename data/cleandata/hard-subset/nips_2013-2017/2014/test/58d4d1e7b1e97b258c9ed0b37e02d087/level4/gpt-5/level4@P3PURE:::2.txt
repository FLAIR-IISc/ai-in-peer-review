This manuscript tackles the important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. It is clearly written and generally easy to follow. However, the main contributions rely on a relatively long list of assumptions that the reader must accept, and this list feels large relative to the impact of the key results. That said, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and I judge the work to be above the acceptance threshold for NIPS.

Key results:
- Section 4.1: The near-interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising (I questioned the need to include both while reading). Please make explicit which condition differences are statistically reliable; the box plots alone made this difficult to discern.
- Section 4.2: It seems essential to demonstrate that if the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, the method would correctly recover that fact as well.

Specific comments:
- Line 106: Why is the stimulus modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution when the underlying variable is continuous? I assume this mirrors typical experimental designs in which discrete stimulus levels are manipulated as independent variables, but this should be clarified.
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Eq. 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, appears to impose a strong constraint on inference. The success of the approach may hinge largely on this assumption; in effect, the paper shows that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.
- Line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4, together with maximizing differential entropy, ensure that the fit to Eq. 3 is unimodal? It seems possible in principle to obtain a bimodal fit.
- Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over a uniform representation? What does the observer know about the true stimulus distribution that justifies a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?
- Figure 1: I found the figure more confusing than illuminating. It might be clearer to integrate Figure 1b into Figure 1a and remove p_est(s^*|x), since it is a derived quantity.
- Line 252: Please comment on the conditions on N under which Eq. 14 is approximately correct—i.e., when Stirling’s approximation is adequate and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]. By the time Eq. 15 is introduced—after abandoning priors on θ and making these approximations—it feels justified mainly because it yields an intuitive rule for assessing similarity of predictions.
- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density is meant here? The dataset distribution?
- Line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC?

Overall: Innovative approach and important problem, but the results are less than overwhelming given the number and strength of the assumptions required. Still, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and I consider the work above threshold for presentation at NIPS.
