- Proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian behavioral models by summarizing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying non-identifiability, and specifying constraints to make components identifiable; demonstrates with assumptions, parameter distinguishability tests, and two case studies (time interval estimation and motion perception).

- Strength: tackles an important, long-standing theoretical problem and is generally well-written.

- Major concern 1: Largely ignores extensive prior literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science, leaving the novelty and contribution unclear (e.g., Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004).

- Major concern 2: Several specific assumptions used to constrain the model appear arbitrary (e.g., choice of p_meas in Eq.3 and parameterization in Eq.12) and lack sufficient justification or sensitivity analysis.

- Recommendation: Authors should thoroughly justify chosen assumptions and assess how critical those choices are to the framework’s conclusions.

- Recommendation: Explicitly situate and contrast the proposed approach with existing methods and critiques (including Bowers & Davis 2012; Jones & Love 2011; Myung/Pitt/Navarro work).

- Summary judgment: Paper is valuable but its contribution is undermined until it addresses the literature gap and provides stronger justification for modeling choices.

- Minor: Line 53 — don’t label the claim "trivial"; provide an explanation instead.

- Minor: Line 73 — likely typo: second "nor" should be "or".

- Minor: Figure 2 — caption unclear about what the star and dashed lines denote (both seem described as the true value).
