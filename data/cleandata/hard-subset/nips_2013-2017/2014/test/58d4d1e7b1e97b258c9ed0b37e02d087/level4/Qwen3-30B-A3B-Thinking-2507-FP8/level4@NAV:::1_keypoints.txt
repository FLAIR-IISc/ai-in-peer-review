- Introduces a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data, summarizing Bayesian Decision Theory and proposing constraints to address non-identifiability.  
- Imposes specific assumptions to make Bayesian model components identifiable and examines parameter differentiability.  
- Applies the method to two behavioral domains: time interval estimation and motion perception.  
- Notes the longstanding importance of identifiability in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978) and recent debates limiting Bayesian approaches.  
- Praises clear writing and effective presentation but raises two major concerns about novelty and assumptions.  
- Major concern 1: Fails to engage with extensive prior work on model comparison (e.g., Pitt et al., Navarro et al.), making the framework’s novelty unclear.  
- Major concern 2: Key assumptions lack justification—e.g., the form of p_meas in Eq. 3 and whether parameters in Eq. 12 adequately capture cognitive/perceptual processes.  
- Recommends explicitly defending the chosen constraints to establish their necessity and validity.  
- Minor comments: explain the statement on Line 53 instead of calling it “trivial,” correct “nor” to “or” on Line 73, and clarify star vs dashed lines in Figure 2 caption.  
- Overall: a well-written article on a critical topic but lacking sufficient connection to foundational prior work.
