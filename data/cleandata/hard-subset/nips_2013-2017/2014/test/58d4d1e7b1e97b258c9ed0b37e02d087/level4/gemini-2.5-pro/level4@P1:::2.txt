This paper presents a framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a critical and often overlooked issue in cognitive modeling. The authors introduce a flexible, parametric class of observer models designed to capture a wide range of perceptual estimation tasks. Their core contribution is a method to explore the "identifiability landscape" by assessing how distinguishable two different observer models are, based on the expected Kullback-Leibler divergence between their predicted response distributions. This allows researchers to evaluate, before collecting data, which model parameters can be reliably recovered for a given experimental design. The framework is demonstrated in two case studies: (1) inferring priors in a time interval estimation task, where they show how different experimental designs affect recovery, and (2) reconstructing the "slow-speed prior" in a speed perception task, where they find the inference to be fairly robust.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this work is its novel and systematic approach to model identifiability. This is a fundamental problem for the field, as the flexibility of Bayesian models can often lead to multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions that explain the same behavioral data. Providing a computational tool to probe these degeneracies *a priori* is a significant contribution that could help design more informative experiments. The methodology is interesting and innovative.

However, the framework's main weakness is that its conclusions are predicated on a long list of specific modeling assumptions. The proposed observer model is complex, with parametric forms for the sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss function. While flexible, a user must accept these specific functional forms. Consequently, the impact of the key results feels somewhat limited relative to the complexity of the machinery required to obtain them. Despite this, the novelty and importance of the problem being addressed make the paper a valuable contribution.

**Critique of Results**

Regarding the case studies, some of the conclusions are not entirely surprising, while others require further validation.
*   In Section 4.1, the finding that sensory and motor noise can be difficult to distinguish is not unexpected, given that they both add variability to the observer's response. To strengthen the claims about the relative merits of different experimental designs (BSL, SRT, MAP, etc.), it would be helpful for the authors to clarify which of the observed differences in reconstruction error are statistically reliable, as this is not immediately apparent from the box plots.
*   In Section 4.2, the analysis demonstrates that a reference observer's slow-speed prior can be successfully recovered. However, a crucial validation is missing: can the framework also recover the *absence* of such a prior? For instance, if the reference observer were initialized with a uniform prior, it is essential to show that the method would correctly infer this, thereby confirming that the result is not an artifact of the model structure.

**Specific Comments and Suggestions**

1.  **Discrete Stimulus Distribution (L106):** The model assumes that stimuli are drawn from a discrete distribution, even though the variable (e.g., time, speed) is continuous. While this likely reflects typical experimental designs where stimulus levels are fixed independent variables, the authors should explicitly state this justification to avoid confusion.

2.  **Role of the Sensory Transform (L134):** The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is well-motivated by psychophysical laws but also appears to be a critical component that strongly constrains the inference process. It seems the paper's claim is more accurately stated as: Bayesian models are identifiable *given* strong, psychologically plausible assumptions about the internal representational space. The authors should discuss the extent to which their conclusions depend on this specific transformation.

3.  **Unimodality of the Likelihood (L143):** The paper states that the two-Gaussian mixture for the likelihood (Eq. 3) is constrained to be unimodal. Could the authors clarify how this is guaranteed? The described procedure—fitting the first four moments and maximizing differential entropy—does not seem to inherently prevent a bimodal solution.

4.  **Observer's Prior Assumptions (L158):** The structure of the observer's prior (Eq. 4) raises two questions. First, what is the justification for the lattice representation (a mixture of regularly spaced Gaussians)? Second, the assumption that the prior's support is 50% wider than the true stimulus range seems arbitrary. Please provide the rationale behind these modeling choices.

5.  **Clarity of Figure 1:** Figure 1 could be improved for clarity. Integrating the schematic of the observer model (Figure 1b) directly into the probabilistic graphical model (Figure 1a) might provide a more unified and intuitive overview. Furthermore, representing the estimated stimulus, p_est(s*|x), as a distinct node could be confusing since it is a derived quantity within the decision stage. It may be better to omit it or represent it differently to avoid implying it is a fundamental random variable.

6.  **Approximations in the Identifiability Metric (L252):** The derivation of the core metric (Eq. 12) from the expected log-likelihood (Eq. 11) involves several approximations. The authors should provide a more detailed justification for these steps and the conditions under which they are valid. As presented, the final metric in Equation 13 seems justified more by its intuitive appeal as a similarity measure based on KL divergence than by a rigorous derivation from a full Bayesian evidence framework. A discussion of the limitations introduced by these approximations is warranted.

7.  **Task-Specific Prior Learning (L365):** In the speed perception study, the authors assume no learning of the experimental stimulus distribution, justifying this by analogy to the original 2AFC task. This is a critical assumption that may not hold in an estimation task, where observers are known to adapt their priors. Could the authors expand on why this is a reasonable simplification and discuss how task-specific learning might affect their conclusions?

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem with an innovative approach. While the results are not overwhelming and the framework relies on many assumptions, the methodological contribution is significant enough to merit acceptance at NIPS. Addressing the points above would further strengthen the manuscript.
