In this paper, the authors present a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data. They review Bayesian decision theory, identify sources of non-identifiability, and specify the kinds of constraints that can render each component of the Bayesian formulation identifiable. They then impose assumptions to constrain each term, examine how well parameters can be distinguished in their implementation, and apply the method to two cases where Bayesian decision theory has been used to account for behavior: time-interval estimation and motion perception.

Questions about the identifiability of internal representations and processes have been central in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when different representational formats can mimic one another, demonstrating that they are indistinguishable without process constraints whenever the representations make equivalent input distinctions.

In recent years, this has been highlighted as a key limitation of the Bayesian approach that has hindered its wider adoption (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012—the latter discusses speed perception explicitly).

Although the manuscript engages an issue of major theoretical importance, is clearly written, and generally does so effectively, I have two main concerns. First, it largely omits the substantial prior literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, which makes it difficult to assess its incremental contribution. The general model-comparison framework the authors propose is not far from existing work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, several of the assumptions used to impose constraints seem somewhat arbitrary, and it is unclear how strongly the framework relies on them. For instance, why should p_meas be defined as in Equation 3? Is fixing the parameter values in Equation 12 truly sufficient to specify and capture all relevant characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? I recognize that the parameterization is robust and can represent a broad class of sensory models. However, that alone may not fully justify the assumption; at minimum, the paper should discuss and defend the specific assumptions used to make the Bayesian models identifiable.
