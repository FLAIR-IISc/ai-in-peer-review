In this article, the authors propose a framework for model comparison using a Bayesian model on behavioral data. To do this, they present a summary of the Bayesian decision theory framework, identify non-identifiable regions, and outline the types of constraints that can be used to make each word identifiable in the Bayesian framework. They then create assumptions to limit each word in the Bayesian framework, determine how differential the parameter values are in their model, and apply the technique to two studies using Bayesian decision theory to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issues of internal representation and process identification have been major concerns within cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when representation patterns can mimic each other, proving that they are indistinguishable without some constraints on the processes (whenever the representations differ by an input equivalent).

In recent years, this has been one of the major shortcomings of the Bayesian framework that has hindered its widespread adoption (for example, Jones and Love, 2011; Bowers and Davis, 2012 – the latter directly addresses motion perception as an example).

Although the article touches on an issue of great theoretical importance, is well-written, and generally does so in a fair and effective manner, I have two major objections to it. First, it ignores the large body of previous literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making it difficult to properly evaluate its contribution. The general framework for model comparison proposed by the authors is not so different from this previous work (for example, Pitt, Myong, and Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, and Myong, 2004). Second, the authors create assumptions that sometimes seem arbitrary, and it is not clear how much their framework depends on them. For example, why should $p_{meas}$ be defined by equation 3? Is defining the parameter values in equation 12 really sufficient to define and capture all the characteristics of the cognitive or conceptual process? I understand that it is robust and can express a large class of conceptual models. However, it is not clear that this is sufficient justification for the assumption, and at the very least, the article should discuss and justify the assumptions used to limit the Bayesian model so that they are identifiable.
