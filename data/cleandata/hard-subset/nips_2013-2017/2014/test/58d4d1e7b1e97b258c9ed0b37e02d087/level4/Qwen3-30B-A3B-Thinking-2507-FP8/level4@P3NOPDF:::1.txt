

This paper addresses a critical challenge in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian models when comparing them against behavioral data. The authors synthesize Bayesian Decision Theory, identify key non-identifiability issues, and propose constraints to render model terms identifiable. They then apply their framework to two empirical domains—time interval estimation and motion perception—demonstrating how differentiable parameterizations can resolve ambiguities. The topic is highly significant, as identifiability problems have long hindered the adoption of Bayesian approaches in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, 1978; Bowers & Davis, 2012; Jones & Love, 2011). The writing is clear, and the technical execution is generally sound.

However, two major concerns undermine the paper’s contribution. First, the work fails to engage meaningfully with the extensive literature on model comparison in cognitive science. The proposed framework overlaps substantially with established methods, such as those by Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004), which provide systematic approaches for distinguishing models based on data. Without explicit comparison to these works, the paper’s novelty is unclear. For instance, Pitt et al. (2002) introduced a framework for selecting among cognitive models using Bayesian model selection, while Navarro et al. (2004) formalized criteria for model distinguishability. The authors’ claim that their framework "fills a gap" is unfounded without addressing how it advances beyond these prior efforts. This omission makes it difficult to assess the paper’s true contribution to the field.

Second, the paper relies on assumptions whose rationale is insufficiently justified. The constraint defining $p_{meas}$ (Equation 3) is presented without justification, raising questions about its necessity or generality. Similarly, the authors assert that defining parameters via Equation 12 "captures all characteristics" of cognitive processes, yet they do not demonstrate why this parametrization is sufficient or how it avoids arbitrariness. While the authors note the framework’s flexibility in expressing sensory models, this is not a substitute for rigorous justification. For example, why must $p_{meas}$ follow this specific form rather than alternatives? Without addressing such concerns, the framework risks appearing ad hoc. The paper should explicitly discuss the assumptions’ scope, limitations, and sensitivity—particularly whether key results depend on these choices. This is especially critical given that identifiability constraints directly determine model interpretations in behavioral studies.

The minor comments further highlight areas needing refinement. Line 53’s claim that "it is a trivial mathematical fact" is unhelpful; a brief explanation would aid readers unfamiliar with the concept. Line 73 contains a grammatical error ("nor" should be "or"). Figure 2’s caption ambiguously states that both star and dashed lines denote the "true value," requiring clarification of their distinct roles (e.g., "star: true parameter; dashed: estimated value").

Despite these issues, the paper tackles an important problem with a well-structured approach. The application to time interval estimation and motion perception demonstrates practical utility, and the focus on identifiability aligns with ongoing debates about Bayesian modeling’s validity in cognitive science (Jones & Love, 2011). The core idea—using explicit constraints to resolve non-identifiability—is valuable, but its significance is diminished by the lack of engagement with existing model comparison methodologies. Addressing these gaps would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution.

In summary, the paper presents a technically competent framework for Bayesian model identifiability, but its novelty is overstated due to insufficient engagement with prior work on model comparison. The assumptions require stronger justification, and minor technical issues need correction. With revisions to contextualize the work within the established literature and clarify the rationale for key constraints, this paper could make a meaningful contribution to cognitive modeling. As it stands, it risks being perceived as incremental rather than transformative. I recommend *major revisions* to address these concerns before acceptance.
