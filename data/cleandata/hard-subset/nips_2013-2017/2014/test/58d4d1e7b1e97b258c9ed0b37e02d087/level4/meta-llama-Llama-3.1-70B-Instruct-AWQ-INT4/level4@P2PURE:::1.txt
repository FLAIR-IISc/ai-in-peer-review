The authors propose a framework for comparing Bayesian models on behavioral data, building upon the Bayesian Decision Theory framework. They identify areas of non-identifiability and outline the types of constraints required to make each term in the Bayesian framework identifiable. The authors then make assumptions to constrain each term and investigate the differentiability of parameter values in their model. They apply their technique to two studies that utilize Bayesian decision theory to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception.

Issues of identifiability of internal representations and processes have long been a concern in cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that representational formats can be indistinguishable without constraints on processes, as they may make equivalent distinctions of their inputs. Recently, the identifiability issue has hindered the widespread adoption of the Bayesian framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

Although the article addresses a theoretically important issue, is well-written, and generally effective, I have two major reservations. Firstly, the article overlooks the extensive literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, making it challenging to evaluate its contribution. The proposed framework for model comparison bears resemblance to previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Secondly, the authors make assumptions that appear arbitrary at times, and it is unclear how much of their framework relies on these assumptions. For example, the definition of $p_{meas}$ by equation 3 seems unjustified. Furthermore, it is unclear whether defining the values of the parameters in Equation 12 is sufficient to capture all characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process. Although the framework can express a large class of sensory models, this does not necessarily justify the assumption. At the very least, the article should discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models and ensure their identifiability.

Minor comments:

Line 53: Instead of stating that it is a trivial mathematical fact, the authors may want to provide an explanation for the statement, as it may not be immediately clear to all readers.

Line 73: I believe the second "nor" should be replaced with "or".

Figure 2: The caption does not clearly explain the meaning of the star and dashed lines. It appears that both lines denote the true value, but this should be explicitly stated.
