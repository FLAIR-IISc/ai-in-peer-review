

This paper proposes a systematic framework for testing identifiability in Bayesian observer models used to explain human perceptual behavior. The authors first review Bayesian Decision Theory, identify sources of non-identifiability in the inverse mapping from behavior to internal representations, and outline constraints that can render model components identifiable. They then develop a flexible observer model with specific parametric assumptions, apply their identifiability framework to two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception), and demonstrate how experimental designs can be optimized to improve recovery of internal representations.

The issue of model identifiability has been a persistent theoretical concern in cognitive science for decades. Anderson (1978) established that different representational formats can be empirically indistinguishable without constraints on underlying processes. More recently, this limitation has significantly hindered the broader adoption of Bayesian frameworks in psychology (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012), with speed perception serving as a specific example where identifiability concerns have been raised.

Although the paper addresses an important theoretical issue and is well-written, I have two major reservations. First, it fails to adequately engage with the substantial body of work on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and contribution of their framework. The authors' approach to model comparison bears significant resemblance to established methods (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), yet they do not discuss these connections or clarify how their approach differs. Second, the paper makes several key assumptions without sufficient justification. For instance, the specific parametric form for pmeas in Equation 3 appears arbitrary without explanation of why this particular mixture of Gaussians is necessary or how it relates to the underlying cognitive processes. While the authors claim their model is flexible and can express a wide range of sensory models, they do not provide adequate theoretical justification for their specific assumptions, making it unclear how much their conclusions depend on these choices.
