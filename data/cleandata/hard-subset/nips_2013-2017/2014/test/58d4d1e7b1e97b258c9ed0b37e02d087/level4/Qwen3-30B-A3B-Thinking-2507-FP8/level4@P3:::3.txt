

The paper addresses a critical yet underexplored challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the inherent non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to degeneracy in the inverse problem. The authors propose a systematic framework for *a priori* identifiability testing, enabling researchers to evaluate whether experimental designs can reliably recover model components. Their approach leverages a flexible Bayesian observer model (parameterized via sensory mapping, prior mixtures, loss functions, and response noise) and quantifies identifiability using expected log-likelihood differences under a reference model. The framework is validated through two case studies: (1) time interval estimation, where they demonstrate that motor noise measurement (MTR design) resolves degeneracy in prior recovery, while narrow loss functions (MAP) worsen it; (2) speed perception, where slow-speed priors and noise characteristics are robustly recoverable.  

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound. The model integrates key components of Bayesian perception (sensation, perception, decision, response) into a unified, computationally tractable form. Crucially, the identifiability framework is rigorously derived (via KL divergence and expected log-likelihood) and validated through synthetic data, avoiding the intractability of sampling over datasets. The case studies provide concrete, non-trivial insights (e.g., the counterintuitive harm of MAP loss functions in timing tasks), demonstrating careful attention to both strengths and weaknesses. The model’s flexibility (e.g., handling stimulus-dependent noise via Eq. 2) and computational efficiency (analytical posterior approximations) are significant technical merits.  

**Clarity**: The exposition is well-structured and clear, with thorough mathematical detail in the supplementary material. The four-stage model construction (Section 2) logically scaffolds the framework. The case studies (Section 4) effectively bridge theory to empirical implications. Minor improvements could include a brief table summarizing parameter roles or a schematic of the identifiability workflow, but the current presentation is sufficiently detailed for expert reproducibility.  

**Originality**: The core contribution—*systematic a priori identifiability testing*—is novel within Bayesian perception modeling. While the model components (e.g., mixture likelihoods, log sensory mappings) build on prior work (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli 2006; Acerbi et al. 2012), the *framework for diagnosing and resolving degeneracy* is distinct. The authors correctly note that prior approaches (e.g., Bayesian transfer in Maloney & Mamassian 2002) were ad hoc, lacking a unified method. This work explicitly bridges to recent NIPS contributions (e.g., Körding et al. on neural priors at NIPS 2010), but the *identifiability framework* itself is a new tool. The draft review’s claim of "incremental" contribution mischaracterizes the work: the model is a *vehicle* for the framework, not the primary novelty. The framework’s utility for experimental design (e.g., ranking BSL vs. MTR) represents a significant advance beyond existing models.  

**Significance**: This work addresses a fundamental barrier to interpreting Bayesian models in perception—unreliable recovery of internal representations. By enabling *a priori* assessment of experimental designs, it empowers researchers to avoid flawed inferences (e.g., in timing tasks where prior recovery fails without motor noise constraints). The speed perception case study validates prior empirical claims (Stocker & Simoncelli 2006) through theoretical identifiability, demonstrating the framework’s practical value. The implications extend beyond perception to any Bayesian modeling scenario (e.g., neural decoding), making it broadly relevant to NIPS.  

**Key Strengths**:  
- Proposes a general, computationally efficient framework for identifiability testing (not just a model).  
- Provides actionable guidance for experimental design (e.g., "measure motor noise" to resolve degeneracy).  
- Case studies reveal non-intuitive insights (e.g., MAP loss functions impair prior recovery).  
- Rigorous theoretical foundation (KL divergence, expected log-likelihood).  

**Key Weaknesses**:  
- The draft review’s confusion between "model identifiability" (paper’s focus) and "parameter identifiability in experiments" (a common misconception) needs clarification. The paper *does* resolve model degeneracy *within the framework*, but it does not claim to distinguish sensory/decision/response noise in real data—this is a separate experimental design issue. The authors should explicitly distinguish these in the revision.  
- The "incremental" critique understates the novelty. The framework is novel; prior work (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014) focused on *modeling* temporal statistics, not *testing* identifiability of those models. A revised manuscript should explicitly contrast the framework with prior work on model *construction* (e.g., "Unlike Acerbi et al. (2012), which developed a temporal model, we provide a *method* to assess whether such models can be identified from data").  

**Recommendation**: **Accept**. The paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to the field by providing a tool to address a pervasive methodological flaw in Bayesian perception modeling. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a critical need for rigorous experimental design. The framework’s applicability beyond the two case studies (e.g., to multisensory integration) further enhances its significance.  

**Revision Suggestion**: The authors should add a concise "Related Work" subsection (1–2 paragraphs) explicitly contrasting their identifiability *framework* with prior Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Acerbi et al., Maloney & Mamassian). They should clarify the scope: the framework resolves *model degeneracy* (multiple parameter sets fitting data), not *experimental confounds* (e.g., sensory vs. response noise), which is a distinct challenge. This would preempt the draft review’s misinterpretation and sharpen the originality claim.  

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
- *Pro*: Novel framework for a priori identifiability testing; validated via case studies with actionable experimental insights; technically rigorous and well-motivated.  
- *Con*: Requires minor clarification to distinguish model identifiability from experimental confounds (easily addressed in revision).  

This work directly advances the field by transforming a theoretical limitation (degeneracy) into a testable experimental design principle—a contribution that aligns perfectly with NIPS’s focus on rigorous, impactful methods.
