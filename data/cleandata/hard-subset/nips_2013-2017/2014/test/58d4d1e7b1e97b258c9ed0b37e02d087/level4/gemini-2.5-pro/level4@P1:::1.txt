This paper addresses the critical issue of model identifiability within the Bayesian observer modeling framework, a popular approach for explaining human performance in perceptual tasks. The authors first highlight the inherent degeneracy in Bayesian Decision Theory, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions. To address this, they propose a systematic framework to test the *a priori* identifiability of a model's parameters for a given experimental design. Their method involves two key components: (1) a flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models capable of representing a wide range of sensory processes, internal beliefs, and decision strategies; and (2) an efficient MCMC-based method for exploring the "identifiability landscape" by sampling from an approximate expected posterior density. The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two case studies: recovering priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing priors and noise characteristics in a speed perception task.

The problem of identifiability for internal representations and processes has been a central issue in cognitive science for decades. Anderson (1978), for instance, formally analyzed the conditions under which different representational formats are behaviorally indistinguishable. More recently, the non-identifiability of components has been raised as a major critique of the Bayesian modeling approach, questioning its ability to provide true process-level explanations (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). This paper is therefore timely and addresses a topic of great theoretical importance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamentally important and widely recognized problem. The non-identifiability of components in Bayesian models is a major obstacle to their interpretation as process models, and a systematic framework for assessing this issue *a priori* is a significant and valuable contribution.
2.  **Quality:** The technical approach is well-conceived. The construction of a flexible yet computationally tractable observer model is a notable achievement, and the use of the expected log-likelihood (approximated via KL-divergence) to map the identifiability landscape is an elegant solution.
3.  **Clarity and Application:** The paper is generally well-written and the framework is clearly explained. The application to two well-known experimental paradigms provides concrete demonstrations of the framework's utility, yielding practical and non-obvious insights into experimental design (e.g., the negative impact of a MAP-like loss function on prior recovery in the timing task).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** A major weakness of the paper is its lack of engagement with the extensive literature on model identifiability and model comparison in cognitive science and mathematical psychology. While the problem is framed within the Bayesian observer literature, the core challenge is one of general model selection and parameter recovery. Decades of work have addressed these issues, developing concepts like parameter confounding and proposing frameworks for assessing model distinguishability. More directly, the proposed methodology bears conceptual similarities to techniques like parameter recovery studies and methods based on the geometry of the likelihood function (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). By not citing or contrasting their approach with this body of work, the authors make it difficult for the reader to assess the novelty and specific advantages of their proposed framework. A discussion of how their method relates to, and potentially improves upon, these established techniques is essential.

2.  **Justification of Modeling Assumptions:** The second significant concern relates to the justification for the specific parametric forms used to build the observer model. While the authors state that their choices (e.g., a mixture of two Gaussians for the sensory likelihood in Eq. 3) are flexible and computationally convenient, these assumptions impose strong constraints on the space of possible models. The paper would be strengthened considerably by a more thorough discussion of these choices. For instance, how sensitive are the identifiability results to these specific functional forms? While flexibility is claimed, it is unclear what kinds of observer models fall *outside* this class and whether this exclusion could bias the conclusions. Justifying these assumptions more rigorously, or at least discussing their potential limitations, is crucial for establishing the generality of the framework.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 53: The phrase "trivial mathematical fact" could be perceived as dismissive. It would be more constructive to briefly explain the source of the degeneracy for readers who may not find it immediately obvious.
*   Line 73: The second "nor" should likely be "or" ("...neither empirical nor theoretical – of their effectiveness, or a framework...").
*   Figure 2: The caption could be clarified. Both the star and the dashed lines appear to represent the true parameter value, which is confusing.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a well-written and technically sophisticated framework for addressing the critical problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive science. The approach is powerful, and the case studies demonstrate its practical value for improving experimental design. However, its contribution is significantly undermined by a failure to connect with the long-standing literature on model selection and identifiability in psychology, and by a lack of strong justification for its core modeling assumptions.

While the work has clear potential, these issues are significant enough that I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. If the authors were to thoroughly situate their work within the broader literature and provide a more rigorous defense of their modeling choices, the paper could represent a valuable contribution to the field.
