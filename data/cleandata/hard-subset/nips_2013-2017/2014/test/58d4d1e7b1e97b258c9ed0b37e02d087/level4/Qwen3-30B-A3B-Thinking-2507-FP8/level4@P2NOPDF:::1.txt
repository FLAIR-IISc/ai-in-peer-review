

This paper addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian cognitive modeling: resolving identifiability issues in model comparison. The authors develop a framework to constrain Bayesian decision theory components, enabling meaningful behavioral model comparisons. They systematically analyze non-identifiability sources, propose constraints for each framework term, and validate the approach using two behavioral studies (time interval estimation and motion perception). While the topic is highly relevant—identifiability has long plagued cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, 1978; Bowers & Davis, 2012)—the paper’s contribution requires clearer justification relative to existing work.  

The authors correctly identify that identifiability constraints are essential for distinguishing between cognitive models (as Anderson, 1978, demonstrated for representational formats). However, the paper overlooks significant prior work on model comparison in cognitive science. The proposed framework bears substantial resemblance to established approaches (Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004), yet fails to engage with these foundations. Without this context, it is difficult to assess the novelty or added value of the authors’ method. For instance, Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) already provide rigorous frameworks for model distinguishability and data informativeness—directly relevant to the current paper’s goals. The authors should explicitly position their work within this literature, clarifying how their constraints advance or differ from prior methods.  

A second major concern is the lack of justification for key assumptions. The paper asserts constraints (e.g., defining $p_{meas}$ via Equation 3, or parameterizing cognitive processes via Equation 12) without explaining *why* these choices are theoretically or empirically warranted. While the authors note the framework’s flexibility in expressing sensory models, this alone does not justify the specific assumptions. For example:  
- Why is Equation 3 the appropriate definition for $p_{meas}$? Could alternative formulations yield different inferences?  
- Does Equation 12’s parameterization fully capture the complexity of cognitive/perceptual processes, or does it risk oversimplification?  
The authors acknowledge the framework’s robustness but do not demonstrate that their constraints are *necessary* for identifiability. Without addressing these questions, the validity of their conclusions remains questionable. The paper would benefit from a dedicated section discussing the rationale behind each constraint, including sensitivity analyses or empirical justifications.  

Minor issues:  
- Line 53: Replace "trivial mathematical fact" with a brief explanation (e.g., "as shown in Appendix A").  
- Line 73: Correct "nor" to "or" ("not X nor Y" → "not X or Y").  
- Figure 2: Clarify the meaning of star vs. dashed lines in the caption (both denote "true value" is ambiguous).  

The paper is well-written and tackles an important problem, but its failure to engage with foundational model comparison literature and justify critical assumptions weakens its contribution. The core idea—that constraints resolve identifiability—aligns with established work, yet the authors present it as novel without sufficient differentiation. For instance, the constraints proposed resemble those in Pitt et al. (2002), which explicitly address model distinguishability in cognitive tasks. Without comparing their approach to these methods, the paper’s significance is unclear.  

The authors’ argument that Bayesian models are hindered by identifiability (citing Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) is valid, but their solution does not clearly advance beyond existing tools. To strengthen the paper, they should:  
1. Explicitly contrast their constraints with prior work (e.g., "Unlike Pitt et al. (2002), we constrain *X* via *Y* because...").  
2. Provide theoretical or empirical justification for key assumptions (e.g., "Equation 3 is derived from *Z* because...").  
3. Discuss limitations (e.g., whether their constraints generalize to non-sensory domains).  

As it stands, the paper offers a useful technical contribution but fails to establish its novelty or necessity within the broader context of cognitive model comparison. The field requires such frameworks, but this work does not clearly demonstrate why it is *needed* over existing methods.
