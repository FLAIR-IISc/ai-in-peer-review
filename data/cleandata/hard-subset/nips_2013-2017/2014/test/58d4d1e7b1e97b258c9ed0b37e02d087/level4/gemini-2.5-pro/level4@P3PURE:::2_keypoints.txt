- Addresses an important, challenging problem (model identifiability in cognitive modeling) and is well-written and clear.
- Paper rests on an extensive list of assumptions that may limit the practical impact of the results.
- Methodology is interesting and innovative, making the work acceptable for NIPS despite reservations.
- Section 4.1: finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising; authors should mark statistical significance on box plots.
- Section 4.2: missing a crucial control—show the model correctly recovers when the reference observer does not use a slow-speed prior.
- Clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution (likely experimental design choice).
- The log-stimulus transform (Eq.2) is a strong, potentially constraining assumption that may drive identifiability results.
- Question whether constraints in Eq.4 plus differential-entropy maximization guarantee unimodality of Eq.3’s distribution or could allow bimodality.
- Justify the observer prior choice favoring a lattice over uniform and explain how an observer could adopt a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range.
- Figure 1 is hard to interpret; consider integrating panels and removing the derived posterior panel to improve clarity.
- The derivation of Eq.15 uses approximations (Stirling-based Eq.14 and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) that need conditions/justification.
- Clarify the phrase “sample from this unnormalized density”—specify which density (e.g., over the dataset) is meant.
- Explain why learning priors is claimed not to matter in a two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task.
