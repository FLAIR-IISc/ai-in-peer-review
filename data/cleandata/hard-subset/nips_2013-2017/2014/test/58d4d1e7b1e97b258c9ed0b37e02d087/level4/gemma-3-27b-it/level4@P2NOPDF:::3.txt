This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing the mathematical formulation of sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior distribution, loss function, and resulting response distribution. A key strength lies in the model’s combination of robustness and computational tractability; it accommodates diverse scenarios – such as stimulus-dependent noise – while remaining analytically convenient for inference. The authors rightly emphasize the model’s identifiability, demonstrating the ability to reliably distinguish between parameter estimates.

Overall, I find this paper promising and recommend it for acceptance to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, with potential applications to a wide range of experimental settings. However, I do have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. 

The approach shares considerable thematic and methodological overlap with recent work, particularly that appearing in *PLoS Computational Biology*. While not identical, the similarities to existing models are substantial enough that I would characterize the advancement as incremental. Specifically, I draw attention to the following related publications:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology, 8* (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology, 10* (6), e1003661.

To address this, I suggest the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should clearly articulate how the current model builds upon, and significantly advances, the existing literature. A more detailed comparison will strengthen the paper’s contribution.

Furthermore, while the authors correctly highlight the mathematical identifiability of the model’s parameters, it’s important to acknowledge the limitations of this identifiability. The parameters are identifiable *because* of the specific mathematical assumptions inherent in the model. This differs from the broader “identifiability problem” prevalent in perceptual science – the difficulty of dissociating contributions from sensory, decision, and response noise. The model, in its current form, doesn’t resolve this latter issue; it simply provides identifiable parameters *within* the model’s assumed framework. I recommend the authors address this nuance in the revised manuscript, clarifying the scope and limitations of the model’s identifiability. 

In summary, this is a mathematically sound and potentially valuable contribution. Addressing the points above – specifically, a stronger articulation of novelty relative to existing work, and a more nuanced discussion of identifiability – will significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
