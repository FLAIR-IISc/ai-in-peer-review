Summary
The paper tackles the long-standing problem of identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors formalize how different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield indistinguishable behavior, and propose an a priori framework to assess practical identifiability for a broad class of estimation tasks. Their approach derives an expected log-likelihood (equivalently, an expected KL divergence) between a “true” reference observer and alternative parameterizations, and samples from the resulting approximate expected posterior over parameters to map identifiability under different experimental designs. They instantiate a flexible observer model (mixture-based likelihoods and losses, parametric sensory mapping, lapse and reporting noise), then apply the framework to two case studies: interval timing and speed perception. The analysis highlights designs that reduce parameter degeneracy in interval timing (e.g., fixing motor noise), and confirms the robustness of recovering a slow-speed prior in speed perception.

Relation to prior work
Identifiability and model distinguishability have deep roots in cognitive science and psychology (e.g., Anderson, 1978). There is also a substantial literature on model selection and distinguishability (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004; see also Myung et al., NIPS 2000) and critiques of the Bayesian program’s underconstrained nature (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). The present work’s use of expected log evidence (cross-entropy/KL) to quantify distinguishability aligns with this literature, but the paper would benefit from explicitly positioning its contribution relative to those frameworks (e.g., differences in scope, computational efficiency, and its explicit focus on experimental design).

Strengths
- Important problem: Identifiability is central to claims about internal representations inferred from behavior.
- Clear framework: Deriving the expected log-likelihood as a function of KL divergences provides a principled and interpretable identifiability criterion that scales with trial numbers and stimulus distributions.
- Practical orientation: The analysis naturally supports comparison of experimental designs (e.g., multiple conditions, fixing motor parameters) and reveals nonobvious trade-offs (e.g., narrower loss functions may harm prior recovery in interval timing).
- Case studies: The interval timing and speed perception examples are well-chosen, illustrating both fragile and robust scenarios.

Weaknesses and suggestions
1) Engagement with prior literature:
- The proposed framework is closely related to distinguishability and data informativeness analyses (Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004). Please clarify what is new: e.g., unified observer parameterization enabling fast expected-evidence sampling, explicit focus on perceptual estimation models, or systematic design comparison within a single toolchain.
- Consider discussing connections to optimal experimental design (e.g., Fisher information, D-/A-optimality) and why the expected-KL approach is preferable or complementary here.

2) Dependence on modeling assumptions:
- Several choices appear ad hoc (e.g., two-Gaussian mixtures for pmeas in Eq. 3, inverted Gaussian mixtures for losses in Eq. 6, lapse drawing from the prior, linear-plus-quadratic reporting noise). The maximum-entropy rationale is helpful, but the paper should justify these choices substantively (empirical precedent, computational tractability, and how they approximate known psychophysical regularities).
- Add sensitivity/ablation analyses: vary the number of mixture components; compare alternative noise shapes (e.g., log-normal, Laplace, heavy-tailed), different lapse models, or different response-noise structures. Show which qualitative conclusions persist.
- Clarify the role of coupling/decoupling internal vs. external likelihoods (the UNC speed-perception variant is a good step). Report how conclusions change as this assumption is relaxed in the timing task.

3) Scope of “model comparison”:
- The current work primarily assesses parameter identifiability within a model class and across experimental designs, rather than selecting among distinct theoretical models. Consider reframing as “a priori identifiability and design analysis” to avoid confusion with model selection across competing families.

4) Methodological details:
- Explain the choice of asymmetric vs. symmetric KL: Eq. 12 uses the asymmetric KL (appropriate given the generative θ*) while later summaries use a symmetric KL for error reporting. Clarify the rationale and implications.
- Discuss computational aspects: MCMC details, convergence diagnostics, and scalability with dimensionality and Nexp.

Quality
- Generally sound, with a principled expected-evidence objective and careful case studies. The main concern is how strongly results depend on specific parametrizations; additional sensitivity checks would increase confidence.

Clarity
- Well-written and organized. Please (a) replace “trivial mathematical fact” with a brief explanation of the degeneracy argument, (b) correct the “nor/or” issue noted below, and (c) clarify figures and notation (e.g., symbols in Figure 2).

Originality
- Solid synthesis with an efficient, unified implementation for perceptual estimation tasks. However, the conceptual core overlaps with prior distinguishability frameworks; clearer differentiation is needed.

Significance
- High potential impact, especially for informing experimental design and for calibrating claims about inferred internal representations. The speed-perception analysis provides useful theoretical support for established empirical results.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a central and timely question with a principled, implementable framework.
- Offers actionable guidance for experimental design.
- Case studies demonstrate both pitfalls and robust settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient discussion of related identifiability/model selection literature and how this work advances it.
- Heavy reliance on specific modeling assumptions without thorough sensitivity analyses.
- Some presentation issues and minor ambiguities.

Minor comments
- Line 53: Replace “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” with a concise derivation or intuitive explanation of the degeneracy (e.g., scaling relationships among prior, likelihood, and loss).
- Line 73: The second “nor” appears to be “or.”
- Figure 2: Clarify what the star and dashed lines denote; the caption suggests both indicate the true value.
- Terminology: Consider replacing “model comparison” with “identifiability/design analysis” where appropriate.
- Add citations to Pitt, Myung, & Zhang (2002); Navarro, Pitt, & Myung (2004); and Myung et al. (NIPS 2000) in the main text when positioning the framework.
