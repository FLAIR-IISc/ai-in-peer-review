The manuscript tackles the important issue in cognitive modeling of model identifiability. The manuscript is nicely written and quite clear. However, the work is premised on a long list of assumptions that one must buy into and make sense of, and this list is quite long relative to the impact of the key results presented. Because the methodology is interesting and innovative, I judge the work to be above threshold for presentation at NIPS.

In terms of key results, the findings in section 4.1 are somewhat expected, as the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is not entirely surprising. It would be helpful to indicate which condition differences were reliable, as this would provide a clearer understanding of the results. Additionally, the use of box plots could be clarified, as it may be difficult for some readers to interpret.

In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior if the reference observer does not have one. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's capabilities and limitations.

Specifically, on line 106, it would be beneficial to clarify why the stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution when it is, in fact, a continuous variable. This is likely due to the typical design of experiments, but a brief explanation would be helpful.

On line 134, the authors note that the log-stimulus transform is motivated by psychophysical laws. It appears that the success of their work may be due in large part to the incorporation of this transform. This suggests that the model's identifiability is contingent upon strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.

On line 143, it is unclear whether the constraints in Eq 4 and the maximization of differential entropy ensure that Eqn 3 is fit to be a unimodal distribution. The authors should clarify whether the fit could, in principle, yield a bimodal distribution.

On line 158, it is unclear why the observer's prior should favor the lattice representation over a uniform representation. The authors should provide more context about what the user knows about the true stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior which is 50% wider than the true range.

Figure 1 is somewhat confusing, and it would be beneficial to integrate Figure 1b into Figure 1a and remove p_est(s^*|x) from the figure, as it is a derived inference.

On line 252, the authors should comment on the conditions on N that make Equation 14 approximately correct, including when Stirling's approximation of the factorial holds and under what circumstances E[xlogx] ~= E[x]logE[x]. By the point where the authors arrive at Equation 15, having abandoned priors on θ and made some questionable approximations, it is unclear whether Equation 15 is justified beyond yielding an intuitive rule for assessing similarity of predictions.

On line 262, it is unclear which density the authors are referring to when they mention "sample from this unnormalized density." It would be helpful to clarify whether this refers to the dataset distribution.

Finally, on line 365, the authors should explain why learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC. This seems like an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less than overwhelming.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The methodology is interesting and innovative
* The work tackles an important issue in cognitive modeling

Arguments against acceptance:

* The work is premised on a long list of assumptions that may be difficult to buy into
* The results are not entirely surprising, and some may be expected
* The manuscript could benefit from additional clarification and explanation of certain points.
