

This paper addresses a critical challenge in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to degeneracies in the inverse mapping from behavior to cognitive components. The authors propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing, enabling researchers to evaluate how experimental designs affect the reliability of recovering these representations. They formalize the problem using Bayesian Decision Theory, derive constraints to resolve degeneracies, and validate their approach through two case studies—time interval estimation and speed perception—demonstrating how their method can rank experimental designs and identify problematic parameters.

The issue of model non-identifiability has long been recognized in cognitive science. Anderson (1978) established that representational formats can be indistinguishable without constraints, and recent critiques (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) have highlighted this as a major barrier to the Bayesian framework's adoption in psychology. The paper’s focus on this problem is therefore timely and well-motivated, aligning with NIPS’ emphasis on foundational methodological contributions. The authors’ framework builds on prior work in psychophysics (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006) but provides a novel, generalizable approach for *systematically* assessing identifiability before experiments are conducted.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly structured, and technically sound. The proposed observer model is flexible yet computationally efficient, covering a broad class of sensory estimation tasks. The application to two well-studied perceptual domains (time estimation and speed perception) effectively demonstrates the framework’s utility. The case studies yield concrete, non-trivial insights—for instance, the finding that imposing a narrow loss function (MAP-like) can *worsen* prior recovery in interval timing, or that speed perception inferences are robust to noise representation assumptions. These results directly address the significance criterion by advancing experimental design practices in perceptual modeling.

**Key Limitations:**  
1. **Insufficient engagement with prior model comparison literature:** The framework overlaps substantially with established cognitive science approaches (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004). While the paper acknowledges "generic techniques" (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), it fails to explicitly position its contribution relative to these methods. For instance, the use of KL divergence (Eq. 12) and MCMC sampling for identifiability analysis mirrors techniques used in cognitive modeling for decades. The authors should clarify how their *a priori* framework differs from and improves upon existing model comparison tools, particularly regarding the practical constraints of perceptual experiments. Without this, the paper’s novelty is unclear to the NIPS audience, which expects work that advances the *state of the art* in computational modeling.

2. **Lack of justification for key assumptions:** The choice of Eq. (3) for sensory noise (mixture of two Gaussians) and the parametric form in Eq. (2) (sensory mapping) requires stronger motivation. While the authors note these allow "a large class of sensory models," they do not justify why *this specific parametrization* is necessary or sufficient. For example:  
   - Why two Gaussians? Could a single Gaussian suffice for the tasks studied?  
   - How do the free parameters (e.g., σ, γ, κ) relate to measurable psychophysical phenomena?  
   The paper assumes these choices are "practical" but does not demonstrate that alternative parametrizations would yield different conclusions. This undermines the claim that the framework is "general" and leaves readers uncertain about the robustness of their results. A brief discussion of sensitivity to these assumptions (e.g., "Varying the number of Gaussian components in Eq. 3 changed identifiability results by X%") would greatly strengthen the contribution.

**Minor Issues:**  
- Line 53: "It is a trivial mathematical fact..." → Replace with "This degeneracy follows directly from the invariance of the optimization problem under the transformation ∏φᵢ(s) = c (see Eq. 1), which is a standard result in inverse problems."  
- Line 73: "nor" should be "or" ("neither empirical nor theoretical").  
- Figure 2: Clarify the meaning of star/dashed lines in the caption (e.g., "solid lines: true prior; dashed: inferred prior; stars: true central moments").  

**Conclusion:** This is a valuable methodological contribution with strong practical implications for perceptual modeling. The paper is technically rigorous, clearly written, and addresses a significant problem in the field. However, its impact is limited by insufficient engagement with the existing cognitive science literature on model comparison and inadequate justification for core model assumptions. To meet NIPS’ standards for significance and novelty, the authors must explicitly contrast their framework with prior work and demonstrate the necessity of their parametric choices. With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. As it stands, it is *conditionally acceptable*—the framework is useful, but its novelty and scope require clearer articulation.
