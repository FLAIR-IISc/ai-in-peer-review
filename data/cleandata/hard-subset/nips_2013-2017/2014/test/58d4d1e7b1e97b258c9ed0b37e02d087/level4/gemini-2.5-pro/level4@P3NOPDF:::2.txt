This manuscript addresses the important and challenging problem of model identifiability within the domain of cognitive modeling. The paper is well-written and the proposed methodology is clearly explained. However, the work is built upon a significant number of assumptions that a reader must accept for the results to be compelling. While the methodology is interesting and innovative, the impact of the key results feels modest relative to the lengthy chain of underlying assumptions. Nevertheless, the novelty of the approach makes the work a worthwhile contribution to the conference.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles a critical and difficult issue in cognitive science and computational modeling: ensuring that a model's parameters are uniquely recoverable from data.
*   The proposed methodology is novel and presents an interesting approach to this problem.
*   The manuscript is generally clear and well-written.

**Major Concerns:**
*   **Reliance on Strong Assumptions:** The framework's success appears to rely heavily on a series of strong assumptions. For example, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, imposes a very strong constraint on the inference process. This raises the question of whether the paper's primary contribution is a general method for ensuring identifiability, or rather a demonstration that Bayesian models become identifiable when supplied with strong, domain-specific constraints on internal representations.
*   **Validation of Results:** The empirical results presented in Section 4 are not entirely convincing.
    *   In Section 4.1, the finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not particularly surprising, and it calls into question the initial decision to include both distinct noise sources in the model. Furthermore, it would strengthen the results to explicitly state which of the condition differences shown in the box plots are statistically significant.
    *   A critical control is missing in Section 4.2. The authors show that their model can recover a slow-speed prior when the observer has one. However, to demonstrate the method's validity, it is essential to also show the converse: that if the reference observer did *not* have a slow-speed prior, the model could correctly recover this absence. Without this demonstration, it is difficult to assess the model's specificity.

**Specific Comments and Questions:**

*   **line 106:** The stimulus is a continuous variable, yet it is drawn from a discrete distribution. While this likely reflects the design of typical psychophysical experiments where stimulus levels are discrete independent variables, a brief clarification on this point would be helpful.

*   **line 143:** Regarding Equation 3, do the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that the resulting distribution will be unimodal? It seems plausible that this fitting procedure could, in principle, yield a multimodal distribution.

*   **line 158:** The justification for the observer's prior favoring a "lattice representation" over a uniform one is unclear. What knowledge does the observer possess about the true stimulus distribution that would lead them to adopt a prior that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range?

*   **line 252:** The derivation of Equation 14 and 15 relies on several approximations. Could the authors comment on the conditions on N under which Stirling's approximation is valid here, and more importantly, when the approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] holds? The chain of reasoning—which includes abandoning priors on θ and employing these approximations—leaves the impression that Equation 15 is justified more by its intuitive appeal as a similarity rule than by a rigorous mathematical derivation.

*   **line 262:** In the phrase "sample from this unnormalized density," it is unclear which density is being referred to. Please clarify if this is the dataset distribution or another density.

*   **line 365:** The text states that the learning of priors does not matter in a two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task. Could the authors briefly elaborate on the reasoning behind this assertion?

*   **Figure 1:** The figure is more confusing than enlightening. Its clarity could be improved by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a. Furthermore, since p_est(s^*|x) is a derived quantity (an inference), its removal from the primary schematic might simplify the diagram and focus the reader's attention on the core components of the model.
