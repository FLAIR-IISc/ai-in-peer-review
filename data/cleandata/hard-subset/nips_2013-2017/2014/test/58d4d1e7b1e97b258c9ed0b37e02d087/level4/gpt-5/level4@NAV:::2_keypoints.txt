- Addresses model identifiability in cognitive modeling; well written, novel, and above the bar for NIPS despite concerns.
- Approach depends on a substantial list of assumptions that feels long relative to the practical impact.
- Section 4.1: near interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; question need to include both.
- Section 4.1: specify which condition differences are statistically reliable (box plots unclear).
- Section 4.2: need to show that the method would recover the absence of a slow-speed prior if the reference observer lacked it.
- line 106: clarify why a discrete stimulus sampling is used when the stimulus is continuous.
- line 134: the log-stimulus transform is a strong assumption and the approach’s success may hinge largely on it.
- line 143: question whether the constraints and entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fit or if bimodality is possible.
- line 158: justify why the observer’s prior favors a lattice over a uniform representation and why it is 50% wider than the true range.
- Figure 1: figure is confusing; suggest merging 1b into 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) as a derived quantity.
- line 252: clarify for which N Equation 14 is valid (Stirling’s approximation) and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]; Equation 15 relies on debatable approximations.
- line 262: clarify what is meant by “sample from this unnormalized density” (which density? dataset distribution?).
- line 365: explain why learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC.
- Overall conclusion: innovative and important problem, but results are not especially compelling given assumptions and presentation issues.
