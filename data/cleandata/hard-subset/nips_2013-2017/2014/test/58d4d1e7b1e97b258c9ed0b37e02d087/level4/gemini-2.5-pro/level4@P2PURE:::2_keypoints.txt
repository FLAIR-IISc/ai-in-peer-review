- Paper tackles model identifiability in cognitive modeling; well-written and innovative but relies on many strong assumptions that may outweigh the impact of results; recommend acceptance.
- Section 4.1: finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising and raises the question of why both were modeled separately; authors should report which condition differences are statistically significant.
- Section 4.2: missing crucial validation — must show the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in observers who lack it.
- line 106: stimulus is continuous but modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution; authors should clarify this modeling choice.
- line 134: the log-stimulus transform may be a strong, central assumption that drives identifiability, implying results hold only under specific representational assumptions.
- line 143: question whether constraints plus differential-entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal distribution, since a multimodal solution seems possible.
- line 158: observer prior choice (lattice vs uniform and 50% wider range) needs justification and explanation of the observer’s knowledge.
- Figure 1: figure is confusing; recommend merging panels and removing p_est(s^*|x) to improve clarity.
- line 252: clarify validity conditions for approximations (Stirling’s approximation, approximating E[x log x] with E[x] log E[x]) and justify Equation 15’s derivation.
- line 262: phrase “sample from this unnormalized density” is ambiguous — specify which density is meant (e.g., dataset distribution).
- line 365: the claim that learning priors does not matter in 2AFC tasks is counterintuitive and requires explanation.
- Summary: methodology is interesting and innovative, but the results’ impact is limited given the strong assumptions and missing validations.
