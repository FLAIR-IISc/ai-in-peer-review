- Introduces a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception research.
- Proposes a flexible, computationally tractable observer model using parametric forms (e.g., Gaussian mixtures) for sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss.
- Presents a method to quantify practical identifiability by computing expected log-likelihood / KL divergence between models' response distributions.
- Uses MCMC sampling of the expected posterior landscape to identify well-constrained and degenerate parameters under a given experimental design.
- Case study 1 (time interval estimation): shows independently measuring motor noise is critical to recovering the prior; simply increasing trials in one condition is less effective.
- Case study 2 (speed perception): provides theoretical support for robust identifiability of the slow-speed prior.
- Strength: Addresses an important, timely problem of model non-identifiability in cognitive neuroscience.
- Strength: Technical construction of the generalized observer model balances flexibility and computational efficiency.
- Strength: Practical utility demonstrated via case studies that yield non-obvious experimental-design insights.
- Strength: Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively presented.
- Weakness: Novelty/positioning relative to prior work (notably Acerbi et al. 2012/2014) is insufficiently distinguished, risking the contribution feeling incremental.
- Suggestion: Add a direct, detailed comparison to related Bayesian observer modeling literature to clarify the unique contribution (a priori identifiability analysis vs. fitting data).
- Weakness: The term “identifiability” is used without fully clarifying it is conditional on the assumed parametric model class and does not resolve theory-neutral degeneracies (sensory vs. decision vs. motor noise).
- Suggestion: Explicitly acknowledge and discuss this limitation, clarifying the analysis addresses parameter recovery within a specific model class.
- Recommendation: Positive overall and recommended for acceptance contingent on revising to address the two main weaknesses (positioning vs. prior work and scope of identifiability).
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework for testing identifiability pre-experiment, sophisticated yet tractable observer model, and compelling case-study insights.
- Arguments against acceptance (to address in revision): insufficient articulation of novelty relative to prior work and the need for a more nuanced discussion of identifiability’s scope.
