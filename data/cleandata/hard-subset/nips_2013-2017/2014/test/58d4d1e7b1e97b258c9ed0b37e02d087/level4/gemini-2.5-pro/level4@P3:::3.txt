### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception research. The authors correctly identify a critical issue in the field: different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce empirically indistinguishable behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover an observer's internal representations from data. To address this, they propose a two-part solution. First, they develop a highly flexible yet computationally tractable class of Bayesian observer models. This model uses parametric forms (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians) to represent the sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss function, allowing it to capture a wide range of perceptual phenomena, including stimulus-dependent noise. Second, they introduce a method to quantify the practical identifiability between a "true" reference model and alternative models. This is achieved by calculating the expected log-likelihood over hypothetical datasets, which is formally related to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the models' response distributions. By sampling from this expected posterior landscape via MCMC, the framework can reveal which parameters are well-constrained by a given experimental design and which are prone to degeneracy. The utility of this framework is demonstrated through two case studies: (1) a time interval estimation task, where the framework is used to compare different experimental designs and reveal that independently measuring motor noise is key to recovering the prior; and (2) a speed perception task, where they provide theoretical support for the robust identifiability of the well-known "slow-speed prior."

### Strengths and Weaknesses

This paper addresses an important and often overlooked problem in computational cognitive science and neuroscience. The proposed framework represents a valuable contribution to the field, offering a principled method for improving experimental design before data collection begins.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a fundamental challenge for the Bayesian approach to cognition. Providing a systematic tool to diagnose and mitigate this issue is a significant contribution that could improve the rigor of research in this area.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The construction of the generalized observer model is technically sound and impressive. The authors have successfully balanced flexibility (approximating various distributions and noise characteristics) with computational efficiency (enabling fast calculation of expected loss and the overall response distribution). This technical achievement is the necessary foundation that makes the identifiability analysis feasible.
3.  **Practical Utility:** The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's practical value. The analysis of the time interval estimation task, for instance, provides concrete, non-obvious insights: simply increasing the number of trials in a single condition is less effective than measuring motor noise independently, and encouraging a MAP-like strategy could unexpectedly worsen the recovery of the prior. This shows the framework can serve as a powerful tool for designing more informative experiments.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the model components are described logically, and the results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, its contribution could be clarified and strengthened in two key areas.

1.  **Originality and Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** My primary concern is that the paper could do more to distinguish its novel contribution from highly related previous work. The modeling approach, in its spirit and components, is very similar to recent comprehensive Bayesian observer models, particularly those developed by Acerbi and colleagues, which are cited in the paper (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, *PLoS Comp Bio*; Acerbi et al., 2014, *PLoS Comp Bio*). While the current submission is not identical, the overlap is substantial enough to make the contribution feel incremental if not framed carefully. The key innovation here is not just the model itself, but its application to *a priori* identifiability analysis.

    *   **Suggestion:** I strongly recommend that the authors revise the manuscript to include a more direct and detailed comparison with this body of work. A focused discussion that explicitly articulates how this framework moves beyond fitting existing data (as in prior work) to proactively analyzing and optimizing experimental designs would substantially clarify the paper's unique contribution.

2.  **Scope and Interpretation of "Identifiability":** The framework provides a powerful method for assessing practical identifiability *given the model's structural assumptions*. However, the term "identifiability" can be interpreted in multiple ways. The analysis here does not resolve the more fundamental theoretical degeneracies that concern many experimental psychologists, such as cleanly separating the contributions of sensory noise, decision noise, and motor response noise without imposing strong parametric constraints. For example, the framework could not be used to definitively distinguish whether an observer's variability arises from the sensory stage or the response stage without assuming their functional forms.

    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation in the manuscript. It would be beneficial to clarify that their analysis addresses parameter recovery *within a specific, albeit flexible, model class*, rather than identifiability in a more general, theory-neutral sense. This would help manage reader expectations and more precisely frame the paper's important, but specific, contribution to the problem.

### Summary of Recommendation

Overall, I am positive about this paper and believe it addresses a topic of great interest to the NIPS community. The framework is elegant and the goal of improving experimental design through a priori analysis is highly laudable. My recommendation for acceptance is contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, primarily by better situating their work with respect to prior literature and by being more precise about the scope of the "identifiability" they are analyzing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and much-needed framework for systematically testing the identifiability of Bayesian perceptual models before running experiments.
*   The underlying observer model is technically sophisticated, combining flexibility with computational tractability.
*   The case studies provide compelling demonstrations of the framework's ability to generate non-obvious, practical insights for experimental design.

**Arguments against Acceptance (to be addressed in revision):**
*   The novelty of the contribution relative to prior work (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012; 2014) is not sufficiently articulated.
*   The discussion of "identifiability" could be more nuanced, acknowledging that the analysis is conditional on the assumed model structure and does not resolve all forms of degeneracy.
