

The paper introduces a comprehensive Bayesian framework for modeling perceptual tasks, providing mathematical formulations for sensory noise, likelihood functions, prior distributions, loss functions, and response distributions. A notable strength of this work is the model's elegant balance between flexibility and computational tractability, enabling it to accommodate diverse experimental scenarios—including stimulus-dependent noise—while maintaining efficient inference procedures. The authors emphasize the model's theoretical identifiability, which allows for systematic evaluation and distinction between parameter estimates.

I find this work compelling and would support its acceptance at NIPS. The framework offers a mathematically rigorous approach to formalizing perceptual estimation across a wide range of experimental paradigms, with clear practical applications for experimental design. However, I have concerns regarding the novelty of the contribution. The model's structure and methodology align closely with recent Bayesian modeling approaches in the literature, particularly those published in PLoS Computational Biology (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012; Acerbi et al., 2014). While the current submission is not identical to these prior works, it represents a relatively incremental advance rather than a significant conceptual leap.

I recommend that the authors strengthen the manuscript by adding a new section that thoroughly reviews recent Bayesian models of perception and explicitly articulates how their framework advances beyond existing approaches. This would better position the work within the current literature and clarify its unique contributions.

Additionally, I note a potential terminological concern regarding the paper's emphasis on "identifiability." While the model is mathematically identifiable under the specified assumptions, it does not resolve the fundamental identifiability challenge most perceptual scientists face: distinguishing between sensory noise, decision noise, and response noise. The authors should clarify this distinction in the revised manuscript to avoid potential misinterpretation of their framework's capabilities.

Strengths: Mathematically elegant framework for perceptual estimation; strong computational tractability; clear practical applications for experimental design.
Weaknesses: Contribution appears incremental compared to recent literature; terminological ambiguity regarding identifiability.
