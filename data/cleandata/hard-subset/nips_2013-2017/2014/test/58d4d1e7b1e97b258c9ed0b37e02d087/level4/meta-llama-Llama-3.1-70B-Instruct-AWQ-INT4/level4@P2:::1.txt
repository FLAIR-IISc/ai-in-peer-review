The authors propose a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. They introduce a novel class of Bayesian observer models, which are both flexible and efficient, and provide a method for efficiently exploring the identifiability of these models. The framework is applied to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The paper addresses a crucial issue in cognitive science and psychology, namely the identifiability of internal representations and processes in Bayesian models. This issue has been a major drawback of the Bayesian framework, hindering its widespread adoption. The authors' framework is a valuable contribution to the field, as it provides a systematic approach to testing the identifiability of Bayesian models.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the relationship between the proposed framework and previous work on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology. For example, the work of Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004) on model comparison and the distinguishability of models is relevant to the authors' framework. A more detailed comparison with these and other related approaches would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of its contributions.

Additionally, the authors make several assumptions throughout the paper that seem arbitrary at times. For instance, the definition of the measurement distribution pmeas(x|s) as a mixture of two Gaussians (Equation 3) is not fully justified. While the authors argue that this definition is robust and can express a large class of sensory models, it is unclear whether this is a sufficient justification for the assumption. A more detailed discussion of the rationale behind these assumptions and their implications for the framework would be helpful.
