### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the critical issue of model identifiability within the framework of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that the inverse problem—recovering an observer's internal representations (prior, likelihood, loss function) from behavioral data—is ill-posed, as multiple combinations of these components can produce identical behavior. To tackle this, they propose a systematic, *a priori* framework to test the identifiability of a model's parameters before an experiment is conducted. The framework consists of two main parts: (1) a flexible and computationally tractable parametric class of Bayesian observer models that can capture a wide range of sensory, perceptual, and decision-making processes; and (2) a method to quantify the distinguishability between a "true" reference model and alternative models by calculating the expected log-likelihood over hypothetical datasets, which is shown to be a function of the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between their respective response distributions.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies. In the first, a time interval estimation task, they compare different experimental designs and find that the recovery of the observer's prior is highly sensitive to degeneracies between sensory and motor noise parameters. Their analysis suggests that independently measuring motor noise is the most effective way to ensure reliable inference. In the second case study, they re-examine the well-known "slow-speed prior" in visual perception and conclude that the inference of this prior, along with key noise characteristics, is theoretically robust within their framework.

### **Relation to Prior Work**

The problem of model and parameter identifiability is a long-standing challenge in cognitive science and psychology. Early work, such as Anderson (1978), established the fundamental difficulty of distinguishing internal representations without constraining the processes that operate on them. More recently, the non-identifiability of Bayesian models has been a central criticism from those questioning their explanatory power (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). The latter explicitly uses the speed perception example to make this point. Furthermore, there is a substantial literature within computational psychology and statistics on methods for model comparison, parameter recovery, and assessing model distinguishability (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), including work previously published at NIPS (Myung et al., 2000, NIPS 13). The current paper aims to provide a practical tool specific to a class of Bayesian observer models, but its relationship to this broader body of work is not discussed.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The paper tackles a fundamental and widely acknowledged weakness of the Bayesian cognitive modeling approach. As these models become more complex and influential, tools for rigorously assessing their identifiability are increasingly necessary.
2.  **Novel A Priori Approach:** The primary contribution is the focus on *a priori* analysis to guide experimental design. Rather than simply fitting models to existing data and hoping for identifiability, this framework provides a principled way to evaluate whether a proposed experiment is even capable of disentangling the parameters of interest. This has the potential to save significant experimental resources and lead to more robust scientific conclusions.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed observer model is flexible, encompassing stimulus-dependent noise, non-Gaussian likelihoods, and a variety of loss functions. The derivation of the identifiability metric based on expected log-likelihood and KL-divergence is sound and provides an efficient way to explore the parameter space without costly simulations of full datasets.
4.  **Clarity and Demonstrative Case Studies:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The case studies are well-chosen and effectively illustrate how the framework can yield concrete, and sometimes non-obvious, recommendations for experimental design (e.g., the negative impact of a MAP-like loss function on prior recovery in the timing task).

### **Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Lack of Engagement with Related Literature:** A major weakness is the paper's failure to connect with the extensive prior literature on model comparison and identifiability. The work of Myung, Pitt, Navarro, and others has developed general-purpose statistical methods for exactly these problems (e.g., parameter recovery simulations, marginal likelihood calculations). It is unclear how the proposed KL-divergence-based method compares to these established techniques in terms of power, efficiency, or scope. Without this comparison, the originality and specific advantages of the proposed framework are difficult to evaluate.
2.  **Dependence on Strong Parametric Assumptions:** The framework's conclusions are contingent on a specific, though flexible, set of parametric forms for the sensory mapping, likelihood, prior, and loss function (e.g., a mixture of two Gaussians for the likelihood). The authors justify these choices based on computational convenience and expressiveness. However, it is not clear how robust the conclusions about a given experimental design would be if the true generative process for a subject's behavior lies outside this assumed model class. The paper would be much stronger if it included a discussion of the sensitivity of its results to these foundational assumptions or demonstrated their robustness.
3.  **Clarity of Justification:** The paper should do more to justify and explain its modeling choices. While the functional forms are claimed to be general, their specific selection (e.g., Equation 3 for `p_meas`) can feel arbitrary. A more thorough motivation for why these specific parametrizations are sufficient and appropriate for modeling the cognitive processes in question would strengthen the paper's foundation.

### **Minor Comments**

*   In Section 1, the authors state the degeneracy in the Bayesian framework is a "trivial mathematical fact". While this may be true for experts in the field, a brief sentence explaining *why* the product of the `φ_i(s)` functions cancels out would improve accessibility for the broader NIPS audience.
*   Line 73 of the original draft: The phrase "nor a framework to perform such study" should likely be "or a framework...".
*   In Figure 2, the caption refers to both a star and dashed lines as denoting the "true value". Please clarify the distinction between these two markers.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a well-developed and valuable framework for addressing the critical problem of identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models. The focus on *a priori* analysis for improving experimental design is a significant strength. However, the paper's contribution is currently weakened by its isolation from the rich existing literature on model comparison and by its reliance on strong parametric assumptions whose influence on the results is not fully analyzed. While the core idea is strong, the paper would need to situate its contribution within the broader context of existing methods and better justify its modeling choices to meet the standards for publication at NIPS.
