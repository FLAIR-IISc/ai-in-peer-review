The paper describes a Bayesian model of perceptual inference (mathematical equations for sensory noise, probability functions, prior probability distribution, loss function, response distribution). A significant strength of this model is that it combines both robustness and mathematical convenience. That is, the model can handle many different types of situations (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) and still be computationally convenient (e.g., be predictable). The paper emphasizes the fact that the model is identifiable (i.e., the two parameter estimates can be easily evaluated and differentiated).

I like this paper and would like to see it accepted at the NIPS conference. The proposed model provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual inference in many experiments. At the same time, I have some objections. I believe that the proposed model is in the same spirit and style as other models that have recently appeared in the literature (often in the journal PLOS Computational Biology), and thus the novel contribution of this paper is incremental. For example, the reader may consider the following papers:

Eserbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., and Vijaykumar, S. (2012). Internal representations calibrate temporal statistics and response motor-sensory interval times. PLOS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Eserbi, L., Vijaykumar, S., and Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origin of sub-fitness in human probabilistic inference. PLOS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

The current submission is not identical to these (or other) previous articles, but it is so similar (to these and other previous articles) that I think it is fair to say that the contribution of the current submission is "incremental."

I suggest that the authors revise the manuscript by including a new section in which they review the recently proposed Bayesian model of perception, and clearly explain why the proposed model in the current submission is a significant advance compared to previously published models.

One more comment. The submission emphasizes the fact that the model is mathematically identifiable. This is true, but the parameters are only identifiable due to the mathematical assumptions of the model. The term "identifiable" can be used in other ways. For example, the model cannot be used to firmly distinguish whether an experimental subject's errors are due to sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Consequently, it would be fair to say that the model does not help with the issue of identification that most perceptual scientists are concerned about. Please comment on this in the revised manuscript.
