This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, specifying the mathematical forms for sensory noise, the likelihood function, the prior probability distribution, the loss function, and the response distribution. A significant strength of this framework is its combination of robustness and mathematical convenience. The model is flexible enough to accommodate various experimental scenarios, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while remaining computationally tractable for inference. The authors also place considerable emphasis on the model's identifiability, noting that parameter estimates can be readily evaluated and distinguished.

The proposed model provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation and has the potential to be a valuable tool for analyzing data from a wide range of experiments. I am generally supportive of this work and would be inclined to see it accepted.

However, I have a significant reservation regarding the novelty of the contribution. The proposed model shares substantial conceptual and methodological overlap with other models that have recently appeared in the literature, particularly in journals like *PLoS Computational Biology*. The approach and style are highly reminiscent of prior work. For instance, readers might consider the following papers:

*   Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology, 8*(11), e1002771.
*   Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology, 10*(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other articles, the similarities are substantial enough to characterize the present contribution as incremental. To strengthen the paper and clarify its unique contribution, I strongly suggest that the authors add a new section to the manuscript. This section should review recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and explicitly articulate why the model proposed here represents a significant advance over previously published work.

A second point concerns the paper's emphasis on model identifiability. It is true that the model's parameters are identifiable within its specific mathematical assumptions. However, this formal property does not address the broader identifiability problem that is of primary concern to most perceptual scientists. Specifically, the model does not provide a mechanism to firmly distinguish whether an experimental subject's behavioral variability arises from sensory noise, suboptimal decision-making, or motor response noise. As the framework cannot dissociate these distinct sources of error, it could be argued that it does not resolve the fundamental identifiability challenge faced by the field. I recommend that the authors comment on this limitation in the revised manuscript to clarify the scope of their identifiability claims.

In summary, the paper introduces a well-defined and elegant framework for modeling perceptual estimation. Its primary weakness is the incremental nature of its contribution when viewed in the context of closely related existing literature. If the authors can revise the manuscript to better situate their model and clearly delineate its novel advantages, and also temper their claims about identifiability, I believe it would constitute a solid contribution to the conference.
