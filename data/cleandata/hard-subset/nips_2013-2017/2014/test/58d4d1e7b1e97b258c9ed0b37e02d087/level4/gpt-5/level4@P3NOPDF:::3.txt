Summary
The paper presents a Bayesian model of perceptual estimation that explicitly specifies sensory noise, likelihood, priors, loss functions, and response distributions. A major advantage is the combination of robustness and tractability: the framework accommodates realistic features such as stimulus-dependent noise while retaining analytically and computationally convenient inference. The manuscript also emphasizes parameter identifiability within the proposed formulation.

Strengths
- Clear, coherent Bayesian formulation that unifies several components (likelihood, priors, loss, response) within a single framework.
- Flexibility to handle a range of experimental scenarios, including stimulus-dependent and potentially non-Gaussian noise.
- Tractable inference that makes the model practical for data analysis and experimental design.
- Careful attention to model identifiability under the assumed generative structure.
- Potentially broad applicability across perceptual tasks and datasets.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Novelty appears incremental relative to recent literature on Bayesian models of perception and human probabilistic inference. The approach is in the same spirit as, and overlaps conceptually with, prior work (e.g., Acerbi, Wolpert & Vijayakumar, 2012; Acerbi, Vijayakumar & Wolpert, 2014, both in PLoS Computational Biology). While the current submission is not identical, the contribution should be positioned more clearly with respect to these and similar models, and the specific advances substantiated.
- The claim of “identifiability” needs to be more precisely framed. The model is structurally identifiable under its assumptions, but in practice it may not permit clear separation of sensory noise, decision noise, and response/execution noise from behavioral data alone. This is a central identifiability concern in perceptual science and should be discussed explicitly, with claims tempered accordingly.
- It is not fully clear how robust the model is to misspecification (e.g., if the true observer deviates from the assumed loss or noise family). Sensitivity analyses or simulations demonstrating parameter recovery and robustness would strengthen the paper.
- The manuscript would benefit from a more explicit statement of what is strictly novel: Is it a new likelihood/prior class, a unifying parameterization that subsumes common models, new identifiability results, or an algorithmic advance in inference and fitting? These contributions should be enumerated and empirically or theoretically demonstrated.

Requests for revision
- Add a dedicated related-work section that systematically compares the proposed framework to recent Bayesian models of perception and probabilistic inference, including but not limited to:
  - Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
  - Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.
  Clearly articulate how the present work differs and why it constitutes a meaningful advance (e.g., broader noise families, new identifiability proofs, improved computational efficiency, or better empirical fit).
- Clarify identifiability claims:
  - Distinguish structural identifiability (given the assumed generative model and infinite data) from practical identifiability (finite, noisy behavioral data).
  - Discuss the limits of discriminating sensory vs decision vs response noise with the proposed formulation. If applicable, include simulations showing when these sources can and cannot be disentangled, and what experimental manipulations would improve separation.
- Provide parameter-recovery and model-recovery analyses:
  - Show that parameters can be reliably recovered under realistic sample sizes and noise levels.
  - Test robustness to modest model misspecification (e.g., mismatched loss or noise distribution).
- If claiming computational advantages, quantify them: report runtime, convergence behavior, and scalability relative to representative baselines.
- Improve clarity on the loss function and its psychological interpretation. If alternative losses are supported, illustrate how inferences change across plausible choices.
- If feasible, include an empirical demonstration across multiple datasets or tasks to underscore generality, and report goodness-of-fit and predictive performance.

Minor comments
- Define “identifiable” precisely at first use and maintain consistent terminology (structural vs practical identifiability).
- Ensure notation is consistent across sections and equations; provide a concise notation table if space permits.
- Consider releasing code to facilitate adoption and reproducibility.

Recommendation
I am positive about the paper’s clarity, elegance, and practical tractability, and I would like to see it accepted. However, its novelty relative to closely related prior work currently appears incremental. I recommend acceptance conditional on a revision that (i) positions the contribution more explicitly with respect to recent Bayesian models of perception, (ii) refines and tempers the identifiability claims, and (iii) includes parameter/model-recovery evidence and robustness analyses. These changes would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact and clarify its specific advances.
