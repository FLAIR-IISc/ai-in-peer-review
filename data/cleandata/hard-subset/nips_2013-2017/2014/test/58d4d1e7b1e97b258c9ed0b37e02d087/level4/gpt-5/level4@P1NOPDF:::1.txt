This article proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data. The authors recap Bayesian decision theory, identify sources of non-identifiability, and describe constraints that render each component of the framework identifiable. They then impose specific constraints, examine how well parameter values can be distinguished in their models, and apply the approach to two domains where Bayesian decision theory has been used to explain behavior: time-interval estimation and motion perception.

Questions of identifiability in internal representations and processes have been central in cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) analyzed when representational formats can mimic one another, showing that—absent process constraints—representations that carve inputs into equivalent distinctions are empirically indistinguishable. In recent years, concerns about identifiability have been a major obstacle to the broader acceptance of Bayesian approaches (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012, which specifically discusses speed perception).

While the paper addresses an important theoretical issue, is clearly written, and generally proceeds in a sensible and effective way, I have two major reservations.

1) The paper largely overlooks a substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, making it hard to gauge the novelty of its contribution. The general comparison framework outlined here appears closely related to earlier work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), including ideas about model distinguishability and data informativeness. Positioning the current framework relative to this literature—highlighting similarities, differences, and advances—would strengthen the paper and clarify its added value.

2) Several key assumptions seem arbitrary or under-justified, and it is unclear how sensitive the framework’s conclusions are to them. For example, why is p_meas defined as in Equation 3? On what empirical or theoretical grounds is that particular form preferred over plausible alternatives? Likewise, is fixing the parameter values in Equation 12 really sufficient to define and capture all relevant characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? I appreciate that this class of parameterizations is flexible and can represent a broad set of sensory models, but flexibility alone does not establish adequacy. At minimum, the paper should more fully discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models for identifiability, and, where possible, assess the robustness of results to alternative, comparably reasonable constraints.
