

The manuscript addresses an important challenge in cognitive modeling: model identifiability. The writing is clear and well-structured. However, the work rests on a substantial set of assumptions that must be accepted, and this list is lengthy relative to the impact of the key results. Despite this, the methodology is innovative and compelling, placing the work above the threshold for acceptance at NIPS.

Regarding key results:  
In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is expected, and the inclusion of both noise sources in the model warrants clarification. The authors should indicate which condition differences are statistically reliable (I may have overlooked this in the box plots).  

In Section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior when the reference observer lacks this prior.  

Specific comments:  
- Line 106: The stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, though it is a continuous variable. This likely reflects standard experimental design practices where stimulus level serves as the independent variable; the authors should clarify this point.  
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. The success of the approach appears heavily dependent on this transform, which imposes strong constraints on inference. Consequently, the work effectively asserts that Bayesian models are identifiable only under strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.  
- Line 143: Do the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that Equation 3 yields a unimodal distribution? The fit could theoretically produce a bimodal distribution.  
- Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over a uniform one? What knowledge of the true stimulus distribution justifies a prior 50% wider than the true range?  
- Figure 1: The figure is confusing and fails to clarify the relationships it intends to illustrate. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and omitting p_est(s^*|x) (a derived inference) would improve clarity.  
- Line 252: The authors should specify conditions on N for which Equation 14 holds (e.g., when Stirling’s approximation for factorials is valid, and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]). By the time Equation 15 is introduced—after abandoning priors on θ and employing questionable approximations—it appears justified primarily by its intuitive appeal for assessing prediction similarity.  
- Line 262: "Sample from this unnormalized density"—which density is referenced (the dataset distribution or another)?  
- Line 365: The claim that prior learning does not matter in 2AFC requires justification.  

Overall, the approach is innovative and tackles an important problem, but the results are less than overwhelming.
