This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing mathematical formulations for sensory noise, the likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and resulting response distribution. A key strength of this model lies in its combination of robustness and computational tractability; it can accommodate various scenarios, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while remaining analytically convenient for inference. The authors rightly emphasize the model’s identifiability – the ability to readily evaluate and distinguish between parameter estimates.

I find this paper promising and would support its acceptance to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in a wide range of experimental contexts. However, I do have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. The approach feels very much aligned with, and in the same style as, recent work appearing in venues such as *PLoS Computational Biology*, representing an incremental advance rather than a substantial departure. For example, readers might find parallels with the following:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *8*(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, *10*(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other earlier works, the similarities are sufficient to warrant characterizing its contribution as incremental. 

I recommend the authors revise the manuscript by adding a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should clearly articulate how the model presented here represents a significant improvement over previously published approaches. 

Furthermore, the paper highlights the mathematical identifiability of the model, which is valid given its underlying assumptions. However, it’s important to acknowledge that this identifiability is a consequence of these mathematical choices and does not necessarily resolve the broader identifiability challenges faced by perceptual scientists. The model, for instance, does not allow for firm distinctions between errors arising from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. The authors should address this nuance in the revised manuscript, clarifying the specific scope of the model’s identifiability and its relationship to real-world perceptual inference.

In summary, the paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalising perceptual estimation. However, the novel contribution is incremental, given closely related work already existing in the literature.
