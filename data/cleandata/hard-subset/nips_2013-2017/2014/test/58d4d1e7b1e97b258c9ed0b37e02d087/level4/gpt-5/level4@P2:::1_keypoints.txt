- Paper addresses identifiability in Bayesian observer models and formalizes how priors, likelihoods, and losses can produce indistinguishable behavior.  
- Proposes an a priori framework using expected log-likelihood / expected KL to quantify practical identifiability across experimental designs.  
- Samples an approximate expected posterior over parameters to map identifiability for a flexible observer model.  
- Implements a flexible observer parameterization (mixture-based likelihoods/losses, parametric sensory mapping, lapse/reporting noise).  
- Demonstrates framework with two case studies: interval timing (fragile identifiability) and speed perception (robust prior recovery).  
- Strength: tackles an important, central problem for interpreting inferred internal representations from behavior.  
- Strength: provides a clear, principled identifiability criterion that scales with trials and stimulus distributions.  
- Strength: practical orientation enables direct comparison of experimental designs and reveals nonobvious trade-offs.  
- Strength: case studies effectively illustrate both fragile and robust identifiability scenarios.  
- Main critique: insufficient engagement and clear positioning relative to prior distinguishability/model-selection literature (Pitt et al., Navarro et al., Myung et al.).  
- Suggestion: relate and compare to optimal experimental design methods (Fisher information, D-/A-optimality) and clarify advantages of expected-KL.  
- Concern: several modeling choices appear ad hoc (two-Gaussian mixtures, inverted-Gaussian losses, lapse priors, noise forms) and need substantive justification.  
- Request: perform sensitivity/ablation analyses (vary mixture components, alternative noise shapes, lapse models, response noise) to test robustness of conclusions.  
- Ask to clarify impact of coupling vs. decoupling internal/external likelihoods and report effects for the timing task.  
- Scope issue: current work focuses on parameter identifiability/design analysis more than selecting among competing theoretical model families—reframe wording accordingly.  
- Methodological details missing: clarify asymmetric vs. symmetric KL choices, MCMC settings, convergence diagnostics, and scalability with parameter dimensionality and Nexp.  
- Quality assessment: generally sound and principled, but results may depend strongly on specific parameterizations—sensitivity checks would boost confidence.  
- Clarity: well written overall, but fix wording (e.g., “trivial mathematical fact”), notation/figure ambiguities (Figure 2 symbols), and minor typos (“nor/or”).  
- Originality: solid synthesis and efficient implementation for perceptual tasks, but conceptual overlap with existing distinguishability frameworks needs clearer differentiation.  
- Significance: high potential impact for experimental design and interpreting perceptual priors; speed-perception results align with empirical findings.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses timely question, provides implementable framework, gives actionable design guidance, and demonstrates instructive case studies.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited engagement with related literature, heavy reliance on specific modeling assumptions without thorough sensitivity analyses, and some presentation ambiguities.  
- Minor fixes: add requested citations (Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004; Myung et al. NIPS 2000), clarify degeneracy derivation, correct wording and figure captions.
