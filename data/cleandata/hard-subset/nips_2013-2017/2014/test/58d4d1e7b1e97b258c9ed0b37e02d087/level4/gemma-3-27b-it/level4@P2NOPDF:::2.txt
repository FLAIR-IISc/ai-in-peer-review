This manuscript addresses the critical issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and the presentation generally well-organized. However, the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions, and the breadth of these assumptions feels disproportionate to the impact of the presented results. Despite this, the methodology is both interesting and innovative, leading me to recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS, albeit above the threshold.

Regarding the key results: The finding in Section 4.1 demonstrating the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise wasn't particularly surprising, and I questioned the necessity of including both noise sources in the model during my initial reading. It would be beneficial to clearly indicate which observed differences were statistically reliable; the box plots were difficult to interpret in this regard. In Section 4.2, a crucial control would be to demonstrate that the model can recover the stimulus distribution even when the reference observer *doesn’t* possess a slow-speed prior.

Specific comments:

Line 106: The use of a discrete stimulus distribution, given that the underlying variable is continuous, warrants clarification. While I understand this likely reflects common experimental design constraints where stimulus levels are discrete independent variables, explicitly stating this would strengthen the manuscript.

Line 134: The authors correctly point out that the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical principles. However, the success of the modeling may be heavily reliant on this transform. This suggests that the work primarily demonstrates the identifiability of Bayesian models *given* strong, pre-defined constraints on representational transformations within the mind. This point should be discussed further.

Line 143: It's unclear whether the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that Equation 3 will fit to a unimodal distribution. The fitting process, in principle, could result in a bimodal distribution.

Line 158: The rationale for the observer’s prior favoring the lattice representation over a uniform representation needs further explanation. What prior knowledge does the observer have about the true stimulus distribution that justifies a prior 50% wider than the actual stimulus range?

Figure 1: The figure currently adds to, rather than clarifies, the complexity of the model. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and removing *p<sub>est</sub>(s<sup>*</sup>|x)* (as it appears to be a derived inference) would significantly improve clarity.

Line 252: Could the authors comment on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct? Specifically, when are Stirling’s approximation of the factorial and the approximation *E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]* valid? By the time Equation 15 is reached—particularly given the abandonment of priors on θ and the aforementioned approximations—the justification for the equation feels largely driven by its intuitive appeal regarding similarity assessment.

Line 262: The phrase "sample from this unnormalized density" is ambiguous. Which density is being referred to – the dataset distribution, or another?

Line 365: The impact of learning priors appears negligible in the 2AFC task. Overall, this is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less substantial than one might hope.
