The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, encompassing mathematical equations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A notable strength of this model lies in its ability to balance robustness and mathematical convenience, allowing it to handle diverse situations (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while maintaining computational tractability (e.g., tractable inference). The authors emphasize the model's identifiability, enabling straightforward evaluation and distinction between parameter estimates.

I find this paper compelling and believe it warrants acceptance to the NIPS conference. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in various experiments. However, I have some reservations regarding its novelty. The model's spirit and style are reminiscent of other recently published models (often in PLoS Computational Biology), leading me to conclude that its contribution is incremental. For instance, the reader may consider the following papers:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10(6), e1003661.

While the current submission is not identical to these or other earlier articles, its similarities lead me to suggest that its contribution is "incremental".

To strengthen the manuscript, I recommend adding a new section that reviews recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and clearly articulates why the proposed model represents a significant advance over previously published models.

Furthermore, I would like to comment on the model's identifiability. Although the model is mathematically identifiable due to its assumptions, this identifiability is limited. The model may not be able to distinguish between experimental subjects' errors caused by sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. This limitation raises concerns about the model's ability to address the identifiability issues that are most relevant to perceptual scientists. I encourage the authors to discuss this point in the revised manuscript.

Overall, the paper presents a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novel contribution is incremental, given the existence of closely related work in the literature.
