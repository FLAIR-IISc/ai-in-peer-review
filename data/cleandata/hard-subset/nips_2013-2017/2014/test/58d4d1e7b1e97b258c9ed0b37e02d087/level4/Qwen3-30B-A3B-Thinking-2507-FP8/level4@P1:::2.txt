

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors propose a systematic framework for testing whether key internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, which is essential for drawing valid conclusions from Bayesian observer models. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation that builds upon prior work in Bayesian decision theory and psychophysics.

The main contribution lies in the development of a practical method for a priori identifiability analysis, which allows researchers to evaluate how different experimental designs affect the recoverability of model components. The framework is applied to two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. In the first case, the authors demonstrate how experimental design choices (such as the number of conditions, trial counts, and assumptions about loss functions) impact the ability to recover priors. They show that imposing a narrow loss function (closer to MAP) can actually degrade reconstruction quality, which is a counterintuitive but important finding. In the second case, they validate the robustness of prior and noise recovery in speed perception, showing that the inferred slow-speed prior is generally reliable.

The methodology is innovative and well-motivated. The authors have developed a flexible Bayesian observer model that accommodates a wide range of sensory and perceptual phenomena while maintaining computational efficiency. The framework for identifiability analysis using expected log-likelihood and KL divergence is theoretically sound and practically implementable. This approach fills a significant gap in the literature, as previous work on model identifiability in perception has been largely ad hoc or limited to specific cases.

However, the paper's impact is somewhat constrained by its reliance on numerous assumptions about the structure of perceptual representations. The authors make strong assumptions about the form of the sensory mapping (Equation 2), the prior representation (Equation 4), and the loss function (Equation 6), which are critical for the framework's operation. While these assumptions are justified by psychophysical principles and prior work, they limit the generalizability of the framework. The authors should more explicitly acknowledge these constraints and discuss how the framework might be extended to accommodate different representational assumptions.

Regarding the case studies: In Section 4.1, the finding that sensory and motor noise parameters are confounded (Figure 2c) is not entirely surprising given the model's structure, but the authors could better highlight the practical implications for experimental design. It would be helpful to explicitly state which experimental design modifications (e.g., MTR) most effectively resolve this confounding.

For Section 4.2, the authors should include a validation that their framework can correctly identify the absence of a slow-speed prior (e.g., by testing with a flat prior as a control). This would strengthen the claim that the framework reliably recovers internal representations.

Some specific technical points for clarification:
- Line 106: The use of discrete stimulus distributions (instead of continuous) is reasonable for computational tractability, but the authors should clarify that this is a standard approximation in psychophysical experiments where stimuli are presented at discrete levels.
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is crucial to the framework's success, as it enforces a specific representational structure. The authors should acknowledge that their identifiability results are conditional on this particular transformation.
- Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and entropy maximization do ensure unimodality of the likelihood, but the authors could briefly explain this to prevent confusion.
- Line 158: The choice of prior width (50% wider than stimulus range) is reasonable for capturing potential prior distributions, but the authors should clarify the theoretical justification for this specific choice.
- Figure 1: The figure could be simplified by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x) to reduce confusion.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a systematic approach to a critical problem in Bayesian modeling of perception. While the assumptions underlying the framework limit its immediate applicability to all perceptual tasks, the methodology itself is sound and could be extended to broader contexts. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility for experimental design optimization and validation.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important methodological gap. The technical approach is innovative and well-executed, and the case studies provide meaningful validation. The work is suitable for NIPS, as it advances the methodological toolkit for cognitive science while maintaining rigorous theoretical grounding. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarifications noted above.
