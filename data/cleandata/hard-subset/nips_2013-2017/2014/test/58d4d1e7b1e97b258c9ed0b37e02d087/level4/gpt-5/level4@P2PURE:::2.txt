The manuscript addresses the important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. It is well written and generally clear. However, the approach relies on a long sequence of assumptions that readers must accept and interpret; this list feels extensive relative to the impact of the main results. Because the methodology is interesting and innovative, I judge the work to be above the acceptance threshold for NIPS.

Regarding the key results: In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising (I was already questioning the inclusion of both sources in the model). Please indicate which condition differences are statistically reliable; the box plots alone are hard to interpret. In Section 4.2, isn’t it essential to show that if the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, the model would also recover that fact?

Specific comments:
- line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is in fact a continuous variable? I assume this mirrors common experimental designs in which stimulus levels are the independent variables, but the authors should clarify.
- line 134: As noted, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. It seems the success of the approach may largely stem from this transform: it imposes a strong constraint on inference. In that sense, the work shows that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.
- line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximization of differential entropy ensure that the fitted distribution in Eq. 3 is unimodal? In principle, the fit could be bimodal.
- line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over a uniform one? What does the observer know about the true stimulus distribution that would justify a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?
- Figure 1: After careful inspection, the figure is more confusing than illuminating. It might be clearer to integrate Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and to remove p_est(s^*|x) since it is a derived quantity.
- line 252: Please comment on the conditions on N under which Equation 14 is approximately correct—that is, when Stirling’s approximation to the factorial holds—and on the circumstances in which E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]. By the time Equation 15 is introduced—after dropping priors on \theta and making several questionable approximations—it seems justified primarily because it yields an intuitive rule for assessing similarity of predictions.
- line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density is meant here—the dataset distribution?
- line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC?

Innovative approach, important problem, but the results are less than overwhelming.
