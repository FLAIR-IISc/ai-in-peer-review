The manuscript addresses the crucial problem of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a growing concern as these models are increasingly used to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability, with potential applications for improved experimental design. The work is well-written and generally clear, though relies on a substantial number of assumptions. While the results presented are not groundbreaking, the methodology itself is interesting and innovative, leading me to judge the paper as above the acceptance threshold for NIPS.

The core contribution lies in providing a method to assess how well model parameters can be recovered given a specific experimental design. In the first case study (Section 4.1), the observed interchangeability of sensory and motor noise, while perhaps unsurprising, highlights the challenges in disentangling these sources of variability. Greater clarity regarding which differences between conditions were statistically reliable would strengthen this analysis. The second case study (Section 4.2) demonstrates the robustness of prior inference in speed perception given a slow-speed prior, but would benefit from demonstrating the model’s ability to correctly identify the *absence* of such a prior when it is not present. This would further validate the framework’s utility.

Specific comments:

Line 106: The use of a discrete stimulus distribution, despite the underlying continuous nature of the variable, should be clarified. While common in experimental design, explicitly acknowledging this approximation would be helpful.

Line 134: The success of the logarithmic stimulus transform (Equation 2), motivated by psychophysical laws, is a key aspect of the work. It’s important to emphasize that the findings may be contingent upon this strong assumption about representational transformations, framing the results as demonstrating identifiability *given* these constraints.

Line 143: The authors should elaborate on whether the constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy definitively ensure that Equation 3 yields a unimodal distribution, or if bimodal solutions are theoretically possible.

Line 158: Justification for the choice of a lattice prior (Equation 4), rather than a uniform prior, is needed. What information does the observer possess about the stimulus distribution that justifies a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?

Figure 1: The figure could be streamlined for improved clarity. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and removing *p<sub>est</sub>(s*|x)*, which is a derived quantity, would simplify the diagram.

Line 252: A discussion of the conditions under which the approximations used to derive Equation 14 (Stirling's approximation, *E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]*) are valid would strengthen the analysis. The reliance on these approximations raises questions about the robustness of Equation 15, which appears largely justified by its intuitive appeal.

Line 262: Clarification is needed regarding which density is being sampled from in the phrase "sample from this unnormalized density."

Line 365: The assertion that prior learning does not matter in the 2AFC task warrants further explanation.

Arguments for acceptance: The manuscript presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for addressing a significant problem in computational cognitive science. The methodology is innovative and could be a valuable tool for researchers developing and evaluating Bayesian models of perception.

Arguments against acceptance: The results are somewhat limited in their impact. The assumptions underlying the framework are numerous and strong, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings. The presentation could be improved through clearer explanations of certain technical details and a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach.
