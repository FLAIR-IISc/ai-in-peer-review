The authors propose a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, which is a crucial issue in the field of cognitive science and psychology. The paper is well-written and effectively addresses a significant problem, but it has some limitations. 

The authors provide a clear summary of the Bayesian Decision Theory framework and identify areas of non-identifiability. They then outline the types of constraints that can be used to make each term in the Bayesian framework identifiable. The proposed framework is applied to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. 

However, the paper largely ignores the previous literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. The general framework for model comparison proposed by the authors is not significantly different from some of this previous work. For example, Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004) proposed similar frameworks for model comparison. 

Furthermore, the authors make several assumptions that seem arbitrary at times, and it is unclear how much of their framework depends on them. For instance, the definition of pmeas(x|s) by equation 3 is not adequately justified. Although the authors claim that this definition can express a large class of sensory models, it is not clear that this is a sufficient justification for the assumption. The article should discuss and justify the assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian models so that they are identifiable.

Additionally, some minor issues need to be addressed. For example, line 53 states that "It is a trivial mathematical fact…", but it might be more helpful to explain why it is true rather than declaring it trivial. In line 73, the second "nor" should be an "or". In Figure 2, the star and dashed lines are not clearly explained; the caption suggests that they both denote the true value.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an issue of great importance and is well-written, it lacks connections to previous related work and requires further justification of its assumptions. 

Arguments pro acceptance:
- The paper addresses a crucial issue in the field of cognitive science and psychology.
- The proposed framework is well-written and effectively applied to two case studies.
- The paper highlights the importance of considering identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.

Arguments con acceptance:
- The paper ignores the previous literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology.
- The authors make several assumptions that seem arbitrary and require further justification.
- The paper lacks clear explanations for some of its mathematical derivations and figures.
