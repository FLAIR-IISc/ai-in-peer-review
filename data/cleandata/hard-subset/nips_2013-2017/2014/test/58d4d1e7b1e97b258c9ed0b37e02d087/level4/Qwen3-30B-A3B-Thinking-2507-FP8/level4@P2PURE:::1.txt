

In this article, the authors propose a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data. They begin by summarizing the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identifying areas of non-identifiability, and outlining constraint types that can render each term in the framework identifiable. The authors then impose assumptions to constrain each term, examine the differentiability of parameter values in their model, and apply the technique to two studies employing Bayesian decision theory to explain behavioral responses: time interval estimation and motion perception.

Issues of identifiability for internal representations and processes have been central to cognitive science and psychology for decades. For example, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that representational formats can mimic each other and are indistinguishable without constraints on processes (specifically, when representations make equivalent distinctions of their inputs).

In recent years, this identifiability problem has been a major drawback hindering the widespread adoption of the Bayesian framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012 – the latter directly addresses speed perception as an example).

Although the article addresses a topic of significant theoretical importance, is well written, and presents its arguments reasonably and effectively, I have two major reservations. First, it overlooks substantial prior literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology, hindering proper evaluation of its contribution. The authors' general framework for model comparison bears considerable resemblance to existing work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, the authors make assumptions that sometimes appear arbitrary, and it is unclear how much their framework depends on them. For instance, why should $p_{meas}$ be defined by Equation 3? Is defining the parameters in Equation 12 sufficient to fully capture all characteristics of a cognitive or perceptual process? While I acknowledge the framework's robustness and ability to express a broad class of sensory models, this does not constitute adequate justification for the assumptions. The article should explicitly discuss and justify the constraints used to achieve identifiability in Bayesian models.
