- Introduces a computational framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models, mapping an identifiability landscape via an approximate expected posterior based on KL divergence.  
- Significant and original: tackles a crucial identifiability problem and offers a tool to inform experimental design before data collection.  
- Methodological innovation: proposes a flexible, tractable parametric class of observer models and uses an expected log-likelihood (approximated via KL) to explore identifiability.  
- Clear presentation and motivation.  
- Main weakness: framework depends on many strong parametric assumptions; sensitivity of conclusions to these choices is not thoroughly explored.  
- Reliance on constrained model space (sensory map form, mixture-of-Gaussians priors/likelihoods/loss) may limit generality; should discuss sensitivity and reframe claims accordingly.  
- Time-interval case study: observed sensory vs. motor noise trade-off is unsurprising given Gaussian assumptions; need clearer statement of which experimental design differences are reliably distinguishable.  
- Speed perception case study: recovering a slow-speed prior is compelling but requires a control showing correct inference of a uniform prior (FLA condition should be moved/discussed in main text).  
- Q1: Justify modeling a continuous stimulus with a discrete stimulus distribution.  
- Q2: Does the mixture-of-two-Gaussians parametrization (constrained by moments and max-entropy) guarantee unimodality or could it yield bimodality?  
- Q3: Motivate the lattice-of-Gaussians prior representation and the choice to set observer prior range 50% wider than true stimulus range.  
- Q4: Improve Figure 1 clarity—integrate panels, clarify workflow, and consider removing derived p_est(s*|x).  
- Q5: Clarify approximations behind deriving expected log-likelihood as KL; state conditions under which they hold.  
- Q6: Specify exactly which density is sampled via MCMC (is it the posterior over θ conditioned on θ*?).  
- Q7: Justify the assumption that observers do not learn the experimental stimulus distribution in 2AFC tasks, given known prior learning in estimation tasks.  
- Overall recommendation: innovative, useful contribution above acceptance threshold but should address assumption sensitivity and add control/clarity.
