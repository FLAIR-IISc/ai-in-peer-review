### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the critical problem of model identifiability within the context of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly point out that since multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, recovering the observer's true "internal representations" from data is an ill-posed problem. The main contribution is a novel computational framework designed to test, *a priori*, the identifiability of a given model and experimental design. This is achieved by first defining a flexible, yet computationally tractable, parametric class of Bayesian observer models. The framework then uses an efficient sampling method based on the Kullback-Leibler divergence between model predictions to map out the "identifiability landscape," revealing which parameters are well-constrained by a proposed experiment and which are prone to degeneracy. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one in time interval estimation, where the framework is used to compare different experimental designs, and one in speed perception, where it is used to validate the robustness of previous findings regarding a slow-speed prior.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of model identifiability is fundamental to computational modeling in cognitive science and neuroscience, yet it is often overlooked. This paper tackles the issue head-on with a novel, proactive approach. Providing a tool that allows researchers to assess the theoretical limits of their experimental designs *before* collecting data is a significant methodological contribution that could improve the rigor of research in the field.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The authors develop a comprehensive and flexible modeling class that captures many key components of perceptual tasks (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise, non-Gaussian likelihoods, flexible priors, and non-quadratic loss functions). The method for assessing identifiability via the expected log-likelihood is clever and computationally efficient.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the core degeneracy problem and how their framework aims to solve it.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Dependence on Strong Modeling Assumptions:** The framework's conclusions are contingent on a large number of specific parametric assumptions. These include the form of the sensory mapping (Eq. 2), the representation of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions as mixtures of Gaussians (Eqs. 3, 4, 6), and the use of maximum entropy principles to fix parameters. While this makes the problem tractable, it raises the question of how the identifiability results would generalize to different model classes. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the extent to which the conclusions depend on these specific choices versus the general structure of the experimental design. For example, the success of the framework may hinge heavily on the psychophysically-motivated log-transform (Eq. 2), which imposes a very strong constraint.

2.  **Limited Insight from Case Studies:** While the case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's mechanics, the main findings are not entirely surprising. The discovery of a trade-off between sensory and motor noise (Sec 4.1) is a well-known issue in modeling. The more insightful result—that a narrow loss function can harm prior recovery—is interesting, but its generality is unclear. The second case study largely confirms previous findings, which is a useful validation but offers less new scientific insight.

3.  **Justification of the Identifiability Metric:** The derivation of the approximate expected posterior density (Eq. 13) involves several approximations and a departure from a fully Bayesian treatment. The justification for using the KL-divergence as a proxy for the expected log-likelihood is intuitive but feels somewhat heuristic. A more detailed theoretical justification in the main text or supplementary material, particularly regarding the conditions under which the approximations hold, would bolster the technical soundness of the approach.

**Specific Comments and Questions**

*   **Section 4.1 (Time Perception):** The finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not surprising. When presenting the box plots in Figure 2, it would be helpful to indicate which differences between conditions (e.g., BSL vs. MTR) are statistically meaningful according to the analysis, as this is not immediately obvious from the plots alone.

*   **Section 4.2 (Speed Perception):** The paper demonstrates that the model can recover a slow-speed prior when the reference observer has one. A crucial control would be to show that if the reference observer had a *flat* prior, the framework would correctly infer this and not erroneously find a slow-speed prior. The text mentions this analysis is in the supplementary material (FLA condition), which is good, but given its importance for validating the method, a brief summary of this result in the main text would be valuable.

*   **Line 106:** The model assumes stimuli are drawn from a discrete distribution, whereas the variable (e.g., time) is continuous. While this reflects typical experimental designs where discrete stimulus levels are chosen, it would be helpful to explicitly clarify this rationale.

*   **Line 143:** The paper states that the shape of the measurement distribution `p_meas(x|s)` is determined by fixing the first four moments and maximizing differential entropy, under the assumption of unimodality. Is it guaranteed that this procedure for a two-Gaussian mixture will always yield a unimodal distribution, or is unimodality an additional constraint that must be actively checked or enforced?

*   **Line 158:** Could the authors provide more justification for the observer's prior being a mixture of regularly spaced Gaussians (Eq. 4)? Furthermore, the assumption that the observer's prior is defined over a range "50% wider than the true stimulus range" seems to imply the observer has extra-task knowledge. What is the motivation for this choice?

*   **Figure 1:** The figure is somewhat difficult to parse. To improve clarity, consider integrating the elements of Figure 1b more directly into the graphical model of Figure 1a. The `p_est(s*|x)` node could potentially be removed, as it represents an intermediate computation rather than a core variable in the generative model.

*   **Line 262:** The text states, "we sample from Eq. 13 via MCMC." Please clarify which density is being sampled from. Is it `E(θ|θ*)` treated as an unnormalized density over `θ`?

*   **Line 365:** The authors state they assume no learning of the experimental distribution of speeds in the 2AFC task. Why is learning of the experimental prior less of a concern in a 2AFC paradigm compared to an estimation task?

**Conclusion**

Despite the weaknesses noted, this paper presents an interesting and innovative methodology for addressing the important problem of model identifiability. The framework is a valuable conceptual and practical contribution. I believe the work is above the threshold for acceptance at NIPS, and I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the points raised above to further strengthen the manuscript.
