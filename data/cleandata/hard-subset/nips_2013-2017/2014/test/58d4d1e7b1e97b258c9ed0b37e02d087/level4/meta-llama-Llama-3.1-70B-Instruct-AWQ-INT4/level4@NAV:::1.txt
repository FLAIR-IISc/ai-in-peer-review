The authors present a framework for comparing Bayesian models using behavioral data, building upon the Bayesian Decision Theory framework. They identify areas of non-identifiability, outline applicable constraints, and make assumptions to render each term identifiable. The framework is then applied to two studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issue of identifiability of internal representations and processes has long been a concern in cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that representational formats can be indistinguishable without constraints on processes. Recently, this issue has hindered the widespread adoption of the Bayesian framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

Although the article addresses a theoretically significant issue and is well-written, two major concerns arise. Firstly, the authors overlook the substantial existing literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, making it challenging to assess the contribution of this work. The proposed framework bears resemblance to previous studies (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Secondly, the authors' assumptions appear arbitrary at times, and it is unclear how these assumptions impact the framework. For example, the definition of $p_{meas}$ by equation 3 seems unjustified. Furthermore, it is unclear whether defining the parameters in Equation 12 sufficiently captures the characteristics of cognitive or perceptual processes. While the framework can express a wide range of sensory models, this alone may not be sufficient justification for the assumptions made.
