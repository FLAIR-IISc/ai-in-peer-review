## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, detailing the mathematical formulations for sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions. A key strength of the model lies in its balance of robustness and computational tractability; it accommodates various scenarios – including stimulus-dependent noise – while remaining amenable to efficient inference. The authors rightly emphasize the model’s identifiability, demonstrating the ability to readily evaluate and distinguish between parameter estimates.

Overall, I find this paper promising and believe it has the potential to be a valuable contribution to NIPS. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, applicable to a broad range of experimental contexts. However, I do have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. 

While well-executed, the model feels conceptually similar to a growing body of recent work in Bayesian models of perception, particularly publications appearing in *PLoS Computational Biology*. This similarity leads me to believe the contribution, as presented, is incremental rather than groundbreaking. Specifically, the work bears notable resemblance to Acerbi et al. (2012) ["Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 8 (11), e1002771"] and Acerbi et al. (2014) ["On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology*, 10 (6), e1003661"].  Although not identical, the conceptual overlap is significant enough to warrant further discussion.

I recommend the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should clearly articulate how the proposed model advances the state-of-the-art and distinguishes itself from existing approaches. A detailed comparison, highlighting the specific advantages and limitations of the present work, would significantly strengthen the paper.

My second comment concerns the emphasis on model identifiability. While mathematically true – the parameters *are* identifiable under the model’s assumptions – this identifiability is limited to the parameters *within the model itself*. The model doesn’t address the broader, long-standing challenge in perceptual science of disentangling the sources of error. Specifically, the model, even with identified parameters, doesn’t allow a definitive determination of whether observed errors originate from sensory noise, decision-making processes, or response execution. Therefore, while the model is internally identifiable, it doesn’t necessarily resolve the broader “identifiability problem” faced by researchers attempting to understand the underlying mechanisms of perceptual errors. 

The authors should acknowledge this distinction in the revised manuscript. A discussion clarifying the scope of the model’s identifiability, and its limitations in resolving the source of perceptual variability, would be beneficial.

In summary, this is a mathematically sound and well-presented paper describing an elegant framework for perceptual estimation. However, to increase its impact, the authors should explicitly position their work within the existing literature, and provide a more nuanced discussion of the model’s identifiability properties. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the contribution and increase the likelihood of acceptance.
