This manuscript introduces a framework for conducting model comparisons among Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. To achieve this, the authors summarize Bayesian Decision Theory, identify specific sources of non-identifiability, and detail the kinds of constraints necessary to render each component of the Bayesian framework identifiable. Subsequently, they introduce specific assumptions to constrain the framework's terms, investigate the differentiability of the model's parameters, and demonstrate their approach on two case studies involving Bayesian decision theory: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The challenge of identifying internal representations and processes has been a central and long-standing issue in cognitive science and psychology. For instance, Anderson (1978) demonstrated that different representational formats can be indistinguishable if they make equivalent distinctions among inputs, highlighting that they can only be differentiated through constraints on their associated processes. More recently, this identifiability problem has been cited as a significant limitation of the Bayesian framework, impeding its broader acceptance within the field (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012, which specifically uses speed perception as an example).

While the manuscript addresses a topic of significant theoretical importance and is well-articulated, I have two primary concerns. First, the paper overlooks a substantial body of existing literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology. This omission makes it challenging to properly situate and assess the novelty of the proposed contribution. The general model comparison framework presented here shares considerable overlap with earlier work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004). Second, several key assumptions appear to be inadequately justified, and it is unclear to what extent the framework's validity hinges on these specific choices. For instance, the rationale for defining $p_{meas}$ according to Equation 3 is not provided. Similarly, it is questionable whether specifying the parameter values in Equation 12 is sufficient to fully characterize a cognitive or perceptual process. While the authors note the robustness and expressive power of their formulation for a wide class of sensory models, this alone does not serve as a complete justification. The manuscript would be substantially strengthened by a thorough discussion and defense of the assumptions made to ensure the identifiability of the Bayesian models.
