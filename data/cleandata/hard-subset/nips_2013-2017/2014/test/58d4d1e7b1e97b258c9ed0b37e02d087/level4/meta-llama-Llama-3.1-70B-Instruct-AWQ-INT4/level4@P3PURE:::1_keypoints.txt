- Paper proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models on behavioral data, identifying non-identifiability and outlining constraints to make terms identifiable, then applies the approach to interval estimation and motion perception studies.
- Addresses an important, long-standing issue in cognitive science and psychology (identifiability of internal representations/processes).
- Well written and generally reasonable in its treatment of the problem.
- Major concern: the paper largely ignores extensive prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science, making its contribution hard to evaluate and possibly not novel (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang; Navarro, Pitt & Myung).
- Major concern: several assumptions used to constrain the Bayesian framework appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified (e.g., why define p_meas as in Eq.3; are parameters in Eq.12 sufficient to capture cognitive/perceptual processes?).
- The paper should more explicitly discuss and justify the assumptions that make the models identifiable and clarify how dependent results are on those assumptions.
- Minor comment: Line 53 — avoid saying “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” without explanation, as it may not be trivial for all readers.
- Minor comment: Line 73 — likely typo: second “nor” should be “or.”
- Minor comment: Figure 2 — caption unclear about what the star and dashed lines denote; they seem to both indicate the true value.
- Overall: important and well-written work but missing links to prior related work and clearer justification of key assumptions.
