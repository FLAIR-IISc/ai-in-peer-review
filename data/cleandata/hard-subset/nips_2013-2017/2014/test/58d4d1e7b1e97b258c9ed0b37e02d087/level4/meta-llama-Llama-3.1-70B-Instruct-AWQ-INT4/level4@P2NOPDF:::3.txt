The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, encompassing mathematical equations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A notable strength of this model lies in its ability to balance robustness with mathematical convenience, allowing it to accommodate various situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining tractable inference.

The paper highlights the model's identifiability, enabling straightforward evaluation and distinction of parameter estimates. I appreciate the paper's contribution to providing a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in numerous experiments.

However, I have some reservations regarding the paper's novelty. The proposed model shares a similar spirit and style with other recently published models, such as those in PLoS Computational Biology. For instance, the reader may consider the following papers: Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014). While the current submission is not identical to these earlier articles, it bears sufficient similarities to be considered an incremental contribution.

To strengthen the manuscript, I suggest the authors include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and explicitly stating why their model represents a significant advance over previously published models. This addition would help clarify the paper's novelty and contribution to the field.

Furthermore, I would like the authors to comment on the concept of identifiability in the context of perceptual science. Although the model is mathematically identifiable, this identifiability is contingent upon the model's assumptions. Moreover, the model may not effectively distinguish between sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise as sources of experimental errors. This limitation raises questions about the model's utility in addressing the identifiability concerns that are most relevant to perceptual scientists.

Overall, the paper provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novel contribution is incremental, building upon closely related work already existing in the literature.
