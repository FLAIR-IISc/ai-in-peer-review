This paper addresses the critical and often-overlooked problem of model identifiability within the popular framework of Bayesian observer models in cognitive science and perception. The authors correctly point out that since multiple combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss function can produce empirically indistinguishable behavior, the ability to uniquely "invert" these models to recover a subject's internal representations is questionable. The core contribution is a novel computational framework to systematically test, a priori, the identifiability of a large class of Bayesian observer models. This framework is then used to evaluate and compare different experimental designs in two case studies: time interval estimation and visual speed perception.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of non-identifiability is a fundamental challenge for Bayesian cognitive modeling. While acknowledged in the literature (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012), there has been a lack of systematic, quantitative tools to address it *before* an experiment is run. This paper provides a novel and principled framework to do just that, offering a significant contribution that could improve the rigor of research in this area.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The authors develop a flexible and computationally tractable parametric observer model that covers sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages. The use of mixture-of-Gaussians representations for the various components (likelihood, prior, loss) is a powerful choice that allows for analytical tractability while maintaining representational flexibility. The proposed identifiability metric, based on the KL divergence between response distributions, is intuitive and well-motivated.

3.  **Clarity and Practical Utility:** The paper is well-written and the proposed framework is clearly explained. The two case studies are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the practical utility of the method. For instance, the analysis in Section 4.1 provides concrete, actionable insights into how to design a better time-estimation experiment (e.g., by independently measuring motor noise), moving beyond simple intuition.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Points for improvement):**

1.  **Dependence on a Complex Model:** The framework's power is derived from a detailed and highly structured observer model. This is also a potential weakness, as the conclusions about identifiability are contingent on the many assumptions embedded in this model (e.g., the specific parametric form of the sensory map `f(s)`, the mixture-of-Gaussians representations, the separation of sensory and motor noise). The work is less a general statement about Bayesian models and more a statement about identifiability *within this specific, albeit flexible, model class*. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion of how sensitive the conclusions might be to these structural assumptions.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the identifiability metric in Section 3 could be more transparent. The step from the expected log-likelihood (Eq. 11) to the KL divergence (Eq. 12) involves approximations that are not fully detailed in the main text. The authors should clarify the conditions under which these approximations are valid to bolster confidence in the proposed metric, `E(θ|θ*)`.

3.  **Impact of Key Results:** While the methodology is strong, some of the specific findings are less surprising. The degeneracy between sensory and motor noise (Fig 2c) is a well-known issue in motor control and perception modeling. The main value here is that the framework correctly identifies this known problem. Conversely, the finding that a narrow loss function worsens prior recovery is interesting and non-obvious, and could be highlighted more. For the second case study, a critical control is to show that if the reference observer had a flat prior, the framework would correctly recover this. The authors mention this "FLA" condition as a sanity check, but these results should be more prominently featured in the main text to strengthen the claims about the robustness of the slow-speed prior inference.

**Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **L106 (Stimulus Distribution):** The assumption of a discrete stimulus distribution could be briefly justified for readers unfamiliar with psychophysical designs. A simple sentence explaining that this reflects the common experimental practice of using a fixed set of stimulus levels would suffice.

*   **L134 (Sensory Map):** The sensory map `f(s)` (Eq. 2) is a very strong and well-motivated constraint based on established psychophysical laws. It's plausible that this constraint is doing a lot of the work in making the model identifiable. The authors should acknowledge this, framing the work's conclusion more as "Bayesian models can be identifiable given strong, psychologically-plausible constraints on the nature of internal representations."

*   **L143 (Unimodality):** The text states that the parameters of the Gaussian mixture for the likelihood (Eq. 3) are constrained to ensure unimodality. Could the authors clarify how this constraint is enforced? In principle, a mixture of two Gaussians can easily be bimodal.

*   **Figure 1:** This figure is quite dense and could be clarified. Integrating Figure 1b more directly into the flow of Figure 1a might help. The distinction between the stages and the distributions can be made more visually intuitive.

*   **L262 ("unnormalized density"):** It should be explicitly stated that the MCMC samples from the approximate expected posterior density `E(θ|θ*)` defined in Eq. 13.

**Summary:**

This is a strong paper that introduces an innovative and valuable methodology to tackle the crucial problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive science. The framework is technically sound, clearly presented, and its utility is well-demonstrated through relevant case studies. While the conclusions are conditional on the specific (though flexible) model class proposed, and some of the results confirm existing intuitions, the overall contribution is significant and original. I believe the work is well above the threshold for acceptance at NIPS and will be of considerable interest to computational modelers in neuroscience and psychology.
