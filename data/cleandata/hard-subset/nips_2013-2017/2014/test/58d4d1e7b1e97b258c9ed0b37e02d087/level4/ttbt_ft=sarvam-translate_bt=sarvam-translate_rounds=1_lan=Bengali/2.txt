This manuscript discusses the important problem of cognitive modeling in model identification. The manuscript is well-written and quite clear. However, the work is based on some long-term assumptions that must be accepted and understood. This list is quite lengthy compared to the impact of the main results presented. Since the method is interesting and innovative, I believe it is a suitable work for presentation at NIPS.

The main results are: In section 4.1, the variability of sensory and motor noise was surprisingly small (I questioned the inclusion of both in the model while reading). It would be helpful to mention which condition differences were reliable. (Perhaps I am too foolish to read box plots.) In section 4.2, isn't it important to show that if the observed reference is not slow, the model can recover this information?

Specific comments:
Line 106: Why is the stimulus taken from a discrete distribution, when it is actually a continuous variable? I think this is due to the general design of the experiment, where the stimulus level is the independent variable. However, the authors may want to clarify this point.
Line 134: As the authors mentioned, the log-stimulus transform (equation 2) is inspired by psychophysical laws. It seems that the success of their work is mainly due to the inclusion of this transform. That is, the transform imposes quite strong constraints on the assumptions, and therefore, this work actually says that Bayesian models are identifiable if there are strong assumptions about the representative transformation in the mind.
Line 143: Does the limitation of equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy ensure that equation 3 is suitable for a unimodal distribution? It seems that this fit can mainly give a bimodal distribution.
Line 158: Why would the observer's prior knowledge favor a lattice representation over a uniform representation? What does the user know about the actual stimulus distribution that allows them to have a prior knowledge that is 50% more extensive than the actual range?
Figure 1: Even after looking at the figure, I feel more confused than enlightened by it. It seems that it would have been clearer if figure 1b was combined with figure 1a and p_est(s^*|x) was removed from the figure, as it is an derived assumption.
Line 252: Can the authors comment on the conditions of N that make equation 14 approximately correct, that is, when the Stirling's approximation of the factorial is held, and under what circumstances E[xlogx] ~= E[x]logE[x]. I must say that the moment the authors came up with equation 15---abandoning the prior knowledge on theta and creating some questionable approximate values---I think equation 15 is justified only because it creates an intuitive rule for evaluating the similarity of the prediction.
Line 262, "Sample from this un-normalized density": What density? The distribution of the dataset?
Line 365: Why is it not important to learn the prior knowledge in 2AFC? Innovative methods, important problems, less than the insurmountable results.
