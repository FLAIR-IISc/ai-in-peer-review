The manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and the manuscript is generally well-presented. However, the work rests on a substantial number of assumptions, and the extent of these assumptions feels disproportionate to the impact of the key results. Despite this, the methodology is interesting and innovative, leading me to judge the work as above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results: the finding in section 4.1 concerning the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising, a point which arose during my reading and led me to question the inclusion of both noise sources in the model. It would be helpful to explicitly indicate which condition differences were statistically reliable, as the box plots are difficult to interpret. In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the case where the reference observer does *not* have a slow-speed prior.

Specific comments:

line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution, given that it is fundamentally a continuous variable? While this likely reflects the typical experimental design where stimulus levels are discrete independent variables, the authors should clarify this point.

line 134: As the authors note, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. The success of this work may be largely attributable to the incorporation of this transform. Consequently, the work demonstrates that Bayesian models are identifiable *given* strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.

line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximization of differential entropy ensure that Eqn. 3 is fit to be a unimodal distribution? It seems plausible that the fit could, in principle, yield a bimodal distribution.

line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over a uniform representation? What knowledge does the user have regarding the true stimulus distribution that justifies a prior 50% wider than the true range?

Figure 1: Despite careful examination, I find Figure 1 more confusing than informative. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a, and removing *p_est(s^*|x)* (as a derived inference), would likely improve clarity.

line 252: Can the authors comment on the conditions on *N* that render Equation 14 approximately correct – specifically, when Stirling’s approximation of the factorial holds, and under what circumstances *E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]*? By the point where the authors arrive at Equation 15—having abandoned priors on *θ* and made several approximations—I feel that the equation is justified primarily by its ability to yield an intuitive rule for assessing the similarity of predictions.

line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density is being referenced – the dataset distribution?

line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC? This is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less than overwhelming.
