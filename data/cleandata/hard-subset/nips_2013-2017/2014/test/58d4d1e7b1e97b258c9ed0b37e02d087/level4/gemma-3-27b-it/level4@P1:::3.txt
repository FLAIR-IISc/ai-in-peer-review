The paper describes a Bayesian observer model with a focus on identifiability, offering a mathematically convenient framework for perceptual estimation. A key strength of the model is its ability to balance robustness—handling diverse scenarios like stimulus-dependent noise—with computational tractability, allowing for analytical solutions and efficient exploration of the model space. The authors highlight the importance of assessing whether different parameter settings can produce indistinguishable behavior, addressing a critical concern for interpreting Bayesian models of perception. 

I find this paper promising and believe it has merit for acceptance to NIPS. The proposed model provides a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in a wide range of experiments. However, I do have some reservations regarding the novelty of the contribution. While technically sound and well-presented, the work builds upon a growing body of literature employing Bayesian approaches to perception, particularly within journals like *PLoS Computational Biology*. The contribution, therefore, feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. Readers may be familiar with related models, such as those presented in:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. *PLoS Computational Biology, 8*(11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. *PLoS Computational Biology, 10*(6), e1003661.

The current submission is distinct from these earlier articles, but sufficiently similar that I believe it would benefit from a more thorough contextualization within the existing literature.

I suggest the authors revise the manuscript to include a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian models of perception. This section should clearly articulate how the model presented here significantly advances the field beyond previous work, specifically outlining its unique features and advantages.

Furthermore, the paper emphasizes the mathematical identifiability of the model’s parameters. While true—the model's structure allows for the differentiation of parameter estimates—this identifiability is constrained by the inherent assumptions *within* the model itself. The model may not resolve the fundamental challenge of disentangling the sources of error in perceptual tasks—whether those errors originate from sensory noise, decision-making processes, or motor responses. Therefore, it could be argued that the model does not address the core identifiability concerns that frequently arise in perceptual science. I recommend the authors address this point in the revised manuscript, acknowledging the limitations of the model’s identifiability and discussing its implications for interpreting perceptual data.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Mathematically elegant and comprehensive framework for modeling perceptual estimation.
*   Addresses an important issue of model identifiability in Bayesian modeling.
*   Provides a potentially valuable tool for experimental design.
*   Demonstrates the framework’s application to well-studied perceptual tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental, building on existing work in Bayesian models of perception.
*   The model's identifiability is limited by its inherent assumptions and may not resolve broader challenges in perceptual inference.
