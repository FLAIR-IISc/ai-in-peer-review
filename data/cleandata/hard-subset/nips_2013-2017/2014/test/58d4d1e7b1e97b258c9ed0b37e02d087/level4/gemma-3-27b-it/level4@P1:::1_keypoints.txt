- Proposes a framework to test identifiability of Bayesian models for behavioral data, summarizes Bayesian Decision Theory, identifies non-identifiability sources, sets constraints, makes assumptions, tests sensitivity, and applies to time-interval estimation and speed perception.

- Notes that identifiability of internal representations/processes is a longstanding concern in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978).

- Observes recent critiques that identifiability limits the adoption of Bayesian models (e.g., Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012).

- Main reservation 1: Overlooks substantial prior work on model comparison and identifiability (e.g., Pitt, Myung & Zhang 2002; Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004), making it hard to evaluate novelty.

- Main reservation 2: Some modeling assumptions seem arbitrary and under-justified (e.g., definition of p_meas in Eq.3), and the dependence of identifiability on these assumptions is not well explored.

- Questions whether parameter definitions (e.g., Eq.12) are sufficient to fully characterize cognitive or perceptual processes.

- Minor comment: Line 53’s claim “It is a trivial mathematical fact…” should be explained for clarity.

- Minor comment: Line 73 likely has a typographical error—second “nor” should be “or.”

- Minor comment: Figure 2’s symbols (star and dashed lines) are unclear since caption indicates both represent the true value.

- Overall: Well-written and tackles an important issue, but needs stronger connection to prior work and clearer justification/discussion of key assumptions.
