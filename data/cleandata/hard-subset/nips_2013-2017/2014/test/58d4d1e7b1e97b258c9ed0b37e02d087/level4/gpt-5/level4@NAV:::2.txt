The paper addresses the critical problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. It is well written and generally clear. However, the approach relies on a substantial number of assumptions that the reader must accept, and this list feels long relative to the practical impact of the main findings. Because the methodology is creative and novel, I judge the work to be above the bar for presentation at NIPS.

With respect to the main results: In Section 4.1, the near interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is not particularly surprising (I questioned the need to include both while reading). It would help to specify which condition differences are statistically reliable. (Perhaps I’m misreading the box plots.) In Section 4.2, isn’t it important to demonstrate that if the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior, the model would recover that as well?

Specific comments:
line 106: Why is the stimulus sampled from a discrete distribution when it is in fact a continuous quantity? I assume this reflects typical experimental designs where stimulus level is the independent variable, but the authors should clarify.
line 134: As noted, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. It seems the success of the approach may largely hinge on this transform. That is, the transform imposes a strong constraint on inference, so the contribution is effectively that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.
line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that the fit in Eq. 3 is unimodal? In principle, the fit could be bimodal.
line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over a uniform one? What knowledge about the true stimulus distribution warrants a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?
Figure 1: Even after careful inspection, the figure is more confusing than illuminating. It might be clearer to merge Figure 1b into Figure 1a and remove p_est(s^*|x), since it is a derived inference.
line 252: Please comment on the regimes of N for which Equation 14 is approximately correct—i.e., when Stirling’s approximation to the factorial holds—and under what conditions E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]. By the time Equation 15 appears—after dropping priors on θ and making some debatable approximations—it seems justified mainly because it yields an intuitive rule for assessing similarity of predictions.
line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density is meant here? The dataset distribution?
line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC? Innovative approach, important problem, results not especially compelling.
