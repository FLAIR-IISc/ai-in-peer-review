- Manuscript tackles an important cognitive-modeling problem (identifiability), is clearly written, methodologically interesting, and merits NIPS presentation despite concerns.  
- Major concern: results rely on many strong assumptions, reducing impact of findings.  
- Section 4.1: Claim that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising; report which condition differences are statistically reliable (box plots insufficient).  
- Section 4.2: Must show model-recovery when the reference observer lacks a slow-speed prior; a recovery analysis without that prior is essential.  
- Line 106: Clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled as drawn from a discrete distribution (experimental justification).  
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform heavily constrains inference; explicitly discuss how identifiability depends on this representational assumption.  
- Line 143: Clarify whether constraints plus entropy maximization guarantee unimodality of Eq. 3, and state conditions preventing bimodality.  
- Line 158: Justify why the observer prior favors a lattice over uniform representation and why the prior is allowed to be 50% wider than the true range; provide sensitivity analysis.  
- Figure 1: Current layout is confusing; integrate panel 1b into 1a and remove derived p_est(s^* | x) for clarity.  
- Line 252: State conditions on N for Eq. 14’s approximations (Stirling’s approximation and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) or tighten derivation/provide empirical validation.  
- Line 262: Specify which unnormalized density is meant by “sample from this unnormalized density” (e.g., dataset distribution?).  
- Line 365: Explain or simulate why learning priors does not matter in 2AFC tasks.  
- Summary recommendation: innovative approach but needs clearer statistical reliability, model-recovery tests (especially without slow-speed prior), and justification/sensitivity analyses for key assumptions; nonetheless recommend acceptance.
