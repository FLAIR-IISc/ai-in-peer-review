The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, with explicit mathematical formulations for sensory noise, the likelihood function, the prior distribution, the loss function, and the response distribution. A key advantage of this model is that it marries robustness with analytical convenience. In particular, it can accommodate a range of scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) while remaining computationally practical (e.g., inference is tractable). The manuscript also stresses that the model is identifiable (i.e., alternative parameter settings can be readily assessed and distinguished).

I like this work and would be pleased to see it accepted to the NIPS conference. The proposed model offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation across many experimental contexts. That said, I do have reservations. The model seems very much in the spirit and style of other recent contributions (often in PLoS Computational Biology), making the novelty primarily incremental. For example, see:
Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

The present submission is not the same as these (or other) prior articles, but it is similar enough (to these and others) that it seems fair to characterize the contribution as incremental.

I recommend the authors revise the paper by adding a section that reviews recent Bayesian models of perception and explicitly articulates why the current model represents a meaningful advance over previously published approaches.

One additional point. The submission highlights that the model is mathematically identifiable. While true, this identifiability stems from the model’s assumptions. The term “identifiable” can be interpreted more broadly. For instance, the model cannot decisively separate whether an experimental participant’s errors arise from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. Consequently, it would be reasonable to state that the model does not resolve the identifiability concerns that most perceptual scientists prioritize. Please address this in the revision.

A mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but the contribution is incremental given closely related prior work.
