- Addresses identifiability of Bayesian perception models and proposes a systematic framework to test recoverability of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavior.  
- Main contribution: practical a priori identifiability analysis to evaluate how experimental designs affect recoverability of model components.  
- Applies framework to two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception.  
- In interval estimation, shows experimental choices (number of conditions, trials, loss assumptions) impact prior recovery.  
- Counterintuitive finding: imposing a narrow loss (closer to MAP) can degrade reconstruction quality.  
- In speed perception, demonstrates robust recovery of prior and noise, supporting the inferred slow-speed prior.  
- Methodology: flexible Bayesian observer model with efficient computation and identifiability analysis using expected log-likelihood and KL divergence.  
- Fills a gap: moves identifiability analysis beyond ad hoc/specific-case approaches to a general, implementable framework.  
- Major limitation: reliance on strong structural assumptions (sensory mapping Eq.2, prior Eq.4, loss Eq.6) that constrain generalizability.  
- Recommendation: authors should explicitly acknowledge these constraints and discuss extensions to different representational assumptions.  
- Section 4.1: sensory and motor noise are confounded—authors should clarify practical implications and which design changes (e.g., MTR) resolve this.  
- Section 4.2: should include a control validation (e.g., flat prior) to show the framework can detect absence of a slow-speed prior.  
- Specific technical clarifications requested: discrete stimulus distributions (Line 106), dependence on log-stimulus transform (Line 134), unimodality/enropy constraints (Line 143), justification for prior width choice (Line 158).  
- Figure suggestion: simplify Figure 1 by integrating panels and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) to reduce confusion.  
- Overall assessment: sound, innovative, well-executed work that addresses an important methodological gap; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
