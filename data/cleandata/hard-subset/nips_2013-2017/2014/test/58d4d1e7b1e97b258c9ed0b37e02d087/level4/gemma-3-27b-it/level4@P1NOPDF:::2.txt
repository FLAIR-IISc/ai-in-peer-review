The manuscript addresses the important problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The writing is clear and well-organized. However, the work relies on a substantial number of assumptions, and the length of this list feels disproportionate to the impact of the key results. Despite this, I believe the methodology is interesting and innovative enough to warrant presentation at NIPS, and thus I rate the work as above the acceptance threshold.

Regarding the key results: The finding in section 4.1 regarding the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was not particularly surprising (I questioned the inclusion of both noise types while reading). It would be helpful to indicate which differences between conditions were statistically reliable. (I may have missed something in the box plots.)

In section 4.2, it seems essential to demonstrate that the model can also recover the stimulus distribution even when the reference observer does *not* have a slow-speed prior. 

Specific comments:

line 106: Why is the stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution when it is, in fact, a continuous variable? I suspect this is due to the typical experimental design, where stimulus levels are treated as discrete independent variables. The authors might consider clarifying this point.

line 134: The authors correctly note that the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. It appears the success of this work may be largely attributable to incorporating this transform. In other words, the transform imposes a strong constraint on inference, suggesting this work demonstrates that Bayesian models are identifiable *given* strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.

line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximization of differential entropy guarantee that Eqn. 3 will fit to a unimodal distribution? It seems a bimodal distribution could, in principle, result from the fit.

line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor the lattice representation over a uniform representation? What information does the observer have about the true stimulus distribution that justifies a prior 50% wider than the actual stimulus range?

Figure 1: Even after careful examination, I found Figure 1 more confusing than illuminating. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a might improve clarity. Additionally, removing p_est(s^*|x) from the figure would be beneficial, as it’s a derived inference.

line 252: Could the authors comment on the conditions on *N* that make Equation 14 approximately correct? Specifically, when are Stirling’s approximation of the factorial and the equality E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x] valid? By the time the authors arrive at Equation 15—having abandoned priors on θ and made some questionable approximations—I feel it is justified primarily by its ability to produce an intuitive rule for assessing the similarity of predictions.

line 262: “sample from this unnormalized density”: Which density is being referenced? The dataset distribution?

line 365: Why does learning of priors appear unimportant in the 2AFC task? This is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the results are less compelling than I had hoped.
