- Tackles an important issue (model identifiability) and is well-written and clear.
- Relies on a lengthy list of assumptions that reduce the impact of the key results.
- Methodology is interesting and innovative and merits presentation at NIPS.
- Section 4.1: interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; report which condition differences are statistically reliable and clarify box plots.
- Section 4.2: must demonstrate the model can recover that the reference observer lacked a slow-speed prior.
- Line 106: clarify why a continuous stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution (likely experimental design) and justify the choice.
- Line 134: the log-stimulus transform may drive much of the success, implying reliance on strong internal representational assumptions rather than a general Bayesian property.
- Line 143: clarify whether constraints and differential entropy maximization guarantee unimodality of Equation 3, since a bimodal fit is possible.
- Line 158: justify why the observer’s prior favors a lattice representation and how they could have a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range.
- Figure 1: improve clarity by integrating panels (1b into 1a) and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x).
- Line 252: state conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct (validity of Stirling’s approximation and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) and justify approximations leading to Equation 15.
- Line 262: specify which unnormalized density is being sampled from (presumably the dataset distribution).
- Line 365: explain why learning priors does not affect the 2AFC scenario.
- Overall: an innovative approach to an important problem, but results are not overwhelming.
