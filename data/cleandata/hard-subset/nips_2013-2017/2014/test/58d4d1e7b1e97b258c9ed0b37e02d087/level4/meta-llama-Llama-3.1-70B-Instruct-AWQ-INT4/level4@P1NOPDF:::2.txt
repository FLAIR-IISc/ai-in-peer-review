The manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The text is well-written and clear, but the work relies on a lengthy list of assumptions that the reader must accept and understand, which somewhat diminishes the impact of the key results. Despite this, the methodology is interesting and innovative, and I believe the work meets the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Regarding the key results, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in section 4.1 was not entirely unexpected (I questioned the inclusion of both in the model while reading). It would be helpful to indicate which condition differences were reliable; perhaps a clearer visualization than box plots could achieve this.

In section 4.2, it is essential to demonstrate that the model can recover the fact that the reference observer did not have a slow-speed prior. This would strengthen the results and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's capabilities.

Specific comments:

Line 106: The stimulus is drawn from a discrete distribution, despite being a continuous variable. I assume this is due to typical experimental design, where stimulus levels are independent variables. However, the authors may wish to clarify this point to avoid confusion.

Line 134: As the authors note, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. The success of their work may be largely due to the incorporation of this transform, which imposes a strong constraint on inference. In essence, this work suggests that Bayesian models are identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.

Line 143: The constraints in Equation 4 and the maximization of differential entropy may not guarantee that Equation 3 yields a unimodal distribution. In principle, the fit could result in a bimodal distribution.

Line 158: It is unclear why the observer's prior should favor the lattice representation over a uniform representation. What knowledge about the true stimulus distribution allows the observer to have a prior that is 50% wider than the true range?

Figure 1: Despite careful examination, I found the figure more confusing than enlightening. Integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing p_est(s^*|x) might improve clarity, as it is a derived inference.

Line 252: The authors should comment on the conditions under which Equation 14 is approximately correct, specifically when Stirling's approximation of the factorial holds and when E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x]. By the time the authors derive Equation 15, having abandoned priors on θ and made some questionable approximations, it seems that Equation 15 is justified primarily because it yields an intuitive rule for assessing similarity of predictions.

Line 262: The text mentions "sample from this unnormalized density," but it is unclear which density is being referred to. Is it the dataset distribution?

Line 365: The manuscript claims that learning of priors does not matter in 2AFC. While this is an innovative approach to an important problem, the results are not overwhelmingly convincing.
