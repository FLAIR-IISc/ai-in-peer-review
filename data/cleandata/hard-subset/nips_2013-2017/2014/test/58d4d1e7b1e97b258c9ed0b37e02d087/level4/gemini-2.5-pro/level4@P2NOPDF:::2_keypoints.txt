- Paper tackles model identifiability in cognitive modeling; well-written but rests on strong assumptions that limit generality.  
- Methodology is interesting and novel, but key results feel insufficiently impactful to justify the extensive theoretical framework; nonetheless recommend acceptance.  

Major comments:
- Section 4.1: Interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; authors should report which condition differences are statistically significant because box plots are unclear.  
- Section 4.2: Add a control showing the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior to demonstrate specificity and rule out bias toward finding that prior.  

Specific comments:
- line 106: Explain modeling a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution (likely due to experimental stimulus levels).  
- line 134: Clarify whether identifiability is a general Bayesian property or the result of the strong log-stimulus transform constraint.  
- line 143: Address whether constraints plus differential-entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fit, or whether bimodal fits are possible.  
- line 158: Justify the observer prior choice (lattice vs. uniform) and the rationale for a prior 50% wider than the true stimulus range.  
- Figure 1: Improve clarity—suggest integrating panel (b) into (a) and removing p_est(s^*|x) to focus on core model components.  
- line 252: Justify approximations leading to Equation 15 (Stirling, E[x log x] ≈ E[x]logE[x]) and specify conditions on N for validity.  
- line 262: Clarify what is meant by "sample from this unnormalized density" (which density is referenced).  
- line 365: The claim that learning priors does not matter in a 2AFC task is counterintuitive and requires a more detailed explanation.
