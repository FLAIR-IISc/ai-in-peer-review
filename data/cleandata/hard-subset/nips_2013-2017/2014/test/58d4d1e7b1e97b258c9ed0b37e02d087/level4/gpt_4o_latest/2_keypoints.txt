- Addresses model identifiability in cognitive modeling; well-written and clear.
- Relies on a large list of assumptions that feel disproportionately extensive relative to the main results.
- Methodology is innovative and intriguing; work is acceptable for NIPS presentation.
- Section 4.1: interchangeability of sensory and motor noise was unsurprising; need clarification which condition differences are statistically reliable (boxplots ambiguous).
- Section 4.2: must show the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior when the reference observer lacks it.
- Line 106: why model a continuous stimulus as drawn from a discrete distribution — needs explicit justification (experimental design vs true continuity).
- Line 134: the log-stimulus transform is central to success and effectively imposes a strong constraint on inference; identifiability may hinge on this representational assumption.
- Line 143: question whether constraints and entropy maximization guarantee unimodality of Equation 3 or if bimodal fits are possible.
- Line 158: why does the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation and can be 50% wider than the true stimulus range — what knowledge justifies this prior?
- Figure 1: current presentation is confusing; suggest integrating 1b into 1a and removing the derived p_est(s*|x).
- Line 252: clarify conditions on N for Stirling’s approximation and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]; Eq.15 seems justified mainly because it yields an intuitive rule after debatable approximations.
- Line 262: the phrase “sample from this unnormalized density” is unclear—specify which density is meant (e.g., dataset distribution).
- Line 365: why is learning priors considered irrelevant in the 2AFC task; results on this point are less compelling.
