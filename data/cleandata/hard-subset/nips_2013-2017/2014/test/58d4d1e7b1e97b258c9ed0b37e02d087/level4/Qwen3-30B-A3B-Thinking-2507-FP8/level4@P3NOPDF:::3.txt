

The paper presents a Bayesian model of perception featuring a mathematically rigorous formulation of sensory noise, likelihood functions, prior distributions, loss functions, and response distributions. A notable strength lies in the model’s dual capacity for robustness across diverse perceptual scenarios (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) and computational tractability, enabling efficient inference. The authors emphasize the model’s identifiability—meaning distinct parameter estimates can be reliably distinguished—a feature they position as critical for empirical validation. The framework offers a clear, elegant formalization for perceptual estimation, making it a valuable tool for experimental design and analysis.

However, the paper’s primary contribution appears incremental relative to recent literature. While the model is well-motivated and technically sound, it shares significant conceptual and methodological overlap with established Bayesian frameworks for perception, particularly those developed in the past five years. For instance, the core approach aligns closely with work by Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) in *PLoS Computational Biology*, which similarly integrates probabilistic representations of sensory noise, priors, and decision processes to explain perceptual biases. The current submission does not diverge substantially from these precedents in structure, assumptions, or analytical scope. This is not a criticism of the model’s quality but rather a clarification of its novelty: the contribution is an incremental refinement rather than a paradigm shift. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this context in the revised manuscript, contrasting their model with key prior work to justify its distinct value.

Specifically, the paper would benefit from a new section (e.g., "Related Work and Novelty") that critically reviews recent Bayesian models of perception, highlighting how the proposed framework advances beyond existing approaches. This should address concrete gaps or limitations in prior work that the current model resolves—such as handling specific experimental paradigms, improving identifiability of noise sources, or enabling new predictions. Without this, the paper risks being perceived as a reiteration of established methodology rather than a meaningful step forward for the field.

A second critical concern pertains to the claim of "identifiability." The authors state the model is identifiable, but this requires precise qualification. The model’s identifiability is strictly a consequence of its *mathematical assumptions* (e.g., specific parametric forms for noise distributions), not an inherent property of perceptual modeling. Crucially, the model does not resolve the *empirical identifiability* challenge central to perceptual science: distinguishing whether observed errors stem from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise. As noted by prior work (e.g., Kersten et al., *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 2004), these noise sources are often confounded in behavioral data, and the current framework does not provide a mechanism to disentangle them. Thus, while parameters are mathematically distinguishable *within the model*, the model itself does not address the core identifiability problem that experimentalists face. The authors should clarify this distinction in the revision, acknowledging that their model’s identifiability is theoretical and model-dependent, not a solution to empirical ambiguities.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1.  **Revise the novelty claim**: Explicitly position the model within the existing literature, demonstrating how it advances beyond Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) and similar works. A table comparing key features (e.g., noise modeling, inference tractability, experimental applications) would be effective.
2.  **Clarify the identifiability claim**: Distinguish between mathematical identifiability (under the model’s assumptions) and empirical identifiability (distinguishing noise sources in data). State clearly that the model does not resolve the latter and discuss its implications for experimental design.
3.  **Strengthen the motivation**: Emphasize specific experimental contexts where the model’s tractability or robustness offers tangible advantages over prior approaches, beyond general claims of "mathematical convenience."

The paper’s mathematical elegance and clear exposition are commendable, and the model is well-suited for formalizing perceptual estimation in controlled experiments. However, the incremental nature of the contribution and the misalignment between the model’s identifiability claim and the field’s empirical needs require substantial revision. If these points are addressed, the paper could make a valuable contribution. As currently written, the novelty is insufficient for acceptance at NIPS without clearer justification of its advancement over the state of the art. I recommend *conditional acceptance* pending major revisions to the novelty and identifiability sections.
