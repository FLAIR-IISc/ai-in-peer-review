- Framework for testing a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models using expected KL divergence to assess model distinguishability.
- Introduces a flexible parametric class of observer models for a range of perceptual estimation tasks.
- Core contribution: method to map an "identifiability landscape" showing which parameters can be reliably recovered given an experimental design.
- Demonstrated on two case studies: (1) inferring priors in time-interval estimation (design-dependent recovery) and (2) reconstructing a slow-speed prior in speed perception (robust recovery).
- Strength: novel, systematic approach addressing a fundamental identifiability problem in cognitive modeling, useful for designing informative experiments.
- Weakness: conclusions rely on many specific modeling assumptions and complex parametric forms, limiting generality relative to the complexity of the machinery.
- Section 4.1 critique: sensory and motor noise being hard to distinguish is expected; need statistical tests to show reconstruction-error differences are reliable.
- Section 4.2 critique: demonstrates recovery of a slow-speed prior but lacks validation that the method can correctly infer the absence of such a prior.
- Specific comment 1: clarify and justify the use of a discrete stimulus distribution for inherently continuous variables.
- Specific comment 2: discuss how strongly the log-stimulus transform constrains inference and the dependence of identifiability on this assumption.
- Specific comment 3: clarify how the two-Gaussian mixture likelihood is constrained to be unimodal given the fitting procedure.
- Specific comment 4: justify the lattice (regular Gaussian mixture) prior representation and the choice that prior support is 50% wider than the stimulus range.
- Specific comment 5: improve Figure 1 by integrating the observer schematic with the probabilistic graphical model and avoid representing p_est(s*|x) as a fundamental node.
- Specific comment 6: provide a more detailed justification for approximations in deriving the identifiability metric and discuss their limitations.
- Specific comment 7: justify assuming no task-specific prior learning in the speed study and discuss how learning would affect conclusions.
- Overall recommendation: important and innovative work that merits acceptance if the above points are addressed.
