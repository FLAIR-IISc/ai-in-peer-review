This manuscript tackles the important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. It is clearly written and generally accessible. However, the approach relies on a long list of assumptions, which feels disproportionate to the strength of the main results. That said, the methodology is innovative and thought-provoking, and I judge it to be above the acceptance threshold for presentation at NIPS.

Key results:
- Section 4.1: The apparent interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is not surprising (I questioned the need to include both while reading). Please indicate which condition differences are statistically reliable; the box plots are difficult to interpret on their own.
- Section 4.2: It seems essential to show that if the reference observer does not have a slow-speed prior, the model can correctly recover that fact as well.

Specific comments:
- Line 106: Why is the stimulus modeled as being drawn from a discrete distribution when it is inherently continuous? I assume this mirrors typical experimental designs where stimulus levels are discretized as independent variables, but the manuscript should clarify this rationale.
- Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (Eq. 2) is motivated by psychophysical laws. It appears that much of the model’s success may stem from this strong assumption, which tightly constrains inference. In effect, the paper shows that Bayesian models can be identifiable given strong assumptions about representational transformations in the mind.
- Line 143: Do the constraints in Eq. 4 and the maximum differential entropy criterion ensure that the fitted distribution in Eq. 3 is unimodal? In principle, could the fit be bimodal?
- Line 158: Why should the observer’s prior favor a lattice representation over a uniform one? On what basis would the observer “know” enough about the true stimulus distribution to adopt a prior that is 50% wider than the actual range?
- Figure 1: The figure is more confusing than illuminating. Consider integrating panel 1b into 1a, and removing p_est(s*|x), since it is derived and adds clutter.
- Line 252: Please specify the conditions on N under which Eq. 14 is a good approximation—i.e., when Stirling’s approximation is accurate and when E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] holds. By the time Eq. 15 is introduced—after dropping priors on θ and making several approximations—it feels as though the justification rests primarily on yielding an intuitive similarity metric for predictions, rather than on rigorous derivation.
- Line 262: “Sample from this unnormalized density” — which density is meant here? The data-set distribution?
- Line 365: Why does learning of priors not matter in 2AFC? Please clarify the conditions under which prior learning is irrelevant and whether this depends on task structure, parameterization, or asymptotic behavior.

Overall assessment: This is an innovative approach to an important problem, but the empirical and diagnostic results are less compelling than the methodological promise. I nonetheless recommend acceptance, with revisions that clarify assumptions, strengthen validations (especially around priors), and improve figure interpretability.
