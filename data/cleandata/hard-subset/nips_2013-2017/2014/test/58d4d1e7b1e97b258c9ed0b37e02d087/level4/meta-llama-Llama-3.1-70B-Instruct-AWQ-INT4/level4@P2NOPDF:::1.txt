This article proposes a framework for comparing Bayesian models of behavioral data, addressing the long-standing issue of non-identifiability in internal representations and processes within cognitive science and psychology. The authors summarize the Bayesian Decision Theory framework, identify areas of non-identifiability, and outline constraints to make each term identifiable. They then apply these constraints, explore parameter differentiability, and demonstrate the technique using two studies on time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issue of identifiability has been a major concern in cognitive science and psychology for decades, with Anderson (1978) showing that representational formats can be indistinguishable without process constraints. Recently, this issue has hindered the widespread adoption of the Bayesian framework (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

Although the article is well-written and addresses a crucial issue, it has two major limitations. Firstly, it overlooks the extensive literature on model comparison in cognitive science and psychology, making it challenging to evaluate its contribution. The proposed framework bears resemblance to previous work (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), and the authors should acknowledge and differentiate their work from existing research.

Secondly, the authors make assumptions that appear arbitrary, and it is unclear how these assumptions impact the framework. For instance, the definition of $p_{meas}$ by equation 3 seems unjustified. Additionally, defining parameter values in Equation 12 may not be sufficient to capture the characteristics of cognitive or perceptual processes. Although the authors claim that their framework can express a large class of sensory models, this alone does not justify the assumptions. The article should discuss and justify these assumptions to ensure the identifiability of the Bayesian models.
