

This paper addresses a critical theoretical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to degeneracies in the inverse problem. The authors propose a framework for systematically testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models, with applications to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception. The paper makes a valuable contribution to a longstanding problem in cognitive science, where model non-identifiability has been recognized as a major limitation of Bayesian approaches (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation. The authors first establish the degeneracy problem in Bayesian observer models (Eq. 1), then introduce a flexible observer model class that covers a wide range of psychophysical tasks. They propose a novel method to assess identifiability a priori through expected log-likelihood comparisons (Eqs. 11-13), which they apply to two well-chosen case studies. The framework is theoretically sound and provides practical guidance for experimental design, such as demonstrating that measuring motor noise parameters (MTR condition) significantly improves identifiability in time interval estimation.

The paper's strengths are clear: it tackles an important theoretical problem with a practical solution, provides concrete examples of how the framework improves experimental design, and demonstrates the framework's application to two distinct perceptual domains. The case studies are well-chosen and effectively illustrate the framework's utility. The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of Bayesian models in perception, showing that some previously accepted findings (e.g., loss functions in interval timing) may require further validation.

However, the paper has significant shortcomings in addressing the criteria for NIPS publication. Regarding Originality, while the specific framework for identifiability testing is novel, the paper fails to adequately contextualize its contribution within the extensive literature on model comparison in cognitive science. The authors cite key works like Bowers & Davis (2012) and Jones & Love (2011), but do not engage with the broader methodological literature on model distinguishability. For instance, the framework for identifiability testing bears striking similarity to the model comparison approaches proposed by Pitt, Myung, and Zhang (2002) and Navarro, Pitt, and Myung (2004), which have been widely adopted in cognitive science. The authors should explicitly discuss how their framework differs from these established approaches and why it is better suited for Bayesian models of perception.

Regarding Quality, the paper makes several assumptions without sufficient justification. For example, the choice of a two-component Gaussian mixture for the sensory model (Eq. 3) is presented as a convenient approximation, but the authors do not demonstrate why this specific form is necessary or sufficient to capture the relevant psychological phenomena. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the assumptions made in the model, including sensitivity analyses showing how results change with alternative model specifications. The claim that the model "can approximate both Weber-Fechner's law and Steven's law" (Supplementary Material) is not substantiated with evidence or comparative analysis.

The paper also fails to adequately address the Significance of the work. While the authors demonstrate how the framework can improve experimental design, they do not convincingly argue why this is more important than existing methods for addressing identifiability. The significance of the framework's contribution to advancing the field of computational perception remains somewhat unclear. For instance, the authors show that certain experimental designs improve identifiability in time estimation, but they do not connect this to specific, important theoretical questions in perception that were previously unanswerable due to identifiability issues.

Regarding Clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but contains some ambiguities. The statement on line 53 that "it is a trivial mathematical fact" is problematic because it dismisses a key insight without explanation, which could confuse readers unfamiliar with the mathematics. Similarly, the caption for Figure 2 does not clearly explain what the star and dashed lines represent, creating unnecessary confusion.

The paper would be substantially strengthened by:
1. More thorough engagement with the model comparison literature in cognitive science
2. A detailed justification of key model assumptions with sensitivity analyses
3. A clearer articulation of how the framework advances the field beyond existing approaches
4. Better explanation of why the specific mathematical forms (e.g., Eq. 3) are appropriate

While the paper addresses an important theoretical problem with a well-executed framework, it falls short of making a significant contribution to the field of computational perception. The framework is valuable, but its novelty is not sufficiently established, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why it represents a meaningful advance over existing methods. The authors would need to substantially revise the manuscript to better situate their work within the broader literature and to more thoroughly justify their modeling choices.

Overall, the paper is well-written and addresses an important theoretical issue, but it fails to make a sufficiently novel contribution to the field. The framework has potential utility for experimental design, but the paper does not adequately demonstrate its significance or differentiate it from existing approaches. The work would benefit from a more critical engagement with the relevant literature and a clearer justification of the model assumptions.
