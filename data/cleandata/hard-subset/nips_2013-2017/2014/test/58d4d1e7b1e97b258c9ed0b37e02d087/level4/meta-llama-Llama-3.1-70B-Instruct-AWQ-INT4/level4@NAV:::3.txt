This paper presents a Bayesian model of perception, encompassing mathematical formulations for sensory noise, likelihood function, prior probability distribution, loss function, and response distribution. A notable advantage of this model lies in its ability to balance robustness with mathematical convenience, allowing it to accommodate diverse situations, such as stimulus-dependent noise, while maintaining tractable inference. The authors highlight the model's identifiability, enabling straightforward evaluation and distinction of parameter estimates.

I appreciate the paper's contribution to providing a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation in various experiments. However, I have some reservations regarding its novelty. The proposed model shares similarities with other recently published models (e.g., in PLoS Computational Biology), suggesting that its contribution is incremental. For instance, readers may draw parallels with the following studies:

Acerbi, L., Wolpert, D. M., & Vijayakumar, S. (2012). Internal representations of temporal statistics and feedback calibrate motor-sensory interval timing. PLoS Computational Biology, 8 (11), e1002771.
Acerbi, L., Vijayakumar, S., & Wolpert, D. M. (2014). On the origins of suboptimality in human probabilistic inference. PLoS Computational Biology, 10 (6), e1003661.

While the current submission is distinct from these earlier articles, its similarities warrant a discussion of its advancements over existing models. I recommend that the authors include a new section reviewing recently proposed Bayesian models of perception and explicitly state the significance of their proposed model.

Additionally, the emphasis on the model's mathematical identifiability is valid, but it is essential to acknowledge that this identifiability is contingent upon the model's mathematical assumptions. Furthermore, the model may not effectively distinguish between experimental subjects' errors resulting from sensory noise, decision noise, or response noise, which is a concern for many perceptual scientists. I suggest that the authors address this aspect in the revised manuscript.

Overall, the paper offers a mathematically elegant framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, but its novelty is incremental due to the existence of closely related work in the literature.
