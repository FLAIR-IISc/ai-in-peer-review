This manuscript addresses the important and challenging problem of model identifiability in cognitive modeling. The paper is well-written and the exposition is generally clear. However, the work is premised on a long list of assumptions that one must accept, and this list seems extensive relative to the impact of the key results presented. Nevertheless, because the methodology is interesting and innovative, I believe the work is above the threshold for presentation at NIPS.

**Critique of Key Results**

*   **Section 4.1:** The finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is not surprising; I questioned the need to incorporate both into the model while reading the manuscript. Regarding the figures, it would be helpful for the authors to indicate which differences between conditions are statistically significant, as this is not immediately clear from the box plots.
*   **Section 4.2:** A crucial control experiment appears to be missing. To validate the model's performance, it is essential to demonstrate that if the reference observer did not use a slow-speed prior, the model would correctly recover this fact.

**Specific Comments**

*   **line 106:** The stimulus, which is a continuous variable, is modeled as being drawn from a discrete distribution. While this likely reflects a typical experimental design where stimulus levels are set as discrete independent variables, the authors should clarify this choice.
*   **line 134:** As the authors note, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is motivated by established psychophysical laws. This transform appears to impose a very strong constraint on the inference process. Consequently, a key takeaway from this work may be that Bayesian models are identifiable primarily when such strong, pre-specified assumptions about internal representational transformations are incorporated.
*   **line 143:** Do the constraints in Equation 4, combined with the maximization of differential entropy, ensure that the distribution in Equation 3 is unimodal? It seems plausible that the resulting fit could, in principle, be bimodal.
*   **line 158:** What is the justification for the observer's prior favoring a lattice representation over a uniform one? Furthermore, what knowledge would allow an observer to adopt a prior that is 50% wider than the true stimulus range?
*   **Figure 1:** I found Figure 1 difficult to interpret. Its clarity might be improved by integrating Figure 1b into Figure 1a and removing the `p_est(s^*|x)` panel, as this posterior is a derived inference rather than a core component of the generative model itself.
*   **line 252:** The derivation of Equation 15 involves several approximations that warrant further justification. First, under what conditions on N is the approximation in Equation 14 (which relies on Stirling's approximation) considered valid? Second, under what circumstances does the approximation E[xlogx] ≈ E[x]logE[x] hold? By the time the authors arrive at Equation 15—having abandoned priors on θ and made these approximations—the equation seems justified more by its intuitive appeal as a rule for assessing predictive similarity than by a rigorous derivation.
*   **line 262:** The text states "sample from this unnormalized density." Please clarify which density is being referred to. Is it the distribution over the dataset?
*   **line 365:** The authors state that the learning of priors does not matter in a two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task. Could they elaborate on why this is the case?
