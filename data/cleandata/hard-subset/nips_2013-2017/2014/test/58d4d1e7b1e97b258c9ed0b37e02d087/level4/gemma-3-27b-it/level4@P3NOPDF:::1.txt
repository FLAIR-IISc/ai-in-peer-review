This paper proposes a framework for model comparison of Bayesian models applied to behavioral data. The authors effectively summarize Bayesian Decision Theory, identify challenges related to identifiability, and outline constraints that can be applied to render each component of the Bayesian framework identifiable. They then explore the differentiability of parameters within their model and demonstrate its applicability through re-analysis of two established studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.

The issue of identifiability in cognitive and psychological modeling is a long-standing concern. As highlighted by the authors, and previously articulated by Anderson (1978), representational formats can often produce equivalent behavioral predictions without constraints on underlying processes. More recently, the lack of identifiability has emerged as a significant obstacle to the broader adoption of Bayesian models within cognitive science (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). This work tackles a theoretically important problem, and the paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. However, I have two primary concerns.

First, the paper largely overlooks relevant existing literature on model comparison within cognitive science and psychology. This omission hinders a thorough evaluation of the contribution this framework makes beyond prior work. The proposed approach shares similarities with established methods for model comparison (e.g., Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002; Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004), and a more detailed discussion of these connections would be valuable. Specifically, addressing how this framework differs from, or builds upon, these prior approaches is essential.

Second, several assumptions made by the authors appear somewhat arbitrary, and the sensitivity of the framework to these assumptions is unclear. For instance, the justification for defining $p_{meas}$ as in equation 3 is not fully developed. While the authors acknowledge the model’s capacity to represent a broad class of sensory models, simply stating this does not suffice as a justification for the specific assumptions employed. It remains unclear whether defining parameter values as in Equation 12 fully captures the characteristics of the cognitive or perceptual processes being modeled. A more thorough discussion and justification of these constraining assumptions are needed, along with an exploration of the potential impact of alternative choices.

Minor comments:

*   Line 53: The phrase "It is a trivial mathematical fact…" could be improved by offering a brief explanation of *why* the statement is true. The term "trivial" may be off-putting to readers who are not already familiar with the concept.
*   Line 73: The second instance of "nor" likely should be "or."
*   Figure 2: The figure legend is unclear regarding the distinction between the star and dashed lines. Both appear to represent the true value, but clarification would improve the figure’s readability.

In summary, this is a well-written and insightful paper addressing a critical issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling. However, strengthening the connections to existing literature on model comparison, and providing more robust justification for key assumptions, would significantly enhance its contribution.
