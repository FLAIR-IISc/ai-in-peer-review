- Paper addresses non-identifiability of internal representations in Bayesian perceptual models and its impact on interpretation.
- Authors propose a systematic framework to assess identifiability of Bayesian observer models.
- Framework demonstrated on two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.
- They show inverse mapping from behavioral data to priors, likelihoods, and loss functions is degenerate—multiple combinations can yield identical behavior.
- Introduce a flexible, computationally tractable observer model class covering many psychophysical tasks.
- Main method quantifies expected support for alternative models using expected log likelihood as a proxy for identifiability.
- Situates work relative to prior cognitive science and Bayesian modeling literature, claiming novelty over existing model comparison techniques.
- Strength: clear articulation of the identifiability problem and its significance for perception models.
- Strength: development of a broadly applicable, efficient observer model class.
- Strength: practical utility shown through well-chosen case studies that inform experimental design.
- Strength: thorough exploration of parameter identifiability and surprising findings (e.g., narrow loss functions harming prior recovery).
- Strength: well-organized presentation with clear math and effective figures.
- Weakness: insufficient engagement with broader literature on model comparison and distinguishability.
- Weakness: key model assumptions (parametric sensory mapping and likelihood forms) are not well justified or sensitivity-tested.
- Weakness: reliance on KL divergence in theoretical identifiability analysis without discussing limitations or alternatives.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, valuable contribution with clear utility; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better contextualize literature, justify assumptions, and clarify the KL-based metric.
