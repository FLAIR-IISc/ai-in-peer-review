- Proposes a framework to test a priori identifiability for a broad class of Bayesian observer models of perception.  
- Builds a flexible, tractable observer model parameterizing sensory mapping/noise, internal prior/likelihood, loss/lapses, and motor noise.  
- Core method computes expected log-likelihood (related to KL divergence) between candidate and reference models to map an "identifiability landscape."  
- Demonstrates framework on two case studies: time-interval estimation design choices and re-analysis of a slow-speed prior in vision.  
- Tackles the common degeneracy problem where different priors/likelihoods/losses produce similar behavior.  
- Strength: addresses a timely, important problem in computational cognitive science with a principled, systematic approach.  
- Strength: technically strong observer model unifying many components (Weber/Stevens laws, non-Gaussian priors/likelihoods, asymmetric loss) with analytic expected-loss computation.  
- Strength: practical utility for experimental design, showing how to compare designs and detect parameter degeneracies (e.g., measuring motor noise vs. adding trials).  
- Weakness: perceived as incremental because it reuses established modeling components (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians, sensory-noise parameterization) from prior work.  
- Weakness: manuscript needs a clearer, explicit comparison to prior related models (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012/2014) to highlight novel conceptual advances.  
- Weakness: scope limited to parameter identifiability within the specified model architecture and does not resolve fundamental ambiguities between sources of behavioral variability.  
- Example limitation: Fig. 2c shows degeneracy between sensory and motor noise—framework diagnoses but cannot resolve such ambiguities without external measures or strong assumptions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical problem, provides a general, practical, technically sound framework, and offers informative case studies.  
- Arguments against acceptance: risk of being viewed as incremental and insufficiently situated relative to existing literature.
