Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a framework for assessing a priori identifiability in a large class of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors: (i) clearly summarize Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT); (ii) formalize a key source of non-identifiability in BDT (the equivalence class induced by reweighting prior, likelihood, and loss with functions whose product is constant); (iii) propose constraints/assumptions to regularize the inverse problem; (iv) derive an expected log-likelihood criterion that reduces to a KL-divergence–weighted sum over stimuli (Eq. 12) to quantify model distinguishability for a given experimental design and trial budget; (v) test “parameter differentiability” by sampling an approximate expected posterior E(θ|θ*) and inspecting its peakedness; and (vi) apply the framework to two case studies: time-interval estimation (Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010) and speed perception with a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006), offering design recommendations (e.g., measure motor noise; avoid overly narrow loss) and showing relatively robust recovery of the slow-speed prior.

The paper addresses a longstanding problem in cognitive science: the non-identifiability of internal representations and processes (e.g., Anderson, 1978). It also responds to recent critiques that identifiability limits the explanatory power and adoption of Bayesian frameworks (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). There is also relevant prior work on model comparison, model recovery, and distinguishability in psychology and cognitive science (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004) and in NIPS venues (e.g., Natarajan et al., 2009; Sanborn & Griffiths, 2010), to which this work is connected, although the connection could be made clearer.

Strengths
- Important problem: Identifiability is central to claims about recovered priors, likelihoods, and loss functions from behavior.
- Methodological clarity: The reduction of expected log evidence to a KL sum (Eq. 12) provides an interpretable, design-dependent identifiability metric. The simulation-based “expected posterior” maps parameter regions that are practically non-identifiable for a given Ntr and design.
- Practical guidance: The interval-timing analyses identify concrete design levers (e.g., measuring motor parameters improves prior recovery; encouraging MAP-like loss can worsen recovery). The speed-perception case offers theoretical validation of robust slow-speed prior recovery.
- Well written and well organized; figures and case studies are easy to follow.

Major concerns
1) Positioning relative to prior literature on model comparison and model recovery
- The paper largely overlooks a substantial literature on model distinguishability/recoverability, which already formalizes many of the goals here. In particular, Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) provide frameworks for assessing model distinguishability and data informativeness; Myung and colleagues’ work on model mimicry and parameter recovery is directly relevant. Please clarify what is substantively new relative to:
  - Recoverability/model recovery studies and metrics (e.g., distinguishability matrices, parameter recovery protocols).
  - Use of expected log-likelihood and information-theoretic criteria for design and identifiability.
  - Prior NIPS work on model comparison and cognitive model inference (e.g., Sanborn & Griffiths, 2010; Natarajan et al., 2009).
Concrete suggestions: add a related-work section contrasting your Eq. 12 criterion and sampling of E(θ|θ*) with distinguishability analyses and Fisher information-based design; clarify advantages (computational tractability for your rich observer class; analytic structure afforded by Gaussian mixtures) and limitations.

2) Insufficient justification and sensitivity analysis for key modeling assumptions
- Measurement model p_meas (Eq. 3): The mixture-of-two Gaussians with max-entropy constraints is elegant, but appears ad hoc. Why two components? How critical are unimodality and the first four-moment constraint? Show sensitivity to: number of components, skew/kurtosis bounds, and alternative likelihood families (e.g., log-normal, Laplace, heavy-tailed).
- Sensory transform f(s) (Eq. 2): Please justify the chosen parametric family and priors on (s0, d). How sensitive are identifiability conclusions to this choice versus alternatives (pure log, power-law)? In interval timing, does allowing deviations from log mapping change the MAP vs. non-MAP conclusion?
- Loss function (Eq. 6): The inverted two-Gaussian mixture is flexible, but the interval-timing case shows σℓ is weakly identifiable. Please analyze how much your prior reconstruction depends on fixing or constraining σℓ. Could a task-informed loss (e.g., measured via separate calibration) change conclusions?
- Eq. 12 and design dependence: While the KL-based expected log-evidence is standard, your identifiability conclusions can depend strongly on Pi (stimulus sampling), Nexp, and Ntr. Please justify these choices and provide design sensitivity (e.g., targeted stimulus placement to maximize KL separation; adaptive designs). Also discuss the asymmetry of KL and whether using symmetric f-divergences or expected log posterior odds would change rankings.
- Prior representation (Eq. 4): Using a 15-component Gaussian lattice is practical, but please examine whether M and the range/lattice spacing affect recovery and identifiability, especially for heavy-tailed priors in speed perception.

Quality
- The derivation to Eq. 12 appears correct and useful; applications are carefully executed. However, the arbitrariness of several parametric choices (likelihood, loss, transforms) without sensitivity analyses weakens the strength of the conclusions about identifiability. Adding systematic ablations would significantly improve rigor.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear. It would benefit from an explicit “Assumptions and constraints that ensure identifiability” section, listing which constraints are under experimental control (feedback/loss, stimulus distribution, independent motor calibration) versus theoretical commitments (noise model, sensory transform), and discussing their justification and impact.

Originality
- The focus on a priori identifiability for Bayesian observer models and the specific observer-class parameterization is valuable. The novelty would be clearer with a more thorough positioning relative to model distinguishability/recovery literatures.

Significance
- High. If strengthened as suggested, this framework could become a standard tool for experimental design and for qualifying claims about recovered internal representations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely theoretical issue with practical guidance for experimenters.
- Clear framework with interpretable identifiability metric.
- Useful case studies that expose non-obvious design trade-offs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient engagement with extensive prior work on model comparison/recovery.
- Several key assumptions appear arbitrary; limited sensitivity analyses leave robustness unclear.
- Some conclusions (e.g., MAP-like loss harms recovery) may be contingent on those assumptions.

Recommendations to authors
- Expand related work and clarify novel contributions relative to Pitt et al. (2002), Navarro et al. (2004), and critiques (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for: number of mixture components (likelihood/loss), prior basis size M and range, transform f(s), and coupling/decoupling of internal vs. external likelihood (beyond the UNC variant).
- Explicitly discuss and justify the constraints/assumptions that make the Bayesian models identifiable, and which can be validated or measured independently (e.g., motor noise calibration, loss calibration).
- Consider complementary identifiability diagnostics (e.g., Fisher information, profile likelihoods, parameter recovery plots) and targeted/adaptive stimulus designs to maximize KL separation.

Minor comments
- Line 53: Please spell out the “trivial mathematical fact” that the argmin in Eq. 1 is invariant to multiplying prior, likelihood, and loss by functions φi(s) whose product is a positive constant; a one-line derivation would help readers unfamiliar with BDT invariances.
- Line 73: Change the second “nor” to “or.”
- Figure 2: Clarify what the star and dashed lines denote in all panels and in the caption; ensure these conventions are consistent across conditions.

Summary judgment
A well-written and important paper that tackles a critical theoretical issue. Its main contribution would be stronger with fuller engagement with prior model comparison/recovery literature and with deeper justification and sensitivity analyses for the modeling assumptions that underwrite identifiability.
