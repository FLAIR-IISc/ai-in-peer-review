This paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a significant issue in the field of psychophysics. The authors propose a general method for a priori investigation of identifiability, allowing for a systematic comparison of different experimental designs. The paper is well-written, and the methodology is interesting and relevant to the field.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a flexible and efficient Bayesian observer model, which can be used to explore the identifiability landscape of various psychophysical tasks. The model is based on a continuous class of Bayesian observer models parametrized by a vector θ, which allows for a large representational basis with a single parametrization. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two case studies: the inference of priors in a time interval estimation task and the reconstruction of prior and noise characteristics in speed perception.

The paper has several strengths. First, it addresses a crucial issue in the field of psychophysics, namely the problem of model non-identifiability. The authors provide a systematic approach to investigating identifiability, which can be used to improve experimental design. Second, the paper presents a novel and flexible Bayesian observer model, which can be used to model a wide range of psychophysical tasks. Finally, the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their methodology and results.

However, there are also some weaknesses in the paper. One major concern is that the main results rely on many strong assumptions, which may reduce their impact. For example, the authors assume that the sensory noise is affected by the scalar property of interval timing, and that the loss function in internal measurement space is almost-quadratic. While these assumptions may be reasonable in certain contexts, they may not be universally applicable. Additionally, some of the results presented in the paper are based on simulations, which may not accurately reflect real-world data.

Furthermore, there are some specific issues with the presentation of the results. For example, in Section 4.1, the authors report that the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising, but they do not provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. Additionally, in Section 4.2, the authors do not demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer. Finally, some of the figures and equations in the paper could be improved for clarity.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on quality, as the methodology is sound and the results are well-presented. However, the paper could be improved in terms of clarity, as some of the assumptions and results are not clearly explained. The paper is also original, as it presents a novel approach to investigating identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. Finally, the paper is significant, as it addresses a crucial issue in the field of psychophysics and provides a systematic approach to improving experimental design.
