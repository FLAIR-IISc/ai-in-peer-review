This paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors propose a mathematically elegant and general approach to formalizing perceptual estimation, which combines robustness with mathematical convenience. The model is based on a continuous class of Bayesian observer models, which are parametrized by a vector θ. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.
* The proposed framework is mathematically rigorous and well-motivated, and it provides a general approach to formalizing perceptual estimation.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by applying it to two case studies, which are well-chosen and well-executed.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized, and it provides a clear and concise presentation of the results.

However, there are also some weaknesses:

* The contribution of the paper is incremental, and it closely resembles recent work (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014).
* The authors do not provide a clear discussion of the practical limitations of their framework, particularly with regards to the identifiability of the model parameters.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results, particularly with regards to the design of experiments and the interpretation of the results.

To address these weaknesses, I recommend that the authors:

* Include a new section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly state how this work advances them.
* Discuss the practical limitations of their framework, particularly with regards to the identifiability of the model parameters.
* Provide a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results, particularly with regards to the design of experiments and the interpretation of the results.

Overall, I believe that the paper is well-written and well-executed, and it provides a valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian models of perception. With some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, I believe that the paper could be even stronger and more impactful.
