This paper presents a novel framework for assessing, *a priori*, the identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors correctly identify a critical issue in the field: the degeneracy of Bayesian models, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, making it difficult to uniquely recover these components from data. The proposed solution consists of two main parts: first, a flexible and computationally efficient class of parametric observer models that can capture a wide range of perceptual tasks and observer characteristics. Second, a method to quantify identifiability by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model, averaged over datasets generated by a "true" reference model. This quantity, related to the KL-divergence between the response distributions of the two models, defines an "identifiability landscape" that can be explored via MCMC. The framework is demonstrated on two case studies: inferring priors in a time interval estimation task and recovering a slow-speed prior in a speed perception task. The authors use the framework to compare different experimental designs and to validate previous findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The problem of model identifiability is a fundamental and often overlooked issue in computational modeling of cognition. This paper provides a concrete, systematic framework for addressing it, which is a significant contribution. The idea of using such a framework to prospectively evaluate and improve experimental design is particularly valuable and could have a substantial impact on research practices in psychophysics and related fields. The methodology for efficiently exploring the identifiability landscape is novel and well-suited for a NIPS audience.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do a good job of explaining the core problem of degeneracy and walking the reader through the components of their proposed observer model and analysis framework.
*   **Quality:** The proposed framework is technically interesting. The use of the expected log-likelihood, and its connection to KL-divergence, provides a principled metric for practical non-identifiability. The case studies effectively demonstrate the utility of the framework for generating concrete, and sometimes non-obvious, predictions about the effectiveness of different experimental designs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reliance on Strong Assumptions:** The main weakness of the paper is that the generality of its conclusions is potentially limited by the large number of strong assumptions embedded in the observer model. While the model is described as "flexible," it relies on very specific parametric forms for every component: a log-like sensory mapping (Eq. 2), mixture-of-two-Gaussians for likelihoods and loss functions (Eqs. 3 & 6), and a mixture of Gaussians on a fixed lattice for the prior (Eq. 4). The conclusions drawn in the Results section (e.g., that a MAP-like loss function worsens prior recovery) may be highly contingent on these specific modeling choices. The impact of the work would be greater if the authors could demonstrate the robustness of their conclusions to some of these assumptions or provide a stronger justification for their necessity.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The conclusion in Section 4.1 that sensory noise (σ) and motor noise (ρ1) are partially non-identifiable is not surprising, as the authors themselves note. Both are sources of variance that, under the chosen logarithmic sensory mapping, are approximately Gaussian in the internal space and thus interchangeable. More critically, when comparing experimental designs (BSL, SRT, MAP, MTR), the paper presents point estimates of the reconstruction error (Fig 2b). To make these comparisons more rigorous, could the authors use the MCMC samples to report confidence intervals on the KL-divergence or perform statistical tests to establish which differences between designs are reliable?

2.  In Section 4.2, the authors demonstrate that their framework can successfully recover a slow-speed prior when one exists in the reference observer. A crucial control experiment is to show the converse: that if the reference observer has a flat (e.g., uniform) prior, the framework correctly infers the absence of a slow-speed bias and does not spuriously find one. The paper mentions a "FLA" condition in passing (line 351) and in the supplement, but this is a critical sanity check that would significantly strengthen the paper's claims if presented more prominently in the main text.

3.  Line 134: The choice of the sensory map `f(s)` seems to be a very powerful constraint. The assumption that there exists a monotonic transformation to an internal space with translationally invariant noise is strong. The identifiability results may depend heavily on this specific representational assumption. A brief discussion of how sensitive the conclusions might be to this choice would be welcome.

4.  Line 143: The paper states that the likelihood is expressed as a mixture of two Gaussians, with parameters fixed by the first four moments and a local maximum-entropy criterion, under the assumption of unimodality. Does this procedure guarantee that the resulting mixture distribution is, in fact, unimodal for all parameter settings? Please clarify or provide a reference.

5.  Line 158: Could the authors provide more justification for modeling the observer's prior as a mixture of Gaussians on a fixed lattice? Why is this basis preferred over others, such as splines or a simple histogram? Additionally, what is the rationale for setting the range of the prior to be "50% wider than the true stimulus range"? This seems like an arbitrary choice that should be justified.

6.  Equation 12: The derivation of the expected log-likelihood in terms of KL-divergence is central to the method. The paper defers the derivation to the supplement. Given its importance, it would be helpful to state the key approximations or assumptions made to get from Eq. 11 to Eq. 12 in the main text and briefly discuss the conditions under which they hold (e.g., large N_tr).

7.  Figure 1 could be improved for clarity. Panel (b) is largely redundant with the graphical model in panel (a). Consider integrating the schematic flow into panel (a) and perhaps simplifying the diagram by focusing on the path from the stimulus `s` to the response `r`, as other quantities are latent or derived.

8.  Line 342: The claim that subjects in a 2AFC task do not learn the experimental distribution of stimuli, unlike in an estimation task, is a strong empirical claim. Could you provide a citation or further justification for this assumption, as it directly influences the setup of the second case study?

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem with a novel and interesting methodology. While the specific implementation relies on a number of strong assumptions that may limit the generality of the findings, the proposed framework itself is a valuable contribution. I believe the paper is above the threshold for acceptance at NIPS, and it would be further strengthened if the authors could address the points raised above.
