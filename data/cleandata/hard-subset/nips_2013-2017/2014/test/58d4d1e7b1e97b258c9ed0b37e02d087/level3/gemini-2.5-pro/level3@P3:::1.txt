This paper presents a computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a class of models widely used to explain human perceptual behavior. The authors formalize a flexible, parametric observer model that encompasses sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages. The core of their contribution is a method to quantify how distinguishable two models are by calculating the expected log-likelihood of data generated by a "true" reference model. This metric, which is proportional to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the models' response distributions, allows the authors to explore the "identifiability landscape" and evaluate how different experimental designs can help resolve degeneracies among model parameters. The framework is applied to two case studies: recovering priors in a time interval estimation task and reconstructing the "slow-speed prior" in motion perception.

The problem of model non-identifiability is a long-standing and fundamental challenge in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, Psychological Review, 1978) and has been raised in recent, influential critiques of the Bayesian modeling enterprise (e.g., Jones & Love, BBS, 2011; Bowers & Davis, Psychological Bulletin, 2012). By providing a constructive, quantitative approach to this problem, the paper tackles a timely and significant issue for the NIPS community and computational cognitive science.

### Strengths

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical theoretical weakness of the "Bayesian brain" hypothesis. The proposed framework offers a principled way for researchers to move beyond post-hoc model fitting and proactively design experiments that can better constrain models and disentangle the contributions of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions. This is a significant conceptual and methodological advance.
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The proposed framework is laid out clearly, from the construction of the general observer model to the derivation of the identifiability metric. The application to two well-chosen case studies effectively demonstrates the utility of the approach and yields non-trivial insights (e.g., that encouraging a MAP-like loss function can, under their model's assumptions, paradoxically worsen the recovery of the prior).
*   **Originality:** The idea of using the expected KL divergence between response distributions as an *a priori* tool for experimental design is novel and powerful. It provides a formal basis for ranking the ability of different experimental setups to identify specific model components of interest.

### Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper has two major weaknesses that temper its overall contribution.

1.  **Insufficient Engagement with Prior Literature:** The paper frames the problem of model identifiability as if it were largely unaddressed by formal methods, stating "there has been no systematic analysis... nor a framework to perform such study a priori". This overlooks a substantial body of work in mathematical and cognitive psychology on model selection, complexity, and identifiability. For example, researchers have long used techniques based on Fisher information, posterior predictive distributions, and geometric analyses of model manifolds to assess model distinguishability (e.g., Pitt et al., Cognitive Psychology, 2002; Navarro et al., Cognitive Psychology, 2004). By not citing or discussing this extensive literature, the authors fail to situate their work appropriately. This makes it difficult to ascertain what is truly novel about their KL-divergence-based approach compared to existing methods and what its specific advantages might be.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Modeling Assumptions:** The framework's conclusions about the relative merits of different experimental designs are contingent on the specific parametric forms and parameter values chosen for the "reference observer". While the model is impressively flexible, several key assumptions appear arbitrary or are not sufficiently justified.
    *   The choice to represent the measurement noise (`p_meas` in Eq. 3) as a two-component Gaussian mixture is justified by a "maximum-entropy approach," but the rationale for fixing the first four moments and the implications of this specific choice are not fully explored.
    *   The parameters for the "reference observer" in the case studies are determined from "pilot experiments, the literature, or educated guesses." It is unclear how robust the paper's main conclusions—for instance, the ranking of experimental designs in Figure 2—are to these specific choices. A different, equally plausible reference observer might lead to a different ranking. Without a sensitivity analysis or a more robust defense of these assumptions, the specific results risk being brittle.

### Recommendation

This is a promising paper that tackles an important problem with a novel approach. However, its contribution is significantly undermined by the weaknesses noted above. I would recommend acceptance, but only if the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address these points. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Add a section that thoroughly discusses the relationship between their proposed framework and the existing literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science (e.g., the work of Myung, Pitt, Navarro, and others). This is essential for clarifying the paper's unique contribution.
2.  Provide stronger justification for the specific modeling choices made, particularly the functional forms for the likelihood and loss. A discussion of how sensitive the conclusions are to the assumptions about the "reference observer" would greatly strengthen the paper's claims about improving experimental design.

### Minor Comments

*   **Line 53:** The "trivial mathematical fact" should be briefly explained for clarity. Not all readers will immediately see why the optimal estimate is invariant to the transformations described.
*   **Line 73:** There appears to be a typo. The second "nor" should likely be "or" ("...effectiveness, or a framework...").
*   **Figure 2:** Please clarify in the caption what the star and the dashed lines denote (e.g., star = true parameter value of the reference observer; dashed lines = 95% credible interval of the posterior samples).

### Summary Judgment

This is a well-written paper that presents a valuable and original framework for addressing the critical problem of non-identifiability in Bayesian models of cognition. However, its impact is currently limited by a failure to connect with significant prior work on model comparison and by a reliance on modeling assumptions that are not fully justified. If the authors can address these major concerns in a revision, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
