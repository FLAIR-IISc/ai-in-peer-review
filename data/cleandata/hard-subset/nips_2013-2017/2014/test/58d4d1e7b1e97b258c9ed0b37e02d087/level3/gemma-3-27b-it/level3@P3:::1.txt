This paper proposes a framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a longstanding issue in cognitive science (Anderson, 1978). The authors correctly highlight recent critiques (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012) emphasizing identifiability as a key limitation hindering the wider adoption of Bayesian frameworks. The paper offers a valuable contribution by systematically analyzing this problem, providing a methodology for a priori investigation of identifiability, and demonstrating its application to time interval estimation and motion perception. The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized, presenting a complex topic in a readily understandable manner.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness and comprehensive approach. The authors carefully outline the mathematical basis for non-identifiability and propose a practical method, utilizing KL divergence and MCMC sampling, to assess identifiability within a defined model space. The case studies, while specific, effectively demonstrate the framework’s applicability and potential for informing experimental design. The model itself, encompassing a wide range of perceptual tasks through a single parametrization, is a notable achievement.

However, the paper suffers from a significant omission: a lack of engagement with the broader literature on model comparison in cognitive science. While the authors acknowledge the issue of identifiability, they largely ignore existing methodologies developed to address it. Approaches like Model Evidence approximation (Pitt et al., 2002), Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC; Navarro et al., 2004), and related techniques have been used extensively for model selection and comparison in cognitive modeling for decades. Failing to contextualize the proposed framework within this established body of work obscures its novel contribution and potentially duplicates existing efforts. It’s unclear how the presented method differs significantly from these existing approaches in terms of computational cost, assumptions, or practical applicability.

A second major concern relates to the arbitrariness of certain modeling assumptions. The definition of *pmeas* in Eq. 3, for example, relies on choosing moments of the distribution. While the maximum entropy approach is justified, the specific choice of moments and the two-Gaussian mixture feels somewhat ad hoc and lacks strong justification. Similarly, the parameter choices in Eq. 12 (defining the reference observer) – while grounded in previous work – are presented without a thorough sensitivity analysis. It's unclear how sensitive the results are to variations in these assumptions and parameters. This lack of robustness analysis weakens the conclusions drawn from the case studies. Specifically, the claim that the slow-speed prior inference is “relatively reliable” needs to be qualified with a discussion of how the results change under different assumptions about the noise characteristics or the loss function.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly address the existing literature on model comparison in cognitive science. A section comparing and contrasting the proposed framework with techniques like BIC or approximate Bayesian computation would be invaluable. Furthermore, a more rigorous justification for the key modeling assumptions is needed, along with a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the findings to variations in these assumptions. Demonstrating how the framework performs under different parameter settings for the reference observer, and with alternative choices for the sensory measurement distribution, would greatly enhance the paper's credibility.

Minor comments include: Line 53 refers to a “trivial mathematical fact” without explanation. This should be briefly clarified for the reader. On Line 73, the second instance of “nor” should be changed to “or” for grammatical correctness. Finally, the meaning of the star and dashed lines in Figure 2 should be explicitly stated in the figure caption.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental and important theoretical issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
*   Presents a novel and well-defined framework for assessing identifiability.
*   Clear and concise writing, well-organized presentation.
*   Demonstrates practical applicability through compelling case studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Significant omission of relevant literature on model comparison in cognitive science.
*   Arbitrariness of some modeling assumptions and lack of sensitivity analysis.
*   Limited discussion of the framework’s limitations and potential biases.

**Summary judgment:**

This is a well-written and important paper that tackles a critical issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling. However, its impact is diminished by the lack of engagement with related work. Addressing this gap, along with providing more robust justification for the modeling assumptions, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its contribution to the field. Therefore, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined above.
