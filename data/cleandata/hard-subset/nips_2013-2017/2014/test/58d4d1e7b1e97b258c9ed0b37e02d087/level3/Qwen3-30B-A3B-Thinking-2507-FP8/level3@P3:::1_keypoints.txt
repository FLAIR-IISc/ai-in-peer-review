- Proposes a systematic framework to test identifiability of parameters in Bayesian observer models and formalizes the degeneracy problem.
- Extends Bayesian Decision Theory by introducing constraints and an a priori method to assess identifiability for experimental design.
- Demonstrates framework utility on two perceptual tasks (time interval estimation and speed perception), revealing non-intuitive design effects.
- Shows that measuring motor parameters can substantially improve identifiability in time estimation.
- Framework is flexible: supports different loss functions and sensory noise models, making it broadly applicable.
- Paper is well-written with clear theory and practical case studies that illustrate the approach.
- Major concern (originality): Fails to situate contribution within prior Bayesian model comparison literature (e.g., Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004), leaving novelty unclear.
- Major concern (quality): Key modeling assumptions (e.g., two-component Gaussian mixture p_meas in Eq.3 and parameter choices in Eq.12 like σ`=0.5) lack justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Lacks explicit differentiation between a priori identifiability analysis and existing posterior model comparison techniques (e.g., Bayes factors).
- Technical approach is sound (appropriate use of KL divergence), but weakened by unjustified assumptions and missing literature engagement.
- Clarity is generally good, but explanations for key modeling choices are missing and reduce clarity.
- Originality is mixed: framework is novel but tackles a long-recognized problem and needs clearer differentiation from prior work.
- Significance is high if contextualized and justified; framework could improve experimental design in perception research.
- Minor issues: explain the "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53, correct "nor" to "or" at Line 73, and clarify star/dashed lines in Figure 2 legend.
- Recommendation: Accept with major revision — require engagement with prior model comparison literature, theoretical justification or sensitivity analyses for key assumptions (Eq.3, Eq.12), and correction of minor issues.
