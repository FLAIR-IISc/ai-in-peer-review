## A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception

This manuscript addresses the crucial issue of model identifiability within the context of Bayesian models of perception, a topic of increasing importance as these models are used to infer internal representations from behavioral data. The paper proposes a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability and demonstrates its application to time interval estimation and speed perception tasks. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, and the methodology presented appears promising and is potentially a valuable contribution to the field, putting it above the threshold for NIPS.

The core idea is to quantify the practical non-identifiability of two observer models based on their ability to generate similar response probability densities. The authors develop a continuous class of observer models and then leverage computational techniques, including KL divergence and MCMC sampling, to explore the identifiability landscape. This approach is a significant step towards a more rigorous assessment of Bayesian modeling in perception.

However, the strength of the conclusions is tempered by the substantial number of assumptions baked into the model. While acknowledging constraints is inherent to model building, the sheer length of the assumptions list—detailed throughout Section 2—reduces the overall impact of the results. The manuscript would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the findings to these assumptions. A crucial question is to what extent the observed identifiability (or lack thereof) is a property of the perceptual phenomena being modeled versus a consequence of the chosen model structure.

Section 4.1, focused on time interval estimation, presents findings regarding the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise. This observation, while not entirely unexpected given the Gaussian nature of these distributions within the model, lacks a clear demonstration of statistical reliability. Reporting confidence intervals or p-values for the observed differences would significantly strengthen this claim.

In Section 4.2, examining speed perception, a critical test of the framework's utility is missing. The authors should explicitly demonstrate that the model can *recover the absence* of a slow-speed prior when the reference observer genuinely has a flat prior. Establishing this capability is essential to validate the method’s ability to correctly identify a lack of prior expectation, and not simply confirm recovery when a prior exists.

Several points regarding clarity and methodological details require attention. On line 106, the justification for modeling a continuous stimulus distribution with a discrete one should be clarified. Is this a reflection of experimental design limitations or a simplifying assumption made for analytical tractability? Line 134 introduces a logarithmic stimulus transform, a strong constraint that potentially heavily influences identifiability. A discussion of how the results might change with alternative transformations would be valuable.

Regarding the likelihood estimation (line 143), it is unclear whether the imposed constraints and entropy maximization guarantee unimodality of the fitted distributions. The possibility of bimodality, and its potential impact on the results, should be addressed. Line 158 raises questions about the rationale for favoring a lattice-based representation of the prior over a uniform distribution and the justification for setting the prior wider than the true stimulus range.

Figure 1 is currently confusing. Integrating panel b into panel a and removing the derived *p<sub>est</sub>(s<sup>*</sup>|x)* would significantly improve its clarity. 

The approximations used in lines 252-253 (Stirling’s approximation, *E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]*) require justification. The conditions under which these approximations hold, and the potential impact of violating those conditions on the results, should be discussed. Equation 15 appears justified primarily by its intuitive outcome but needs more formal backing.

On line 262, the term “unnormalized density” is used without clear specification. The authors need to explicitly state which distribution is being sampled from.

Finally, line 365 asserts that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks. This claim requires further clarification, as prior expectations can demonstrably influence performance even in such paradigms. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel and potentially valuable framework for assessing model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception.
*   Methodologically sound, employing relevant techniques like KL divergence and MCMC sampling.
*   Demonstrates application to two established perceptual tasks (time interval estimation and speed perception).
*   The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a large number of assumptions limits the generalizability and impact of the findings.
*   Lack of statistical rigor in Section 4.1.
*   Missing validation demonstrating recovery of the *absence* of a prior in Section 4.2.
*   Several points of clarification needed regarding methodological details and justifications for key assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but requires significant revisions to address the concerns outlined above. Addressing the issues related to assumptions, statistical rigor, and methodological clarity would substantially strengthen the manuscript and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
