

This paper presents a systematic framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks, addressing a critical gap in the field. The authors formalize a flexible Bayesian model class with explicit equations for sensory noise (via stimulus-dependent mapping), likelihood, prior, loss, and response distribution, enabling tractable inference. The core contribution lies in a novel method to a priori assess parameter identifiability via KL divergence-based expected posterior analysis, demonstrated through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception. The framework reveals practical insights for experimental design—e.g., that imposing a narrow loss function can *hinder* prior recovery in timing tasks, while decoupling motor noise measurements improves identifiability. The mathematical elegance and clear application to real psychophysical problems are significant strengths.

The work builds substantially on Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), which already explored identifiability in Bayesian perception models. While the authors’ systematic testing approach is valuable, the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. Crucially, the identifiability claims rely on strict model assumptions (e.g., fixed loss function form), and the framework does not resolve practical confounds between sensory, decision, and response noise—limiting its immediate applicability. The paper also inadequately contextualizes its relationship to prior work; for instance, Acerbi’s 2014 *Neural Computation* paper directly addresses similar identifiability challenges in sensorimotor tasks.

To strengthen the paper, the authors must: (1) Add a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models (including Acerbi 2012/2014, Maloney 2010, etc.) and explicitly state how this work advances them; (2) Discuss the practical limitations of their identifiability claims (e.g., noise confounds) in a new subsection. These revisions would clarify the novelty and scope of the contribution.

The paper excels in technical quality (sound derivations, clear case studies) and significance (provides a tool to improve experimental design in perception research). While the incremental nature is a concern, the framework’s practical utility and mathematical rigor make it a solid contribution. With the requested revisions, it would be a strong fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
