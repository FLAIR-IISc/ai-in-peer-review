- Proposes a framework for a priori identifiability testing of Bayesian observer models to address degeneracy in parameter recovery.  
- Uses KL-divergence (Eq. 12) as a principled metric to quantify expected support for different parameter configurations.  
- Demonstrates practical application via two case studies: time-interval estimation (shows experimental design affects identifiability) and speed perception (recovers slow-speed priors).  
- Paper is clearly written and mathematical derivations are technically sound.  
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions (log-stimulus transform Eq. 2, lattice-based prior Eq. 4, prior width heuristic) undermines generalizability.  
- Authors do not justify the choice of log transform, lattice prior, or the claim that priors should be 50% wider than stimulus range.  
- Sensory vs. motor noise interchangeability is noted but statistical significance across parameter space is not reported.  
- Fails to show recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior, a critical missing validation test.  
- Unclear explanation for why learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks, contradicting prior work.  
- Several technical clarifications needed: discrete stimulus binning justification, entropy-maximization unimodality guarantee, validity of Stirling and E[x log x] approximations (Eqs. 14–15), and which unnormalized density is sampled.  
- Pros for acceptance: fills a methodological gap, useful for experimental design, supported by case studies, and technically solid in core methods.  
- Cons for acceptance: unjustified assumptions, missing key validation, and insufficient justification for several technical claims.  
- Overall assessment: technically valuable but limited by assumptions; recommend accept with minor revisions to address validation and clarify modeling choices.
