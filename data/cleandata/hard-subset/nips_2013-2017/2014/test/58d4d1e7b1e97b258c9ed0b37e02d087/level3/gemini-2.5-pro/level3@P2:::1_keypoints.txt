- Presents a computational framework to test a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models for 1D stimulus estimation.  
- Constructs a flexible parametric observer model allowing stimulus-dependent noise, non-Gaussian likelihoods/priors, and varied loss functions.  
- Proposes mapping the "identifiability landscape" by sampling an approximate expected posterior based on KL divergence between response distributions.  
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies: time-interval estimation (ranks experimental designs) and speed perception (slow-speed prior inference appears robust).  

- Strength: tackles a fundamental, timely problem in cognitive and computational neuroscience with clear practical value for experimental design.  
- Strength: paper is very clear, well-organized, and communicates core ideas accessibly.  
- Strength: model class scope is broad and applications show potential generality across perceptual domains.  

- Weakness: insufficient engagement with substantial prior literature on model identifiability, parameter recoverability, and model comparison methods.  
- Weakness: originality unclear because the expected-likelihood-based metric is not clearly differentiated from existing approaches (e.g., parameter recovery, posterior predictive checks, marginal likelihoods).  
- Weakness: key modeling assumptions (e.g., representing likelihoods and loss functions as two-Gaussian mixtures with a local max-entropy solution) are weakly justified and appear arbitrary.  
- Weakness: lack of sensitivity analyses—conclusions may depend strongly on chosen functional forms and constraints.  
- Weakness: framework may resolve degeneracy only by imposing restrictive parametric constraints rather than demonstrating general identifiability.  

- For acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers a priori experimental-design tool, and is exceptionally well-written.  
- Against acceptance: fails to situate itself within existing identifiability literature and relies on inadequately justified modeling assumptions, weakening technical soundness.  

- Detailed comments: explain the "trivial mathematical" degeneracy more explicitly (show cancellation of phi_i(s) terms).
