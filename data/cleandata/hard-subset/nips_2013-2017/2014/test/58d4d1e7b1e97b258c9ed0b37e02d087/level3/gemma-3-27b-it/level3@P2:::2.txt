This manuscript addresses the important and often overlooked problem of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing whether parameters of these models can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, with practical applications for improving experimental design. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, and the methodology presented appears sound and above the threshold for NIPS, given its methodological contribution. However, the heavy reliance on strong assumptions, and the somewhat limited impact of some key results, temper the overall significance.

The core idea – to assess a priori identifiability by exploring the parameter space and quantifying the overlap between models – is valuable. The authors build a flexible Bayesian observer model encompassing a range of psychophysical tasks and introduce a practical method based on Kullback-Leibler divergence to evaluate identifiability. The application to time interval estimation and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework for systematic testing of identifiability is a significant contribution, particularly given the widespread use of Bayesian models in perception.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly. The mathematical derivations appear correct.
*   **Methodological Rigor:** The use of KL divergence and MCMC sampling provides a principled approach to assess identifiability.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The framework directly addresses a challenge faced by researchers fitting Bayesian models to data and offers a means to improve experimental design.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Strong Assumptions:** The model relies on a substantial number of assumptions (e.g., specific functional forms for priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and the sensory mapping). While some are motivated, the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated. This limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Limited Impact of Results (Section 4.1):** The finding that sensory and motor noise are often interchangeable is not particularly surprising. The authors should clarify which specific differences in experimental conditions lead to statistically reliable improvements in parameter recovery. Reporting confidence intervals or statistical significance tests would strengthen these conclusions.
*   **Lack of Demonstration (Section 4.2):** The authors must demonstrate that their model can actually recover the *absence* of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer. Showing that the model performs well when the prior *is* present is not sufficient. This is crucial to validate the framework’s ability to detect non-identifiability.
*   **Technical Details & Clarifications:** Several points require further clarification:
    *   Line 106: The motivation for modeling a continuous stimulus distribution with a discrete approximation should be explained more thoroughly. Is this solely an artifact of the experimental design?
    *   Line 134: The log-stimulus transform (f(s)) represents a strong constraint. The authors should discuss how sensitive the results are to this specific representational assumption.
    *   Line 143: It’s unclear whether the constraints and entropy maximization used to define the likelihood guarantee unimodality, or whether bimodality is possible.
    *   Line 158: The choice of a lattice prior and the justification for making it wider than the true stimulus range are not entirely convincing.
    *   Lines 252-254: The approximations used (Stirling’s approximation, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) need justification, including a discussion of the conditions under which they are valid and the impact of violating those conditions for the specific N values used. Eq. 15 appears justified more by intuitive outcome than rigorous derivation.
    *   Line 262: The "unnormalized density" being sampled from needs to be precisely specified.
    *   Line 365: The assertion that learning priors doesn’t matter in 2AFC tasks is questionable. Clarification is needed.

*   **Figure 1:** The figure is somewhat confusing. Integrating panel (b) into (a) and removing the derived `p_est(s^*|x)` would improve clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a critical issue in cognitive modeling—model identifiability—that is often ignored.
*   The proposed framework provides a novel and principled methodology for assessing identifiability.
*   The application to two well-studied perceptual tasks demonstrates the practical relevance of the framework.
*   The clarity of writing and organization are commendable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions underlying the model limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   The impact of the results in Section 4.1 is somewhat limited, and statistical validation is missing.
*   The crucial demonstration of recovering the *absence* of a prior (Section 4.2) is lacking.
*   Several technical details require clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the assumptions, statistical validation, and the demonstration of recovering the absence of a prior, would significantly strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised. The authors should prioritize clarifying the assumptions and providing stronger statistical support for their conclusions.
