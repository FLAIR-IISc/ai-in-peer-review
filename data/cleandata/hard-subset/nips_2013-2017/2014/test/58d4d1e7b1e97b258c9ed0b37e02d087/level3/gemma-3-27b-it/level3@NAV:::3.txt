## Review of “A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception”

This paper presents a framework for systematically analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a critical issue given their widespread use in perceptual modeling. The authors develop a flexible Bayesian model incorporating sensory noise, prior distributions, loss functions, and response distributions, and provide equations for each component. A key strength is the emphasis on mathematical identifiability, moving beyond simply fitting models to data and directly addressing the inherent degeneracy in the Bayesian framework. The work is mathematically elegant and offers a generalized approach for formalizing perceptual estimation – a valuable contribution to the field.

The model's flexibility and the explicit formulation of each component are commendable, allowing for a nuanced investigation of parameter recovery. The authors demonstrate their framework through two case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception – which showcase its practical application.  The analysis of the time interval estimation task reveals how experimental design impacts identifiability, specifically highlighting the detrimental effect of imposing a narrow loss function. The validation of existing findings in speed perception further bolsters confidence in the framework’s utility. 

However, the contribution feels incremental. While well-executed, the core issue of identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models is not entirely new.  Work by Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), for example, directly tackles the problem of degeneracy and proposes constraints to address it, echoing themes present in this paper. The authors would significantly strengthen their work by including a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models (specifically addressing work on identifiability and parameter recovery) and clearly articulating how their framework advances these existing approaches. 

Furthermore, the authors’ claim of identifiability hinges on specific model assumptions. The distinction between sensory, decision, and response noise, which is practically difficult to disentangle, is glossed over. The paper would benefit from a discussion of this practical limitation. While mathematical identifiability is crucial, acknowledging the practical challenges in separating these noise sources would add a layer of realism and nuance. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Mathematically sound and well-developed framework.
*   Addresses a critical issue (identifiability) in Bayesian perceptual modeling.
*   Demonstrated through relevant and insightful case studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution, lacking a comprehensive comparison to related work (e.g., Acerbi et al.).
*   Limited discussion of practical identifiability limitations.



**Recommendation:**  Accept with minor revisions. The authors should include a more thorough review of related work and a discussion of the practical limitations of their identifiability claims. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
