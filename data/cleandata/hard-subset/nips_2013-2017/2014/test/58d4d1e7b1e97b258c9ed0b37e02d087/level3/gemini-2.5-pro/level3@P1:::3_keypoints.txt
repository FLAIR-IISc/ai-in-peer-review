- Presents a framework to test a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks.  
- Addresses the problem of model degeneracy where different priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavior.  
- Constructs a flexible, tractable four-stage observer model (sensation, perception, decision, response) with mixture-model parameterizations and sensory mapping.  
- Derives an analytical expression for expected loss and uses expected log-likelihood / KL divergence to map the identifiability landscape.  
- Enables researchers to assess which parameters can be reliably recovered before running experiments.  
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies: time-interval estimation (showing design-dependent prior recovery) and speed perception (validating a slow-speed prior).  
- Significant and timely contribution: provides a practical tool to improve experimental design and robustness of inferences in computational cognitive science.  
- Strong technical quality: mathematically elegant, general formalization balancing flexibility and tractability, with crucial analytical efficiency.  
- Clear and well-organized presentation with systematic model description and effective case studies.  
- Practical insights: shows counterintuitive findings (e.g., narrow loss functions can worsen prior recovery) that guide experiment design.  
- Originality concern: core modeling framework is incremental and closely related to prior work by the authors (Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014); novelty mainly in applying it to a priori identifiability.  
- Scope limitation: identifiability claims hold only within the assumed parametric model class and cannot fully resolve fundamental degeneracies without strong structural assumptions.  
- Recommendation to add explicit comparisons to prior Bayesian perceptual models and clarify how this work extends them.  
- Suggestion to discuss and qualify the distinction between theoretical identifiability within a model class and practical conflation of noise/bias sources in real experiments.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound and valuable for NIPS acceptance if the authors clarify relation to prior work and temper identifiability claims.
