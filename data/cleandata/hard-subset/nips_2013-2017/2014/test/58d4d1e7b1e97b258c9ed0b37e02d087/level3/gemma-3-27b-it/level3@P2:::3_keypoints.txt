- Presents a novel framework for testing a priori identifiability of Bayesian perceptual models, applied to time-interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Targets the critical problem of model degeneracy where different priors/likelihoods/losses produce identical behavioral predictions.  
- Proposes a systematic, principled alternative to ad-hoc constraints for assessing identifiability.  
- Builds on and acknowledges extensive prior Bayesian-perception literature and the shift toward treating Bayesian observers as process models.  
- Correctly highlights earlier non-identifiability findings and aims to provide a principled remedy.  
- Strength: mathematically rigorous, general, and analytically tractable Bayesian observer formulation parameterized by θ.  
- Strength: explicit formulation of sensation, perception, decision, and response stages with clear equations.  
- Strength: sensible use of maximum entropy to define distributions under limited information.  
- Strength: framework enables comparison of identifiability across experimental designs and can guide optimized experiments.  
- Demonstrates usefulness with a time-interval estimation case study and validates with speed perception results consistent with past findings.  
- Weakness: originality is somewhat incremental since core ideas (Bayesian model selection, identifiability, KL divergence) are well-established.  
- Weakness: insufficient engagement with recent Bayesian perceptual model work (notably Acerbi et al.) and lacks a dedicated comparative discussion.  
- Conceptual caveat: claimed “identifiability” is conditional on model assumptions (functional forms for priors, likelihoods, losses) and needs clearer nuance.  
- Practical limitation: framework does not fundamentally solve the empirical difficulty of disentangling sensory, decision, and response noise that co-vary in real data.  
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on revisions — add comprehensive related-work comparison (especially to Acerbi et al.) and explicitly discuss identifiability limitations.
