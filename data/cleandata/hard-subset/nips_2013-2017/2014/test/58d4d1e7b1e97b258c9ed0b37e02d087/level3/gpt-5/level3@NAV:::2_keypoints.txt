- Proposes an a priori framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian observer models via expected log-likelihood differences and KL divergences.  
- Instantiates a flexible unified parametric observer (mixture likelihoods/loss, lattice Gaussian-mixture priors, stimulus transform) and applies it to interval timing and speed perception.  
- Contribution is timely and useful for experimental design and diagnosing model degeneracies.  
- Methodology is interesting and generally sound but depends on several strong assumptions that need tighter justification and ablations.  
- The KL-based derivation is elegant, but approximations leading to Eqs. 14–15 require explicit conditions and error bounds.  
- Manuscript is clearly written and well organized, but some technical choices are under-specified and Figure 1 is confusing.  
- Originality is high: mapping identifiability landscapes via expected evidence is novel and the unified parameterization is a useful synthesis.  
- Significance is strong if the framework is strengthened with additional validation and statistical reporting.  
- Clarify why continuous stimuli are modeled as discrete and provide convergence guidance as Nexp increases (L106).  
- Test sensitivity of identifiability to the stimulus transform f(s) (e.g., power-law vs log) because the log-transform is a strong constraint (L134).  
- State conditions under which entropy-maximization constraints guarantee unimodality and whether bimodality can occur (L143).  
- Justify the lattice prior representation versus a uniform basis, explain the 50% wider range choice, and guidance for setting priors beyond stimulus support (L158).  
- Simplify Figure 1 by integrating panels and removing derived pest(s*|x) to reduce cognitive load.  
- Report statistical reliability (credible intervals/effect sizes) and quantify degeneracy, especially regarding interchangeability of sensory and motor noise (Sec. 4.1).  
- Demonstrate recovery of a flat (no slow-speed) prior in the main text with power analysis, not only in the supplement (Sec. 4.2).  
- Provide derivations and conditions (N, variance constraints) for Stirling and the E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] approximations and quantify error (L252/Eqs. 14–15).  
- Specify precisely which unnormalized density is sampled and the MCMC target (L262).  
- Clarify why learning priors would not matter in 2AFC and reconcile with evidence for rapid prior learning (L365).  
- Pros: clear problem, general framework, thoughtful case studies, actionable design insights.  
- Cons: strong assumptions with limited validation, missing statistical reliability reporting, and under-justified derivations/specifications.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on addressing clarifications and adding the flat-prior recovery demonstration.
