- Paper presents a formal framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science.
- Proposes a general four-stage perceptual estimation model: sensory noise, internal prior/likelihood, loss function, and response noise.
- Model combines representational flexibility (stimulus-dependent noise, nonparametric priors) with computational tractability for efficient inference.
- Main contribution: method to quantify practical identifiability between a reference and alternative models via expected log-likelihood/KL divergence of response distributions.
- Demonstrated with two case studies: effects of experimental design on temporal-prior recovery and robustness of inferring a slow-speed visual prior.
- Builds on established Bayesian decision-theory literature and directly addresses model degeneracy issues in perception.
- Strength: significant and technically sound tool that helps assess recoverability of internal representations before data collection.
- Strength: clear, well-structured exposition with explicit mathematical details aiding reproducibility.
- Strength: impressively general model class capturing Weber-Fechner/Stevens mappings, non-Gaussian noise, flexible priors, and asymmetric loss.
- Weakness: originality is potentially incremental—framework resembles recent Acerbi et al. work and needs clearer positioning versus that prior art.
- Suggestion: add a dedicated related-work section or expanded introduction explicitly stating what is novel about this framework.
- Weakness: identifiability claims are conditional on the model class assumptions and cannot resolve ambiguities arising from different true generative structures.
- Suggestion: add a discussion paragraph acknowledging limitations of identifiability analysis conditional on assumed noise/response structures.
- Recommendation: accept at NIPS, contingent on revisions to clarify novelty relative to recent work and to temper identifiability claims with explicit assumptions.
