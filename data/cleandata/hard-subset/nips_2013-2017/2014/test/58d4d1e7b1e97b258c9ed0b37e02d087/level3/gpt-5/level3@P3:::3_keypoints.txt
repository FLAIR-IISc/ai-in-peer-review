- Proposes a general, explicit Bayesian observer model for 1D estimation and an identifiability framework based on expected log-likelihood / KL-weighted divergences.  
- Model specifies stimulus-dependent sensory noise, monotonic measurement transform f(s), Gaussian-mixture priors, inverted-Gaussian-mixture loss, lapses, and motor noise, yielding analytic response distributions.  
- Applies framework to two case studies: interval timing (shows poor prior recovery and motor–sensory confounds) and speed perception (robust recovery of a slow-speed prior).  
- Situates work within Bayesian perceptual modeling and identifiability literature (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.).  
- Strength: clear, explicit formalization with equations for likelihood, prior, loss, lapse, and response noise enabling efficient computation.  
- Strength: flexible mixture-based likelihoods capture stimulus-dependent and non-Gaussian noise while remaining computationally tractable for MCMC.  
- Strength: interpretable identifiability metric (expected log-evidence = KL-weighted sum) that scales with trial number and can guide design.  
- Strength: actionable insights from case studies revealing when priors are recoverable and highlighting motor–sensory confounds.  
- Strength: general parameterization that can cover many 1D tasks and potentially extend to angles, multi-cue, or discrete-choice settings.  
- Weakness: largely incremental—many ingredients mirror prior work; novelty mainly in packaging and KL-based objective.  
- Weakness: identifiability claims are conditional on strong parametric assumptions (mixture forms, unimodality, shared params) and do not resolve classic sensory/decision/response confounds.  
- Weakness: limited empirical validation—results are simulation-based with no new human data or prospective design optimization study.  
- Weakness: related-work coverage needs expansion and clearer positioning relative to recent recovery/identifiability and design literatures.  
- Quality: technically sound and internally consistent; KL derivation appropriate; more sensitivity analyses to misspecification would strengthen claims.  
- Clarity: generally clear with precise notation; recommend unifying notation, summarizing assumptions, adding schematic pipeline, and reporting MCMC/prior/computational details.  
- Originality: moderate—useful unification and identifiability emphasis but substantial overlap with earlier frameworks.  
- Significance: moderate-to-high relevance for Bayesian perception community and experimental design guidance.  
- Revision suggestions: add a dedicated related-work section, temper identifiability claims, and give practical experimental strategies to disentangle noise sources.  
- Revision suggestions: include sensitivity analyses to model misspecification and mixture order, illustrate Eq.12 as a design utility, and provide implementation details and code.  
- Pro-accept arguments: elegant, explicit, tractable framework with a clear identifiability criterion and informative case studies.  
- Against-accept arguments: incremental relative to recent work, identifiability depends on restrictive assumptions, and lacks empirical/robustness analyses.  
- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on addressing related-work positioning and practical identifiability limitations.
