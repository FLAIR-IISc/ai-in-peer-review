This paper presents a computational framework to systematically test the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly identify that the internal components of Bayesian models (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) are often degenerate, meaning multiple combinations can produce identical behavior, which poses a serious challenge to interpreting them as veridical representations of cognitive processes. To address this, the authors develop a flexible, parametric class of Bayesian observer models for one-dimensional stimulus estimation tasks. They then propose a method to map the "identifiability landscape" by sampling from an approximate expected posterior density, which is derived from the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of a "true" reference model and other candidate models. The utility of the framework is demonstrated through two case studies: one on time interval estimation, showing how the framework can rank different experimental designs, and another on speed perception, showing that the inference of a slow-speed prior is theoretically robust.

The problem of model identifiability is a long-standing and fundamental issue in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, Psychol Rev, 1978), and it has been raised as a major critique of the Bayesian modeling approach in particular (e.g., Jones & Love, BBS, 2011; Bowers & Davis, Psychol Bull, 2012). This paper tackles this important problem head-on, which is a commendable and timely contribution.

### Strengths

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical, unresolved issue at the heart of cognitive and computational neuroscience. The ability to assess whether an experimental design can, in principle, distinguish between different internal representations would be an invaluable tool for researchers. The promise of using this framework to improve experimental design *before* data collection is a significant practical contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, the four-stage observer model is explained methodically, and the case studies provide concrete illustrations of the framework's application. The core ideas are accessible and effectively communicated.
*   **Scope:** The proposed observer model is commendably flexible, encompassing stimulus-dependent noise, non-Gaussian likelihoods and priors, and a range of loss functions. The application to two different, well-known perceptual domains demonstrates the potential breadth of the framework.

### Weaknesses

*   **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** My primary concern is that the paper does not adequately situate its contribution within the extensive existing literature on model identifiability and comparison in cognitive science and statistics. The authors state that there has been "no systematic analysis – neither empirical nor theoretical," which overlooks a significant body of work. For instance, researchers have long developed and applied methods for assessing model mimicry and parameter recoverability (e.g., Pitt et al., Cog Psych, 2002; Navarro et al., J Math Psych, 2004). While the specific application to Bayesian observer models of perception is valuable, the paper's core methodological contribution (using an expected likelihood-based metric) is not clearly differentiated from previous approaches. A thorough discussion comparing this framework to existing methods (e.g., parameter recovery simulations, posterior predictive checks, marginal likelihood estimation) is necessary to clarify the novelty of the proposed technique.
*   **Quality and Justification of Assumptions:** The framework's power rests on a series of modeling assumptions, several of which appear arbitrary and are insufficiently justified. For instance, the choice to represent likelihoods (Eq. 3) and loss functions (Eq. 6) as mixtures of two Gaussians, with free parameters determined by a "local maximum-entropy" solution, is not well motivated. Why this specific form? How sensitive are the conclusions of the case studies to this choice? If a different functional form were used, would the identifiability landscape change dramatically? The paper's conclusions about which experimental designs are better depend entirely on the chosen model class. Without a stronger justification for these choices or a sensitivity analysis, the generality and robustness of the framework's findings are questionable. The paper argues for a framework to overcome the "ill-posed" inverse problem, but it appears to do so by introducing a new set of strong, unvalidated assumptions.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  The paper tackles a fundamentally important and timely problem for the field of computational modeling.
2.  The goal of providing an *a priori* tool for improving experimental design is highly significant and could have a substantial impact.
3.  The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

### Arguments Against Acceptance
1.  The manuscript fails to engage with a substantial body of prior literature on model identifiability, making it difficult to assess the originality of the proposed method.
2.  The technical soundness of the approach is weakened by several key modeling assumptions that are not adequately justified, potentially limiting the generality of the conclusions.

### Detailed Comments
*   Line 53: The authors refer to the degeneracy of the Bayesian model as a "trivial mathematical fact." It would be helpful to briefly explain why, perhaps by showing how the `phi_i(s)` terms cancel out in the posterior or the expectation, to make the paper more self-contained.
*   Line 73: "nor a framework to perform such study a priori, before running an experiment." The second "nor" should probably be "or".
*   Figure 2: The caption should explicitly state what the stars and dashed lines represent (presumably the parameters/prior of the reference observer `theta*`).
*   The authors should more explicitly discuss the role of the constraints they introduce. The point of the paper is to show how to test identifiability, but much of the work is done by imposing a restrictive (though flexible) parametric model class. A more direct discussion of how these constraints help resolve degeneracy would strengthen the paper's message.

In summary, this is a well-written paper on a very important topic. However, its contribution is undermined by a lack of engagement with relevant prior work and insufficient justification for its core modeling assumptions. If the authors can address these two major points, particularly by situating their work within the broader context of model selection literature and by motivating their modeling choices more thoroughly, this could be a strong contribution to the field.
