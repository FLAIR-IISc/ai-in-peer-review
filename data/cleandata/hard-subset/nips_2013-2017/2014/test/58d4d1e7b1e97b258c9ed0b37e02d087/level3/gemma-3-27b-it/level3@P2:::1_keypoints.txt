- Addresses identifiability of internal representations from behavioral data in Bayesian perceptual models, highlighting inherent degeneracy of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions.
- Proposes a novel framework to test identifiability a priori and to evaluate experimental designs for resolving degeneracies.
- Builds on Bayesian Decision Theory and prior work treating BDT as a process model for mental representations.
- Demonstrates the framework with two perceptual case studies: time-interval estimation and speed perception.
- Clear mathematical formulation and a flexible observer model enable systematic exploration of the identifiability landscape.
- Case studies effectively illustrate practical use and reveal counterintuitive results (e.g., narrow loss functions can be detrimental).
- Major weakness: insufficient engagement with substantial prior literature on model comparison and identifiability methods (e.g., Pitt et al., Navarro et al.).
- Claims of novelty are overstated without explicit differentiation from existing model-comparison toolkits.
- Several modeling choices (functional form of p_meas, specific parameter values) lack clear justification.
- Authors do not provide sensitivity analyses showing how assumptions or parameter priors affect conclusions.
- Robustness of results to variations in prior distributions over parameters is unclear and unaddressed.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem, offers a practical a priori identifiability method, shows applicable case studies, and is mathematically rigorous.
- Arguments against acceptance: overlooks prior work, contains arbitrary/unjustified modeling assumptions, and lacks robustness analysis.
- Minor comments: clarify “trivial mathematical fact” (Line 53); change “nor” to “or” (Line 73); explain star and dashed lines in Figure 2.
- Overall assessment: important and well-written but needs revision—acceptance recommended contingent on justifying constraints/assumptions and situating the framework within existing model-comparison literature.
