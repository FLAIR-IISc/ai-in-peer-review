This paper proposes a novel framework for systematically testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, accompanied by a flexible and computationally efficient observer model. The authors correctly identify a critical issue in the field: the degeneracy inherent in Bayesian inference, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavioral predictions. They address this by presenting a framework to *a priori* investigate identifiability and, crucially, to leverage this analysis for improved experimental design. The work is grounded in a rigorous mathematical formulation, with explicit equations provided for each stage of the perceptual process – sensation, perception, decision-making, and response. The authors demonstrate the framework's utility through two well-chosen case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception, drawing connections to established findings in these domains.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work employing Bayesian decision theory to model human perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3]). It appropriately acknowledges the success of these models in explaining a wide range of behavioral data and the increasing interpretation of BDT as a process model reflecting underlying neural representations [4, 5, 6]. The authors correctly point out the inherent non-uniqueness in inverting these models to recover these representations [17, 18, 19], a problem that has spurred research into constraints and regularization techniques [12, 19].  This paper goes a step further by offering a systematic way to analyze *how* those constraints impact identifiability, rather than simply applying them.  The proposed model extends prior work [12, 14] by achieving a balance between flexibility – capable of representing a large class of psychophysical tasks – and computational efficiency, vital for exploring the complex model space.

The strengths of this work are numerous. The mathematical clarity and completeness of the model are commendable. The framework’s potential to inform experimental design is a valuable contribution, and the case studies provide concrete examples of its application. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the challenges associated with parameter estimation in Bayesian models. The inclusion of lapse rates and noise in the response stage adds realism to the model.  Furthermore, the analysis of the impact of loss function assumptions in the time interval estimation task is particularly insightful.

However, the paper's contribution feels somewhat incremental. While the framework is well-presented and the mathematical treatment is rigorous, it's important to contextualize it more thoroughly within the existing literature.  Recent work, particularly by Acerbi et al. (e.g., [16, 15]), has already explored the identifiability and limitations of Bayesian models of perception, and the connection to these prior efforts isn’t sufficiently highlighted.  Specifically, the analyses of model degeneracy and the need for experimental constraints overlap significantly with their findings.  To strengthen the paper, the authors should include a dedicated section reviewing relevant recent work in Bayesian perceptual modeling and clearly articulate how their framework advances the field beyond these existing approaches.  What novel tools or insights does this work offer that were not previously available?

A further point to consider is the practical limitations of identifiability. While the framework provides a theoretical analysis of identifiability under specific model assumptions, it doesn't fully address the challenges of distinguishing between different sources of noise (sensory, decisional, motor) in real-world data. The identifiability claims hinge on the assumptions made within the model itself – particularly regarding the form of the likelihood, prior, and loss function. In practice, these assumptions are often untestable and can significantly influence the results.  The authors should acknowledge this practical limitation in the revised manuscript, discussing how real-world complexities might impact the identifiability of model parameters, even when the framework predicts theoretical identifiability. This discussion should also cover the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental issue in Bayesian modeling of perception (identifiability).
*   The framework provides a systematic approach to analyze identifiability *a priori*.
*   The mathematical formulation is clear and complete.
*   The case studies demonstrate the framework’s practical utility.
*   The potential for improved experimental design is valuable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution feels incremental relative to recent work (e.g., Acerbi et al.).
*   The discussion of practical limitations of identifiability is limited.
*   Insufficient contextualization within the existing literature.



**Overall:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper, contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding its novelty and the discussion of practical limitations. Specifically, the authors should add a section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clarify how their work builds upon and extends these efforts. They should also expand the discussion of the challenges in disentangling different sources of noise and the sensitivity of the results to model assumptions.  Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and elevate its contribution to the field.
