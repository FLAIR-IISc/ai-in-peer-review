

This paper addresses a critical issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling: the identifiability of internal representations. The authors propose a systematic framework for testing identifiability in Bayesian observer models, which is particularly important given longstanding concerns about model degeneracy (Anderson 1978) and recent critiques highlighting identifiability as a major limitation (Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012). The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and makes a theoretically significant contribution by demonstrating how identifiability can be systematically evaluated a priori for experimental design. The application to time interval estimation and speed perception provides concrete evidence of the framework's utility.

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear articulation of the identifiability problem and its practical demonstration of how experimental design affects parameter recovery. The authors effectively show that certain experimental setups (e.g., measuring motor parameters) significantly improve identifiability, while others (e.g., enforcing narrow loss functions) can be detrimental. The methodological approach using KL divergence to quantify expected model support is theoretically sound and computationally efficient.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution. Most critically, it largely ignores substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science (Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004), making the novelty of the proposed framework unclear. The authors fail to position their work within existing methodological discussions of model identifiability in cognitive science. Additionally, key modeling assumptions (e.g., the definition of p_meas in Equation 3 and parameter choices in Equation 12) appear arbitrary and insufficiently justified. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how sensitive the results are to these assumptions, which is particularly concerning given the central role they play in the identifiability analysis.

The minor comments are valid: the "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53 should be explained, the "nor" at Line 73 should be changed to "or," and Figure 2's legend should clarify the star and dashed lines. 

While the paper addresses an important theoretical issue and is well-written, its failure to engage with prior work on model comparison significantly limits its contribution. The framework would be substantially stronger if the authors clearly articulated how it differs from and improves upon existing approaches. The paper would benefit from a thorough discussion of the chosen constraints and assumptions, explaining why they are reasonable and how results might change with different choices.
