This paper proposes a principled, a priori framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors introduce a flexible one-dimensional estimation model that parameterizes sensation (stimulus-to-measurement mapping and noise), prior, loss, lapses, and response noise using mixtures of Gaussians and a parametric stimulus transform. They derive an expected log-evidence criterion that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions, and use MCMC over an “expected posterior” to map practical identifiability under candidate experimental designs. Two case studies illustrate the approach: (i) time-interval reproduction (Jazayeri & Shadlen-style task), where they compare designs and show strong degeneracies (notably trade-offs between sensory and motor noise and sensitivity to assumed loss); and (ii) speed perception with a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli), where they find the prior exponent and noise parameters are more robustly recoverable across contrasts, even when decoupling internal from external noise.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on Bayesian observer modeling (Geisler; Knill & Richards; Körding & Wolpert), empirical reverse-engineering of priors/likelihoods/losses (Girshick et al.; Stocker & Simoncelli; Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.), and model distinguishability (Navarro, Pitt & Myung). Its contribution is to turn identifiability into a design-time, computationally efficient exercise by leveraging analytic structure (Gaussian mixtures, transformed spaces) and an expected-KL objective over response distributions. This is timely given debates on degeneracy in Bayesian cognitive models (Jones & Love; Bowers & Davis).

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and emphasis on identifiability as a design question rather than a post hoc concern.
- Efficient, coherent modeling toolkit with consistent parameterizations across components enabling tractable expected-loss computations.
- Useful, interpretable identifiability metric (expected log-likelihood = KL sum) and practical sampling procedure for exploring sloppiness and parameter trade-offs.
- Case studies concretely demonstrate when and why designs succeed or fail; the interval-timing results provide nontrivial guidance (e.g., measuring motor noise can be crucial; imposing narrow losses can harm prior recovery).
- Well-situated in the literature; addresses common critiques of degeneracy with a constructive methodology.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Many strong modeling assumptions accumulate (fixed functional family for f(s); mixture-of-Gaussians for likelihood and loss; lattice prior; quasi-quadratic loss; specific lapse model), and main results hinge on these. Identifiability conclusions may change materially if representational choices are relaxed.
- Section 4.1: the interchangeability between sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; please report which condition/design differences are statistically reliable (e.g., credible intervals for symmetric KL to the true prior; posterior correlations between σ and ρ parameters across designs).
- Section 4.2: you note a “sanity check” with a flat prior in Supplementary Material. This is essential for validating recovery of “no prior.” Please move summary results to the main text (estimation error on kprior→0 or equivalent flatness metric, with CIs) and report performance under UNC as well.
- Line 106: clarify whether discretization reflects the experimental design (finite set of stimuli) or is an approximation to a continuous generative process. Include a sensitivity analysis to Nexp/bins (show convergence of identifiability as Nexp increases).
- Line 134: the log-like stimulus transform is a strong representational constraint. Identifiability may depend critically on fixing vs inferring f(s). Please (i) quantify sensitivity to misspecification of d and s0, and (ii) show identifiability differences when f(s) is learned vs fixed.
- Line 143: mixtures of Gaussians can be bimodal. Do your constraints plus entropy maximization guarantee unimodality? If so, provide the parameter inequalities or reparameterization that ensures a single mode; otherwise, state the conditions under which bimodality can occur and how you preclude it in inference.
- Line 158: justify the lattice-based prior representation versus alternatives (e.g., spline bases, log-normal mixtures with adaptive centers, or Dirichlet-process mixtures). Explain the rationale for setting the prior range to 50% wider than the stimulus range, and show sensitivity to this choice (boundary effects, leakage).
- Figure 1: the current split across panels is confusing. Consider integrating b into a; remove intermediate pest(s*|x) from the main diagram and keep it in methods. A single end-to-end generative plate diagram could improve clarity.
- Lines 252–265 (Equations 14–15): please provide a precise derivation with stated approximations: where Stirling is used, where E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x], and the residual error terms as functions of Ntr and Nexp. State the regime (e.g., Ntr Pi ≫ 1 for all i) where Eq. 15 is accurate, and provide an empirical check (simulation gap to exact expectation).
- Line 262: specify exactly which unnormalized density is sampled via MCMC. If E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp(⟨log P(D|θ)⟩), include the full expression with constants that do not depend on θ (and confirm whether tempering or priors over θ are used).
- Line 365: the claim that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC requires justification. Rapid learning of task statistics is documented (Chalk et al.; Gekas et al.). Either argue theoretically why the prior cancels (e.g., symmetric alternatives with matched noise) or include controls showing negligible effect on inferred parameters.

Quality
- Generally sound with clear derivations, but the expected-evidence approximation requires more rigorous justification and diagnostics. The case studies are thoughtfully executed; however, in Sec. 4.1 the conclusions would benefit from formal uncertainty quantification and ablations (e.g., fixing loss width, fixing f(s), known motor noise).
Clarity
- Well organized and readable. Address the noted ambiguities (discretization, unnormalized target, unimodality, figure) to further improve accessibility.
Originality
- A valuable synthesis: explicit a priori identifiability analysis for Bayesian observer models with a tractable expected-KL objective and a unified parametric framework. Distinct from general distinguishability work and prior Bayesian “cognitive tomography.”
Significance
- Practical impact on experimental design in perception/cognition; the framework should be useful to practitioners selecting stimuli, conditions, and auxiliary measurements. Conclusions in speed perception bolster confidence in earlier inferences.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem (identifiability) with a concrete, usable methodology.
- Clear insights for design choices in common perceptual paradigms.
- Technically competent, with potential to influence best practices.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on specific representational assumptions; generality of conclusions is uncertain without broader sensitivity analyses.
- Some derivational steps and modeling choices insufficiently justified; key validation (flat-prior recovery) not in main text.
- A few presentation issues impede clarity.

Overall: A strong, interesting submission above the NIPS threshold. With the requested clarifications, sensitivity checks, and minor presentation fixes, the paper will make a useful contribution.
