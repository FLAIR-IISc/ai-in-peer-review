This paper proposes a framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a critical issue in computational cognitive science. The authors summarize Bayesian Decision Theory, highlight the intrinsic degeneracy where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, and propose a method to assess parameter recoverability before running an experiment. This framework is then applied to two case studies: time interval estimation and visual speed perception. The problem of model identifiability is a long-standing challenge in cognitive modeling (e.g., Anderson, 1978) and has been raised as a significant limitation of the Bayesian approach in particular (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). This work thus addresses a timely and important theoretical problem.

**Quality & Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its direct engagement with this foundational issue. The approach is technically interesting, and the paper is well-written and clearly structured. However, there are two major concerns. First, the paper largely overlooks a substantial body of existing work on model comparison, selection, and identifiability in cognitive science (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004). By failing to connect with or differentiate itself from this prior literature, the novelty and specific contribution of the proposed framework remain unclear. Second, the technical execution relies on key assumptions that appear arbitrary and are insufficiently justified. For instance, the specific mixture-of-Gaussians form for the measurement probability `p_meas` (Eq. 3) and the parameter choices used to quantify model support (Eq. 12) lack strong theoretical grounding. It is not demonstrated how dependent the framework's conclusions are on these specific choices, which raises questions about the robustness and generality of the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but some points could be improved. For example, the "trivial mathematical fact" (Line 53) should be briefly explained for the reader. There is a minor typo on Line 73 (the second "nor" should likely be "or"). In Figure 2, the meaning of the star and dashed lines should be explicitly stated in the caption.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for high significance if the methodological concerns are addressed. A robust framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis would be a valuable tool for researchers designing psychophysical experiments.

In summary, this is an interesting paper on a very important topic. However, its contribution is currently undermined by its failure to situate itself within the relevant literature and by its reliance on poorly justified assumptions. The authors should thoroughly discuss the chosen constraints that enable identifiability and connect their approach to previous work on model comparison.
