This paper presents a computational framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception. The authors rightly point out that such models often suffer from a fundamental degeneracy, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, making it difficult to recover an observer's true internal representations. The proposed solution is a flexible, parametric class of observer models and an efficient MCMC-based method to explore the "identifiability landscape." This landscape is defined by an approximate expected posterior density, which quantifies how well a given model can be distinguished from a "ground truth" reference model given a specific experimental design. The framework is demonstrated on two case studies: a time interval estimation task, where it is used to compare different experimental designs, and a speed perception task, where it is used to validate the robustness of previous findings about a slow-speed prior.

This work addresses a critical and often underappreciated problem in computational cognitive science, which has clear relevance to the NIPS community. The challenge of model identifiability is well-known (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004), but this paper offers a novel, tailored, and practical approach for a widely used class of models in perceptual science.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is that it tackles the fundamental problem of model identifiability in a principled and systematic way. Providing a tool to evaluate experimental designs *before* data collection is a significant contribution that could improve the rigor of research using Bayesian observer models. The proposed framework is novel and well-motivated.
*   **Methodology:** The core idea of mapping the identifiability landscape by sampling from an approximate expected posterior based on KL divergence is clever and computationally efficient. The construction of a general, flexible observer model class is also a valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the motivation for the work is articulated clearly. The problem is introduced effectively, and the structure of the proposed model is laid out logically.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**

While the framework is promising, its current presentation relies on a number of strong assumptions and contains several points that require clarification and justification.

1.  **Dependence on Strong Assumptions:** The main results and the framework's utility are contingent on a long list of specific parametric assumptions. The identifiability conclusions may be heavily dependent on these choices.
    *   (L. 134) The log-stimulus transform `f(s)` is a strong constraint derived from Weber-Fechner/Steven's laws. The analysis of identifiability might be quite different under alternative sensory mappings.
    *   (L. 143, 158, 201) The choice of mixture-of-Gaussians for the likelihood, prior, and loss function is computationally convenient but restrictive. It is unclear if the identifiability issues observed are general properties or artifacts of these specific functional forms. For example, why should an observer's prior be represented on a regular lattice that is wider than the experienced stimulus range (L. 158)? This choice feels arbitrary and needs justification.

2.  **Methodological Justification:** Several key steps in the methodology are not sufficiently justified.
    *   (L. 252 / Eq. 14-15) The derivation of the expected log-likelihood relies on approximations (Stirling's, and `E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]`) that are presented without justification. The conditions on the number of trials `N` under which these hold should be stated and defended. In particular, the approximation involving the expectation of a product is a strong application of Jensen's inequality that is not generally valid. Equation 15 seems primarily justified by its intuitive outcome rather than a rigorous derivation.
    *   (L. 143) The paper states that the likelihood and loss function shapes are chosen by maximizing entropy under moment constraints. Does this procedure guarantee a unimodal distribution, or could it result in bimodal shapes, which might violate the model's assumptions?
    *   (L. 262) When sampling from the approximate expected posterior, it is unclear what "unnormalized density" is being sampled from. This needs to be specified. Is it the distribution of the dataset `D`?

3.  **Interpretation of Case Studies:**
    *   (Sec 4.1) The finding that sensory noise and motor noise are interchangeable is not surprising, given that both are modeled as approximately Gaussian on the same log-transformed internal space. To make the comparison of experimental designs more compelling, the authors should report whether the differences in reconstruction error (Fig 2b) between conditions (e.g., BSL vs. MTR) are statistically reliable based on the posterior samples.
    *   (Sec 4.2) To convincingly demonstrate that the model can recover the slow-speed prior, a crucial control is needed: showing it can also recover the *absence* of such a prior. The authors mention a flat-prior reference observer (FLA) in the text, but this result (presumably in the supplement) is essential and should be explicitly reported and discussed in the main paper as a sanity check.
    *   (L. 365) The claim that learning priors would not matter in a 2AFC task is questionable. Observers are known to learn stimulus statistics even in 2AFC paradigms, which can influence their decision criteria. This claim needs to be clarified or softened.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   (L. 106) It should be clarified why a continuous stimulus space is modeled with a discrete distribution. Is this purely for computational convenience, or does it reflect common experimental designs?
    *   (Figure 1) This figure is confusing. The flow from stimulus to response is hard to follow. I suggest integrating panel (b) into panel (a) to show the full generative process in a single diagram. Removing the derived quantity `p_est(s*|x)` and focusing on the core components (`s -> x -> t* -> r`) would improve clarity.

**Conclusion:**

This paper is above the threshold for acceptance at NIPS because it addresses an important problem with a novel and interesting methodology. However, its impact is currently limited by its reliance on many strong, under-justified assumptions and a lack of clarity on key methodological details. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the points above, particularly by providing better justification for the approximations and modeling choices, and by strengthening the analysis of the case studies with the suggested controls and statistical comparisons. Addressing these issues would significantly improve the quality and convincingness of this promising work.
