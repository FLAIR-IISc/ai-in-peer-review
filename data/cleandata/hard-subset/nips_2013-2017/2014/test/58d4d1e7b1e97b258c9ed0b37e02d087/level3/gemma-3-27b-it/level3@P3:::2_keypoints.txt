- Addresses important problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling.  
- Proposes a novel framework for systematic, a priori identifiability testing of observer-model classes.  
- Manuscript is clearly written, well-organized, and potentially above conference threshold.  
- Analytical approach and continuous class of observer models are methodological strengths.  
- Conclusions are weakened by numerous strong assumptions that limit generalizability.  
- Results appear sensitive to representational choices (e.g., log-stimulus transform) without robustness checks.  
- Constraints on priors (lattice vs. uniform, allowing priors wider than stimulus range) lack sufficient justification.  
- Claim that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising under Gaussian assumptions and needs statistical tests of differences.  
- Must demonstrate recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior, not just recovery when it exists.  
- Modeling a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution (line 106) needs explicit justification.  
- Stirling approximation and the E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] approximation lack stated conditions and rigorous justification.  
- The "unnormalized density" sampled from (line 262) is ambiguous and needs explicit definition (dataset distribution, posterior predictive, etc.).  
- Figure 1 is confusing and overly complex; recommend integrating panels and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) for clarity.  
- The claim that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks is questionable and requires clarification.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel a priori identifiability framework, clear writing, useful for experimental design, analytical parameter-space exploration.  
- Rejection arguments: heavy reliance on assumptions, sensitivity to modeling choices, insufficient approximation justification, some unsurprising findings, confusing figure.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions contingent on justifying assumptions or showing robustness, providing technical justifications for approximations, clarifying ambiguous points, improving figures, and exploring limitations.
