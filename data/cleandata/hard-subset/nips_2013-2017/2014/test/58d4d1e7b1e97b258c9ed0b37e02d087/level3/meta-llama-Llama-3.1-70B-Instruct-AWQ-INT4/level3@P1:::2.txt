This paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue in the field of cognitive modeling. The authors provide a clear and well-written manuscript that is above the threshold for NIPS due to its interesting methodology.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a framework for studying the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors propose a general class of observer models and an efficient technique to explore their vast identifiability landscape. They apply this framework to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the authors tackle a crucial issue in cognitive modeling, namely the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. This is a timely and important contribution, as Bayesian models are widely used in the field, but their identifiability has not been thoroughly investigated. Secondly, the authors provide a clear and well-written manuscript that is easy to follow. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their methodology and results.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. One of the main limitations is that the results rely on many strong assumptions, which reduces their impact relative to the length of the assumptions list. For example, the authors assume a specific form for the sensory mapping, the prior distribution, and the loss function. While these assumptions are necessary to make the problem tractable, they may not be realistic in all cases.

Another limitation is that the authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the statistical reliability of their results. For example, in Section 4.1, the authors report the results of their analysis, but they do not provide any information about the statistical significance of the differences between conditions. This makes it difficult to evaluate the robustness of their findings.

In addition, the authors should provide more justification for some of their modeling choices. For example, in Line 106, the authors model a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution, but they do not clarify why this is necessary. Is it due to the experimental design or the true stimulus? Similarly, in Line 134, the authors use a log-stimulus transform, but they do not discuss how this representational assumption affects the identifiability of the model.

Furthermore, the authors should provide more information about the constraints and entropy maximization used in their model. For example, in Line 143, the authors use a mixture of two Gaussians to model the sensory measurement, but they do not discuss whether this distribution is guaranteed to be unimodal. Could the fitted distribution be bimodal?

Finally, the authors should provide more clarification about some of their modeling choices. For example, in Line 158, the authors use a lattice representation for the observer's prior, but they do not explain why this is preferable to a uniform representation. How would a user justifiably set a prior wider than the true stimulus range?

In terms of the figures and tables, Figure 1 is confusing and could be improved by integrating panel b into panel a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x). This would make the figure more concise and easier to understand.

In terms of the equations, the authors should provide more justification for some of their approximations. For example, in Line 252/Equation 14-15, the authors use the Stirling approximation and the approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]. However, they do not discuss the conditions under which these approximations hold. Similarly, in Line 262, the authors sample from an "unnormalized density," but they do not specify which density this is. Is it the dataset distribution?

Overall, this paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. While it has some limitations, it is a valuable contribution to the field of cognitive modeling. With some revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.
* The authors provide a clear and well-written manuscript that is easy to follow.
* The paper tackles a crucial issue in cognitive modeling, namely the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results rely on many strong assumptions, which reduces their impact relative to the length of the assumptions list.
* The authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the statistical reliability of their results.
* Some of the modeling choices are not well-justified, and the authors should provide more clarification about these choices.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Provide more justification for the assumptions used in the model.
* Analyze the statistical reliability of the results and provide information about the statistical significance of the differences between conditions.
* Clarify the modeling choices and provide more information about the constraints and entropy maximization used in the model.
* Improve Figure 1 by integrating panel b into panel a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x).
* Provide more justification for the approximations used in the equations and discuss the conditions under which these approximations hold.
* Specify which density is being sampled from in Line 262.
