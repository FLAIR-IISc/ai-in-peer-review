This paper proposes an a priori framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models. The authors build a flexible parametric class (monotonic sensory map f(s), mixture-of-Gaussians likelihood and loss, Gaussian-mixture priors, lapse and motor noise), then approximate expected model support via a KL-based criterion, sampling the induced “expected posterior” to map parameter identifiability. Two case studies illustrate the approach: interval timing (where priors are hard to recover unless motor noise is constrained and narrow losses can be harmful) and speed perception under a slow-speed prior (where priors and noise parameters are comparatively well-recovered). The work is motivated by longstanding concerns about the non-identifiability of internal representations (e.g., Anderson 1978) and recent critiques of Bayesian modeling in cognition (Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012).

Quality: The KL derivation and use of expected log-likelihood are sensible, and the case studies are carefully executed. However, key modeling choices feel arbitrary or under-justified: the likelihood family p_meas in Eq. 3 (two-component Gaussian mixture fixed via a local maximum-entropy heuristic) and several fixed parameterizations (e.g., priors’ basis size, loss family, and the choices driving Eq. 12) may critically shape the identifiability maps. The paper would be strengthened by systematic sensitivity analyses to these choices (vary number of components, non-Gaussian noise, alternative f(s), asymmetric/heavy-tailed losses, and different stimulus schedules Pi and Ntr).

Clarity: Overall well written and organized, with clear case-study narratives. Please: explain the “trivial mathematical fact” at Line 53; change the second “nor” to “or” at Line 73; and clarify what the star and dashed lines denote in Figure 2.

Originality: The framing is useful, but the paper under-cites prior work on model distinguishability/model mimicry and principled model comparison in cognitive science (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004). The contribution would be clearer if positioned relative to these traditions and to design-optimization tools used in cognitive modeling.

Significance: Addressing identifiability is important, and the framework could influence experimental design. Its impact hinges on demonstrating robustness beyond the current parametric family and on clearer guidance about which constraints make models identifiable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Important question with a practical, testable framework.
- Clear demonstrations linking design choices to identifiability.
- Useful tool for pre-registered design evaluation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient engagement with prior literature; novelty unclear.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions without robustness checks.

Recommendation: Discuss and justify the constraints/assumptions that render models identifiable; add sensitivity and ablation studies; broaden related work and clarify positioning.
