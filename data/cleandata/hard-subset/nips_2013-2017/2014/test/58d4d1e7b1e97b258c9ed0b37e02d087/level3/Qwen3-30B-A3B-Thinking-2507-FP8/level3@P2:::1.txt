

The paper "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception" addresses a fundamental theoretical issue in Bayesian models of perception: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions). The authors propose a systematic framework for a priori testing of identifiability, which can inform experimental design to better recover these internal representations from behavioral data. The paper demonstrates the framework's utility through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The paper correctly identifies the degeneracy problem in Bayesian models of perception, where multiple combinations of model components can yield identical behavior. This is a well-known issue in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978; Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012), and the authors are right to highlight it as a concern for the validity of Bayesian models in perception. However, the paper largely fails to connect with the substantial literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science. The authors cite Mamassian & Landy (2009) for the degeneracy issue but do not engage with the extensive work by Pitt, Navarro, and colleagues on assessing model distinguishability (Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004), which provides a more general framework for addressing the identifiability problem.

The paper's framework is well-structured and the two case studies demonstrate its utility. The authors present a general Bayesian observer model that can be used to explore identifiability, and they develop a method to quantify expected model support using KL divergence. The results from the case studies provide valuable insights for experimental design, such as the importance of measuring motor parameters independently to improve prior recovery in time interval estimation.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that affect its quality and significance. First, the key modeling assumptions (e.g., the parametric form of the sensory map in Eq. 2, the mixture models in Eqs. 3 and 6) lack sufficient justification. The paper does not explore how sensitive the identifiability results are to these assumptions, which is critical for establishing the framework's robustness. Second, the paper fails to clearly articulate how its framework advances beyond existing model comparison techniques. The authors state that "there has been no systematic analysis... of their effectiveness," but they do not properly acknowledge the relevant prior work that has addressed this issue.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good figures that effectively illustrate the main results. The authors make a reasonable effort to explain the framework, though some technical details (like the "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53) could be clarified. The paper also has minor grammatical issues (e.g., "nor" should be "or" at Line 73) and unclear figure legends (e.g., the meaning of star and dashed lines in Figure 2).

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important issue in Bayesian models of perception, but its contribution is diminished by the lack of connection to prior work and the insufficient justification of key assumptions. The framework could be valuable for experimental design, but its utility is limited by the lack of robustness analysis and the failure to situate it within the broader literature.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that addresses an important theoretical issue, but it would benefit from a more thorough engagement with the existing literature on model comparison in cognitive science. The framework is not sufficiently novel or well-justified to stand out from existing approaches. The authors should clarify their contribution relative to prior work, provide stronger justification for their modeling assumptions, and explore the robustness of their results to these assumptions.

Recommendation: Weak Reject

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses an important theoretical issue in Bayesian models of perception, presents a clear framework, and demonstrates its utility through two case studies.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper fails to adequately connect with and differentiate from substantial prior work on model comparison in cognitive science. Key modeling assumptions lack sufficient justification, and the novelty of the framework is questionable.
