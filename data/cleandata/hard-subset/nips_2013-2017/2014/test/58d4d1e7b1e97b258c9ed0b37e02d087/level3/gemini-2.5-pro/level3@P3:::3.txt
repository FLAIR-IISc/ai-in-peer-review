This paper presents a formal framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors propose a general and flexible four-stage model of perceptual estimation that includes explicit parameterizations for sensory noise, the observer's internal prior and likelihood, a loss function, and response noise. A key feature of the model is its combination of representational flexibility (e.g., stimulus-dependent sensory noise, non-parametric priors) with computational tractability, enabling efficient inference. The central contribution is a method to quantify the practical identifiability between a "true" reference model and alternative models by calculating the expected log-likelihood over hypothetical datasets. This is elegantly formulated as the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the models' response distributions. The framework's utility is demonstrated through two case studies: one analyzing how different experimental designs affect the recovery of temporal priors, and another validating the robustness of inferring a slow-speed prior in vision.

The work builds upon a rich history of applying Bayesian decision theory to perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 1996; Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006) and directly addresses the critical, often-underappreciated problem of model degeneracy, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior (Mamassian & Landy, 2002). The modeling approach, particularly the use of flexible representations and stimulus-dependent noise, is methodologically related to very recent work aiming to build more powerful and realistic observer models (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Quality:** The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem. The ability to reliably recover an observer's internal representations is a cornerstone of the Bayesian "process model" interpretation of cognition. This work provides a principled, quantitative tool for experimenters to assess the feasibility of this goal *before* collecting data, potentially leading to more robust and informative experimental designs. The framework is mathematically elegant, technically sound, and well-motivated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The decomposition of the observer model into sensation, perception, decision, and response stages provides a logical and understandable flow. The mathematical details are presented explicitly, which should aid in reproducibility.
*   **Generality:** The proposed model class is impressively general, capturing many key features discussed in the literature, such as Weber-Fechner/Stevens' laws for sensory mapping, non-Gaussian noise characteristics (skewness, kurtosis), flexible priors, and asymmetric loss functions, all within a single, coherent mathematical structure.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** My main concern is that the contribution, while valuable, may be more incremental than presented. The flexible modeling approach, including the use of mixtures of Gaussians for priors and likelihoods and the handling of stimulus-dependent noise, bears a very strong resemblance to the framework recently developed by Acerbi and colleagues (PLoS Comp Bio, 2012; PLoS Comp Bio, 2014). The current manuscript does not sufficiently contextualize itself with respect to this and other related work. I strongly recommend the authors add a dedicated section or expand the introduction to thoroughly review these recent advances in Bayesian perceptual modeling. This section should explicitly state what is novel about the current framework. Is it the specific parameterization that allows for a more efficient exploration of the identifiability landscape? Is it the first application of such a model to *a priori* identifiability analysis? Clarifying the precise advance over this closely related prior art would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of originality.

2.  **Scope of Identifiability Claims:** The paper makes strong claims about testing identifiability. However, these claims are valid only *within the assumptions of the proposed model class*. A well-known practical difficulty in perceptual modeling is distinguishing between different sources of variability, such as sensory noise, decision noise, and motor/response noise. The proposed framework assumes a specific structure (e.g., additive Gaussian noise in task space for the response stage). The identifiability analysis can reveal degeneracies within this structure (as shown nicely in Fig 2c between sensory and motor noise), but it cannot, by definition, resolve ambiguities that would arise if the true generative process for the data had a different structure (e.g., if response noise was non-Gaussian or signal-dependent in a different way). I would ask the authors to add a paragraph in the discussion that acknowledges this limitation. Clarifying that the framework tests for identifiability conditional on the model's structural assumptions would provide important context for the reader and make the claims more precise.

**Summary of arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a mathematically elegant and powerful framework for addressing a critical issue in computational cognitive science. It is technically sound, clearly written, and provides a potentially very useful tool for researchers. The case studies effectively demonstrate its value.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The primary weakness is the paper's positioning relative to very recent and similar work, making the contribution appear incremental. The claims about identifiability could also be tempered with a more explicit discussion of their dependence on the model's structural assumptions.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS. The problem it tackles is significant, and the proposed framework is a valuable contribution. My concerns can be fully addressed in a revision. By adding a more comprehensive review of related work to clarify the paper's specific contribution and by discussing the practical limitations of the identifiability analysis, the authors can produce an even stronger and more impactful paper.
