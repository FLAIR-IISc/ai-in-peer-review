## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper tackles the important and longstanding problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a critical issue hindering wider adoption of these frameworks in cognitive science. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing the identifiability of a class of Bayesian observer models, focusing on the degeneracy inherent in inverting models to recover internal representations. The approach involves summarizing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying sources of non-identifiability, prescribing constraints, testing parameter differentiability, and applying the framework to time interval estimation and motion perception tasks. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the challenges and proposed solutions.

A key strength lies in the authors’ attempt to provide a practical, a priori method for assessing identifiability *before* conducting experiments, potentially improving experimental design. The application to both time interval estimation and speed perception provides concrete examples of how the framework can be used. The mathematical formulation appears sound, and the use of KL-divergence to quantify model similarity is a reasonable approach. 

However, the paper suffers from a significant omission: a lack of engagement with a substantial body of prior work on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science. While the introduction acknowledges the issue of non-identifiability (citing Jones & Love 2011, Bowers & Davis 2012), it largely ignores relevant methodologies developed to address these challenges. For example, the work of Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) on Bayesian model selection and model averaging, which directly address the issue of non-identifiable parameters, are not discussed. This omission weakens the claim of novelty and makes it difficult to assess the contribution relative to existing approaches. 

Furthermore, several key modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified. The definition of *pmeas* in Eq. 3 and the specific parameter choices in Eq. 12, for instance, lack clear rationale. The authors do not adequately explore how sensitive the results are to these assumptions. A more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper addresses a critical theoretical issue in Bayesian modeling of perception, is clearly written, and proposes a potentially useful framework for experimental design.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** The paper lacks sufficient engagement with prior literature on model comparison and identifiability, and key modeling assumptions are not adequately justified.

**Minor Comments:**
* Line 53: Clarify the “trivial mathematical fact.”
* Line 73: Change “nor” to “or.”
* Figure 2: Clarify what the star and dashed lines denote.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The authors should significantly expand the related work section, thoroughly justify the chosen constraints and assumptions, and perform a sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of their findings. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper and its contribution to the field.
