This paper proposes a practical framework for assessing a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks. The authors (i) formalize the degeneracy inherent in Bayesian Decision Theory whereby different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield indistinguishable behavior; (ii) introduce a flexible, computationally efficient observer class for 1D estimation tasks, with parametric sensory mappings, mixture-of-Gaussians measurement and loss models, lapse and motor noise; (iii) derive an expected log-likelihood criterion for discriminability between a reference observer θ* and a candidate θ that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimulus conditions; and (iv) use MCMC to map the “approximate expected posterior” over θ given θ*. Two case studies illustrate how this informs experimental design: in interval timing, measuring motor parameters markedly improves recoverability of the prior, while imposing a narrow, near-MAP loss harms it; in speed perception with a slow-speed prior, both the prior exponent and noise characteristics are recovered robustly.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits within a mature literature on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Körding & Wolpert 2004; Girshick et al. 2011; Stocker & Simoncelli 2006) and speaks to longstanding concerns about identifiability and model mimicry raised in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson 1978; Pitt, Myung & Navarro 2002; Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004) and critiques of Bayesian “just-so” explanations (Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012). The use of KL-based criteria relates to model comparison practice (AIC/BIC/DIC) and to Bayesian experimental design/model discrimination (not cited; there is relevant work on expected information gain/Fisher information). Positioning against these literatures would clarify novelty.

Strengths
- Important problem: Non-identifiability is central to interpreting inferred priors/likelihoods/losses; an a priori tool for design is valuable.
- Methodological clarity: The reduction of expected log evidence to weighted KLs across stimuli is elegant and computationally tractable; the observer class allows fast evaluation via mixtures with analytic expected loss.
- Actionable insights: Concrete design guidance emerges (e.g., independently measuring motor noise reduces degeneracies; near-MAP losses can hinder prior recovery in timing).
- Validation across tasks: Case studies cover both interval timing and motion speed, and the framework recovers known effects (slow-speed prior) with reasonable robustness.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Quality: Several modeling choices seem arbitrary or under-justified. The two-component Gaussian mixtures for both measurement (Eq. 3) and loss (Eq. 6), the maximum-entropy selection within those families, and the specific sensory map (Eq. 2) could materially shape identifiability conclusions. The paper does not provide sensitivity analyses (e.g., more mixture components; alternative loss families such as Laplace or asymmetric power; different lapse models; spline or nonparametric prior bases; varying M for the prior mixture).
- Quality: The identifiability metric is tied to KL asymmetry and to the assumed θ*. While appropriate given the generative stance, comparisons to alternatives (symmetrized KL/JS, Hellinger) or local analyses (Fisher Information/profile likelihood) would bolster confidence that conclusions are not metric-specific.
- Clarity: Some derivations and choices need more explanation (see minor comments). Details about MCMC (priors, proposals, convergence diagnostics, runtime) and computational cost as a function of Nexp, Ntr, and model size are missing, limiting reproducibility and practical use.
- Originality: The contribution overlaps with established work on model distinguishability and experimental design in cognitive modeling. The paper cites Navarro et al. (2004) but does not deeply engage with that literature or with Bayesian optimal design, leaving the novelty relative to prior methods somewhat unclear.
- Scope: The framework is restricted to 1D continuous estimation; claims about extension to 2AFC/discrete choice are deferred. Since much of the speed prior literature is 2AFC, a concrete demonstration on discrete-choice data would strengthen impact.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: Generally sound derivations and thoughtful applications, but needs sensitivity checks and fuller algorithmic details.
- Clarity: Well written and organized; figures are helpful, though some annotations are unclear.
- Originality: Moderate; the particular synthesis (mixture-based observer + KL landscape exploration for design) is useful, but the paper should better delineate differences from prior distinguishability and design work.
- Significance: High for perception and, more broadly, cognitive modeling; the framework could influence how experiments are planned and how claims about internal representations are validated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Justify and probe assumptions: Provide ablations/sensitivity analyses for (i) number of mixture components; (ii) loss family; (iii) sensory map parameterization; (iv) lapse model; (v) prior basis size M. Show that key qualitative conclusions (e.g., MAP-like loss harms prior recovery; motor noise measurement helps) are robust.
- Contextualize more fully: Compare against established methods for model discrimination/identifiability (e.g., Fisher information, expected information gain, Bayesian optimal design), and clarify what your framework adds in scalability or applicability.
- Report computational details: MCMC settings, priors over parameters, convergence checks, and typical wall-clock times. Provide guidance for practitioners on Ntr allocation and stimulus set design given a time budget.
- Clarify Eq. 12 usage: Discuss dependence on Ntr, Pi, and asymmetry; consider reporting whether different divergences lead to the same design ranking.
- Extend or sketch 2AFC/discrete-choice instantiation, even in Supplementary, to increase generality.

Minor comments
- Explain the “trivial mathematical fact” (Line 53): explicitly show that scaling prior, likelihood, and loss by functions φi with product constant leaves the argmin in Eq. 1 unchanged.
- Line 73: change the second “nor” to “or.”
- Figure 2: Clarify what stars and dashed lines denote in all panels; ensure captions define all markers/CI bands.
- Specify whether lapse draws from the prior in task space or internal space and how the Jacobian is handled (Eq. 7).
- Provide references for Bayesian experimental design/model discrimination if discussed.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, important challenge with a concrete, usable framework.
- Clear derivation and actionable design recommendations.
- Case studies that replicate known effects and reveal non-obvious design pitfalls.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient engagement with prior model-identifiability/design literature.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric assumptions without sensitivity analyses.
- Limited scope to 1D estimation; missing empirical or discrete-choice demonstration.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on strengthening the literature positioning and adding sensitivity analyses to demonstrate robustness of the main conclusions.
