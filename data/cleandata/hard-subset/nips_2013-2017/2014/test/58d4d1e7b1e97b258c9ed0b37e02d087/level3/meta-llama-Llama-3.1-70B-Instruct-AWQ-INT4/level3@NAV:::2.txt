This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a critical issue in the field. The authors provide a clear and well-organized manuscript, making it easy to follow their arguments. The methodology is interesting and well-suited for the problem at hand.

The paper's main strength lies in its ability to provide a systematic analysis of the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, which has been lacking in the field. The authors' approach is rigorous, and their framework is flexible and efficient, allowing for the exploration of a large class of observer models.

However, the main results rely heavily on strong assumptions, which reduces their impact. For instance, the authors assume that the observer's internal representation of the likelihood is expressed in the same measurement space as the true likelihood, which might not always be the case. Additionally, the authors' use of a maximum-entropy approach to determine the shape of the measurement distribution might not be justified in all situations.

The authors' analysis of the time interval estimation task and the speed perception task is thorough, but some of the results are not surprising. For example, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in the time interval estimation task is expected, given the assumptions made about the reference observer. The authors should report which condition differences are statistically reliable.

In the speed perception task, the authors' results suggest that the reconstruction of internal representations is relatively reliable, but they must demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer. This is crucial to validate the model's performance.

The authors' use of a log-stimulus transform is a strong constraint, and it is unclear how the identifiability of the model depends on this representational assumption. Furthermore, the authors' assumption that the constraints and entropy maximization guarantee the unimodality of the fitted distribution is not justified, and they should investigate the possibility of bimodality.

The paper's figures are not always clear, and Figure 1 is confusing. The authors should integrate panel b into panel a and remove the derived p_est(s^*|x) to improve clarity.

The authors' approximations, such as the Stirling approximation and the assumption that E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x], need justification. The conditions under which these approximations hold should be clearly stated.

The paper's significance lies in its contribution to the understanding of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors' framework has the potential to improve experimental design and provide a more nuanced understanding of the underlying degeneracy of parameters of interest.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses a critical issue in the field of Bayesian models of perception.
* The authors provide a rigorous and systematic analysis of the identifiability of Bayesian models.
* The paper's methodology is interesting and well-suited for the problem at hand.
* The authors' framework has the potential to improve experimental design and provide a more nuanced understanding of the underlying degeneracy of parameters of interest.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The main results rely heavily on strong assumptions.
* Some of the results are not surprising, given the assumptions made about the reference observer.
* The authors' use of a maximum-entropy approach and log-stimulus transform is not justified in all situations.
* The paper's figures are not always clear, and some of the approximations need justification.
