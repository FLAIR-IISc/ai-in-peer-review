- Presents a systematic framework to test identifiability of Bayesian observer models, formalizing degeneracy where different priors/likelihoods/losses yield identical behavior.  
- Introduces a flexible Bayesian observer model with explicit, tractable equations for sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions.  
- Uses expected log-likelihood (Eq. 12) / KL divergence to quantify identifiability between models.  
- Demonstrates the framework with two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Shows experimental design can resolve degeneracies (e.g., measuring motor noise), with speed perception robust and interval timing requiring careful design.  
- Finds non-intuitive result that imposing a narrow loss function can worsen recovery of the prior in time estimation.  
- Strength: mathematically elegant, general, and computationally tractable framework advancing systematic identifiability testing.  
- Strength: model flexibility (stimulus-dependent noise) and clear practical utility in case studies.  
- Strength: acknowledges limitations and dependence on experimental assumptions, showing intellectual honesty.  
- Weakness: contribution perceived as incremental relative to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) and lacks clear differentiation from their degeneracy approaches.  
- Weakness: identifiability claims rely on strong model assumptions (e.g., sensory mapping), and practical separation of sensory/decision/response noise remains unaddressed.  
- Weakness: speed perception result may depend on model design (assumed heavy-tailed prior) rather than novel insight.  
- Recommendation: add a dedicated related-work section explicitly comparing to Acerbi et al. and clarifying advances.  
- Recommendation: discuss practical identifiability limitations and experimental confounds (e.g., lapses) more explicitly.  
- Recommendation: clarify novelty in abstract/introduction by emphasizing first systematic a priori identifiability testing across designs.  
- Quality/significance: technically sound with rigorous derivations; a useful methodological tool that can improve experimental design and reproducibility.  
- Clarity: well-organized with clear equations; suggest adding a schematic and brief discussion of computational costs.  
- Originality: novel in providing a systematic identifiability-testing framework, though model components borrow from prior work.  
- Verdict: Accept with minor revisions to better position the work against prior literature and clarify limitations.
