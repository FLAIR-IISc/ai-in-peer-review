## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses the critical issue of model identifiability within the increasingly popular framework of Bayesian cognitive modeling. The authors propose a novel methodology for systematically testing whether parameters of Bayesian observer models can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, offering a valuable tool for both model building and experimental design. The work is clearly written and well-motivated, building upon the established success of Bayesian approaches in perception (e.g., [2, 3, 4]) while acknowledging the inherent degeneracy in inferring internal representations [17, 18, 19]. The approach of utilizing KL-divergence to quantify model similarity and explore the parameter space via MCMC is a clever and efficient way to tackle a complex problem. This work is above the threshold for NIPS due to its interesting methodology and potential impact on the field.

However, the strength of the conclusions is significantly tempered by the numerous assumptions made throughout the analysis. While the authors attempt to justify these choices, the sheer number of assumptions – regarding functional forms for priors, likelihoods, loss functions, and noise distributions – raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The impact of the results is reduced by this reliance on strong prior beliefs. 

Specifically, Section 4.1’s finding regarding the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise feels unsurprising given the Gaussian assumptions and should be supported by statistical significance testing. The authors should report which differences are statistically reliable. In Section 4.2, demonstrating the model’s ability to recover the *absence* of a slow-speed prior (as opposed to simply recovering its parameters when it *is* present) is crucial for demonstrating the framework’s utility. 

Several points require clarification. Line 106: the rationale for modeling a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution needs to be explicitly stated – is this an artifact of the experimental design or a deliberate modeling choice? Line 134: the log-stimulus transform is a strong constraint; the authors should discuss how sensitive the identifiability results are to this representational assumption. Line 143: the authors should address whether the constraints and entropy maximization guarantee a unimodal fitted distribution, or if bimodality is possible. Line 158: the justification for favoring a lattice prior over a uniform representation, and the permissibility of priors wider than the true stimulus range, needs further elaboration.

Figure 1 is confusing and could be improved by integrating panel b into a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x). Finally, the approximations in Lines 252-256 (Stirling’s approximation, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) require justification and a discussion of the conditions under which they hold. Line 262: the “unnormalized density” being sampled from needs to be explicitly specified. The claim in Line 365 that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks requires further clarification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel methodology, clear writing, addresses an important problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Heavy reliance on strong assumptions, limited generalizability, lack of statistical rigor in some results, several points requiring clarification.
