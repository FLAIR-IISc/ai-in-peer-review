This paper presents a novel computational framework for systematically evaluating the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a critical issue in computational cognitive science. The authors argue that since multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior, the recovery of these internal representations is an ill-posed problem. Their proposed framework introduces a flexible, parametric class of observer models and an efficient method based on the Kullback-Leibler divergence to map the "identifiability landscape" for a given experimental design. This approach allows researchers to assess parameter recoverability before data collection. The method's utility is demonstrated in two case studies from the literature: interval timing and speed perception.

The main strength of this manuscript is that it addresses a fundamental, difficult problem in the field with a novel and systematic approach. The originality of providing a concrete tool to improve experimental design by analyzing identifiability *a priori* is a significant contribution. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and the methodology is interesting and technically sophisticated, making it a good fit for the NIPS audience.

However, the primary weakness of the work is its reliance on a long list of strong assumptions, which reduces the generalizability and impact of its conclusions. For example, the specific log-stimulus transform (L134) is a major constraint, and it is unclear if the identifiability results hold under different representational assumptions. The justification for several key approximations (L252, Eqs. 14-15) and modeling choices, such as the lattice prior (L158), is insufficient. The analysis in the case studies also requires strengthening. The finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable (Sec 4.1) is not surprising, and the authors should report which condition differences are statistically reliable. Critically, for the speed perception case (Sec 4.2), the authors must demonstrate that the model can recover the *absence* of a slow-speed prior in a reference observer to validate their method's specificity.

To improve the manuscript, the authors should rigorously justify their assumptions or test the framework's robustness to them. Several points require clarification for reproducibility, including the rationale for the discrete stimulus model (L106), whether the entropy maximization guarantees unimodality (L143), and the nature of the "unnormalized density" (L262). Figure 1 is confusing and should be redesigned for clarity. While this paper addresses an important problem with a promising method, it requires major revisions to substantiate its claims and address these foundational concerns.
