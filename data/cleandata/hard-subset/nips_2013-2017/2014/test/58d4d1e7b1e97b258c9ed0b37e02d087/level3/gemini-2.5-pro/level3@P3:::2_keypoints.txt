- Paper introduces a framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models via an identifiability landscape and MCMC sampling.
- Offers a novel, practical tool to evaluate experimental designs before data collection, addressing an important problem in perceptual modeling.
- Uses a flexible parametric observer class and KL-based approximate expected posterior to map identifiability efficiently.
- Demonstrates the approach on time-interval estimation and speed perception case studies, including validation of a slow-speed prior result.
- Main results depend on strong parametric assumptions (e.g., log-stimulus transform f(s)) that may bias identifiability conclusions.
- Reliance on mixture-of-Gaussians for likelihood, prior, and loss may be restrictive and could produce artifacts; choices like a lattice prior need justification.
- Key methodological approximations (Stirling’s, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) are used without conditions or rigorous justification regarding trial counts N.
- The max-entropy rationale for selecting likelihood and loss shapes needs clarification on whether it guarantees unimodality or could produce multimodal forms.
- It is unclear exactly what unnormalized density the MCMC samples from (e.g., distribution over datasets D) and this should be specified.
- The interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in the first case study is unsurprising given modeling choices; statistical tests on reconstruction differences are needed.
- The speed-perception case study should explicitly show recovery of a flat prior as a crucial control and report that result in the main paper.
- The claim that learning priors doesn't matter in 2AFC is questionable and should be softened or supported with evidence.
- Presentation issues: clarify modeling of continuous stimulus space with a discrete distribution and simplify/confine Figure 1 to improve clarity.
- Overall recommendation: accept with revisions — promising and relevant work but needs stronger justification of assumptions, methodological details, and additional controls/statistics.
