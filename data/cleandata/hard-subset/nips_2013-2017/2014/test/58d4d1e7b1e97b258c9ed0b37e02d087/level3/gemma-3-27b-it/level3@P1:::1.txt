This paper tackles the critical issue of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a field where these models have become increasingly prevalent but whose validity rests on the ability to recover meaningful internal representations from behavioral data. The authors correctly point out the inherent degeneracy within the Bayesian framework – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield the same observed behavior – and propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability *a priori*. This framework, applied to time interval estimation and speed perception tasks, offers a promising method for improving experimental design and assessing the robustness of inferences about internal representations.

The paper builds upon the established foundation of Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) and highlights its growing role not just as a normative benchmark but as a descriptive process model. The ability to ‘invert’ BDT models to reveal underlying priors, likelihoods, and loss functions is presented as key to understanding complex cognitive processes like statistical learning and neural representations of uncertainty. However, the authors rightfully raise concerns about the unique mapping from behavior to these internal representations, referencing prior work that acknowledges this fundamental challenge (e.g., Anderson 1978; Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012). The strength of this work lies in its systematic approach to quantifying and addressing this non-identifiability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a crucial and increasingly relevant problem in the application of Bayesian models to cognitive science. The question of whether recovered internal representations are genuinely reflective of underlying cognitive processes or merely artifacts of model assumptions is paramount.
*   **Novel Framework:** The proposed framework offers a concrete method for evaluating identifiability *before* data collection, which has significant implications for experimental design and hypothesis testing.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear explanation of the mathematical framework and its application to specific psychophysical tasks.
*   **Practical Applications:** The case studies demonstrate the potential of the framework to provide actionable insights for improving experimental designs, as evidenced by the recommendations for the time interval estimation task.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The approach leverages established mathematical principles (KL divergence, maximum entropy) to provide a rigorous assessment of identifiability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Connection to Related Work:** A significant weakness is the relative lack of engagement with a substantial body of literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science. While the authors acknowledge issues of non-identifiability, they do not adequately situate their work within the broader context of methods developed to address these issues (e.g., Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004). This omission makes it challenging to fully assess the novelty of their approach.
*   **Arbitrary Assumptions:** The paper relies on several modeling assumptions that appear somewhat arbitrary or lack sufficient justification. For example, the specific functional form of the measurement distribution *p<sub>meas</sub>* (Eq. 3) and the choice of parameter values within the framework seem driven more by convenience than by strong theoretical or empirical motivations. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions needs further investigation.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Prior Distributions:** While discussing priors is central to the paper’s motivation, the choice of prior distributions *within* the framework itself (for model parameters during the identifiability analysis) is not fully explained. This is a critical aspect of Bayesian inference, and the potential impact of these choices on the results should be acknowledged.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem in the field.
*   The proposed framework is novel and has the potential to significantly improve the rigor of Bayesian modeling in perception.
*   The case studies provide compelling demonstrations of the framework's utility.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited engagement with related work weakens the claim of novelty.
*   The reliance on potentially arbitrary modeling assumptions raises concerns about the robustness of the results.
*   The lack of detailed discussion regarding prior distributions within the framework is a notable omission.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 53: The phrase "trivial mathematical fact" is vague and could be clarified.
*   Line 73: Change "nor" to "or" for grammatical correctness.
*   Figure 2: Clarify the meaning of the star and dashed lines.

**Overall:**

This is a well-written and important paper that addresses a crucial theoretical issue in the field of Bayesian modeling of perception. However, the limited connection to existing literature on model comparison and the potentially arbitrary nature of some modeling assumptions warrant further consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to expand their discussion of related work and to provide a more thorough justification for the key modeling choices made within the framework. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution and increase its impact on the field.
