- Presents a systematic framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks.  
- Formalizes a flexible Bayesian model class with explicit equations for sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distribution to enable tractable inference.  
- Core methodological contribution: a priori parameter identifiability assessment using KL divergence-based expected posterior analysis.  
- Demonstrates the method through two case studies: temporal interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Provides practical experimental-design insights (e.g., narrow loss can hinder prior recovery in timing tasks; decoupling motor noise measurements improves identifiability).  
- Strength: mathematical elegance and clear application to real psychophysical problems.  
- Technical quality is high: sound derivations and clear case studies.  
- Significance: offers a useful tool to improve experimental design in perception research.  
- Limitation: builds substantially on Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), making the contribution incremental rather than transformative.  
- Key caveat: identifiability claims depend on strict model assumptions (e.g., fixed loss form) and do not resolve confounds between sensory, decision, and response noise.  
- Inadequate contextualization of prior work—specifically Acerbi 2014 and related Bayesian perceptual model literature.  
- Required revisions: add a dedicated literature review of recent Bayesian perceptual models (e.g., Acerbi 2012/2014, Maloney 2010) and explicitly state advances.  
- Required revisions: include a new subsection discussing practical limitations of identifiability claims (e.g., noise confounds).  
- Overall verdict: solid, practically useful, and rigorous work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions for NIPS.
