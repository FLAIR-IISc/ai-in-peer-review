This paper tackles an important theoretical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the identifiability of internal representations inferred from behavioral data. The authors correctly identify the inherent degeneracy in the Bayesian framework – multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical observed behavior – and propose a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability *a priori*. The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the problem, along with a proposed solution illustrated through two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception.

The core idea builds on Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT), acknowledging its success in describing human performance (Geisler, 1989; Knill, 2004) and the increasing trend of interpreting BDT as a process model revealing underlying mental representations (Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Vilares et al., 2015). The authors argue that reliably recovering these representations is crucial for understanding various cognitive processes, yet the non-unique mapping from behavior to model components poses a significant challenge (Anderson, 1978; Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). Their framework aims to provide a means to assess, and potentially improve, experimental designs to address this issue.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear mathematical formulation, the development of a flexible observer model, and the demonstration of the framework's applicability to concrete perceptual tasks. The authors skillfully navigate the complexities of the Bayesian framework, providing a practical method for exploring the identifiability landscape. The case studies are well-chosen and demonstrate how the framework can be used to evaluate the effectiveness of different experimental setups and parameter constraints. The analysis of the time interval estimation task, for instance, provides a counterintuitive finding regarding the potential detrimental effects of imposing a narrow loss function.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. The most significant concern is the limited engagement with a substantial body of prior literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science. While the authors acknowledge the issue of non-identifiability, they largely overlook relevant work on techniques for model selection and assessing the informativeness of data (Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro et al., 2004).  Discussing these approaches, and explicitly differentiating the current framework, would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution and contextualize its novelty. The assertion of a "novel framework" feels overstated given the existing toolkit for model comparison.

Furthermore, several key modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or lack sufficient justification. For example, the choice of the functional form for the measurement distribution *p<sub>meas</sub>* (Eq. 3) and the specific parameter values used throughout the analysis (e.g., in Eq. 12) are not thoroughly explained. While the authors mention a maximum-entropy approach, the rationale behind the specific constraints imposed remains unclear. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of these assumptions on the results would be highly valuable.  It is also unclear how robust the conclusions are to variations in the chosen prior distributions over parameters.  A discussion of the limitations imposed by these choices would increase the credibility of the findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in Bayesian modeling of perception.
*   The proposed framework offers a practical method for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
*   The case studies demonstrate the applicability and potential utility of the framework.
*   The mathematical formulation is clear and rigorous.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper overlooks substantial prior work on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science.
*   Several key modeling assumptions are arbitrary and lack sufficient justification.
*   The robustness of the results to variations in assumptions is not adequately explored.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 53: Clarify what is meant by the “trivial mathematical fact.”
*   Line 73: Change “nor” to “or.”
*   Figure 2: Clarify what the star and dashed lines denote.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and important paper that raises a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception. However, the lack of engagement with prior work on model comparison and the insufficiently justified modeling assumptions prevent it from reaching its full potential.  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing these concerns by: 1) thoroughly discussing and justifying the chosen constraints/assumptions that make Bayesian models identifiable, and 2) explicitly situating their framework within the broader landscape of model comparison techniques in cognitive science.  Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s contribution and strengthen its impact on the field.
