- Addresses the important problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling.  
- Proposes a novel framework to systematically test identifiability with practical implications for experimental design.  
- Paper is well-written with a clear exposition of problem, methods, and results.  
- Methodology efficiently explores the identifiability landscape and can evaluate and rank experimental designs.  
- Applied to two case studies (time-interval estimation and speed perception), demonstrating utility and providing robust theoretical validation of prior work.  
- Relies on strong parametric assumptions (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) that limit generalizability.  
- Specific assumptions problematic: log-stimulus transform constraints and insufficient justification for a lattice-favoring prior.  
- Some findings are unsurprising (sensory vs motor noise interchangeability) and lack clear condition differences.  
- Model fails to recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer, raising robustness concerns.  
- Results are modest in impact relative to the paper’s ambitions.  
- Figure design (e.g., Figure 1) is confusing and reduces clarity.  
- Technical clarifications needed (why use a discrete stimulus distribution; conditions for Stirling’s approximation in Eq. 14).  
- Pros for acceptance: tackles a critical problem, innovative methodology, and generalizable framework.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited impact, strong assumptions, and needed technical and figure refinements.  
- Overall: valuable and suitable for NIPS with revisions to clarify assumptions, improve figures, and strengthen result discussion.
