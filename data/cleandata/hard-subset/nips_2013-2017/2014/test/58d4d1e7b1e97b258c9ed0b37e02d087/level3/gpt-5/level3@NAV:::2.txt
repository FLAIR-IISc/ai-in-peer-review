This paper proposes an a priori framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian observer models by computing expected log-likelihood differences via KL divergences (Eq. 12) and sampling an “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) (Eq. 13). The authors instantiate a flexible, unified parametric observer (mixtures for likelihood and loss; lattice Gaussian-mixture priors; stimulus transform to internal space), and apply the framework to interval timing and speed perception, relating to foundational Bayesian perception work (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli), cognitive tomography, and model distinguishability literature (Navarro, Myung & Pitt). The contribution is timely and useful for experimental design.

Quality: The methodology is interesting and generally sound, but many strong assumptions—unimodality plus maximum entropy for likelihood and loss, log-like stimulus transform, discrete-stimulus approximation, lattice priors—bear directly on identifiability and need tighter justification and ablations. The KL-based derivation is elegant, yet the approximations leading to Eqs. 14–15 require conditions and error bounds.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized, but some technical choices are under-specified. Figure 1 is confusing in separating generative and inference paths.

Originality: The approach of mapping identifiability landscapes via expected evidence is novel in this domain, and the unified observer parameterization is a useful synthesis.

Significance: If strengthened, this framework will be valuable for designing psychophysical experiments and diagnosing degeneracies in practice.

Detailed comments and requests:
- L106: Clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled as discrete: is this purely an experimental design choice or an approximation to a truly continuous s? Provide convergence guidance as Nexp increases.
- L134: The log-stimulus transform is a strong constraint; please test identifiability sensitivity to f(s) (e.g., power-law vs log).
- L143: Do the constraints and entropy maximization guarantee unimodality of the fitted distribution, or can bimodality leak in? State conditions.
- L158: Justify the lattice prior representation vs uniform basis and why its range is 50% wider than true stimulus; explain how a user could set a prior wider than the stimulus support.
- Fig. 1: Consider integrating panel b into a and removing the derived pest(s*|x) from the graphic to reduce cognitive load.
- Sec. 4.1: The interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising; report which condition differences are statistically reliable (credible intervals/effect sizes) and quantify degeneracy.
- Sec. 4.2: Demonstrate recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior (flat prior) in the main text, not only in the supplement, with power analysis.
- L252/Eqs. 14–15: Provide derivations and conditions (N, variance constraints) under which Stirling and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] hold; quantify approximation error.
- L262: Specify precisely which “unnormalized density” is sampled (E(θ|θ*)? dataset-generating distribution?), and the MCMC target.
- L365: Clarify why learning priors would not matter in 2AFC; reconcile with evidence for rapid prior learning.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, relevant problem; useful, general framework; thoughtful case studies; actionable design insights.

Cons against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions with limited validation; missing statistical reliability reporting; several key derivations and specifications under-justified.

Recommendation: Weak accept conditional on addressing the above clarifications and adding the flat-prior recovery demonstration.
