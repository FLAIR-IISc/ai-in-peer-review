- Proposes an a priori framework for assessing identifiability in Bayesian observer models using flexible 1D estimation models parameterized with Gaussian mixtures and a parametric stimulus transform.  
- Derives an expected log-evidence criterion that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between response distributions.  
- Uses MCMC over an “expected posterior” to map practical identifiability under candidate experimental designs.  
- Two case studies: interval reproduction (shows degeneracies and trade-offs between sensory/motor noise and sensitivity to loss) and speed perception (finds prior exponent and noise parameters recoverable across contrasts).  
- Builds on prior Bayesian observer and identifiability/distinguishability literature and reframes identifiability as a design-time computational exercise.  
- Strength: Clear problem formulation emphasizing design-time identifiability rather than post hoc fixes.  
- Strength: Efficient, coherent modeling toolkit with consistent parameterizations enabling tractable expected-loss computations.  
- Strength: Interpretable identifiability metric (expected log-likelihood = KL sum) and practical sampling procedure to explore sloppiness and parameter trade-offs.  
- Strength: Case studies provide concrete, actionable guidance (e.g., measure motor noise; narrow losses can harm prior recovery).  
- Strength: Well-situated in literature and addresses degeneracy critiques constructively.  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on many strong modeling assumptions (fixed f(s), mixture-of-Gaussians, lattice prior, quasi-quadratic loss, specific lapse model) that may drive identifiability conclusions.  
- Request: Quantify which design differences are statistically reliable for sensory vs motor noise interchangeability (e.g., credible intervals, posterior correlations).  
- Request: Move flat-prior “sanity check” results into main text and report estimation error with CIs and performance under UNC.  
- Request: Clarify discretization (experimental vs approximation) and provide sensitivity analysis to number of stimulus bins/Nexp.  
- Request: Quantify sensitivity to misspecification of the log stimulus transform (d, s0) and show results when f(s) is learned rather than fixed.  
- Request: Clarify whether Gaussian mixtures are constrained to unimodality (provide parameter inequalities or state when bimodality occurs and how it’s handled).  
- Request: Justify lattice prior representation and the choice to make prior range 50% wider than stimulus range, with sensitivity checks for boundary effects.  
- Request: Simplify/clarify Figure 1 (integrate b into a, remove intermediate pest(s*|x), provide single generative plate diagram).  
- Request: Provide precise derivation and approximations for Eqs.14–15 (where Stirling, approximations used, residual errors as functions of Ntr and Nexp, empirical checks).  
- Request: Specify the exact unnormalized density sampled by MCMC (full expression, constants, tempering/prior use).  
- Request: Justify the claim that learning priors wouldn’t matter in 2AFC or provide controls showing negligible effects given evidence of rapid learning.  
- Quality: Generally sound with clear derivations, but expected-evidence approximation needs more rigorous justification and diagnostics; add formal UQ and ablations.  
- Clarity: Well organized and readable but address noted ambiguities to improve accessibility.  
- Originality: Valuable and distinct synthesis providing an explicit a priori identifiability analysis with a tractable expected-KL objective.  
- Significance: Practical impact for experimental design in perception/cognition; supports confidence in some prior inferences (e.g., speed perception).  
- Verdict: Strong, above-threshold submission contingent on requested clarifications, sensitivity analyses, and presentation fixes; recommend acceptance after revisions.
