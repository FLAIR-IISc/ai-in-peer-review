This paper proposes a framework for Bayesian model comparison on behavioral data, tackling the longstanding problem of identifiability of internal representations/processes in cognitive science. The authors provide a thorough review of the Bayesian Decision Theory and its limitations, particularly the non-uniqueness of the inverse mapping from observed behavior to elements of the decision process. They then introduce a novel class of Bayesian observer models that can be used to study the identifiability of internal representations in various psychophysical tasks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the theoretical issues surrounding Bayesian model comparison. The proposed framework is efficient and flexible, allowing for the exploration of a large class of observer models. The application of the framework to two case studies, time interval estimation and speed perception, provides valuable insights into the identifiability of internal representations in these tasks.

One of the major concerns with the paper is that it largely ignores substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science. For example, the work by Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) on model identifiability and distinguishability is not mentioned. This omission makes it unclear what the novel contribution of the paper is and how it differs from previous work.

Another concern is that some of the modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified. For instance, the definition of p_meas in Eq. 3 and the parameter choices in Eq. 12 seem to be based on convenience rather than empirical evidence. It is unclear how dependent the results are on these assumptions, and the authors should discuss and justify the chosen constraints and assumptions that make Bayesian models identifiable.

The paper could also benefit from more detailed explanations of some of the technical aspects, such as the "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53. Additionally, there are some minor errors, such as the second "nor" that should be changed to "or" at Line 73.

Despite these concerns, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of cognitive science, and the proposed framework has the potential to be widely applicable. The authors' conclusion that the reconstruction of internal representations in interval timing requires strong experimental constraints and validations is well-supported by their analysis. The paper also provides a useful tool for ranking experimental designs by the quality of the inferred features of interest.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper scores high on clarity, as it is well-organized and clearly written. The paper also scores high on significance, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and provides a unique theoretical approach. However, the paper scores lower on originality, as it builds on previous work without providing a radically new perspective. The paper also scores lower on quality, as some of the modeling assumptions and technical aspects could be improved.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors address the concerns mentioned above and provide more detailed explanations of their modeling assumptions and technical aspects.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper tackles an important theoretical issue in cognitive science and provides a valuable framework for Bayesian model comparison.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The proposed framework is efficient and flexible, allowing for the exploration of a large class of observer models.
* The application of the framework to two case studies provides valuable insights into the identifiability of internal representations in these tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper largely ignores substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science.
* Some of the modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified.
* The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of some of the technical aspects.
* There are some minor errors in the paper.

* Provide more detailed explanations of some of the technical aspects, such as the "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53.
* Address the concerns mentioned above and provide more detailed explanations of the modeling assumptions and technical aspects.
* Consider including a more detailed review of the prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science.
