This paper proposes a Bayesian model of perception that provides a mathematically elegant and general framework for formalizing perceptual estimation. The model combines robustness, such as stimulus-dependent noise, with mathematical convenience, allowing for tractable inference. A key feature of the model is that it is mathematically identifiable, which is crucial for reliable recovery of model components and internal representations from data.

The paper presents a novel class of Bayesian observer models that is both flexible and efficient, allowing for the modeling of various psychophysical tasks. The model is constructed in four stages: sensation, perception, decision-making, and response. The sensation stage describes how the physical stimulus determines the internal measurement, while the perception stage describes how the internal measurement is combined with the prior to yield a posterior distribution. The decision-making stage describes how the posterior distribution and loss function guide the choice of an optimal estimate, and the response stage describes how the optimal estimate leads to the observed response.

The paper also presents a method for efficiently exploring the identifiability of a given observer model within the proposed framework. The method involves defining the support for an observer model as its log likelihood and computing the expected support for the model as the average log likelihood over all possible datasets. The paper demonstrates the application of this method to two case studies: the inference of priors in a time interval estimation task and the reconstruction of prior and noise characteristics in speed perception.

The paper's strengths include its ability to provide a general framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, its mathematical elegance, and its ability to combine robustness with mathematical convenience. The paper also presents a novel method for exploring the identifiability of observer models, which is a crucial aspect of Bayesian modeling.

However, the paper's contribution is incremental and closely resembles recent work in the field, such as Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014). The paper's main contribution is the development of a novel class of Bayesian observer models and a method for exploring their identifiability, but it does not significantly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

One of the main concerns with the paper is the identifiability claims, which hinge on model assumptions and do not resolve practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise. The paper acknowledges this limitation and requests that the authors discuss this practical identifiability limitation in the revised manuscript.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and well-organized, but it assumes a high level of technical expertise in Bayesian modeling and psychophysics. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the mathematical derivations and a clearer discussion of the implications of the results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel class of Bayesian observer models and a method for exploring their identifiability, but it does not significantly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper's contribution is incremental, and it closely resembles recent work in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a general framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, which is a crucial aspect of psychophysics. The paper's results have implications for the design of experiments and the interpretation of data in psychophysics, but they do not significantly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Recommendations:

* Include a new section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly state how this work advances them.
* Discuss the practical identifiability limitation in the revised manuscript.
* Provide a more detailed explanation of the mathematical derivations.
* Clearly discuss the implications of the results.

Overall, I support the acceptance of this paper to NIPS, but I recommend that the authors address the concerns and recommendations outlined above in the revised manuscript.
