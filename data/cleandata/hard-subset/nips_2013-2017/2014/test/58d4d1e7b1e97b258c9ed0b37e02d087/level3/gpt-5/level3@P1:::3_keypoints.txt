- Proposes a general framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models for 1D estimation tasks.
- Defines a flexible, tractable observer class parameterizing sensory map, measurement noise, prior, loss, lapse rate, and response noise.
- Derives an expected log-likelihood criterion that reduces to a sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions.
- Uses an “expected posterior” explored by MCMC to map identifiability under a chosen experimental design.
- Case study (interval timing): recovering priors is fragile unless motor noise is constrained; encouraging MAP-like losses can harm identifiability.
- Case study (speed perception): prior shape and noise parameters are relatively well identified, even when internal likelihood is decoupled from generative measurement noise.
- Connects to existing literature on Bayesian observer models, model distinguishability, and inference of internal representations.
- Main novelty is synthesizing a unified, experiment-design-aware identifiability measure that is mathematically elegant and computationally efficient.
- Framework is technically sound; rich parameterization remains computationally convenient via Gaussian-mixture reductions and KL sums.
- Case studies are carefully designed with principled perturbations (trials, loss width, motor noise, likelihood coupling).
- Limitations partially acknowledged (e.g., sensory vs motor noise trade-offs; weak identifiability of loss width).
- A fuller analysis of practical indistinguishability between sensory, decision, and response noise is needed.
- Paper is generally well written and organized, with clear stage decomposition and explicit equations.
- Key derivations and modeling choices are deferred to the Supplement—bringing them into the main text would aid reproducibility.
- Should state priors used during sampling and summarize MCMC convergence diagnostics more explicitly.
- Observer formulation and KL-based identifiability are close to prior work (Acerbi et al., Houlsby et al., Navarro et al.); contribution is largely a useful synthesis rather than a radical innovation.
- Framework has practical value for experiment design and diagnosing parameter trade-offs; adoption likely among Bayesian observer modelers, broader impact needs code/design recipes.
- Pros for acceptance: unified framework, tractable computations, practical guidance, and insightful case studies.
- Cons for acceptance: incremental relative to prior work, identifiability depends on assumed parameterization, unresolved confounds between sensory/decision/response noise, and limited empirical validation.
- Suggested improvements: add a focused related-work section, explicitly discuss practical identifiability limits and helpful external constraints, clarify/validate 2AFC-to-estimation mapping, and provide implementation details plus code release.
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on stronger literature positioning and clearer discussion of practical identifiability limits.
