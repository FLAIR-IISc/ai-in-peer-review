This paper proposes a framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of a large class of Bayesian observer models used in perceptual science. The authors begin by summarizing the core components of Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT) as applied to perception, highlighting the well-known non-identifiability problem: multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavior. To address this, they develop a flexible and computationally efficient parametric model that can approximate a wide range of observer models. Using this framework, they define a measure of identifiability based on the expected log-likelihood, which is proportional to the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the response distributions of a "true" reference model and a candidate model. This allows them to map out the "identifiability landscape" and assess how well model parameters can be recovered for a given experimental design. The framework is demonstrated on two case studies: a time interval estimation task, where they compare different experimental designs, and a speed perception task, where they re-examine the inference of a slow-speed prior.

The problem of model identifiability is a longstanding and fundamental issue in cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, Psychological Review, 1978). Recent critiques of the Bayesian modeling approach have specifically emphasized this issue as a major challenge to the interpretation of modeling results (e.g., Jones & Love, BBS, 2011; Bowers & Davis, Psychological Bulletin, 2012). This paper is therefore timely and addresses a problem of significant importance to the NIPS community.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The paper tackles a critical and widely acknowledged weakness of Bayesian observer models. Providing a systematic framework to assess identifiability *before* running an experiment could save significant resources and lead to more robust and interpretable scientific conclusions.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is built up in logical stages, and the identifiability analysis is well-explained. The application to two concrete examples from the literature makes the utility of the framework easy to grasp.
3.  **Technical Contribution:** The development of a flexible, parametric class of observer models that remains computationally tractable is a valuable contribution. Combining mixtures of Gaussians for various components (likelihood, prior, loss) while maintaining an efficient way to compute the observer's response distribution is a clever approach that enables the proposed identifiability analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** A major concern is that the paper does not adequately connect its contribution to the substantial existing literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science and statistics. While the problem is framed well, the proposed solution—exploring the likelihood landscape to see how well parameters can be recovered—is not conceptually new. For decades, researchers have used methods like parameter recovery simulations and analysis of the Fisher Information Matrix to assess identifiability. More sophisticated frameworks for model comparison and selection that implicitly deal with identifiability have also been proposed (e.g., Pitt et al., Cognitive Psychology, 2002; Navarro et al., J. Math. Psych., 2004). The authors briefly mention "generic techniques" but do not discuss how their framework improves upon or differs from these established methods. The novelty seems to lie in the specific application and efficient implementation for a particular class of Bayesian models, but this is not made explicit. It would significantly strengthen the paper to position their work within this broader context and clarify its unique contribution.

2.  **Justification of Modeling Assumptions:** The framework's conclusions are contingent on a number of specific, and at times seemingly arbitrary, modeling assumptions. For example, the sensory likelihood (`p_meas` in Eq. 3) and the internal representation of the likelihood (`q_meas`) are modeled as a two-component mixture of Gaussians. The paper states this is for "computational convenience" and to match the first four moments, but provides little justification for why this specific form is appropriate or how robust the results are to this choice. The conclusions drawn—for instance, that a narrow loss function is detrimental to recovering the prior in the time estimation task—may be highly dependent on these specific parametric forms. A crucial part of a framework for testing identifiability should be an analysis of how the framework's own assumptions impact the conclusions. The authors should provide a more thorough justification for their modeling choices or demonstrate that their key findings are robust to variations in these assumptions.

**Recommendation:**

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach. The core ideas are valuable and the demonstrations are compelling. However, the two major weaknesses outlined above currently limit its impact. The authors should be encouraged to:
1.  Thoroughly discuss the relationship between their framework and existing methods for model identifiability analysis in the literature, clarifying the paper's novel contribution.
2.  Provide stronger justification for the specific parametric forms chosen for the observer model components (likelihood, loss function, etc.) and, if possible, discuss the robustness of their case-study conclusions to these assumptions.

Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.
