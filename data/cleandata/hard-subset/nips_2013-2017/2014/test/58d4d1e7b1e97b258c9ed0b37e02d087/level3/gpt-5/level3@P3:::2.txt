Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a central, under-discussed problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling: the practical identifiability of component representations (prior, likelihood, loss). It proposes a general framework that maps expected log-likelihood comparisons between a generative “reference observer” and alternative models to a sum of KL divergences across stimulus conditions, and then samples a surrogate “expected posterior” to chart identifiability under candidate experimental designs. Two case studies illustrate the approach: temporal interval reproduction (Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010) and speed perception with a slow-speed prior (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006). The work is well situated in the literature on Bayesian observer models (Geisler; Knill & Richards) and responds directly to concerns about degeneracy and over-interpretation (Bowers & Davis; Jones & Love), while connecting to psychometric identifiability analyses (Navarro, Pitt & Myung) and to recent attempts to recover internal representations (Acerbi et al.; Houlsby et al.).

Quality
The core technical contribution—reducing expected model evidence to a weighted sum of KL divergences (Eqs. 11–13)—is sound and useful, and the MCMC exploration of the induced landscape is practical. The two case studies are informative and the design-level insights for interval timing are especially valuable. That said, several key results depend on strong modeling assumptions (parametric sensory mapping, maximum-entropy mixture forms, imposed unimodality, specific lapse/motor noise structures). This reduces the generality of the conclusions unless their impact is carefully bounded; in several places the paper relies on approximations that need justification and conditions of validity.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized, but a few definitions and figure choices make it harder to follow than necessary (see detailed comments on Fig. 1 and specific lines). Reporting which condition differences are statistically or practically reliable would greatly help interpretation.

Originality
The framework for a priori identifiability analysis—casting design comparison as expected log-likelihood/KL and sampling the resulting landscape—is novel for this domain and goes beyond ad hoc checks seen in prior Bayesian observer work. The unifying parametric class (sensation, prior, loss) is thoughtfully engineered for computational tractability.

Significance
Identifiability is foundational. Providing a method to compare designs and expose parameter degeneracies should influence future empirical work, especially on reconstructing priors/likelihoods. The interval-timing insights are actionable; the speed-perception validation lends confidence to prior inferences in that literature.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and timely topic with a principled, general framework.
- Clear connection to experimental design; practical guidance in the interval-timing case.
- Elegant reduction to expected KL and efficient exploration via MCMC.
- Good coverage of related work and thoughtful case-study setups.

Weaknesses / requests for clarification
- Many strong assumptions (parametric f(s), maximum-entropy/unimodality constraints, discrete stimulus sets, fixed lapse models) underpin the main results; their cumulative effect is insufficiently stress-tested.
- Several approximations lack explicit conditions of validity.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Section 4.1: The interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising given near-Gaussianity in log space; please quantify it. For each design variant (BSL, SRT/LNG, MAP, MTR), report which differences in reconstruction error (e.g., symmetric KL to the true prior) are statistically reliable (credible intervals or bootstrap CIs). Also provide uncertainty on key parameters (σ, ρ1, σℓ).
- Section 4.2: You mention a flat-prior sanity check in the Supplement. Please bring a concise version into the main text (e.g., a panel showing recovery of kprior≈0 or equivalent) to demonstrate the method can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer.
- Line 106 (discrete vs continuous stimuli): Clarify whether discretization reflects the experimental design (finite set of si) or an approximation to a truly continuous generative stimulus. If it is purely for computation, discuss how Nexp and binning affect identifiability (bias/variance trade-off) and whether conclusions change as Nexp grows.
- Line 134 (log-stimulus transform): This is a strong representational assumption. Please assess sensitivity: show identifiability when the inference model mis-specifies d (e.g., true d=1, fit d∈{0.7,1.3}); provide ablations comparing log-like vs power-law transforms. Several degeneracies (e.g., σ vs ρ1) may hinge on this choice.
- Line 143 (unimodality): Do your constraints and maximum-entropy construction guarantee unimodality of the fitted measurement and loss distributions? Could the mixture parametrization permit unintended bimodality under some parameter settings? Please state conditions under which unimodality is enforced, or add a check/penalty in inference.
- Line 158 (prior lattice): Why favor a fixed Gaussian lattice over a uniform or adaptive basis (e.g., splines, log-normals with learned centers)? Also justify setting the prior support 50% wider than the true stimulus range—how would a user choose this without knowing the true range? Consider a hierarchical prior over [µmin, µmax] or show robustness to misspecification (edge effects).
- Figure 1: The current panelization is confusing. Consider integrating panel b into a single pipeline in panel a and removing the derived pest(s*|x); instead annotate flows and key densities at each stage. This should improve readability.
- Lines 252/Eqs. 14–15: Please justify all approximations precisely. For Stirling, specify the N (counts per bin or trials per stimulus) beyond which the error is negligible relative to your effect sizes. The approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] is not generally valid (x log x is convex). Provide an error bound (e.g., via Taylor/delta method with Var[x]) and state conditions (small CV of counts) under which the induced bias is acceptable. Eq. 15 currently reads as justified mainly by intuitive outcome; a formal bound or empirical validation plot would strengthen it.
- Line 262 (unnormalized density): Specify exactly which unnormalized density you sample from (Eq. 13, E(θ|θ*)). Clarify the role of Pi (dataset distribution), the proposal, diagnostics (ESS, R̂), and how you handle scale with Ntr (e.g., tempering).
- Line 365 (2AFC and priors): The claim that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC needs clarification. Priors can affect PSE and slopes unless they cancel symmetrically. Please state the conditions under which priors are irrelevant (e.g., symmetric priors, matched noise) and cite evidence; otherwise, nuance the claim or analyze the 2AFC variant within your framework.
- Reporting: Where you say parameters are “weakly identifiable,” please add quantification (posterior SDs, profile likelihoods) and, where possible, power analyses (how large Ntr must be for acceptable identifiability). Discuss computational cost (runtime, samples) and provide code to facilitate adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Important problem; principled, general framework with clear practical value.
- Solid derivation and efficient method; instructive case studies.
- Thoughtful design recommendations for a widely studied task (interval timing).

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on strong modeling assumptions; limited sensitivity analyses.
- Some approximations lack clear validity conditions.
- A few claims (e.g., 2AFC prior irrelevance) require clarification or tempering.

Overall evaluation
Above the NeurIPS threshold for methodology and clarity. With stronger justification of approximations, added robustness checks (especially around f(s) and basis choices), explicit uncertainty reporting, and clearer figures, this will be a valuable contribution that others can build on for experimental design and model validation in perception.
