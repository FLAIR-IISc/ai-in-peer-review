This paper presents a Bayesian framework for analyzing the identifiability of perceptual models, offering explicit equations for sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions. The core contribution lies in providing a systematic method to assess whether different combinations of model parameters can lead to indistinguishable behavioral predictions. The authors support their framework with two case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception – demonstrating its application and highlighting potential implications for experimental design. I support acceptance of this paper to NIPS, though with a significant revision request detailed below.

The paper is well-motivated, correctly identifying a critical issue in the application of Bayesian models to perception: the inherent degeneracy that prevents unique recovery of internal representations. The authors rightly emphasize the importance of identifiability, particularly as Bayesian models are increasingly interpreted as process models reflecting actual neural computations. The proposed framework builds on established Bayesian machinery and extends it with a practical method for exploring the parameter space and assessing potential non-identifiabilities. The mathematical formulation is rigorous and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying principles. The choice of case studies is relevant and illustrates the potential utility of the framework.

However, the contribution, in its current form, feels incremental. The identified issues of model non-identifiability are not entirely novel; the degeneracy inherent in Bayesian inference has been discussed previously (e.g., Bowers & Davis, 2017, cited in the paper, and earlier works like references [17, 18, 19] discussed within the manuscript). More critically, related work in this area – particularly Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) – is not adequately contextualized. While the authors acknowledge related work, they don’t sufficiently articulate *how* this framework advances beyond those prior efforts. Acerbi et al. (2014), for example, also addressed identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models, proposing methods based on Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling to evaluate the support for different parameter combinations. The present work's specific approach and advantages over existing techniques (such as Acerbi’s) need to be clearly delineated. 

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend the authors include a new section dedicated to reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models addressing identifiability, specifically including a detailed comparison to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014). This section should explicitly state how this work builds upon or differs from prior approaches, highlighting any novel algorithmic improvements, broader applicability, or increased computational efficiency. A clear articulation of these advancements is crucial for establishing the paper’s originality and significance.

Furthermore, the authors’ claim of “identifiability” needs qualification. As acknowledged, the identifiability conclusions hinge on the model assumptions *within* the framework. The paper rightly points out that the framework doesn’t resolve practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise. However, this limitation is somewhat understated. Even *if* a model is theoretically identifiable under specific assumptions, the real-world problem of disentangling these noise sources remains challenging. It is difficult to independently validate assumptions about noise distributions or loss functions. The framework provides a tool for analyzing *model* identifiability, but doesn’t offer a solution to the more fundamental problem of *practical* identifiability – i.e., determining which noise source is truly responsible for observed variability.

**Request:** The revised manuscript should include a more thorough discussion of this practical limitation. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on how model assumptions impact the interpretation of identifiability results and acknowledge the challenges of disentangling different noise sources in real-world data. Considering discussing strategies for validating model assumptions or acknowledging the need for complementary experimental approaches to address practical identifiability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Technically Sound:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous and the framework appears well-defined.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The issue of identifiability is crucial for the valid interpretation of Bayesian perceptual models.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are clearly explained.
*   **Potential for Impact:** The framework could be a valuable tool for researchers designing perceptual experiments and interpreting their results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance (mitigated by revision):**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** The core ideas are not entirely novel and build on existing work.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper does not adequately contextualize its contribution within the broader literature, particularly regarding Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014).
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Identifiability:** The paper downplays the challenges of disentangling noise sources in real-world data.



In conclusion, this is a solid paper with the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. However, addressing the concerns regarding originality and the discussion of practical limitations, through a revised manuscript and incorporation of a more comprehensive comparison to related work, is crucial for justifying acceptance to NIPS.
