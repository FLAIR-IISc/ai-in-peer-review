This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, specifically addressing the issue of degeneracy in the Bayesian framework where multiple combinations of elements can yield empirically indistinguishable results. The authors introduce a continuous class of Bayesian observer models parametrized by a vector θ, which encompasses a wide range of sensorimotor estimation tasks. They then develop a method to efficiently explore the identifiability of a given observer model within this framework.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a general framework for studying a priori identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework through two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task. The results show that the reconstruction of internal representations in interval timing requires strong experimental constraints and validations, while the inference of internal representations in speed perception is generally robust and not prone to major degeneracies.

The strengths of this paper include its clear and concise presentation of the framework, the thorough analysis of the case studies, and the discussion of the practical implications of the results. The authors also provide a detailed description of the Bayesian observer model, which is essential for understanding the framework.

However, there are some weaknesses to this paper. Firstly, the contribution of this work is incremental and closely resembles recent work in the field (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014). While the authors do provide some new insights and results, the overall framework and approach are not significantly different from existing work. Secondly, the identifiability claims made in the paper hinge on model assumptions and do not resolve practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise. This limitation should be discussed in more detail in the revised manuscript.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. However, as mentioned earlier, the contribution is incremental and builds upon existing work in the field. The authors could strengthen their contribution by clearly stating how their work advances existing Bayesian perceptual models.

The significance of this paper lies in its potential to improve experimental design in psychophysics and to provide a more robust understanding of internal representations in perception. The results of the case studies demonstrate the importance of considering identifiability in experimental design and provide insights into the robustness of internal representations in different tasks.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of the framework and the case studies. The use of equations and figures is effective in illustrating the key concepts and results.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS, but with some revisions. The authors should include a new section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly state how their work advances them. Additionally, they should discuss the practical identifiability limitation in more detail in the revised manuscript.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of two case studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of their framework.
* The results have practical implications for experimental design in psychophysics and provide insights into internal representations in perception.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The contribution of this work is incremental and closely resembles recent work in the field.
* The identifiability claims made in the paper hinge on model assumptions and do not resolve practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise.

Recommendations for revision:

* Include a new section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly state how this work advances them.
* Discuss the practical identifiability limitation in more detail in the revised manuscript.
* Consider strengthening the contribution by providing more novel insights and results that differentiate this work from existing research in the field.
