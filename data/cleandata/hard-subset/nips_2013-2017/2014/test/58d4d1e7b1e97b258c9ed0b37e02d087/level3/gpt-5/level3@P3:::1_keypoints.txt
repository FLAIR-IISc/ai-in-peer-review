- Proposes a framework for a priori identifiability in Bayesian observer models, formalizing invariances and deriving an expected log-likelihood (Eq. 12) as a KL-weighted identifiability metric.  
- Introduces constraints/assumptions to regularize the inverse problem and tests identifiability via sampling an expected posterior E(θ|θ*) and inspecting peakedness.  
- Applies the framework to two case studies (interval timing; speed perception) and gives practical design recommendations (e.g., measure motor noise; avoid overly narrow loss).  

Strengths:
- Tackles an important, longstanding identifiability problem in cognitive science.  
- Provides methodological clarity: reduces expected log evidence to an interpretable KL-sum and offers simulation-based identifiability maps.  
- Delivers practical experimental guidance and clear, well-organized case studies and figures.  

Major concerns:
- Insufficient engagement with prior model comparison/recovery literature (e.g., Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004; Myung’s work; Sanborn & Griffiths 2010), needing clearer novelty claims.  
- Measurement model (Eq. 3) appears ad hoc (two-component Gaussian mixture); requires justification and sensitivity to number of components, skew/kurtosis, and alternative likelihood families.  
- Sensory transform f(s) (Eq. 2) lacks justification; sensitivity to alternative mappings (log, power-law) and priors on (s0, d) must be tested.  
- Loss function parameterization (Eq. 6) is flexible but shows weak identifiability for σℓ; need to assess dependence of prior reconstruction on constraining σℓ or measuring loss separately.  
- Eq. 12 and identifiability conclusions depend strongly on stimulus distribution Pi, Nexp, and Ntr; require design-sensitivity analyses and consideration of KL asymmetry vs. other divergences.  
- Prior representation (Eq. 4) using a 15-component Gaussian lattice needs checks on M, range, and spacing, especially for heavy-tailed priors.  

Quality:
- Derivation to Eq. 12 is correct and useful, but arbitrary parametric choices without systematic ablations weaken conclusions.  

Clarity:
- Paper is clear but should add an explicit “Assumptions and constraints that ensure identifiability” section distinguishing experimentally controllable vs. theoretical commitments.  

Originality:
- Focus on a priori identifiability and the observer-class parameterization is valuable, but novelty is unclear without stronger positioning vs. existing distinguishability/recovery work.  

Significance:
- High potential impact for experimental design and qualifying claims about recovered internal representations if concerns are addressed.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important issue with a clear, interpretable metric and useful case studies offering non-obvious design trade-offs.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks sufficient engagement with prior literature, contains arbitrary modeling choices, and draws conclusions that may depend on those choices.  

Recommendations to authors:
- Expand related work and clarify what is new relative to prior model recovery/distinguishability literature.  
- Provide sensitivity analyses for mixture components (likelihood/loss), prior basis size/range, transform f(s), and internal vs. external likelihood coupling.  
- Explicitly justify constraints enabling identifiability and indicate which can be independently measured (e.g., motor/loss calibration).  
- Add complementary diagnostics (Fisher information, profile likelihoods, parameter recovery plots) and consider targeted/adaptive stimulus designs to maximize KL separation.  

Minor comments:
- Spell out the one-line derivation for the invariance under multiplicative φi(s) in Eq. 1; fix a wording typo (“nor” → “or”); clarify figure symbols and caption conventions.  

Summary judgment:
- Well-written and important paper that would be substantially strengthened by deeper engagement with prior work and systematic sensitivity analyses of key modeling assumptions.
