- Paper tackles identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss) in Bayesian perceptual models and the inverse problem’s degeneracies.  
- Proposes a systematic a priori identifiability-testing framework using KL divergence to quantify experimental design’s ability to resolve parameter ambiguity.  
- Builds on prior Bayesian perceptual work (e.g., Maloney & Mamassian, Acerbi et al.) but focuses on a diagnostic methodology rather than a new perceptual model.  
- Framework is mathematically rigorous and practically applicable, demonstrated in two case studies.  
- Temporal interval estimation case shows experimental design choices (e.g., measuring motor noise, avoiding narrow loss functions) strongly affect prior recovery and identifiability.  
- Speed perception case validates previous slow-speed prior findings and shows robust identifiability under standard designs.  
- Model integrates stimulus-dependent noise via f(s), flexible likelihoods/priors (mixture Gaussians), and loss functions (inverted Gaussian mixtures) in a tractable framework.  
- Provides an efficient computational approach (MCMC on expected log-likelihood) to explore identifiability without expensive simulations, making it scalable.  
- Fills a gap where identifiability was often handled ad hoc in prior work, offering a systematic a priori tool for experimental design.  
- Novelty is incremental: using KL divergence for identifiability is conceptually similar to prior statistical techniques and has been implicitly used in perceptual modeling.  
- Paper doesn’t sufficiently distinguish its contribution from recent NIPS works (Acerbi 2012/2014); needs clearer emphasis on quantifying degeneracy a priori versus resolving it post hoc.  
- Framework depends on assumed observer models (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific loss functions); real-world confounds (motor noise, decision lapses) may undermine identifiability and require extra constraints.  
- Authors should add a dedicated comparison to recent Bayesian perceptual models and explicitly discuss practical identifiability caveats and required empirical validations.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong, generalizable, and useful for experimental design; novelty is modest but merits acceptance after minor revisions.
