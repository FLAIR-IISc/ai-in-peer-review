- Paper proposes a practical framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks.  
- Formalizes degeneracy in Bayesian Decision Theory: different priors, likelihoods, and losses can produce indistinguishable behavior.  
- Introduces a flexible, computationally efficient 1D observer class with parametric sensory maps, mixture-of-Gaussians measurement and loss models, lapse and motor noise.  
- Derives an expected log-likelihood discriminability criterion that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimulus conditions.  
- Uses MCMC to map the “approximate expected posterior” over parameters θ given a reference θ*.  
- Case study (interval timing): measuring motor parameters improves prior recoverability; narrow near-MAP loss harms prior recovery.  
- Case study (speed perception): recovers slow-speed prior exponent and noise characteristics robustly.  
- Situates work within Bayesian perception and longstanding identifiability/model-mimicry literature, but could better position against model discrimination and Bayesian design methods.  
- Strength: addresses an important problem—non-identifiability—providing an a priori design tool.  
- Strength: elegant, tractable reduction of expected log evidence to weighted KLs and fast evaluation via mixture-based observer.  
- Strength: yields actionable experimental guidance (e.g., measure motor noise; avoid near-MAP loss regimes).  
- Strength: validated across two perceptual tasks and reproduces known effects.  
- Weakness: several modeling choices (two-component mixtures, max-entropy selection, sensory map) appear arbitrary and under-justified.  
- Weakness: lacks sensitivity analyses for mixture components, alternative loss families, lapse models, nonparametric priors, and prior-basis size M.  
- Weakness: identifiability metric depends on asymmetric KL and assumed θ*; lacks comparisons to symmetrized distances (JS, Hellinger) or local analyses (Fisher information).  
- Weakness: missing MCMC and computational details (priors, proposals, convergence diagnostics, runtime) limiting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: originality moderate—overlaps with existing model distinguishability and experimental design work and insufficiently engages that literature.  
- Weakness: scope limited to 1D continuous estimation; lacks discrete-choice/2AFC demonstration which is common in speed-prior literature.  
- Quality: derivations generally sound but require sensitivity checks and fuller algorithmic details.  
- Clarity: well written and organized, but some annotations and derivations need clearer explanation.  
- Originality: moderate—useful synthesis but novelty relative to prior design/distinguishability methods should be clarified.  
- Significance: high for perception and cognitive modeling; could influence experimental planning and claims about internal representations.  
- Suggestion: perform ablations/sensitivity analyses for mixture components, loss families, sensory maps, lapse models, and prior basis size.  
- Suggestion: compare framework to Fisher information, expected information gain, and Bayesian optimal design to clarify added value.  
- Suggestion: report MCMC settings, parameter priors, convergence checks, runtimes, and guidance on Ntr/stimulus allocation.  
- Suggestion: discuss dependence of Eq.12 on Ntr and Pi and test alternative divergences to verify design rankings.  
- Suggestion: provide a 2AFC/discrete-choice instantiation or sketch in supplement to broaden applicability.  
- Minor: explicitly show scaling degeneracy proof, fix wording (Line 73), clarify Figure 2 markers and CI bands.  
- Minor: specify whether lapse samples are in task or internal space and Jacobian handling (Eq.7); add references on Bayesian experimental design.  
- Pros for acceptance: timely problem, concrete usable framework, clear derivations, actionable recommendations, informative case studies.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient literature engagement, heavy reliance on parametric assumptions without sensitivity checks, limited to 1D estimation.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on stronger literature positioning and added sensitivity analyses to show robustness.
