This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue in cognitive science. The authors provide a thorough review of the Bayesian Decision Theory and its applications in psychophysics, highlighting the problem of non-identifiability of internal representations. They then introduce a new class of Bayesian observer models, which are both flexible and efficient, and present a method for exploring the identifiability of these models. The framework is applied to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The paper is well-written, and the authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the theoretical issues surrounding Bayesian models of perception. The framework they propose is a significant contribution to the field, as it provides a systematic way to test the identifiability of Bayesian models. The application of the framework to two case studies demonstrates its potential for improving experimental design and understanding the robustness of internal representations.

However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the paper largely ignores substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science, making its novel contribution unclear. For example, the work of Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) on model comparison and identifiability is not mentioned. The authors should discuss and justify the chosen constraints and assumptions that make Bayesian models identifiable, and clarify how their framework relates to previous work in the field.

Secondly, some key modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified. For instance, the definition of the measurement distribution pmeas in Eq. 3 and the parameter choices in Eq. 12 seem to be based on convenience rather than theoretical justification. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of these assumptions and their impact on the results.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the authors should be more explicit about how their framework differs from previous work and what new insights it provides.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a crucial issue in cognitive science and provides a new tool for improving experimental design and understanding internal representations. The results of the case studies demonstrate the potential of the framework for advancing our understanding of perception and cognition.

Overall, this is a well-written and important paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of cognitive science. However, the authors should address the concerns mentioned above to strengthen their contribution and provide a clearer understanding of their framework's novelty and significance.
