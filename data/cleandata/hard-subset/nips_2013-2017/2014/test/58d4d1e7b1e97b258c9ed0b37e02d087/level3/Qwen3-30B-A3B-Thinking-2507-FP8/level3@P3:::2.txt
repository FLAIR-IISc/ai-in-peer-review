

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian observer models. The authors propose a systematic framework for testing whether parameters of Bayesian models (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, given the well-known degeneracy in the Bayesian framework where multiple combinations of model components can produce identical behavior. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it provides a tool to validate the conclusions drawn from Bayesian models of perception, which have been widely used to recover hidden mental representations.

The paper builds upon the extensive literature on Bayesian models of perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 2003; Maloney & Mamassian, 2002; Körding & Wolpert, 2004) and addresses a gap in the field: the lack of systematic analysis of model identifiability. The authors develop a general class of Bayesian observer models that encompasses a wide range of psychophysical tasks, and present an efficient method to explore the identifiability landscape of these models. The framework is applied to two case studies: time interval estimation and speed perception, demonstrating how experimental design choices affect the reliability of recovered internal representations.

**Strengths:**
The paper is clearly written and presents an interesting methodology that addresses a fundamental problem in cognitive modeling. The framework is well-motivated, and the case studies provide concrete applications of the approach. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation and demonstrate how their method can guide experimental design to improve identifiability. The work is technically sound and represents a significant contribution to the field of cognitive modeling.

**Weaknesses:**
While the paper makes an important contribution, several issues reduce the impact of the results relative to the length of the assumptions list.

1. The main results rely on many strong assumptions that require justification. For example, the log-stimulus transform (Line 134) is a strong constraint that may significantly affect identifiability, yet the authors do not adequately justify why this representational assumption is necessary or reasonable.

2. In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising (as noted in the key points), but the authors should report which condition differences are statistically reliable rather than making qualitative claims.

3. In Section 4.2, the authors claim that the reconstructed representations in speed perception are robust, but they must demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer (as shown in the FLA case in the supplementary material, but not clearly stated in the main text).

4. The assumption of a lattice prior (Line 158) is not adequately justified. The authors should explain why the observer prior should favor a lattice over uniform representation or how a user would justifiably set a prior wider than the true stimulus range.

5. Figure 1 is confusing; integrating panel b into a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x) would improve clarity.

6. The approximations in Line 252/Equations 14-15 (Stirling, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) require justification, including conditions on N where they hold. Equation 15 seems justified mainly by intuitive outcome rather than theoretical derivation.

7. Line 262 is unclear about which "unnormalized density" is being sampled from—this should be specified (dataset distribution?).

8. The claim in Line 365 that "learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks" requires clarification, as this contradicts previous work on prior learning in perceptual tasks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-structured, but the reliance on strong assumptions without sufficient justification weakens the conclusions. The authors acknowledge some limitations but don't fully address how these assumptions impact their results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but several technical points require clarification as noted above. The supplementary material provides additional details, but key methodological choices should be better justified in the main text.

**Originality:** The framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian models is novel and fills an important gap in the literature. The approach extends previous work on model identifiability (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004) to the specific context of Bayesian models of perception.

**Significance:** This work is significant because it provides a tool to validate the conclusions drawn from Bayesian models of perception, which have been used to make strong claims about internal representations. By demonstrating how experimental design affects identifiability, the paper helps researchers avoid drawing incorrect conclusions from their data.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable framework for addressing a fundamental problem in cognitive modeling. While the current version has some weaknesses that need addressing (primarily related to assumptions and justifications), these are fixable in a revision. The methodology is sound, the problem is important, and the paper makes a clear contribution to the field. The authors should address the specific concerns raised above to strengthen their conclusions and increase the impact of their work.
