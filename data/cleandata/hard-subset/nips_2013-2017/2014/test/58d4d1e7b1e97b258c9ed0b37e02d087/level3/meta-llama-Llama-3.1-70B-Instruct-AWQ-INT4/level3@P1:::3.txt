This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. The authors introduce a flexible and efficient class of observer models that can be used to model a wide range of psychophysical tasks. They then develop a method for exploring the identifiability of these models within their framework. The authors apply their framework to two case studies, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving experimental design and recovering internal representations.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of their framework and its applications. The model they propose is mathematically elegant and provides a general framework for formalizing perceptual estimation. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach, including the potential for non-identifiability of certain model parameters.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a systematic and a priori investigation of identifiability. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it allows researchers to evaluate the effectiveness of different experimental designs before running an experiment. The authors also demonstrate the practical applicability of their framework by applying it to two real-world case studies.

However, the reviewer notes that the contribution of the paper is incremental and closely resembles recent work in the field. To address this concern, the authors could include a new section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly stating how their work advances them. This would help to provide a clearer understanding of the significance of their contribution and how it builds upon existing research.

Another concern raised by the reviewer is that the identifiability claims made by the authors hinge on model assumptions and do not resolve practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise. The authors should discuss this practical identifiability limitation in the revised manuscript, providing a clearer understanding of the limitations of their approach and how they can be addressed in future research.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian perception and provides a valuable tool for researchers to evaluate the effectiveness of different experimental designs. With some revisions to address the concerns raised by the reviewer, the paper has the potential to make an even more significant impact.
