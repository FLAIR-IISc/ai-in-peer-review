This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, addressing a long-standing issue in cognitive science. The authors provide a comprehensive and well-written treatment of the topic, tackling an important theoretical issue. 

The paper's main contribution is the development of a framework for a systematic, a priori investigation of identifiability in Bayesian observer models. The authors introduce a novel class of observer models that is both flexible and efficient, allowing for the exploration of a vast identifiability landscape. They apply their framework to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task.

The paper's strengths include its clear and concise writing, making it easy to follow for non-experts in the field. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the topic, including a review of the relevant literature. The framework they propose is well-motivated and based on a solid understanding of Bayesian decision theory.

However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's originality and contribution to the field. The authors largely ignore substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science, making their novel contribution unclear. For example, Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) have previously addressed similar issues. The authors should discuss and justify the chosen constraints/assumptions that make Bayesian models identifiable, as some of these assumptions appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified.

Additionally, the paper's results could be more thoroughly validated. While the authors provide some theoretical validation, it would be beneficial to see more empirical support for their claims. The paper's conclusions, although well-motivated, could be more thoroughly discussed in the context of existing literature.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized treatment of the topic. The paper is well-written, and the authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

Regarding originality, while the paper's framework is novel, it builds upon existing work in the field. The authors should more clearly highlight how their contribution differs from previous work.

Finally, the paper's significance is high, as it addresses a long-standing issue in cognitive science. The authors' framework has the potential to be widely applicable and could contribute to a better understanding of Bayesian models of perception.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper addresses a long-standing issue in cognitive science.
* The authors provide a comprehensive and well-written treatment of the topic.
* The framework they propose is well-motivated and based on a solid understanding of Bayesian decision theory.
* The paper has the potential to be widely applicable and could contribute to a better understanding of Bayesian models of perception.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The authors largely ignore substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science.
* Some of the assumptions made by the authors appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified.
* The paper's results could be more thoroughly validated.
* The authors should more clearly highlight how their contribution differs from previous work.
