- Paper proposes a systematic framework to test identifiability of Bayesian observer models (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data.  
- Framework addresses degeneracy in Bayesian models and helps validate conclusions about internal representations.  
- Develops a general class of Bayesian observer models and an efficient method to explore identifiability across psychophysical tasks.  
- Applied framework to two case studies (time-interval estimation and speed perception) showing how experimental design affects identifiability.  
- Strength: clearly written, well-motivated methodology with solid theoretical foundation and practical guidance for experimental design.  
- Strength: technically sound contribution that fills a gap in cognitive modeling literature on identifiability.  
- Weakness: many strong assumptions (e.g., log-stimulus transform) lack sufficient justification and may strongly affect identifiability.  
- Weakness: Section 4.1 claims about interchangeability of sensory and motor noise are unsurprising; statistical tests should be reported rather than qualitative statements.  
- Weakness: Section 4.2 claims robustness in reconstructed speed priors but fails to demonstrate recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior in the main text.  
- Weakness: assumption of a lattice prior is not justified—authors must explain why observers would favor lattice priors or how to set priors wider than true stimulus range.  
- Weakness: Figure 1 is confusing; recommend merging panels and removing redundant derived quantities for clarity.  
- Weakness: approximations in Equations 14–15 (Stirling, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) need justification and conditions on N where they hold.  
- Weakness: unclear statement about which "unnormalized density" is sampled (dataset distribution vs. other); needs specification.  
- Weakness: claim that "learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks" needs clarification as it contradicts prior literature on prior learning.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and novel framework with important implications, but reliance on several unjustified assumptions weakens conclusions; recommend acceptance pending revisions to address these issues.
