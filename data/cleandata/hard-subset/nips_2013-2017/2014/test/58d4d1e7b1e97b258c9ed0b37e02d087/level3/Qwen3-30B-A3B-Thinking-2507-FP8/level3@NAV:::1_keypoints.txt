- Proposes a systematic framework for testing identifiability in Bayesian observer models, addressing a critical issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, making a theoretically significant contribution by enabling a priori identifiability evaluation for experimental design.  
- Demonstrates framework utility with concrete applications to time interval estimation and speed perception.  
- Strength: clearly articulates the identifiability problem and shows experimentally that design choices affect parameter recovery.  
- Shows that measuring motor parameters improves identifiability while enforcing narrow loss functions can harm it.  
- Methodological strength: uses KL divergence to quantify expected model support; this approach is theoretically sound and computationally efficient.  
- Major weakness: largely ignores substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science (e.g., Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004), leaving novelty unclear.  
- Fails to position the framework within existing methodological discussions and to explain how it differs from or improves on prior approaches.  
- Key modeling assumptions (e.g., definition of p_meas in Eq. 3 and parameter choices in Eq. 12) appear arbitrary and are insufficiently justified.  
- Lacks sensitivity analysis showing how results depend on those assumptions, which undermines confidence in the identifiability conclusions.  
- Minor fixes: explain the "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53, change "nor" to "or" at Line 73, and clarify the star and dashed lines in Figure 2's legend.
