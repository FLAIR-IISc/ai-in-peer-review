- Proposes a novel, clear, and well-organized framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian perception models.  
- Methodology is interesting, rigorous, flexible, and efficient for exploring large classes of observer models.  
- Main strength: systematic analysis of identifiability, addressing a gap in the field and aiding experimental design.  
- Main results depend on strong assumptions, reducing their generality and impact.  
- Assumes observer's internal likelihood is expressed in the same measurement space as the true likelihood, which may not hold.  
- Use of a maximum-entropy approach to shape the measurement distribution is not universally justified.  
- Time-interval estimation analysis is thorough but yields some unsurprising results (e.g., interchangeability of sensory and motor noise).  
- Authors should report which condition differences are statistically reliable.  
- In the speed-perception task, reconstruction appears reliable but must demonstrate recovery of an absent slow-speed prior in the reference observer.  
- Strong constraint from the log-stimulus transform; identifiability dependence on this assumption is unclear.  
- Claim that constraints and entropy maximization guarantee unimodality is unjustified; bimodality should be investigated.  
- Figures are unclear (Figure 1 confusing); suggest integrating panel b into a and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) for clarity.  
- Approximations (Stirling, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) need justification and conditions for validity.  
- Significance: framework can improve experimental design and clarify parameter degeneracies in Bayesian perception models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses critical issue, rigorous systematic analysis, strong methodological fit, and potential practical impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on strong assumptions, some predictable results, unjustified use of max-entropy and log-transform, unclear figures and unsupported approximations.
