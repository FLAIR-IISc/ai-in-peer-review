- Proposes a framework for testing identifiability of Bayesian perceptual models and demonstrates it on time interval estimation and motion perception.  
- Highlights the longstanding identifiability problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling and recent critiques motivating this work.  
- Writing is clear and the paper is well-organized, making a complex topic accessible.  
- Technically sound and comprehensive: provides mathematical analysis of non-identifiability and a practical method using KL divergence and MCMC.  
- Case studies effectively demonstrate applicability and potential to inform experimental design.  
- The unified model parametrization covering many perceptual tasks is a notable achievement.  
- Major omission: fails to engage with broader model-comparison literature (e.g., Model Evidence, BIC, ABC) and doesn’t clarify differences in cost, assumptions, or applicability.  
- Modeling assumptions feel arbitrary (e.g., choice of moments for pmeas and two-Gaussian mixture) and lack strong justification.  
- Parameter choices for the reference observer (Eq. 12) lack sensitivity analysis, leaving robustness of results uncertain.  
- Claim that slow-speed prior inference is “relatively reliable” needs qualification under different noise models and loss functions.  
- Recommended fixes: compare and contrast with existing model-comparison techniques and provide rigorous justification plus sensitivity analyses for key assumptions.  
- Minor edits: clarify the “trivial mathematical fact” on Line 53, change “nor” to “or” on Line 73, and explain the star/dashed lines in Figure 2 caption.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a fundamental issue, presents a novel framework, is well-written, and includes compelling case studies.  
- Rejection concerns: omission of relevant literature, arbitrary assumptions without sensitivity analysis, and limited discussion of limitations and biases.  
- Summary judgment: recommend acceptance with minor revisions contingent on addressing the literature gap and strengthening justification/sensitivity analyses.
