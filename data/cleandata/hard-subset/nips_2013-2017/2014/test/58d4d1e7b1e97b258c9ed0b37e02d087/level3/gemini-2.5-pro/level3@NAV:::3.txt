This paper presents a formal framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science. The authors address a critical and well-known issue: the degeneracy of Bayesian models, where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield empirically indistinguishable behavior. The proposed solution is a mathematically elegant and general Bayesian model that combines representational flexibility (e.g., stimulus-dependent sensory noise, flexible priors) with computational tractability. The core of their method involves calculating an expected posterior density over model parameters to map an "identifiability landscape," allowing researchers to evaluate experimental designs before data collection. This work builds upon a significant body of recent literature aiming to fit complex, psychologically plausible Bayesian models to behavioral data (e.g., Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2009).

The primary strength of this paper is its formal and systematic approach to a fundamental problem in computational modeling. The proposed observer model is comprehensive, with explicit parametric forms for sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response stages, making it a valuable tool in its own right. The framework's application to case studies in time and speed perception effectively demonstrates its utility for comparing the merits of different experimental designs. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a significant issue for researchers using Bayesian models to infer latent cognitive representations.

The main weakness, however, is that the contribution feels incremental. The observer model, while well-specified, closely resembles the structure and components of models in very recent work (particularly Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014), which is not sufficiently discussed. To strengthen the paper's claim to originality, I strongly recommend adding a new section that systematically reviews these recent models and clearly articulates how the current framework advances beyond them.

A second concern is the claim of identifiability. While the framework can assess *mathematical* identifiability within its own set of assumptions, it does not resolve the more stubborn *practical* difficulty of distinguishing between different sources of variability, such as sensory, decision, and motor/response noise. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation in a revised manuscript to provide a more nuanced account of what their framework can and cannot achieve.

In summary, this is a high-quality contribution that formalizes an important methodological problem. I support its acceptance to NIPS, provided the authors revise the manuscript to better situate their work within the existing literature and clarify the practical limits of their identifiability analysis.
