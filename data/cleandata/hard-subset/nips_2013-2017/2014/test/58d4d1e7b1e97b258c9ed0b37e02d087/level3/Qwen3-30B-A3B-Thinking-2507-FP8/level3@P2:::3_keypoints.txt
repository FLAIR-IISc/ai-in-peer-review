- Introduces a framework to test identifiability of Bayesian observer models a priori, addressing that different model component combinations can yield identical behavior.  
- Extends prior Bayesian observer models with a flexible parametrization of sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response while keeping computations tractable.  
- Formalizes identifiability using KL divergence between response probability densities as a measure of model support.  
- Demonstrates framework utility with two case studies: interval timing and speed perception.  
- Interval timing result: narrow (MAP-like) loss functions can worsen reconstruction of internal priors; experimental design strongly affects prior recovery.  
- Speed perception result: prior and noise characteristics can be reliably recovered, supporting slow-speed prior findings.  
- Strength: rigorous mathematical foundation linking identifiability to practical experimental design choices.  
- Strength: case studies reveal non-intuitive design effects; model is flexible and reproducible.  
- Weakness: contribution seems incremental and closely related to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014); insufficient explicit differentiation.  
- Weakness: identifiability claims heavily depend on model assumptions and do not resolve distinctions among sensory, decision, and response noise.  
- Weakness: limited demonstration of practical impact—more concrete examples of how the framework would change real experiments are needed.  
- Recommendations: add a section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clarify advances; more thoroughly discuss practical limitations (especially noise distinctions); clarify scope and extensions to complex tasks.  
- Overall: solid, useful methodological contribution with practical implications; recommends acceptance pending the suggested revisions.
