The paper "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception" proposes a novel framework for addressing the long-standing problem of model identifiability in Bayesian models of perception. The authors provide a clear and concise summary of Bayesian Decision Theory and its application to psychophysical tasks, highlighting the issue of non-identifiability that arises due to the degeneracy of the Bayesian framework.

The authors propose a framework for testing the identifiability of a significant class of Bayesian observer models, which includes a general class of observer models parametrized by a vector θ. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework through two case studies: the inference of priors in a time interval estimation task and the reconstruction of prior and noise characteristics in speed perception.

The paper is well-written and tackles an important theoretical issue in the field of cognitive science. However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the contribution and the justification of key modeling assumptions.

One major concern is that the paper largely ignores substantial prior literature on model comparison in cognitive science, making its novel contribution unclear. For example, the work of Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science is not discussed. The authors should provide a more thorough review of the existing literature and clarify how their framework builds upon or differs from previous work.

Another major concern is that some key modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or insufficiently justified. For example, the definition of pmeas in Eq. 3 and the parameter choices in Eq. 12 seem to be based on convenience rather than empirical evidence or theoretical justification. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation and justification for these assumptions and clarify how dependent the results are on these assumptions.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their framework and its application to the two case studies.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a good introduction to the problem of model identifiability and its significance in cognitive science. The technical details are well-explained, and the results are clearly presented.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception. However, as mentioned earlier, the novelty of the contribution is unclear due to the lack of discussion of prior literature.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important theoretical issue in cognitive science, and the results have implications for the design of experiments and the interpretation of data in psychophysical tasks.

Overall, I would recommend that the authors address the concerns mentioned above by providing a more thorough review of the existing literature and justifying their key modeling assumptions. With these revisions, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of cognitive science.
