Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a general, explicit Bayesian observer model for 1D perceptual estimation and a framework to assess a priori identifiability of its components under different experimental designs. The model specifies, with equations, the full decision pipeline: stimulus-dependent sensory noise and a monotonic mapping f(s) to internal space (Eqs. 2–3), a flexible prior as a Gaussian-mixture lattice (Eq. 4), an explicit loss function as an inverted Gaussian mixture (Eq. 6), optimal Bayesian estimation (Eq. 5) with lapses (Eq. 7), and a response stage with additive motor noise (Eq. 8), yielding the observable response distribution (Eq. 9). Identifiability is quantified via the expected log-likelihood over datasets generated by a reference observer, which reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions (Eq. 12); sampling an approximate expected posterior (Eq. 13) maps practically indistinguishable parameter regions.

The framework is applied to two case studies: interval timing (showing poor recovery of priors unless motor parameters are constrained; MAP-like losses can worsen inference) and speed perception (recovering a slow-speed prior and noise characteristics robustly). The paper is squarely in the line of Bayesian observer modeling in perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Maloney & Mamassian) and builds on recent model-based inference of internal representations and suboptimality (Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al. 2013) and on general model identifiability work (Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004). The authors’ emphasis is on mathematical identifiability and tractable computations within a flexible, yet analytically convenient, parameterization.

Strengths
- Clear, explicit formalization: likelihood, prior, loss, lapse, and response noise are all parameterized with equations that admit efficient computation (e.g., analytical mixtures for expected loss).
- Robust yet tractable: mixture-based likelihoods capture stimulus-dependent noise and non-Gaussian features while preserving computational convenience and enabling MCMC over an expected-posterior objective.
- Identifiability metric with design utility: the expected log-evidence equals a KL-weighted sum across stimuli, providing an interpretable, scalable proxy for discriminability that naturally increases with Ntrials.
- Actionable insights from case studies: demonstrates when priors are or are not recoverable; highlights motor–sensory confounds; validates robustness of slow-speed prior inference.
- Generality: a single parameterization covers many 1D estimation tasks and can, in principle, extend to angles, multi-cue settings, or discrete-choice variants.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Incremental contribution: Many modeling ingredients (measurement transform, Gaussian-mixture likelihoods/priors, explicit loss, lapse, motor noise) and the focus on recovering internal representations closely resemble existing Bayesian perceptual models (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al. 2013). The novelty lies primarily in packaging, an explicit KL-based expected log-evidence, and an emphasis on design-level identifiability.
- Scope of identifiability claims: Mathematical identifiability is demonstrated within a specific parametric family and under strong assumptions (e.g., unimodality, mixture forms, shared parameters across conditions). In practice, the framework does not resolve the classic confound between sensory noise, decision noise/loss, and response (motor) noise—indeed, the timing example re-exposes these degeneracies (σ vs ρ1, weak identifiability of σ`). The claims should be tempered and clearly framed as conditional on assumptions.
- Limited empirical validation: Results are simulation-based; no new human data or prospective design optimization study. The speed example is a reanalysis cast as estimation, not a full re-fit to existing 2AFC data.
- Related work coverage: The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion situating this framework among recent Bayesian perceptual modeling, model recovery/identifiability, and broader Bayesian experimental design literatures.

Quality
Technically sound and internally consistent. The KL-based derivation is appropriate, the model is well-specified, and the case studies are informative. More sensitivity analyses to misspecification (e.g., true loss not in the mixture family; different f(s)) would strengthen robustness claims.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized with precise notation and explicit equations. Suggestions: unify p vs q notation, summarize assumptions in a table/box, and provide a schematic of the identifiability pipeline and design choices. Report MCMC details, priors on parameters, computational cost, and sensitivity to the number of prior mixture components (M).

Originality
Moderate. The unification and identifiability emphasis are valuable, but overlap with earlier frameworks is substantial.

Significance
Moderate to high for the Bayesian perception community. The framework can inform experimental design and calibrate expectations about what can be recovered from behavior.

Specific suggestions for revision
- Add a dedicated related-work section that reviews recent Bayesian perceptual models aimed at recovering priors/likelihoods/losses (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al. 2013; Natarajan et al. 2010; Hedges et al. 2011; Kwon & Knill 2013), and clearly state how your framework advances them (unified parameterization, analytic expected loss, KL-based identifiability objective, and design comparison).
- Explicitly discuss practical identifiability limits: clarify that, absent independent constraints or manipulations, the framework cannot disentangle sensory, decision, and response noise. Provide guidance on experimental strategies that help (e.g., motor-only calibrations, feedback manipulations to probe loss, Bayesian transfer across tasks/conditions, multiple response modalities).
- Include sensitivity analyses to model misspecification (non-Gaussian heavy-tailed loss; different f(s); incorrect mixture orders) and to the choice of M, and demonstrate how violations impact identifiability maps.
- Connect Eq. 12 to experimental design: frame it as a design utility and illustrate automatic optimization over stimulus sets or condition allocations.
- Provide implementation details and code for reproducibility (parameter priors, MCMC settings, run times).

Arguments pro acceptance
- Elegant, general, and explicit modeling framework with tractable inference.
- Clear, quantitative identifiability criterion with practical design implications.
- Insightful case studies that reveal nontrivial confounds and robust regimes.
- High relevance to a large and active research area at NIPS.

Arguments against acceptance
- Contribution is incremental relative to closely related recent work.
- Identifiability claims hinge on restrictive assumptions; practical disentanglement of noise sources remains unresolved.
- No new empirical data; limited robustness analyses and related-work coverage.

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The paper offers a mathematically elegant and practically useful framework for formalizing and probing identifiability in Bayesian perceptual estimation. Please address the related-work positioning and expand the discussion of practical identifiability limitations as outlined above.
