This paper presents a comprehensive framework for testing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual estimation tasks. The authors address a critical issue in computational cognitive science: the intrinsic degeneracy in Bayesian models, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral predictions. To tackle this, they construct a flexible and computationally tractable observer model that covers a wide class of one-dimensional estimation tasks. The model explicitly parameterizes each stage of the perceptual process: sensation (including stimulus-dependent noise via a sensory mapping), perception (flexible priors and likelihoods represented as Gaussian mixtures), decision-making (flexible loss functions, also as Gaussian mixtures), and response (motor noise and lapses). The central contribution is a method, based on the expected log-likelihood and Kullback-Leibler divergence, to map the "identifiability landscape" of a given model and experimental design. This allows researchers to assess, before running an experiment, which parameters can be reliably recovered. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework through two case studies: one on time interval estimation, showing how different experimental designs affect the recovery of the observer's prior, and another on speed perception, validating the robustness of inferring a "slow-speed prior." This work relates to a significant body of literature in psychophysics and computational neuroscience that uses Bayesian models to infer internal representations (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006; Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010; Acerbi et al., 2012).

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of model non-identifiability is a major, often overlooked, challenge for the field. As Bayesian observer models become more complex and are used to make stronger claims about internal representations, a principled framework to assess their limitations is highly valuable. This paper provides a concrete tool to improve experimental design and the robustness of scientific conclusions.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed modeling framework is a key strength. The authors have developed a mathematically elegant and general formalization for perceptual estimation. The use of mixture models for the prior, likelihood, and loss function, combined with a sensory mapping to a space with invariant noise, is a clever way to balance representational flexibility with computational tractability. The ability to derive an analytical expression for the expected loss is crucial for the framework's efficiency.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is laid out clearly in the introduction. The four-stage observer model is described systematically, with explicit equations and justifications for the chosen parameterizations. The case studies effectively demonstrate the practical application of the proposed methodology.

4.  **Practical Utility:** The framework offers a tangible benefit to experimentalists. The analysis in the time interval estimation task, for instance, provides non-obvious insights, such as the finding that encouraging a MAP-like strategy (narrow loss function) could paradoxically worsen the recovery of the prior. This demonstrates the power of *a priori* analysis to guide the design of more informative experiments.

**Weaknesses / Points for Improvement**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** My main concern is that the contribution, particularly the modeling aspect, appears incremental and is very closely related to recent work by some of the same authors and their collaborators. The flexible observer model, with its component-wise parameterization of sensory noise, priors, and decision strategies, strongly resembles the models presented in Acerbi et al. (PLoS Comp Bio, 2012) and Acerbi et al. (PLoS Comp Bio, 2014). While the application of this machinery to the problem of *a priori* identifiability is novel, the core modeling framework is not. The paper would be significantly strengthened by explicitly acknowledging this and clarifying what distinguishes the current model from these previous versions.

2.  **Scope of Identifiability Claims:** The paper claims to provide a framework for testing identifiability, but it's important to be precise about what this means. The framework tests identifiability *within the constraints of the assumed parametric model*. It does not, and cannot, fully resolve the fundamental, practical degeneracy between different sources of noise and bias. For example, a particular behavioral pattern could arise from a skewed sensory likelihood, an asymmetric loss function, or biased motor noise. The current framework can distinguish these possibilities only because they are forced into separate, parameterized components of the model. In a real-world scenario, without these strong structural assumptions, these sources would remain conflated. The manuscript would be more rigorous if it included a discussion of this limitation.

**Detailed Suggestions for the Authors**

*   To address the issue of originality, I strongly recommend adding a new paragraph or subsection that reviews recent Bayesian perceptual models (specifically citing Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014, and potentially others like Houlsby et al., 2013). This section should clearly delineate how the current framework builds upon and advances that previous work. The key novelty to emphasize is the focus on *a priori* identifiability analysis as a tool for experimental design, rather than just the model itself.

*   Regarding the scope of the claims, I suggest adding a paragraph in the discussion that clarifies the distinction between theoretical identifiability within an assumed model class and the practical challenge of distinguishing different functional sources of suboptimality. Acknowledging that the framework's power to disentangle components like sensory noise and loss functions relies on its parametric assumptions would make the paper's claims more precise and scientifically robust.

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem with a technically sound and well-presented approach. The framework for *a priori* analysis of experimental designs is a valuable contribution. My main reservations concern the incremental nature of the underlying model and the need to qualify the claims about resolving non-identifiability. However, these are addressable issues. I believe the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS, provided the authors can clarify the relationship to prior work and the scope of their claims in a revised manuscript.
