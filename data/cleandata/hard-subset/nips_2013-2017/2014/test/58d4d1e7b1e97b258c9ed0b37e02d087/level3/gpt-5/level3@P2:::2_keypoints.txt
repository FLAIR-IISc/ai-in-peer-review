- Paper addresses parameter non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models and proposes an expected-log-likelihood / expected-KL a priori identifiability metric sampled via MCMC for a flexible 1D observer class.
- Contributions: unified parametric observer class, principled expected-marginal-likelihood identifiability metric, and design-relevant case studies in interval timing and speed perception.
- Strength: Timely focus on identifiability, important for interpreting Bayesian brain inferences.
- Strength: Clear exposition and an intuitive, computationally convenient expected-KL perspective.
- Strength: Flexible mixture-Gaussian observer model with closed-form mixtures enabling efficient evaluation across large parameter spaces.
- Strength: Design-relevant findings—sensory vs motor noise trade-offs in interval timing and robustness of slow-speed prior inference across contrasts.
- Strength: Potentially useful tool for planning experiments and pre-registering identifiability checks.
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on strong representational assumptions (specific transforms, unimodal mixture forms, lattice priors, near-quadratic loss) with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: Sensory and motor noise interchangeability under near-Gaussian log-space assumptions is expected; the paper must quantify which inferred differences are reliable vs artifacts.
- Weakness: Claim of robustness in Section 4.2 needs an explicit negative control (flat-prior recovery) in the main text, not only supplement.
- Weakness: Key derivations/approximations (Eqs. 14–15) need stronger justification, error bounds, and a fully specified sampling target density.
- Weakness: Modeling choices (discretizing stimuli, prior lattice, prior range, unimodality enforcement) lack clear motivation and practical guidance.
- Quality: Implementation appears sound, but approximations need stated conditions/error bounds and case studies need more systematic sensitivity to representational choices and dataset size.
- Quality: Reporting of uncertainty (credible intervals, KL error distributions) is currently insufficient and would improve rigor.
- Clarity: Generally well-written but several lines/sections need clarification and Figure 1 should be simplified for readability.
- Clarity: Section 4.1 should indicate which differences are statistically reliable and provide quantitative recovery metrics (bias/variance) across simulations.
- Originality: While identifiability analyses exist, the specific combo of flexible observer class, expected-KL landscape sampling, and actionable design comparisons is novel and valuable.
- Significance: Practical impact for psychophysics and cognitive modeling—encourages pre-experiment recoverability checks and informs experimental design choices.
- Detailed comment: Line 106—clarify modeling of continuous stimuli as discrete and implications for Eq. 12 and results under finer/continuous stimuli.
- Detailed comment: Line 134—report sensitivity to alternative monotone transforms f, clarify estimation vs fixing of d and s0, and test whether Section 4.1 conclusions persist under alternate f.
- Detailed comment: Line 143—justify unimodality enforcement and state conditions to detect/handle potential bimodality.
- Detailed comment: Line 158—justify fixed lattice of Gaussians, choice of prior range and M, and report sensitivity to these settings.
- Detailed comment: Figure 1—integrate panels for clarity, omit confusing p_est box, and emphasize observable presp(r|s).
- Detailed comment: Lines 252–262—provide clearer derivation of approximations, state conditions on Ntr and Nexp, report approximation errors, and specify the exact unnormalized density sampled.
- Detailed comment: Section 4.1—report posterior credible intervals, reconstruction error distributions, and quantify how independent motor calibration reduces posterior correlations.
- Detailed comment: MAP analysis—provide sensitivity analysis for weakly identifiable σℓ and whether informative priors or task manipulations alleviate degeneracy.
- Detailed comment: Section 4.2—move flat-prior negative control to main text with quantitative recovery metrics and discuss systematic biases (s0, σ̃High) across seeds/subjects.
- Detailed comment: Line 262—explicitly define the unnormalized density being sampled (is it Eq. 13?) and provide MCMC proposal/adaptation and convergence diagnostics.
- Detailed comment: Line 365—clarify or justify the claim that learned priors do not matter in 2AFC tasks, or analyze the impact of learned experimental priors on identifiability.
- Pros for acceptance: Addresses an important problem, broadly applicable methodology, and gives actionable experimental guidance with interesting findings.
- Cons against acceptance: Heavy dependence on representational assumptions, insufficiently justified derivations, missing quantitative uncertainty reporting, and key sanity checks relegated to supplement.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept pending stronger derivation justifications, inclusion of flat-prior recovery in main text, and expanded sensitivity analyses to core assumptions.
