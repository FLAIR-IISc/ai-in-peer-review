- Proposes a general Bayesian observer model with tractable, explicit components for sensory noise (including stimulus-dependent and non-Gaussian mixtures), prior (Gaussian lattice mixture), loss (inverted Gaussian mixture), lapses, and motor noise, yielding closed-form expected loss and computable response distributions.
- Develops an a priori identifiability framework using expected log-likelihood differences expressible as KL divergences, sampled via MCMC to map empirically indistinguishable parameter regions.
- Demonstrates the approach in two case studies: interval timing (identifiability sensitive to design choices like motor noise measurement and loss width) and speed perception with a slow-speed prior (more robust inference).
- Modeling choices are coherent and enable efficient inference; the KL-based expected evidence is a principled proxy for distinguishability.
- Empirical demonstrations are careful but the paper sometimes conflates structural/mathematical vs practical identifiability, and key conclusions depend on specific parametric assumptions (Gaussian mixtures, monotone warping f, lapse model).
- Paper is generally clear and well organized with useful equations and case studies, but lacks a dedicated related-work section covering recent Bayesian perceptual modeling (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012/2014).
- Framework is mathematically elegant and integrates robustness with tractability, but conceptual advance over closely related work is incremental since components have strong precedents.
- Despite incrementality, the tool is practical and likely to influence experimental design and interpretation across perceptual tasks.
- Pros: clear, general, and tractable observer model with explicit components.
- Pros: principled identifiability metric that yields actionable design insights.
- Pros: solid case studies and a code-ready methodology implied.
- Cons: incremental relative to prior work (Acerbi et al., Navarro/Pitt/Myung).
- Cons: identifiability claims depend on the model class and do not resolve trade-offs among sensory, decision, and response noise.
- Requested revision: add a comprehensive review of recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly articulate advances over them.
- Requested revision: clarify structural vs practical identifiability and explicitly discuss limits in separating sensory, decision, and response noise, suggesting design manipulations (e.g., motor calibration, reward changes, external noise).
- Requested revision: comment on sensitivity to chosen parameterizations (mixture orders, f(s), lapse model) and contrast with alternatives (Fisher information, profile likelihood).
- Reviewer supports acceptance pending these clarifications.
