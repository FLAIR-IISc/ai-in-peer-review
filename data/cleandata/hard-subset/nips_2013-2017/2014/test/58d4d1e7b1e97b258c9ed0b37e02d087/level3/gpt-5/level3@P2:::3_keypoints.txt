- Summary: Introduces a flexible, tractable class of 1D Bayesian observer models and a KL-based a priori identifiability criterion plus MCMC sampling of E(θ|θ*) to map non-identifiable directions and compare designs.

- Model & method: Parameterizes internal mapping f(s), stimulus-dependent noise, priors, loss (mixtures), lapses, and motor noise; derives expected log-likelihood/KL objective for design ranking.

- Case study — interval timing: Baseline designs poorly recover priors (sensory vs motor noise confound); increasing trials in one range helps little; MAP-like losses can worsen recovery; measuring motor noise improves identifiability.

- Case study — speed perception: Robust parameter recovery including slow-speed prior exponent; slight bias in noise lower bounds when decoupling internal and objective likelihoods.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on Bayesian observer/model inversion literature (Acerbi et al., Houlsby et al.); main novelty is unifying parameterization and KL-based a priori identifiability objective for design ranking.

- Strength: Timely focus on identifiability with an elegant, computationally efficient mixture-based parameterization enabling closed-form expected loss and actionable design guidance.

- Strength: KL-based formulation is principled and naturally connects to model evidence-based comparisons.

- Weakness: Incremental relative to recent work; many modeling components are known and novelty centers on the identifiability mapping.

- Suggestion: Add a dedicated related-work section contrasting specific advances (e.g., loss parameterization, unified objective) with prior methods.

- Weakness: Claims of “mathematical identifiability” depend on parametric assumptions and shared-internal-space constraints; framework diagnoses but does not resolve structural equivalences.

- Suggestion: Discuss structural vs practical identifiability and which invariances are broken by which constraints (e.g., fixing loss family, measuring motor noise).

- Weakness: Limited exploration of model misspecification; identifiability may change if true loss or f(s) are misparameterized.

- Suggestion: Include sensitivity analyses over prior basis size M, loss-family rigidity, alternative f(s) forms, and stimulus sampling densities.

- Weakness: Design objective relates closely to optimal design/expected information gain but connection is not made explicit.

- Suggestion: Discuss links to mutual information/expected information gain and potential for iterative/active design integration.

- Quality: Technically sound with clear derivations and appropriate simulations; request reporting of computational costs, MCMC diagnostics, and code/pseudocode for reproducibility.

- Clarity: Generally clear but notation-heavy; recommend moving parametric details to appendix, adding a schematic of parameters, and an algorithm box for computing/sampling E(θ|θ*).

- Originality: Moderate — useful unification and KL-based mapping, but many components extend prior work.

- Significance: Moderate-to-high for practitioners designing psychophysics experiments; offers concrete guidance on recoverability and when extra measurements are needed.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important gap, is methodologically solid, provides practical design insights in timing, and validates robustness in speed perception.

- Arguments against acceptance: Incremental novelty, identifiability contingent on model class, limited misspecification and computational analyses.

- Questions for authors: How do design rankings change with M and alternative f(s) parameterizations? How sensitive are conclusions to loss-family misspecification? Can you relate your objective to mutual information/expected information gain and support adaptive stimulus selection? Will you release code and configs?

- Recommendation: Accept, pending strengthened related-work, candid discussion of identifiability limits, sensitivity analyses, and reproducibility materials.
