- Tackles the important problem of identifiability in Bayesian observer models and intrinsic degeneracy of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions.  
- Proposes a systematic framework to evaluate identifiability of Bayesian observer models.  
- Applies the framework to two behavioral case studies: time interval estimation and motion perception.  
- Demonstrates how experimental design choices can mitigate parameter degeneracy (e.g., fixing motor noise, avoiding narrow loss functions).  
- Provides detailed mathematical formulations and computational techniques for reproducibility.  
- Shows the framework’s versatility by validating prior empirical findings in speed perception.  
- Practical implications for improving experimental design and inferring internal representations from behavior.  
- Overlooks significant prior work on model comparison and identifiability, limiting contextualization.  
- Assumes parametric forms for priors and loss functions without sufficient justification, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Some mathematical claims are unclear and figures have ambiguous annotations, hindering comprehension.  
- Strength: novel, well-motivated, theoretically grounded approach with clear computational methods.  
- Weakness: insufficient engagement with hierarchical Bayesian approaches and alternative identifiability metrics.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better integrate prior work and justify assumptions.
