This paper presents a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, accompanied by applications to time interval estimation and speed perception. The authors address a critical issue in the field: the inherent degeneracy of Bayesian models, where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can yield identical behavioral predictions. They propose a method for systematically investigating this identifiability *a priori*, and demonstrate its potential for improving experimental design.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of previous NIPS and related literature on Bayesian inference in perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4]), recognizing the increasing trend of interpreting Bayesian observers not merely as normative benchmarks, but as process models reflecting internal representations [5, 6]. The authors correctly identify the fundamental problem of non-identifiability highlighted in earlier works [17, 18, 19], where different parameterizations can lead to the same observed behavior. The proposed framework offers a principled way to tackle this challenge, moving beyond ad-hoc constraints (e.g., specific noise models [21], reward structures [3]) to a more systematic analysis.

The strength of the paper lies in its mathematical rigor and generality. The authors develop a flexible and efficient Bayesian observer model, parameterized by a vector θ, encompassing a wide range of psychophysical tasks.  The explicit formulation of the sensation, perception, decision-making, and response stages, with equations for each component, is a significant contribution.  The use of maximum entropy principles to define distributions where limited information is available is a sensible approach, and the analytical tractability achieved is commendable.  The framework's ability to compare identifiability across different experimental setups is particularly promising for optimizing experimental design, as demonstrated in the time interval estimation case study. The application to speed perception further validates the framework by confirming previously established findings [9, 29].

However, while technically sound and well-executed, the originality of the work feels somewhat incremental.  The core ideas of Bayesian model selection, identifiability analysis, and the use of KL divergence to quantify model differences are well-established within the broader statistical and machine learning communities.  A key weakness is the lack of a thorough review of *recent* Bayesian perceptual models and a clear articulation of how this work advances beyond them.  Specifically, the authors should engage with papers like Acerbi et al. [7, 15], which also explore parameter identifiability and model complexity in the context of Bayesian perceptual inference, and clearly delineate the novel contributions of this framework.  While the authors cite relevant work, a dedicated section comparing and contrasting with these recent advances would strengthen the paper considerably.

Furthermore, the claim of achieving “identifiability” requires careful nuance.  The framework's identifiability analysis relies heavily on the assumptions *built into* the model itself (e.g., specific functional forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions).  While this is a common practice in model building, it’s crucial to acknowledge that this “identifiability” is conditional on these assumptions.  The framework doesn’t fundamentally *resolve* the practical challenge of distinguishing between sensory, decision, and response noise, which often co-vary in real data. The authors briefly allude to this limitation but should explicitly discuss it in the revised manuscript, perhaps framing the results as a way to identify *potentially* identifiable parameters within a given model, rather than claiming absolute identifiability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically rigorous and general framework for analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception.
*   The framework has practical implications for experimental design, as demonstrated through the time interval estimation case study.
*   The work is technically sound and well-written, with clear explanations of the underlying concepts and methods.
*   The application to speed perception provides further validation of the framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The originality of the work is somewhat incremental, lacking a thorough comparison to recent advances in Bayesian perceptual modeling.
*   The claim of “identifiability” is conditional on model assumptions and doesn’t address the practical challenge of separating different sources of noise.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The framework is a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception, and the insights gained from the case studies are likely to be useful.  I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised above, particularly by providing a more comprehensive review of related work and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the identifiability analysis.  Specifically, a new section comparing the current work with Acerbi et al. [7, 15] and a discussion of the practical limitations stemming from the difficulty of disentangling different noise sources would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
