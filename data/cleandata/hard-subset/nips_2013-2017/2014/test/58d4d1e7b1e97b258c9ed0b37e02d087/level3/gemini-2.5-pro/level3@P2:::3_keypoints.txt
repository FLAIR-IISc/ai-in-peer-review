- Presents a formal, computationally tractable framework for a priori identifiability analysis of Bayesian observer models.  
- Introduces a flexible parametric observer model that parameterizes sensory mapping, prior, likelihood, and loss using mixtures of Gaussians and stimulus-dependent noise.  
- Core contribution: a KL-divergence–based measure to quantify expected statistical support and map the "identifiability landscape" between a reference model θ* and candidate models θ.  
- Demonstrates utility with two case studies: recovery of priors in time-interval estimation and robustness of inferring a slow-speed prior in vision.  
- Builds on established Bayesian decision-theory literature and closely relates to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014).  
- Strength — Significance: tackles the important problem of model identifiability and helps optimize experimental design to mitigate identifiability issues.  
- Strength — Quality: mathematically sound, KL-based approach is efficient, and model balances flexibility with computational tractability.  
- Strength — Clarity: clearly written, well-organized, with effective case studies that yield non-obvious experimental insights.  
- Weakness — Originality: observer model is highly similar to recent work (Acerbi et al.), and the manuscript insufficiently positions itself relative to that literature.  
- Weakness — Qualified claims: claims about resolving identifiability should be more cautious because degeneracies remain within the assumed model class (e.g., sensory vs. response/motor noise).  
- Suggestion: add a targeted discussion comparing and contrasting the model to recent similar Bayesian perceptual models (especially Acerbi et al.).  
- Suggestion: explicitly state limitations of the identifiability analysis, emphasizing dependence on model assumptions and that it highlights but does not eliminate fundamental degeneracies.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical problem, provides a novel and efficient framework with practical utility, and is technically sound and clear.  
- Arguments against acceptance: modeling component seems incremental and the relation to recent work and scope of identifiability claims need clearer treatment.  
- Conclusion: recommend acceptance pending revisions to better contextualize related work and to clarify limitations of the identifiability analysis.
