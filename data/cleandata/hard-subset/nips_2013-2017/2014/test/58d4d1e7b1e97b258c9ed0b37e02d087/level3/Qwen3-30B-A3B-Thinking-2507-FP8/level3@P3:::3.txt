

This paper presents a systematic framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual tasks, addressing a critical gap in the field. The authors formalize the degeneracy problem where multiple combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions yield identical behavioral outputs, making reliable recovery of internal representations impossible without constraints. They introduce a flexible Bayesian observer model with explicit equations for sensory noise (via stimulus-dependent mapping in Eq. 2), likelihood (Eq. 3), prior (Eq. 4), loss (Eq. 6), and response distribution (Eq. 7-9), all designed for tractable inference. The framework uses expected log-likelihood under the true model (Eq. 12) to quantify identifiability, demonstrated through two case studies: time interval estimation (Sec. 4.1) and speed perception (Sec. 4.2). The authors show how experimental designs (e.g., measuring motor noise independently) can resolve degeneracies, with speed perception proving robust to identifiability challenges while interval timing requires careful design.

**Strengths:**  
The paper offers a mathematically elegant, general framework that advances beyond prior work by providing *systematic* identifiability testing rather than just proposing a model. The model’s flexibility (e.g., handling stimulus-dependent noise via Eq. 2) while maintaining computational tractability is impressive. The case studies demonstrate clear practical utility: for time estimation, they reveal that imposing a narrow loss function *worsens* prior recovery (Fig. 2d), a non-intuitive insight. The identifiability metric (Eq. 12) is theoretically sound, leveraging KL divergence to quantify how well parameters can be distinguished from data. The paper also acknowledges limitations (e.g., Eq. 13’s dependence on experimental assumptions), showing intellectual honesty.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
The main limitation is that the contribution appears incremental relative to Acerbi et al. (2012, *J. Vis.*) and (2014, *PLoS Comput. Biol.*), which established Bayesian observer models for perceptual tasks and introduced Bayesian transfer. While the authors generalize the *analysis* of identifiability, they do not clearly articulate how their framework *differs* from Acerbi’s approach to degeneracy (e.g., their Eq. 12 vs. Acerbi’s constraint-based methods). The identifiability claims also hinge on strong assumptions (e.g., the sensory mapping in Eq. 2), and the paper does not address whether practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise can be resolved (as noted in the caveat). The speed perception case study (Sec. 4.2) assumes the prior is heavy-tailed *a priori*; the framework’s robustness here may stem from the model’s design rather than new insights.

**Recommendations for Revision:**  
1. **Add a dedicated section (e.g., "Related Work on Bayesian Identifiability")** explicitly comparing this framework to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), clarifying how it advances beyond their work (e.g., *systematic a priori testing* vs. *ad hoc constraints*).  
2. **Discuss practical identifiability limitations** (caveat) in Sec. 5: Emphasize that while the framework identifies *theoretical* degeneracies, experimental confounds (e.g., undetected lapses) may still obscure real-world recovery.  
3. **Clarify the novelty** in the abstract/introduction: Highlight that this is the *first* framework for *a priori* identifiability testing across experimental designs, not merely a new model.

**Quality & Significance:**  
The paper is technically sound, with rigorous derivations (e.g., Eq. 12) and well-executed case studies. It addresses a fundamental problem in Bayesian modeling of perception, making it highly relevant to NIPS. The significance lies in enabling better experimental design—e.g., the finding that motor noise measurement (MTR) resolves degeneracy in timing tasks (Fig. 2c)—which could improve reproducibility in the field. While the framework itself is not a new perceptual model, it provides a *tool* to validate existing models, advancing the field’s methodological rigor.

**Clarity:**  
The paper is well-organized, with clear equations and intuitive case studies. The model’s parametrization (Eq. 10) is comprehensive yet accessible. Minor improvements could include a schematic of the framework (beyond Fig. 1) and a brief discussion of computational costs.

**Originality:**  
The framework is novel for its *systematic approach* to identifiability testing, though the model components (e.g., mixture likelihoods) are derived from prior work. The incremental nature is acknowledged but not sufficiently distinguished from Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), which is the primary concern.

**Verdict:**  
**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to methodological rigor in Bayesian perceptual modeling. The proposed revisions (adding a comparative section and clarifying limitations) would strengthen its impact. The practical insights (e.g., "narrow loss functions worsen prior recovery") are non-obvious and useful for experimentalists. While not revolutionary, the work is a solid, necessary step toward reliable model inversion in perception science. The reviewer supports acceptance but emphasizes that the paper must clearly position itself against recent work to avoid being dismissed as incremental.
