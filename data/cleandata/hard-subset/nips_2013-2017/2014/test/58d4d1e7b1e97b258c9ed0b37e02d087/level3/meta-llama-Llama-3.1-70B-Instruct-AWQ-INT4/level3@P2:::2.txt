This paper proposes a novel framework for testing the identifiability of Bayesian models of perception, a crucial issue in cognitive modeling. The authors introduce a flexible and efficient class of Bayesian observer models, which can be used to model various psychophysical tasks. They then present a method for exploring the identifiability of these models within their framework, using a combination of theoretical analysis and empirical simulations.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a systematic approach to studying model identifiability, which is a critical issue in Bayesian modeling. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it to two case studies: a time interval estimation task and a speed perception task. The results show that the framework can be used to rank experimental designs by their ability to resolve the underlying degeneracy of parameters of interest, and to identify parameters that may critically affect the inference.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The methodology is interesting and has the potential to be applied to a wide range of cognitive modeling tasks. However, the main results rely on many strong assumptions, which reduces their impact relative to the length of the assumptions list.

One of the limitations of the paper is that the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise in Section 4.1 is unsurprising, and the authors should report which condition differences are statistically reliable. Additionally, in Section 4.2, the authors must demonstrate that the model can recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer.

There are also some minor issues with the presentation. For example, the log-stimulus transform is a strong constraint, and identifiability may depend heavily on this representational assumption. The authors should clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled with a discrete distribution (experimental design vs true stimulus). Furthermore, the constraints and entropy maximization do not guarantee unimodality of the fitted distribution, and could potentially allow bimodality.

The paper could also benefit from some minor revisions to improve clarity. For example, Figure 1 is confusing, and integrating panel b into a and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) could improve clarity. Additionally, the authors should justify the approximations used in Equation 14-15 and specify the conditions on N where they hold.

Overall, the paper is above the threshold for NIPS due to its interesting methodology and clear writing. However, the authors should address the limitations and minor issues mentioned above to strengthen their contribution.
