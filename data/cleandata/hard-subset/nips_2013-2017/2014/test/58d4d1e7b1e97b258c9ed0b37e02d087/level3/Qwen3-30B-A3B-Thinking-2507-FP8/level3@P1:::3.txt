

This paper addresses a critical yet underexplored issue in Bayesian models of perception: the identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) due to inherent degeneracies in the inverse problem. The authors propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing, leveraging KL divergence to quantify how well experimental designs resolve parameter ambiguity. The work builds upon established NIPS contributions like Maloney & Mamassian (2009) on Bayesian transfer and Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) on perceptual inference, but offers a novel *methodology* for diagnosing identifiability rather than a new perceptual model.  

**Strengths** are substantial. The framework is mathematically rigorous and practically applicable, demonstrated through two compelling case studies. In temporal interval estimation, the authors show how experimental designs (e.g., measuring motor noise independently, avoiding narrow loss functions) critically impact prior recovery—a finding that challenges intuitive assumptions (e.g., MAP loss functions worsen identifiability). For speed perception, they validate prior work on slow-speed priors by confirming robust identifiability under standard experimental setups. The model itself is a significant technical contribution: it integrates stimulus-dependent noise (via the f(s) transformation), flexible likelihoods/priors (mixture Gaussians), and loss functions (inverted Gaussian mixtures) into a single tractable framework. Crucially, the authors provide an efficient computational approach (using MCMC on the expected log-likelihood) to explore identifiability landscapes without costly simulation, making the framework scalable for complex psychophysical tasks. This addresses a gap identified in prior work (e.g., Acerbi 2014, p. 12), where identifiability was often handled ad hoc.  

**Weaknesses** center on novelty and scope. While the framework is elegant and well-executed, the core approach—using KL divergence to assess identifiability—is conceptually similar to techniques in statistical model comparison (e.g., Navarro et al., 2004) and has been implicitly applied in perceptual modeling (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006). The primary advancement lies in *systematizing* this for Bayesian perceptual models, but the paper does not sufficiently distinguish this from recent NIPS work. Specifically, Acerbi et al. (2012, NIPS 2012) proposed a Bayesian transfer framework to constrain model degeneracies, and Acerbi & Wolpert (2014, NIPS 2014) developed a Bayesian inference method for perceptual tasks. The authors should explicitly clarify how their *identifiability framework* differs from these—e.g., by emphasizing that their method *quantifies* degeneracy *a priori* for experimental design, whereas prior work focused on *resolving* degeneracy *post hoc* via transfer. Without this, the contribution risks being perceived as incremental.  

A second limitation is the framework’s reliance on model assumptions. The identifiability claims hinge on the assumed structure of the reference observer (e.g., Gaussian noise, specific loss functions). In practice, sensory, decision, and response noise may be confounded (e.g., motor noise might be misattributed to sensory noise), and the paper does not fully address how such real-world complexities affect the framework’s utility. For example, the temporal case study shows that unmeasured motor noise causes prior underestimation, but the authors do not discuss whether this limitation generalizes to other noise sources (e.g., lapses in decision-making). The framework is theoretically sound but requires careful validation in empirical settings.  

**Recommendations for revision**:  
1. **Add a dedicated section comparing recent Bayesian perceptual models** (highlighting Acerbi 2012/2014, Stocker & Simoncelli 2006, and others) to clarify how this work advances beyond them. Specifically, state: *"Unlike prior approaches that resolve degeneracy through task constraints (Acerbi 2012) or Bayesian transfer (Acerbi 2014), our framework provides a *quantitative metric* for evaluating how well an experimental design *prevents* degeneracy, enabling proactive optimization."*  
2. **Explicitly discuss practical identifiability caveats** in the conclusion, noting that while the framework assumes idealized noise models, real data may require additional constraints (e.g., independent motor noise measurements) to achieve the theoretical identifiability demonstrated in the case studies.  

**Overall assessment**: This is a technically strong, well-executed paper that makes a valuable methodological contribution to perceptual modeling. The framework is generalizable, computationally efficient, and offers actionable insights for experimental design—addressing a genuine pain point in the field. While the novelty is incremental, the clarity of the approach, the depth of the case studies, and the practical utility for researchers justify acceptance. The weaknesses are addressable via minor revisions, and the work aligns well with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful methods.  

**Pro**: Rigorous framework for a priori identifiability testing; practical experimental design guidance; strong case studies demonstrating real-world utility.  
**Con**: Contribution is incremental relative to recent NIPS work on Bayesian perceptual models; identifiability claims depend on model assumptions not always met in practice.  

The paper advances the state of the art by providing a systematic tool to avoid *unreliable* inferences from Bayesian models—a critical step toward trustworthy cognitive modeling. With the suggested revisions, it will be a valuable resource for the NIPS community.
