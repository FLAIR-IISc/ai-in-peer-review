- Paper presents a formal framework for assessing a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perceptual science.
- Addresses the degeneracy problem where different priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce indistinguishable behavior.
- Proposes a mathematically elegant, general Bayesian observer model combining representational flexibility (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise, flexible priors) with computational tractability.
- Core method computes an expected posterior density to map an "identifiability landscape" for evaluating experimental designs before data collection.
- Builds on and relates to substantial recent literature fitting complex Bayesian models to behavioral data (e.g., Acerbi et al., Stocker & Simoncelli).
- Strength: formal, systematic approach to a fundamental modeling problem with explicit parametric forms for noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response.
- Strength: demonstrated utility via case studies in time and speed perception; technically sound and clearly written.
- Main weakness: contribution feels incremental and closely resembles recent models (especially Acerbi et al.); prior work not sufficiently discussed.
- Recommendation: add a systematic review section comparing recent models and clearly articulate how this framework advances beyond them.
- Second concern: identifiability claims are limited to mathematical identifiability under the model’s assumptions and do not resolve practical difficulties in separating sensory, decision, and motor/response noise.
- Recommendation: explicitly discuss this practical limitation and clarify what the framework can and cannot achieve.
- Overall: support acceptance conditional on revisions to better situate the work in the literature and clarify practical limits of the identifiability analysis.
