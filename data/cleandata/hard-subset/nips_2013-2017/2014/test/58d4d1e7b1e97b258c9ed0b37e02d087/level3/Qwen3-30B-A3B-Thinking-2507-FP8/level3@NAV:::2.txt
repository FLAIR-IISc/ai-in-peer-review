

This paper proposes a novel framework for systematically testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models in cognitive science, addressing a critical gap in the field where multiple combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss functions can produce identical behavioral outputs. The authors extend previous work on Bayesian models of perception [9, 12, 24] by introducing a flexible class of observer models and a method to explore identifiability a priori through KL-divergence analysis. The framework is applied to two case studies: time interval estimation (Section 4.1) and speed perception (Section 4.2), demonstrating how experimental design choices affect parameter recovery.

The paper's strengths include clear writing, an interesting methodological contribution, and practical applications for experimental design. The case studies provide valuable insights into how to constrain experimental setups to improve identifiability. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the methodological toolkit for cognitive modeling.

However, the paper's impact is reduced by its reliance on numerous strong assumptions. The log-stimulus transform (line 134) represents a significant representational constraint that may dictate identifiability outcomes. The assumption that priors favor lattice representations over uniform distributions (line 158) lacks justification, as does the claim that learning priors wouldn't matter in 2AFC tasks (line 365). Section 4.1 fails to report statistically reliable differences when claiming interchangeability of sensory/motor noise, and Section 4.2 does not demonstrate the model's ability to recover the absence of a slow-speed prior in the reference observer. The log-likelihood approximations in Equations 14-15 (lines 252) require justification for their validity conditions. Figure 1 is confusing, particularly the separation of derived p_est(s*|x), which should be integrated into panel a.

While the framework is promising, the numerous unexamined assumptions and lack of rigorous validation for key claims weaken the conclusions. The paper would benefit from addressing these technical concerns, particularly the justification for representational constraints and the demonstration of model capabilities in critical edge cases. The methodology is interesting and potentially valuable, but the current implementation requires more careful treatment of modeling assumptions to achieve the impact suggested by the framework's potential.
