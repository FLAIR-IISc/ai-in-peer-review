Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the long-standing issue of parameter non-identifiability in Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors introduce a flexible generative class for 1D estimation tasks (sensation via a monotone transform and mixture-Gaussian noise; mixture-of-Gaussian priors on a lattice; mixture-Gaussian loss; lapses and motor noise) and propose an a priori identifiability analysis based on the expected log-likelihood of candidate parameter vectors under data generated by a reference observer. They show that the expected log-likelihood reduces to a sum of KL divergences between response distributions across stimuli, and use this to define an approximate “expected posterior” E(θ|θ*) to be sampled via MCMC. Two case studies illustrate how the method can inform experimental design: interval timing (Jazayeri & Shadlen) and speed perception with slow-speed priors (Stocker & Simoncelli).

This work connects to identifiability and model distinguishability analyses in cognitive modeling (e.g., Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004), to earlier Bayesian inverse modeling for priors/likelihood/loss (e.g., Körding & Wolpert; Maloney & Mamassian; Girshick et al.; Acerbi et al.), and to information-theoretic design. Relative to these, the contributions are: a unified parametric observer class enabling fast evaluation; a principled a priori identifiability metric tied to the expected marginal likelihood; and concrete, design-relevant insights for two classic tasks.

Strengths
- Timely focus on identifiability, a critical issue for interpreting “Bayesian brain” inferences.
- Clear exposition and well-motivated framework; the expected-KL perspective is intuitive and computationally convenient.
- Flexible observer model with closed-form mixtures enabling efficient evaluation across large parameter spaces.
- Design-relevant findings: (i) in interval timing, unidentifiability between sensory and motor noise and sensitivity to loss-function assumptions; (ii) in speed perception, robustness of slow-speed prior inference across contrasts.
- Potentially broadly useful tool for planning experiments and pre-registering identifiability checks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy reliance on strong representational assumptions (log/power transform; unimodal two-Gaussian mixtures; lattice prior; near-quadratic loss). The main conclusions may depend on these choices, but sensitivity analyses are limited.
- In Section 4.1, the interchangeability of sensory and motor noise under near-Gaussian assumptions in log space is expected. The paper should quantify which inferred differences are statistically reliable vs. artifacts of these assumptions.
- For Section 4.2, the claim of robustness should be accompanied by an explicit negative control in the main text: demonstrate recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior (not only in Supplementary Material).
- Some derivations and approximations (Equations 14–15) require stronger justification or bounds. The sampling target density is not fully specified.
- Certain modeling choices (discretizing continuous stimuli; prior lattice; choice of prior range; unimodality enforcement under max-entropy) need clearer motivation and practical guidance.

Quality
- Generally sound and carefully implemented. The expected-KL derivation appears correct in spirit, but key approximations need conditions and error bounds (see detailed comments). Case studies are informative but would benefit from more systematic sensitivity analyses to representational assumptions (f, loss, basis for priors, lapse) and to dataset size. Reporting of uncertainty (e.g., credible intervals for recovered parameters, KL error distributions) would increase rigor.

Clarity
- Well-written overall. A few places need clarification (see below on lines 106, 134, 143, 158, 252–262, 365). Figure 1 can be simplified to improve readability. Please indicate which differences are statistically reliable in Section 4.1 and provide quantitative recovery metrics (bias/variance) across simulations.

Originality
- While identifiability analyses per se are not new, the specific combination—flexible observer class, expected-KL-based landscape sampling, and actionable design comparisons in classic perceptual tasks—is novel and valuable for the NIPS audience.

Significance
- Practical impact for psychophysics and cognitive modeling: encourages pre-experiment checks of recoverability and informs design choices (conditions, feedback/loss, motor calibration). The framework could be adopted broadly and extended to discrete-choice tasks and multi-cue settings.

Detailed comments and requests
- Line 106: Please clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled with a discrete distribution. Is this meant to reflect experimental designs with discretized stimuli (si, Pi), or an approximation to a truly continuous generative process? Discuss implications for Eq. 12 (sum over Nexp) and whether results change if stimuli are continuous or more finely binned.
- Line 134 (Eq. 2): The log/power transform f(s) is a strong representational constraint. Since identifiability hinges on relative shapes of prior/likelihood/loss in this space, please: (i) report sensitivity to alternative monotone transforms (e.g., pure power law, identity, spline-based f); (ii) clarify how d and s0 are estimated vs. fixed; (iii) discuss whether conclusions in Sec. 4.1 persist under alternative f.
- Line 143 (Eq. 3): You impose unimodality and then choose the remaining DoF by local maximum entropy. Does the procedure guarantee unimodality for all s, or could parameter settings yield bimodality? Please state sufficient conditions and how violations are detected/handled.
- Line 158 (Eq. 4): Why a fixed lattice of Gaussians rather than, e.g., RBFs with learnable centers or spline bases? How is the range [µmin, µmax] chosen “50% wider” justified in practice, and how sensitive are results to this choice and to M? Please report sensitivity to M and range width.
- Figure 1: The current separation of panels and the explicit p_est(s*|x) box are confusing. Consider integrating panel b into a single end-to-end diagram and omit p_est if it is not directly observable; emphasize observable presp(r|s) and latent variables.
- Lines 252–262 (Eqs. 14–15): Provide a clearer derivation and justification of approximations (e.g., use of Stirling; E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]). Under what conditions on Ntr and Nexp are these accurate? Can you state error terms or bounds, or at least show empirical approximation error across typical N? Also, when you write “approximate expected posterior,” please specify the exact unnormalized density you sample (E(θ|θ*) ∝ exp⟨log P(D|θ)⟩).
- Section 4.1: The trade-off between σ (sensory) and ρ1 (motor) noise under near-Gaussian assumptions in log space is unsurprising. Please (i) report which parameter differences are statistically reliable (e.g., posterior credible intervals), (ii) quantify reconstruction error distributions (not just means), and (iii) show how independent motor calibration (MTR) reduces posterior correlations and improves recovery, with numeric effect sizes.
- Section 4.1 (MAP): Since σℓ appears weakly identifiable, please provide a sensitivity analysis showing how inference quality varies with σℓ and whether informative priors or additional task manipulations (e.g., variable feedback asymmetries) alleviate degeneracy.
- Section 4.2: Move the flat-prior negative control (FLA) into the main text with quantitative recovery metrics (e.g., estimates of kprior and credible intervals). Also discuss the underestimation of s0 and σ̃High: is this systematic across seeds/subjects? Does allowing decoupled internal noise fully capture that bias?
- Line 262: “Unnormalized density sampled from” needs explicit definition (is it Eq. 13?), including proposal, adaptation, and convergence diagnostics for MCMC.
- Line 365: The claim that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks needs clarification. Many 2AFC paradigms induce task-specific priors over the stimulus distribution. Please justify the no-learning assumption for this mapping to estimation, or analyze the impact of learned experimental priors on identifiability.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-served problem; methodology is generally sound and broadly applicable.
- Provides actionable guidance for experimental design; interesting, non-obvious findings (e.g., downsides of narrow loss).
- Clear, well-structured paper of interest to NIPS community working on probabilistic perception and model-based inference.

Cons against acceptance
- Dependence on strong representational assumptions with limited sensitivity analyses.
- Some derivations and approximations insufficiently justified; missing quantitative uncertainty reporting.
- Key sanity check (recovery of flat prior) relegated to Supplement; several clarifications needed.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The framework is interesting, clearly presented, and useful for the community. Addressing the derivation justifications, adding the flat-prior recovery to the main text with quantitative metrics, and expanding sensitivity analyses to core representational assumptions would substantially strengthen the paper.
