Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses an important and timely question: when and how can internal components of Bayesian observer models (priors, likelihoods, loss) be identified from behavioral data? The authors (i) formalize the intrinsic degeneracy of Bayesian Decision Theory (BDT)—that multiple combinations of prior, likelihood, and loss can produce indistinguishable behavior; (ii) propose a flexible, computationally efficient class of one-dimensional estimation models (with parametric stimulus-to-internal-space mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians for sensory noise and loss, lapse and motor noise) that enables systematic, a priori identifiability analysis; and (iii) derive an expected log-evidence criterion that reduces to a weighted sum of KL divergences across stimuli, allowing efficient MCMC sampling of an “identifiability landscape” around a reference observer. They use this to compare experimental designs and diagnose parameter degeneracies in two case studies: interval timing (where recovering priors is fragile unless motor noise is independently constrained) and speed perception with a slow-speed prior (where they find relatively robust recovery of both prior and noise characteristics).

The work connects to long-standing concerns about model non-identifiability in psychology and cognitive science (e.g., Anderson, 1978), and to more recent critiques of Bayesian cognitive models (Jones & Love, 2011; Bowers & Davis, 2012). It also relates to prior work on model distinguishability and experimental informativeness (e.g., Pitt et al., 2002; Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004), Bayesian model comparison (AIC/BIC/DIC), and optimal experimental design. While [23] is cited, the positioning relative to this literature could be strengthened (see Major concerns).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation that the expected log-likelihood under a true reference observer equals a stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences (Eq. 12) is sound and provides a principled, scalable proxy for expected posterior mass. The model class is carefully constructed to permit efficient evaluation (mixtures enabling closed-form or fast numerical expected loss).
- Empirical support: The two simulation studies are well chosen. The interval timing example convincingly shows non-identifiability (notably σ vs. motor noise, and weak identifiability of σℓ), and the speed perception example shows favorable recovery of the slow-speed prior exponent.
- Limitations: Results hinge on specific parametric choices (e.g., two-component Gaussian mixtures for measurement and loss; particular f(s) mapping; sharing assumptions across conditions). Sensitivity analyses are limited, and the paper does not quantify how conclusions change under alternative parameterizations or misspecification.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The model is clearly described, and the pipeline from expected evidence to MCMC sampling is easy to follow. Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; providing brief intuition in the main text (e.g., for Eq. 12) would help. Figures are informative but could be better annotated (see Minor comments).

Originality
The specific combination of (a) a unifying parametric observer class enabling fast evaluation across priors/likelihoods/losses, with (b) an expected-evidence KL framework to map identifiability and (c) comparative analysis of experimental designs for perceptual estimation, is valuable. However, the novelty would be clearer if more directly contrasted with the extensive literature on model distinguishability, parameter recovery, and optimal experimental design (e.g., Myung & Pitt’s work on model mimicry and design for discrimination; Bayesian experimental design approaches).

Significance
Identifiability is central to interpreting inferred “internal representations” in perception. Providing a practical, simulation-based tool to anticipate parameter confounds and to rank experimental designs is impactful for both psychophysics and Bayesian cognitive modeling. The case studies yield actionable lessons (e.g., importance of independently constraining motor noise; potential downside of narrow loss/MAP-like feedback in timing tasks).

Major concerns and suggestions
1) Positioning and novelty relative to prior cognitive modeling literature:
- Clarify how the proposed framework differs from, complements, or extends prior distinguishability/identifiability approaches (e.g., Pitt, Myung & colleagues on model mimicry and design informativeness; Navarro et al., 2004; parameter recovery studies; Fisher-information-based local identifiability; Bayesian experimental design). Discuss pros/cons of your expected-KL approach versus these alternatives, and connections to predictive checks or simulation-based calibration.
- Consider including a small comparison (conceptual or empirical) to a Fisher information/local identifiability analysis or to the distinguishability metrics in [23], to validate that your conclusions align.

2) Justification and robustness of modeling assumptions:
- The choices of (i) two-Gaussian mixtures for pmeas and L, (ii) the specific f(s) mapping (Eq. 2), and (iii) the coupling/decoupling assumptions between true and internal likelihoods warrant further justification. Please explain why two components suffice, how results change with one vs. more components, and whether skew/kurtosis or mixture unimodality constraints are critical.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: vary mixture complexity, f(s) family (e.g., pure log vs. Stevens law), loss parameterization (single Gaussian vs. mixture), lapse models, and see how identifiability conclusions in both case studies change. Explicitly report which parameters/design conclusions are robust and which are not.
- Eq. 12: motivate the choice of KL (and its asymmetry) as the expected evidence metric, and discuss alternatives (e.g., symmetric divergences, Hellinger). Clarify how Ntr and stimulus frequencies Pi influence the landscape and how this can guide design optimization.

3) From ranking to optimizing designs:
- The framework naturally supports stimulus-set and trial-allocation optimization (maximize expected information/identifiability for a subset of parameters). Including at least a proof-of-concept design optimization (e.g., selecting stimuli or contrasts to best recover σℓ vs. prior moments) would substantially enhance the contribution.

4) Scope and generality:
- Discuss extensions beyond 1-D estimation to multi-cue integration and discrete-choice tasks (2AFC), including whether the expected-evidence derivation and computational efficiencies carry over. A brief simulation for a discrete-choice model would strengthen generality claims.

Minor comments
- Please spell out the “trivial mathematical fact” (around the product φ1φ2φ3 = c leading to identical argmin in Eq. 1) to make the degeneracy transparent.
- Line 73: change the second “nor” to “or.”
- Figure 2: clarify the meaning of star and dashed lines in the captions/legends. Indicate which correspond to reference θ*, posterior mean, and credible intervals.
- Provide brief intuition for Eq. 12 in the main text (expected log-likelihood under θ∗ equals negative KL plus constant).
- Typographic/formatting: ensure all references include full bibliographic details and consistent formatting.

Pros
- Tackles an important, under-discussed issue with practical consequences for experiment design.
- Clear, efficient framework (expected-KL) with intuitive outputs and actionable insights.
- Well-executed case studies illustrating both fragile and robust regimes.

Cons
- Insufficient engagement with prior work on identifiability/distinguishability and design.
- Heavy reliance on specific parametric choices; limited robustness checks.
- No real-data validation; limited exploration of optimization of designs.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper is well written and addresses a high-impact problem with a useful, implementable framework. Strengthening the positioning relative to prior cognitive modeling work and adding targeted robustness and/or design-optimization analyses would make it a strong accept.
