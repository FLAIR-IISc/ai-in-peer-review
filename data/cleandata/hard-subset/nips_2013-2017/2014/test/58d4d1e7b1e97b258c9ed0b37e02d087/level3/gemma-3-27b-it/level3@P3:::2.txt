This paper addresses the important problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive modeling. The authors propose a framework for systematically testing the identifiability of a class of Bayesian observer models, aiming to improve experimental design and provide a more rigorous foundation for inferring internal representations from behavioral data. The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized, and the methodology presented is novel and potentially valuable, placing it above the NIPS threshold. However, the conclusions are significantly tempered by the numerous strong assumptions underlying the analysis, diminishing the overall impact relative to their length.

The core idea—to explore the parameter space and assess how well different models can account for observed data—is sound and connects to previous work on Bayesian inference in perception (e.g., [1, 2, 3]) and the challenges of inverse modeling ([17, 18, 19]). The framework extends prior efforts by offering a method for *a priori* identifiability analysis, a crucial step often overlooked. The choice of a continuous class of observer models and the analytical approach to exploring the parameter space are strengths.

However, the reliance on a large number of assumptions is a major weakness. While the authors attempt to justify these assumptions, many are strong and potentially limit the generalizability of the findings. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions needs further exploration. For instance, the choice of a log-stimulus transform (line 134) feels particularly constraining. Identifiability may heavily depend on this specific representational assumption, and the authors haven’t demonstrated robustness to alternative transformations. Similarly, the constraints on the prior distribution (line 158) – favoring a lattice over a uniform representation and allowing priors wider than the stimulus range – require stronger justification. Why should a user *arbitrarily* set such a wide prior?

Specific concerns arise in the results sections. In Section 4.1, the finding that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising, given the Gaussian assumptions. The authors should report which specific differences between conditions are statistically reliable, rather than focusing solely on the interchangeability. In Section 4.2, demonstrating that the model can *recover* the absence of a slow-speed prior (as opposed to merely showing it's identifiable when present) is critical. Simply showing that the algorithm accurately estimates the prior when it exists doesn't address the core issue of identifying its absence.

Several technical points require clarification. Line 106 needs to clarify why a continuous stimulus is modeled with a discrete distribution. Is this an inherent property of the experimental design, or a simplification made for analytical tractability? The justification for the Stirling approximation and the approximation E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] (lines 252/Equations 14-15) is insufficient. The authors must state the conditions under which these approximations hold, and provide some justification beyond the intuitive outcome. Equation 15 feels particularly justified *post hoc*.  On line 262, the "unnormalized density" being sampled from needs explicit specification – is it the dataset distribution, the posterior predictive distribution, or something else? 

The figure presentation can be improved. Figure 1 is confusing and overly complex. Integrating panel b into panel a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x) would significantly improve clarity.

Finally, the claim on line 365 that learning priors would not matter in 2AFC tasks is questionable. The authors need to clarify this statement. While 2AFC tasks often focus on relative preferences, priors can still influence the decision process, particularly when stimuli are ambiguous or response biases are present.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel framework for *a priori* identifiability analysis.
*   Clear writing and organization.
*   Potentially valuable methodology for improving experimental design in cognitive modeling.
*   Analytical approach to exploring parameter space.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous strong assumptions limit generalizability and impact.
*   Results are sensitive to modeling choices.
*   Insufficient justification for certain assumptions and approximations.
*   Some findings are unsurprising given the model’s assumptions.
*   Figure 1 is confusing.



**Overall:**

The paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but its impact is diminished by the substantial reliance on strong assumptions. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, contingent on addressing the concerns raised above. Specifically, the authors should: (1) rigorously justify their assumptions or demonstrate the robustness of their findings to alternative assumptions; (2) provide more detailed technical justifications for the approximations used; (3) clarify ambiguous statements; and (4) improve the clarity of Figure 1. A more thorough exploration of the limitations of the framework is also essential.
