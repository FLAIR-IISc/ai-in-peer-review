- Paper addresses identifiability of priors, likelihoods, and loss in Bayesian observer models and formalizes intrinsic BDT degeneracy.
- Authors propose a flexible 1-D observer model class (parametric stimulus mapping, mixture-of-Gaussians noise/loss, lapse and motor noise) enabling identifiability analysis.
- They derive an expected log-evidence criterion that reduces to a stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences and use MCMC to map “identifiability landscapes.”
- Two simulation case studies: interval timing (fragile prior recovery without constrained motor noise) and speed perception (robust recovery of slow-speed prior exponent).
- The work relates to longstanding and recent critiques of Bayesian cognitive models and to literature on model distinguishability and optimal experimental design, but positioning could be clearer.
- Technical soundness: derivation of expected log-likelihood as weighted KL sum is sound and offers a scalable proxy for posterior mass.
- Model construction is computationally efficient (mixtures permit closed-form or fast numerical expected loss).
- Empirical support: simulations are appropriate and convincingly illustrate both non-identifiability and robust recovery regimes.
- Limitations: conclusions depend on specific parametric choices (two-component mixtures, chosen f(s), coupling assumptions) with limited sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity: generally well written and organized, but some derivations are deferred and Eq.12 needs brief intuition in the main text; figures need better annotation.
- Originality: valuable combination of a parametric observer class, expected-KL identifiability mapping, and design comparison, but novelty should be contrasted more directly with prior distinguishability/experimental-design work.
- Significance: provides a practical simulation-based tool for anticipating parameter confounds and ranking experimental designs with actionable insights.
- Major concern 1 — Positioning: clarify differences and connections to prior work on model mimicry, local identifiability/Fisher information, and Bayesian experimental design; consider an empirical or conceptual comparison.
- Major concern 2 — Modeling assumptions: justify two-Gaussian mixtures and f(s), test sensitivity to mixture complexity, skew/kurtosis, loss parameterization, lapse models, and discuss KL choice vs. alternatives.
- Major concern 3 — Design optimization: include a proof-of-concept stimulus/trial-allocation optimization to demonstrate how the framework supports design improvement.
- Major concern 4 — Generality: discuss and, if possible, demonstrate extension beyond 1-D estimation (e.g., multi-cue integration or discrete-choice tasks).
- Minor: spell out the “trivial mathematical fact” about degenerate likelihoods, fix small typos (e.g., “nor”→“or”), clarify figure legends (stars/dashed lines), add Eq.12 intuition, and ensure reference formatting.
- Pros: tackles an important issue, provides an intuitive and efficient expected-KL framework, and presents well-executed case studies.
- Cons: insufficient engagement with prior identifiability/design literature, heavy reliance on parametric choices with limited robustness checks, and no real-data validation or design optimization.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — strengthen prior-work positioning and add targeted robustness and/or design-optimization analyses to reach a strong accept.
