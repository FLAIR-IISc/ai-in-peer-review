

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling: the identifiability of internal representations. The authors propose a systematic framework for testing whether parameters of Bayesian observer models can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, which is essential for drawing valid conclusions about mental representations. They extend Bayesian Decision Theory by formalizing the degeneracy problem (multiple parameter combinations yielding identical behavior), introducing constraints to address it, and developing a method to assess identifiability a priori for experimental design optimization.

The paper's main contribution lies in its practical application to two well-known perceptual tasks: time interval estimation and speed perception. The authors demonstrate how their framework can identify which experimental designs best resolve degeneracies, revealing non-intuitive findings (e.g., imposing a narrow loss function may worsen prior recovery in time estimation). This directly addresses longstanding concerns about model non-identifiability in cognitive science (Anderson 1978), and recent critiques that have hindered Bayesian frameworks' adoption (Jones & Love 2011; Bowers & Davis 2012).

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written, with clear theoretical exposition and practical case studies that demonstrate the framework's utility.
- The authors successfully formalize a problem that has been implicitly acknowledged in the field but never systematically analyzed.
- The case studies provide concrete, non-trivial insights about experimental design (e.g., measuring motor parameters significantly improves identifiability in time estimation).
- The framework's flexibility (allowing for different loss functions, sensory noise models, etc.) makes it broadly applicable to a wide range of Bayesian perceptual models.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Major concern 1 (Originality):** The paper fails to adequately situate its contribution within the extensive literature on model comparison in cognitive science. Key prior work by Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004) on Bayesian model comparison is entirely absent, making the paper's novel contribution unclear. The authors should explicitly differentiate their a priori identifiability analysis from existing model comparison techniques that focus on posterior model selection (e.g., Bayes factors).
  
- **Major concern 2 (Quality):** Several key modeling assumptions lack justification. The choice of p_meas in Eq. 3 (a two-component Gaussian mixture for sensory noise) appears arbitrary without explanation of why this specific form is necessary or how results would change with alternative noise models. Similarly, the parameter choices in Eq. 12 (e.g., setting σ` = 0.5) are not justified, leaving unclear how sensitive the results are to these choices. The authors should provide a sensitivity analysis or theoretical justification for these assumptions.

- **Minor issues:** The "trivial mathematical fact" at Line 53 should be explained for clarity. The second "nor" at Line 73 should be corrected to "or." Figure 2's legend should clarify what the star and dashed lines denote.

**Assessment against review criteria:**

*Quality:* The technical approach is sound, with clear derivations and appropriate use of KL divergence as a measure of model distinguishability. However, the lack of justification for key assumptions and failure to engage with prior literature on model comparison weakens the theoretical foundation.

*Clarity:* The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though the missing explanations for key modeling choices reduce clarity. The case studies effectively demonstrate the framework's utility.

*Originality:* While the specific framework is novel, the core problem (identifiability) has been recognized in cognitive science for decades. The paper's contribution would be stronger with clearer differentiation from prior model comparison work, particularly the cognitive science literature.

*Significance:* The framework addresses a genuine and important problem in Bayesian cognitive modeling. If properly contextualized and justified, it could significantly improve experimental design in perception research. However, the current presentation underestimates the existing work on this problem.

**Recommendation:**
This is a well-written paper addressing an important theoretical issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling. The framework has practical utility for experimental design, as demonstrated in the case studies. However, the paper's failure to properly situate its contribution within the existing literature on model comparison and the lack of justification for key assumptions significantly weaken its impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper tackles a critical problem in cognitive science that has been largely neglected in the Bayesian modeling literature. The framework is well-developed and offers practical guidance for experimental design. The case studies provide meaningful insights.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The failure to engage with substantial prior literature on model comparison (Pitt et al. 2002; Navarro et al. 2004) makes the novel contribution unclear. Key assumptions are not adequately justified, undermining confidence in the results. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues.

**Final recommendation:** Accept with major revision. The authors should (1) thoroughly engage with prior work on Bayesian model comparison in cognitive science, (2) provide theoretical justification for key modeling assumptions (particularly Eq. 3 and Eq. 12), and (3) clarify the minor technical issues noted above. With these revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.
