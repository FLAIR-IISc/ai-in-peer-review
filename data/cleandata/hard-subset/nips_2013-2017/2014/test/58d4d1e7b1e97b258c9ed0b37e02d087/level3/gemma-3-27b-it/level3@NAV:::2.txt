## Review of “A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception”

This paper addresses the critical issue of model identifiability within the increasingly prevalent Bayesian cognitive modeling framework. The authors propose a novel methodology for systematically testing whether parameters of Bayesian observer models can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, and apply this framework to time interval estimation and speed perception tasks. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, presenting a complex topic in a relatively accessible manner, and demonstrates a solid understanding of relevant literature, positioning itself well within the field. The proposed approach is a valuable contribution and easily exceeds the threshold for NIPS due to its methodological novelty. 

However, the strength of the conclusions is tempered by a large number of assumptions made throughout the analysis. While acknowledging the need for assumptions is reasonable, the sheer quantity—regarding prior distributions, noise structures, loss functions, and mappings—detracts from the generalizability of the findings. The results, while interesting, feel contingent upon these specific choices.  The core of the framework relies on specifying the observer model *a priori*, and the identifiability analysis then tests the sensitivity of that specific model to parameter variations. 

Section 4.1’s finding that interchangeability of sensory and motor noise is unsurprising feels trivial without demonstrating which specific differences between conditions yield statistically reliable results.  The authors should report p-values or confidence intervals to strengthen this claim. In Section 4.2, demonstrating the model's ability to recover the *absence* of a slow-speed prior in a reference observer is crucial for demonstrating the framework’s utility; currently, the paper only shows recovery *when* the prior is present. 

Specific concerns include the justification for modeling a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution (line 106) – is this an experimental constraint or a modeling choice? The strong constraint of a log-stimulus transform (line 134) warrants further discussion regarding its impact on identifiability.  The authors should also address whether the imposed constraints and entropy maximization genuinely guarantee a unimodal fitted distribution (line 143), or if bimodality remains a possibility.  Additionally, the justification for favoring a lattice prior over a uniform representation (line 158) is unclear, as is the allowance for priors wider than the true stimulus range.  

Figure 1 is confusing; integrating panel b into a and removing the derived p_est(s^*|x) would substantially improve clarity. The approximations used in lines 252-253 (Stirling's approximation and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) require justification, including a discussion of the conditions under which they hold. Finally, the claim in line 365 regarding the irrelevance of learning priors in 2AFC tasks needs further elaboration, especially given the established role of priors in perceptual decision-making.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Methodologically novel and potentially impactful.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.
*   Addresses a critical issue in Bayesian modeling.
*   Demonstrates a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Relies on a large number of assumptions, limiting generalizability.
*   Some results lack statistical rigor.
*   Figure 1 needs improvement.
*   Several points require further justification and clarification.
