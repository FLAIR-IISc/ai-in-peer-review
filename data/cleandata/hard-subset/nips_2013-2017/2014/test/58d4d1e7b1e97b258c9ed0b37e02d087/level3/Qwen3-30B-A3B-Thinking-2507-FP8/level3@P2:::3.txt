

The paper introduces a framework for systematically testing identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception, addressing a critical issue where multiple combinations of model components (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) can yield identical behavioral predictions. This problem has been recognized in the literature [17, 18, 19], but the authors present a novel method to explore identifiability *a priori* before conducting experiments, with practical applications for improving experimental design. The framework builds upon previous Bayesian observer models [7, 9, 12, 24] by introducing a more flexible parametrization of sensory noise, likelihood, prior, loss, and response distributions, while maintaining computational tractability.

The authors formalize identifiability using KL divergence between response probability densities, which they use as a measure of model support. They demonstrate the framework's utility through two case studies: interval timing and speed perception. In the interval timing case study, they show how different experimental designs affect the recovery of internal priors, finding that imposing a narrow loss function (closer to MAP) can worsen prior reconstruction quality. In the speed perception case study, they demonstrate that prior and noise characteristics can be reliably recovered, supporting previous findings about slow-speed priors.

Strengths:
The paper's theoretical foundation is sound, with rigorous mathematical derivations that clearly connect the identifiability problem to practical experimental design. The case studies effectively illustrate how the framework can identify non-intuitive design choices (e.g., the detrimental effect of narrow loss functions in interval timing). The model's flexibility—combining robustness (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) with mathematical convenience—makes it applicable to a wide range of perceptual tasks. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

Weaknesses:
The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative, closely resembling recent work by Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014). The authors should add a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and explicitly stating how their framework advances existing approaches. The identifiability claims depend heavily on model assumptions, and the framework does not resolve practical distinctions between sensory, decision, and response noise—this limitation should be more thoroughly discussed. While the framework is theoretically elegant, its practical utility could be better demonstrated with more concrete examples of how it would change experimental design in real-world settings.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The framework addresses a genuine problem in the field with practical implications for experimental design
- The theoretical foundation is sound and well-supported
- The case studies provide concrete demonstrations of the framework's utility
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

Con arguments for rejection:
- The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative
- The framework's practical limitations are not sufficiently acknowledged
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from recent work in the field

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the methodology for Bayesian modeling of perception. The authors have developed a useful tool for assessing identifiability before conducting experiments, which could improve the reliability of inferences from Bayesian models of perception. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, though it would benefit from clearer differentiation from recent work and a more thorough discussion of practical limitations.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1) Adding a new section that reviews recent Bayesian perceptual models (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014) and explicitly states how this work advances them
2) Discussing the practical limitations of identifiability claims more thoroughly, particularly regarding the distinction between sensory, decision, and response noise
3) Clarifying the scope of the framework and potential extensions to more complex perceptual tasks

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS, providing a valuable tool for researchers working with Bayesian models of perception. I recommend acceptance.
