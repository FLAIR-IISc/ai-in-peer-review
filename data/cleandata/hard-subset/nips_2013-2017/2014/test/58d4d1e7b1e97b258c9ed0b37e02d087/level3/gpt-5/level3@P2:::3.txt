Summary
This paper addresses a central and under-discussed issue in Bayesian observer modeling: whether internal components (prior, likelihood, loss, lapse, and motor noise) are identifiable from behavioral data. The authors construct a flexible yet tractable class of estimation models for one-dimensional stimuli, parameterizing: (i) a stimulus-to-internal mapping f(s) with stimulus-dependent noise (mixtures of Gaussians with controlled mean/variance/skew/kurtosis), (ii) a prior as a Gaussian mixture basis, (iii) a loss function as a mixture of (inverted) Gaussians, (iv) lapses, and (v) motor noise. They then propose an a priori identifiability analysis grounded in expected log likelihood, yielding a KL-based criterion: the expected support for a model θ given data generated by θ* reduces to a stimulus-weighted sum of KL divergences between predicted response distributions. Sampling from the induced “approximate posterior” E(θ|θ*) via MCMC maps non-identifiable directions and allows comparison of experimental designs before data collection.

Two case studies illustrate utility. In interval timing, the framework shows that: baseline designs poorly recover the prior (confounding between sensory and motor noise), increasing trials in a single range helps little, encouraging MAP-like losses can worsen recovery (σℓ weakly identifiable), but independently measuring motor noise markedly improves prior recovery. In speed perception, the method recovers parameters robustly, including the slow-speed prior exponent, even when decoupling internal from objective likelihoods; estimates of certain noise lower bounds remain slightly biased.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on a large literature using Bayesian decision theory to explain perception (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli; Jazayeri & Shadlen; Körding & Wolpert) and inversion of internal representations (e.g., Houlsby et al. “cognitive tomography”). It is close in spirit and technique to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), who developed tractable observer models, mixture-based approximations, and analyses of suboptimality; Navarro, Pitt & Myung (2004) studied model distinguishability. The present contribution is to (i) unify a broad, analytically convenient parameterization (notably for loss) and (ii) formalize a priori identifiability via a KL-based expected-evidence objective that can rank designs without costly data resampling.

Strengths
- Timely focus on identifiability, a foundational issue often sidelined in Bayesian perception papers.
- Elegant, general, and computationally efficient parameterization (mixture-based likelihood, prior, and loss in a common internal space) enabling closed-form expected loss and fast evaluation.
- Clear, actionable design implications (e.g., measure motor noise; be cautious with narrow loss manipulations).
- Case studies are well-chosen: they replicate known ambiguities in timing and validate robustness in speed perception.
- The KL-based formulation is principled and connects naturally to evidence-based model comparison.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Incremental relative to recent work (especially Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al., and the model distinguishability literature). Much of the modeling toolkit (mixture approximations, lapse, internal mapping) is known; the main novelty is the a priori KL-based identifiability mapping and its use for design ranking.
  • Suggestion: add a dedicated related-work section situating this framework among recent Bayesian perceptual models and model recovery/identifiability methods, and articulate the specific advances (e.g., new loss parameterization; unified identifiability objective; experimental design guidance) with side-by-side contrasts.
- Claims about “mathematical identifiability” hinge on assumed parametric families and shared-internal-space constraints; the framework usefully diagnoses practical non-identifiability but does not resolve fundamental equivalences between prior, likelihood, and loss.
  • Suggestion: add an explicit discussion of structural vs practical identifiability in this setting, clarify which invariances are broken by which constraints (e.g., fixing loss family, measuring motor noise), and temper generality claims accordingly.
- Limited exploration of model misspecification. Identifiability conclusions may change if, e.g., the true loss is not well captured by the chosen mixture family, or if f(s) is misparameterized.
  • Suggestion: include sensitivity analyses varying M (prior basis size), loss family rigidity (single vs mixture), alternative f(s) forms, and stimulus sampling densities; report how design recommendations change.
- The design objective is closely related to optimal design criteria (e.g., maximizing expected information gain). The paper would benefit from making this connection explicit and discussing whether iterative/active design could be integrated.

Quality
Technically sound and well-supported with derivations (KL formulation) and simulations. The case studies are appropriate and conclusions are cautious where needed (e.g., σℓ weak identifiability). Please report computational costs (runtime, MCMC diagnostics) and provide pseudocode or code to aid reproducibility.

Clarity
Generally clear, though notation-heavy. The four-stage model is explained well. Minor suggestions:
- Move some parametric details to the appendix and add a summary schematic with parameter roles and typical ranges.
- Provide a short algorithm box describing how E(θ|θ*) is computed and sampled.
- Clarify the “decoupled likelihood” case in speed perception and how contrastspecific parameters are shared.

Originality
Moderate. The unification and KL-based a priori identifiability mapping are useful, but many components build directly on prior work. The main novelty lies in turning these into a general, design-oriented framework.

Significance
Moderate to high for practitioners designing psychophysics experiments and for the Bayesian perception community. The method offers concrete guidance on what can and cannot be recovered and when additional measurements are essential.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (a priori identifiability and design).
- Methodologically solid and practically useful.
- Clear, nontrivial design insights in timing; reassuring validation in speed perception.
- Likely to be used by researchers fitting Bayesian observer models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental relative to closely related recent papers.
- Identifiability assurances remain contingent on model class; fundamental dissociations (sensory vs decision vs motor noise) remain practically unresolved.
- Limited analysis of misspecification and computational details.

Questions for authors
1) Can you quantify how design rankings change with M, the prior basis size, and with alternative f(s) parameterizations?
2) How sensitive are conclusions to loss-family misspecification (e.g., single Gaussian vs mixture)?
3) Can you relate your objective to mutual information/expected information gain and discuss prospects for adaptive stimulus selection?
4) Will you release code and example configs for the two case studies?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a principled and practical framework for testing identifiability and improving experimental design in Bayesian observer studies. With a strengthened related-work section and a candid discussion of practical identifiability limits, this will be a valuable contribution.
