

This paper addresses a critical yet underexplored issue in Bayesian cognitive modeling: the non-identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) in perceptual tasks. The authors propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing, leveraging KL divergence to quantify how well experimental designs recover model parameters. This work extends prior NIPS contributions on Bayesian observer models (e.g., Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Maloney & Mamassian, 2002) by providing a *practical methodology* for experimental design—not just theoretical analysis—addressing a gap noted in critiques of model interpretation (e.g., Jones & Love, 2011). The framework is applied to two case studies: time interval estimation (Section 4.1) and speed perception (Section 4.2), demonstrating how design choices (e.g., loss function constraints, motor noise measurement) impact identifiability.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with a novel, efficient framework that directly addresses a pressing methodological challenge. The case studies provide concrete, actionable insights: for time estimation, the framework reveals that imposing a "MAP-like" loss function (Section 4.1, MAP design) *hinders* prior recovery (counterintuitive but well-justified via parameter degeneracy), while measuring motor noise (MTR) resolves identifiability. In speed perception (Section 4.2), the framework validates prior work on slow-speed priors, showing robust recovery even with decoupled noise representations. The generative model (Section 2) is flexible yet computationally tractable, enabling systematic exploration of the identifiability landscape—unlike prior ad-hoc approaches (e.g., Maloney et al., 2002). The paper’s significance lies in its practical utility for experimental design, a clear contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:** The primary limitation is the *heavy reliance on strong assumptions*, which undermines the generality of conclusions. For instance:  
- The log-stimulus transform (Eq. 2) is presented as a "natural" constraint (Line 134), but its impact on identifiability is untested. The framework’s validity may depend entirely on this choice, yet the paper does not explore alternatives (e.g., linear transforms) or justify why it is necessary.  
- The entropy-maximization approach for sensory likelihoods (Lines 143–144) is claimed to guarantee unimodality, but the derivation (Supplementary Material) does not rule out bimodal solutions. This is critical, as bimodality could invalidate the model class.  
- The lattice prior (Eq. 4) assumes a 50% wider range than the true stimulus (Line 158), but the rationale for this specific choice is absent. A user might reasonably ask: *Why not 100% wider? How is this set in practice?*  
- Section 4.1’s conclusion that sensory/motor noise are interchangeable (Fig. 2c) is unsurprising (as noted by the reviewer), yet the paper does not report *statistical reliability* of this degeneracy (e.g., via confidence intervals over sampled parameters).  
- Section 4.2 must explicitly validate recovery of the *absence* of a slow-speed prior (e.g., via the flat-prior sanity check mentioned in the abstract but not emphasized in results). The paper states this was done (Supplementary Material), but it should be highlighted as a key validation.  

**Technical Concerns:**  
- The approximation in Eq. 15 (E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) lacks justification for the required sample size *N* (Line 252). The authors claim it "holds intuitively," but this needs empirical or theoretical bounds.  
- Line 262’s "unnormalized density" (E(θ|θ*) ∝ e^〈log Pr(D|θ)〉) is ambiguous. Is this a posterior over θ or a dataset distribution? Clarification is essential for reproducibility.  
- Line 365’s dismissal of "learning priors in 2AFC tasks" lacks justification. The original speed perception study (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006) used 2AFC, yet the paper assumes no learning—this assumption needs explicit defense.  

**Recommendation:** This is a **weak accept**. The framework is methodologically sound, novel, and addresses a genuine gap in cognitive modeling, making it a strong fit for NIPS. However, the overreliance on untested assumptions (log transform, lattice prior, entropy constraints) weakens the generalizability of the case studies. The paper’s impact would be significantly enhanced by:  
1) Explicitly testing the log-stimulus transform’s necessity (e.g., via alternative mappings),  
2) Validating the flat-prior recovery in Section 4.2,  
3) Justifying all key assumptions with sensitivity analyses or citations,  
4) Clarifying technical ambiguities (entropy maximization, unnormalized density).  

The authors’ focus on *experimental design* rather than just model fitting is a major strength, and the framework could become a standard tool if assumptions are properly contextualized. With revisions addressing these points, the paper would make a significant contribution to the field. As is, the assumptions list is longer than the results, tempering its impact. The core methodology, however, is sufficiently innovative and well-executed to warrant acceptance pending minor revisions.
