

This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked issue in Bayesian modeling of perception: the identifiability of internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. While Bayesian observer models have become dominant in psychophysics (e.g., Knill & Richards, 1996; Maloney & Mamassian, 2002), their validity hinges on the uniqueness of the inverse mapping from behavior to mental representations—a problem highlighted by Jones & Love (2011) and Bowers & Davis (2012) as a major limitation. The authors propose a systematic framework to test identifiability *a priori* for a broad class of Bayesian observer models, leveraging KL divergence to quantify the distinguishability of models under different experimental designs. They apply it to two case studies: time interval estimation (showing that motor noise measurement and loss function constraints critically affect prior recovery) and speed perception (demonstrating robust reconstruction of slow-speed priors).  

**Strengths**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with a compelling motivation grounded in the empirical success and theoretical fragility of Bayesian models in perception. The framework is technically sound, efficiently implemented via MCMC over the identifiability landscape, and directly addresses a gap in experimental design methodology. The case studies are relevant and well-executed: the time interval analysis reveals non-intuitive findings (e.g., imposing a narrow loss function *worsens* identifiability), while the speed perception results validate prior claims (Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006). The model’s flexibility—covering sensory noise, priors, loss functions, and motor errors via a unified parametrization—makes it broadly applicable.  

**Weaknesses**: The paper’s most significant flaw is its neglect of foundational work on model identifiability in cognitive science. The framework’s core approach (using KL divergence to assess distinguishability) directly parallels Navarro et al. (2004) and Pitt et al. (2002), which established similar methods for comparing cognitive models *at NIPS* and beyond. These papers are essential for contextualizing the contribution, yet they are omitted. Without this, the "novelty" claim is undermined—this is not a new technique but a *domain-specific application* of established model comparison principles. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this and clarify how their framework *extends* prior work (e.g., by focusing on *a priori* experimental design optimization rather than post-hoc model selection).  

Second, key modeling choices lack justification. The sensory model (Eq. 3) assumes a two-component Gaussian mixture for p_meas(x|s), but the rationale for this specific parametrization over alternatives (e.g., Laplace, exponential) is absent. Similarly, the KL divergence approximation in Eq. 12 relies on assumptions about the data-generating process (e.g., fixed Ntr, uniform stimuli) whose impact on results is untested. The paper claims "the framework allows to test robustness" but provides no analysis of how results change under alternative assumptions (e.g., varying the number of Gaussian components or stimulus distributions). For instance, the time interval study’s conclusion that "measuring motor noise resolves degeneracy" (Figure 2c) hinges entirely on the assumed parametrization of motor noise (σ²report(s) = ρ₀² + ρ₁²s²)—a choice that could be invalidated if motor noise follows a different functional form.  

**Recommendation**: The paper tackles an important problem with a well-executed, technically rigorous approach. However, its failure to engage with prior model comparison literature (especially NIPS-relevant work) and justify key assumptions weakens its contribution. It is not *incredibly* novel for NIPS, but the case studies offer concrete value for experimentalists. I recommend **Weak Accept** with the following conditions:  
- **Major revision required**: Add a detailed comparison with Navarro et al. (2004) and Pitt et al. (2002), clarifying how this framework advances the state of the art in *experimental design* rather than model comparison methodology.  
- **Justify assumptions**: In the supplementary material, demonstrate sensitivity analysis for key choices (e.g., Gaussian mixture components in p_meas, motor noise parametrization) to show results are robust.  
- **Clarify terminology**: Replace "trivial mathematical fact" (Line 53) with a brief justification, and fix the "nor/or" typo (Line 73).  

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
*Pro*: Addresses a high-impact problem in perception modeling; clear case studies; practical framework for experimental design; strong technical execution.  
*Con*: Overlooks critical prior work (NIPS 2002, 2004); unjustified modeling assumptions; novelty overstated relative to existing model comparison literature.  

The paper’s significance for cognitive science is high, but for NIPS—where methodological novelty in model comparison is expected—it falls short without the necessary contextualization. With the suggested revisions, it would make a valuable contribution. As is, it risks being seen as a reapplication of standard techniques rather than a novel methodological advance.
