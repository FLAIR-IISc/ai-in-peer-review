This paper introduces a novel framework for assessing, a priori, the identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors correctly identify that a fundamental problem in this modeling approach is the intrinsic degeneracy where different combinations of priors, likelihoods, and loss functions can produce empirically indistinguishable behavior. This non-identifiability threatens the core goal of using these models to recover an observer's internal representations. The proposed framework consists of two main parts: first, a flexible and computationally efficient class of parametric Bayesian observer models that can capture a wide range of perceptual tasks; second, a method to map the "identifiability landscape" by calculating the expected log-likelihood of a candidate model given a "ground truth" reference model. This expectation is elegantly shown to be proportional to the KL divergence between the response distributions of the two models, allowing for efficient exploration via MCMC sampling. The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework with two case studies: one in time interval estimation, used to compare the effectiveness of different experimental designs, and another in speed perception, used to validate the robustness of inferring a "slow-speed prior".

While the general problem of model identifiability is well-known in cognitive science, this work provides a valuable, concrete, and computationally tractable tool specifically tailored to the widely used class of Bayesian observer models.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and often-neglected issue in computational cognitive science. The ability to prospectively evaluate an experimental design's power to identify parameters of interest is highly significant. The proposed framework, combining a general model class with a principled KL-divergence-based metric for identifiability, is a novel and important contribution.
*   **Methodology:** The formulation of the identifiability problem in terms of the expected log-likelihood, and its connection to KL divergence, is elegant and computationally efficient. It cleverly avoids the need for expensive data simulation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation and a logical structure. The case studies effectively illustrate the practical application of the proposed framework.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The main weakness of the paper lies in the strong assumptions of the model and some lack of justification for specific modeling choices and analytical steps. While the framework is powerful, its conclusions are conditional on these assumptions.

1.  **Dependence on Strong Assumptions:** The proposed observer model, while flexible, relies on a long list of specific parametric assumptions (e.g., the log-like sensory mapping in Eq. 2, the mixture-of-Gaussians forms for all components). The impact of the conclusions is therefore tied to the validity of these choices. For instance, how sensitive are the identifiability results to the specific form of the sensory map `f(s)` (line 134)? The ability to identify model components might depend heavily on this assumed representational space.

2.  **Missing Control and Statistical Rigor in Case Studies:**
    *   **Section 4.2:** The analysis of the slow-speed prior is a compelling example. However, a crucial control is needed to demonstrate the method's validity. The authors should show that their framework can correctly recover the *absence* of a structured prior if the reference observer is defined with a flat prior. While the text mentions a "FLA" condition, these results are not presented or discussed.
    *   **Section 4.1:** The conclusion that measuring motor parameters (MTR condition) best resolves the degeneracy is plausible. However, to compare the different experimental designs more rigorously, it would be useful to have a measure of statistical reliability for the differences in reconstruction error (Fig 2b) between conditions.

3.  **Clarity of Technical Details and Justifications:**
    *   **Derivation of Eq. 12:** The main metric relies on the derivation in the supplementary material. The reviewer notes refer to approximations (e.g., Stirling's, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]). The validity of these approximations is critical to the soundness of the entire framework. Please provide justification for them and specify the conditions (e.g., on the number of trials N) under which they hold.
    *   **Modeling Choices:** Several choices could benefit from further justification. Why model a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution (line 106)? What is the rationale for the lattice-based prior representation over other flexible forms, and for setting its range 50% wider than the stimulus range (line 158)?
    *   **MCMC Sampling:** The procedure is briefly described as sampling from an "approximate expected posterior density" (line 262). Please provide more detail on the target distribution for the MCMC sampler.
    *   **Assumption in 2AFC task:** The claim that subjects do not learn the experimental prior in a 2AFC task (line 365) is a strong one and contrary to some findings. Could you please provide further justification or references to support this assumption, as it is central to the second case study?
    *   **Unimodality Constraint:** Regarding the mixture-of-Gaussians likelihood (line 143), does the maximum-entropy approach with fixed moments guarantee unimodality, or is this an additional enforced constraint?
    *   **Figure 1:** This figure is difficult to parse. I suggest integrating the components of panel (b) into the graphical model in (a) and perhaps removing the derived quantity `p_est(s*|x)` to improve clarity.

### Summary of Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and methodologically interesting approach to a significant problem in computational modeling. It is well-written and the potential impact is high. However, its conclusions rest on a series of strong assumptions, and several key technical details and modeling choices require further clarification and justification. The case studies would also be strengthened by the inclusion of a key control experiment and more rigorous comparison between conditions. I believe the paper is above the acceptance threshold for NIPS due to its methodological contribution, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the points raised above to improve the work's quality and impact.
