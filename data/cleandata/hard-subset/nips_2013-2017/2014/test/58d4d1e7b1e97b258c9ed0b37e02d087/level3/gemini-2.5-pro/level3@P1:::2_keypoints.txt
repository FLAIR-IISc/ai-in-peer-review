- Presents a novel framework to assess a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models in perception, addressing model degeneracy where different components produce identical behavior.
- Proposes two components: a flexible parametric class of observer models and a method to quantify identifiability via expected log-likelihood (related to KL-divergence) averaged over datasets from a reference model.
- Uses MCMC to explore an "identifiability landscape" and demonstrates the framework on time-interval estimation and speed-perception case studies.
- Strength: Tackles a fundamental, under-addressed problem and offers a systematic, experimentally useful approach to improve experimental design.
- Strength: Generally clear writing and logical structure, with good exposition of degeneracy and the proposed model/analysis framework.
- Strength: Technically solid use of expected log-likelihood/KL-divergence and effective case-study demonstrations that yield non-obvious design predictions.
- Main weakness: Conclusions may be limited by strong, specific parametric assumptions (log sensory map, mixture-of-two-Gaussians likelihoods/losses, fixed-lattice Gaussian prior).
- Suggestion: Demonstrate robustness of conclusions to modeling assumptions or justify the necessity of those choices.
- Request 1: Use MCMC samples to report confidence intervals or statistical tests on KL-divergence to rigorously compare experimental designs rather than point estimates.
- Request 2: Provide a prominent control showing recovery of a flat/uniform prior (no slow-speed bias) to avoid spurious inference, not just supplemental mention.
- Request 3: Discuss sensitivity of identifiability results to the choice of sensory map f(s) and the strong assumption of translationally invariant internal noise.
- Request 4: Clarify whether the moment-matching/local max-entropy procedure for the two-Gaussian likelihood guarantees unimodality for all parameter settings or provide references.
- Request 5: Justify modeling the prior as a fixed-lattice Gaussian mixture versus alternatives (splines, histograms) and the choice to make the prior range 50% wider than the stimulus range.
- Request 6: Bring key steps/assumptions from the supplement derivation linking expected log-likelihood to KL-divergence into the main text and state conditions for validity (e.g., large N_tr).
- Request 7: Simplify/merge Figure 1 panels to improve clarity and focus on the stimulus-to-response path rather than redundant graphical elements.
- Request 8: Support the claim that subjects in 2AFC tasks do not learn the experimental stimulus distribution with a citation or further justification, as it influences the second case study.
- Overall: Valuable and above-threshold contribution to NIPS, but would be strengthened by addressing robustness, controls, clarifications, and clearer presentation.
