- Addresses the important, often-overlooked problem of model identifiability in Bayesian perceptual models.  
- Proposes a novel, systematic framework to test parameter recoverability with practical experimental-design implications.  
- Uses a flexible Bayesian observer model and applies a KL-divergence-based method to evaluate identifiability.  
- Demonstrates the framework on time-interval estimation and speed perception tasks.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and mathematically sound.  
- Methodologically rigorous: principled use of KL divergence and MCMC sampling.  
- Practical relevance: directly helps researchers fitting Bayesian models and improving experimental designs.  
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions (priors, likelihoods, loss, sensory mapping) limits generalizability.  
- Sensitivity to those assumptions is not thoroughly investigated.  
- Result that sensory and motor noise are interchangeable is unsurprising; needs clarification of conditions yielding reliable improvements and statistical validation.  
- Fails to demonstrate recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior in reference observers—crucial for validating non-identifiability detection.  
- Specific technical clarifications needed: rationale for discrete approximation of continuous stimulus (L106).  
- Need discussion of sensitivity to log-stimulus transform f(s) as a strong representational constraint (L134).  
- Clarify whether likelihood constraints and entropy maximization guarantee unimodality or allow bimodality (L143).  
- Justify lattice prior choice and why it’s wider than true stimulus range (L158).  
- Justify approximations (Stirling; E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) and conditions under which they hold for used N (L252–254); Eq.15 needs firmer derivation.  
- Precisely specify the "unnormalized density" being sampled from (L262).  
- Question assertion that learning priors doesn’t matter in 2AFC tasks—needs clarification (L365).  
- Figure 1 is confusing; suggest integrating panel (b) into (a) and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) for clarity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses critical identifiability issue, offers novel principled methodology, demonstrates practical relevance, and is clearly written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limiting assumptions, limited impact/statistical support for key results, missing demonstration of absence-of-prior recovery, and several technical details unresolved.  
- Overall recommendation: valuable contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions focusing on assumptions, statistical validation, and the missing demonstration.
