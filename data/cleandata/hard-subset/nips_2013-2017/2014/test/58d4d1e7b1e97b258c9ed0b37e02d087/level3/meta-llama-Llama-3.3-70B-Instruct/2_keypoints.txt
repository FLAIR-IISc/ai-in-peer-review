- Tackles important issue of model identifiability in cognitive modeling.  
- Methodology is interesting, innovative, and worthy of presentation.  
- Relies on a long list of assumptions that are hard to understand and justify.  
- Results are less than overwhelming despite methodological novelty.  
- Key results in Section 4.1 are unsurprising.  
- Incorporation of both sensory and motor noise in the model is questionable.  
- Model should be able to recover that the reference observer lacked a slow-speed prior (issue in Section 4.2).  
- Stimulus drawn from a discrete distribution may not well represent the intended continuous variable.  
- Log-stimulus transform imposes strong constraints that may drive the apparent success.  
- Constraints in Eq. 4 may not guarantee Eqn. 3 is unimodal and could allow bimodality.  
- Unclear why the observer's prior favors a lattice representation over a uniform one.  
- Figure 1 is confusing; suggest integrating panels and removing p_est(s^*|x).  
- Conditions for Equation 14’s approximate correctness are unclear; Stirling and E[x log x] approximations are questioned.  
- Ambiguity about which density is meant in line 262; dataset distribution may be intended.  
- Learning of priors being irrelevant in 2AFC is an interesting and innovative result that needs more discussion and implications.  
- Paper is technically sound and well-organized but needs stronger theoretical/experimental support for claims and clearer justifications for assumptions.  
- High originality and significance with potential to impact understanding of perception and decision-making.  
- Recommend acceptance with revisions to address assumptions, clarity, and robustness of results.  
- Pros: innovative methodology, high originality, high significance, technically sound.  
- Cons: weak results, many assumptions, some unsurprising findings, confusing figure, unclear equations/assumptions.
