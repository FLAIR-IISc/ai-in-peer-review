## Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper tackles the important and longstanding problem of identifiability in Bayesian models of perception, a crucial hurdle limiting their widespread adoption in cognitive science. The authors propose a novel framework for systematically testing whether parameters of these models can be reliably recovered from behavioral data, focusing on the degeneracy inherent in the Bayesian approach. Their method involves summarizing Bayesian Decision Theory, identifying sources of non-identifiability, prescribing constraints, testing parameter differentiability, and applying the framework to time interval estimation and motion perception tasks. 

The paper is well-written and logically structured, presenting a clear explanation of the problem and a detailed description of their proposed solution. The application of the framework to established psychophysical phenomena (time interval estimation and speed perception) provides concrete examples and demonstrates its potential utility. The authors appropriately highlight the need for careful consideration of model assumptions and experimental design in order to achieve identifiable models.

However, a significant weakness lies in the limited engagement with prior literature on model comparison and identifiability in cognitive science. The paper largely overlooks relevant work, such as Pitt et al. (2002) and Navarro et al. (2004), which have addressed similar concerns and developed techniques for model selection and parameter estimation in complex cognitive models. This omission makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty and contribution of the presented framework.

Furthermore, certain key modeling assumptions appear arbitrary or lack sufficient justification. For example, the definition of *p_meas* in Eq. 3 and the specific parameter choices in Eq. 12 are not thoroughly explained. It remains unclear how sensitive the results are to these assumptions, and a more detailed sensitivity analysis would strengthen the findings. While the authors acknowledge the need for constraints, the rationale for *these* specific constraints isn’t always compelling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical theoretical issue in Bayesian modeling of cognition.
*   Provides a clear and well-structured framework for assessing model identifiability.
*   Demonstrates the framework's applicability through concrete examples.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks sufficient engagement with related literature on model comparison.
*   Key modeling assumptions are insufficiently justified and potentially arbitrary.
*   The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions remains unclear.



**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 53: Clarify the “trivial mathematical fact.”
*   Line 73: Change “nor” to “or.”
*   Figure 2: Clarify the meaning of the star and dashed lines.

**Overall:** This is a valuable contribution that addresses an important problem. However, addressing the concerns regarding the lack of contextualization within existing literature and justifying key modeling choices would substantially improve the paper. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
