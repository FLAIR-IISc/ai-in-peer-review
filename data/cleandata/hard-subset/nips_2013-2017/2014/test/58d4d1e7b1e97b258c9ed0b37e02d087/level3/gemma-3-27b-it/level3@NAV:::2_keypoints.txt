- Paper proposes a novel, systematic framework to test parameter identifiability in Bayesian observer models and applies it to time interval estimation and speed perception.
- Writing and organization are clear, with good positioning in the relevant literature.
- Methodological contribution is potentially impactful and exceeds the conference threshold.
- Conclusions are weakened by a large number of strong assumptions (priors, noise structures, loss functions, mappings) that limit generalizability.
- Framework tests identifiability conditional on an a priori-specified observer model, so results may be model-specific.
- Claim in Section 4.1 that sensory and motor noise interchangeability is unsurprising needs statistical support (p-values or CIs).
- Section 4.2 should demonstrate recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior in a reference observer, not only recovery when the prior is present.
- Justification is needed for modeling a continuous stimulus with a discrete distribution (line 106): experimental constraint or modeling choice?
- The strong constraint of a log-stimulus transform (line 134) requires discussion of its impact on identifiability.
- Authors should clarify whether entropy maximization and imposed constraints guarantee a unimodal fitted distribution or allow bimodality (line 143).
- Rationale for preferring a lattice prior over a uniform representation and for allowing priors wider than the true stimulus range is unclear (line 158).
- Figure 1 is confusing; suggest integrating panel b into a and removing derived p_est(s^*|x) for clarity.
- Approximations using Stirling’s formula and E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x] (lines 252–253) need justification and conditions for validity.
- The claim that learning priors is irrelevant in 2AFC tasks (line 365) needs further elaboration given priors’ known role in perception.
- Acceptance arguments: methodological novelty, clarity, relevance to Bayesian modeling, and strong literature grounding.
- Rejection arguments: excessive assumptions limiting generalizability, insufficient statistical rigor in some results, figure and presentation issues, and several unresolved justifications.
