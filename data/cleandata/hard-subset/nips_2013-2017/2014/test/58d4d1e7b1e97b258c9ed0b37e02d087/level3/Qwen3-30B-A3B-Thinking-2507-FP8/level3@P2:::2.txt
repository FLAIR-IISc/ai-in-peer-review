

# Review of "A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception"

This paper addresses a critical issue in cognitive modeling: the identifiability of Bayesian observer models used to infer internal representations (priors, likelihoods, loss functions) from behavioral data. The authors propose a systematic framework for a priori identifiability testing, which is particularly important given the well-documented degeneracy in Bayesian models where multiple parameter combinations can yield identical behavior (as noted by Mamassian and Landy, 2001; Bowers and Davis, 2012). The paper builds on previous work in Bayesian cognitive modeling (e.g., Körding and Wolpert, 2006; Maloney and Mamassian, 2007) but extends it by providing a method to assess and resolve identifiability issues before experimental execution, rather than after model fitting.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes several important contributions. First, it introduces a novel, general framework for identifiability analysis that can be applied to a wide class of Bayesian observer models in perception research. The theoretical foundation using KL-divergence to quantify expected support for different parameter configurations (Equation 12) is sound and well-motivated. Second, the two case studies demonstrate practical applications: the time interval estimation task shows how experimental design choices (e.g., single vs. dual conditions, loss function constraints) impact identifiability, while the speed perception study validates previous findings about slow-speed priors. Third, the paper is clearly written with good organization, and the mathematical derivations are technically sound.

**Weaknesses:**
The main limitation is the heavy reliance on strong assumptions that may limit the generalizability of the findings. For instance, the log-stimulus transform (Equation 2) is a significant constraint that may affect identifiability results, but the authors don't sufficiently justify why this specific transformation is necessary or how results would change with different transformations. Similarly, the choice of a lattice-based prior (Equation 4) over a uniform representation lacks justification, and the claim that priors "should be 50% wider than the true stimulus range" (Line 158) is arbitrary without empirical support.

In Section 4.1, the finding that sensory and motor noise parameters are interchangeable is unsurprising, but the authors don't report whether this interchangeability is statistically significant across the parameter space. In Section 4.2, the paper claims the model can recover slow-speed priors, but it fails to demonstrate recovery of the absence of such a prior (as requested in the key points), which would be a critical test of the framework's validity. The paper also doesn't clarify why learning priors wouldn't matter in 2AFC tasks (Line 365), which contradicts established findings (e.g., Chalk et al., 2010).

Several technical points need clarification. The discrete stimulus distribution (Line 106) requires justification for modeling a continuous stimulus with discrete bins. The entropy maximization approach (Line 134) needs clarification on how it guarantees unimodality of the fitted distribution. The approximations in Equations 14-15 (Stirling approximation, E[x log x] ≈ E[x] log E[x]) lack justification for the conditions under which they hold. The sampling procedure (Line 262) needs specification of which "unnormalized density" is being sampled from.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The framework addresses a genuine gap in the literature for Bayesian cognitive modeling.
- The method has clear practical utility for experimental design.
- The two case studies demonstrate the framework's applicability.
- The paper is well-written and technically sound in its core methodology.

*Con:*
- Heavy reliance on strong, unjustified assumptions limits the generalizability of the results.
- Critical validation tests (e.g., recovery of absence of prior) are missing.
- Some key technical claims lack sufficient justification.
- The impact of the findings is diluted by the extensive list of assumptions required.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper that makes an important contribution to the methodological toolkit of cognitive scientists using Bayesian models. While the framework is novel and potentially valuable for experimental design, the heavy reliance on strong assumptions significantly reduces the impact of the reported results. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the key limitations noted above, particularly by demonstrating recovery of the absence of a slow-speed prior in Section 4.2 and providing clearer justification for the modeling choices.

The paper meets the threshold for NIPS as a good scientific contribution, but it is not among the most significant papers in the field. It should be accepted with minor revisions to address the technical concerns and strengthen the validation of the framework. The authors should clarify the assumptions, provide the missing validation test, and better justify the modeling choices to demonstrate the robustness of their framework across a broader range of scenarios.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
