This paper proposes a general framework for assessing a priori identifiability of Bayesian observer models of perception. The authors build a flexible, tractable class of observers for one-dimensional estimation tasks that explicitly parameterizes the sensory mapping, measurement noise, prior, loss function, lapse rate, and response noise. They derive an expected log-likelihood criterion that reduces to a sum of KL divergences between model-predicted response distributions across stimuli, yielding an “expected posterior” over model parameters that can be explored by MCMC to map identifiability under a chosen experimental design. Two case studies illustrate the approach: (1) interval timing, where they show that recovering priors is fragile unless motor noise is constrained and that encouraging MAP-like losses can harm identifiability; and (2) speed perception with a slow-speed prior, where they find that both prior shape and noise parameters are relatively well identified, even when internal likelihood is decoupled from the generative measurement noise. The work connects to a large body of literature on Bayesian observer models [e.g., Geisler; Knill & Richards; Stocker & Simoncelli], the psychology literature on model distinguishability [Navarro, Pitt & Myung], and recent attempts to infer internal representations from behavior [e.g., Houlsby et al.; Acerbi et al.]. The main novelty is to turn identifiability into an efficiently computable, experiment-design-aware quantity in a unified observer class.

Quality
- The framework is technically sound. The observer parameterization is rich (mixtures for likelihood, prior, and loss; monotone sensory map; lapse; motor noise) yet remains computationally convenient because expected loss reduces to Gaussian-mixture calculations and the expected support reduces to KL sums.
- The case studies are carefully set up, with clear “true” observers and principled perturbations of design (number of trials, loss width, known motor noise, coupling/decoupling of internal likelihood).
- Limitations are partially acknowledged in the timing example (e.g., sensory vs motor noise trade-offs; weak identifiability of loss width). However, a fuller analysis of practical indistinguishability between sensory, decision, and response noise would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The decomposition into sensation, perception, decision, and response stages is clear, and the equations are explicit.
- Some derivations and modeling choices are deferred to the Supplement (e.g., maximum-entropy construction of mixtures, details of the MCMC over the expected posterior, mapping 2AFC to estimation). Bringing key parts of these into the main text would help reproducibility.
- It would be helpful to more explicitly state prior choices on parameters used during sampling and to summarize convergence diagnostics.

Originality
- The observer formulation and use of mixtures for prior/likelihood/loss is close in spirit to earlier Bayesian perceptual modeling and “cognitive tomography” work (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014; Houlsby et al. 2013), and to the model distinguishability literature (Navarro, Pitt & Myung, 2004). The use of expected log-likelihood/KL as an identifiability proxy is standard in model comparison and design-of-experiments.
- The main contribution is the synthesis: a single, mathematically elegant and computationally efficient pipeline that (i) spans many observer variants with a common parameterization and (ii) directly connects identifiability to experimental design choices. This strikes me as incremental but useful.

Significance
- The framework has practical value for the community: it can guide experimental design, diagnose parameter trade-offs, and set expectations about what can be recovered from behavioral data. The interval timing case yields nontrivial design advice (e.g., measuring motor noise can be more valuable than increasing trials; narrow losses can hurt prior recovery). The speed perception results provide theoretical reassurance for prior inferences of slow-speed priors.
- Adoption is likely among groups already using Bayesian observer models; broader impact would grow if code and design recipes are released.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unified framework with explicit equations and tractable computations.
- Practical guidance for experimenters; insightful case studies that reveal non-obvious design consequences.
- Careful connection to Bayesian observer literature; addresses a timely concern (identifiability) with a concrete tool.

Cons for acceptance
- Contribution is incremental relative to recent Bayesian perceptual modeling and identifiability work; the relationships to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014), Houlsby et al. (2013), and formal optimal design methods are underdeveloped.
- Identifiability claims hinge on the assumed parameterization (mixtures, lapse model, monotone sensory map) and do not resolve practical confounds between sensory, decision, and response noise.
- Empirical validation beyond simulations is limited; the 2AFC-to-estimation mapping is assumed rather than tested.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a dedicated related-work section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and inference frameworks (e.g., cognitive tomography; adaptive internal models; identifiability/distinguishability) and clearly articulate advances over them.
- Discuss practical identifiability limitations explicitly: when sensory, loss, and motor parameters are provably confounded within this class; what external constraints (e.g., independent motor measurements, transfer across tasks, manipulated payoffs) most help; and how sensitive conclusions are to the mixture parameterizations.
- Clarify the 2AFC-to-estimation equivalence and, if possible, include a small empirical check or simulation demonstrating robustness of the identifiability conclusions across paradigms.
- Provide more implementation detail (priors, MCMC settings, convergence checks) and, ideally, release code to facilitate adoption.

Recommendation
- Accept. While not a radical departure from prior work, the paper offers a coherent, mathematically elegant framework with immediate utility for designing and interpreting Bayesian observer studies. Addressing the literature positioning and explicitly discussing practical identifiability limits would strengthen the final version.
