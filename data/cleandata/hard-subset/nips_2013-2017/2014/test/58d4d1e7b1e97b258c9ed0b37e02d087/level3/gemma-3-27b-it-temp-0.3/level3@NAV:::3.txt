## Review of “A Framework for Testing Identifiability of Bayesian Models of Perception”

This paper presents a framework for systematically analyzing the identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a crucial issue given their increasing use in perceptual modeling. The authors develop a continuous class of observer models with explicit equations for sensation, perception, decision-making, and response, aiming for both robustness (e.g., stimulus-dependent noise) and mathematical tractability. A key contribution is the proposal of a method to explore the identifiability landscape *a priori*, potentially improving experimental design. The work is mathematically elegant and offers a general framework for formalizing perceptual estimation, aligning well with the goals of NIPS. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous mathematical formulation and the clear articulation of the identifiability problem within the Bayesian perceptual modeling context. The authors successfully demonstrate the framework’s application through two case studies – time interval estimation and speed perception – providing concrete examples of how it can be used. The emphasis on mathematical identifiability is commendable, as it moves beyond simply fitting models to data and addresses the fundamental question of whether the recovered parameters are truly meaningful. 

However, the contribution feels somewhat incremental. The core issue of non-identifiability in Bayesian models is not new, and the presented approach builds heavily on previous work, particularly Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) which also tackled the degeneracy of Bayesian models and explored the implications for experimental design. While the authors acknowledge the existence of related work, a dedicated section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and explicitly outlining how this framework advances those efforts is crucial. This would better position the contribution within the existing literature and highlight its novelty. 

Furthermore, the authors’ claim of “identifiability” should be qualified. As they implicitly acknowledge, identifiability is contingent on the model assumptions (e.g., specific functional forms for priors, likelihoods, and loss functions). The framework doesn’t resolve the practical difficulty of distinguishing between sensory, decision, and response noise, which can lead to similar behavioral predictions. A discussion of this practical limitation and its implications for interpreting results from real-world experiments would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Mathematically sound and rigorous.
*   Addresses an important issue in Bayesian perceptual modeling.
*   Provides a useful framework for evaluating experimental designs.
*   Clear and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution, closely related to existing work.
*   Identifiability claims are model-dependent and don't address practical challenges.



**Recommendation:** I support acceptance to NIPS contingent upon the authors including a new section reviewing recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly stating how this work advances them. Additionally, they should discuss the practical limitations of the identifiability claims in the revised manuscript.
