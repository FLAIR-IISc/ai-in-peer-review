- Presents a flexible Bayesian framework formalizing sensory noise, priors, loss functions, and response distributions for perceptual estimation.
- Emphasizes mathematical identifiability, addressing inherent degeneracy beyond mere model fitting.
- Provides clear equations and an elegant, generalized approach useful for formalizing perceptual models.
- Demonstrates practical application via two case studies: time-interval estimation and speed perception.
- Shows how experimental design affects identifiability (e.g., narrow loss functions harm identifiability).
- Validates existing findings in speed perception, supporting the framework’s utility.
- Contribution feels incremental given prior work on degeneracy and constraints (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012, 2014).
- Lacks a dedicated literature review comparing and contrasting recent work on identifiability and parameter recovery.
- Claims of identifiability rely on specific model assumptions and gloss over practical difficulties.
- Does not sufficiently discuss the practical challenge of disentangling sensory, decision, and response noise.
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions: add a thorough related-work review and discuss practical identifiability limitations.
