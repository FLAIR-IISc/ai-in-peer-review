This paper presents a formal framework for assessing the *a priori* identifiability of Bayesian observer models, a widely used tool for understanding human perception. The authors first develop a flexible and computationally tractable class of observer models that parameterizes the entire perceptual and decision-making pipeline, including a sensory mapping that accounts for stimulus-dependent noise, and representations of the prior, likelihood, and loss function using mixtures of Gaussians. The core contribution is a method to quantify the expected statistical support for a given model (θ) when the data is generated by a known reference model (θ*). This measure, based on the KL-divergence between the models' response distributions, allows one to map out the "identifiability landscape" and assess which parameters can be reliably recovered. The framework's utility is demonstrated in two case studies: one analyzing how different experimental designs affect the recovery of priors in a time-interval estimation task, and another validating the robustness of inferring a "slow-speed prior" in visual perception.

This work builds upon a rich literature that uses Bayesian decision theory to model human perception (e.g., Knill & Richards, 1996; Körding & Wolpert, 2004; Stocker & Simoncelli, 2006). More specifically, the modeling approach of using flexible, parametric representations for the components of the Bayesian model is closely related to recent work by Acerbi et al. (PLoS Comp Bio, 2012; 2014), which also aimed to recover internal representations from behavioral data. The key innovation of the present work is to move beyond simply fitting such models and to provide a systematic, formal method for prospectively analyzing their identifiability before an experiment is even run.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and often-underestimated problem in computational modeling. The conclusions drawn from complex models are only meaningful if the model parameters are identifiable. This paper provides a principled and practical tool for researchers to assess the reliability of their inferences and, crucially, to optimize their experimental designs to mitigate identifiability issues. This is a significant contribution to the fields of computational neuroscience and cognitive science.
*   **Quality:** The proposed framework is mathematically sound and elegant. The use of KL-divergence to approximate the expected log-likelihood provides an efficient way to explore the high-dimensional parameter space. The parametric observer model is well-designed, striking a good balance between flexibility (e.g., capturing non-Gaussian noise, asymmetric loss functions, and Weber-like scaling) and the computational tractability needed for the analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem of non-identifiability and explaining their methodological solution step-by-step. The case studies effectively illustrate how the framework can be applied to generate concrete, non-obvious insights about experimental design (e.g., that a MAP-like loss function might worsen the recovery of the prior).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the identifiability analysis framework is novel and important, the Bayesian observer model itself appears highly similar to recent work, particularly Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014). The current manuscript does not sufficiently discuss this related work, which makes the modeling contribution seem more incremental than it might be. A clearer positioning with respect to this literature would strengthen the paper.
*   **Qualified Claims on Identifiability:** The paper's claims about resolving identifiability should be stated more cautiously. The analysis reveals degeneracies *within the assumed model class*. It does not, for example, solve the fundamental problem of practically distinguishing sensory noise from response noise if both have similar statistical properties. The analysis in Figure 2c correctly identifies this trade-off, but the discussion could be clearer that the framework highlights these deep-seated ambiguities rather than eliminating them. The solution offered (the MTR condition, where motor noise is measured separately) is an experimental one, which the framework helps to motivate, but the framework itself doesn't break the degeneracy.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  To better highlight the paper's novel contributions, I strongly recommend adding a paragraph or short section that explicitly reviews recent, similar Bayesian perceptual models (especially Acerbi et al., 2012, 2014). The authors should clarify how their proposed model builds upon or differs from these, thereby separating the novelty of the model from the novelty of the identifiability analysis.
2.  I would suggest adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the proposed identifiability analysis. Specifically, it would be beneficial to state explicitly that the analysis is contingent on the structural assumptions of the model and that it helps identify, but does not in itself resolve, fundamental degeneracies between different noise sources (sensory, decision, motor). This would add nuance and precision to the paper's claims.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses the critical and challenging problem of model identifiability in Bayesian cognitive science.
    *   Provides a novel, principled, and computationally efficient framework for performing *a priori* identifiability analysis.
    *   The framework has clear practical utility for improving experimental design, as demonstrated by the case studies.
    *   The paper is technically sound and clearly presented.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The modeling component of the work appears incremental and its relationship to very recent, similar work is not adequately discussed.
    *   The paper could be more precise about the scope and limitations of its claims regarding identifiability.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that makes an important methodological contribution to the field. The proposed framework for analyzing model identifiability is a valuable tool that could have a significant impact on how researchers design experiments and interpret computational models. While the originality of the observer model itself could be better contextualized, the novelty and significance of the identifiability analysis are clear. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors address the minor points regarding related work and the limitations of the analysis in the final version.
