- Proposes a Bayesian framework to systematically assess identifiability of observer models in perceptual tasks, addressing solution degeneracy.
- Combines mathematical elegance with computational efficiency and flexibility for wide experimental applicability.
- Demonstrates the framework on two case studies (time interval estimation and speed perception) to evaluate recovery of priors and noise characteristics.
- Emphasizes identifiability as a foundational but often overlooked issue in Bayesian perceptual modeling.
- Uses a novel MCMC-based approach to systematically explore parameter space and evaluate reliability of inferred parameters.
- Case studies yield actionable experimental-design insights (e.g., measuring motor noise improves prior recovery; slow-speed priors are robustly inferred).
- Shows identifiability under theoretical assumptions, enabling distinction between parameter estimates.
- Contribution is incremental—builds on existing Bayesian models without introducing fundamentally new concepts.
- Lacks explicit comparison with recent Bayesian models to clarify advances over state of the art.
- Identifiability analysis relies on theoretical assumptions and does not fully address practical challenges like disentangling sensory, decision, and response noise.
- Assumes motor noise can be independently measured, which may be infeasible in many real-world experiments.
- Mathematical exposition and parameterization sections are dense and could be made more accessible with intuitive explanations or visualizations.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to include comparisons to recent models and discussion of practical limitations.
