This paper proposes a general Bayesian observer model for perceptual estimation with explicit, tractable formulations for sensory noise (including stimulus-dependent and non-Gaussian features via mixtures), prior (Gaussian lattice mixture), loss (inverted Gaussian mixture), lapses, and motor noise, yielding a closed-form expected loss and a computable response distribution. Building on this model, the authors develop an a priori identifiability framework based on expected log-likelihood differences expressible as KL divergences, sampled via MCMC to map parameter regions that are empirically indistinguishable. Two case studies illustrate the approach: interval timing, where identifiability depends critically on design choices (e.g., measuring motor noise improves recovery; narrow losses harm it), and speed perception with a slow-speed prior, where inference appears robust. The work is well aligned with prior NIPS/NeurIPS themes on Bayesian perception and model assessment (e.g., Stocker & Simoncelli 2006; Houlsby et al. 2013; Navarro, Pitt & Myung 2004), and explicitly engages the degeneracy issue highlighted in the broader psychology literature.

Quality: The modeling choices are coherent and lead to efficient inference; the KL-based expected evidence is an appropriate, principled proxy for distinguishability. The empirical demonstrations are careful, though the paper sometimes blurs “structural/mathematical” vs “practical” identifiability, and several key conclusions hinge on specific parametric assumptions (Gaussian mixtures, monotone warping f, lapse model).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful equations and case studies. However, a dedicated related-work section covering recent Bayesian perceptual modeling (e.g., Acerbi et al. 2012; Acerbi, Vijayakumar & Wolpert 2014; and follow-ons) would help situate contributions and sharpen claims.

Originality: The framework is mathematically elegant and integrates robustness with tractability, but the conceptual advance over closely related work is incremental; several components (e.g., parametric observer, KL-based distinguishability) have strong precedents.

Significance: Despite incrementality, the tool is practical and likely to influence experimental design and interpretation across perceptual tasks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, general, and tractable observer model with explicit components.
- Principled identifiability metric and actionable design insights.
- Solid case studies; code-ready methodology is implied.

Cons:
- Incremental relative to Acerbi et al. (2012, 2014) and Navarro/Pitt/Myung.
- Identifiability claims rely on model class and do not resolve sensory vs decision vs response noise trade-offs.

Requested revisions:
- Add a comprehensive review of recent Bayesian perceptual models and clearly articulate how this framework advances them.
- Clarify structural vs practical identifiability; explicitly discuss limits in separating sensory, decision, and response noise (and suggest design manipulations—e.g., independent motor calibration, reward manipulations, external noise—to address them).
- Comment on sensitivity to the chosen parameterizations (mixture orders, f(s), lapse model), and contrast with alternatives (e.g., Fisher information, profile likelihood).

I support acceptance pending these clarifications.
