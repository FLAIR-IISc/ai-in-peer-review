Summary
The paper tackles a core limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): at test time, attribute classifiers are often unreliable, yet most ZSL methods treat their outputs as ground truth. The authors propose a random forest (RF) framework that trains directly on class-attribute signatures, while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability using ROC statistics estimated from attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes in proportion to attribute TP/FP rates at node-specific operating points, yielding splits that are both discriminative and learnable given expected errors. They also handle uncertainty in class signatures and extend the framework to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with standard data-based information gain via a tunable prior weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP [8,9] and a signature-only RF baseline; controlled noise experiments further illustrate robustness to attribute unreliability.

Relative to prior ZSL work that operates in attribute space [3,8,18,30] or leverages semantic/label embeddings [1,4,13,14,23], the main novelty is to incorporate attribute error models into the training objective, rather than only using attribute confidences at test time (as in DAP). The method is also related to decision trees with missing/uncertain features [16,21,28], but adapts those ideas to the ZSL regime where training instances are signatures and test instances are attribute predictions.

Quality
- Soundness: The ROC-driven fractional propagation and information gain redefinition are well-motivated and technically sound. Node-specific ROC estimation is a strength, capturing dependencies induced by previous splits. The few-shot extension is principled, treating signatures as a prior via a convex combination of IG terms.
- Empirical support: Results on three datasets with >250 attributes, 20 random trials, and ablations (without signature uncertainty; without node-specific ROC propagation) support the claims. Controlled noise experiments cleanly isolate the proposed contribution and show larger gains when attribute noise is heterogeneous—exactly the setting the method targets.
- Limitations: The approach depends on attribute-labeled validation data whose distribution should approximate unseen classes’ attribute error modes. While the authors provide an “upper bound” with TRUE ROC and note attempts to pick nearest seen signatures, an analysis of domain shift between seen and unseen classes would strengthen the claims. ROC estimates at deeper nodes can be data-sparse; the fallback to global DV is reasonable, but a principled smoothing (e.g., Beta priors or hierarchical shrinkage from parent nodes) could reduce variance. The one-vs-all forest training per unseen class may affect scalability when K is large; complexity/runtime are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The training/test-time distinctions and notations I, I′, DV(n) are explained and related to intuition. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Include full pseudocode in the main text or a more explicit algorithm box; precisely detail how thresholds t are sampled for binary signatures and how many (m,t) pairs are evaluated per node across datasets.
- Provide computational complexity and wall-clock runtimes vs. DAP and SIGNATURE RF, and memory impact of node-specific ROC tracking.
- Clarify the few-shot baseline (“dotted lines”): model choice, hyperparameters, features, and whether it uses the same RF family.

Originality
The key contribution—integrating attribute error tendencies into ZSL training via node-specific ROC-aware fractional propagation—is novel and non-trivial. Prior ZSL methods typically assume reliable attributes or only use probabilistic outputs at test time. While related to decision trees with uncertain features, the adaptation to the signature-vs.-data dichotomy in ZSL and the combined signature/data IG in few-shot training are original.

Significance
Addressing attribute unreliability is central to making ZSL practical. Gains over DAP across three datasets and stronger improvements when attributes are less reliable (aPY, SUN) indicate practical impact. The framework is applicable beyond named attributes (e.g., discovered pseudo-attributes [30]) and potentially to other semantic mid-levels (poselets, mid-level patches). The methodological idea (error-aware splitting via validation-derived ROCs) could influence other weakly supervised or transfer settings.

Strengths
- Clear, principled treatment of attribute unreliability directly in the training objective.
- Node-specific ROC estimation captures conditional dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Solid empirical evaluation with controlled noise studies, real datasets, and ablations.
- Natural few-shot extension with a signature prior that remains helpful even with 200 images.
- Applicable to both named and discovered attributes.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on the quality/representativeness of attribute validation data; limited analysis of domain shift between seen and unseen classes.
- Potential data sparsity for ROC estimation at deeper nodes; fallback is heuristic.
- Scalability and runtime not reported; one-vs-all forests may be heavy for large K.
- Limited comparisons to alternative ZSL paradigms that handle uncertainty differently (e.g., label/semantic embeddings [1,4,14], calibrated stacking or cost-sensitive approaches).
- The treatment of inter-attribute correlations is implicit; oblique or multivariate splits might help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide runtime/complexity analysis and scaling behavior with M, K, tree depth, and (#m,#t) samples.
- Replace the ROC fallback with Bayesian smoothing (e.g., Beta-Binomial posteriors), or hierarchical shrinkage from parent nodes to improve stability with few validation samples per node.
- Explore oblique/multivariate splits or group tests to cope with correlated attributes, as noted in the conclusion.
- Expand comparisons to representative embedding-based ZSL (e.g., [1,4,14]) on common splits to contextualize gains.
- Analyze sensitivity to attribute calibration (Platt scaling) and to misspecified signatures; quantify the contribution of the signature-uncertainty component across datasets.
- Report few-shot baselines in more detail and add strong baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with the same features).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a critical and under-explored limitation of attribute-based ZSL with a principled method.
- Demonstrates consistent, meaningful improvements across datasets with careful ablations.
- Methodological idea is general and likely to influence follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical comparisons to broader ZSL baselines could be expanded.
- Limited analysis of robustness to domain shift in attribute errors; reliance on seen-class validation.
- Missing computational analysis and scalability discussion.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically solid contribution with clear empirical benefits and good potential impact on zero-/few-shot learning.
