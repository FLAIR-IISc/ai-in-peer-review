This paper addresses the critical problem of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The authors propose a random forest approach that explicitly models attribute prediction errors during training, leading to more robust discriminative models for unseen classes. The method extends to few-shot learning and unreliable class-attribute associations. The experimental results on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, including the widely used DAP approach.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea – acknowledging and mitigating the impact of unreliable attributes – is a crucial step forward for ZSL. Existing ZSL methods (e.g., [8, 3, 18, 23]) largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, a simplification that severely limits their practical applicability. The paper correctly identifies this as a key bottleneck. Related work in attribute discovery ([11, 22, 26, 30]) attempts to circumvent the problem by learning more robust mid-level features, but these often lack the semantic interpretability necessary for human-defined classes.  This work cleverly operates *within* the attribute framework, improving its reliability rather than abandoning it. The connection to techniques handling missing or uncertain data in decision trees ([21, 28, 16]) is accurately highlighted, while also clearly differentiating the unique challenges posed by the ZSL setting. The paper builds logically on prior work, identifying a core limitation and offering a pragmatic solution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Importance:** The explicit modeling of attribute unreliability is a significant contribution. It addresses a fundamental limitation of existing ZSL methods and moves the field closer to practical application.
* **Technical Soundness:** The random forest framework is well-motivated and the proposed modifications (fractional propagation, ROC-based splitting) are logically sound. The incorporation of validation data for error estimation is a clever and effective technique. The extension to few-shot learning is a natural and valuable addition.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The gains over DAP and SIGNATURE-RF are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The controlled noise experiments effectively validate the method's robustness to attribute prediction errors.  The performance on discovered attributes is also a strong result.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The use of equations is appropriate and helps to formalize the approach.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors provide comprehensive experimental results, including ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of the method. The comparisons to existing baselines are fair and thorough.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The use of ROC curves and validation data introduces additional computational overhead. While not explicitly discussed, the scalability of the method to very large attribute vocabularies and datasets could be a concern.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method likely depends on the choice of parameters such as the tree depth, the number of trees, and the weighting factor λ in the few-shot extension.  A more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Validation Data Assumption:** The method relies on having access to a separate validation dataset for estimating attribute error rates. This dataset may not always be available in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, exploring alternative methods for estimating attribute reliability (e.g., using confidence scores from the attribute classifiers) would be a valuable addition.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the experiments demonstrate improved performance, a deeper error analysis would be helpful. Identifying *which* types of attribute errors the method is most effective at mitigating would provide further insight into its strengths and weaknesses.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation of existing ZSL methods.
* Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Compelling experimental results on multiple datasets.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Significant potential impact on the field of ZSL.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity might be a concern for large-scale applications.
* Parameter sensitivity needs further investigation.
* Reliance on validation data could limit applicability in some scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.  The paper is likely to be highly cited and will inspire further research in this important area.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

* Discuss the computational complexity of the method and potential strategies for improving scalability.
* Perform a more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity.
* Explore alternative methods for estimating attribute reliability that do not require a separate validation dataset.
* Include an error analysis to identify the types of attribute errors that the method is most effective at mitigating.
* Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations and future research directions.
