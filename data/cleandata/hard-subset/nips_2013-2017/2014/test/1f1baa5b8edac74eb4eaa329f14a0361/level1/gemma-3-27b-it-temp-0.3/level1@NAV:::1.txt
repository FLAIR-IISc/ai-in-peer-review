## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses the critical issue of attribute prediction unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The authors argue, correctly, that standard ZSL methods often fail due to noisy attribute predictions, and propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly models and accounts for these inaccuracies. The core idea – leveraging ROC statistics of attribute classifiers to guide tree splitting – is a clever and well-motivated approach to building more robust ZSL models. The paper is well-structured, clearly articulating the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. It appropriately situates the work within the existing ZSL literature, referencing key papers like [8, 18, 23] and highlighting the limitations of existing methods. 

The strengths of this paper are significant. The technical contribution is sound, and the proposed method demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines (DAP and a standard signature RF) across three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN). The controlled experiments with synthetic noise effectively demonstrate the method’s ability to handle unreliable attributes. The extension to the few-shot setting is a valuable addition, showcasing the versatility of the approach. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their method, and the ablation studies provide insights into the contribution of different components.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the explanation of the ROC-based splitting is clear, the mathematical formulation could benefit from further clarification, particularly regarding the handling of signature uncertainty. The choice of hyperparameters (tree depth, number of trees, λ) feels somewhat arbitrary, and a more systematic exploration of these parameters would strengthen the results. The discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more critical analysis of the limitations of existing approaches beyond simply stating they don't account for attribute unreliability. For example, a discussion of why existing attribute learning methods struggle with reliability would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a critical problem in ZSL.
*   Consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines.
*   Well-designed experiments and ablation studies.
*   Clear writing and good organization.
*   Extension to the few-shot setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Mathematical formulation could be clearer.
*   Hyperparameter selection feels somewhat arbitrary.
*   Related work discussion could be more critical.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
