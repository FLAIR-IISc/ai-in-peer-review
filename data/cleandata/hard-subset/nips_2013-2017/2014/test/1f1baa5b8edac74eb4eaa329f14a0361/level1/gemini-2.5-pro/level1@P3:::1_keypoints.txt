- Problem: Addresses attribute-based zero-shot learning's unrealistic assumption of reliable attribute classifiers and the resulting performance gap.  
- Core contribution: Proposes a zero-shot random forest that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability during training via fractional propagation of class signatures.  
- Mechanism: Uses ROC statistics estimated on a validation set to fractionally route signatures and modifies information gain to prefer splits robust to classifier errors.  
- Extensions: Handles uncertainty in class-attribute signatures and adapts to few-shot learning by using signatures as priors.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on attribute-based ZSL (e.g., DAP) but uniquely integrates classifier error modeling into the learning process.  
- Technical novelty: Fractional propagation and recursive, node-specific ROC estimation are principled and well-motivated adaptations of decision-tree ideas to ZSL.  
- Empirical evidence: Controlled noise experiments and benchmarks (AwA, aPY, SUN) show substantial gains over strong DAP baselines.  
- Ablation: Thorough ablation study isolates benefits of signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation.  
- Writing and presentation: Paper is very clear, well-organized, and uses effective motivating examples.  
- Validation-set dependence: Method assumes ROC statistics from seen-class validation data generalize to unseen classes, which may fail under domain shift.  
- Scalability concern: One-vs-all random forest training per unseen class may not scale to large numbers of unseen classes.  
- Attribute correlation limitation: Splits remain univariate, which may be suboptimal when attributes are strongly correlated despite recursive conditioning.  
- Suggestions: More discussion/analysis of DV representativeness, strategies for selecting relevant validation subsets, and scalable multi-label alternatives would strengthen the work.  
- Overall verdict: Significant, technically sound, well-evaluated contribution that meaningfully advances practical ZSL—recommended for acceptance.
