This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors develop a random forest algorithm that leverages statistics about each attribute's error tendencies to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. This approach is particularly relevant in scenarios where attribute predictions are hard to get right, and the standard zero-shot paradigm falls short.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the limitations of existing zero-shot methods. The proposed approach is thoroughly described, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness on three large datasets. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the benefits of their approach over existing methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to handle unreliable attribute predictions. The authors propose a novel way to propagate training instances through the tree, taking into account the error tendencies of each attribute classifier. This approach allows the model to learn more robust discriminative models for the unseen classes.

The paper also explores the extension of the approach to the few-shot setting, where a small number of category-labeled images are available for training. The authors demonstrate that their approach can be adapted to this setting by using the attribute signatures as a prior for selecting good tree splits.

The experimental results are thorough and well-presented. The authors compare their approach to several state-of-the-art methods, including the Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method, and demonstrate its superiority on three challenging datasets. The results are also analyzed in detail, highlighting the benefits of the proposed approach.

One potential weakness of the paper is the assumption that the attribute vocabulary is expressive enough to discriminate between the categories. While the authors acknowledge this assumption, it is not entirely clear how the approach would perform if the attribute vocabulary is not sufficient.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough overview of existing zero-shot methods and highlight the differences between their approach and others. However, it would be beneficial to see a more detailed comparison with other methods that also account for attribute unreliability.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed approach is novel and effective. The experimental results are thorough, and the analysis is detailed. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning and is likely to be of interest to researchers in the field.
