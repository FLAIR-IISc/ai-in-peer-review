

# Peer Review: Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. While zero-shot learning theoretically allows training classifiers via semantic attributes (e.g., "zebra" = "striped, black, white"), existing methods assume perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic in practice. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability by leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies. Their method trains zero-shot models by selecting discriminative decision nodes based on attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristics. The paper further extends the approach to handle few-shot learning and unreliable attribute descriptions.

The work builds upon standard zero-shot learning approaches like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8] and attribute-based classification [3, 18, 24, 19], but identifies a key limitation in the field that has hindered practical application. While related to work on handling missing values in decision trees [21], the paper's zero-shot setting and application to attribute-based recognition represent a novel contribution. The authors demonstrate their method on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) with comprehensive experiments, including controlled noise experiments and real-world attribute predictions.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical justification. The authors provide a rigorous formulation of how to incorporate attribute classifier error statistics into the random forest training process. Their ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different components (e.g., signature uncertainty modeling, ROC propagation). The experiments are thorough, with multiple datasets, controlled noise experiments, and comparison to strong baselines including DAP, the current standard in zero-shot learning. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the need for validation data to estimate ROC characteristics.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The method is clearly explained with appropriate mathematical formulations and intuitive examples. The authors provide helpful visualizations (Figure 1) and detailed experimental results (Table 1). The distinction between attribute unreliability (the focus of this paper) and attribute strength (previously studied) is clearly articulated.

*Originality:* The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning that has been overlooked in prior work. The core idea of incorporating attribute classifier error statistics into the training process is novel and well-motivated. While related to work on handling missing values [21], the application to zero-shot learning is distinct and valuable. The few-shot extension further enhances the practical utility of the approach.

*Significance:* The results demonstrate consistent, substantial improvements over DAP (the current standard baseline) across all three datasets, with gains as large as 10.2% on specific categories. The method outperforms DAP even when using only 80% of the attribute-labeled images, demonstrating that modeling attribute unreliability is more important than having more attribute classifiers. The few-shot extension further broadens the applicability of the approach. This work makes a compelling case for the practical viability of attribute-based zero-shot learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The paper could provide more detail on the practical implementation of the ROC propagation. While the authors mention using a limited number of (m,t) combinations per node, the specific selection criteria for these combinations could be better explained. The paper also doesn't thoroughly address how the method might scale to very large numbers of attributes or classes.

*Clarity:* While the paper is generally clear, the explanation of how the validation data is recursively propagated through the tree could benefit from a more detailed example. The connection between the ROC characteristics and the information gain calculation could also be made more explicit.

*Significance:* The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, but could better articulate the broader implications beyond visual recognition. The authors briefly mention applications to poselets for action recognition and discriminative mid-level patches, but this could be expanded to show the wider applicability of the approach.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical limitation that has hindered practical application of existing methods. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are comprehensive, and the results consistently demonstrate substantial improvements over strong baselines. The paper is well-written and makes a compelling case for the value of attributes in low-cost visual category learning.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning that has been overlooked
- Demonstrates consistent, substantial improvements over DAP (the standard baseline)
- Provides a theoretically grounded method for incorporating attribute unreliability
- Includes thorough ablation studies and controlled noise experiments
- Extends naturally to the few-shot setting with practical benefits

*Con:*
- Limited discussion of scalability to very large attribute sets
- Implementation details of the (m,t) selection could be more specific
- Broader implications beyond visual recognition could be expanded

**Specific Suggestions:**
1. Add a brief discussion of how the method would scale to larger attribute sets (e.g., 1000+ attributes).
2. Provide more detail on the selection criteria for the (m,t) combinations during tree construction.
3. Expand on the potential applications of the approach to other domains beyond visual recognition.
4. Consider adding a sentence to clarify why the method is not vulnerable to the same issues as the "attribute strength" approach (e.g., [19]).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical practical limitation. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing approaches.
