- Tackles zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling unreliable attribute predictions rather than treating them as ground truth.
- Core method: train random forests using ROC-curve-informed splitting criteria to account for attribute classifier errors.
- Extensions include handling few-shot learning and unreliable class-attribute associations.
- Novelty: simultaneous propagation of attribute signatures and data uncertainty is distinct from prior ZSL and decision-tree work.
- Empirical strength: consistently outperforms baselines (including DAP) across three datasets with convincing controlled noise experiments.
- Thorough experimental evaluation with ablations and results on discovered attributes strengthens the empirical claims.
- Clear, well-written exposition with convincing theoretical justification for the ROC-based splitting idea.
- Significance: improves robustness of ZSL to attribute noise, advancing practical applicability.
- Computational cost and scalability concerns: training modified random forests may be expensive for many attributes/classes.
- Requires a validation set to estimate attribute error rates; sensitivity to validation size is not fully explored.
- Lacks in-depth analysis of failure cases and identification of problematic attributes or classes.
- Parameter sensitivity (tree depth, λ for few-shot weighting) needs more systematic study beyond cross-validation details.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental ZSL limitation, presents a novel effective approach, and is thoroughly evaluated and well-written.
- Arguments against acceptance: potential scalability/computational limits, dependency on validation data, and limited analysis of failures and parameter sensitivity.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — valuable and significant contribution to ZSL literature.
