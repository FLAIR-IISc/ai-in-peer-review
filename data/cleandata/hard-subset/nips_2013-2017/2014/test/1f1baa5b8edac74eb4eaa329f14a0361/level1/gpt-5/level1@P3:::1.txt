Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a key failure mode of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): test-time attribute predictions are noisy and biased, yet most methods treat them as reliable. The authors propose a random forest (RF) approach that trains directly on class attribute signatures and explicitly models attribute unreliability using ROC statistics estimated on held-out attribute-labeled validation data. The core idea is to fractionally propagate class signatures down the tree according to attribute TP/FP rates at candidate thresholds, yielding splits that are both discriminative and “learnable” by the attribute detectors. The method also includes (i) a mechanism for signature uncertainty (class-attribute associations that may be noisy) and (ii) a few-shot extension that combines signature-driven information gain with data-driven information gain using a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP/L2-compatibles and a signature-only RF.

Relative to standard two-stage ZSL methods (DAP [8], attribute-similarity matching [3,18,30]), the advance is to account for attribute unreliability during training, not just at test-time via confidence. Compared to alternatives that eschew attributes for discovered mid-levels [11,22,26,27,30] or semantic embeddings (label/feature embeddings [1,4], DeViSE [4]), the approach preserves the human-interpretable attribute interface while improving robustness. The fractional routing is related to fuzzy/uncertain decision trees [16,21,28], but repurposed to the ZSL setting with training on signatures and node-conditional ROC estimates to capture inter-attribute dependencies induced by earlier splits.

Quality
- Methodology: The fractional propagation guided by node-specific ROC estimates is well-motivated and technically sound. The information gain objective appropriately reflects uncertainty. The few-shot extension is natural, interpreting signatures as a prior and images as data via a convex combination of gains.
- Correctness/analysis: The arguments for why unlearnable attributes yield zero information gain are convincing; the dependence on node-conditional DV(n) is an important detail to avoid inconsistent TP/FP estimates. However, the signature uncertainty model is deferred to the supplement; its assumptions (e.g., independent flip noise? per-attribute priors?) should be summarized in the main text to assess its reasonableness.
- Experiments: Strong empirical evidence on three datasets, with controlled noise studies, ablations (no ROC propagation, no signature uncertainty), and comparisons to DAP and prior published numbers. Reporting mean and SE over 20 trials is appropriate. The “upper-bound” experiment with true ROC from unseen classes is informative.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The training/test distinction, one-vs-all forests, and the role of DV are clearly explained. Some points could be clarified:
- Few-shot section: clarify whether IGbasic on DT uses attribute predictions â(x) as features (seems so) or raw image descriptors. The reference to Sec. 3.2.1 (which uses signatures) may confuse readers.
- Complexity: add training/testing time and memory costs, especially since a one-vs-all forest per unseen class may raise scalability concerns when K is large.
- Signature uncertainty: include the main formula and assumptions in the paper body.

Originality
The combination of (i) learning trees from class signatures, (ii) fractional propagation based on node-conditional ROC from attribute validation images, and (iii) integrating signature/data information gains for few-shot is novel in the ZSL context. While related to fuzzy/uncertain trees, the adaptation to ZSL with explicit modeling of attribute detector behavior and node-conditional conditioning is a clear contribution.

Significance
Addressing attribute unreliability is central to making attribute-based ZSL practical. The empirical gains are substantial, particularly on datasets where attributes are harder to learn (aPY, SUN). The approach maintains the interpretability and human-in-the-loop convenience of attribute-based ZSL while improving robustness, and the idea should transfer to other mid-levels (poselets, parts) and beyond vision. Limitations (see below) temper, but do not negate, impact.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated modeling of attribute unreliability via ROC-driven fractional routing.
- Practical training procedure needing only attribute-labeled validation data, which is standard in attribute ZSL.
- Strong empirical results with ablations and controlled noise studies.
- Natural and effective few-shot extension that treats signatures as priors.
- Retains semantic transparency of attributes; supports both named and discovered attributes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on seen-class validation for ROC: If unseen classes exhibit different attribute statistics (domain shift), DV-derived ROC may be biased. Consider importance weighting or domain adaptation (e.g., condition ROC on seen classes most similar in signature to the unseen, with Bayesian smoothing rather than hard fallback to global DV).
- Scalability: One RF per unseen class may be costly for large K or frequent on-the-fly category addition. A single multiclass forest or multi-label variant would improve scalability; the authors mention this as future work.
- Limited split family: Univariate tests may not capture inter-attribute correlations beyond those induced by the path. Consider oblique splits or pairwise tests that can exploit correlated attributes while still using ROC-based uncertainty.
- Calibration: The method relies on Platt-calibrated probabilities and chosen thresholds. Investigate calibration robustness (e.g., temperature scaling) and sensitivity analyses for threshold sampling (#t).
- Missing baselines: While DAP is a strong attribute baseline, comparisons to label-embedding methods (e.g., ALE [Akata et al., CVPR’13]) or semantic-embedding approaches (DeViSE [Frome et al., NIPS’13]) on AwA/aPY would strengthen the case.
- Details deferred to supplement: Bring the signature uncertainty model into the main text and provide more implementation details (e.g., minimum DV(n) size, smoothing of ROC estimates).

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the size and composition of DV? Does stratifying DV by seen-class similarity to unseen signatures help?
- In few-shot, do you use â(x) or raw features to compute IGbasic on DT? If the former, is there a risk of compounding attribute errors when enough images are available?
- Could you report training/inference times and memory footprint for K=10 vs. larger K?

Pros vs. cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a core practical limitation of attribute ZSL with a principled, effective method.
- Strong empirical gains and thorough ablations.
- Maintains semantic interpretability; elegant few-shot extension.

Cons:
- Scalability concerns with one-vs-all forests.
- Potential bias from DV to unseen-class attribute distributions.
- Missing comparisons to contemporary semantic embedding baselines; some details in supplement.

Recommendation: Weak accept. This is a solid, well-executed contribution likely to influence practical ZSL with attributes. Confidence: High.
