- Proposes a novel zero-shot learning method: a random forest that accounts for attribute prediction unreliability using per-attribute error statistics.  
- Well-motivated paper with a clear overview of limitations in existing zero-shot methods.  
- Method explicitly models attribute classifier error tendencies to select discriminative and predictable tree splits.  
- Novel training-instance propagation through trees that accounts for attribute error tendencies, yielding more robust models for unseen classes.  
- Extends naturally to few-shot learning by using attribute signatures as priors for selecting tree splits.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates superiority over several state-of-the-art methods (including DAP) on three large datasets.  
- Detailed result analysis highlights benefits of the approach and supports the claims.  
- Technically sound with supporting theoretical analysis and careful evaluation of strengths and weaknesses.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-presented, and accessible.  
- Original contribution: explicitly modeling attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning is novel and effective.  
- Significant contribution likely to interest the zero-shot learning community.  
- Weakness: assumes attribute vocabulary is expressive enough to discriminate categories; unclear performance when it is not.  
- Related work coverage is thorough but lacks detailed comparison to other methods that also handle attribute unreliability.  
- Suggestions: analyze performance when attribute vocabulary is insufficient, add comparisons to other unreliability-aware methods, and explore applicability beyond computer vision.
