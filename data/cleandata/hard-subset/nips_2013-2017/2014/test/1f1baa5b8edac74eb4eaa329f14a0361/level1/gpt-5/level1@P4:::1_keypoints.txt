- Proposes a random-forest method that trains directly on unseen-class attribute signatures while accounting for attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC statistics.  
- Splits use information gain computed by fractionally propagating class signatures according to attribute TP/FP rates, favoring discriminative and realizable splits.  
- Models class-signature uncertainty and extends to few-shot by combining signature-based and data-based information gain with weight λ.  
- Novelty: incorporates per-attribute reliability into unseen-class classifier training rather than trusting test-time attribute scores.  
- Related to fuzzy/fractional decision trees but distinct because it trains on signatures, uses node-wise ROC propagation, and targets zero/few-shot settings.  
- Empirical validation: controlled-noise studies, three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), ablations, ROC upper-bound, and few-shot λ analysis.  
- Demonstrates consistent gains over DAP and signature-only RF baseline; competitive with methods using discovered attributes.  
- Uses same attribute-labeled data as baselines and shows modeling error tendencies can outweigh extra attribute data.  
- Conceptually general and can leverage discovered pseudo-attributes.  
- Depends on attribute-labeled validation data and assumes TP/FP rates transfer to unseen classes; domain gap concern highlighted by ROC upper-bound.  
- Scalability concerns: one-vs-all forests per unseen class and node-wise ROC estimation may be costly as unseen classes and attributes grow.  
- Limited head-to-head comparisons with contemporary embedding-based ZSL methods (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) under identical settings.  
- Uses univariate splits only; attribute correlations are handled indirectly, not via joint or oblique tests.  
- Calibration sensitivity: relies on Platt probabilities, threshold sampling, and hyperparameters (#m, #t, depth, λ) that are only partially explored.  
- Clarity issues: workflow for training attribute SVMs vs ROC estimation (held-out then retrain) needs explicit description to avoid leakage/mismatch concerns.  
- Methodologically sound with clear fractional propagation/IG formulation and supportive ablations; no theoretical guarantees but convincing empirical evidence.  
- Presentation mostly clear but would benefit from an algorithm box and clearer description of attribute training/ROC pipeline.  
- Originality: novel ROC-aware fractional propagation applied to signature-based ZSL and a clean few-shot extension.  
- Significance: tackles a central practical ZSL obstacle and is likely influential, though practical impact depends on availability/transferability of validation data and scalability.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to strong embedding-based baselines, analyze sensitivity to DV size/domain and calibration, report computational cost, explore multi-attribute/oblique splits, and broaden discovered-attribute evaluation.  
- Open questions: sensitivity to ROC/classifier mismatches, runtime/memory scaling with K and M, and performance when DV is small or biased.  
- Arguments for acceptance: well-motivated, clear method with strong empirical gains and extensibility to few-shot.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited comparisons to non-attribute methods, reliance on transferable ROC estimates, and incomplete scalability/complexity characterization.
