- Paper addresses noisy attribute predictors in zero-shot learning by training random forests on class-attribute signatures while modeling attribute unreliability via per-attribute ROC stats.
- During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated using node/threshold-specific TP/FP rates and information gain is adjusted for these fractional counts.
- Method is extended to handle uncertain class signatures and few-shot learning by combining signature-driven and data-driven gains with a tunable prior λ.
- Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, robustness to controlled noise, informative ablations, and competitive results with discovered attributes.
- Positions clearly against two-stage attribute ZSL methods and complementary embedding/hierarchical approaches, keeping attribute semantics while mitigating unreliability.
- Fractional propagation relates to fuzzy trees/missing-feature work but is novel in applying ROC-driven propagation for ZSL with signature training and predicted-attribute testing.
- Strength: conceptually novel and clear integration of attribute ROC into tree-building to turn ideal signatures into realistic test-time distributions.
- Strength: practical approach using standard attribute SVMs, lightweight validation calibration, support for named and discovered attributes, and natural few-shot extension.
- Strength: technically sound objective (modified information gain) with node-specific ROC estimation to capture inter-attribute dependencies.
- Strength: strong empirical validation across datasets, controlled-noise tests, ablations, and sensible few-shot λ behavior.
- Strength: maintains interpretability by preserving attribute semantics while improving robustness.
- Weakness: relies on attribute-labeled validation data and assumes ROC transferability from seen to unseen classes; TRUE ROC upper bound shows residual domain shift.
- Weakness: limited comparisons to non-attribute ZSL baselines (e.g., DeViSE/label-embeddings) in main tables across datasets.
- Weakness: some implementation and modeling details (signature uncertainty, pseudocode, complexity, sampling effects) are deferred to the supplement.
- Weakness: potential calibration bias from estimating ROC on SVMs trained on 80% then using SVMs trained on 100%, and no analysis of this drift.
- Weakness: scalability concerns—one-vs-all forests per class may not scale to large K, and runtime/error bars per class are not reported.
- Quality: method is coherent and experimentally supported; main remaining concern is ROC transferability and sensitivity to validation set size/composition.
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized, but needs clearer explanation of SVM/ROC timing and a brief complexity/sensitivity discussion in the main text.
- Originality: high — novel application of ROC-driven fractional propagation to attribute-based ZSL and principled few-shot prior.
- Significance: solid — addresses a key ZSL bottleneck, is simple to adopt, and broadens impact via few-shot and discovered-attribute applicability.
- Suggested analyses: sensitivity to validation set size/composition, comparisons to embedding baselines on same splits, runtime/scalability discussion, clarification of SVM/ROC pipeline, and exploring per-attribute calibration/domain adaptation.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled solution to a real ZSL problem with consistent empirical gains, interpretability, and extensibility.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on ROC transfer, incomplete baseline comparisons, missing main-text details on complexity and sensitivity.
- Recommendation: weak accept to accept — novel, clear, and empirically persuasive with practical impact despite some analysis gaps.
