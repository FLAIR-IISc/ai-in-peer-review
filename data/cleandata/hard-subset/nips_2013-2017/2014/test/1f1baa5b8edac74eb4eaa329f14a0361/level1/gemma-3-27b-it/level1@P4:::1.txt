This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning, acknowledging the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest approach that explicitly models attribute error tendencies during training, improving the robustness of zero-shot classifiers. They extend this to few-shot scenarios and unreliable class-attribute associations. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a novel approach with promising experimental results.

The core idea of incorporating attribute reliability into the learning process is a strong contribution. Existing zero-shot learning methods largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is unrealistic. The proposed random forest approach, utilizing ROC curves to guide splitting, offers a principled way to mitigate this issue. The extensions to few-shot learning and handling unreliable class-attribute associations further broaden the applicability of the method. The experiments on three datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over existing baselines, including the widely used DAP method, and ablation studies confirm the effectiveness of the proposed components.

However, there are some aspects that could be strengthened. While the authors provide a good overview of related work, a more detailed discussion of existing methods for handling noisy data within decision trees (beyond the brief mention of fractional tuples) would be valuable. Specifically, exploring the differences and advantages of their approach compared to techniques like soft decision trees or probabilistic decision trees would strengthen the novelty argument. The ROC propagation mechanism, while effective, adds complexity. A more intuitive explanation of why this specific approach to modelling uncertainty works better than alternative approaches (e.g., directly weighting predictions by confidence scores) could enhance understanding. 

The experimental setup, while thorough, relies on pre-defined features. Evaluating the performance with end-to-end learned features (e.g., from a CNN) would demonstrate the generalizability of the method and its applicability to modern visual recognition pipelines. Additionally, the impact of the validation data size on performance should be investigated further. While 20% is used, exploring different allocations could provide insights into the trade-off between training the attribute classifiers and estimating their reliability. The parameter sensitivity analysis for λ in the few-shot extension is good but could be expanded, potentially exploring automatic adaptation strategies for λ.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of explicitly modelling attribute unreliability within a random forest framework is novel and addresses a significant limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The explanation of the approach is detailed and the experimental setup is clearly described.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important – enabling learning with limited or no labeled data is crucial for many real-world applications. The demonstrated improvements suggest that this work can contribute to more practical zero-shot learning systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The ROC propagation mechanism adds complexity to the approach, and a more intuitive explanation of its benefits would be helpful.
*   **Feature Reliance:** The evaluation relies on pre-defined features, potentially limiting the generalizability of the findings to end-to-end learned representations.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons to existing methods are presented, a deeper discussion of related techniques for handling noisy data in decision trees would strengthen the novelty argument.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and well-supported by experimental results. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
