- Problem: attribute-based zero-shot learning degrades when noisy attribute predictors are treated as ground truth at test time.
- Proposal: train random forests on per-class attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability.
- Mechanism: use per-attribute ROC statistics from held-out attribute-labeled data to fractionally propagate class signatures through tree splits.
- Criterion: compute information gain on soft (fractional) counts to favor splits that are both discriminative and robust to expected attribute errors.
- Extensions: handle uncertainty in class–attribute associations and incorporate few-shot learning by combining signature- and example-based gains with a tunable prior.
- Experiments: evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN with >250 attributes, showing consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline.
- Ablations: ROC-guided propagation and signature uncertainty ablation studies isolate their benefits; controlled-noise studies included.
- Discovered attributes: method remains competitive when using discovered attributes, relating to embedding and knowledge-transfer literature.
- Quality: technical contribution is sound and well-motivated; fractional propagation per-node ROC estimates are principled.
- Clarity: generally clear, but key details (pseudocode, signature-noise model) are relegated to the supplement and should be moved into the main text.
- Missing detail: clarify fallback behavior when per-node positives/negatives are scarce.
- Analysis gaps: deeper study of computational complexity and sensitivity to validation set size/shift is needed.
- Originality: novel integration of attribute unreliability into tree-building for zero-shot training, despite connections to prior work on uncertain/missing data.
- Cons: reliance on held-out attribute-labeled validation data with potential domain shift to unseen classes.
- Cons: limited comparison to recent embedding-based zero-shot methods on identical splits.
- Cons: scalability concerns from one-vs-all forests and per-node ROC estimation; inter-attribute dependencies largely unmodeled beyond per-node conditioning.
