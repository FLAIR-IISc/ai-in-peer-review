Summary
This paper tackles a central weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictors are noisy, and existing methods treat their outputs as if reliable. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains directly on class-attribute signatures but explicitly models attribute unreliability using per-attribute ROC statistics measured on attribute-labeled validation images. During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated down both branches according to the estimated TP/FP rates at each node and threshold, and the information gain is adjusted to account for these fractional counts. The forest thus prefers splits that are both discriminative across signatures and learnable given the actual attribute error profiles. The method is extended to: (i) uncertain class signatures (annotation noise), and (ii) few-shot learning by combining signature-driven information gain with data-driven gain via a tunable prior weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, robustness under controlled noise, ablations clarifying the role of ROC propagation and signature uncertainty, and competitive results when using discovered “pseudo-attributes.”

Relation to prior work
The paper clearly positions itself against two-stage attribute ZSL methods (e.g., DAP, similarity-to-signature methods, topic models), and complementary zero-shot strategies using label/feature embeddings or hierarchical classifier transfer (DeViSE, label-embeddings, ImageNet hierarchy). Unlike discovery-based mid-level features that sacrifice semantics, this method keeps semantic control while mitigating unreliability. The fractional propagation idea is related to fuzzy trees and handling missing/uncertain features, but the application to ZSL with signature training and predicted-attribute testing is new and well-motivated.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and novelty: neat integration of attribute ROC into tree-building, turning ideal signatures into realistic distributions that reflect test-time errors.
- Practicality: uses standard attribute SVMs; adds a lightweight validation-based calibration; works with both named and discovered attributes; natural few-shot extension.
- Technical soundness: well-defined objective (modified information gain), careful node-specific ROC estimation to capture inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Empirical validation: consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-RF baseline across three datasets; controlled noise experiments; informative ablations; few-shot curves showing sensible behavior of λ.
- Interpretability: retains attribute semantics while improving robustness.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data and the assumption that ROC measured on seen classes transfers to unseen ones; the “TRUE ROC” upper bound highlights remaining domain shift.
- Limited comparison to non-attribute ZSL baselines (e.g., DeViSE/label-embeddings) on shared splits/datasets in the main tables; results are mentioned for AwA but details are mainly in supp.
- Some implementation details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., signature uncertainty model, pseudocode, complexity), and the effect of the (#m, #t) sampling choices is not analyzed.
- Potential mismatch between ROC-estimating attribute SVMs (trained on 80%) and final attribute SVMs (trained on 100%); while fair across methods, this could bias calibration.
- Scalability and calibration: forests per class (one-vs-all) may become heavy for large K; no error bars on per-class performance or runtime/scale analysis.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The fractional propagation and entropy-based objective are coherent, and the node-specific ROC estimation properly models attribute thresholding and inter-node dependencies. The ablations (removing signature uncertainty, removing node-specific ROC) are convincing. The experiments cover three datasets and both zero- and few-shot settings. A minor concern is the ROC transferability to unseen classes; the paper acknowledges this and even provides an upper bound, which is commendable. More analysis of sensitivity to the validation set size/distribution would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The training/testing distinction (signatures vs. predicted attributes) is explicit; equations are readable. Some important details are in the supplement (signature uncertainty term; pseudocode; dataset split for SUN). It would help to more clearly explain the timing of SVM training vs. ROC estimation to remove ambiguity, and to provide a brief complexity/sensitivity discussion in the main text.

Originality
High. While related ideas exist for trees with uncertain/missing features, applying ROC-driven fractional propagation in the zero-shot setting with class signatures as “training data,” and combining this with a principled few-shot prior, is novel. The paper also differentiates “unreliability” from “attribute strength,” which is an important conceptual point relative to prior probabilistic/relative attribute work.

Significance
Solid. Attribute-based ZSL is a popular paradigm, and unreliability is a key blocker. Showing consistent improvements over DAP and robustness to noise across multiple datasets is valuable. The few-shot extension and applicability to discovered attributes broaden impact. The method is simple enough to be adopted and could influence subsequent ZSL approaches that combine semantics with calibrated predictability.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive are results to the amount and composition of DV? Any results varying DV size or focusing DV on seen classes with signatures closest to unseen classes?
- Can the authors report comparisons to DeViSE/label-embedding baselines on the same splits (especially SUN/aPY), even if those methods do not use attributes?
- Provide a brief runtime and scalability discussion (trees per class, memory, inference time) and how performance scales with K and M.
- Clarify the pipeline: are ROC statistics estimated using attribute SVMs trained on 80% and then final SVMs retrained on 100% for test-time? If so, any observed calibration drift?
- Could per-attribute calibration (e.g., Platt/Isotonic) or domain adaptation of ROC improve transfer to unseen classes?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real pain point in attribute-based ZSL with a principled, effective method.
- Strong empirical gains on multiple datasets, with ablations and controlled-noise studies.
- Method is simple, interpretable, and extensible (few-shot, discovered attributes).

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on seen-class ROC transfer; limited analysis of when ROC transfer fails.
- Comparisons to non-attribute ZSL baselines are not uniformly presented across datasets.
- Some crucial details in the supplement; limited discussion of complexity and sensitivity.

Recommendation: Weak accept to accept. The paper is novel, clear, and empirically persuasive on an important ZSL problem, with practical impact and a sound technical core.
