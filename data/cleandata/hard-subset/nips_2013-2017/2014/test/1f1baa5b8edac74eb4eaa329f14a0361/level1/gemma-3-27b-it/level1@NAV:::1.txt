## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper tackles the critical issue of attribute prediction unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The authors correctly identify that the performance of ZSL hinges on accurate attribute predictions, which are often noisy in practice. They propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly models this unreliability during training by leveraging receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers. The method is extended to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and a few-shot setting.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the established ZSL paradigm [8, 18] and highlighting its limitations. The proposed approach is a logical extension, addressing a core challenge that has hindered wider adoption of ZSL.  The use of ROC curves to inform the splitting criteria within the random forest is a clever idea, allowing the model to prioritize more reliable attributes. The extensions to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning further enhance the method’s practical applicability.  The related work section is comprehensive, referencing key publications in ZSL and related areas like attribute discovery [11, 22] and handling missing data [21, 28]. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness, the novelty of the approach, and its demonstrated performance improvements over existing methods (DAP [8, 9]) on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The experiments, including controlled noise studies, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. The extension to few-shot learning is a valuable addition. The clarity of the writing is good, although the technical details of the random forest algorithm could benefit from a more illustrative example.

However, the paper could be improved by providing a more detailed ablation study dissecting the contributions of signature uncertainty modeling and ROC propagation.  The impact of different validation data sizes on performance could also be investigated. While the authors acknowledge the limitations regarding inter-attribute correlations, addressing this aspect could be a direction for future work.  Additionally, the discussion of the parameter λ in the few-shot extension could be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a key problem in ZSL.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
*   Well-motivated and comprehensive related work.
*   Clear writing and presentation of results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   More detailed ablation study could strengthen the analysis.
*   Further investigation of validation data size and inter-attribute correlations could be beneficial.



**Overall Score:** 8/10 – A strong paper with significant contributions to the field of zero-shot learning. I recommend acceptance.
