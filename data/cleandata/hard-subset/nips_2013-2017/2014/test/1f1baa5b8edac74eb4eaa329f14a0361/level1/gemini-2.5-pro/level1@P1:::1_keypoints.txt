- Problem: Zero-shot learning (ZSL) fails when mid-level attribute classifiers are noisy and biased, as in standard DAP approaches.
- Contribution: Proposes a zero-shot random forest trained on class attribute signatures that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability.
- Mechanism: Introduces fractional propagation during tree construction, distributing signatures to children by expected TPR/FPR estimated from a held-out validation set.
- Extensions: Models uncertainty in class signatures and adapts to few-shot learning by using signatures as a descriptive prior.
- Experimental intuition: Controlled noise experiments demonstrate robustness and the method’s ability to prefer more learnable attributes.
- Empirical results: Significant and consistent gains over DAP on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets.
- Ablations: Table 1 shows both signature uncertainty modeling and recursive ROC propagation provide measurable benefits.
- Upper bound analysis: OURS+TRUE ROC indicates greater potential if ROC estimates for unseen classes can be improved.
- Clarity: Paper is very well-written with clear motivation, related work placement, and a logical progression of ideas.
- Strengths summary: Novel, elegant, practically impactful idea; technically sound with convincing evaluation.
- Weakness: Method depends on a held-out validation set (DV) for ROC estimates; sensitivity to DV size/composition is not explored.
- Weakness: Scalability concern due to one-vs-all random forests (one forest per unseen class) may not scale to many classes.
- Weakness: Node-specific ROC estimation reverts to global stats when data is sparse; frequency and effect of this are unclear.
- Recommendation: Strong accept—strengths and empirical gains outweigh the noted limitations.
- Counterpoints against acceptance: Limited DV analysis and potential scalability issues are the main reservations.
