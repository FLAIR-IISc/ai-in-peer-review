This paper addresses the important problem of zero-shot learning, specifically tackling the unreliability of attribute predictions – a known bottleneck in the standard paradigm. The authors propose a random forest approach that explicitly models attribute error tendencies during training, offering improvements over existing methods, particularly when attribute predictions are noisy. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and demonstrates solid empirical results on three datasets.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea builds on the standard zero-shot learning framework [8, 18, 3, 19, 24], where classes are defined by attribute signatures.  However, unlike previous approaches that treat attribute predictions as ground truth, this work acknowledges and models their inherent unreliability.  This is achieved by incorporating attribute classifier ROC curves into the random forest training process, allowing for more robust decision boundaries. The authors relate their work to fractional tuples [21] and methods handling probabilistic features [28], highlighting the novelty of applying these concepts to the specific zero-shot learning context with signature-based training.  They also position their work against recent approaches focusing on discovering mid-level features [11, 22, 26, 15, 30, 27, 1], emphasizing the advantage of retaining human-interpretable attribute descriptions. The extension to the few-shot setting is a valuable addition, building on prior work in that area [31, 26, 14, 1].

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach appears technically sound. The use of ROC curves to model attribute uncertainty is a clever and well-justified idea. The formulation for propagating fractional signatures through the random forest is clearly explained and appears logically consistent.  The extension to few-shot learning is also a natural and reasonable extension.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and the authors' contribution. The approach section is detailed and easy to follow, and the experimental setup is described thoroughly.
* **Originality:**  While building upon established zero-shot learning techniques, the explicit modeling of attribute unreliability within a random forest framework is a novel contribution. The recursive propagation of validation data and the consideration of attribute correlations are also innovative.
* **Significance:** The results demonstrate a significant improvement over state-of-the-art methods (specifically DAP [8,9]), especially in scenarios with noisy attribute predictions.  Addressing the reliability issue is crucial for making zero-shot learning practical. The potential to extend this work to other areas relying on mid-level semantic representations is also promising.  The fact that the method works well with both hand-designed and discovered attributes is a bonus. The well-designed ablation studies help pinpoint the value of each component.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The process of recursively propagating validation data for ROC estimation potentially increases computational complexity. The paper doesn’t explicitly address the scalability of this approach to very large datasets or attribute vocabularies.
* **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on a separate validation dataset for estimating attribute classifier error rates.  The performance could be sensitive to the quality and representativeness of this validation set. More discussion on the sensitivity of performance to the size and characteristics of this validation set would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Attribute Discovery:** While the authors show results with discovered attributes [30], the analysis is somewhat brief. Exploring the interplay between attribute discovery and their uncertainty modeling in more detail could be a fruitful direction for future work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  Addresses a critical limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods (attribute unreliability).
*  Proposes a novel and technically sound solution based on random forests and ROC curves.
*  Demonstrates significant performance improvements on three benchmark datasets.
*  Well-written, clear, and thoroughly evaluated.
*  Offers a practical and potentially impactful contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*  Computational complexity of the approach might limit scalability.
*  Dependency on a separate validation dataset for ROC estimation.
*  Limited analysis of interaction with attribute discovery methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors have identified a key challenge and proposed a well-motivated and effective solution. The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the evaluation further strengthen the paper’s merit. Despite some minor concerns regarding computational complexity and validation data dependency, the benefits of the proposed approach outweigh the drawbacks. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I suggest the authors address the scalability concerns by providing a brief analysis of computational costs, and provide a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the quality and size of the validation dataset.
