- Problem: Attribute-based zero-shot learning is brittle because attribute classifiers are noisy and prior methods ignore this unreliability.  
- Main contribution: A zero-shot random forest that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability during training.  
- Core mechanism: Train on class attribute signatures and fractionally propagate signatures down tree splits using attribute ROC/error statistics.  
- Error estimation: Attribute error statistics (TP/FP rates) are estimated on a held-out attribute-labeled validation set (DV).  
- Extensions: Handles uncertainty in class signatures and a few-shot setting by combining signature priors with labeled examples.  
- Relation to prior work: Improves over DAP-style methods by modeling classifier error tendencies; connects to work on missing/soft features but applies it novelly to ZSL.  
- Technical quality: Method is mathematically sound with a clear formulation for incorporating classifier uncertainty.  
- Experimental strength: Convincing results with controlled noise experiments, evaluations on AwA/aPY/SUN, ablations, and state-of-the-art results on AwA.  
- Ablations: Show isolated contributions of signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation.  
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow; a concrete numerical example could further clarify fractional propagation.  
- Originality: Novel synthesis of random forests and fractional propagation to train on idealized signatures while modeling test-time noise.  
- Significance: Addresses a practical bottleneck in attribute ZSL, improving robustness and applicability; framework is general and extends to few-shot learning.  
- Main weakness: Reliance on a representative attribute-labeled validation set (DV); performance may degrade if DV is not representative of unseen classes.  
- Practical concern: One-vs-all random forest training may not scale efficiently to very large numbers of unseen classes.
