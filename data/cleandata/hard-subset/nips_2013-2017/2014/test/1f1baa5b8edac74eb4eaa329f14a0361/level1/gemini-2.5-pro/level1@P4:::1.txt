### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a key practical limitation of zero-shot learning (ZSL) based on semantic attributes: the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The standard ZSL paradigm defines a new class by its "signature"—a vector specifying which attributes it possesses—and then classifies test images by comparing their predicted attribute vectors to these signatures. The authors argue that this approach is brittle because attribute predictions are often noisy and inaccurate, a problem largely ignored by prior methods which take attribute predictions at face value.

The main contribution is a novel zero-shot random forest model that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier unreliability during training. Instead of training on images, the random forest is trained directly on the attribute signatures of the unseen classes. The core novelty lies in the tree-building process. At each node, the decision of how to split the data (i.e., which attribute and threshold to use) is guided not just by the discriminative power of the attribute for the ideal signatures, but also by its predicted reliability. This is achieved by fractionally propagating each class signature down both children of a split, with the proportions determined by the attribute classifier's error statistics (e.g., true/false positive rates). These error statistics are estimated on a held-out set of attribute-labeled validation images. This process effectively selects for splits that are both discriminative and robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The framework is also extended to handle uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning setting, where it combines the signature-based prior with information from a small number of labeled examples.

This work builds upon the line of attribute-based ZSL, notably contrasting with the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) model [8, 9] and similar approaches [3, 18]. While those methods model the probability of an attribute's presence, they do not explicitly model the classifier's error tendencies (e.g., its ROC curve) when learning the zero-shot model itself. The paper's core technical mechanism of fractional propagation in decision trees relates to prior work on handling missing values [21] or soft/probabilistic features [28, 16]. However, its application to the ZSL problem is novel, particularly in its use of attribute signatures as training "exemplars" and its recursive propagation of validation data to compute node-specific error statistics, which helps account for dependencies between attributes selected at different levels of the tree.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the mathematical formulation for incorporating classifier uncertainty via fractional propagation is clear and appropriate. The experimental evaluation is strong and convincing.
*   The controlled noise experiments (Fig. 1) provide clear, intuitive validation for the central hypothesis that modeling attribute-specific noise improves robustness.
*   Experiments are conducted on three standard and diverse ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating the general applicability of the approach.
*   The ablation studies successfully isolate the contributions of key components of the model (signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation), strengthening the claims.
*   The authors are careful in their evaluation, including comparisons to strong baselines like DAP and demonstrating state-of-the-art results on the AwA benchmark against a wide range of published methods. The extension to the few-shot setting is also empirically validated.

A potential weakness is the method's reliance on a validation set of attribute-labeled images (`DV`) to estimate the ROC statistics. The paper assumes this set is representative of the error patterns that will be seen on test images of the unseen classes. If the visual appearance or attribute co-occurrence statistics of unseen classes differ significantly from the seen classes (from which `DV` is drawn), the estimated error rates might be biased, potentially limiting performance. The "upper bound" experiment using unseen class images for validation highlights this gap, but the sensitivity to the composition of `DV` is not deeply explored.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly frames the problem and motivates the solution. The methodology section is logically structured, starting with a basic formulation and incrementally adding the novel components for handling unreliability. The distinction between training on signatures and testing on attribute predictions is made clear. Figures and tables are informative and support the main claims effectively. The description of the fractional propagation mechanism is intuitive, though a small, concrete numerical example might have further clarified the mechanics.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While individual components like random forests and fractional data propagation exist, their synthesis to solve the problem of attribute unreliability in ZSL is novel. The idea of training a classifier on idealized class descriptions (signatures) while explicitly modeling the noise process that will affect the inputs at test time is a significant conceptual advance over prior work that treats the stages of attribute prediction and class prediction as independent. This work directly confronts a known but previously unaddressed weakness of attribute-based ZSL.

**Significance:** The work is significant as it addresses a critical bottleneck for the practical application of attribute-based ZSL. By demonstrating substantial improvements over the popular DAP model, it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The proposed technique makes attribute-based models more robust and usable in real-world scenarios where attribute classifiers are imperfect. The framework is general and could potentially be applied to other hierarchical recognition systems that rely on predictions from mid-level concept detectors. The successful extension to few-shot learning also broadens its impact, providing a principled way to incorporate descriptive priors into learning with limited data.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a well-defined, important, and practical problem in zero-shot learning.
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability.
*   The methodology is technically sound and the paper is written with outstanding clarity.
*   Experimental results are comprehensive and show significant performance gains over strong, widely-used baselines on multiple standard datasets.

**Con:**
*   The method's performance is dependent on the availability and representativeness of an attribute-labeled validation set, and the sensitivity to this is not fully analyzed.
*   The one-vs-all training scheme for the random forests may not scale efficiently to scenarios with a very large number of unseen classes.
