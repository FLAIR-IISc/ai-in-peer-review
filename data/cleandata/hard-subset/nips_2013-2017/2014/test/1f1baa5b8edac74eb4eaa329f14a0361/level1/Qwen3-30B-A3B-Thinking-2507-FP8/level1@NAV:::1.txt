

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for the error tendencies of attribute classifiers during training, rather than treating attribute predictions as perfect. The key insight is to propagate training signatures through the tree in proportion to the attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristics (ROC), thereby building in appropriate "cushion" for expected errors. The method extends to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and few-shot scenarios.

The paper builds on seminal zero-shot work like DAP [8], which is widely used but fails to account for attribute unreliability. While related to work on handling missing values in decision trees [21], the authors clearly distinguish their zero-shot setting where training uses attribute signatures rather than labeled images. The experimental evaluation on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrates significant improvements over DAP and other baselines, particularly when attribute classifiers are less reliable. The results are robust across different noise levels and attribute configurations, with the authors providing clear ablation studies showing the contribution of each component.

Strengths include a well-motivated problem statement, clear technical contribution, and thorough empirical validation. The approach is conceptually elegant and directly addresses a gap in the literature. The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the method's novelty and the experimental setup. The significance is high as it enables more reliable zero-shot learning, which is crucial for rare categories or dynamic category definitions.

Weaknesses are minor: the reliance on validation data for ROC estimation might be challenging in some settings, and the few-shot extension, while promising, is less developed than the core zero-shot contribution. However, these do not undermine the main contribution.

Pro: Addresses a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning; clear experimental validation; strong theoretical grounding; practical significance.
Con: Validation data requirement; few-shot extension could be more extensive.

This paper makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning that advances the state of the art. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches. I recommend acceptance.
