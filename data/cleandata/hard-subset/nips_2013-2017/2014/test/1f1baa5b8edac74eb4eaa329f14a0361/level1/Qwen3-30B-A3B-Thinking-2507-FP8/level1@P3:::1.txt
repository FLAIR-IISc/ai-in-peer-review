

# Peer Review: Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes

This paper addresses a critical yet overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for the error tendencies of attribute classifiers during training, rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions as in prior work. The core insight is to use the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers to fractionally propagate training signatures through the tree, accounting for expected errors during test time. The method is further extended to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning.

The paper makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by tackling the practical limitation that has prevented the field from moving beyond proof-of-concept results. While zero-shot learning has been widely studied (e.g., [8, 18, 3, 19, 23]), prior work assumes perfect attribute prediction, which is unrealistic. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this assumption is the primary reason for the performance gap between zero-shot learning and other transfer learning methods. Their method, which incorporates attribute classifier error statistics during training, outperforms the state-of-the-art DAP method [8] by a substantial margin across three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), with gains of up to 10.2% on specific categories.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The methodology is thoroughly explained with precise mathematical formulations, and the experimental design is robust. The controlled noise experiments (Figure 1) provide compelling evidence that the method specifically addresses the unreliability problem, while the real-world experiments (Table 1) demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is particularly valuable, showing how attribute signatures can effectively guide learning when only limited labeled examples are available.

The approach builds on the random forest framework but introduces two key innovations: (1) fractional propagation of training signatures based on attribute classifier error rates, and (2) recursive validation data propagation to capture dependencies between attributes. This distinguishes it from related work on handling missing values [21] or probabilistic features [28], as the authors correctly note that the zero-shot setting creates unique challenges. The method's strength lies in its practical implementation of incorporating uncertainty during training, rather than attempting to correct errors at test time.

The paper excels in quality and significance. The experimental results are thorough, with careful ablation studies that isolate the contributions of each component (OURS W/O ROC PROP, SIG UNCERTAINTY). The authors demonstrate that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than having more attribute-labeled data (Section 4.1). The significance is clear: this work makes zero-shot learning practically viable for real-world applications where new categories emerge dynamically with minimal training data.

However, the paper has a few limitations. First, the method relies on validation data (DV) to estimate attribute classifier error rates, but the paper doesn't thoroughly discuss how representative this data is of the unseen classes. While the authors note that using unseen class images as DV would provide an "upper bound," this is impractical, and it's unclear how well the validation data from seen classes generalizes to unseen classes. Second, the paper doesn't explore computational complexity or scalability to large attribute spaces (e.g., hundreds of attributes), which could be a concern for real-world applications. Third, while the few-shot extension is promising, the paper could better justify the weighting parameter λ and how it should be set in practice.

The related work section is comprehensive and well-motivated, but it could more explicitly contrast with recent zero-shot methods that use learned embeddings (e.g., [1, 4, 14]). While the authors note that their approach focuses on language-based descriptions, they could more clearly explain why this is advantageous for dynamic category definition compared to embedding-based methods.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in visual recognition with a technically sound solution. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are significant. The authors make a compelling case that zero-shot learning can be practical when we account for attribute unreliability.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a critical, overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning (attribute unreliability)
- Proposes a novel, theoretically sound approach that incorporates error statistics during training
- Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods across three datasets
- Provides clear ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each component
- Successfully extends to few-shot learning, broadening applicability
- Well-written with clear experimental design and thorough analysis

**Weaknesses:**
- Reliance on validation data for error estimation lacks discussion of representativeness for unseen classes
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Few-shot extension could be more thoroughly justified
- Could better contrast with recent embedding-based zero-shot methods

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a fundamental limitation in the field. The method is technically sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear practical value. While there are some limitations in the validation data assumption and few-shot justification, these do not undermine the core contribution. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has the potential to influence future zero-shot learning research.
