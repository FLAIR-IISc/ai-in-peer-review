- Addresses the unrealistic assumption of perfect attribute predictions in zero-shot learning by modeling attribute prediction unreliability.  
- Proposes a novel random forest method that incorporates attribute classifier error statistics (ROC) into training to select robust decision nodes.  
- Extends the approach to few-shot learning and to handle unreliable attribute descriptions.  
- Builds on existing attribute-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DAP) but fills a key practical gap, making the contribution novel.  
- Theoretical formulation is rigorous and the method is technically sound, with clear justification for incorporating error statistics.  
- Ablation studies effectively isolate contributions (signature uncertainty, ROC propagation) and validate design choices.  
- Experiments are thorough across three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), including controlled noise and real-world attribute prediction tests.  
- Demonstrates consistent, substantial improvements over DAP, sometimes by as much as 10.2%, and outperforms even with fewer attribute-labeled images.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and includes helpful visualizations and detailed results.  
- Clarifies distinction between attribute unreliability (this work) and attribute strength (prior work).  
- Weakness: lacks detail on practical implementation and selection criteria for (m,t) combinations in ROC propagation.  
- Weakness: does not fully address scalability to very large numbers of attributes or classes.  
- Weakness: explanation of recursive validation-data propagation and connection between ROC and information gain could be clearer with examples.  
- Recommendation: accept — significant, practically valuable contribution with strong empirical support.  
- Suggestions: discuss scaling to 1000+ attributes, detail (m,t) selection during tree construction, expand applications beyond vision, and clarify robustness versus "attribute strength" approaches.
