Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a core limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictions at test time are noisy and biased. The authors propose a random forest (RF) approach that trains directly on the unseen classes’ attribute signatures but explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability via receiver operating characteristic (ROC) statistics estimated from attribute-labeled validation data. Each decision node selects an attribute m and threshold t by maximizing an information gain that propagates class signatures fractionally to left/right children according to the attribute classifier’s TP/FP rates at (m, t). This induces trees that prefer splits that are both discriminative among unseen classes and likely to be realizable given attribute error tendencies. The method also models uncertainty in class-level signatures and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with that from few labeled images via a weighting parameter λ.

Relative to prevalent two-stage ZSL methods (e.g., DAP by Lampert et al., similarity-to-signature matching [Farhadi et al., Palatucci et al.], topic/embedding models [Akata et al., Frome et al.]), the key novelty is to incorporate per-attribute reliability into the training objective of the unseen-class classifiers themselves, rather than trusting test-time attribute scores at face value. The approach is also related to handling uncertainty/missing values in decision trees (fractional propagation, fuzzy trees), but differs by: (1) training on attribute signatures rather than labeled instances, (2) using node-specific ROC estimates propagated from validation data to capture dependencies between attributes, and (3) targeting the zero/few-shot regime. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, and competitive performance with methods using discovered attributes.

Strengths
- Clear technical idea: redefine RF splitting with an information gain computed over fractionally propagated class signatures using node-specific ROC estimates (IGzero).
- Sound handling of attribute unreliability: prefers both reliable attributes and discriminative operating points; avoids splits that are unlearnable given observed TP/FP.
- Accounts for signature noise (class-attribute association uncertainty) and extends to few-shot via a principled prior (weighted combination of signature- and data-based gains).
- Empirical validation is thorough: controlled noise studies, three diverse datasets, ablations (without signature uncertainty, without node-wise ROC propagation), “upper bound” with true ROC, and few-shot behavior across λ.
- Uses the same attribute-labeled data as baselines; shows that modeling error tendencies can outweigh marginal gains from more attribute data.
- Conceptually general; can leverage discovered pseudo-attributes.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data from seen classes; assumes TP/FP rates transfer to unseen-class images. The “upper bound” with true ROC highlights a potential domain gap.
- Scalability: one-vs-all forests per unseen class and node-wise ROC estimation/propagation may become costly with many unseen classes and attributes; complexity analysis is limited.
- Comparisons focus primarily on attribute-based ZSL (DAP) and an attribute-decoding method; limited head-to-head with contemporary embedding-based zero-shot methods (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) under identical features/splits.
- The approach still operates with univariate tests (one attribute per split); correlations are handled indirectly via node-wise ROC subsets but not through joint tests.
- Calibration issues: relies on Platt probabilities and threshold sampling; sensitivity to calibration and to (#m, #t), depth, and λ is only partially explored.
- Some clarity concerns: the workflow for training attribute SVMs vs. ROC estimation (using a held-out subset and then retraining on all data) would benefit from explicit description to avoid confusion about leakage and mismatch between ROC-estimated and deployed classifiers.

Quality
- Methodologically sound, with a precise formulation of fractional propagation and information gain (Eqns. 4–5) and a coherent few-shot extension (Eqn. 6).
- Ablations support claims about the contribution of ROC propagation and signature uncertainty.
- Results are reported with mean ± SE over multiple trials and include controlled-noise stress tests.
- Theoretical guarantees are not provided, but the empirical evidence is convincing for the stated scope.

Clarity
- Generally well organized; core algorithmic components are described clearly with equations and intuitive explanations.
- Some details deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, signature uncertainty term); including a concise algorithm box in the main text would improve reproducibility.
- Clarify the two-stage attribute training/ROC estimation to avoid ambiguity.

Originality
- Novel application of ROC-aware, fractional propagation to train ZSL classifiers on signatures; distinct from prior attribute-based ZSL that ignores attribute reliability in training.
- Few-shot integration via an information gain prior is a clean formulation.
- Relation to decision trees for uncertain data is acknowledged and the zero-shot-specific adaptations are meaningful.

Significance
- Addresses a central practical obstacle for attribute-based ZSL and demonstrates consistent improvements on standard benchmarks.
- The idea is likely to influence future ZSL designs that must cope with noisy mid-level predictors and may generalize to other semantic intermediates.
- Practical impact hinges on availability and transferability of attribute-labeled validation data and on scalability to larger vocabularies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide comparisons to strong embedding-based ZSL baselines (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) using the same features/splits.
- Analyze sensitivity to the quantity and domain match of DV, and to attribute calibration quality.
- Report computational cost and memory vs. number of attributes/unseen classes; consider multi-class forests to reduce one-vs-all overhead.
- Explore multi-attribute split tests or oblique splits to more directly capture attribute correlations.
- Expand evaluation with discovered attributes beyond AwA and analyze when discovered vs. named attributes benefit most.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to mismatches between the ROC-estimating attribute classifiers and the final deployed attribute classifiers (trained on more data)?
- What is the runtime/memory footprint as K and M grow? Can trees be shared across classes?
- How does the method perform when DV is small or biased relative to unseen classes?

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated method that directly tackles a known weakness of attribute-based ZSL.
- Strong empirical gains over a widely used baseline (DAP) across multiple datasets and settings.
- Conceptually simple, general, and extendable to few-shot learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited comparisons to non-attribute ZSL approaches popular at NIPS.
- Reliance on transferable ROC estimates may limit robustness under larger domain shifts.
- Scalability and complexity trade-offs are not fully characterized.
