- Paper addresses noisy, biased test-time attribute predictions in attribute-based ZSL by training random forests on class attribute signatures and modeling attribute unreliability via ROC stats from attribute-labeled validation data.
- Core idea: fractionally propagate class signatures down trees using node-specific TP/FP rates at candidate thresholds to favor discriminative and learnable splits.
- Extensions: models signature uncertainty (noisy class-attribute associations) and a few-shot variant that mixes signature-driven and data-driven information gain.
- Empirical results: consistent gains on AwA, aPY, and SUN with controlled noise studies, ablations, and reporting mean ± SE over 20 trials.
- Advance over standard two-stage ZSL (e.g., DAP): accounts for attribute detector unreliability during training rather than relying on test-time confidences.
- Preserves human-interpretable attribute interface compared to mid-level discovery or semantic-embedding methods while improving robustness.
- Fractional routing relates to fuzzy/uncertain decision trees but is adapted to ZSL via training on signatures and node-conditional ROC estimates.
- Methodology is well-motivated and technically sound; information gain objective appropriately reflects uncertainty.
- Few-shot extension is natural, treating signatures as priors combined with image data via convex combination of gains.
- Correctness: arguments about unlearnable attributes yielding zero information gain are convincing; node-conditional DV(n) dependence is important to avoid inconsistent ROC estimates.
- Signature uncertainty model is deferred to the supplement and its assumptions should be summarized in the main text for assessment.
- Experiments are strong with informative “upper-bound” using true ROC from unseen classes.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; key concepts like DV and one-vs-all forests are well explained.
- Clarify few-shot section whether IGbasic uses predicted attributes â(x) or raw image descriptors to avoid reader confusion.
- Add training/testing time and memory cost analysis, since one-vs-all forest per unseen class raises scalability concerns for large K.
- Bring the main formula and assumptions for signature uncertainty into the paper body rather than the supplement.
- Originality: novel combination of training trees from signatures, ROC-driven fractional propagation, and integrating signature/data gains for few-shot in ZSL.
- Significance: tackles a central practical limitation of attribute ZSL, shows substantial empirical gains, and retains interpretability useful beyond vision.
- Strength: practical ROC-driven fractional routing requiring only standard attribute-labeled validation data.
- Strength: strong empirical results, ablations, and controlled noise studies; effective few-shot extension.
- Weakness: reliance on seen-class validation (DV) for ROC may bias estimates under domain shift to unseen classes.
- Weakness: scalability concerns with one RF per unseen class; single multiclass or multi-label forests suggested.
- Weakness: limited split family (univariate tests); consider oblique or pairwise splits to capture attribute correlations.
- Weakness: method depends on calibration and threshold sampling; robustness to calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) should be investigated.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to label-embedding/semantic-embedding baselines like ALE or DeViSE on AwA/aPY.
- Weakness: implementation/details deferred to supplement (e.g., min DV(n) size, ROC smoothing) should be included in main text.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to DV size/composition, whether stratifying DV by seen-class similarity helps, whether few-shot IGbasic uses â(x) or raw features, and training/inference time and memory for varying K.
- Pros: principled and effective treatment of attribute unreliability, strong empirical gains, semantic interpretability, and elegant few-shot extension.
- Cons: scalability and potential DV bias, some missing baselines, and supplementary details that should be moved to the main text.
- Recommendation: Weak accept; solid contribution with high confidence.
