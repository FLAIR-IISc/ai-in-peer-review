

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the assumption that attribute predictions are reliable, which is often not the case in practice. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability by leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies, using receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers to select discriminative decision nodes during training. This is a well-motivated contribution that tackles a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning that has been overlooked in prior work.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-supported by extensive experiments. The authors provide a clear methodological contribution with proper ablation studies (see Table 1), demonstrating that both components of their approach—modeling signature uncertainty and recursive propagation of validation data—contribute to performance. Their results show significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), with gains up to 10.2% on specific classes. The method is particularly effective on datasets with less reliable attribute classifiers (aPY and SUN), precisely where the authors' approach is needed most. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for validation data to estimate error rates, and discusses how this could be improved in future work. The experimental setup is thorough, using standard datasets and baselines, with results reported as mean and standard error over 20 trials.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The main idea is clearly presented early, followed by detailed technical explanations. The method is described with sufficient precision for experts to reproduce the work, including details about the random forest training procedure, ROC statistics, and few-shot extension. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the method's behavior under controlled noise conditions. The paper properly contextualizes their work within the zero-shot learning literature, clearly distinguishing their approach from existing methods.

**Originality**: The paper identifies a key gap in zero-shot learning literature that has been overlooked: the unreliability of attribute predictions during training. While previous work (e.g., DAP [8]) has focused on attribute-based zero-shot learning, none have explicitly accounted for attribute unreliability during model training. The authors' approach of incorporating ROC statistics into random forest training is novel and directly addresses this gap. The paper also extends their method to the few-shot setting, further enhancing its practical utility. The authors properly cite related work, including recent NIPS papers on zero-shot learning (e.g., [8, 18, 23]), and clearly articulate how their work differs from prior contributions.

**Significance**: This work has substantial significance for the field. Zero-shot learning has been limited by the difficulty of reliable attribute prediction, and this paper demonstrates that the problem can be mitigated through a simple yet effective modification to the training procedure. The authors show that their method outperforms the leading DAP method on all three datasets, with particularly large gains on AwA for classes like "giant panda" (10.2%), "whale seal" (9.4%), and "persian cat" (7.4%). When using discovered attributes from [30], their method achieves state-of-the-art results on AwA. This work has the potential to significantly advance the practical applicability of zero-shot learning in real-world scenarios where labeled data is scarce.

**Strengths**: 
- Identifies and addresses a critical, overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning
- Proposes a novel, well-motivated method that incorporates ROC statistics into random forest training
- Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets
- Provides thorough ablation studies to isolate contributions of different components
- Extends to few-shot learning, increasing practical utility

**Weaknesses**: 
- Relies on a separate validation set to estimate attribute classifier error rates, which may be challenging to construct for truly novel classes with no related seen classes
- Doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the method to the size of the validation set (they use 20% of attribute-labeled data, but it's unclear if this is optimal)

The paper makes a strong contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a fundamental practical limitation. The method is simple yet effective, and the results clearly demonstrate its value. The authors have made a significant advance in making zero-shot learning more robust and practical. The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission: it's technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a clear path for future research on zero-shot learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
