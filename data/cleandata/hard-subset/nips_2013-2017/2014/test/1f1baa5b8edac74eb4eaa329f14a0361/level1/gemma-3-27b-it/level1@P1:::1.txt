This paper addresses the challenging problem of zero-shot learning (ZSL), specifically focusing on the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea is to build a random forest model that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier errors during training, using ROC curves to inform the splitting criteria. The method is further extended to few-shot learning and unreliable class-attribute associations. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and demonstrates promising results on three datasets.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on the established ZSL paradigm of defining unseen classes through attributes (e.g., [8, 3, 18]). Unlike most prior work, which treat attribute predictions as ground truth, this paper directly tackles the issue of unreliable attributes. This contrasts with work focusing on discovering better mid-level features [11, 22, 26, 30] as the latter moves away from human-defined descriptions.  The approach of propagating training instances proportionally to uncertainty is related to fractional tuples for handling missing values in decision trees [21], but the application to the unique zero-shot setting and simultaneous propagation of signatures and data points differentiates it.  The paper effectively positions itself within the ZSL landscape, acknowledging related work while clearly highlighting its novel contributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a crucial limitation of ZSL:** The paper directly confronts the practical difficulty of accurate attribute prediction, a major bottleneck in ZSL performance.
* **Novel approach:**  Leveraging ROC curves during random forest training to account for attribute unreliability is a clever and well-motivated idea.
* **Strong empirical results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing methods (including the widely used DAP [8]) across three datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The controlled experiments isolating the impact of attribute noise are particularly convincing.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is easy to follow, with a logical structure and clear explanations of the method.
* **Extension to few-shot learning:**  The extension to the few-shot setting adds further value and broadens the applicability of the method.
* **Thorough experimental evaluation:** The paper includes comprehensive experiments, ablations, and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of results on discovered attributes [30] is a strength.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational cost:** Training random forests, even with these extensions, can be computationally expensive, especially for large numbers of attributes and classes. The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss computational complexity or scalability.
* **Validation Data Requirement:** The method requires a validation set for estimating attribute error rates. While this is reasonable, the paper doesn’t fully explore the sensitivity of performance to the size of this validation set.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, a more in-depth analysis of failure cases would be beneficial.  Identifying specific attributes or classes where the method struggles would provide valuable insights for future improvements.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters like tree depth and the weighting parameter λ in the few-shot extension may require careful tuning. The paper provides some details on cross-validation, but a more systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity would be useful.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported by experimental results. The theoretical justification for incorporating ROC curves into the splitting criteria is convincing. The approach appears complete and not merely a position paper. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their method.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The explanations of the method are clear and concise, and the figures and tables are well-presented.
* **Originality:** The idea of explicitly accounting for attribute unreliability during ZSL training is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The combination of random forests with ROC-based splitting criteria is a creative and effective approach.
* **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to significantly impact the field of ZSL.  Improving the robustness of ZSL models to unreliable attribute predictions is a critical step towards making these methods more practical for real-world applications.  The method addresses a challenging problem and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental limitation of zero-shot learning.
* Presents a novel and effective approach with strong empirical results.
* Is well-written, clearly explained, and thoroughly evaluated.
* Offers a valuable contribution to the ZSL literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost could be a limitation for large-scale applications.
* Requires a dedicated validation dataset for estimating error rates.
* Lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases and parameter sensitivity.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work addresses a critical problem in ZSL, presents a novel and effective solution, and is supported by thorough experimental evaluation. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the overall significance of the contribution. This paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
