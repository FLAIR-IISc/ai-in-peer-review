- Addresses zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling attribute prediction unreliability during training.
- Proposes a random forest approach that incorporates attribute error tendencies using ROC-guided splitting.
- Extends the method to few-shot learning and to handle unreliable class-attribute associations.
- Well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a novel principled way to mitigate noisy attribute predictions.
- Experimental results on three datasets show consistent improvements over baselines (including DAP) and ablation studies validate components.
- Core contribution: integrating attribute reliability into the learning process rather than treating predictions as ground truth.
- Related-work coverage is good but should include deeper discussion of noisy-data decision tree techniques (e.g., soft/probabilistic trees).
- ROC propagation mechanism is effective but adds complexity and needs a more intuitive justification vs. simpler alternatives (e.g., confidence-weighting).
- Evaluation relies on pre-defined features; testing with end-to-end learned features (e.g., CNNs) would strengthen generalizability claims.
- Impact of validation set size on reliability estimation should be analyzed beyond the fixed 20% split.
- Parameter study for λ in few-shot is useful but could be expanded and consider automatic adaptation strategies.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and technically sound work with experimental support; recommendation for acceptance despite some areas for improvement.
