**Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"**

**Summary:**
This paper tackles a critical and often overlooked problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The standard ZSL paradigm assumes that given an image, one can accurately predict the presence of various semantic attributes, which are then used to classify the image into a novel category defined only by its "signature" of attributes. The authors correctly argue that this assumption is flawed, as attribute classifiers are often noisy and inaccurate, limiting the practical performance of ZSL systems.

The core contribution is a novel zero-shot random forest (RF) model that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier uncertainty during training. Instead of training on idealized, noise-free attribute signatures, the proposed method leverages a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate the error characteristics (i.e., the ROC curve) of each attribute classifier. During the construction of the decision trees, class signatures are "fractionally" propagated down different branches based on the estimated true positive and false positive rates of the attribute classifiers. This novel training procedure encourages the RF to select splits that are not only discriminative for the ideal signatures but are also robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The authors further extend this framework to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and to the "few-shot" learning setting.

This work builds upon the foundational attribute-based ZSL literature, particularly methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) by Lampert et al. (CVPR 2009). However, it makes a clear departure by moving beyond simply using attribute predictions as features and instead integrating a model of their unreliability directly into the learning process. The method is also conceptually related to techniques for handling missing or uncertain data in decision trees, but is uniquely adapted to the specific challenges of the ZSL problem where the training "data" (signatures) and test data (classifier predictions on images) come from different domains.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical bottleneck in attribute-based recognition. The idea of "preemptively" making the zero-shot model robust by baking in knowledge of attribute classifier fallibility is both novel and insightful. It moves the field from a "proof of concept" towards a more practical and robust application of the ZSL paradigm.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and elegant. The fractional propagation of signatures based on ROC statistics is a clever mechanism for translating classifier error rates into the decision tree learning process. The corresponding modification of the information gain criterion (Eq. 5) is well-motivated and principled. The recursive propagation of the validation data to estimate node-specific ROC statistics is a particularly strong aspect, as it allows the model to capture dependencies between attributes in a hierarchical fashion.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the methodology is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner, from the basic signature RF to the full model, and the extensions are presented coherently. The conceptual explanation using the "elephant" example is very effective.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling.
    *   The controlled noise experiment (Fig. 1) provides direct and intuitive evidence for the method's core claim of robustness.
    *   The results on three diverse and standard benchmark datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) show substantial improvements over the widely used and strong DAP baseline, demonstrating the practical value of the approach.
    *   The ablation study in Table 1 is excellent, clearly isolating the contributions of the key components of the model (signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation).
    *   The extension to the few-shot setting is valuable and the results convincingly demonstrate the benefit of using the attribute signatures as a prior.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Representativeness of the Validation Set:** The method's effectiveness hinges on the assumption that the ROC statistics computed on the validation set `DV` (drawn from "seen" classes) are representative of the classifier performance on the "unseen" classes. This may not always hold true due to domain shift; for example, a 'striped' attribute classifier trained on tigers might perform differently on zebras. The "upper bound" experiment (OURS+TRUE ROC) highlights this gap. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of this limitation. How sensitive is the performance to the composition of `DV`? Have the authors considered any strategies for selecting a subset of `DV` that might be more relevant to the unseen classes?

2.  **Scalability to Many Unseen Classes:** The current approach trains one-vs-all random forests, meaning a separate forest is trained for each unseen class. This could be computationally prohibitive in scenarios with a very large number of potential unseen classes. While the authors mention multi-label RFs as future work, the current scalability is a potential concern that should be acknowledged.

3.  **Handling Attribute Correlations:** The paper claims that recursive sub-selection of validation data captures attribute dependencies. While this does condition the error estimate at a child node on the parent's split, the splitting criterion itself remains univariate (i.e., it tests a single attribute). This might be suboptimal when attributes are highly correlated. A brief discussion on the limitations of the current univariate test in the presence of strong correlations would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and practical problem in ZSL with a novel and effective solution.
*   The methodology is technically sound, elegant, and well-explained.
*   Extensive and rigorous experiments validate the claims and show significant performance gains on multiple benchmarks over a strong baseline.
*   The work is of high quality and is likely to have a significant impact on future research in zero-shot and few-shot learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The assumption about the representativeness of the validation set is a key dependency that could be further investigated.
*   The one-vs-all training scheme may not scale well to scenarios with a very large number of unseen classes.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The core idea of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability is novel, the technical execution is sound, and the experimental results are strong and convincing. The paper is clearly written and rigorously evaluated. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and represent interesting avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. This work significantly advances the state of the art in attribute-based recognition and is a clear accept for NIPS.
