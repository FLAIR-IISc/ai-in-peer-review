This paper tackles a core limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are error-prone, so treating their outputs as ground truth at test time degrades performance. The authors propose a random forest framework trained not on images but on per-class attribute signatures, while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. Using per-attribute ROC statistics estimated from held-out attribute-labeled data, class signatures are fractionally propagated through candidate splits according to attribute TP/FP rates; information gain is computed on these soft counts to prioritize splits that are both discriminative and learnable given expected attribute errors. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in class–attribute associations and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-based and example-based information gains with a tunable prior weight. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN using >250 attributes show consistent gains over Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations isolating the benefits of ROC-guided propagation and signature uncertainty. Results with discovered attributes also remain competitive, relating to work on semantic/feature embeddings (e.g., Lampert et al.’s DAP; Farhadi et al.; Akata et al.; Frome et al.) and knowledge-transfer methods (Mensink et al.).

Quality: The technical contribution is sound and well-motivated. Fractional propagation grounded in per-node ROC estimates is principled, and the few-shot extension is reasonable. Evaluation spans three datasets, includes controlled-noise studies and ablations, and reports mean±SE over multiple trials. Some analyses (e.g., computational complexity, sensitivity to validation set size/shift) could be deeper.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear. Key details (pseudocode, signature-noise model) are deferred to the supplement; moving a concise version into the main text would aid reproducibility. Clarify fallback behavior when per-node positives/negatives are scarce.

Originality: Modeling attribute unreliability within the tree-building objective for zero-shot training on signatures is novel, despite connections to decision trees for uncertain/missing data.

Significance: Addressing attribute unreliability materially improves zero-shot performance and offers a practical few-shot prior. The idea is broadly applicable to other semantic mid-levels.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, novel integration of ROC-guided uncertainty into zero-shot training.
- Strong, consistent improvements over DAP and baselines; informative ablations.
- Few-shot extension with demonstrable benefit.

Cons against acceptance:
- Reliance on held-out attribute-labeled validation data; potential domain shift to unseen classes is only partially addressed.
- Limited comparison to recent embedding-based zero-shot methods (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) on the same splits.
- Scalability concerns: one-vs-all forests and per-node ROC estimation; complexity not analyzed.
- Inter-attribute dependencies mostly untreated beyond per-node conditioning.
