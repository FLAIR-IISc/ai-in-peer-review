## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper tackles the crucial problem of attribute reliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The core idea – incorporating attribute classifier uncertainty, measured via validation set performance (TPR/FPR), into the information gain calculation during random forest construction – is a compelling and practical approach. The authors convincingly argue that acknowledging and modeling attribute unreliability is key to bridging the gap between the theoretical promise and practical performance of ZSL. This work builds upon existing attribute-based ZSL methods [8, 3, 18] by moving beyond simply *using* attribute predictions to actively *modeling* their trustworthiness during model training.

The empirical results presented on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) are strong and consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, outperforming the widely-used DAP baseline [8, 9]. The controlled experiments, introducing varying levels of noise to attribute predictions, further highlight the benefits of accounting for attribute unreliability. The extension to few-shot learning is a logical and valuable addition, showing the adaptability of the framework. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup. 

However, while the quantitative results are impressive, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis. It would be beneficial to understand *which* attributes and categories particularly benefit from the proposed method. Visualizing the decision trees or providing examples of cases where the uncertainty modeling leads to improved predictions would strengthen the understanding of the method’s inner workings. Furthermore, the rationale behind key hyperparameter choices, such as the 80/20 train-validation split for attribute classifiers and tree depth selection, is not fully explained. A more detailed discussion of sensitivity to these parameters would be appreciated.

The novelty lies in explicitly modeling attribute *reliability* (as opposed to attribute *strength*), a less explored angle despite related implicit approaches. This is a significant contribution, as it directly addresses a major bottleneck in ZSL.  The significance of the work is also considerable.  The idea of using validation-measured unreliability to improve ZSL is interesting and relevant, though the random forest component itself isn’t groundbreaking.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong quantitative results demonstrating improvement over strong baselines.
*   Well-written and clear presentation of a novel idea.
*   Addresses a key limitation of existing ZSL methods.
*   Logical and valuable extension to the few-shot setting.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of qualitative analysis to understand which attributes benefit the most.
*   Insufficient explanation of hyperparameter choices.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of zero-shot learning, and the proposed method shows promising results. Addressing the weaknesses regarding qualitative analysis and hyperparameter justification would further strengthen the paper.
