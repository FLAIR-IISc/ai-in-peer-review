- Proposes a novel ZSL approach using random forests that explicitly models attribute prediction unreliability.  
- Incorporates ROC-based estimates and fractional propagation of attribute signatures to handle noisy attributes.  
- Models joint attribute error statistics to improve robustness in ZSL scenarios.  
- Extends method to few-shot learning, though this extension is less developed.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and situates the work within related literature.  
- Provides detailed experimental setups and ablation studies that enhance reproducibility.  
- Validated on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets, showing effectiveness especially under noisy attribute conditions.  
- Controlled noise experiments further demonstrate the method’s robustness.  
- Few-shot learning component lacks detail, polish, and stronger theoretical grounding.  
- Method depends heavily on a large validation set to estimate attribute reliability, limiting practical applicability.  
- Quantitative gains over baselines (e.g., DAP) are modest except in high-noise scenarios.  
- Originality is moderate—builds on existing ZSL methods and adds complexity to handle attribute noise.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a critical ZSL limitation with a well-motivated, experimentally supported solution.  
- Argument for acceptance: methodological contributions and ablation studies are rigorous and convincing.  
- Argument for acceptance: clear writing makes the paper accessible to a wide audience.  
- Argument against acceptance: few-shot extension is underdeveloped and unclear.  
- Argument against acceptance: reliance on large validation set may limit real-world use.  
- Argument against acceptance: improvements are consistent but not a paradigm-shifting advance.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to elaborate the few-shot extension and address the validation-set dependency.
