This paper tackles a central practical barrier in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute detectors are imperfect, and existing ZSL pipelines typically treat their outputs as reliable. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains on class-attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability via ROC statistics estimated from attribute-labeled validation data. The key idea is to fractionally propagate class signatures through candidate tree splits according to node-specific TP/FP rates, yielding split decisions that are both discriminative and learnable given anticipated attribute errors. They further handle uncertainty in class signatures (to reflect class-level annotation noise) and extend the formulation to few-shot learning by combining signature-driven information gain with that from a small set of class-labeled images using a weighting parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest, with controlled noise analyses and ablations highlighting the contributions of ROC-based propagation and signature uncertainty modeling. Results also transfer to discovered attributes and few-shot settings.

Relation to prior work: Most ZSL methods (e.g., DAP, attribute-signature matching, topic models) are two-stage and assume reliable attribute predictions; some explore embeddings or hierarchy/label relations (DeViSE, label embeddings, ImageNet-based transfer), while others learn non-semantic mid-level features to bypass attributes. The proposed approach is closest to decision-tree strategies for missing/probabilistic features, but differs materially: trees are trained on class signatures (not images), decision statistics are driven by per-node ROC estimates to reflect anticipated test-time uncertainties, and an explicit few-shot integration uses signature priors in the split criterion. This is a novel and appropriate adaptation of random forests to ZSL.

Quality:
- Technically sound with a clear learning objective: maximize information gain under fractional, ROC-driven propagation that emulates test-time attribute errors. The few-shot extension is principled via a convex combination of information gains with interpretable λ.
- Empirical support is strong: three datasets, comparisons to DAP and published results, controlled noise stress tests, and ablations isolating ROC propagation and signature noise.
- Some points to clarify: (i) potential optimism in ROC estimation since the 20% “validation” data are later pooled to train the final attribute classifiers; a strictly held-out or cross-validated ROC would be preferable. (ii) Computational cost of per-node ROC estimation and its scalability as M, K grow; a runtime/memory profile would help situate practicality.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized and readable. The distinction between the basic signature RF (Sec. 3.2.1) and the reliability-aware variant (Sec. 3.2.2) is clear. The explanation of thresholds t in Sec. 3.2.1 is initially confusing (binary signatures make t moot there), but the role of t becomes essential once ROC-based unreliability is introduced; a forward reference would help. Pseudocode/figures in the supplementary are important to fully grasp the fractional propagation mechanism; consider promoting a schematic to the main paper.

Originality:
- The central contribution—using per-node ROC-driven fractional propagation of class signatures to learn reliability-aware trees for ZSL—is original and well matched to the problem. The few-shot integration via a signature prior in the split criterion is a neat and pragmatic extension.

Significance:
- Addresses a fundamental bottleneck for attribute-based ZSL and delivers state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks, especially where attribute detectors are weaker (aPY, SUN). The generality to discovered attributes and few-shot is valuable. The approach should be of interest to the ZSL, transfer learning, and decision-tree communities.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled modeling of attribute unreliability that maps directly to improved generalization.
- Strong empirical gains over a widely used baseline (DAP) and competitive with best published results; thorough ablations and stress tests.
- Method is simple to implement, interpretable (node-specific ROC behavior), and extensible (few-shot, signature noise).
- Bridges semantic attribute ZSL with robust decision-tree training, offering a path to practical deployment.

Cons/limitations:
- Reliance on attribute-labeled data for ROC estimation; domain shift between seen-class validation and unseen-class test could degrade calibration. The TRUE-ROC “upper bound” highlights this gap.
- Potential bias in ROC estimation if the held-out set is reused to train attribute classifiers; recommend out-of-fold or separate validation for ROC.
- Attribute independence is implicitly assumed in split selection; modeling correlations (as noted by the authors) could further help.
- Scalability analysis is limited: complexity as a function of attributes, thresholds per node, and unseen class count is not reported; memory/time comparisons to DAP or embedding models would strengthen the case.

Suggestions and questions:
- Provide ROC estimation with cross-validation or a strict hold-out to avoid optimistic bias; report sensitivity to the amount and class composition of DV.
- Analyze robustness to calibration drift (e.g., Platt scaling under domain shift) and consider recalibration or temperature scaling.
- Report training/inference time and memory versus DAP and a signature RF; discuss scalability to hundreds of unseen classes and larger M.
- Explore simple correlation-aware node tests (e.g., 2D splits or oblique trees) or grouped attributes to capture dependencies.
- Detail the procedure for sampling (#m, #t) per node and its impact on accuracy and cost; include a brief complexity discussion.
- In few-shot, show how λ is selected and whether a data-driven schedule (e.g., as a function of |DT|) can replace cross-validation.

Overall assessment: This is an elegant, well-executed contribution that advances attribute-based ZSL by addressing a critical practical weakness. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is careful, and the gains are meaningful. I recommend acceptance after minor clarifications, particularly around ROC estimation protocol and computational profiling.
