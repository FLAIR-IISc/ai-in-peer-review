Summary
This paper addresses zero-shot recognition using semantic attributes by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers when training unseen-class models. The pipeline is: (1) train one SVM per attribute on attribute-labeled images; (2) train a random forest per unseen class using the class-attribute signatures as training “exemplars”. The core idea is to incorporate per-attribute error characteristics when choosing tree splits: a held-out validation set of attribute-labeled images is propagated through the growing tree to estimate node-specific ROC operating points (TPR/FPR) for each attribute threshold. Class signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes according to these ROC statistics, yielding an information gain computed over soft counts that reflect expected test-time errors. The approach is extended to handle uncertainty in class signatures and to a few-shot setting by combining a signature-driven information gain with the standard data-driven gain using a mixing weight.

The paper relates to prior zero-shot work in which attributes serve as a semantic intermediary (e.g., binary/signature matching, and DAP’s probabilistic treatment) as well as to decision-tree variants handling missing or probabilistic features (e.g., fuzzy decision trees, fractional propagation). Compared to DAP and similar two-stage methods, the novelty lies in learning the second-stage classifier while explicitly accounting for attribute error tendencies and conditioning those estimates on the decisions made higher in the tree. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF, as well as ablations and controlled-noise studies. A few-shot extension demonstrates gains when mixing the signature prior and limited labeled images.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The random forest training objective using soft signature propagation and node-conditioned ROC estimates is well motivated and carefully described. The use of a held-out attribute-labeled set to estimate TPR/FPR at each node is reasonable and distinguishes this from simply trusting Platt probabilities. The few-shot extension is straightforward.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are appropriate (three datasets, named and discovered attributes, controlled noise studies, ablations). Gains over strong baselines (DAP) are consistent, though often modest, and largest where attributes are less reliable. The ablations isolating signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation are informative.
- Concerns: The method can be data-hungry for validation because the tree recursively partitions DV; the fallback heuristic to global DV when fewer than 10 positives/negatives reach a node may bias node-specific error estimates. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to the size/composition of DV, nor analyze how deep-node sparsity affects reliability. The few-shot formulation (a convex combination of two information gains) feels ad hoc; alternative probabilistic combinations or priors at the leaf/posterior level could be more principled.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and the related work discussion is thorough. The technical sections are easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Temper the claim of being the first to handle “unreliability”; clarify how this differs from probabilistic attribute models (e.g., DAP’s calibrated attribute posteriors) and from decision-tree methods that propagate uncertainty. Emphasize the node-conditioned ROC estimation and joint-error conditioning as the distinctive aspects.
- Provide more details on the sampling of thresholds and attributes per node, and report sensitivity to (#m, #t), tree depth, and λ.
- Justify the DV fallback heuristic and report ablations varying the “<10” threshold.

Originality
Novelty is moderate. Modeling attribute error characteristics is not new per se—DAP already leverages calibrated probabilities; fuzzy decision trees and fractional propagation have long handled uncertain features. The key originality here is applying node-specific ROC-based fractional propagation to zero-shot training on class signatures, leveraging validation subsets to approximate joint attribute error dependencies along different paths. That combination is interesting, but the claim of uniquely addressing unreliability is overstated. It may help to reframe the contribution as improved modeling of attribute noise and correlations during second-stage learning; there is related work on calibration and open-set/robust recognition (e.g., Scheirer et al., CVPR 2013) worth citing and contrasting.

Significance
The study advances zero-/few-shot recognition by delivering consistent improvements over a widely used baseline with a relatively simple, scalable modification. The impact is incremental rather than transformative: gains are often a few points and rely on a reasonably large validation set. The idea may interest practitioners building attribute-based systems and could generalize to other semantic mid-levels. The few-shot extension is promising but underdeveloped.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated method that directly encodes attribute error tendencies via node-conditioned ROC estimates.
- Elegant use of fractional propagation to approximate expected test-time behavior; avoids naïvely trusting attribute outputs.
- Thorough experiments across three datasets, including ablations and controlled-noise analyses.
- Works with both named and discovered attributes; consistent gains over DAP.
- Simple extension to few-shot that yields additional improvements.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is modest; overlap with probabilistic attributes and prior decision-tree treatments of uncertainty is substantial; claims should be tempered.
- Dependence on a sizable validation set; potential sparsity at deeper nodes; heuristic fallback lacks strong justification or analysis.
- Few-shot formulation is somewhat ad hoc; alternatives could be more principled.
- Improvements, while consistent, are not always decisive; limited analysis of computational cost and scalability to many unseen classes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reframe the contribution to emphasize node-conditioned ROC estimation and joint error conditioning, and cite closely related robustness/calibration work (e.g., open-set recognition and calibrated probabilities).
- Analyze sensitivity to the size/composition of DV and to the node-sparsity threshold; consider Bayesian smoothing of ROC estimates or hierarchical sharing across nodes.
- Compare against additional strong zero-shot baselines that model dependencies (e.g., label/feature embeddings) on the same splits; report top-k metrics.
- Explore more principled few-shot combinations (e.g., priors over leaf distributions, posterior-level mixing) and provide a deeper analysis of λ selection.
- Report training/testing time and memory, and discuss scalability to larger K and M.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, technically sound method with an intuitive mechanism for modeling attribute error.
- Consistent improvements over a strong baseline on multiple datasets with ablations.
- Practical and generalizable idea for zero-/few-shot recognition with semantic mid-levels.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental originality; overlap with existing treatments of uncertainty and calibration.
- Reliance on validation data with limited analysis of robustness to sparsity; heuristic fixes.
- Few-shot extension underdeveloped; improvements modest in places.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-presented incremental contribution to zero-/few-shot learning that improves attribute-based zero-shot classifiers by conditioning on attribute error tendencies within a random forest. I find it technically sound and useful, but the novelty and empirical margin are moderate, and some claims should be tempered. Borderline positive.
