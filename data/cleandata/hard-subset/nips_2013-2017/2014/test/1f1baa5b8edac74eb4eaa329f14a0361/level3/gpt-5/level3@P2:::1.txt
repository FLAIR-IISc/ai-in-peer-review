Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a central practical weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute detectors are imperfect, and their errors harm the second-stage class prediction. The authors propose a random forest (RF) framework that is trained not on images but on class-attribute signatures, while explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. At each split, class signatures are fractionally propagated down both branches according to node-specific estimates of each attribute detector’s ROC (TPR/FPR) obtained from a held-out attribute-labeled validation set that is recursively filtered along the tree. The split criterion is a modified information gain that accounts for the fractional presence of signatures at child nodes. The method naturally extends to (i) uncertain class signatures and (ii) few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with standard image-based information gain via a mixing parameter.

Relative to standard two-stage ZSL methods such as DAP [8,9] and signature matching [3,18,30], the key novelty is to incorporate attribute unreliability at training time when constructing the unseen-class decision rules, rather than treating attribute predictions at face value or only modeling per-image confidence at test time. The approach is also related to decision trees with missing/probabilistic features [21,28,16], but adapted to the ZSL setting where training “instances” are class signatures and test inputs are attribute predictions. It complements alternative zero-shot strategies based on label/feature embeddings or hierarchies [1,4,14,23], while preserving direct semantic control via attributes.

Strengths
- Clear and elegant formulation: fractional propagation guided by node-specific ROC curves gives an intuitive and principled way to “pre-compensate” for attribute detector errors.
- Strong empirical evidence: three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), comprehensive comparisons to DAP and other published results, ablations (signature uncertainty, ROC propagation), and controlled noise experiments isolating the core effect.
- Practical relevance: handles both zero-shot and few-shot regimes; the prior from signatures materially helps in few-shot even with hundreds of labeled examples.
- Careful experimental protocol: 20 trials, standard features/splits, validation for ROCs, sensitivity to λ for few-shot, and analysis of scenarios with varying attribute reliability.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data: The method assumes ROCs estimated on seen classes transfer to unseen classes. While the authors discuss this and report an upper bound with “OURS+TRUE ROC,” some additional analysis of domain shift would help (e.g., stratifying DV by similarity of signatures to unseen classes, or reporting performance as a function of overlap in attribute co-occurrences).
- Node-level ROC estimation stability: When few validation samples reach deeper nodes, the method reverts to global DV. Please report how often this fallback is triggered, its impact on accuracy, and any sensitivity to the 10 positives/negatives threshold.
- Scalability and multi-class training: The current one-vs-all forests per unseen class are trained for up to K=12. How does training/test time scale for larger K (e.g., 100 unseen classes)? A brief complexity analysis and discussion of multi-label trees (noted in the conclusion) would be useful.
- Clarity: The role of the threshold t in Sec. 3.2.1 is initially confusing because with binary signatures any 0<t<1 is equivalent until unreliability is introduced. A forward pointer there to Sec. 3.2.2 would avoid confusion. Also, the signature-uncertainty model is deferred to the supplement; a concise inline description would improve readability.
- Comparisons beyond DAP: The paper does include broader comparisons on AwA (including discovered attributes); if space allows, a brief report against label/feature embedding baselines [1,4] on at least one dataset would strengthen the message, even if supervision differs.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and well supported. The modified information gain and fractional propagation are correct and motivated. Experiments are thorough: real-data ZSL gains over DAP are consistent, particularly where attributes are less reliable (aPY, SUN). Controlled noise studies convincingly isolate the value of modeling detector errors. Few-shot results demonstrate a sensible λ trend and cross-validated selection. Minor concerns remain about ROC transfer across domains and deep-node statistics, as noted above.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The method is explained cleanly; figures/tables (as described) align with claims. Minor clarity issues: early explanation of t, and moving some details on signature uncertainty from the supplement into the main text.
- Originality: A novel and appropriate adaptation of random forests to ZSL using per-node ROC-based fractional propagation. While components relate to prior work on uncertain decision trees, the zero-shot training on signatures plus node-wise ROC conditioning and the few-shot combination are new and well justified.
- Significance: Addresses a fundamental blocker for attribute-based ZSL. The improvements over widely used DAP are substantial and consistent across datasets, making the approach likely to influence subsequent ZSL methods and other tasks using semantic mid-level concepts. The few-shot extension broadens applicability.

Questions for the authors
1. How frequently does the DV(n)→DV fallback occur, and how sensitive are results to the 10-sample threshold?
2. Can you report training/test time and memory as a function of M and K? Any tricks to reduce per-class forests (e.g., shared trees with multi-label leaves)?
3. Did you try weighting attributes by calibration quality (e.g., AUC) when sampling candidate splits?
4. How sensitive are results to the proportion of data reserved for DV (20%)?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and practically important solution to attribute unreliability in ZSL.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains on three benchmarks, with careful ablations and analyses.
- Extensible to few-shot learning with a simple, effective prior mechanism.
- Relevant and timely for NIPS topics in transfer learning, recognition, and structured prediction.

Cons for acceptance
- Relies on the availability and representativeness of attribute-labeled validation data; transfer to unseen classes may vary with domain shift.
- Limited discussion of scalability to many unseen classes and very deep trees.
- Some details (signature uncertainty) deferred to the supplement; minor clarity issues around split thresholds.

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-executed contribution that advances attribute-based zero-/few-shot learning by addressing a key practical obstacle. I recommend acceptance.
