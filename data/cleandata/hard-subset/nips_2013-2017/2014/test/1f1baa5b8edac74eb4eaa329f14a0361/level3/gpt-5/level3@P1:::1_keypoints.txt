- Paper addresses attribute-based ZSL problem by explicitly modeling unreliable attribute detectors during training.  
- Proposes a random forest framework trained on class-attribute signatures with per-node ROC-driven fractional propagation.  
- Fractional propagation uses node-specific TP/FP rates to route class signatures through candidate splits reflecting expected attribute errors.  
- Also models class-signature uncertainty to account for annotation noise at the class level.  
- Extends to few-shot learning by combining signature-driven and labeled-image information gains via a weighting parameter λ.  
- Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest.  
- Ablations and controlled noise tests demonstrate the benefit of ROC-based propagation and signature uncertainty modeling.  
- Method transfers to discovered (non-semantic) attributes and improves few-shot performance.  
- Novelty: adapting random forests to train on class signatures with per-node ROC statistics is original and well-suited to ZSL.  
- Quality: technically sound objective maximizing info gain under fractional, ROC-driven propagation.  
- Few-shot extension is principled as a convex combination of information gains with interpretable λ.  
- Empirical evaluation is strong with multiple datasets, baselines, and stress tests.  
- Writing is overall clear; distinction between basic and reliability-aware RF is well explained.  
- Suggest promoting a schematic/pseudocode from the supplement to the main paper for clarity on fractional propagation.  
- Main concern: potential optimistic ROC estimates because validation data used for ROC may later train attribute classifiers.  
- Recommend strict hold-out or cross-validated ROC estimation to avoid bias.  
- Concern about computational cost and scalability of per-node ROC estimation as attribute count and classes grow.  
- Suggest reporting runtime/memory profiles and complexity as a function of attributes, thresholds, and unseen classes.  
- Attribute independence is implicitly assumed in split selection; modeling correlations could improve performance.  
- Suggest exploring simple correlation-aware splits (e.g., 2D/oblique trees) or grouped attributes.  
- Ask for details on sampling (#m, #t) per node, its cost/accuracy tradeoff, and complexity discussion.  
- In few-shot, request clarification on how λ is selected and whether a data-driven schedule could replace cross-validation.  
- Overall assessment: elegant, well-executed contribution addressing a key practical ZSL weakness; recommend acceptance after minor clarifications.
