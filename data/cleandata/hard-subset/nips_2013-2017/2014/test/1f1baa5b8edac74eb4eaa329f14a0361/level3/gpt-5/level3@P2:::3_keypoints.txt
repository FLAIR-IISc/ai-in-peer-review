- Method: train one SVM per attribute and a random forest per unseen class using class-attribute signatures, incorporating per-attribute error by node-specific ROC estimates from a held-out validation set and fractionally propagating signatures to compute information gain; few-shot extension mixes signature-driven and data-driven gains.

- Technical soundness: training objective with soft signature propagation and node-conditioned ROC estimates is well motivated and carefully described; held-out validation for TPR/FPR is reasonable.

- Empirical evaluation: experiments on AwA, aPY, SUN (named and discovered attributes), ablations, and controlled-noise studies show consistent but often modest gains over DAP and signature-only RF, largest when attributes are unreliable.

- Core novelty: applying node-specific ROC-based fractional propagation to zero-shot training on class signatures and conditioning errors along tree paths; originality is moderate given related probabilistic attribute and fuzzy-tree work.

- Main concern 1: requires a sizable validation set—deep-node sparsity can bias ROC estimates and the fallback heuristic (<10 samples) may distort node-specific errors.

- Main concern 2: sensitivity to size/composition of the validation set and to deep-node sparsity is not quantified or analyzed.

- Few-shot critique: convex combination of two information gains is ad hoc; more principled posterior/prior-level combinations would be preferable.

- Clarity: paper is clear and well organized; should temper claims of being first to handle “unreliability” and better differentiate from calibrated attribute and uncertain-feature decision-tree literature.

- Reporting gaps: need details on sampling of thresholds/attributes per node, sensitivity to (#m, #t), tree depth, λ, and justification/ablation of the DV fallback threshold.

- Originality nuance: contribution is better framed as improved modeling of attribute noise and correlations during second-stage learning, and should cite calibration/open-set robustness literature.

- Significance: practical, incremental advance for attribute-based zero-/few-shot recognition; useful to practitioners but not transformative due to reliance on validation data and modest margins.

- Strengths: direct encoding of attribute error via node-conditioned ROC, elegant fractional propagation approximating test-time behavior, thorough experiments, works with both named and discovered attributes, simple few-shot extension that yields improvements.

- Weaknesses: modest novelty, dependence on validation data and heuristic fixes for sparsity, underdeveloped few-shot formulation, limited runtime/scalability analysis.

- Suggested improvements: analyze DV sensitivity and fallback threshold, consider Bayesian smoothing or hierarchical sharing for ROC estimates, compare to more dependency-modeling baselines, explore principled few-shot combinations, and report training/testing time and memory.

- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, intuitive mechanism for modeling attribute error, consistent empirical improvements with informative ablations.

- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty with overlap to prior uncertainty treatments, reliance on validation data with limited robustness analysis, underdeveloped few-shot approach and modest gains.

- Overall assessment: solid, well-presented incremental contribution to attribute-based zero-/few-shot learning—technically sound and useful but with moderate novelty and empirical margins; borderline positive.
