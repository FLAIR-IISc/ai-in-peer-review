

This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling attribute classifier uncertainty through validation-measured TPR/FPR statistics during random forest training. The authors recognize that existing zero-shot methods treat attribute predictions as reliable, while in practice, attribute classifiers are often error-prone. Their key insight is to incorporate the receiver operating characteristics of attribute classifiers into the information gain calculation during tree learning, allowing the model to prefer more reliable attributes and operating points.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across three datasets, showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods like DAP. The authors effectively address the core challenge of attribute unreliability, which has been overlooked in previous work. The method's extension to few-shot learning is also well-motivated and effective. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to readers.

Strengths include strong technical quality with well-supported empirical results, clear exposition, and a novel perspective on modeling attribute reliability (distinct from attribute strength). The significance is high, as it demonstrates that accounting for attribute unreliability is more valuable than simply improving attribute classifiers. The approach could benefit other semantic-based learning scenarios beyond zero-shot recognition.

Weaknesses include the lack of qualitative analysis showing which specific attributes and categories benefit most from the method, which would provide deeper insight into the model's behavior. The rationale for the 80/20 train-validation split is unclear, and the paper doesn't explain how hyperparameters were selected or validated. While the random forest component itself is not novel, the specific application to zero-shot learning with uncertainty modeling is innovative.

The novelty is clear and well-justified against related work, particularly in distinguishing attribute reliability from attribute strength. The significance is demonstrated through substantial performance gains across multiple datasets. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a practical limitation of zero-shot learning that has been largely ignored.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable in revision. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the hyperparameter selection rationale and add qualitative analysis.
