- Addresses the practical problem of unreliable attribute classifiers in attribute-based zero-shot learning.  
- Central idea: train a zero-shot model that is explicitly aware of attribute predictor error tendencies.  
- Method: novel random forest where class attribute signatures are fractionally propagated down trees using attribute true/false positive rates.  
- Error statistics are estimated on a held-out validation set of attribute-labeled images.  
- Encourages splits based on attributes that are both discriminative for unseen classes and reliably predictable at test time.  
- Extensions: handles uncertainty in class-attribute signatures and incorporates few-shot labeled examples.  
- Positioned within two-stage attribute-based ZSL, but distinct in explicitly modeling classifier unreliability rather than assuming reliable attributes or modeling attribute strength.  
- Related to methods for handling uncertain/missing data in trees, but novel in propagating idealized signatures using classifier error stats for ZSL.  
- Quality: technically sound; principled modification to information gain (Eq. 5) and well-supported by experiments.  
- Experiments: controlled noise studies and main results on AwA, aPY, SUN show significant, consistent improvements over DAP.  
- Ablation studies effectively isolate contributions (signature uncertainty, recursive ROC propagation).  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and explanation of differences between unreliability and strength.  
- Originality: novel contribution in explicitly modeling attribute classifier unreliability during training.  
- Significance: practical impact by improving attribute-based ZSL and potential applicability to other classifier-chaining problems; few-shot extension increases applicability.  
- Weakness: lacks qualitative analysis showing which attributes the model trusts or ignores and which classes benefit most.  
- Weakness: key hyperparameters (e.g., 80/20 split for attribute training/ROC estimation) lack justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: scalability concern—training a separate one-vs-all forest per unseen class may be computationally expensive for many classes.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important problem, novel and principled method, clear writing, strong empirical gains and thorough evaluation.  
- Rejection arguments: mainly missing qualitative insights and some hyperparameter justification, rather than fundamental flaws.  
- Overall recommendation: high-quality work with strengths outweighing weaknesses; reviewer recommends acceptance.
