This paper proposes a zero-shot framework that first trains SVM attribute classifiers, then learns class discriminants as random forests built over class–attribute signature vectors. The key idea is to account for attribute unreliability during tree construction by estimating ROC-based true/false positive rates on a held-out, attribute-labeled validation set and propagating both signatures and validation data down the tree. This propagation captures joint error tendencies across attributes at deeper nodes. The paper also sketches a few-shot extension that interpolates information gain from signatures and from a small set of class-labeled images.

Relation to prior work: The two-stage formulation (attributes → class) follows DAP and related methods (e.g., Farhadi et al.; Lampert et al.; Palatucci et al.). While the paper claims to uniquely handle unreliable attributes, prior work already trains attribute classifiers and then a second-stage class estimator that uses validation/calibration (e.g., DAP with Platt scaling). The technical novelty here is the use of ROC-based, node-specific reliability estimates and the induced modeling of joint attribute-noise correlations via recursive propagation; this is a meaningful refinement. The framing would benefit from positioning as improved modeling of attribute error correlations/noise (cf. uncertainty handling in decision trees, missing/soft features, and related robustness literature such as Scheirer et al. on reliability/open-set concerns).

Quality: Technically sound and carefully executed. The validation-data-driven splitting criterion is well-motivated and correctly implemented; experiments on three datasets are appropriate. However, gains are generally modest, and the validation-data requirement is potentially heavy, since data is subdivided across nodes; the fallback to global ROC statistics (line 323) is heuristic and insufficiently justified.

Clarity: Very clear, well written, and comprehensive in summarizing related work. The method is easy to follow.

Originality: Incremental. The main mechanism (ROC-driven, node-local reliability integrated into RF training) is new within zero-shot trees, but the overall pipeline and claim of uniquely handling unreliability are overstated.

Significance: Moderate. The approach is practical and likely to be adopted by researchers working with attribute-based ZSL, but the empirical improvements are not decisive by themselves.

Pros:
- Clear paper; solid, technically sound method
- Sensible ROC-based reliability modeling; captures joint error statistics
- Broad and appropriate experiments

Cons:
- Main novelty is modest and claims are overstated relative to DAP-like baselines
- Requires substantial validation data; heuristic fallback lacks analysis
- Few-shot section is brief; Eq. 6 weighting is less intuitive than alternatives (e.g., combining class–attribute priors with image-level attribute posteriors)

Overall: A well-presented, careful incremental contribution; recommend acceptance only if room permits for solid but modest advances.
