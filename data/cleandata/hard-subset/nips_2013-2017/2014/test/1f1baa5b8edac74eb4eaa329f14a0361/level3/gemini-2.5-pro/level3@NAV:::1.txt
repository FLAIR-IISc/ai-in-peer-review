This paper addresses a crucial and often-overlooked problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of the underlying attribute classifiers. The authors propose a novel method to bridge the gap between ZSL theory, which assumes perfect attribute information, and practice, where attribute predictions are noisy. The core contribution is a random forest framework trained not on images, but on the attribute signatures of unseen classes. Crucially, the model accounts for attribute detector fallibility by propagating an attribute-labeled validation set through the trees during training. This allows each split to be chosen based not only on its discriminative power but also on its expected reliability, using node-specific ROC statistics. This approach is in contrast to prior leading methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP), which often use attribute predictions at face value. The authors also show a natural extension to the few-shot setting.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem that is a major bottleneck for the practical application of attribute-based ZSL. By making the ZSL pipeline more robust to real-world imperfections, this work significantly advances the field.
*   **Quality & Originality:** The proposed model is elegant, theoretically sound, and a novel application of the random forest framework. The idea of using a validation set to estimate attribute reliability during tree construction is a clever and effective mechanism. The quality of the work is further demonstrated by an exceptionally strong experimental protocol, including thorough ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, and state-of-the-art results on three standard datasets.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete contribution, addressing the problem from formulation to extensive evaluation and also providing a valuable extension to few-shot learning.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized overall. A minor point of confusion exists in Section 3.2.1, where the role and selection of the threshold `t` for binary signatures is not immediately clear. However, this is largely resolved in the subsequent section when the main contribution is introduced, so it is a minor issue that could be easily clarified.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-executed, and effective solution to an important problem in zero-shot learning. The claims are strongly supported by rigorous experiments. The work is a clear contribution to the field and is highly suitable for publication.
