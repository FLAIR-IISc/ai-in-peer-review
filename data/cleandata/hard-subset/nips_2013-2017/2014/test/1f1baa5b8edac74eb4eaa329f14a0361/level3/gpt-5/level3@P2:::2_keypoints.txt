- Proposes a random-forest framework for zero-shot learning that incorporates attribute classifier unreliability via validation-measured ROC stats during training.  
- Trains one-vs-all random forests on class attribute signatures and fractionally propagates signatures using attribute TPR/FPR to modify information gain.  
- Extends the model to handle class-signature uncertainty and a few-shot setting by mixing signature-driven and data-driven information gain.  
- Shows clear improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline on AwA, aPY, and SUN, with robustness to attribute noise and strong few-shot performance.  
- Controlled-noise experiments demonstrate robustness, especially when noise varies by attribute.  
- Formulation is well-motivated and technically sound; fractional propagation and modified information gain are principled.  
- Empirical support is strong: multi-dataset results, ablations, controlled-noise analyses, and few-shot experiments.  
- Missing analyses: qualitative insight into which attributes/classes drive gains and visualization of selected splits.  
- Missing analyses: sensitivity to hyperparameters and the 80/20 train–validation split, and behavior when node-specific DV(n) is small.  
- Missing analyses: runtime/complexity and scalability with larger numbers of attributes (M) and classes (K).  
- Assumes validation attribute errors represent test-time errors; domain shift could bias ROC estimates.  
- Node-specific ROC estimates may be noisy with few validation samples; fallback to global ROC needs quantification.  
- Inter-attribute correlations are only implicitly handled, not explicitly modeled.  
- Paper is clearly written; key ideas, pipeline, and math are well presented, though some implementation details are deferred to the supplement.  
- Clarification needed on 80/20 split rationale, threshold sampling for attributes, and signature-uncertainty modeling.  
- Originality: adapts known uncertain-feature tree ideas to zero-shot learning with node-specific ROC conditioning and principled fractional propagation.  
- Significance: practically important and broadly applicable; reliability-aware training is impactful despite using a standard base learner.  
- Pros: principled alignment of training with test-time noise, strong empirical results, handles signature uncertainty, and is simple/implementable.  
- Cons: limited qualitative analysis, insufficient hyperparameter/sensitivity justification, potential domain-shift sensitivity, and no computational cost discussion.  
- Suggestions: provide visualizations of top attributes/splits and per-class errors, sensitivity studies for validation size and tree hyperparameters, strategies to mitigate ROC domain shift, and runtime/memory reporting.  
- Suggestion for few-shot: analyze automatic λ selection vs. labeled-image count and compare to alternatives or DAP fine-tuning.  
- Overall recommendation: lean accept; idea is simple, well-executed, and empirically validated despite some missing analyses.  
- Reviewer confidence: medium.
