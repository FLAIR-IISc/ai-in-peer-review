Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a central weakness in attribute-based zero-shot learning: at test time, attribute classifiers are often unreliable. The authors propose a random-forest framework that explicitly incorporates validation-measured attribute unreliability when training zero-shot models. Concretely, they train one-vs-all random forests on class attribute signatures (rather than on images), but, unlike prior signature-based methods, they propagate each class signature fractionally down the tree according to the attribute classifiers’ true/false positive rates estimated from held-out attribute-labeled validation data. This modifies the information gain criterion to prefer splits that remain discriminative under expected attribute noise. They further extend the model to (i) accommodate uncertainty in class-attribute signatures and (ii) a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven and data-driven information gain via a mixing parameter.

Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN demonstrate clear improvements over DAP (Lampert et al.) and a signature-only random forest baseline. Controlled-noise experiments highlight robustness to attribute noise, especially when noise varies by attribute. The few-shot extension outperforms using the few labeled images alone across varying numbers of labeled examples.

Relative to prior zero-shot approaches that map predicted attributes to classes (e.g., DAP, nonprobabilistic signature matching, topic-model variants) or use semantic embeddings/classifier adaptation (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE, hierarchical/label-cooccurrence transfer), the novelty here is to explicitly model attribute unreliability via ROC statistics inside the learning algorithm. Related work on decision trees for uncertain/missing features and soft node memberships is acknowledged; the contribution is to adapt those ideas to zero-shot learning with signature “training data,” node-specific ROC estimation, and a principled few-shot combination.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well-motivated and precise. The fractional propagation of signatures using node-specific ROC estimates is a principled way to align the training objective with test-time noise. The modified information gain is appropriate, and the method naturally downweights unlearnable attributes.
- Empirical support: Strong. Results on three datasets with >250 attributes, ablations (removing signature uncertainty; removing node-specific ROC propagation), controlled-noise analyses, and a few-shot extension support the claims. Gains over DAP are substantial, particularly when attribute predictors are weak (aPY, SUN), consistent with the paper’s thesis.
- Missing analyses: The paper would benefit from (i) qualitative analysis of which attributes and classes drive gains (e.g., visualizations of frequently selected splits, per-attribute reliability vs. usage), (ii) sensitivity to hyperparameters and the 80/20 train–validation split (how does performance vary with validation size and with the fallback to global ROC when DV(n) is small?), and (iii) runtime/complexity and scalability with larger M and K. Statistical testing beyond mean±SE would also be useful.
- Assumptions/limitations: The approach assumes the attribute-labeled validation set is representative of test-time errors on unseen classes; when this domain shift is large, ROC estimates may be biased. Node-specific ROC estimation with few validation samples could be noisy; the fallback strategy is reasonable but its impact should be quantified. Inter-attribute correlations are only implicitly handled via node-specific conditioning, not explicitly modeled.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The high-level idea, training/testing pipeline, and mathematical definitions (fractional indicators, IGzero) are well presented. It is clear how few-shot training combines two gains via λ. Some details could be clarified:
- Rationale and sensitivity for the 80/20 split; how were (#m, #t), tree depths, and stopping criteria chosen across datasets?
- The sampling of thresholds t when attributes are binary vs. continuous, and how operating points are selected in practice.
- More explicit description of signature uncertainty modeling in the main text (currently deferred to the supplement).

Originality
While random forests and uncertain-feature trees are established, the specific use of validation-measured ROC to train zero-shot forests on signatures, with node-specific ROC conditioning and a principled fractional propagation, is original and addresses an underexplored aspect (attribute reliability vs. strength). The few-shot combination via information gain is a neat, simple extension. The conceptual contribution is clear relative to DAP/embedding methods and to prior tree-based handling of uncertainty.

Significance
The contribution is practically important for zero/few-shot recognition, where attribute unreliability is the main bottleneck. The consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple datasets suggest other researchers/practitioners could benefit. The idea is broadly applicable to other mid-level semantic pipelines (e.g., parts/poselets). While the base learner (RF) is standard, the reliability-aware training is impactful and timely.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that matches train-time objective to test-time noise.
- Strong empirical results on three datasets; controlled-noise experiments and ablations substantiate claims.
- Addresses both attribute classifier unreliability and class signature uncertainty; extends cleanly to few-shot.
- Simple, interpretable mechanism (ROC-weighted information gain), likely easy to implement.

Cons
- Limited qualitative analysis of which attributes/categories benefit and how trees behave.
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (including the 80/20 split and λ) not thoroughly justified.
- Potential sensitivity to validation domain shift and to small DV(n); fallback behavior not analyzed.
- No discussion of computational cost and scalability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide qualitative insights: list or visualize top attributes/splits selected and their estimated TPR/FPR; per-class confusion matrices; analyze per-attribute reliability vs. usage in trees.
- Add sensitivity studies for the validation split size, tree depth, number of candidate (m, t), and the minimum DV(n) threshold; report learning curves vs. validation size.
- Explore strategies to mitigate domain shift in ROC estimation (e.g., weighting validation samples toward signatures closest to unseen classes, or calibration techniques).
- Report training and inference times and memory footprint; discuss scalability to larger M and K.
- Clarify the signature uncertainty model in the main paper; include details for continuous attributes.
- For few-shot, analyze automatic selection of λ as a function of the number of labeled images; compare to alternative priors or to fine-tuning DAP.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a simple, well-executed, and empirically validated idea that directly addresses a core obstacle in attribute-based zero-shot learning. While some analyses are missing, the contribution is clear and likely to influence subsequent work.
