## Review of “Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes”

This paper proposes a random forest approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly models attribute prediction unreliability by leveraging ROC curves to select informative decision nodes and propagating validation data down the trees to capture joint attribute error statistics. The method also extends to the few-shot scenario. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains a complex idea, effectively summarizing related work in the zero-shot learning domain.

The core contribution, while technically sound, feels incremental. The notion of accounting for attribute uncertainty in zero-shot learning is not entirely novel. Prior work, particularly DAP, already addresses attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and then using a second-stage estimator (often probabilistic) on the predicted attributes. The key technical difference here lies in using ROC-based reliability estimates rather than, for example, Platt scaling, and in the propagation of validation data through the trees to better model joint attribute errors. This propagation is a clever idea and likely contributes to the observed performance gains, but the gains themselves (Table 1) are modest and not overwhelmingly decisive, especially given the added complexity. 

The framing of the contribution as uniquely handling “unreliability” is overstated. A more accurate characterization would be improved modeling of attribute correlations and noise, as suggested by the authors' supervisor. Citing related work like Scheirer et al. on handling noisy labels would further strengthen this positioning. I also share the concern about the validation data requirement – the recursive subdivision of data in the trees could necessitate a large validation set, and the proposed heuristic fix (line 323) seems insufficiently justified.

The experiments are appropriate and demonstrate the benefits of the approach, particularly under controlled noise scenarios. However, the few-shot extension (Section 3.3) feels less polished and the weighted information-gain combination (Equation 6) lacks intuitive justification compared to alternative approaches like combining class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-presented.
*   Addresses a practical challenge in zero-shot learning – the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions.
*   The propagation of validation data is a novel and potentially valuable technique.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modest novelty – similar ideas have been explored in prior work.
*   Performance gains are not substantial enough to warrant a significant contribution.
*   Validation data requirements and heuristic fix are concerns.
*   The few-shot extension is underdeveloped.



Overall, this is a solid paper that demonstrates a reasonable approach to improving zero-shot learning. However, given the limited novelty and modest gains, I lean towards **weak accept**. It’s a worthwhile contribution, but might not raise the bar significantly for this conference.
