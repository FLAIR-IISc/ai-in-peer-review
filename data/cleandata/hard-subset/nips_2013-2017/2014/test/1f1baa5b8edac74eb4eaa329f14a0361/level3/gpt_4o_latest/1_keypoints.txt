- Paper proposes a random forest-based ZSL framework that models attribute detector reliability and extends to few-shot learning, showing SOTA on AwA, aPY, and SUN.
- Strength (Quality): Technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundation, controlled noise experiments, ablations, and comparisons to strong baselines demonstrating robustness to unreliable attributes.
- Strength (Originality): Novel use of random forests and incorporation of ROC into tree construction to model attribute unreliability in ZSL.
- Strength (Significance): Addresses a critical practical gap in ZSL (noisy attributes and uncertain class-attribute associations) and scales to FSL, improving real-world applicability.
- Strength (Clarity): Generally well-written and organized with clear methodology and experiments.
- Weakness: Section 3.2.1 initially lacks clarity on the threshold t in random forest splits, which may confuse some readers.
- Weakness: Scalability to much larger numbers of unseen classes or attributes is not thoroughly discussed and may limit real-world deployment.
- Argument for acceptance: Tackles an important underexplored problem with a theoretically sound, well-validated, and novel approach that achieves strong empirical results.
- Argument for acceptance: Extends ZSL applicability to noisy and low-data scenarios, increasing practical impact.
- Argument against acceptance: Minor clarity issues in Section 3.2.1 could impede comprehension.
- Argument against acceptance: Insufficient analysis of scalability to larger datasets or more complex attribute vocabularies.
- Recommendation: Despite minor clarity and scalability concerns, the paper is a significant, high-quality contribution and should be accepted.
