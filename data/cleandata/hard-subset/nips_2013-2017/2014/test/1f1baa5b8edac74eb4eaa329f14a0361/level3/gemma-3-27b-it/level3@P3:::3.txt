This paper proposes a random forest approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The method trains SVM attribute classifiers and then builds a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors, leveraging validation data to estimate attribute reliability at each tree node. An extension to the few-shot setting is also presented. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains its approach, effectively summarizing related work in the field.

The core idea is technically sound and the experimental results, while not overwhelmingly large, support the claims made. The use of ROC-based reliability estimates, rather than simpler approaches like Platt scaling, and the propagation of validation data down the trees to capture joint attribute error statistics are clever technical contributions. The inclusion of a few-shot extension broadens the applicability of the method. 

However, the overall novelty of the approach feels modest. The claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated. Prior work, specifically Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8, 9], already addresses attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and then using a second-stage class estimator with validation data to refine predictions. The authors acknowledge DAP in the related work, but downplay its relevance by focusing on the *explicit* modeling of unreliability. While the proposed method differs in implementation details, the fundamental principle of using validation data to account for attribute error is present in DAP.

A more accurate characterization would frame this work as an *improved* modeling of attribute correlations and noise, rather than a fundamentally new way to handle unreliability. The authors should more explicitly cite and contrast their work with related research on handling noisy or uncertain features in decision trees, such as fractional tuples [21] and probabilistic feature representations [28], as they briefly mention. 

A potential concern is the requirement for a sizable validation set. The method subdivides the validation data as it propagates it down the trees, potentially requiring a significantly larger dataset than methods like DAP. While the authors propose a heuristic fix (line 323), its justification is insufficient and its impact on performance with limited validation data is not thoroughly investigated. This could limit the practical applicability of the method in scenarios where obtaining a large labeled validation set is challenging.

The experiments are appropriate and well-designed, including controlled noise experiments to validate the method's robustness to attribute prediction errors. However, the quantitative gains over baselines, particularly DAP, are modest and not always decisive. While statistically significant in some cases, the magnitude of the improvements doesn’t immediately establish a compelling advantage.

The few-shot extension, while a valuable addition, feels less polished than the zero-shot portion of the work. The weighted information-gain combination (Eq. 6) seems less intuitive than alternative approaches, such as combining class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates. The explanation of the parameter λ is clear, but its sensitivity is not deeply explored, and the automatic selection with cross-validation isn't detailed enough.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-presented.
*   Addresses an important problem in zero-shot learning – attribute unreliability.
*   Provides a novel, if incremental, improvement over existing methods.
*   Experiments support the claims, and the method demonstrates robustness to noise.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core novelty is limited, as similar approaches to handling attribute uncertainty already exist (e.g., DAP).
*   The validation data requirement could be a practical limitation.
*   Quantitative gains are modest and not always decisive.
*   The few-shot extension feels less fully developed.



**Overall:** This is a technically solid and well-written paper. However, its main selling point – a fundamentally new approach to handling unreliable attributes – is questionable given the similarities to prior work and some experimental/validation shortcomings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to: 1) More thoroughly acknowledge and contrast their work with DAP and related research on noisy features in decision trees. 2) Provide a more detailed justification for the validation data heuristic. 3) Investigate the impact of limited validation data on performance. 4) Expand the discussion of the few-shot extension, providing more intuition and analysis of the parameter λ.
