- Proposes a random-forest zero-shot method that models attribute prediction unreliability using ROC curves and propagates validation data through trees; extends to few-shot.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and explains the idea clearly, with a good related work summary.
- Core contribution is technically sound but feels incremental given prior work addressing attribute uncertainty (e.g., DAP).
- Key technical novelties are ROC-based reliability estimates and propagation of validation data to capture joint attribute errors.
- Propagating validation data is a clever idea and likely aids performance, but empirical gains are modest (Table 1).
- Claim of uniquely handling “unreliability” is overstated; better characterized as improved modeling of attribute correlations and noise.
- Should cite related work on noisy labels (e.g., Scheirer et al.) to strengthen positioning.
- Validation-data requirement is a concern: recursive subdivision may need a large validation set and the proposed heuristic fix is not well justified.
- Experiments are appropriate and show benefits, particularly under controlled noise scenarios.
- Few-shot extension is underdeveloped; the weighted information-gain formulation lacks intuitive justification versus alternatives.
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, well-presented, addresses practical attribute unreliability, and introduces a novel propagation technique.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, modest performance improvements, validation-data and heuristic concerns, and weak few-shot treatment.
- Overall assessment: solid paper deserving a weak accept—worthwhile but not a conference-defining advance.
