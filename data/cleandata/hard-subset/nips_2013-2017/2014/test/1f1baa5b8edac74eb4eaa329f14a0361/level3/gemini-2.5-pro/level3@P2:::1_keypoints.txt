- Problem: Attribute classifier unreliability undermines practical zero-shot learning by making predicted attributes noisy and degrading performance of methods like DAP.  
- Proposal: Train random forests discriminatively on unseen-class attribute signatures while modeling attribute prediction noise.  
- Key innovation: Use a validation set to estimate each attribute classifier's ROC and fractionally propagate signatures down tree splits according to TPR/FPR.  
- Effect: Builds expected test-time noise into tree structure, favoring splits robust to attribute prediction errors.  
- Extensions: Framework handles uncertainty in attribute signatures and a few-shot learning setting.  
- Significance: Addresses a central ZSL bottleneck with a principled, likely influential solution.  
- Originality: Novel application of fractional propagation guided by per-attribute ROC statistics in ZSL.  
- Experimental strength: Strong controlled-noise experiments and results on AwA, aPY, SUN showing consistent, significant improvements over DAP.  
- Ablations: Studies isolate contributions of signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation.  
- State-of-the-art: Achieves SOTA on AwA, competitive with methods using discovered attributes; few-shot extension shows clear benefits.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and figures/tables support claims.  
- Weakness — exposition: Section 3.2.1 initial description of threshold t is slightly confusing and could use a brief forward-reference.  
- Weakness — validation dependence: Method depends on a representative attribute-labeled validation set; sensitivity to DV size/composition is not fully explored.  
- Weakness — scalability: One-vs-all framework requires K forests for K classes, raising computational concerns for large target sets.  
- Recommendation: Minor weaknesses only; strong acceptance recommendation based on novelty, solid experiments, and clear presentation.
