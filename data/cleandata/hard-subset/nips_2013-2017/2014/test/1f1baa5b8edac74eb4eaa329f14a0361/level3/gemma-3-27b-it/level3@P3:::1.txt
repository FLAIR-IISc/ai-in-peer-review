This paper addresses a crucial bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly models and accounts for this unreliability during training, leading to significant performance gains on three standard datasets. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented, representing a strong contribution to the field.

The core idea of propagating validation data through the decision trees to estimate attribute error tendencies is elegant and effective. Existing zero-shot methods largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is a limiting assumption. By acknowledging and mitigating this issue, the authors demonstrate a substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art DAP method, particularly in scenarios with less reliable attributes – as evidenced by the controlled noise experiments. The extension to the few-shot setting is a natural and valuable addition, further broadening the applicability of the method.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded in the random forest framework and builds upon existing techniques (e.g., fractional tuples) in a novel and effective way. The theoretical justification for incorporating attribute reliability is clear.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results consistently outperform baselines, including the widely used DAP method, across multiple datasets and settings (zero-shot and few-shot). The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components of the proposed approach. The controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's robustness to attribute prediction errors.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The explanations of the core concepts and the experimental setup are clear and concise.  The inclusion of pseudocode and a schematic illustration (mentioned in the text) would further enhance clarity. The clarification regarding the threshold *t* in Section 3.2.1 is appreciated.
* **Originality:** Applying a random forest framework to explicitly address attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning is a novel and insightful approach. While random forests are a well-established technique, their adaptation to this specific problem demonstrates a creative and valuable application.
* **Significance:** The problem of attribute unreliability is a fundamental challenge in zero-shot learning. Addressing this issue has the potential to significantly improve the practicality and applicability of this learning paradigm. The demonstrated performance gains and the potential for broader impact make this work highly significant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, propagating validation data through the trees likely increases the computational complexity of training compared to standard random forests. A discussion of the scalability of the method to larger datasets and more complex attribute vocabularies would be beneficial.
* **Validation Data Dependency:** The performance relies on having a representative validation set to accurately estimate attribute error tendencies.  The impact of the size and quality of this validation set could be further investigated.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the ablation studies are helpful, a more in-depth analysis of the types of attributes or categories where the method performs poorly could provide valuable insights for future improvement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical bottleneck in zero-shot learning.
* Demonstrates significant and consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Clear and well-written paper with comprehensive experimental evaluation.
* Novel application of a random forest framework to a challenging problem.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity might limit scalability to very large datasets.
* Reliance on a representative validation set for accurate error estimation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors have identified a key limitation of existing methods and proposed a novel and effective solution. The thorough experimental evaluation and clear presentation further strengthen the paper’s impact.  I strongly recommend acceptance.
