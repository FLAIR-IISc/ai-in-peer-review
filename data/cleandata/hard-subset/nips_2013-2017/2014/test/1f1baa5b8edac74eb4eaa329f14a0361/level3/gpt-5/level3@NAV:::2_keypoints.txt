- Proposes zero-shot random forests trained on class-attribute signatures that explicitly model attribute unreliability using validation-measured TPR/FPR per attribute and threshold.
- Key technical idea: fractionally propagate signatures during tree splits according to expected test-time ROC behavior to bias toward robust, learnable attributes.
- Extends to handle uncertain signatures and few-shot learning by mixing signature-based and image-based information gains.
- Contrasts with DAP/two-stage methods that assume perfect attribute predictions and with embedding approaches that ignore per-attribute reliability.
- Technically sound and well-motivated; node-specific ROC propagation is a principled way to prefer reliable attributes.
- Empirical results on AwA, aPY, and SUN are strong with ablations isolating ROC propagation and signature uncertainty benefits.
- Hyperparameter selection (80/20 split, number of (m,t) candidates, tree depth) is under-specified and sensitivity analyses are limited.
- ROC estimation uses a held-out 20% then retrains on 100%, creating a potential mismatch that should be clarified or quantified.
- Paper is very clear and well written; suggest adding a main-text algorithm box to aid reproducibility.
- Originality is moderate: applying random forests is standard, but modeling attribute reliability within tree learning is a novel angle for zero-shot.
- Significance is good: simple idea with practical impact and gains over strong baselines; broadly useful when semantic detectors are noisy.
- Needs more qualitative analysis: show top-level attributes, per-category effects, per-attribute selection frequencies, and confusion matrices.
- Should clarify and justify hyperparameters, provide sensitivity curves, and report runtime.
- Discuss potential calibration drift between seen-class validation and unseen-class test (domain shift mitigation).
- Recommend comparisons to recent label/feature embedding baselines and a DAP variant with calibrated per-attribute operating points.
- Pros: clear modeling of attribute unreliability, strong empirical support and ablations, applicable to zero- and few-shot settings.
- Cons: limited qualitative insight, unclear hyperparameter rationale and sensitivity, and unaddressed ROC–classifier mismatch.
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
