- Problem addressed: zero-shot learning with unreliable attribute predictions using a random forest that accounts for attribute prediction errors via ROC statistics.  
- Method summary: train SVM attribute classifiers, build random forest from class-attribute signatures, propagate validation data through trees to estimate per-node reliability.  
- Extension: method is extended to few-shot learning using a weighted information-gain combination.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on two-stage zero-shot (DAP); claim of novelty is overstated because prior methods also use validation data to model attribute uncertainty.  
- Technical novelty: meaningful refinement by using ROC-based reliability estimates and propagating validation data to capture joint attribute error statistics.  
- Strength: clarity — paper is exceptionally well written with clear explanations and related work summaries.  
- Strength: technical quality — sound approach with thorough experiments on three standard datasets.  
- Strength: significance — demonstrates clear improvements over DAP, especially on datasets/classes with less reliable attribute classifiers.  
- Weakness: originality — core contribution is modest and should be framed as an improved modeling of attribute noise/correlations.  
- Weakness: validation-data requirement — needs substantial validation data as trees split, and the proposed heuristic is insufficiently justified or analyzed.  
- Weakness: few-shot extension — brief, less polished, and the weighted gain formulation feels arbitrary compared to more intuitive alternatives.  
- Weakness: quantitative gains are modest (e.g., 7–10% on some classes) and may not justify added complexity.  
- Pro acceptance: well-motivated, technically sound, integrates ROC statistics effectively, and shows practical benefits on standard benchmarks.  
- Con acceptance: novelty overstated, unclear scalability/practicality due to validation needs, modest gains, and underdeveloped few-shot component.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound incremental contribution; recommend acceptance conditional on reframing novelty, citing related work (e.g., Scheirer et al.), justifying validation-data needs, and improving the few-shot part.
