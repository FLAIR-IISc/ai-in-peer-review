- Addresses a key limitation of zero-shot learning: attribute prediction errors make the usual assumption of reliable attributes unrealistic.  
- Proposes a random forest method that explicitly models attribute unreliability by incorporating ROC statistics during tree construction.  
- Uses validation data to estimate true positive and false positive rates, and uses these to compute information gain for split selection.  
- Distinguishes attribute strength (presence) from attribute reliability (predictability) and explicitly models the latter.  
- Extends the approach to handle few-shot learning and signature uncertainty.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on AwA, aPY, and SUN, outperforming DAP and SIGNATURE RF, especially where attribute classifiers are less reliable.  
- Ablation studies show both recursive propagation of node-specific ROC stats and modeling signature uncertainty contribute to performance.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with effective figures and helpful pseudocode.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis identifying which specific attributes or categories benefit most from the method.  
- Unclear justification and sensitivity analysis for the chosen 80/20 train-validation split.  
- Method requires attribute validation data, which may not always be available in practice.  
- The adaptation of random forests is novel and significant, though the base random forest algorithm itself is not new.  
- Argues convincingly that modeling attribute unreliability can matter more than improving attribute classifiers; high practical significance.  
- Reviewer recommendation: accept.
