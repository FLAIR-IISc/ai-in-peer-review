This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning (ZSL) that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The core idea is to augment a random forest framework by incorporating validation-measured performance metrics (TPR/FPR) into the information gain calculation during tree learning. This allows the model to favor attributes that are not only discriminative but also reliably predictable. This work builds upon a well-established line of research in attribute-based ZSL (e.g., Lampert et al., 2009; Farhadi et al., 2009) but distinguishes itself through its novel focus. While prior work often takes attribute predictions at face value or models attribute strength, this paper's originality lies in explicitly modeling attribute *reliability*, a less-explored but critical aspect of the ZSL problem.

The paper's primary strengths are its clarity, technical quality, and strong empirical support. The manuscript is very well-written and easy to follow, clearly motivating the problem and detailing the proposed solution. The claims are substantiated by comprehensive experiments on three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), where the proposed method consistently and significantly outperforms the widely-used DAP baseline and other state-of-the-art approaches. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of the model, supporting the quality of the research.

The main weakness of the paper is a lack of deeper analysis. While the quantitative results are compelling, the work would be strengthened by a qualitative analysis showing which specific attributes and categories benefit most from the proposed uncertainty modeling. This would provide valuable insight into the method's practical behavior. Furthermore, the paper could improve its clarity regarding methodological choices. For example, the rationale for key hyperparameters, such as the 80/20 train-validation split, is not discussed. Justifying these choices would improve the paper's completeness and reproducibility.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution. The significance of the work lies in its pragmatic approach to a key limitation in attribute-based ZSL. The idea of leveraging validation-measured error statistics to build more robust zero-shot models is both interesting and likely to be impactful for researchers in this area. Despite the minor need for more qualitative analysis and hyperparameter justification, the paper presents a complete and well-supported piece of research that demonstrably advances the state of the art.
