- Addresses zero-shot learning by training a random forest on class attribute signatures rather than images, explicitly modeling attribute classifier unreliability.  
- Key idea: use attribute-labeled validation images to estimate per-attribute ROC stats and fractionally propagate signatures down trees to be robust to prediction errors.  
- Extends method to handle uncertainty in class signatures and a few-shot learning scenario.  
- Builds on the two-stage zero-shot paradigm (e.g., DAP) but proposes a more sophisticated second-stage model that incorporates attribute error statistics.  
- Strength: paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow.  
- Strength: technically clever approach—recursively estimating node-specific, conditional ROC statistics can capture joint error dependencies between attributes.  
- Strength: experiments are thorough, including a controlled noise study and evaluations on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets.  
- Weakness: claim of being first to handle attribute unreliability is overstated; prior work (e.g., DAP with calibrated probabilistic outputs) also addresses uncertainty—novelty is in how unreliability is modeled, not the idea of modeling it.  
- Weakness: relies on sizeable attribute-labeled validation set; recursive partitioning produces small samples at deep nodes, making local ROC estimates noisy and the fallback heuristic (use global stats when <10 samples) is under-justified.  
- Weakness: empirical gains over strong baselines are modest (≈3–4% on AwA and aPY), limiting perceived impact.  
- Weakness: few-shot extension is underdeveloped and uses an ad-hoc weighted information-gain combination instead of a clearer probabilistic integration.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear presentation, novel and sound noise-aware RF mechanism, and consistent experimental improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: overstated novelty, practical concerns about validation-data requirements and sparsity, limited empirical gains, and a weak few-shot contribution.  
- Overall: good, interesting, and technically sound paper but incremental in impact with unresolved practical limitations.
