- Core idea: Incorporates attribute classifier uncertainty (TPR/FPR from validation) into information gain during random forest construction to model attribute unreliability.  
- Motivation: Modeling attribute unreliability is crucial to improve practical zero-shot learning performance beyond using attribute predictions alone.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on attribute-based ZSL methods [8,3,18] by explicitly modeling trustworthiness rather than just attribute strength.  
- Empirical results: Strong and consistent improvements over DAP on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets.  
- Robustness tests: Controlled noise experiments show benefits of accounting for attribute unreliability.  
- Few-shot extension: Logical and valuable extension demonstrating framework adaptability to few-shot learning.  
- Presentation: Exceptionally well-written with clear methodology and experimental setup.  
- Novelty: Significant contribution in explicitly modeling attribute reliability, a relatively unexplored angle.  
- Limitation: Lack of qualitative analysis—no visualization of trees or examples showing which attributes/categories benefit most.  
- Limitation: Hyperparameter choices (e.g., 80/20 train-validation split, tree depth) lack justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Significance: Idea of using validation-measured unreliability is interesting and relevant; random forest component itself is not novel.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance, suggesting addressing qualitative analysis and hyperparameter explanations to strengthen the paper.
