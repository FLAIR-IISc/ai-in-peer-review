## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute detectors. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for this unreliability during training by propagating validation set statistics through each decision tree. This is a well-motivated and elegantly designed method, extended to the few-shot setting, and demonstrably achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard datasets. 

The work builds upon the established two-stage zero-shot learning paradigm [8, 3, 18], but crucially departs from simply accepting attribute predictions at face value. Instead, it leverages the ROC characteristics of attribute classifiers to build more robust decision boundaries. This is a significant contribution, as previous work [18, 23] has largely focused on handling attribute strength or discovering better attributes, rather than directly addressing prediction *reliability*. The extension to few-shot learning via a weighted combination of signature-based and data-driven learning is a natural and effective addition.

The experimental evaluation is particularly strong. The authors provide a comprehensive protocol including ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, and comparisons to several baselines, including the widely used DAP method [8]. The results consistently demonstrate the benefits of their approach, especially in scenarios with noisy attribute predictions. The inclusion of results on discovered attributes [30] further highlights the method’s versatility. 

The paper is generally well-written and clear, though Section 3.2.1 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the threshold *t* before its clarification later in the section. The originality of applying a random forest framework to explicitly model attribute unreliability is noteworthy, and the significance of addressing this fundamental problem in zero-shot learning is high. This work is likely to be influential, providing a practical and theoretically sound approach to improving the robustness of zero-shot learning systems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning.
*   Novel and elegant method with strong theoretical grounding.
*   State-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Section 3.2.1 could be clarified for better readability.
*   While thorough, the ablation studies could be expanded to investigate the impact of different validation set sizes.



**Overall Score:** 8.5/10 - Strong Accept. This paper represents a significant contribution to the field and is highly recommended for publication.
