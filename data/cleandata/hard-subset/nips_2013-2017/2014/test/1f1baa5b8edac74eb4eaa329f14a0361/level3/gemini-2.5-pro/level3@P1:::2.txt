This paper addresses a critical and practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The standard two-stage ZSL pipeline first predicts an attribute vector for a test image and then classifies the image based on the similarity of this predicted vector to predefined class attribute signatures. The authors argue, correctly, that this process is brittle because attribute classifiers are often noisy and inaccurate. The core contribution is a novel random forest framework that explicitly models and accounts for this unreliability during training. Instead of training on idealized, noise-free attribute signatures, the proposed method uses a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate the performance (specifically, the receiver operating characteristic) of each attribute classifier. During tree construction, the information gain criterion is modified to account for these error statistics. This is achieved by fractionally propagating each class signature down multiple paths of a decision tree, with the fractional weights determined by the attribute classifiers' true positive and false positive rates. This novel training procedure encourages the forest to learn splits that are not only discriminative for the ideal class signatures but also robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The paper also presents extensions for handling uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and for the few-shot learning scenario.

The approach is situated well within the ZSL literature. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8], which models attribute presence probabilistically but does not explicitly model the classifier's error tendencies during training. It also differs from methods that focus on attribute strength [19] or discover non-semantic mid-level features [22, 26], by retaining the interpretable, language-based nature of attribute definitions. The fractional propagation idea is related to methods for handling missing values in decision trees [21], but the paper effectively argues that its application to the ZSL problem, where training is on signatures and testing is on classifier outputs, is a novel contribution.

### Strengths

*   **Well-motivated and Novel Idea:** The paper tackles a real, well-known bottleneck in attribute-based ZSL. The idea of directly incorporating classifier error statistics (TPR/FPR) into the learning objective of the zero-shot model is both intuitive and novel. While others have implicitly acknowledged attribute unreliability, this work offers an explicit and principled mechanism to mitigate its effects within a random forest framework.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by extensive experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The controlled noise experiments (Fig 1) provide a clear and compelling demonstration of the method's robustness compared to a baseline that ignores attribute noise. Crucially, the method significantly outperforms the widely used DAP baseline on all three real-world datasets (Table 1), demonstrating its practical value. The ablation study effectively isolates the contributions of the key components of the model.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical approach is explained progressively from a basic formulation to the full model, and the experiments are well-designed and presented. The connection to and distinction from related work is also clearly articulated.

### Weaknesses

*   **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The quantitative results are strong, but the paper would benefit from a qualitative analysis that provides insight into *why* the method works. The authors claim the method favors more "learnable" attributes. It would be powerful to visualize this. For example, showing some of the top splits selected by the proposed method versus the baseline `SIGNATURE-RF` could demonstrate which attributes are being prioritized and why. A per-class error analysis could also reveal which types of classes (e.g., those with visually ambiguous attributes) benefit most from this robustness.
*   **Hyperparameter Justification:** The rationale for certain key hyperparameters is not fully explained. For example, the attribute-labeled data is split 80/20 for training/validation. While the paper notes that the method still outperforms DAP despite using less data for attribute training, it is unclear how this split was chosen or how sensitive the model's performance is to this ratio. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper's claim of robustness.
*   **Assumption on Validation Data:** The method relies on ROC statistics estimated from a validation set, which is drawn from the "seen" classes. This carries an implicit assumption that the attribute classifiers' error patterns on seen classes will generalize to the unseen classes. While this is a reasonable starting point, the performance gap shown in the "OURS+TRUE ROC" experiment suggests this is a potential limitation. A brief discussion of this assumption and its potential impact would be welcome.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

1.  Regarding the lack of qualitative analysis: Could the authors include a table or figure in the appendix showing a few of the attributes that were most frequently selected at the top nodes of the trees for both the proposed method and the `SIGNATURE-RF` baseline? This would help substantiate the claim that the model learns to rely on more robust attributes.

2.  Could you provide more justification for the 80/20 split of attribute-labeled data? Was this tuned, or was it an arbitrary choice? How does performance change if this is varied to, say, 90/10 or 70/30? Understanding this trade-off is important for practitioners.

3.  In the few-shot learning experiments, the parameter λ is selected via cross-validation. How was this cross-validation performed, given the very small number of training examples per class? Was it a leave-one-out procedure on the training set `DT`? More detail would improve reproducibility.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective solution to a significant problem in zero-shot learning. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the core idea and the strong empirical results, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to a lack of deeper analysis and justification for some design choices. These weaknesses can be addressed in a final version. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to researchers in zero-shot learning, transfer learning, and computer vision more broadly.
