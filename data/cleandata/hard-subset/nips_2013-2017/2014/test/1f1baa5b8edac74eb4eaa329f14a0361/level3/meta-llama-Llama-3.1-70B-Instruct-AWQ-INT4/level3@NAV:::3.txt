This paper proposes a novel random forest approach to train zero-shot models that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors demonstrate the idea on three large datasets of object and scene categories and show its clear advantages over status quo models.

The main idea of the paper is to leverage statistics about each attribute's error tendencies to obtain more robust discriminative models for the unseen classes. The authors develop a random forest algorithm that uses the attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristics to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. They further generalize the idea to account for unreliable class-attribute associations and extend the solution to the "few-shot" setting, where a small number of category-labeled images are also available for training.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear summary of related work, effectively positioning their contribution within the existing literature. The technical soundness of the paper is also evident, with a thorough explanation of the proposed approach and its components.

However, the overall novelty of the paper is modest, and the claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated or misleading. Prior methods, such as DAP, already address attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and a second-stage class estimator using validation data.

The key technical differences between the proposed approach and prior work lie in the use of ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt-sigmoid models and the effective capture of joint attribute error statistics by propagating validation data down trees. While these differences are notable, they do not necessarily constitute a fundamentally new way to handle unreliability.

The paper's results are supportive of the proposed approach, but the quantitative gains are modest and not decisive by themselves. The few-shot extension is also brief and less polished, with the weighted information-gain combination feeling less intuitive than alternatives.

In terms of the reviewer guidelines, the paper scores well on clarity, with a clear and well-organized presentation of the ideas. However, the originality of the paper is modest, and the significance of the results is not entirely clear. While the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, the impact of the proposed approach is not fully demonstrated.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper is technically sound and well-presented.
* The proposed approach is a useful extension of existing methods for handling attribute uncertainty.
* The results demonstrate a clear advantage over status quo models.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The overall novelty of the paper is modest.
* The claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated or misleading.
* The few-shot extension is brief and less polished.
* The quantitative gains are modest and not decisive by themselves.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should revise the paper to better position their contribution within the existing literature and tone down the claims of novelty. Additionally, they should provide more details on the few-shot extension and clarify the significance of the results.
