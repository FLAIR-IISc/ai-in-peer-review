This paper proposes a novel random forest approach for zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors demonstrate that their method, which leverages statistics about each attribute's error tendencies, obtains more robust discriminative models for unseen classes.

The main idea of the paper is to train a zero-shot model that acknowledges the known biases of mid-level attribute models. The authors develop a random forest algorithm that uses attribute signatures for each category and exploits the attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristics to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. The method is further generalized to account for unreliable class-attribute associations and extended to the few-shot scenario.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on three large datasets of object and scene categories, showing its clear advantages over status quo models. The results suggest that the method can benefit zero-shot learning with both manually defined and discovered attributes.

One of the strengths of the paper is its empirical and quantitative results, which support the approach well. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including controlled noise experiments and real unreliable attributes experiments.

However, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis showing which attributes and categories benefit from the method. It would be interesting to see a more in-depth analysis of the attributes and categories that are most affected by the method.

Additionally, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the hyperparameters were chosen. For example, the authors use an 80/20 train-validation split, but the rationale behind this choice is not explained.

Despite these limitations, the paper is well-written and easy to read. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and results.

In terms of novelty, the paper explicitly models attribute reliability, which is a less-explored angle despite related implicit approaches. The idea of using validation-measured unreliability to improve zero-shot learning is interesting and relevant.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve zero-shot learning in a variety of applications. The authors demonstrate that their method can be used with both manually defined and discovered attributes, making it a versatile approach.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning and is a worthwhile read for researchers in this area.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for attribute uncertainty.
