## Review of “Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes”

This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning (ZSL) by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute predictions within a random forest framework. The core idea is to leverage validation-measured receiver operating characteristics (ROC – specifically TPR/FPR) of attribute classifiers to inform the information gain calculation during tree learning, effectively weighting splits based on attribute reliability. The authors extend this to few-shot learning and also address unreliable class-attribute associations. 

The work builds upon the established two-stage ZSL paradigm of attribute prediction followed by class prediction [8, 3, 18]. However, it distinguishes itself by moving beyond simply using attribute predictions at face value and instead incorporating a measure of their trustworthiness. This is a valuable contribution, as previous work has largely focused on attribute *strength* or discovering better attributes [11, 22], rather than explicitly modeling attribute *reliability* as done here. The extension to few-shot learning is a logical and welcome addition, demonstrating the method’s adaptability.

The empirical evaluation is strong, with consistent improvements over baseline methods (including the widely used DAP [8]) across three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN). The controlled experiments with synthetic noise further validate the approach’s ability to handle unreliable attribute predictions. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed method. 

However, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis. While quantitative results are compelling, it would be insightful to understand *which* attributes and categories benefit most from the proposed approach. Visualizing the learned trees or providing examples of cases where the method succeeds due to its handling of unreliable attributes would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the rationale behind certain hyperparameter choices, such as the 80/20 train-validation split, is not clearly explained. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to ZSL by explicitly modeling attribute reliability.
*   Strong empirical results across multiple datasets.
*   Well-written and clear presentation.
*   Relevant and potentially impactful contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of qualitative analysis.
*   Insufficient justification for hyperparameter choices.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with a clear and novel idea. While the lack of qualitative analysis and justification for hyperparameters are weaknesses, the strong empirical results and the significance of the approach outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised above, particularly adding a qualitative analysis to provide further insight into the method’s behavior.
