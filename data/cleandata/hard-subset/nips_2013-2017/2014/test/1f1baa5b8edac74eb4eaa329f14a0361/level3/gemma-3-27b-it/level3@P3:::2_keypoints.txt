- Proposes a random-forest method for zero-shot learning that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability using validation-measured TPR/FPR in information gain.  
- Novel focus on attribute reliability (not just strength) within the two-stage attribute→class ZSL paradigm.  
- Uses ROC curves and validation data to quantify and propagate attribute unreliability during tree learning.  
- Empirically outperforms baselines (DAP, SIGNATURE RF) on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets.  
- Demonstrates robustness to attribute prediction noise via controlled experiments.  
- Extends naturally to few-shot learning, showing the approach’s flexibility.  
- Ablation studies isolate contributions of signature uncertainty modeling and ROC propagation.  
- Writing is clear, concise, and easy to follow.  
- Builds on prior work but correctly highlights distinct challenges of zero-shot/signature-based learning versus generic uncertainty handling.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis (e.g., which attributes/categories benefit, tree/attribute importance visualizations).  
- Hyperparameter choices (80/20 split, tree depth, split sampling) are insufficiently justified and sensitivity is unclear.  
- The random forest machinery itself is not highly novel; the contribution is in how unreliability is incorporated.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, accept with minor revisions to add qualitative insights and more hyperparameter discussion.
