- Proposal: Zero-shot framework that trains SVM attribute classifiers then learns class discriminants as random forests over class–attribute signature vectors.  
- Key idea: Account for attribute unreliability during tree construction by estimating ROC-based TPR/FPR on held-out validation data and propagating signatures and validation samples down the tree.  
- Effect: Propagation captures joint attribute error tendencies at deeper nodes, improving robustness to correlated attribute noise.  
- Few-shot extension: Sketches interpolation of information gain from signatures and from a small set of class-labeled images.  
- Relation to prior work: Two-stage attributes→class follows DAP and related methods (Farhadi, Lampert, Palatucci).  
- Prior-art overlap: Other methods already train attribute classifiers and use validation/calibration (e.g., DAP with Platt scaling), so claims of uniquely handling unreliability are overstated.  
- Technical novelty: New use of ROC-based, node-specific reliability estimates and induced modeling of joint attribute-noise correlations via recursive propagation—meaningful refinement.  
- Framing suggestion: Should be positioned as improved modeling of attribute error correlations/noise and related robustness/uncertainty literature.  
- Quality: Technically sound, correctly implemented, and experiments on three datasets are appropriate.  
- Empirical impact: Gains are generally modest and not decisive.  
- Data requirement concern: Requires substantial attribute-labeled validation data, with data subdivided across nodes; fallback to global ROC stats is heuristic and under-analyzed.  
- Clarity: Very clear, well written, and comprehensive in summarizing related work.  
- Originality: Incremental contribution—main mechanism is new within zero-shot trees but overall pipeline is not novel.  
- Significance: Moderate practical value for attribute-based ZSL researchers but limited empirical effect.  
- Pros: Clear paper, sensible ROC-based reliability modeling that captures joint error statistics, and broad/appropriate experiments.  
- Cons: Modest novelty and overstated claims, substantial validation-data needs with weak fallback analysis, and brief/less intuitive few-shot treatment (Eq.6 weighting).  
- Overall recommendation: Careful incremental contribution; recommend acceptance only if room permits modest advances.
