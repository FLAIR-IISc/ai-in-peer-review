Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a core bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute detectors are imperfect, and existing ZSL methods typically take their outputs at face value. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains per-class forests using only class–attribute signatures and explicitly models attribute unreliability. During training, a held-out attribute-labeled validation set is propagated through each node to estimate node-specific ROC operating characteristics for every attribute. Class signatures are then fractionally routed left/right according to those ROC statistics, and the split is chosen to maximize a redefined information gain over these fractional memberships. The approach naturally prefers attributes (and thresholds) that are both discriminative and reliably predictable. The model is extended to (i) handle uncertainty in class signatures and (ii) few-shot learning by combining signature-driven gain with standard gain on a small labeled set via a mixing weight λ. At test time, attribute classifiers provide scores that traverse the learned forests.

This work connects to the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) framework (Lampert et al.) and related two-stage ZSL methods using attribute signatures (e.g., Farhadi et al., Parikh & Grauman), as well as to zero-shot approaches based on semantic/label embeddings (Mensink & Verbeek; Akata et al.; Frome et al.). Unlike those, it explicitly accounts for attribute detector reliability during training. The use of fractional propagation is reminiscent of decision trees for uncertain or missing values, but the zero-shot setting here is distinct: the “training instances” are class signatures, while validation data informs attribute error models and node-dependent dependencies.

Strengths
- Clear goal and elegant method: A simple yet principled modification of random forests that bridges theory and practice for attribute-based ZSL by accounting for detector reliability.
- Reliability handling: Node-specific ROC-driven fractional routing is well-motivated and technically sound; it explicitly couples discriminativeness with predictability.
- Extension to few-shot: The λ-weighted information gain neatly brings signatures in as a prior that fades as labeled data grows.
- Thorough evaluation: Strong protocol with ablations (no ROC propagation, no signature uncertainty), artificial-noise stress tests, multiple datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), and comparisons to DAP and other published methods (including discovered attributes).
- Results: Consistent state-of-the-art across zero- and few-shot settings; largest gains where attributes are least reliable (SUN, aPY), and robustness corroborated by controlled noise experiments.
- Clarity/quality: Generally well written, careful experimental design, and claims well supported.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on validation data representativeness: The ROC statistics are estimated on seen-class images. If unseen classes exhibit attribute appearances that are systematically different, calibration may drift. The “TRUE ROC” upper bound and negligible gains from selecting similar seen classes suggest this is a real challenge. A discussion or attempt at domain-shift correction (e.g., importance weighting, hierarchical priors for ROC curves) would strengthen the paper.
- Computational considerations: Propagating a validation set at every node to compute node-conditional ROCs may be costly for large M, K, and deep trees. Complexity analysis, wall-clock times, or pruning strategies would be helpful.
- Attribute interactions: While node-wise validation subsets implicitly capture some dependencies, all tests are axis-aligned single-attribute thresholds. Oblique or pairwise tests could capture correlated attributes more directly (authors mention this as future work).
- Evaluation scope: The paper focuses on “closed-world” ZSL among unseen classes. A generalized ZSL setting (seen+unseen at test) would further demonstrate robustness, though it was less standard at the time.
- Clarity nit: Section 3.2.1’s discussion of the threshold t is confusing because, with binary signatures, any 0 < t < 1 is equivalent; only later does t become meaningful via ROC-based routing in Sec. 3.2.2. A brief forward reference or example would prevent confusion.

Quality
Technical soundness is high. The fractional propagation tied to node-conditional ROC estimates is a principled way to align training with test-time attribute uncertainty. The information gain reformulation is appropriate and reduces to standard gain in the perfect-attribute limit. Experiments are comprehensive, with meaningful ablations, artificial noise studies that isolate the mechanism, and consistent SOTA results across three datasets and both zero- and few-shot regimes. The few-shot extension is clean and effective, with sensible trends in λ as data increases.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and readable. The main algorithmic ideas are clearly conveyed, and the intuition behind fractional routing is strong. The only notable clarity issue is the early treatment of threshold t in Sec. 3.2.1; adding a short note that t becomes consequential when ROC-based unreliability is introduced would help. Providing pseudocode (as referenced) in the main text rather than only in the supplement could also aid reproducibility.

Originality
Applying random forests to class signatures and integrating attribute unreliability via node-conditional ROC-driven fractional routing is a novel and appropriate contribution. It goes beyond prior ZSL work that used attribute confidences but did not incorporate detector error tendencies during training. The few-shot combination as a descriptive prior in the split criterion is a neat twist.

Significance
The paper addresses a fundamental blocker for attribute-based ZSL: unreliable detectors. The method is practical, easy to adopt, and demonstrably advances the state of the art on standard benchmarks. The idea is also broadly applicable to other semantic mid-level representations (poselets, mid-level patches) and to settings where noisy intermediate predictions feed downstream classifiers.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled handling of attribute unreliability; elegant integration into a standard learner.
- Strong, diverse empirical validation with ablations and stress tests.
- Clear improvements over widely used baselines (DAP) and competitive with methods using discovered attributes.
- Useful few-shot extension with consistent gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Sensitivity to domain shift between validation data and unseen classes is acknowledged but unresolved.
- Limited analysis of computational cost and scalability.
- Axis-aligned splits may underuse attribute correlations.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the size of the validation split? Any guidance on minimal validation set size per attribute?
- Can you report training time and memory versus DAP and signature-only RF?
- Did you try simple domain adaptation for ROC calibration (e.g., temperature scaling or isotonic regression on seen vs. held-out seen classes)?
- How does the method fare in generalized ZSL (seen+unseen test space)?

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is elegant, technically sound, and advances the practical viability of attribute-based ZSL. The thorough experiments and clear gains make it publication-worthy.
