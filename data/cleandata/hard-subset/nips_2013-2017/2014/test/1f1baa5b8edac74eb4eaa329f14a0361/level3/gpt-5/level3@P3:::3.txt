Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles zero-shot recognition when attributes are predicted unreliably. The method first trains one SVM per attribute (with Platt probabilities). Zero-shot classifiers are then learned as one-vs-all random forests built over class-attribute signature vectors (not images). The key idea is to inject attribute-labeled validation images into tree construction to estimate node-specific ROC operating characteristics for each attribute. During training, class signatures are fractionally propagated down candidate splits according to the TP/FP rates observed on the validation set at that node, leading to splits that are both discriminative among classes and robust to expected attribute errors. The approach is extended to handle class-signature uncertainty and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-based IG with standard IG computed over a small set of class-labeled images via a convex combination (Eq. 6).

The setup is squarely in the two-stage attribute-based zero-shot tradition, in line with DAP and related approaches that first predict attributes, then predict the class. The authors contrast their approach with prior work by claiming that earlier methods “do not account for attribute unreliability.” While this paper models unreliability differently and arguably better, methods like DAP already incorporate probabilistic attribute estimates and learn a second-stage class estimator using validation data. What is technically new here is the use of ROC-based, node-specific reliability estimates and the propagation of validation data down the tree, which implicitly captures joint error statistics and inter-attribute dependencies at different operating points.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The random forest training with fractional propagation based on node-local ROC statistics is well-motivated and clearly specified. The revised information gain (Eq. 5) appropriately reflects fractional counts. The few-shot extension is consistent with the core idea.
- Empirical support: Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN are appropriate; ablations isolate contributions (e.g., removing signature uncertainty or ROC propagation). The controlled noise study is insightful and aligns with the method’s design. Quantitative gains over DAP and a signature-only RF are consistent, though generally modest; the largest improvements appear where attributes are less reliable (e.g., SUN, aPY).
- Weaknesses in evaluation: The method’s reliance on a validation set that is recursively subdivided raises data efficiency concerns. When node-level counts are small, the fallback to global ROC (line 323) undercuts the main benefit of node-specific calibration. This heuristic is not sufficiently justified or compared against principled alternatives (e.g., hierarchical/shrinkage estimation of ROC rates, isotonic regression, or Bayesian binomial smoothing), and it leaves unclear how large the validation pool must be to realize the claimed advantages. The few-shot section is brief, and the mixing rule in Eq. 6 is less intuitive than alternatives that explicitly combine class-attribute priors with per-image attribute posteriors (e.g., a MAP or product-of-experts view).

Clarity
- The paper is very clear, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation, algorithmic details, and ablations are well presented. Related work is broadly and fairly summarized. Notation and figures (as described) aid understanding. The paper would benefit from a more careful presentation of how “handling unreliability” differs from prior two-stage probabilistic approaches.

Originality
- The overall framing—two-stage attribute-based zero-shot—is established. The novelty lies in:
  - Using ROC-based reliability estimates rather than Platt-sigmoid probabilities during training.
  - Propagating validation data down the tree so node-specific TP/FP rates reflect conditional attribute error statistics, thereby capturing joint dependencies among attributes encountered along the path.
- The claim that prior methods “do not account for attribute unreliability” is overstated; DAP and related work explicitly model attribute uncertainty and tune using validation data. A more accurate characterization is that this paper improves how unreliability and attribute correlations/noise are modeled within a discriminative tree learner. Related perspectives on calibrated/uncertain recognition (e.g., Scheirer et al., open set and calibrated probability work) would strengthen positioning.

Significance
- The approach is practical and likely to be used by researchers working on attribute-based zero/few-shot learning. Gains are consistent but modest; the paper’s main contribution is a better way to inject attribute reliability and inter-attribute error dependencies into the classifier rather than a fundamentally new zero-shot paradigm. The few-shot extension is promising but underdeveloped relative to the zero-shot core.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Better frame the contribution as improved modeling of attribute correlations and noise induced by unreliable attributes, not a uniquely new way to “handle unreliability.” Please clarify the conceptual differences from DAP and similar two-stage probabilistic methods.
- Validation data efficiency: Provide learning curves varying the size of the validation set; report how often the node-level fallback triggers and its impact on accuracy. Consider shrinkage or hierarchical Bayesian estimates for TP/FP with credible intervals, to retain node specificity without overfitting.
- Few-shot: Justify Eq. 6 more thoroughly and compare to alternatives that directly combine class-attribute priors with per-image attribute posteriors (e.g., MAP over classes using attribute likelihoods), or to a stacking baseline (logistic model on attribute probabilities).
- Calibration choice: Discuss trade-offs between Platt-sigmoid scores and ROC-based operating points; include a baseline that uses calibrated probabilities in a second-stage discriminative classifier (e.g., logistic regression on attribute scores) trained with the same validation data.
- Analysis: Report which attributes/splits are selected and how selection correlates with ROC separability; quantify the extent to which node-specific propagation captures attribute dependencies relative to a global-ROC variant.

Pros
- Clear, well-written, and easy to reproduce.
- Sound idea: node-specific ROC-based fractional propagation that captures joint error statistics.
- Appropriate experiments and ablations; controlled noise study is informative.
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines; applicable to both named and discovered attributes.
- Natural few-shot extension.

Cons
- Novelty is modest; overlap with prior two-stage probabilistic approaches is larger than acknowledged.
- Dependence on a sizable validation set; heuristic fallback weakens the core claim and lacks principled justification.
- Few-shot section is brief; Eq. 6 weighting feels ad hoc and less intuitive than alternatives.
- Reported gains, while consistent, are not decisive on their own.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and well-presented paper with a neat idea for incorporating attribute reliability and dependencies into zero-shot trees. The main selling point would be stronger if positioned as improved modeling of attribute correlations/noise rather than a fundamentally new way to handle unreliability. Given the clarity and solid experiments but modest novelty and some concerns about validation data requirements and the few-shot extension, I lean slightly positive, especially if the framing and analysis are adjusted and the validation/few-shot issues are addressed.
