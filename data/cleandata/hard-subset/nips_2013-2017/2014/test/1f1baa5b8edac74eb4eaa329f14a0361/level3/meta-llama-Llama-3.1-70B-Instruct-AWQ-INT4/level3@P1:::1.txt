This paper presents a novel approach to zero-shot learning, which enables the training of recognition models without requiring labeled training images for each class. The authors propose a random forest method that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions, a common issue in zero-shot learning. The method uses a validation set to estimate the error tendencies of each attribute classifier and incorporates this information into the decision tree construction process. This approach allows the model to learn more robust and discriminative decision boundaries.

The authors also extend their method to the few-shot setting, where a small number of labeled training images are available for each class. The results show that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard attribute datasets across zero- and few-shot settings.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages. The experimental protocol is strong, with ablation studies, artificial noise tests, multiple settings, and literature comparisons. The model is elegant, theoretically sound, and well-executed.

One minor criticism is that Section 3.2.1 has a confusing explanation about threshold t, which is clarified later. However, this does not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

The originality of the paper lies in its application of a random forest framework to the problem of zero-shot learning. The authors' approach is a nice and appropriate solution to the problem of attribute detector reliability.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The authors' method has the potential to be widely applicable in various domains, including computer vision, natural language processing, and robotics.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and elegant approach to zero-shot learning.
* The authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages.
* The experimental protocol is strong, with ablation studies, artificial noise tests, multiple settings, and literature comparisons.
* The model is theoretically sound and well-executed.
* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning.

Arguments con acceptance:

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning and is considered publication-worthy.
