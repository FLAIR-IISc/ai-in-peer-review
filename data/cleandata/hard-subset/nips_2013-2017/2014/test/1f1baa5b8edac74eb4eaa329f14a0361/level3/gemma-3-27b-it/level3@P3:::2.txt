This paper proposes a random-forest approach for zero-shot learning that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability, leveraging validation-measured performance (TPR/FPR) in information gain during tree learning. The idea is novel in its explicit focus on attribute *reliability* rather than strength, and empirically demonstrates improvements over existing methods like DAP on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The writing is clear and concise, making the paper easy to follow.

The work builds upon the well-established two-stage zero-shot learning paradigm [8, 3, 18, 30], where attribute prediction precedes class prediction. Existing methods largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, or model their *strength* [19, 8]. This paper differentiates itself by directly addressing the *unreliability* inherent in these predictions. Related work in handling missing or uncertain features [21, 28, 16] is acknowledged, but the authors correctly highlight the distinct challenges posed by the zero-shot setting and the specific requirements of signature-based learning. The motivation is strong – attribute prediction is a bottleneck in zero-shot learning, and addressing this weakness has the potential to significantly improve performance. The cognitive science connection to human attribute-based categorization [25, 17, 5] is a nice touch, lending further justification for the approach.

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The core idea – incorporating attribute reliability into the random forest learning process – is insightful and well-executed. The use of ROC curves and validation data to quantify attribute unreliability is a clever and practical approach. The experimental results are compelling, consistently showing improvements over strong baselines (DAP and SIGNATURE RF) across multiple datasets. The controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate the method’s robustness to attribute prediction errors. The extension to the few-shot setting is a valuable addition, demonstrating the flexibility of the approach. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of signature uncertainty modeling and ROC propagation.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the quantitative results are strong, there’s a notable lack of qualitative analysis. It would be insightful to see *which* attributes and *which* categories benefit most from the proposed method. Visualizing the learned decision trees, or analyzing the attribute importance scores, could provide valuable insights into the model’s behavior.  For example, are certain unreliable attributes consistently downweighted, and does this correlate with improved performance on specific categories?

The choice of hyperparameters, particularly the 80/20 train-validation split for ROC estimation and the tree depth/split sampling parameters, isn’t fully justified. While the authors mention cross-validation was used, providing more detail on the hyperparameter search process would strengthen the paper. Why 80/20 specifically? How sensitive are the results to different splits? 

While the novelty of explicitly modeling attribute reliability is acknowledged, the random forest component itself is not particularly innovative. The significance of the work stems primarily from *how* the random forest is used – specifically, the incorporation of attribute unreliability – rather than the random forest itself. This isn't a major flaw, but it’s important to recognize.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to address a critical limitation in zero-shot learning (attribute unreliability).
*   Strong empirical results on multiple datasets, consistently outperforming existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Relevant and potentially impactful contribution to the field.
*   Extension to few-shot learning demonstrates versatility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of qualitative analysis to understand *why* the method works.
*   Insufficient justification for hyperparameter choices.
*   Random forest component itself isn’t highly innovative.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically supported. While the lack of qualitative analysis and the limited discussion of hyperparameter choices are weaknesses, they are not severe enough to outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, I encourage the authors to include some qualitative analysis to provide deeper insights into the model’s behavior and a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter selection process.
