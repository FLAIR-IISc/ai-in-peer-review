## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute detectors. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for this unreliability during training, leading to more robust discriminative models for unseen classes. The method cleverly propagates validation data through the decision trees to capture attribute error tendencies, and extends naturally to the few-shot setting. 

The work builds on the established two-stage zero-shot paradigm [8, 3, 18], but diverges significantly by focusing on the *reliability* of the attribute predictions rather than solely their strength. This is a valuable distinction, aligning with observations that attribute learning is often more difficult than category classification itself [6]. Compared to existing approaches like DAP [8], the proposed method offers a more principled way to handle noisy attributes, moving beyond simply assuming accurate predictions. The application of random forests, while not entirely novel in machine learning, represents a creative and appropriate adaptation to the zero-shot learning problem. 

The paper’s strengths are numerous. It presents a theoretically sound method, elegantly addressing the problem of unreliable attributes. The experimental evaluation is strong, featuring ablation studies, controlled noise tests, and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The results consistently demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches, particularly in scenarios with lower-quality attribute classifiers. The extension to the few-shot setting is a logical and valuable addition. While Section 3.2.1’s explanation of the threshold *t* is initially somewhat convoluted, it is clarified later in the text.

A minor weakness is the reliance on a validation set for estimating attribute reliability, which adds an additional data requirement. However, the authors convincingly demonstrate that the benefits of modeling uncertainty outweigh this cost. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to simpler alternatives.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets across both zero- and few-shot settings.
* Strong experimental validation with thorough ablation studies and comparisons.
* Theoretically sound and elegantly executed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on a validation set for estimating attribute reliability.
* Computational complexity not fully analyzed.

**Overall:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and practically effective, and the experimental results are compelling. I strongly recommend acceptance to the conference.
