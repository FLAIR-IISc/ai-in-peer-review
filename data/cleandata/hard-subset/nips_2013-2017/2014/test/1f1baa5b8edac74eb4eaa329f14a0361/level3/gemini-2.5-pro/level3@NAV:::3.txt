This paper proposes a random forest approach for zero-shot learning (ZSL) that explicitly models the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The method first trains SVMs for each attribute and then learns a random forest for each unseen class using its class-attribute signature vector. The core idea is to leverage a validation set of attribute-labeled images during forest construction. By propagating this data down the tree, the model can estimate node-specific, ROC-based reliability statistics for each potential split. This allows the forest to favor attributes that are both discriminative for the class signatures and reliably predictable in practice.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, effectively summarizing the problem and related work. Its primary strength lies in its novel mechanism for modeling attribute noise. Unlike prior methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP), which also use validation data to train a second-stage classifier on noisy attribute predictions, this approach integrates reliability estimation directly into the tree-building process. This allows it to capture joint attribute error statistics and select splits based on both discriminative power and robustness to prediction errors—a technically sound and interesting idea.

However, the paper's overall novelty is modest, and the claim of being unique in handling unreliable attributes is overstated. The contribution is more accurately framed as an improved technique for modeling attribute noise and correlations rather than a fundamentally new ZSL paradigm. A significant practical concern is the method's reliance on the validation set, which is recursively subdivided at each node, potentially leading to sparse data and unstable error estimates in deeper parts of the trees. The proposed heuristic fix for this issue is not sufficiently justified.

Furthermore, while the experiments are appropriate and show positive results, the quantitative gains over strong baselines are moderate. The few-shot learning extension also feels underdeveloped and less polished than the main contribution, with the proposed information-gain combination in Eq. 6 being somewhat ad-hoc.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper presenting a technically solid method. Its main value is in its more sophisticated noise modeling. However, its contribution is more incremental than claimed, with concerns about data requirements and the decisiveness of its empirical gains limiting its overall impact.
