This paper proposes a zero-shot recognition method that builds random forests directly over class-level attribute signatures, while explicitly modeling unreliability in attribute predictions. The pipeline is: (1) train SVMs for M attributes; (2) train a class-specific random forest whose split tests are on attribute dimensions and thresholds, using class signatures as “training exemplars”; (3) use a held-out, attribute-labeled validation set to estimate node-specific ROC statistics for each attribute classifier and threshold; (4) propagate class signatures fractionally through the tree according to TP/FP rates at candidate splits, redefining the information gain to reflect the soft counts; (5) extend to signature-uncertainty and a few-shot variant that blends signature-based and data-based information gains. At test time, attribute predictions from SVMs route an image down each tree to yield class posteriors.

Relation to prior work: The method sits within the two-stage, attribute-based zero-shot family (e.g., DAP and non-probabilistic signature matching), but changes how reliability is accounted for when learning the second-stage classifier. Unlike DAP’s class posterior computed from calibrated attribute probabilities (often assuming conditional independence), this model uses ROC-driven, per-node reliability to select split attributes and thresholds that are both discriminative and learnable given observed error tendencies. The approach is also connected to decision trees for uncertain inputs and missing data (fractional tuple/soft node membership) but adapts these ideas to the zero-shot setting by training on signatures while testing on attribute predictions, and by propagating validation data to capture inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits. The work also contrasts with label/feature embedding methods and class-relations-based transfer, keeping the advantage of human-specifiable semantic attributes.

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized presentation; the motivation, method, and experimental setup are easy to follow. The related work section is thorough.
- Technically sound formulation of ROC-based fractional propagation and a principled information gain criterion that rewards splits robust to attribute errors.
- Nice insight: propagating validation data down the tree induces node-specific error statistics, partially capturing dependencies among attributes.
- Comprehensive experiments across three datasets with ablations (no signature uncertainty, no node-wise ROC propagation), controlled noise studies, and results for discovered attributes.
- The few-shot extension is simple and practical, and the cross-validated λ behavior matches intuition.

Weaknesses and evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach is carefully executed, but the reliance on node-specific ROC estimates can lead to sparse validation counts deep in trees. The fallback to global ROC when <10 positives/negatives reach a node is a heuristic whose adequacy is not well justified; more analysis is needed to show it does not undermine the claimed benefits. Statistical significance tests for the gains would help.
- Clarity: Very clear overall. However, the signature-uncertainty model is deferred to the supplement; core equations and intuitions should be in the main paper.
- Originality: The novelty is incremental. Prior methods already model attribute uncertainty (e.g., DAP’s probabilistic attributes with calibrated outputs), and ROC-based thresholding and soft/fractional trees for uncertain inputs are established. The main technical contribution is how ROC estimates are integrated into split selection and propagated to capture local attribute dependencies. The claim of uniquely handling unreliability should be moderated and better contextualized with related literatures (e.g., ROC-threshold selection, calibration, and open set recognition such as Scheirer et al., CVPR 2013).
- Significance: Improvements are consistent but often modest; some datasets show larger gains where attributes are noisier, which aligns with the paper’s thesis. The idea is practically useful and likely to be adopted as a robustifier for attribute-based zero/few-shot systems, but it does not fundamentally change the zero-shot paradigm.

Experimental comments and suggestions
- Validation-data dependence: Provide sensitivity to validation set size, the per-node minimum-count threshold, and tree depth. Report how often the fallback to global ROC is invoked and its effect on accuracy. Consider importance-weighting validation examples to better match the unseen-class distribution.
- Calibration vs ROC: Compare against a variant that uses calibrated attribute probabilities (Platt/Isotonic) and expected information gain under a simple (possibly correlated) error model without per-node ROC, to isolate the value of node-specific ROC.
- Joint error modeling: Explore confusion-matrix-based propagation (modeling FN/FP asymmetries per attribute jointly) or a low-rank/shared-structure model of attribute errors to mitigate sparsity at deeper nodes.
- Few-shot: The λ-weighted IG mixture is reasonable but feels ad hoc. Alternatives include: (a) a Bayesian prior over leaf class counts derived from signatures (i.e., signature-derived pseudo-counts), (b) joint training that routes both signatures and few-shot images and fuses their posteriors at leaves, or (c) combining class–attribute priors with image-level attribute estimates directly as a baseline. Include baselines that fine-tune DAP with few labeled examples.
- Broader context: Strengthen discussion of connections to ROC operating-point selection and open set recognition (e.g., Scheirer et al., CVPR 2013) to situate the contribution among reliability-aware recognition methods.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, careful paper with a principled and practical improvement to a widely used zero-shot formulation.
- Insightful use of node-wise ROC to prefer reliable and discriminative tests; partial capture of attribute dependencies.
- Consistent improvements across datasets; ablation studies support the claimed mechanisms.
- Simple few-shot extension with sensible behavior.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty is modest; claims of uniqueness in handling unreliability are overstated given DAP and reliability-aware tree literature.
- Dependence on a sizable validation set and the heuristic fallback raise questions about robustness; more analysis is required.
- Gains, while consistent, are often modest; significance tests and stronger baselines would strengthen the case.
- Few-shot section is brief and less compelling relative to alternatives.

Overall: A solid and well-presented incremental contribution that improves robustness of attribute-based zero/few-shot recognition. I recommend borderline acceptance if the claims are moderated and additional analysis of the validation dependence and few-shot baselines is added; otherwise, borderline reject.
