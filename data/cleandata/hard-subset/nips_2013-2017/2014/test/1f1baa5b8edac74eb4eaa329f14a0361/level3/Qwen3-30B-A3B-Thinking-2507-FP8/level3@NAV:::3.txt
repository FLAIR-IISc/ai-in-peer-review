

This paper proposes a random forest approach for zero-shot recognition that accounts for unreliable attribute predictions by leveraging ROC-based reliability estimates during tree construction. The authors train SVM attribute classifiers and then build a random forest using class-attribute signature vectors, propagating validation data down tree nodes to estimate attribute error tendencies. They further extend the method to few-shot learning.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the technical approach and effective summary of related work. The method is technically sound and experiments are appropriately designed across three benchmark datasets. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods like DAP and SIGNATURE RF, particularly when attribute classifiers are less reliable.

However, the novelty claim is overstated. The core idea—using validation data to account for attribute uncertainty in a second-stage classifier—has been previously explored in DAP and similar methods. The key technical contribution (ROC-based propagation instead of Platt-sigmoid models) is significant but modest, and the paper would be stronger if framed as improved modeling of attribute correlations/noise rather than a fundamentally new approach to handling unreliability. The authors should cite relevant work like Scheirer et al. (2013) that addresses similar issues.

The validation data requirement presents a concern: the method subdivides validation data at each tree node, potentially requiring a large validation set. The heuristic fix proposed (line 323) is insufficiently justified. The few-shot extension is notably weaker than the zero-shot approach, with the weighted information gain combination (Eq. 6) feeling less intuitive than alternatives. Quantitative gains are modest (e.g., 8-10% improvements on specific categories), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these improvements are transformative for the field.

While the paper is well-executed and makes a modest contribution to zero-shot learning, the main selling point—uniquely handling unreliable attributes—is questionable given prior work. The technical contribution is incremental rather than breakthrough, and the paper would benefit from more thorough discussion of limitations. The authors should reframe the contribution as an improved modeling of attribute noise rather than a fundamentally new approach to unreliability. The paper is technically sound and well-presented but lacks the significant novelty and impact expected for NIPS.
