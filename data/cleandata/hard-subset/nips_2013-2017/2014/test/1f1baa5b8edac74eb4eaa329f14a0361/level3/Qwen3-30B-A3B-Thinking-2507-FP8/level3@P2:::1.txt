

# Review for "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions in novel images. While zero-shot learning theoretically allows training classifiers by specifying category attributes (e.g., "zebra is striped, black and white"), existing methods assume perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability by leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies during training. Their method propagates validation data through decision trees to model how attribute classifiers' errors would affect classification, and extends this to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning scenarios.

The work builds on the DAP method [8], which has become a standard for attribute-based zero-shot learning, but identifies a key gap: DAP and similar approaches ignore the unreliability of attribute predictors. This paper's approach is distinct from prior work on handling missing features [21, 28] because it specifically addresses the zero-shot setting where training data consists solely of attribute signatures, not image features.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors provide a rigorous formulation for propagating attribute prediction uncertainty through decision trees and validate it through comprehensive experiments. The controlled noise experiments (Figure 1) effectively demonstrate how the method handles varying levels of attribute unreliability. The ablation studies (Table 1) isolate the impact of key components, showing that both modeling signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation are essential for performance. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is logically consistent with the main method and well-validated. The experimental protocol is strong, with multiple datasets, 20 random trials, and thorough comparison to relevant baselines including SIGNATURE RF (a direct ablation of their method) and DAP (the current standard).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written overall, with clear explanations of the core idea. The main contribution is effectively presented, though Section 3.2.1's explanation of threshold t is somewhat confusing; this is clarified later in the paper. The figures (Figures 1-2) effectively illustrate key findings. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce their results.

**Originality:** The application of random forests to zero-shot learning with explicit handling of attribute unreliability is novel and appropriate. The idea of propagating validation data through decision trees to model attribute prediction errors is particularly creative. While random forests have been used in other computer vision tasks [2], this is the first to apply them to zero-shot learning with a principled approach to attribute unreliability. The method builds on existing work but makes a significant contribution to addressing a fundamental challenge in the field.

**Significance:** Zero-shot learning is an important problem with practical applications for rare categories and dynamically emerging classes. This paper demonstrates that attribute-based zero-shot learning can be made practical by addressing the attribute unreliability problem, achieving state-of-the-art results on three standard datasets. The few-shot extension further broadens the method's applicability. The results on AwA (Figure 2b) show the method outperforms all prior approaches using both named and discovered attributes, demonstrating its value for visual category learning with minimal training examples.

**Strengths:** The method is elegant, theoretically sound, and well-executed. The authors carefully address a critical gap in the field, and their approach is both practical and principled. The comprehensive experimental evaluation, including controlled noise experiments and ablation studies, provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The few-shot extension is a natural and valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:** The paper could more clearly articulate why their approach to handling attribute unreliability is better than simply using more accurate attribute classifiers (though they do address this in their experiments). The connection between the method's design and the specific error characteristics of attribute classifiers could be further emphasized.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning (attribute unreliability) that has been overlooked in prior work
- Presents a novel, principled approach that achieves state-of-the-art results
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with multiple datasets, ablation studies, and controlled noise experiments
- Clear theoretical foundation with practical implementation
- Extends to few-shot learning, broadening the method's applicability

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The method requires a separate validation set for attribute classifiers, which might be seen as an additional constraint
- The explanation of threshold selection in Section 3.2.1 is slightly confusing

This paper makes a significant contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by bridging the gap between theory and practice. The authors demonstrate that attribute-based zero-shot learning can be made practical by properly accounting for attribute prediction unreliability, a critical challenge that has previously limited the approach's effectiveness. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and addresses an important problem with clear practical implications. I recommend acceptance.
