## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The core idea is to move beyond simply *using* attribute predictions and instead explicitly model their uncertainty during the training of a random forest classifier. This is achieved by propagating validation data through the trees, leveraging ROC curves to estimate attribute reliability, and incorporating this information into the splitting criteria. The authors also present a few-shot extension, combining attribute signatures with limited labeled data. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. It provides a thorough summary of related work in ZSL, correctly positioning its contribution within the existing landscape. The method is presented logically, and the mathematical formulation is accessible. The experimental setup is appropriate, utilizing established datasets and baselines, and the results are presented clearly. 

However, while technically sound, the novelty of the approach feels somewhat incremental. The claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated. Prior work, such as DAP, already addresses attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and then using validation data to estimate class probabilities. The key technical difference here lies in using ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt scaling and, more importantly, in effectively propagating validation data down the trees to capture joint attribute error statistics. This propagation is a valuable contribution, but it’s more accurately framed as improved modeling of attribute correlations and noise rather than a fundamentally new paradigm for handling unreliability. Citing Scheirer et al. would help contextualize this.

A concern is the potential requirement for a large validation set, as data is repeatedly subdivided within the trees. The proposed heuristic fix (line 323) feels insufficiently justified and warrants further investigation. While the experiments support the claims, the quantitative gains are modest and not decisively superior to existing methods, particularly DAP. The few-shot extension is also relatively brief and less polished; the weighted information-gain combination (Eq. 6) lacks intuitive justification compared to alternative approaches like combining class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-presented.
*   Effective propagation of validation data captures joint attribute error statistics.
*   Demonstrates improvements over strong baselines on multiple datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; similar approaches already address attribute uncertainty.
*   Overstated claim of uniquely handling unreliability.
*   Modest quantitative gains.
*   Validation data requirement and heuristic fix are concerning.
*   Few-shot extension is underdeveloped.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should reframe the novelty claim, address the validation data concern, and provide a more compelling justification for the few-shot extension. A more thorough comparison to related work, including a discussion of Scheirer et al., would also strengthen the paper.
