- Addresses a critical gap in zero-shot learning by modeling attribute prediction unreliability rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions.
- Proposes a novel random forest method that propagates validation data through trees to model attribute classifier error tendencies.
- Introduces a theoretically sound formulation with recursive propagation of validation data and a redefined information gain.
- Builds on prior ZSL frameworks but innovates by explicitly accounting for attribute unreliability (ROC propagation, signature uncertainty).
- Experiments are comprehensive: controlled noise tests on AwA and comparisons on AwA, aPY, SUN.
- Ablation studies show both signature uncertainty modeling and ROC propagation are necessary for best performance.
- Results significantly outperform DAP (4.5–10.2% gain) and achieve state-of-the-art even with discovered attributes.
- Demonstrates that modeling attribute error tendencies can be more valuable than improving attribute classifiers (works with only 80% attribute-labeled data).
- Presents a well-motivated and effective few-shot extension that improves performance with limited labeled examples.
- Clarity is generally good but Section 3.2.1’s explanation of threshold t is confusing and should be moved/clarified in the main text.
- Originality is appropriate: novel application of random forests to handle attribute unreliability in ZSL.
- Main limitation: performance depends on quality and representativeness of the attribute validation set for unseen classes.
- Additional weakness: few-shot extension and analysis across varying numbers of labeled examples could be explored more deeply.
- Could provide more insight into which specific attributes drive performance gains.
- Overall recommendation: strong, practically valuable contribution with minor clarity/analysis issues; recommend acceptance.
