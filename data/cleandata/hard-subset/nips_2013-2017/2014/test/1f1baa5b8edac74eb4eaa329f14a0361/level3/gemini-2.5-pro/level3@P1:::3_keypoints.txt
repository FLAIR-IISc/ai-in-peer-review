- Problem: Tackles zero-shot learning (ZSL) where unreliable attribute classifiers limit performance.  
- Core idea: Train a random forest on ideal class-attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute classifier unreliability using ROC-derived error statistics estimated from a validation set.  
- Mechanism: Propagate validation data down tree nodes so splits are chosen for both discriminability and robustness to expected attribute prediction errors.  
- Extensions: Framework extended to handle uncertainty in attribute signatures and a few-shot learning scenario.  
- Clarity: Paper is very well written, well organized, and clearly motivated.  
- Technical quality: Sound formulation; controlled noise experiments and ablations convincingly support the method; consistent improvements over strong DAP baseline on three datasets.  
- Originality caveat: Claim of being first to handle attribute unreliability is overstated—prior two-stage methods (e.g., DAP) implicitly model noisy attributes; the true novelty is the ROC-based, conditional/error-joint modeling via recursive partitioning.  
- Novel contributions: Uses ROC TP/FP rates instead of calibrated probabilities and captures conditional joint error statistics by partitioning validation data through the tree.  
- Framing suggestion: Reframe emphasis to “how” unreliability is modeled (capturing correlated attribute noise) and cite related work on meta-recognition/classifier fusion.  
- Significance: Statistically significant but modest gains; main value is the general idea of injecting classifier error statistics into downstream training.  
- Validation-data concern: Reliance on node-specific ROC estimates can be unreliable when validation subsets are small; the proposed heuristic fallback feels ad-hoc and needs sensitivity analysis.  
- Few-shot concern: Few-shot extension is underdeveloped; linear combination heuristic for combining signature priors and training evidence is not well justified—more principled prior-updating approaches suggested.  
- Overall verdict: Solid technical contribution, well-presented and interesting, but novelty claims should be toned down and practical issues (validation-data sensitivity, few-shot formulation) need deeper treatment.
