This paper addresses a key practical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The central idea is to train a zero-shot model that is explicitly aware of the error tendencies of its underlying attribute predictors. The authors propose a novel random forest approach where, during training, the class attribute "signatures" are not treated as perfect exemplars. Instead, they are propagated fractionally down the decision trees based on the true positive and false positive rates of the attribute classifiers. These error statistics are estimated on a held-out validation set of attribute-labeled images. This procedure encourages the forest to select splits based on attributes that are not only discriminative for the unseen classes but also reliably predictable at test time. The framework is also extended to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and to incorporate a small number of labeled examples in a "few-shot" setting.

This work fits within the popular paradigm of two-stage attribute-based classification for zero-shot learning, where attribute predictions are first made on a test image, which are then used to predict the class label (e.g., Lampert et al., Farhadi et al.). However, whereas most prior work implicitly assumes that attribute classifiers are reliable, this paper directly confronts their imperfection. This is distinct from modeling attribute *strength* (e.g., Parikh & Grauman, NIPS 2011) and provides a semantic alternative to discovering mid-level features that may be discriminative but lack nameable meaning (e.g., Rastegari et al., ECCV 2012). The proposed modification to the random forest training is related to methods for handling missing or uncertain data in decision trees, but its application to propagating idealized class signatures based on classifier error statistics is novel for the zero-shot problem.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed modification to the information gain criterion (Eq. 5) is a clever and principled way to incorporate classifier uncertainty into the tree-building process. The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The controlled noise experiments (Fig. 1) provide a clear and intuitive demonstration of the method's robustness, especially in the realistic scenario where noise levels vary per attribute. The main results on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) show significant and consistent improvements over the widely-used DAP baseline, which is a strong result. The ablation studies (Table 1) effectively isolate the contributions of the different components of the model (signature uncertainty, recursive ROC propagation). This is a complete piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the approach is explained methodically, building from a basic signature-based random forest to the full proposed model. The distinction between attribute *unreliability* (the paper's focus) and attribute *strength* is made clear. The writing makes a complex idea easy to understand, which is a significant strength.

**Originality:** The core contribution—explicitly modeling attribute classifier unreliability during the training of a zero-shot model—is novel. While the problem of imperfect mid-level representations is well-known, most prior work either seeks better features or implicitly hopes the final classifier is robust to the noise. This paper's approach of characterizing and leveraging the known error tendencies of the attribute classifiers is a fresh and valuable perspective. The use of random forests is not new, but its adaptation to this specific zero-shot scenario is innovative.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to have an impact. By significantly outperforming a strong and frequently used baseline like DAP, the paper demonstrates a practical way to improve the performance of attribute-based zero-shot learning. This could make attribute-based methods more viable for real-world applications where obtaining perfect attribute classifiers is infeasible. The idea of training with an explicit model of test-time input uncertainty is a general one that could potentially be applied to other problems that rely on chains of classifiers. The extension to the few-shot setting further broadens the applicability of the work.

Despite the many strengths, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved:

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The quantitative results are strong, but the paper would be more insightful with some qualitative analysis. For example, it would be illustrative to see which specific attributes the model learns to rely on, and which it learns to ignore due to their unreliability. A comparison of the top splits chosen by the proposed method versus the baseline SIGNATURE-RF could provide compelling evidence for the paper's claims. Similarly, an analysis of which specific classes benefit most from the proposed model could yield interesting insights.

2.  **Hyperparameter Justification:** Several important hyperparameters and design choices are not fully justified. For instance, the attribute-labeled data is split 80/20 for training the attribute classifiers and estimating their ROC statistics. What is the rationale for this specific split? How sensitive is the model's performance to this ratio? A brief sensitivity analysis or discussion would strengthen the paper's completeness and aid in reproducibility.

3.  **Scalability:** The method trains a separate one-vs-all random forest for each unseen class. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but it's worth noting in the main body that this could be computationally demanding for scenarios with a very large number of unseen classes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and practical problem in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute classifiers.
*   The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated.
*   The paper is very clearly written and easy to understand.
*   Demonstrates significant performance gains over strong, widely-used baselines on multiple challenging datasets.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough, including controlled experiments and ablation studies that validate the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper lacks qualitative results to provide deeper insight into *why* the method works better (e.g., which attributes are favored/ignored).
*   Justification for some key design choices, like the train/validation split, is missing.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective solution to an important problem. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to providing deeper analysis rather than fundamental flaws. I recommend acceptance.
