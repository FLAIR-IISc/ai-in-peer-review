- Proposes a zero-shot method that trains random forests on class-attribute signatures while explicitly accounting for attribute classifier unreliability using ROC statistics.  
- Key insight: propagate validation data through trees to estimate how attribute prediction errors affect class discrimination rather than assuming perfect attributes.  
- Extends the approach to few-shot learning by combining signature-based priors with limited labeled examples.  
- Builds on standard zero-shot frameworks (e.g., DAP) and claims unique handling of attribute unreliability, but that claim is overstated given related prior work (e.g., Scheirer et al. [23]).  
- Main technical contribution is the specific implementation of ROC-based uncertainty propagation through decision trees rather than a fundamentally new concept.  
- Strength: exceptionally clear writing, detailed methodology, pseudocode, and good articulation of attribute unreliability vs. attribute strength.  
- Strength: thorough experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets with controlled noise tests demonstrating robustness to attribute errors.  
- Strength: technically sound treatment of attribute unreliability and class signature uncertainty.  
- Weakness: originality is modest; similar validation-based approaches exist and need clearer differentiation from prior work.  
- Weakness: requires a substantial validation set (~20% of attribute-labeled images), and the heuristic for insufficient validation data is under-justified and may not scale.  
- Weakness: few-shot extension is ad hoc (weighted information gain) and lacks strong theoretical justification and depth in experiments.  
- For acceptance: method is well-motivated, technically solid, shows consistent improvements over strong baselines, and is clearly presented and reproducible.  
- Against acceptance: modest novelty, validation-data dependency limits applicability, few-shot component is weaker, and empirical gains are modest (1–10%).  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better position contributions relative to prior work, clarify validation-data requirements, and strengthen the few-shot theoretical and experimental treatment.
