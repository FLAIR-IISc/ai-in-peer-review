This paper tackles a core limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictions at test time are error-prone. The authors propose a random-forest approach that explicitly models attribute unreliability during training. Instead of taking attribute signatures at face value, they use a held-out validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate per-attribute TP/FP rates (ROCs) at candidate thresholds. These estimates are then used to fractionally propagate class signatures through tree nodes and to redefine information gain so that split selection favors discriminative attributes and operating points that are predicted to be reliable at test time. They also add a mechanism for signature uncertainty (class-attribute noise) and extend the method to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and data-based information gain with a mixing parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF, with controlled noise analyses that clearly illustrate the benefit of modeling attribute unreliability. The approach also works with discovered (pseudo-)attributes (e.g., [30]), and the few-shot extension yields sensible gains as labeled images increase.

Relation to prior work: This paper builds on the standard two-stage attribute ZSL pipeline (e.g., Farhadi et al., Palatucci et al., DAP by Lampert et al.) by explicitly incorporating attribute classifier reliability, which those methods do not. It contrasts with alternative ZSL strategies that rely on label or feature embeddings (e.g., Akata et al., Frome et al.) or class relationship transfer (Mensink et al.), emphasizing human-describable attributes. While random forests themselves are standard, the key novelty lies in training forests directly on class signatures with node-specific ROC-informed fractional propagation, which is distinct from missing/uncertain feature handling in decision trees and from prior attribute ZSL formulations.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated idea: exploit validation-measured unreliability (TPR/FPR) to select robust splits during tree learning; elegant fractional propagation and modified information gain.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent gains over DAP and signature-RF on three datasets; controlled noise studies illuminate why the method helps; ablations isolate contributions (signature uncertainty; node-specific ROC propagation).
- Practical and extensible: works with named and discovered attributes; few-shot extension via a principled IG combination; cross-validated λ behaves intuitively as data increases.
- Addresses an underexplored angle: models attribute reliability rather than only attribute “strength,” complementing lineages on probabilistic/relative attributes and embedding methods.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited qualitative analysis: it would be useful to show which attributes and thresholds are most selected by the trees, for which unseen categories, and how this differs from a signature-only RF. Visualizing per-attribute reliability-vs-discriminativeness trade-offs could deepen insight.
- Hyperparameters and rationale: the choice of 80/20 split for ROC estimation, tree depths, and the (#m, #t) candidate tests per node deserve clearer justification and sensitivity analysis. How does performance vary with the size of the ROC validation set?
- ROC estimation details: the paper reserves 20% of attribute-labeled data as DV to compute ROCs and then retrains attribute classifiers on the full data. Please clarify the sequencing to ensure DV is not used to fit the models whose ROC it evaluates. Also, how sensitive is the method to imperfect probability calibration of the attribute SVMs (Platt scaling quality)?
- Scalability: computing node-specific ROCs over DV(n) at each split can be costly. Some complexity analysis (in terms of M, K, J, |DV|) and wall-clock times would help. How does the method scale to larger attribute vocabularies (e.g., M > 500)?
- Attribute dependencies: while DV(n)-restricted ROCs partially reflect dependencies, explicit modeling of correlated attributes is limited. Empirical analysis of the benefit of node-specific ROCs over global ROCs in more correlated settings would strengthen the claim.
- Domain shift: DV comprises seen-class images; unseen classes may exhibit different attribute statistics. The “TRUE ROC” oracle indicates headroom; discussing strategies to reduce this gap (e.g., importance weighting or selecting DV images with signatures closest to unseen classes) would be valuable, even if initial attempts showed small gains.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulations (fractional propagation, IGzero, few-shot IGfew) are coherent and justified. The recursive use of DV(n) is a thoughtful way to handle attribute dependencies. Experiments are thorough, with ablations and controlled noise tests.
- Empirics: Results are convincing across datasets and settings. Additional reporting on variance, per-class breakdowns, and runtime would further solidify the empirical case.

Clarity
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow. A small addition clarifying the attribute training/validation schedule, hyperparameter selection, and threshold sampling would improve reproducibility. Providing pseudocode (as referenced) and releasing code would be ideal.

Originality
- The idea of embedding attribute unreliability into the training objective for ZSL is novel relative to DAP and related methods. The RF machinery is standard, but its adaptation to signature-based ZSL with ROC-driven fractional propagation is, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
- The contribution is practical and relevant: attribute unreliability is a real barrier to ZSL in vision and beyond. The demonstrated gains over a widely used baseline (DAP) on standard datasets are meaningful. The approach should be of interest to researchers working on ZSL, transfer learning, and interpretable mid-level representations.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a key pain point in attribute-based ZSL with a principled, effective method.
- Strong experimental results and illuminating analyses.
- Simple, general approach compatible with both named and discovered attributes, and extendable to few-shot.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited qualitative insights and sensitivity analyses (hyperparameters, DV size, calibration).
- Computational and scalability aspects are not fully characterized.
- Some ambiguity around ROC estimation protocol and potential domain shift.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add qualitative analyses of selected attributes/thresholds and per-class gains.
- Provide sensitivity studies for DV size, tree depth, (#m, #t), and λ; include runtime/complexity.
- Clarify training/validation procedure for attribute SVMs and calibration; report calibration metrics (e.g., reliability diagrams, ECE).
- Explore simple strategies to mitigate DV–unseen domain shift (e.g., weighting DV by similarity to unseen signatures).

Overall assessment: A strong and timely contribution to zero-shot learning that advances the practical utility of attributes by explicitly accounting for unreliability. Despite some missing analyses, the paper is well-executed and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
