- Problem: Addresses unreliability of attribute detectors in attribute-based zero-shot learning (previous methods assume perfect attributes).
- Prior assumption: Contrasts with DAP [8] which assumes perfect attribute predictions.
- Method: Proposes a random forest approach that propagates validation data through trees to model attribute prediction uncertainty.
- Extension: Extends the method to few-shot learning by incorporating a small number of category-labeled examples.
- Theoretical/implementation quality: Method is theoretically sound and well-executed.
- Empirical results: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on AwA, aPY, and SUN in zero-shot and few-shot settings.
- Experimental protocol: Strong evaluation with comprehensive ablations, controlled noise experiments, and comparisons to multiple baselines.
- Key finding: Modeling attribute reliability is more important than merely improving attribute classifiers.
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written; Section 3.2.1’s explanation of threshold t is initially confusing and needs minor clarification.
- Originality: Novel and well-motivated use of random forests to explicitly model attribute unreliability, building on prior attribute-based methods.
- Significance: High — bridges theory-practice gap in zero-shot learning and improves real-world applicability via few-shot extension.
- Recommendation: Strong accept for NIPS, conditional on minor clarification of the threshold explanation.
