This paper tackles a core practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute detectors. The authors propose a random-forest framework trained not on image features but on class attribute signatures; crucially, they inject attribute reliability into the training objective by propagating a held-out, attribute-labeled validation set down each candidate split and fractionally routing class signatures using node-conditional ROC estimates. This biases split selection toward attributes and operating points that will survive the errors of the attribute classifiers at test time. They further extend the model to few-shot learning via a convex combination of signature-driven and data-driven information gain. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN report state-of-the-art results in both zero-shot and few-shot regimes, with comprehensive ablations and noise robustness tests. The work is well situated with respect to DAP and related attribute ZSL (Farhadi et al.), label- and feature-embedding methods (e.g., DEVISE, label embeddings), and decision trees for uncertain/missing data.

Quality: Technically sound and carefully executed. The fractional propagation using node-specific ROCs is a neat, principled way to bridge ideal signatures and noisy attribute predictions, and the empirical protocol is strong (ablations, controlled noise, few-shot, prior SOTA comparisons). Minor concerns: scalability when training one forest per class, computational overhead of per-node ROC estimation, and reliance on the validation set’s representativeness for unseen classes. Some discussion on calibration of attribute probabilities and correlation among attributes would strengthen the analysis.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. However, Section 3.2.1’s discussion of the threshold t is confusing on first read and only becomes clear after Sec. 3.2.2; please clarify earlier that t becomes meaningful once reliability is modeled.

Originality: A thoughtful and appropriate application of random forests to ZSL, distinct from DAP-style pipelines by pushing attribute unreliability into the learner rather than only into post-hoc scoring. The connection to fuzzy/missing-value trees is acknowledged, but the ZSL setting and node-conditional ROC use are novel.

Significance: Addresses a fundamental pain point in attribute ZSL and shows consistent, meaningful gains across datasets and in few-shot settings. Likely to influence subsequent robust ZSL designs.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, principled method with strong empirical gains and ablations
- Robust handling of attribute unreliability; clean few-shot extension
- Clear relation to and improvement over canonical DAP

Cons against acceptance:
- Scalability to many unseen classes and attributes not fully analyzed
- Sensitivity to validation distribution and attribute correlations merits deeper study

Overall: Publication-worthy.
