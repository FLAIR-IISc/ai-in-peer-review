Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes a zero-shot learning framework that trains one-vs-all random forests directly on class-attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. The key idea is to incorporate validation-measured TPR/FPR for each attribute and threshold into the information gain at each split, fractionally propagating signatures according to expected test-time ROC behavior. Extensions handle uncertain signatures and few-shot learning by mixing signature-based and image-based gains. This approach contrasts with DAP and related two-stage methods [Lampert et al.; Farhadi et al.], which take attribute predictions at face value, and with semantic/label-embedding approaches [Akata et al.; Frome et al.], which do not explicitly model attribute reliability. It is also related to decision trees for uncertain/missing data [Quinlan; Olaru & Wehenkel; Tsang et al.], but adapts these ideas to the zero-shot setting with attribute ROCs.

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The fractional propagation tied to node-specific ROCs is a principled way to bias splits toward robust, learnable attributes. Empirical results on AwA, aPY, and SUN are strong, with ablations that isolate the contributions of ROC propagation and signature uncertainty. However, the hyperparameter selection is under-specified (e.g., choice of 80/20 split; number of (m,t) candidates; tree depths), and sensitivity analyses are limited. Also, ROC estimation uses a held-out 20% but the attribute classifiers are then retrained on 100%, potentially introducing a mismatch; please clarify or quantify the impact.

Clarity: Very well written and easy to follow. The training/testing pipeline and distinctions between signatures, validation data, and test-time attribute predictions are clearly described. Adding a brief algorithm box in the main text (currently in supp.) would further aid reproducibility.

Originality: Moderate. Random forests are standard, but explicitly modeling attribute reliability (not strength) within tree learning for zero-shot is a fresh and less-explored angle compared to DAP and embeddings.

Significance: The idea is simple, relevant, and appears broadly useful wherever attributes or other semantic detectors are noisy. Gains over strong baselines suggest practical impact.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Lack of qualitative analysis: show which attributes rise to top-level splits and for which categories the method helps/hurts; include per-category confusion and per-attribute selection frequencies.
- Clarify and justify hyperparameters (80/20 split, λ selection, (#m,#t), depths) and provide sensitivity curves; report runtime.
- Discuss calibration drift between seen-class validation and unseen-class test; any domain shift mitigation?
- Consider comparisons to recent label/feature embedding zero-shot baselines, and to a DAP variant that uses calibrated operating points per attribute.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated modeling of attribute unreliability
- Strong empirical support and ablations
- Applicable to zero- and few-shot settings

Cons:
- Limited qualitative insight
- Hyperparameter rationale and sensitivity unclear
- Potential ROC–classifier mismatch not analyzed

Recommendation: Accept.
