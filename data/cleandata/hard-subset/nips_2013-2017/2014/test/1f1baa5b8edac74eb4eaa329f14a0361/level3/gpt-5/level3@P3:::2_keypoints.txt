- Paper addresses noisy attribute predictions in zero-shot learning by incorporating attribute classifier unreliability into random forest construction.  
- Core idea: use validation-measured ROC (TPR/FPR) to fractionally propagate class attribute signatures and redefine information gain for splits.  
- Trees prefer splits that are discriminative among unseen classes and likely to be realized by imperfect attribute predictors.  
- Method extends to handle signature uncertainty and to few-shot learning via a mix of signature-based and data-based gains with parameter λ.  
- Novelty: explicitly modeling attribute reliability (node-specific TPR/FPR) during learning, adapting uncertain-tree ideas to ZSL signature→attribute-output mismatch.  
- Empirical evaluation on AwA, aPY, and SUN shows consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline.  
- Controlled-noise experiments and ablations support the method’s behavior and the importance of node-specific ROC.  
- Strength: clear, well-motivated formulation targeting a key ZSL failure mode.  
- Strength: principled derivation integrating node-specific ROC into split criterion with fractional propagation.  
- Strength: strong empirical support and insightful diagnostic experiments.  
- Strength: handles named and discovered attributes and provides a simple interpretable few-shot trade-off.  
- Strength: writing and related-work positioning are clear and accessible.  
- Weakness: limited qualitative analysis — lacks selected attributes/thresholds, per-class gains, confusion matrices, and example images.  
- Weakness: hyperparameter choices (80/20 split, tree depth, candidates per node, number of trees, λ) are under-explained and need sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: ambiguity in use of the 20% validation split and whether it is pooled back for final SVM training or not.  
- Weakness: reliance on validation ROC may be brittle under domain shift when unseen classes differ from seen-class attribute distributions.  
- Weakness: few-shot baselines are weak; stronger baselines (e.g., metric-learning, nearest-class-mean) should be compared.  
- Weakness: signature-uncertainty component is relegated to the supplement and needs a concise main-paper description and ablation.  
- Weakness: no discussion of computational cost, training time, ROC estimation overhead, or test-time complexity.  
- Quality assessment: formulation is coherent and sound; unlearnable attributes yield zero gain; empirical support is broad but some analyses are missing.  
- Clarity assessment: paper is readable but experimental-protocol ambiguity and absence of a main-text schematic/tree example reduce clarity.  
- Originality: moderate — technique adapts known RF/uncertainty ideas but novel in explicitly using measured attribute unreliability for ZSL.  
- Significance: solid and practical; approach likely transferable to other settings despite RF component not being new.  
- Questions for authors: clarify 20% validation handling, sensitivity to validation size and λ, provide qualitative analyses, and report runtime/overhead.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses important ZSL limitation with principled, effective, and adoptable method plus strong empirical results.  
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient qualitative/sensitivity analyses, protocol ambiguities, and weak few-shot comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak Accept.
