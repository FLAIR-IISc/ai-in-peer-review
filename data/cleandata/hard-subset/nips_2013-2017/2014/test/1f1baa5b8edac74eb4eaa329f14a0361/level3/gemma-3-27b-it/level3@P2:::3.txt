This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors train attribute classifiers, then construct a random forest on class-attribute signatures, incorporating validation data to estimate attribute reliability at each tree node. A few-shot extension is also presented.

The central idea builds on the established paradigm of attribute-based zero-shot learning [8, 3, 18], where unseen classes are described by attribute signatures. The paper rightly identifies the core challenge – the inherent difficulty of accurately predicting attributes from visual data. Instead of treating attribute predictions as ground truth, the authors leverage Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves, derived from a validation set, to model the uncertainty associated with each attribute. This is incorporated into the random forest training process, effectively weighting splits based on attribute reliability.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. The related work section is thorough and accurately situates the contribution within the existing literature. The authors acknowledge the limitations of attribute-based zero-shot learning and provide a convincing argument for addressing attribute unreliability.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness and careful presentation. The use of ROC curves to model attribute reliability is a sensible and well-motivated choice. The propagation of validation data down the decision tree, enabling the capture of joint attribute error statistics, is a thoughtful and potentially impactful extension. The experimental evaluation on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate consistent improvements over several baselines, including the widely-used DAP method [8, 9].

However, the originality of the approach is somewhat overstated. While the specific combination of ROC-based reliability estimation and random forest training is novel, the core concept of addressing attribute uncertainty is not entirely new. Prior work, such as DAP [8, 9], implicitly tackles this problem by training attribute classifiers and then performing a probabilistic inference step to predict class labels. The authors’ approach can be viewed as a more sophisticated way to model and propagate attribute uncertainty within the classification framework. A recharacterization of the contribution, framing it as improved modeling of attribute correlations and noise rather than a fundamentally new approach to handling “unreliability,” would be more accurate and acknowledge related work in areas like noise-robust classification [24]. Specifically, the paper should cite work relating to handling noise and uncertainty in machine learning models beyond just the zero-shot learning domain.

A potential weakness is the requirement for a substantial validation dataset. The recursive subdivision of validation data within the decision trees could become problematic if the initial validation set is too small, potentially leading to unstable reliability estimates. The heuristic described on line 323 to address this issue feels insufficiently justified and requires further exploration.

The few-shot extension, while a logical extension of the core idea, feels less polished and the motivation for the weighted information gain combination (Eq. 6) is not fully convincing. Alternative approaches, such as directly incorporating class-attribute priors into the attribute estimation process, might be more intuitive.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in zero-shot learning.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a thorough related work section.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines.
*   Demonstrates the importance of modeling attribute reliability, leading to improved performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claimed novelty is somewhat overstated; related work implicitly addresses attribute uncertainty.
*   The validation data requirement could be a limitation in scenarios with limited labeled data.
*   The few-shot extension feels less developed and lacks strong justification for its specific formulation.
*   Quantitative gains, while consistent, are modest and not overwhelmingly decisive.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. While the core idea builds upon existing concepts, the specific implementation and experimental evaluation are well-executed. The authors have identified a critical challenge – the unreliability of attribute predictions – and proposed a sensible and effective solution. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a compelling case for its approach. Despite the modest gains, the results are encouraging and suggest that modeling attribute uncertainty is a fruitful avenue for future research. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to better acknowledge related work and address the concerns regarding the validation data requirement and the few-shot extension.
