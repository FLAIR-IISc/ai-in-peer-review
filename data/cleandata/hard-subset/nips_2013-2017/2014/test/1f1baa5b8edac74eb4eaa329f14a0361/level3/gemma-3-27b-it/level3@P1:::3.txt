This paper proposes a novel random forest approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The method trains attribute classifiers, then builds a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors, leveraging a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate attribute reliability at each tree node. An extension to the few-shot setting is also presented. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides a comprehensive overview of related work in zero-shot learning and attribute-based recognition. 

The core idea builds upon the established paradigm of two-stage zero-shot classification: attribute prediction followed by class label prediction based on those attributes. The key novelty lies in its method of handling the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions during the *training* phase of the random forest. Rather than treating attribute predictions as ground truth, the authors estimate attribute reliability using Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves derived from a validation set, and propagate this information through the decision tree construction process. This is done by fractionally propagating signatures down the tree based on the attribute classifiers’ error tendencies, rather than a simple binary split. Furthermore, they introduce an extension to the few-shot scenario by weighting the information gain from signatures and training images.

The strengths of this work are numerous. The clarity of the exposition is particularly commendable. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature and highlight the limitations of prior approaches. The experimental evaluation on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) is thorough, and the results demonstrate a consistent, though often modest, improvement over baseline methods, including the widely-used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) approach. The inclusion of controlled noise experiments further strengthens the claim that the method is robust to attribute prediction errors.  The few-shot extension, while less detailed, provides a promising direction for future research.

However, the claim of significant novelty in addressing attribute "unreliability" is somewhat overstated. Prior methods, such as DAP, inherently address attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and subsequently estimating class labels based on probabilistic attribute predictions. The authors acknowledge this point in their related work section but downplay its significance.  The technical difference – employing ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt-sigmoid models (as used in DAP) and capturing joint attribute error statistics through validation data propagation – is valuable, but perhaps not as groundbreaking as presented. It would be beneficial to more directly acknowledge and contrast with work like Scheirer et al. (2011) on decision trees for uncertain data and related techniques for handling feature noise. 

A potential weakness lies in the reliance on a substantial validation dataset. The recursive splitting of validation data within the trees could necessitate a larger dataset than typically available, particularly for datasets with a limited number of labeled attribute images. The heuristic proposed to mitigate this concern (line 323 in the paper) is insufficiently justified and requires further investigation. 

The few-shot extension, while conceptually sound, is somewhat underdeveloped. The weighted information gain combination in Equation 6 feels less intuitive than alternative approaches, such as directly incorporating class-attribute priors into the attribute estimation process for the few available images. A more detailed analysis of the impact of the weighting parameter λ and its sensitivity to the number of training examples would strengthen this section.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-executed.
*   Clear and insightful presentation of a challenging problem.
*   Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over strong baselines.
*   Provides a valuable analysis of the impact of attribute reliability on zero-shot learning.
*   Introduces a conceptually interesting approach to incorporating uncertainty into decision tree construction.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claim of substantial novelty is questionable given similarities to prior work.
*   The reliance on a large validation dataset is a potential limitation.
*   The few-shot extension is less developed and lacks thorough analysis.
*   Quantitative gains, while consistent, are not always decisive.



**Overall, I rate this paper as a solid contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. While the novelty is perhaps incremental rather than revolutionary, the technical approach is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by empirical results. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty claim and the validation data requirements in the camera-ready version.**
