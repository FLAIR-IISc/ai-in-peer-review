This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot recognition by leveraging a random forest algorithm that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on three large datasets of object and scene categories, showing its clear advantages over status quo models.

The paper is well-written and clearly summarizes related work effectively. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, which is technically sound and well-presented. The idea of using a random forest algorithm to handle unreliable attributes is related to fractional tuples for handling missing values in decision trees, but the authors' approach is distinct and tailored to the zero-shot scenario.

The key contribution of the paper is the development of a random forest model that can handle attribute unreliability by leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies. The authors demonstrate that their method can obtain more robust discriminative models for unseen classes by accounting for attribute classifier unreliability. They also provide an extension to the few-shot scenario, where a small number of category-labeled images are available for training.

The experiments and results are appropriate and support the authors' claims. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on three challenging datasets, showing significant improvements over the existing DAP method. The results also highlight the importance of modeling attribute classifier unreliability, as the authors' method outperforms the SIGNATURE-RF baseline, which does not account for attribute uncertainty.

However, the overall novelty of the paper is modest. The authors' claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated, as prior methods, such as DAP, already address attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and a second-stage class estimator using validation data. The key technical differences between the authors' approach and prior methods lie in the use of ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt-sigmoid models and the effective capture of joint attribute error statistics by propagating validation data down trees.

A more accurate characterization of the authors' approach would be as an improved modeling of attribute correlations/noise rather than a fundamentally new way to handle unreliability. The authors could benefit from citing related work, such as Scheirer et al., to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the context and contributions of their method.

One concern with the authors' approach is the requirement for a large validation set, as the data gets subdivided in trees. The proposed heuristic fix (line 323) is insufficiently justified, and the authors could provide more details on how to address this limitation.

The few-shot extension is brief and less polished than the rest of the paper. The weighted information-gain combination (Eq. 6) feels less intuitive than alternatives, such as combining class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates. The authors could provide more details on the design choices and trade-offs involved in this extension.

In conclusion, the paper is technically sound and well-presented, but the main selling point is questionable given the similarities to prior work and some experimental/validation shortcomings. The authors could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and potential improvements of their method.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot recognition that effectively handles attribute unreliability.
* The authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness on three challenging datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly summarizes related work effectively.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The overall novelty of the paper is modest, and the authors' claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated.
* The paper requires a large validation set, and the proposed heuristic fix is insufficiently justified.
* The few-shot extension is brief and less polished than the rest of the paper.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above, provide more details on the design choices and trade-offs involved in the few-shot extension, and tone down their claims of novelty and uniqueness.
