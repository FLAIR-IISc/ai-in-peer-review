

# Review for "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical limitation of zero-shot learning: the assumption that attribute predictions are reliable. While zero-shot learning theoretically enables classifiers for novel categories by specifying attribute signatures, in practice, attribute prediction errors severely limit performance. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier unreliability by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) statistics during tree construction. Rather than treating attribute predictions as perfect, their method uses validation data to estimate true positive and false positive rates, which are then used to compute information gain during tree training. This allows the model to select discriminative splits that remain effective despite attribute prediction errors.

The work builds on the standard zero-shot paradigm (e.g., DAP [8]) but makes a crucial distinction between attribute strength (how strongly an attribute is present) and attribute reliability (how reliably the attribute can be predicted). While previous work has acknowledged attribute prediction errors (e.g., PAC bounds in [18]), this paper is the first to explicitly incorporate these error statistics into the zero-shot learning process. The paper also extends this approach to handle few-shot scenarios and signature uncertainty.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), showing significant improvements over DAP (the current standard in zero-shot learning) and the baseline SIGNATURE RF method. The authors carefully analyze the impact of their two key components: (1) recursive propagation of validation data to account for node-specific ROC statistics, and (2) modeling signature uncertainty. The ablation studies convincingly show both components contribute to the overall performance gain. The results are particularly strong on datasets where attribute classifiers are less reliable (aPY and SUN), precisely where the method is needed most.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical contributions accessible. The figures effectively illustrate the method's operation and performance gains. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis showing which specific attributes or categories benefit most from the approach, which would help understand the method's limitations and strengths. Additionally, the rationale for the 80/20 train-validation split is unclear—why not use a different ratio, and how sensitive is the method to this choice?

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with thorough experiments and appropriate baselines. The method is complete, not merely a position paper, and the authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses (e.g., noting that their approach requires attribute validation data, which may not be available in all settings). The clarity is excellent, with well-structured explanations and helpful pseudocode.

The originality is strong in the specific adaptation of random forests to account for attribute unreliability, though the core random forest algorithm itself is not novel. The paper adequately references related work, including the standard zero-shot approaches (DAP, [3, 18, 23]) and recent work on attribute learning (e.g., [11, 22, 30]). The key novelty—explicitly modeling attribute reliability rather than strength—is clearly articulated and distinct from prior work.

The significance is high. Attribute prediction errors are a major bottleneck in practical zero-shot learning, and this paper provides a principled solution that significantly improves performance without requiring additional labeled data. The results demonstrate that modeling attribute unreliability is more important than having better attribute classifiers, a valuable insight for the field. The method's applicability to few-shot learning further broadens its relevance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solves a significant practical problem in zero-shot learning that has been largely overlooked
- Provides strong empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness across multiple datasets
- Clearly articulates the distinction between attribute reliability and strength
- Well-written with thorough experimental evaluation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks qualitative analysis of which attributes/categories benefit most
- Unclear rationale for the 80/20 train-validation split
- The random forest component itself is not novel, though its adaptation is

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by addressing a critical practical limitation. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While the paper could be strengthened with more qualitative analysis and justification of hyperparameters, the core contribution is significant and the results are compelling. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

I recommend acceptance.
