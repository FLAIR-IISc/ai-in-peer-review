- Problem & approach: Addresses unreliable attribute detectors in attribute-based ZSL by training per-class random forests that explicitly model attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC statistics.

- Training method: Use a held-out attribute-labeled validation set propagated through nodes to estimate node-conditional ROC, fractionally route class signatures, and select splits by a redefined information gain.

- Extensions: Handles uncertainty in class signatures and supports few-shot learning by mixing signature-driven gain with standard labeled-data gain via a weight λ.

- Test-time: Attribute classifiers produce scores that traverse the learned forests to predict classes.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on DAP and two-stage ZSL/embedding methods but novelly incorporates detector error tendencies during training using fractional propagation.

- Strength — principled idea: Simple, elegant modification of random forests that aligns training with test-time attribute uncertainty.

- Strength — reliability modeling: Node-specific ROC-driven fractional routing couples attribute discriminativeness with predictability in split selection.

- Strength — few-shot: λ-weighted information gain cleanly treats signatures as a prior that fades with labeled data.

- Strength — evaluation: Thorough experiments with ablations, artificial-noise stress tests, and multiple datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) compared to strong baselines.

- Strength — results: Consistent state-of-the-art performance and robustness, especially where attributes are least reliable.

- Strength — clarity: Well written with careful experimental design and supported claims.

- Weakness — validation representativeness: ROC estimates from seen-class validation may not generalize if unseen classes have systematically different attribute appearances.

- Weakness — computational cost: Propagating validation data at every node may be costly for large M, K, and deep trees; lacks complexity or runtime analysis.

- Weakness — attribute interactions: Axis-aligned single-attribute thresholds may miss correlated/oblique attribute interactions.

- Weakness — evaluation scope: Focuses on closed-world unseen-only ZSL; lacks generalized ZSL (seen+unseen) evaluation.

- Weakness — clarity nit: Early discussion of threshold t is confusing until ROC-based routing is introduced; needs a brief forward reference.

- Quality assessment: Technically sound; fractional propagation and reformulated gain are principled and experiments are comprehensive.

- Originality: Novel application of random forests to class signatures with node-conditional ROC-driven routing and a neat few-shot prior.

- Significance: Practical solution to a fundamental ZSL bottleneck, broadly applicable to other noisy mid-level representations.

- Pros for acceptance: Principled reliability handling, strong empirical validation, clear gains over baselines, and useful few-shot extension.

- Cons for acceptance: Sensitivity to domain shift, limited computational analysis, and potential underuse of attribute correlations.

- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to validation set size; training time/memory vs DAP and signature-only RF; attempts at ROC calibration/domain adaptation; performance in generalized ZSL?

- Overall recommendation: Strong accept.
