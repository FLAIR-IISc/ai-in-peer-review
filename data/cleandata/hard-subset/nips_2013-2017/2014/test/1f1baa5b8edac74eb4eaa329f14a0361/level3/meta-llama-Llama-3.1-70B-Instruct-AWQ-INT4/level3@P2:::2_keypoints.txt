- Proposes a novel random-forest-based zero-shot learning method that explicitly models attribute prediction unreliability.  
- Uses attribute classifier ROC statistics to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes from class attribute signatures.  
- Generalizes to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and extends to few-shot learning.  
- Demonstrates clear empirical advantages on three large object and scene datasets with both manual and discovered attributes.  
- Provides thorough quantitative experiments including controlled noise and real unreliable-attribute analyses.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis showing which specific attributes and categories benefit most from the method.  
- Does not clearly justify hyperparameter choices (e.g., rationale for 80/20 train-validation split).  
- Random forest component itself is not novel, reducing novelty in part.  
- Novelty lies in explicitly modeling attribute reliability using validation-measured unreliability.  
- Well-written and easy to follow; clarity is a strong point.  
- Quality rated 8/10: technically sound but limited by missing qualitative analysis and unclear hyperparameter selection.  
- Significance rated 9/10: promising and versatile contribution likely to improve zero-shot learning in practice.
