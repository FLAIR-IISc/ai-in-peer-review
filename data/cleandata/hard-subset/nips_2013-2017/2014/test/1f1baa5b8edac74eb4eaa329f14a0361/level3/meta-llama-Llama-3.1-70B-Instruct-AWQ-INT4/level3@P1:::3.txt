This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot recognition that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The method trains a random forest model using attribute signatures and a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate attribute reliability at tree nodes. The authors also extend their approach to the few-shot setting by incorporating a small number of category-labeled images.

The paper is well-written and clearly summarizes related work in the field. However, the overall novelty of the approach is modest, and the claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated or misleading. Prior methods, such as the Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method, already address attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and a second-stage class estimator using validation data.

The key technical differences between the proposed method and prior work lie in its use of ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt-sigmoid models and its effective capture of joint attribute error statistics by propagating validation data down trees. These differences are notable, but the approach is better framed as improved modeling of attribute correlations/noise rather than a fundamentally new way to handle unreliability.

One concern with the proposed method is its requirement for a large validation set, as the data gets subdivided in trees. The authors propose a heuristic fix for this issue, but it is insufficiently justified. Additionally, the few-shot extension section is brief and less polished, and the weighted information-gain combination (Eq. 6) feels less intuitive than alternatives.

The experiments and results presented in the paper are appropriate and support the claims made by the authors. However, the quantitative gains are modest and not decisive by themselves. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on three challenging datasets, but the improvements over prior methods are not dramatic.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and well-presented. However, the main selling point is questionable given the similarities to prior work and some experimental/validation shortcomings. The authors could strengthen their contribution by providing a more nuanced discussion of the relationship between their approach and prior work, as well as addressing the concerns raised above.
