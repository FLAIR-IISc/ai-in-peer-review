- Addresses zero-shot learning failure caused by unreliable attribute predictors and proposes explicitly modeling attribute detector unreliability.  
- Introduces a novel random forest training approach that incorporates attribute error statistics and propagates validation data through trees.  
- Key insight: standard zero-shot methods assume perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic and harmful to performance.  
- Method trains on attribute signatures while using validation ROC-like information to model how attribute classifier errors affect decisions.  
- Relates to prior work: builds on attribute-based zero-shot learning (e.g., Lampert et al., DAP) and adapts Breiman’s random forests to handle attribute uncertainty.  
- Distinguishes itself from mid-level feature approaches by making semantic attributes practical despite detector unreliability.  
- Theoretical soundness: clear justification for the information gain formulation and proper handling of uncertainty (Equation 5).  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough: ablation studies, controlled noise tests, and comparisons on AwA, aPY, and SUN in zero- and few-shot settings.  
- Results are strong and consistent state-of-the-art, with analyses (e.g., Figure 1) explaining why the method outperforms baselines.  
- Few-shot extension is elegant, naturally incorporating labeled examples into the zero-shot framework.  
- Ablation studies show the importance of recursive ROC propagation and signature uncertainty modeling to the method’s gains.  
- Clarity is generally good, though Section 3.2.1’s explanation of threshold t is confusing and needs a simple example.  
- A limitation: the paper could explore performance when the attribute vocabulary is learned without seen-class supervision.  
- Overall assessment: original, significant, well-executed work with strong theoretical and practical contributions; recommendation to accept.
