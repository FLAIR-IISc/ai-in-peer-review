

This paper presents a compelling solution to a fundamental challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute detectors. While previous work (e.g., DAP [8]) assumes perfect attribute predictions, this paper demonstrates how to explicitly account for attribute classifier errors during model training. The authors propose a random forest approach that propagates validation data through decision trees to model attribute prediction uncertainty, then extends the method to few-shot learning by incorporating a small number of category-labeled examples.

The paper excels in quality: the method is theoretically sound, well-executed, and achieves state-of-the-art results across three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) in both zero-shot and few-shot settings. The experimental protocol is particularly strong, featuring comprehensive ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, and thorough comparisons with multiple baselines. The authors convincingly demonstrate that modeling attribute reliability is more important than improving attribute classifiers themselves.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though Section 3.2.1's explanation of threshold t is initially confusing before being clarified later in the paper. This minor issue doesn't detract from the overall readability.

The originality is appropriate for the field: while random forests have been used in other contexts, applying them to explicitly model attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning represents a novel and well-motivated contribution. The work builds thoughtfully on prior attribute-based zero-shot methods while addressing their key weakness.

The significance is high: the paper successfully bridges the theory-practice gap in attribute-based zero-shot learning, demonstrating that this approach can be practical despite the inherent difficulty of learning reliable attribute detectors. The few-shot extension further enhances its applicability to real-world scenarios where some training examples may be available.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, addressing a critical limitation in zero-shot learning with an elegant solution that achieves superior performance across multiple benchmarks. The method is both theoretically principled and practically valuable, making it an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor clarification of the threshold explanation.
