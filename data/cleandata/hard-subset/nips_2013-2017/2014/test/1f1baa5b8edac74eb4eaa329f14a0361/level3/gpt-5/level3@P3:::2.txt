Summary
This paper tackles a central shortcoming of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictions are noisy in practice. The authors propose a random forest framework that explicitly incorporates attribute classifier unreliability during tree construction. The core idea is to use validation-measured ROC statistics (TPR/FPR at candidate thresholds) to fractionally propagate class attribute signatures down the tree, and to redefine information gain accordingly. Intuitively, the trees prefer splits that are both discriminative among unseen classes and likely to be realized by imperfect attribute predictors at test time. The method also handles uncertainty in class-attribute signatures and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with standard data-based gain via a mixing parameter λ.

Relative to prior ZSL work (DAP; Farhadi et al.; Lampert et al.; Palatucci et al.; Mensink et al.; Akata et al.; DeViSE), the key novelty is not the use of attributes per se, but the explicit modeling of attribute reliability (as distinct from attribute strength or confidence) during learning. The fractional propagation idea is related to decision-tree treatments of missing/uncertain features, but the paper adapts it in a new way to the ZSL setting where training inputs are class signatures and test inputs are attribute classifier outputs. The empirical evaluation on AwA, aPY, and SUN shows consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, plus controlled-noise experiments that diagnose the method’s behavior. The few-shot extension also shows gains over using images alone.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that directly addresses a key failure mode in attribute-based ZSL.
- Technically sound integration of node-specific ROC estimates into the split criterion, with a principled information gain derivation and a neat fractional propagation mechanism.
- Strong empirical support: improvements over DAP across datasets; controlled noise experiments that isolate where the method helps; ablations (no signature uncertainty, no node-specific ROC).
- Handles both named and discovered (pseudo-)attributes; offers a few-shot extension with a simple, interpretable λ trade-off.
- Writing is very clear and easy to follow; good positioning w.r.t. related work (including fractional tuples/uncertain trees and recent ZSL variants).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited qualitative analysis. It would be valuable to show which attributes and thresholds the trees actually select, per-class improvements, confusion matrices, and example images where the proposed method wins or fails. This would help readers understand which categories/attributes benefit most from reliability-aware training.
- Hyperparameter selection is under-explained. The rationale for the 80/20 train–validation split, the chosen tree depths, number of candidate tests per node, number of trees, and λ selection should be clarified, with sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying validation set size and λ).
- Ambiguity around use of the validation data: some text implies the 20% split is later pooled to train attribute SVMs; elsewhere results are interpreted as if only 80% were used. Please clarify the exact protocol and ensure baselines use the same training data.
- Reliance on validation ROC to approximate test-time attribute errors may be brittle when unseen classes lie off the seen-class attribute distribution. Consider analyzing performance vs. degree of signature shift, or validating the authors’ “OURS+TRUE ROC” upper bound with intermediate proxies (e.g., weighting validation data by signature similarity).
- Few-shot baselines could be stronger. Beyond “images only” RF, consider comparisons to label/feature embedding approaches adapted to few-shot, or to simple nearest-class-mean/metric-learning baselines available at the time (e.g., Mensink & Verbeek).
- The signature-uncertainty component is deferred to the supplement; the main paper would benefit from a concise description and an ablation isolating its effect per dataset.
- No discussion of computational cost. Reporting training time, node-wise ROC estimation overhead, and test-time complexity would help practitioners.

Quality
- Soundness: The formulation is coherent; the use of node-specific ROC computed from recursively filtered validation subsets is a strong design choice that implicitly models inter-attribute dependencies induced by earlier splits. The analysis that unlearnable attributes (TPR≈FPR) yield zero gain is convincing.
- Empirical support: Broad and consistent across datasets; controlled noise studies are insightful. Some missing analyses remain (qualitative insights; sensitivity to validation size and λ; stronger few-shot baselines).
- Limitations are acknowledged (e.g., “OURS+TRUE ROC” as an upper bound suggests domain-shift sensitivity).

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and readable. A minor concern is the ambiguity around the 80/20 split usage; a clearer experimental protocol description would help. Including a brief schematic of fractional propagation and a small tree example in the main text (not only supp.) would further improve clarity.

Originality
- Moderate. Random forests and ZSL via attributes are known; the novel contribution is explicitly folding attribute reliability (validation-measured TPR/FPR at node-specific operating points) into the split selection. This is a less-explored angle compared to modeling attribute strength or confidence. The adaptation of “uncertain trees” to the ZSL signature-training/test-prediction mismatch is original and well justified.

Significance
- Solid. Attribute unreliability is a central bottleneck for attribute-based ZSL; the proposed remedy is practical and shows measurable gains. While the RF component is not new, the idea of using measured unreliability to guide zero/few-shot learning should transfer to other architectures and modalities, and is likely to be used or extended by others.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the exact handling of the 20% validation split: is it pooled back to train the attribute SVMs before final evaluation, and do baselines get identical training sets?
- How sensitive is performance to the size/selection of the validation set and to λ in few-shot?
- Can you provide qualitative analyses (selected attributes/thresholds per class, per-attribute gains, confusion matrices)?
- What is the runtime overhead of node-wise ROC estimation and fractional propagation?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and practical limitation in ZSL with a principled and effective method.
- Strong empirical results with clear ablations and diagnostic experiments.
- Clear presentation; method is simple enough to adopt.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient qualitative insight and limited sensitivity analyses.
- Ambiguities in experimental protocol and hyperparameter choices.
- Few-shot comparisons could be stronger.

Overall recommendation: Weak Accept. The core idea is interesting, well executed, and empirically validated; addressing the noted analysis and clarity gaps would make the contribution even stronger.
