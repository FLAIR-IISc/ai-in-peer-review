This paper proposes a novel random forest approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea is to incorporate the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers – specifically, their true and false positive rates – into the tree-building process, effectively weighting splits based on the trustworthiness of the underlying attribute predictions. The authors also extend this framework to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and to the few-shot learning scenario. The method is evaluated on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN), demonstrating improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP).

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for addressing attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning is convincingly presented, and the proposed approach is logically explained. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers from NIPS and other conferences. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to strong baselines and ablation studies. The results presented are strong and consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios with noisy attribute predictions.

One of the key strengths of this work lies in its novelty. While zero-shot learning and the use of attributes are well-established areas, explicitly modeling attribute *reliability* (as opposed to strength or confidence) is a less-explored angle. The approach represents a sensible and well-motivated extension to existing techniques. The significance of this work is also noteworthy. Addressing the unreliability of attribute predictions is crucial for making zero-shot learning practical, and the authors’ proposed solution offers a promising path forward. The extension to the few-shot setting further enhances the practical relevance of the method.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. A notable omission is a qualitative analysis showcasing *which* attributes and categories benefit the most from the proposed method. Providing examples of cases where the uncertainty modeling leads to improved predictions would strengthen the paper’s narrative and provide further insight into the method’s behavior. While quantitative results are strong, a deeper understanding of *why* the method works well in certain scenarios would be valuable.

Another minor concern is the lack of detailed explanation regarding hyperparameter selection. The rationale behind the 80/20 split for training and validation data, and the specific values chosen for parameters like tree depth and the number of trees in the forest, could be clarified. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters would be beneficial. While the authors mention cross-validation was used, more specifics would improve reproducibility.

The paper appropriately cites related work, but it might be beneficial to briefly discuss the connection to Bayesian approaches for handling uncertainty. While the ROC-based approach is distinct, acknowledging the broader context of uncertainty modeling in machine learning could enrich the discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in zero-shot learning – the unreliability of attribute predictions.
*   The proposed method is novel and well-motivated, offering a clear improvement over existing approaches.
*   The experimental results are strong and consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow.
*   The extension to the few-shot setting enhances the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of qualitative analysis limits the insight into the method’s behavior.
*   The hyperparameter selection process is not fully explained.
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of connections to related uncertainty modeling techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. While there are minor weaknesses, the novelty, significance, and strong experimental results outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include a qualitative analysis and a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection. The method provides a practical and effective approach to address a key limitation of current zero-shot learning techniques and holds the potential to significantly advance the field.
