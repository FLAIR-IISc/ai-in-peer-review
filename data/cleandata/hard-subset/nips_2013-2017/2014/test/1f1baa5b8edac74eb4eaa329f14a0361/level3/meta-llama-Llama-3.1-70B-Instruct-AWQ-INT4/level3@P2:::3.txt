This paper presents a novel approach to zero-shot recognition that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest method that leverages statistics about each attribute's error tendencies to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. The paper is well-written and clearly summarizes the related work, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the field.

The proposed method consists of training SVM attribute classifiers and then a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors. A key innovation is the use of a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate attribute reliability at tree nodes. The authors also present a few-shot extension of their method, which combines the signature-based prior with traditional training examples.

While the paper is technically sound, the overall novelty of the approach is modest. The claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated, as prior methods, such as the Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method, already address attribute uncertainty by training attribute classifiers and a second-stage class estimator using validation data. However, the authors' use of ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt-sigmoid models and their effective capture of joint attribute error statistics by propagating validation data down trees are key technical differences that set their approach apart.

A more accurate characterization of the approach would be as an improved modeling of attribute correlations and noise, rather than a fundamentally new way to handle unreliability. Related work, such as Scheirer et al., has also explored the idea of modeling attribute correlations and noise, and the authors should acknowledge and build upon these contributions.

One concern with the proposed method is the requirement for a large validation set, as the data gets subdivided in trees. The authors propose a heuristic fix, but it is insufficiently justified and may not be effective in all cases.

The experiments and results presented in the paper are appropriate and support the authors' claims, but the quantitative gains are modest and not decisive by themselves. The few-shot section is brief and less polished, and the weighted information-gain combination (Eq. 6) feels less intuitive than alternatives, such as combining class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates.

In conclusion, while the paper is technically sound and well-presented, the main selling point is questionable given the similarities to prior work and some experimental/validation shortcomings. However, the authors' approach to modeling attribute correlations and noise is a valuable contribution to the field, and their method may be useful in certain scenarios where attribute reliability is a concern.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to zero-shot recognition that explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability.
* The authors' use of ROC-based reliability estimates and their effective capture of joint attribute error statistics are key technical differences that set their approach apart.
* The paper is well-written and clearly summarizes the related work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The overall novelty of the approach is modest, and the claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated.
* The method may require a large validation set, and the proposed heuristic fix is insufficiently justified.
* The few-shot section is brief and less polished, and the weighted information-gain combination (Eq. 6) feels less intuitive than alternatives.
