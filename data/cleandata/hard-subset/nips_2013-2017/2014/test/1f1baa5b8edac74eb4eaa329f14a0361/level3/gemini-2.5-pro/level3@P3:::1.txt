This paper addresses a fundamental and practical problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. Standard ZSL methods, such as Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP), often rely on a two-stage process where attribute predictions from a novel image are used to infer its class, implicitly assuming the attribute classifiers are reliable. This work argues that this assumption is a major bottleneck in practice and proposes a method to directly model and account for attribute classifier errors during training.

The core contribution is a novel random forest framework for ZSL. Instead of training on images, the decision trees are trained using the attribute *signatures* of the unseen classes. The key innovation is how attribute unreliability is handled: during the tree-building process, a validation set of attribute-labeled images is propagated down the tree alongside the class signatures. At each potential split, this validation set is used to compute node-specific ROC statistics for the attribute classifier under consideration. This allows the information gain criterion to favor splits that are not only discriminative for the idealized class signatures but are also based on attributes that can be reliably predicted at test time. The framework is further extended to handle uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning scenario, where it elegantly combines the signature-based prior with information from a few labeled examples.

### **Strengths**

**Quality and Significance:** The paper is of high quality and addresses a significant problem in the ZSL literature. The core premise—that accounting for attribute unreliability is critical for practical ZSL—is well-motivated and convincingly demonstrated. By moving beyond the ideal world of perfect attribute predictors, this work offers a valuable contribution towards making attribute-based recognition more robust and effective. The model is elegant, theoretically sound, and the connection between the training-time signature propagation and the expected test-time classifier behavior is clever.

**Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental protocol is exemplary. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation that builds a strong case for their method.
1.  The controlled experiments using synthetic noise (Figure 1) provide clear intuition and empirical validation for the method's core mechanism, showing its robustness precisely in the scenarios it was designed for.
2.  The method achieves state-of-the-art results on three challenging and standard ZSL benchmarks (AwA, aPY, SUN), significantly outperforming the widely used DAP baseline.
3.  The ablation studies are thorough and effectively tease apart the contributions of the main components of the model (signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation).
4.  The extension to the few-shot setting is well-executed and demonstrates the flexibility of the framework, showing a clear advantage over training on the few examples alone.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel and appropriate application of the random forest framework to the ZSL problem. While ideas like propagating fractional instances exist in the decision tree literature for handling missing values, their application here is non-trivial and uniquely tailored to the ZSL setting. The idea of using a validation set to estimate classifier reliability *locally* at each node to guide the training of a model built from abstract signatures is a creative and effective combination of familiar techniques to solve a new problem.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized for the most part. The motivation is clear, the method is explained logically, and the experiments are easy to interpret.

My only minor criticism regards the clarity of the explanation in Section 3.2.1. The text describes sampling split thresholds `t` when introducing the basic signature random forest. However, as a footnote correctly points out, for the binary signatures used in this section, any threshold `t` between 0 and 1 is equivalent. This can be slightly confusing for the reader, as the importance of optimizing `t` is not apparent until the next section (3.2.2), where the reliability model is introduced and test-time classifier scores are considered. A small clarification in the main text of 3.2.1, perhaps briefly mentioning that the threshold `t` will become critical when accounting for real-valued classifier outputs, would preempt this confusion and make the narrative flow even smoother. This is a very minor point in an otherwise excellent paper.

### **Summary of Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. It identifies a key weakness in existing attribute-based methods and proposes an elegant, well-executed, and empirically powerful solution. The comprehensive experiments, including insightful synthetic tests and strong results on multiple benchmarks, provide convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness. I strongly recommend acceptance.
