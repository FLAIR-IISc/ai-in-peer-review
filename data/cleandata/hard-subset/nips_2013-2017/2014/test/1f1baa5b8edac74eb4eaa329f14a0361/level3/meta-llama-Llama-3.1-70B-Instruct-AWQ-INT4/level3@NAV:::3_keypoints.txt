- Proposes a random forest method for zero-shot learning that explicitly models unreliability of attribute predictions.  
- Demonstrates the approach on three large object/scene datasets and reports advantages over baseline models.  
- Core idea: use per-attribute error statistics (ROC curves) to build more robust, discriminative decision nodes.  
- Extends the method to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and to a few-shot setting.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly positioned in related work, and technically sound.  
- Novelty is modest; the claim of uniquely handling unreliable attributes is overstated—prior methods (e.g., DAP) also address attribute uncertainty.  
- Key technical differences are ROC-based reliability estimates instead of Platt-sigmoids and capturing joint attribute error statistics via propagation down trees.  
- Those technical differences are notable but do not constitute a fundamentally new paradigm for handling unreliability.  
- Experimental results support the approach but quantitative gains are modest and not decisive.  
- Few-shot extension is brief and underdeveloped; the weighted information-gain combination is less intuitive than alternatives.  
- Overall: high clarity, modest originality, unclear significance—the impact is not fully demonstrated.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better situate contributions in literature, tone down novelty claims, expand few-shot details, and clarify result significance.
