- Paper addresses unreliable attribute predictions in attribute-based zero-shot learning with a novel random-forest training that builds trees over class attribute signatures.  
- Key innovation: ROC-aware fractional propagation using per-attribute ROC stats from held-out validation data to account for attribute unreliability.  
- Extensions include handling uncertain class–attribute associations and a few-shot variant blending signature-derived and image-based information gain.  
- Well-situated relative to prior work (DAP, similarity/signature matching, embeddings, decision trees); main novelty is ROC-aware fractional propagation in zero-shot forests.  
- Method is technically sound and carefully evaluated with controlled-noise studies isolating the mechanism.  
- Ablation studies (removing signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC) clarify component contributions.  
- Empirical gains shown over DAP and signature-only RF on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets; few-shot extension is empirically useful.  
- Concern: distribution shift — ROC curves estimated on seen classes may misestimate error tendencies on unseen classes (true ROC upper bound highlights gap).  
- Inter-attribute dependencies are only partially addressed via node-specific validation filtering, not explicitly modeled.  
- Clarity is good; training/inference and notation are easy to follow, but some implementation details are in the supplement.  
- Complexity/memory costs of one-vs-all forests are not explicitly stated.  
- Suggestions: domain-adaptive ROC calibration, explicit modeling of attribute correlations (oblique splits or graphical priors), multiclass forests to reduce K models, sensitivity analyses to validation size/imbalance, joint end-to-end attribute/tree learning, and releasing code.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear problem, novel idea, thorough experiments, significant gains, and practical impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on seen-class ROC generalization, limited analysis of scalability and attribute correlations.  
- Overall verdict: solid, constructive contribution to zero-/few-shot recognition with practical relevance.
