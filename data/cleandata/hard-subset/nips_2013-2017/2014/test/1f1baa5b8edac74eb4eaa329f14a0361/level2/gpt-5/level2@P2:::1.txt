Summary
The paper addresses a core weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are often unreliable, and most existing zero-shot methods take their outputs at face value. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains directly on class-attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability using ROC statistics estimated on attribute-labeled validation data. At each node, signatures are fractionally propagated to children according to the estimated TPR/FPR of the split attribute at a chosen threshold, yielding splits that favor both discriminative and learnable attributes. The method naturally handles uncertainty in the class signatures (annotation noise) and extends to few-shot learning by combining signature-driven information gain with standard data-driven information gain via a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations demonstrating the impact of ROC-driven propagation and signature uncertainty. Controlled noise experiments further validate the robustness to heterogeneous attribute reliability. The paper situates the approach with respect to DAP, topic-model variants, and knowledge-transfer/embedding methods, while emphasizing semantic attribute supervision and human-controllable class specification.

Strengths
- Clear and principled treatment of attribute unreliability: the fractional propagation using node-specific ROC estimates is well-motivated and technically sound; the information gain objective is a natural generalization to soft counts.
- Practical and general: works with named or discovered attributes; requires only attribute-labeled data plus class signatures (and optionally few labeled class examples).
- Strong empirical support: three datasets, both controlled noise and real classifiers, sensible ablations, and competitive or SOTA results vs. widely used DAP. The gains are largest where attributes are less reliable, which directly validates the premise.
- Few-shot extension is simple yet effective, providing a descriptive prior on splits that blends gracefully as labeled examples increase.
- Discussion of limitations (upper bound with “true ROC”) and the role of validation data is honest and helpful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on attribute validation data distribution: The ROC statistics are estimated from seen classes’ images. If unseen classes exhibit different appearance distributions for the same attribute, ROC mismatch could degrade performance (the “TRUE ROC” upper bound illustrates this). The paper briefly mentions attempts to select similar seen classes, but this is not deeply explored.
- Potential data usage ambiguity: The setup “reserves 20% for ROC validation, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers” suggests the ROC is estimated for an earlier classifier than the final one used at test time. This mismatch could bias split selection. Clarification is needed, and an out-of-sample ROC for the final classifiers (e.g., via cross-validation or a held-out calibration set) would be preferable.
- Scalability: Training one-vs-all forests per unseen class is O(K) and evaluated on small K (10–12). For larger K, a single multi-class forest or a shared forest with class posteriors at leaves would be more scalable. The paper mentions multi-label forests as future work but does not demonstrate them.
- Attribute correlations: While node-wise conditioning with DV(n) partially captures dependencies, splits are univariate; correlated attributes (a known issue in attribute learning) are not explicitly modeled. This may limit gains in regimes with strong inter-dependencies.
- Baselines: DAP is the canonical attribute baseline, but additional comparisons to contemporary zero-shot embedding approaches (e.g., ALE/label embeddings, DeViSE) would strengthen the story, even if they are not strictly attribute-based. For few-shot, comparisons to metric-learning or label-embedding methods adapted to low-shot would be informative.
- Stability of node-specific ROC estimates: When few validation positives/negatives reach a node, the fallback to global DV may cause inconsistencies. Some smoothing or Bayesian estimation could improve robustness.

Quality
- Sound algorithmic development; the information gain with soft indicators is appropriate and the fractional propagation aligns with the ROC interpretation. The few-shot objective is straightforward and sensible.
- Experiments are generally thorough, with useful ablations and sensitivity to noise. More details on computational cost and sensitivity to the amount of validation data would be beneficial.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and easy to follow. The role of DV and how ROC is estimated and used per node is clear. However, the training pipeline for attribute classifiers versus ROC estimation needs clearer separation to avoid confusion about “double-dipping” and classifier mismatch.

Originality
- Novel and insightful integration of attribute unreliability into zero-shot training via ROC-aware random forests trained on signatures. To my knowledge, prior attribute-based zero-shot methods did not incorporate attribute error tendencies in the learning objective.

Significance
- Addresses a key practical bottleneck of attribute-based zero-shot learning and shows consistent improvements on multiple datasets. The idea is likely to influence follow-up work on robust zero-shot and few-shot learning with semantic supervision and could transfer to other mid-level concept pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the attribute classifier training/validation protocol and ensure ROC is estimated out-of-sample for the final classifiers (e.g., K-fold CV with per-fold ROC aggregation, or a held-out calibration split not used in classifier training).
- Explore domain adaptation or importance weighting of validation data to better match unseen classes (e.g., weighting DV by similarity of signatures to unseen classes).
- Report computational complexity and training/inference times; discuss memory and scaling to larger K and M. Consider a shared multi-class forest variant.
- Add comparisons to representative label-embedding/semantic embedding methods and few-shot baselines; include numeric results in the main paper wherever possible.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: size of DV, number of thresholds/tests per node, and effect of ROC estimation noise; add smoothing for ROC estimates at deeper nodes.
- Discuss handling continuous/relative attributes in the main text with at least one concrete example and result, rather than relegating to the supplement.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical problem with a principled and effective solution.
- Strong empirical gains on multiple benchmarks; thorough ablation studies.
- Conceptually clean framework with natural extension to few-shot.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on seen-class ROC estimates without strong mitigation of distribution shift to unseen classes.
- Limited scalability discussion and limited baselines beyond DAP in the main comparisons.
- Minor clarity issues around data usage for classifier training vs. ROC estimation.

Overall: A solid and timely contribution to zero-shot learning that improves robustness in a principled way. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifications and minor additions.
