Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles a core weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictions are unreliable at test time. The key claim is a random-forest training procedure that (i) builds trees over class attribute signatures rather than images and (ii) explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability by propagating signatures fractionally according to per-attribute ROC statistics estimated on held-out validation data. The authors extend this to handle uncertain class–attribute associations and a few-shot variant that blends signature-derived information gain with image-based gain. This is well-situated relative to DAP (Lampert et al.), similarity/signature matching [Farhadi et al.], label/feature embeddings (Akata et al., DeViSE), and decision-tree treatments of missing/soft inputs; the main novelty is bringing ROC-aware fractional propagation into zero-shot forests.

Quality: The method is technically sound and carefully evaluated. Controlled-noise studies convincingly isolate the mechanism; ablations (without signature uncertainty, without node-specific ROC propagation) clarify contributions; and results on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF. The few-shot extension is natural and empirically useful. One concern is distribution shift: ROC curves estimated on “seen” classes may misestimate error tendencies on unseen classes; the “true ROC” upper bound underscores the gap. Inter-attribute dependencies are only partially captured via node-specific validation filtering, not explicitly modeled. Statistical significance beyond standard errors would help.

Clarity: Writing is clear and well-organized; the training/inference pipelines and notation are easy to follow. Some implementation details are relegated to the supplement (e.g., signature-noise model, split sampling), and complexity/memory costs of one-vs-all forests could be stated explicitly.

Originality: The ROC-aware split criterion and fractional propagation in a zero-shot setting are novel and non-trivial extensions beyond prior attribute-based and embedding approaches.

Significance/usefulness: Improving robustness to attribute noise is practically important; the demonstrated gains over a strong baseline (DAP) on multiple datasets suggest others can build on this, including beyond vision wherever semantic attributes exist. The few-shot priorization is a nice bridge to realistic settings.

Limitations and suggestions: Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data may constrain domains; consider domain-adaptive calibration of ROC curves, explicit modeling of attribute correlations (e.g., oblique/multivariate splits or graphical priors), multiclass forests to reduce K one-vs-all models, and sensitivity analyses to validation set size/imbalance. Jointly learning attributes and trees end-to-end is a promising direction. Public code would aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance: clear problem, novel idea, thorough experiments, significant gains, practical impact. Arguments against: reliance on seen-class ROC generalization, limited analysis of scalability and correlations. Overall, a solid, constructive contribution to zero-/few-shot recognition.
