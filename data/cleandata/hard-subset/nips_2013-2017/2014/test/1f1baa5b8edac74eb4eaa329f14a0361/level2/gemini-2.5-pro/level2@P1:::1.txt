This paper addresses a critical and practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The standard ZSL paradigm assumes that novel classes can be recognized based on their attribute descriptions, but this is undermined when the underlying attribute predictions from images are noisy. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly models and accounts for this unreliability during the training of the zero-shot classifier. The core idea is to train an ensemble of decision trees not on images, but on the attribute "signatures" of the unseen classes. Crucially, during tree construction, the model incorporates knowledge about attribute classifier performance. Instead of a hard split, each class signature is propagated fractionally down the tree's branches, with the proportions determined by the attribute classifier's ROC statistics (e.g., true/false positive rates) estimated on a validation set. This allows the forest to learn splits that are not only discriminative for the ideal signatures but also robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The authors further extend this framework to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning setting. The approach is evaluated on three standard ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating significant improvements over strong baselines, including the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known but often overlooked problem that is a major bottleneck for the practical application of attribute-based ZSL. While prior work acknowledges attribute prediction errors, this paper presents a novel and principled way to directly integrate statistics about these errors into the learning of the zero-shot model itself. The idea of fractionally propagating signatures based on classifier ROC curves is both elegant and effective. This moves beyond simply modeling confidence at test time and instead builds robustness into the model from the outset.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The formulation for the modified information gain (`IGzero`) is a natural extension of the standard RF framework to handle the fractional "exemplars". A particularly strong aspect is the recursive propagation of the validation data subset (`DV(n)`) down the tree. This allows for node-specific ROC estimation, cleverly capturing dependencies between attributes selected at different levels of the tree, an issue not addressed by prior work on decision trees with uncertain data. The extension to the few-shot setting is also natural and provides a new way to incorporate descriptive priors into RFs.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The controlled noise experiment (Figure 1) provides excellent intuition and a clear validation of the method's core hypothesis. It effectively shows that the model is robust to noise and particularly adept at handling varying noise levels across attributes by favoring more reliable ones.
    *   The results on three challenging real-world datasets show substantial improvements over a very strong and widely-used baseline (DAP), as well as an ablated version of their own method (SIGNATURE-RF). This demonstrates the practical value of the contribution.
    *   The ablation study in Table 1 effectively teases apart the contributions of the different model components.
    *   The demonstration that the method achieves state-of-the-art results on the AwA benchmark, both with named and discovered attributes, solidifies its position as a significant advance in the field.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The authors do an excellent job of first providing a high-level intuition for their approach and then detailing the technical formulation. The figures and tables are clear and support the main claims effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Scalability with Unseen Classes:** The current approach trains one-vs-all random forests for each unseen class. While effective, this could be computationally expensive if the number of unseen classes is very large. It would be beneficial for the authors to include a brief discussion on the computational complexity with respect to the number of unseen classes and whether a multi-class RF formulation could be adapted to this framework as a more scalable alternative.

2.  **Role of the Validation Set:** The method's performance relies on the ROC statistics estimated from a held-out validation set (`DV`). While reserving 20% of the attribute training data is a reasonable approach, the quality of these statistics depends on how well this validation set represents the data distribution of the unseen classes. The "OURS+TRUE ROC" experiment shows there is a gap, which is expected. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the size and composition of this validation set would strengthen the paper. For instance, how does performance change if only 5% or 10% of the data is reserved for validation?

3.  **Details on Generalization:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results using discovered attributes from Yu et al. [30] and mentions this required "a generalization of our method to handle the continuous attribute strength signatures". This is a strong result and an interesting extension. While details may be in the supplement, adding a sentence or two in the main text to briefly explain how the fractional propagation or split selection was adapted for continuous signature values would be valuable for the reader.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. It identifies a key weakness in existing attribute-based methods and proposes a novel, technically sound, and well-executed solution. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over the state of the art. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with additional discussion. I strongly recommend acceptance.
