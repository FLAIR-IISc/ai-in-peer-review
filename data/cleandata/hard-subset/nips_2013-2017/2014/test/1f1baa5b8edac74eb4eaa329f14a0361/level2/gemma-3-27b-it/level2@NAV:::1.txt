## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses the critical challenge of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The core idea – to explicitly model and account for the error tendencies of attribute classifiers during training – is a valuable contribution. The authors propose a random forest approach that leverages Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes, and extend this to few-shot scenarios and unreliable class-attribute associations. The approach is well-motivated, building on the observation that attribute prediction is a significant bottleneck in practical ZSL systems.

The paper is strong in its technical detail, thoroughly explaining the modifications to the random forest algorithm to handle attribute uncertainty. The extension to few-shot learning with a weighting parameter λ offers a practical way to incorporate limited labeled data. The experimental evaluation on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) is comprehensive, demonstrating consistent gains over strong baselines like DAP and a standard signature-based random forest.  The ablation studies, particularly isolating the impact of signature uncertainty and ROC propagation, provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of different components. The results showing improvement even with noisy attributes are compelling. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations. While the authors acknowledge the complexity of modeling inter-attribute correlations, further exploration of this aspect would be beneficial. The sensitivity analysis of the tree depth and (m,t) testing parameters could also be expanded. While thorough, the experiments primarily focus on existing datasets. A demonstration on a novel, dynamically defined category (as suggested in the introduction) would strengthen the argument for the practical utility of the approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant problem hindering practical ZSL.
*   Novel and well-motivated approach with strong theoretical grounding.
*   Comprehensive experimental validation demonstrating consistent improvements.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of inter-attribute correlations.
*   Lack of demonstration on a truly dynamically defined category.
*   Parameter sensitivity analysis could be more thorough. 

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and experimental validation warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations outlined above. The idea of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability is a significant step forward, and the proposed method has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working with rare or dynamically defined categories.
