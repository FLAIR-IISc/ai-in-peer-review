

The paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the reliance on perfect attribute predictions, which are often unreliable in practice. It proposes a novel random forest approach that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability during training by leveraging receiver operating characteristic (ROC) statistics from validation data. The method propagates attribute signatures fractionally through trees according to estimated true/false positive rates, avoiding splits that become uninformative due to attribute errors. Extensions handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning by incorporating labeled training images with a weighted information gain criterion. Experiments on Animals with Attributes (AwA), aPascal/aYahoo (aPY), and SUN datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods like DAP and signature-based random forests, particularly when attribute classifiers are unreliable (e.g., SUN and aPY show 5-10% gains over DAP). The few-shot extension also shows clear benefits with minimal labeled examples.

Strengths include a well-motivated solution to a genuine problem in zero-shot learning, sound technical execution with clear theoretical grounding in ROC-based propagation, and thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets. The controlled noise experiments effectively illustrate the method's robustness to attribute errors, and the few-shot extension provides practical utility. The writing is clear, and the approach generalizes to both hand-designed and discovered attributes (e.g., matching state-of-the-art on AwA with [30]'s attributes).

Weaknesses involve the dependency on validation data for ROC estimation, which requires additional attribute-labeled images not used for final classifier training. While the paper acknowledges this, it would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the cost of acquiring such data relative to the gains. The SUN results, though improved over baselines, remain lower than AwA (e.g., 28.0% vs. 30.4% on AwA), and the paper does not deeply analyze why attribute reliability is consistently lower for SUN (e.g., scene attributes vs. object attributes). The "signature uncertainty" component (Section 3.2.3) is underdeveloped in the main text, with details relegated to supplementary material; a brief clarification of its role would strengthen the presentation.

The work is original in its adaptation of fractional propagation to zero-shot learning, distinct from prior methods that treat attributes as perfect (e.g., DAP [8]) or ignore error statistics. It builds on but significantly extends [21,28] by accounting for node-specific ROC dependencies in the tree structure, which is novel for zero-shot settings. The significance is high: it enables more practical zero-shot learning by mitigating a key bottleneck, with potential applications in rare-category recognition and dynamic category definition.

Minor suggestions: (1) Discuss the trade-off between validation data size and performance in the experiments; (2) Clarify how the method handles multi-modal class signatures (e.g., "whales are big and gray OR black") in the main text; (3) Compare to recent attribute discovery work [30] in the related work to better position the method's applicability to discovered attributes. These are reasonable revisions that would enhance clarity without requiring major reimplementation.

Overall, the paper makes a strong technical contribution that addresses a well-identified problem with rigorous methodology and compelling results. The method is sound, well-validated, and significantly advances the state of zero-shot learning. It meets all NIPS criteria: high quality (clear experimental support), clarity (well-structured), originality (novel ROC-based propagation), and significance (practical impact on a core challenge). The minor weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution.
