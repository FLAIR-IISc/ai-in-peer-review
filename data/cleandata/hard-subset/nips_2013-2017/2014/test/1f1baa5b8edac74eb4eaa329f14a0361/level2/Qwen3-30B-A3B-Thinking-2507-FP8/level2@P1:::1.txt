

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the common assumption that attribute predictions are reliable. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability during training, using ROC statistics from validation data to guide tree splits. The method accounts for both imperfect attribute predictions (via fractional propagation of signatures) and class signature uncertainty, with extensions to few-shot learning.  

**Strengths**  
The core contribution is highly original and addresses a well-known but understudied flaw in zero-shot paradigms. Prior work (e.g., DAP, [8, 18, 30]) treats attribute predictions as ground truth, yet the authors convincingly demonstrate that even small attribute errors catastrophically degrade performance (Fig. 1). Their solution—integrating ROC characteristics into random forest training—is elegant and theoretically sound. The ablation studies (Table 1, "OURS W/O ROC PROP") clearly isolate the impact of each component, showing that modeling attribute unreliability alone yields significant gains over SIGNATURE RF (e.g., +10.2% on "giant panda" in AwA). The few-shot extension (Sec. 3.3) is a natural and well-motivated contribution. Experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) are thorough, with clear statistical significance (20 trials) and comparisons to strong baselines (DAP, SIGNATURE RF). Notably, their method achieves state-of-the-art results on AwA even with named attributes (Fig. 2b), and generalizes to discovered attributes [30].  

**Weaknesses**  
The reliance on attribute validation data (20% of attribute-labeled images) is a practical constraint. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s unclear how the method would scale if such data is scarce or unavailable (e.g., for new attribute sets). The tree depth selection (AwA-9, aPY-6, SUN-8) is not justified beyond cross-validation—more discussion of this hyperparameter’s sensitivity would strengthen the work. Additionally, the few-shot experiments (Fig. 2a) show diminishing returns as training images increase, but the paper does not explore how the method performs with *very* few samples (e.g., 1–5 images), which is a key zero-shot scenario.  

**Clarity and Presentation**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear problem formulation (Sec. 1), precise technical contributions (Sec. 3), and compelling results (Sec. 4). The connection to fractional tuples [21] is well-explained, and the supplemental material (pseudocode, ROC illustrations) is valuable. Minor points: the transition from "attribute strength" (Sec. 2) to "unreliability" could be smoother, and the few-shot λ parameter’s cross-validated selection (star in Fig. 2a) merits a brief mention in the main text.  

**Significance and Originality**  
This work advances zero-shot learning by resolving a fundamental bottleneck: attribute unreliability. Unlike prior "semantic" approaches [1, 14], it retains human-describable attributes while making them robust. The significance is high—zero-shot learning is critical for rare categories (e.g., medical imaging, new species), and this method enables practical deployment. The originality is strong; no prior NIPS work (2013–2014) addresses attribute unreliability in this manner, and the technique is broadly applicable (e.g., to poselets [12] or mid-level patches [27]).  

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong contribution to NIPS. It identifies a subtle but pervasive flaw in the field’s assumptions, proposes a simple yet effective fix, and validates it rigorously across multiple datasets. The method is technically sound, the experiments are comprehensive, and the writing is clear. While the validation data dependency is a limitation, it is a reasonable trade-off for the substantial performance gains. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a valuable tool for practitioners.  

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*: Novel solution to a key zero-shot bottleneck; robust empirical results; clear ablations; strong significance.  
*Con*: Requires attribute validation data; tree depth hyperparameters not deeply analyzed; few-shot gains with very low samples not tested.  

**Overall**: Accept. The paper makes a meaningful, well-executed contribution to a core challenge in vision. It avoids overclaiming, acknowledges limitations, and provides actionable insights for future work (e.g., modeling error rates on unseen attribute combinations). The authors have clearly engaged with the field’s literature and addressed the most relevant baselines.
