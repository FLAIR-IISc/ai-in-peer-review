Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition with attribute-based descriptions, focusing on a key practical limitation: attribute classifiers are unreliable, and errors propagate to the zero-shot stage. The proposed method trains random forests directly on class-level attribute signatures but explicitly models attribute unreliability by incorporating per-attribute ROC statistics estimated from held-out attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes according to node-specific TPR/FPR at the candidate threshold, and the information gain is redefined to account for fractional counts. This encourages splits that remain discriminative under expected attribute errors and avoids “unlearnable” attributes/operating points. The framework also handles uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and example-based information gains with a trade-off parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP [8,9] and a signature-only RF baseline, with controlled-noise studies, ablations, and results using both named and discovered attributes [30]. A few-shot extension demonstrates benefits of the signature prior over pure example-based training.

Relation to prior work
Most attribute-based zero-shot methods use a two-stage pipeline taking attribute predictions at face value (e.g., [3,8,18,30,24,19]) or exploit semantic embeddings/relations [1,4,13,14,23]. The core novelty here is to train the zero-shot classifier while accounting for attribute unreliability via ROC-aware fractional propagation in decision trees. While related in spirit to decision-tree handling of missing/uncertain values [21,28,16], the zero-shot setting differs: trees are trained on class signatures (not examples), use attribute validation data only to estimate ROCs, and propagate both signatures and validation data node-wise to capture interdependencies along a path.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and principled approach that directly addresses attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC modeling.
- Simple, scalable implementation building on random forests, with an intuitive few-shot extension as a descriptive prior.
- Strong empirical validation across three datasets, including controlled-noise analyses isolating the benefit of reliability modeling, ablations (no signature uncertainty, no node-wise ROC propagation), and results with discovered attributes [30].
- The method preserves semantic control over unseen classes while remaining robust to imperfect attributes—a practical advantage over purely discovered mid-level features [11,22,26,27,30].
- The few-shot extension shows consistent gains and a sensible dependence on the prior weight λ.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Essential clarifications/revisions:
- Data usage inconsistency: Section 4 states both that 20% of data is reserved for ROC validation and then pooled back to train the attribute classifiers, and also that only 80% is used to train attributes when comparing to DAP. This needs clarification to ensure fairness and reproducibility. Please report exact counts used to train attribute classifiers for all methods and confirm that baselines use identical training sets.
- Evaluation metric: clarify whether accuracies are per-class or overall. Zero-shot evaluations typically report per-class accuracy to mitigate class imbalance; ensuring consistency with [8,9] would aid comparison.
- Complexity and scalability: provide time/memory costs and asymptotic complexity in K (unseen classes), M (attributes), tree depth, and number of trees, and compare to DAP. One-vs-all forests imply O(K) models; discuss training/testing time for larger K and the feasibility of multi-label forests alluded to in the conclusion.
- Signature uncertainty: substantial details are deferred to the supplement. A concise description in the main text (formula, intuition, and hyperparameters) would improve self-containment.

Desirable but optional improvements:
- Robustness to domain shift: the approach assumes that ROC statistics estimated on seen-class images transfer to unseen classes. The “upper bound” using true unseen ROC is informative; reporting how performance varies with the amount/composition of DV (e.g., stratified by signature similarity to unseen classes) would strengthen the case. Lightweight covariate-shift corrections (e.g., recalibration by temperature scaling per attribute) could be explored.
- Attribute correlations and oblique tests: the method partially captures dependencies via node-wise conditioning of DV, but tests remain axis-aligned. Including limited oblique splits (e.g., two-attribute linear tests) or pairwise conjunctions at candidate splits could further improve robustness when attributes are correlated [6].
- Broader baselines: beyond DAP, adding semantic embedding baselines on aPY and SUN (e.g., label embeddings [1], DeViSE [4]) would situate the gains more comprehensively across paradigms, as done on AwA.
- Interpretability: provide examples of top-level splits and their selected operating points to illustrate how the model prioritizes reliable attributes; a small visualization would aid intuition.
- Sensitivity analyses: report performance vs. tree depth/number and the number of candidate (m,t) tests, and the impact of low positive/negative counts at nodes (where global DV is used).

Quality
The methodology is sound and well-motivated. The fractional propagation with node-specific ROC estimation is a coherent way to encode expected test-time noise into training. Experiments are thorough: three datasets, controlled-noise studies demonstrating the mechanism, ablations isolating components, and few-shot evaluation. The main outstanding issues are clarity on data usage, scalability analysis, and more detailed reporting of evaluation metrics and runtime.

Clarity
Overall well-written and organized. The training procedure is explained clearly, with helpful notation. However, several critical details are referenced only in the supplement (signature uncertainty, pseudocode). The noted inconsistency about DV pooling vs. 80% usage should be resolved.

Originality
The work offers a novel combination: zero-shot training on class signatures with reliability-aware decision-tree learning using ROC-driven fractional routing. While related ideas exist for uncertain/missing features in trees [21,28,16], applying them in a zero-shot setting with node-wise ROC conditioning and using attribute validation to shape the splits appears new. The few-shot formulation as a prior-guided RF is a clean extension.

Significance
Addressing attribute unreliability is central to making attribute-based zero-shot learning practical. The demonstrated gains over DAP across datasets, and competitive performance with discovered attributes, suggest real impact. The approach is simple to adopt and potentially extensible to other semantic mid-levels (e.g., poselets [12]).

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, elegant treatment of attribute unreliability with clear empirical benefits.
- Solid experimental validation and ablations across three datasets.
- Useful few-shot extension; generally applicable framework.

Cons against acceptance
- Ambiguity about data usage for attribute training vs. validation.
- Limited analysis of scalability and runtime; some key details deferred to supplement.
- Comparisons beyond DAP are limited on aPY and SUN.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely contribution to zero-/few-shot recognition. With clarifications on data usage, evaluation metrics, and scalability, this work would be a valuable addition to the conference.
