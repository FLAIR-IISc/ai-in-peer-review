- Summary: Proposes a random-forest framework for attribute-based zero-shot learning that trains on class-attribute signatures while modeling attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC estimates, enabling fractional propagation of signatures and a natural few-shot extension.

- Strength: Principled treatment of attribute unreliability using ROC-driven fractional propagation and a soft-count information gain objective.

- Strength: Practical and general method that works with named or discovered attributes and needs only attribute-labeled data plus class signatures.

- Strength: Strong empirical results across AwA, aPY, and SUN, including controlled-noise experiments and ablations showing ROC-driven propagation and signature-uncertainty benefits.

- Strength: Simple, effective few-shot extension that blends signature-driven and data-driven information gains.

- Strength: Honest discussion of limitations (e.g., “true ROC” upper bound) and role of validation data.

- Weakness: Dependence on ROC estimates from seen classes — distribution mismatch with unseen classes can degrade performance.

- Weakness: Possible data-usage ambiguity / “double-dipping”: ROC may be estimated on a validation split inconsistent with the final classifiers, risking biased split selection.

- Weakness: Scalability concerns: training one-vs-all forests per unseen class is O(K) and not demonstrated at larger K; shared or multi-class forests are not evaluated.

- Weakness: Attribute correlations not explicitly modeled — univariate splits may miss inter-dependent attribute structure.

- Weakness: Limited baselines — comparisons focus on DAP; lacking comparisons to label-embedding/semantic-embedding and few-shot metric-learning methods.

- Weakness: Stability of node-specific ROC estimates when few validation samples reach a node; fallback to global DV may be inconsistent without smoothing/Bayesian estimates.

- Quality: Algorithmic development is sound; information gain with soft indicators is appropriate and experiments are generally thorough.

- Clarity: Generally clear writing and presentation of DV/ROC usage, but pipeline for attribute classifier training vs. ROC estimation needs clearer separation to avoid confusion.

- Originality: Novel integration of attribute error tendencies into zero-shot training via ROC-aware random forests; likely new to attribute-based ZSL work.

- Significance: Addresses a practical bottleneck in attribute-based ZSL with consistent improvements and potential influence on robust zero/few-shot learning.

- Suggestion: Clarify and ensure ROC is estimated out-of-sample for final classifiers (e.g., K-fold CV or separate calibration split).

- Suggestion: Explore domain adaptation or importance weighting to better match validation DV to unseen-class distributions.

- Suggestion: Report computational complexity, training/inference times, and discuss memory/scaling and shared/multi-class forest variants.

- Suggestion: Add comparisons to representative label-embedding and few-shot baselines and move key numeric results into the main paper.

- Suggestion: Provide sensitivity analyses for DV size, thresholds per node, ROC estimation noise, and add smoothing for deep-node ROC estimates.

- Suggestion: Discuss handling continuous/relative attributes in the main text with concrete examples and results.

- Pros for acceptance: Principled, effective solution to a key problem with strong empirical gains and thorough ablations.

- Cons for acceptance: Reliance on seen-class ROC estimates without strong mitigation for distribution shift, limited scalability discussion, and limited baselines beyond DAP.

- Overall: Solid, timely contribution that improves robustness in attribute-based ZSL; leans toward acceptance pending clarifications and minor additions.
