- Problem: Addresses unreliability of attribute classifiers in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL).
- Main claim: Explicitly modeling attribute classifier error tendencies during training yields more robust ZSL models.
- Method: Novel random forest built on semantic attribute signatures of unseen classes instead of image data.
- Key innovation: Fractional propagation of signatures down trees using attribute classifiers' ROC statistics estimated on a validation set.
- Contrast to prior work: Differs from Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) by explicitly accounting for attribute classifier biases.
- Technical merit: Clever, well-executed use of classifier ROCs to guide fractional splitting of class signatures.
- Empirical results: Thorough experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN showing significant improvements over DAP.
- Robustness evidence: Controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate method robustness.
- Extensions: Framework handles signature uncertainty and a few-shot learning setting, showing flexibility.
- Minor weakness 1: Performance depends on the representativeness of validation data used to estimate attribute error statistics.
- Minor weakness 2: One-vs-all forest training may face scalability challenges with a very large number of unseen classes.
- Overall verdict: Strong, well-written paper with convincing claims and a strong recommendation for acceptance.
