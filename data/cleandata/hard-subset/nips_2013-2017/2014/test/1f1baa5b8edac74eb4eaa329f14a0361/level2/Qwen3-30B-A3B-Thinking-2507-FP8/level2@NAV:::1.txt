

This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions, a critical challenge in the field. The main claim is that by leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies during training, their random forest-based method produces more robust discriminative models for unseen classes compared to existing approaches. The authors further extend their method to handle few-shot scenarios and unreliable attribute descriptions.

The paper makes a strong contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in current zero-shot learning paradigms. The authors correctly identify that existing methods (like DAP) treat attribute predictions as perfect, which is unrealistic in practice. Their solution of using receiver operating characteristics to propagate fractional training signatures through the tree structure is both theoretically sound and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) provides compelling evidence of their method's superiority, with significant gains over DAP (the leading baseline) and SIGNATURE-RF. The controlled noise experiments particularly illustrate how their approach effectively handles attribute unreliability.

The paper demonstrates excellent quality through rigorous experiments, with proper statistical evaluation (20 random trials) and thorough ablation studies. The methodology is clearly described, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors also appropriately contextualize their work within the field, citing relevant prior work on attribute-based zero-shot learning and clearly articulating how their approach differs.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a practical barrier to real-world adoption of zero-shot learning. By making zero-shot learning more robust to unreliable attribute predictions, the method enables more practical applications for rare categories or dynamically defined classes. The few-shot extension further enhances the method's applicability.

A minor limitation is that the method requires additional attribute-labeled validation data for ROC estimation, which might not always be readily available. The authors acknowledge this but could provide more discussion on how to handle cases with limited validation data. Additionally, the computational complexity of the approach merits more explicit discussion.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical practical limitation. The method is well-supported by experiments, clearly presented, and has clear practical significance. I recommend acceptance.
