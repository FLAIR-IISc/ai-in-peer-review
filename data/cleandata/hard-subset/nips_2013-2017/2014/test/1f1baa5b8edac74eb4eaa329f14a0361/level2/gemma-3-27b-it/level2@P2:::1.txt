This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning, specifically focusing on the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea is to build a random forest model that explicitly accounts for the error tendencies of the attribute classifiers, improving robustness and discriminative power for unseen classes. The approach is extended to the few-shot scenario and also handles unreliable class-attribute associations. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a novel approach with compelling experimental results on three datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed method of incorporating attribute classifier reliability into the random forest training process is a novel and insightful approach to tackling a critical issue in zero-shot learning. Existing methods largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is unrealistic.
* **Technical Soundness:** The approach appears technically sound, with a clear explanation of the method and a well-defined information gain calculation that incorporates attribute prediction uncertainty. The extensions to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning are logical and well-motivated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN), demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods, including the widely used DAP. The controlled noise experiments provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach in handling unreliable attributes. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different components of the method.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The explanations of the technical details are concise and easy to follow. The use of equations is appropriate and well-explained.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is significant, as zero-shot learning is crucial for handling rare or dynamically defined categories. The proposed solution has the potential to improve the practicality and performance of zero-shot learning systems in real-world applications. The ability to extend the method to the few-shot setting further enhances its practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on having a separate validation dataset to estimate the ROC of attribute classifiers. While the authors acknowledge this, it adds an extra requirement and could be a limitation in scenarios where such data is scarce. The impact of the size and quality of the validation data on performance is not thoroughly explored.
* **Computational Cost:** Building and training the random forest with the proposed extensions is likely more computationally expensive than simpler methods like DAP. The paper doesn’t provide detailed information on the computational overhead.
* **Limited Exploration of Signature Uncertainty:** While the paper introduces a method to account for uncertainty in class signatures, the experiments exploring this aspect are relatively limited. Further investigation into the impact of different noise models and the optimal way to model signature uncertainty could be beneficial.
* **Attribute Discovery:** The paper mentions results using discovered attributes, but the description is somewhat brief. A more detailed discussion of the challenges and benefits of using discovered attributes within the proposed framework would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to address a critical limitation of zero-shot learning.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to understand.
* The problem addressed is significant, and the proposed solution has the potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method relies on a separate validation dataset, which might not always be available.
* The computational cost of the proposed approach is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and well-validated empirically. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the method and explore the impact of validation data size and quality on performance. They could also elaborate on the challenges and benefits of using discovered attributes.
