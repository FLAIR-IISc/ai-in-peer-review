This paper addresses a critical and well-known problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The main claim is that by explicitly modeling the error tendencies of attribute classifiers during training, one can build more robust ZSL models. The authors propose a novel random forest approach where, instead of training on image data, the trees are built using the semantic attribute signatures of unseen classes. The key innovation is a training procedure that fractionally propagates these signatures down the tree based on the attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristic (ROC) statistics, estimated on a validation set. This approach is contrasted with prior methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP), which typically use the output of attribute classifiers without explicitly accounting for their known biases.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. The primary contribution is both original and significant. Addressing attribute unreliability is crucial for making attribute-based ZSL practical, and the proposed method is an elegant solution. The technical quality is high; the idea of using classifier ROCs to guide the fractional splitting of idealized class signatures in a random forest is clever and well-executed. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. Experiments on three standard benchmarks (AwA, aPY, SUN) show significant improvements over the widely used DAP baseline. The controlled noise experiments provide excellent intuition and clear evidence for the method's robustness. Furthermore, the extensions to handle signature uncertainty and the few-shot learning setting demonstrate the framework's flexibility and increase its utility.

The paper has minor weaknesses. The method's performance depends on the validation data used to estimate the attribute error statistics. While the results are strong, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model to the choice and representativeness of this validation set, especially since its statistics (derived from seen classes) are used as a proxy for performance on unseen classes. Additionally, the one-versus-all training approach for the forests may face scalability challenges with a very large number of unseen classes, a point acknowledged by the authors as future work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective solution to an important problem. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments, and the work advances the state-of-the-art in attribute-based recognition. The paper is clearly written and should be of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
