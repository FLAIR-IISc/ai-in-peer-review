This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors introduce a random forest algorithm that leverages statistics about each attribute's error tendencies to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. The method is extended to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and the few-shot scenario.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and highlight the key differences between their method and existing approaches. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three large datasets and comparing the proposed method to several state-of-the-art baselines.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed approach is novel and addresses a critical issue in zero-shot learning, namely the unreliability of attribute predictions.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the method's performance, including controlled noise experiments and real-world evaluations on three datasets.
* The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the theoretical foundations of the proposed approach. While the authors provide some intuition about why their method works, a more rigorous analysis would strengthen the paper.
* The experimental evaluation could be improved by including more baselines and comparing the proposed method to other state-of-the-art approaches in zero-shot learning.
* The paper assumes that the attribute vocabulary is expressive enough to discriminate between categories. However, this assumption may not always hold, and the authors could discuss potential limitations and future work in this area.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that addresses a critical issue in the field. The method is distinct from existing approaches and has the potential to advance the state of the art.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to impact the field of computer vision and machine learning. Zero-shot learning is an important problem, and the proposed approach could have practical applications in areas such as image classification, object detection, and robotics.
