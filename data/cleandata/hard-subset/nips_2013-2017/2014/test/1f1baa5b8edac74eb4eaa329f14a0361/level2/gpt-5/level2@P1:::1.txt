Summary
The paper tackles a central weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are often unreliable, and existing two-stage approaches typically take their outputs at face value. The authors propose a random forest framework that explicitly models attribute unreliability during the training of unseen-class classifiers. Each unseen class is represented by an attribute signature. Trees are trained one-vs-all using signatures as “exemplars,” but splits are selected by propagating fractional counts derived from the attribute classifiers’ ROC statistics computed on held-out attribute-labeled validation data. This allows the training procedure to prefer attributes, thresholds, and node contexts that will remain discriminative given expected TP/FP rates at test time. The method also includes (i) a mechanism for uncertainty in class-attribute associations and (ii) a few-shot extension combining information gain from signatures with that from a small set of class-labeled images. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show substantial improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with controlled-noise studies clarifying the advantages.

Relation to prior work
The approach is situated within attribute-based zero-shot learning (e.g., Farhadi et al., Lampert et al./DAP, Parikh & Grauman), but departs by incorporating attribute error models during the learning of the unseen classifier rather than only at prediction time. It contrasts with label- or feature-embedding methods (e.g., DeViSE, Akata et al.) by preserving the ability to define new classes directly via semantic attributes. The fractional propagation idea is related to decision tree methods for missing/uncertain features (Quinlan; Olaru & Wehenkel; Tsang et al.), but is adapted to a zero-shot setting where training instances are signatures and test inputs are attribute predictions. The few-shot extension connects to prior work combining semantic priors with small labeled sets.

Strengths
- Clear, principled treatment of attribute unreliability via ROC-aware fractional propagation and node-specific operating points, moving the field beyond “assume-perfect-attributes” training.
- Sound intuition: the trees will: (a) prefer attributes that are both discriminative and reliable, (b) choose thresholds that optimize discriminability under realistic error rates, and (c) discard splits that are unlearnable (TPR≈FPR).
- Careful experimental design: three datasets, controlled-noise analyses, ablations (signature uncertainty, node-specific ROC vs. global), and few-shot results. Gains over DAP are consistent and largest when attributes are weakest—exactly where modeling unreliability should help.
- Practicality: uses standard attribute learners; few additional hyperparameters; good reporting of implementation details (depths, (#m,#t), ROC fallback).
- Few-shot extension provides a simple and effective way to inject a semantic prior into discriminative trees.

Weaknesses and questions
- Domain shift in ROC estimation: The ROC statistics are computed on seen-class attribute-labeled data and assumed to transfer to unseen classes. The paper acknowledges this and shows an “upper bound” with true ROC on unseen images. Still, the main method would benefit from analysis of when transfer fails (e.g., attributes whose visual manifestation shifts across seen→unseen). Could calibration methods or covariate-shift correction improve robustness?
- Data sparsity at nodes: When DV(n) has few positives/negatives, the method falls back to global DV. How sensitive are results to the threshold of 10 examples? Some stability analysis (e.g., varying the minimum count) would help.
- Attribute dependence: While the node-specific ROC helps capture context, the method still uses univariate splits. Strongly correlated attributes can induce misleading local ROC estimates. Oblique or multivariate tests could better account for attribute interactions.
- Scalability: Training one forest per unseen class scales linearly in K. For larger K, can a multi-class forest or shared structure reduce cost without losing the benefits of one-vs-all?
- Few-shot comparisons: The few-shot results show clear gains over a “train-on-few-images-only” baseline, but comparisons to alternative zero/few-shot methods that also blend semantics and data (e.g., label embeddings, metric learning with priors) would strengthen claims in this regime.
- SUN split: The SUN unseen classes are randomly selected; while repeated trials help, fixed community splits would aid comparability.
- Signature uncertainty: The paper mentions a revised propagation for annotation noise; details are deferred to the supplement. A brief intuition and one ablation demonstrating its isolated effect (beyond the existing ablation) would improve clarity.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound, and the fractional propagation based on ROC estimates is well-motivated. The information gain derivations and behavior in edge cases (TPR=FPR) are convincing. Experiments substantiate claims with both synthetic and real settings and include useful ablations.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The step-by-step build-up (basic signature RF → ROC-aware propagation → signature uncertainty → few-shot) is easy to follow. Including schematic pseudocode (in the supplement) and concrete implementation choices helps reproducibility. A bit more detail in the main text on signature uncertainty and complexity would be helpful.

Originality
The idea to “train for test-time unreliability” by integrating attribute ROC behavior into the tree-building process for zero-shot is novel and distinct from prior attribute-based and embedding-based approaches. The adaptation of fractional propagation to the zero-shot signature-training regime is original.

Significance
Zero/few-shot learning remains important, and the demonstrated improvements over widely used baselines (DAP) across datasets are meaningful. The approach is practical and likely to be adopted by researchers using attributes or other mid-level semantic detectors. The few-shot extension is simple and useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Analyze sensitivity to the amount and composition of DV; e.g., how performance varies with the fraction reserved, and whether focusing DV on seen classes with signatures closest to unseen helps.
- Evaluate calibration strategies (e.g., isotonic regression) for attribute scores and their impact on ROC stability.
- Report computational cost and training time per dataset and per K to assess scalability.
- Include a small study of multivariate splits or correlation-aware tests, or at least quantify attribute correlation and discuss potential impact.
- Provide additional few-shot baselines that also leverage semantic priors.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and under-explored weakness of zero-shot attribute methods.
- Methodologically sound, simple, and effective.
- Strong empirical gains and informative analyses/ablations.
- Practical and extensible to few-shot learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on ROC transfer from seen to unseen classes without deeper domain-shift handling.
- Limited exploration of attribute dependence and scalability to large K.
- Few-shot comparisons could be broader.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution that advances zero/few-shot learning with a principled and effective treatment of attribute unreliability. I recommend acceptance after minor clarifications and additional sensitivity analyses as suggested above.
