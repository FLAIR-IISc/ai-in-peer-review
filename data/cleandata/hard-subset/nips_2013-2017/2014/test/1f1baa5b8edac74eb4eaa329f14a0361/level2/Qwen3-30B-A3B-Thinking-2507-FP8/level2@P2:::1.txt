

This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions, a critical limitation of existing methods. The authors introduce a random forest framework that leverages the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers to select discriminative decision nodes, thereby building more robust models for unseen classes. The method is well-motivated, addressing a genuine gap in zero-shot learning literature where existing approaches treat attribute predictions as perfect.

The paper's main contributions are clear and well-executed. The core innovation lies in the fractional propagation of training signatures through the forest, where each class signature is distributed across tree paths according to the attribute classifiers' error tendencies. This is a significant improvement over prior work that simply uses attribute signatures as idealized input. The authors further extend their approach to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning, with the few-shot extension being particularly valuable for practical applications.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines (DAP and SIGNATURE RF) across three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). Their controlled noise experiments effectively illustrate how the method handles varying levels of attribute unreliability, with particularly strong results when noise levels differ per attribute. The ablation studies clearly isolate the contributions of each component (signature uncertainty modeling and ROC propagation), providing strong evidence for their approach's effectiveness.

The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the technical approach and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The related work section properly contextualizes their contribution, distinguishing their approach from existing zero-shot methods and highlighting how they address limitations of prior work. The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers like [8, 18, 30].

I have a few minor concerns that could be addressed with minor revisions. First, while the paper demonstrates that modeling attribute unreliability is important, it doesn't provide much insight into why certain attributes are more reliable than others. Understanding this could help guide future work on attribute selection. Second, the reliance on validation data for ROC estimation might be problematic in settings where such data is scarce. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could provide more discussion on practical implications.

The paper's significance is strong. Zero-shot learning has the potential to address important real-world problems like recognizing rare categories or dynamically defining new classes, but current methods suffer from the unreliability of attribute predictions. This paper demonstrates that by properly modeling this unreliability, zero-shot learning can achieve meaningful performance gains. The results on AwA using discovered attributes are particularly compelling, showing state-of-the-art performance with a generalization of their method.

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't fully explore the relationship between attribute reliability and class discriminability. For instance, it's unclear whether the method's improvements stem from better handling of unreliable attributes or from implicitly filtering out less discriminative attributes. A more detailed analysis of which attributes are most reliable and how they contribute to the final classification would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a substantive contribution to zero-shot learning. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is technically sound, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the approach's advantages. The paper addresses a critical limitation of current zero-shot learning methods that has been overlooked in prior work, and the proposed solution is both elegant and practical.

The authors have made a strong case for the value of considering attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning, which has been a significant barrier to practical applications. Their method is generalizable to other semantic mid-level concepts beyond visual attributes, potentially extending its impact beyond computer vision. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it's technically sound, clearly written, original in its approach, and significant for the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to zero-shot learning that addresses a critical limitation of current methods, with strong empirical results and clear technical insights. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable in the camera-ready version and don't detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
