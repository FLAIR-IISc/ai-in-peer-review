This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning, specifically tackling the issue of unreliable attribute predictions. The work proposes a random forest approach that incorporates attribute error tendencies during training, leading to more robust discriminative models for unseen classes. The approach is extended to the few-shot scenario and also handles unreliable class-attribute associations. The experiments on three datasets demonstrate improvements over existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated. Zero-shot learning is a challenging problem, and the observation that attribute prediction inaccuracies limit practical application is valid and insightful. The proposed approach, leveraging ROC curves to account for attribute unreliability, is a novel and reasonable direction. The extension to few-shot learning and handling unreliable class-attribute associations further broaden the applicability of the work. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons to established baselines (DAP) and ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of different components. The presentation of results, with clear tables and figures, allows for straightforward assessment of the method’s performance.

A strength of the work is its focus on a practical limitation of zero-shot learning – unreliable attributes. While prior work acknowledges attribute strength, the explicit modeling of *unreliability* using ROC curves is a significant contribution. The extension to account for signature uncertainty and the few-shot setting demonstrates a thoughtful approach to generalizing the method. The results are convincing, consistently showing improvements over the baseline methods on all datasets, and the ablation studies confirm the effectiveness of the proposed components. The discussion of related work is comprehensive and accurately positions the proposed method within the existing landscape. The paper also clearly explains the motivation behind each design choice.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. While the experimental results are strong, the discussion of *why* the method works could be expanded. A deeper analysis of which attributes benefit most from the proposed approach, and how the learned decision trees utilize these attributes, would provide valuable insights. The parameter settings (tree depth, λ) are reported but the justification for these values could be elaborated, perhaps with a brief sensitivity analysis. It would also be helpful to discuss the computational cost of the approach, particularly the overhead of computing and storing ROC curves.  Although the paper mentions scalability as a direction for future work, discussing the current limitations would be beneficial. Additionally, the connection to existing work on handling missing or uncertain data in decision trees (mentioned in the related work section) could be explored more thoroughly, highlighting the specific contributions of this work in the context of zero-shot learning.  Finally, the phrase "OURS+TRUE ROC" in Table 1 is unclear – a more descriptive explanation would be helpful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to addressing a practical limitation of zero-shot learning.
*   Strong experimental results on multiple datasets.
*   Thorough ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The explanation of *why* the method works could be more in-depth.
*   Discussion of computational cost and scalability is limited.
*   Connection to related work on handling uncertainty could be strengthened.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by thorough experiments. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.  The potential impact of this work is significant, as it offers a practical solution to a key challenge in zero-shot learning, paving the way for more robust and reliable applications in scenarios with limited labeled data.
