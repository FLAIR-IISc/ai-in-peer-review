- Proposes zero-shot learning using random forests that incorporate attribute classifier unreliability via validation-estimated TPR/FPR in split information gain.
- Method propagates category–attribute signatures along multiple tree paths proportionally to attribute uncertainty for more robust splits.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough and generally supports the method’s effectiveness quantitatively.
- Lacks stronger qualitative analysis showing which attributes or categories benefit and representative success/failure cases.
- Under-specified methodological choices: rationale for 80/20 train–validation split and sensitivity to split ratio and validation set size.
- Hyperparameter selection (number of trees, depth, etc.) and protocol to avoid overfitting are not clearly described.
- Stability of reliability estimates across random splits and statistical significance of improvements across seeds are not reported.
- Computational cost (training and inference) relative to standard random forests is not discussed.
- Paper is well written and clear; a schematic of how reliability-modified information gain routes signatures would aid intuition.
- Explicit modeling of attribute reliability (TPR/FPR) is a novel and principled contribution compared to prior implicit handling of noise.
- Methodological novelty is moderate but practically relevant and broadly applicable for robustness in attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Likely to stimulate research on calibrated attribute use and uncertainty-aware decision criteria.
- Suggested ablations: compare standard RF, attribute-strength-only RF, and the proposed reliability-aware RF.
- Suggested additional analyses: per-attribute/per-class breakdowns, sensitivity to train–validation split and validation sample size, comparisons with calibrated probabilities, variance/ statistical significance reporting, and computational overhead.
