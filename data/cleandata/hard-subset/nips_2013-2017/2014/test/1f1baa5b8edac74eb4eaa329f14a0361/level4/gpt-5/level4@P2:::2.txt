Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. Instead of taking predicted attributes at face value, the authors train one-vs-all random forests over class-attribute signatures and, during tree construction, propagate each signature fractionally to child nodes based on the attribute classifiers’ node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) estimated on a held-out validation set. This yields an information gain criterion that prefers splits likely to remain discriminative under realistic attribute prediction noise. The method also extends to (i) uncertain class signatures and (ii) a few-shot setting by combining signature-based and data-based information gain with a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show improvements over DAP and a baseline random forest trained directly on signatures, with ablations for signature uncertainty and ROC propagation.

The work is well positioned relative to prior zero-shot methods that rely on two-stage attribute prediction (e.g., DAP) or embeddings (e.g., label embeddings, DeVISE) and those that discover mid-level features that may not be nameable. Unlike prior attribute-based approaches, this method explicitly incorporates attribute unreliability into the learning objective. The fractional propagation idea connects to decision trees that handle missing/uncertain data, but the zero-shot training-from-signatures setting is distinct.

Quality:
- Strengths: The technical approach is sound and carefully implemented. Using node-specific ROC estimates to modulate split decisions is principled and aligns with the test-time uncertainties. The controlled-noise experiments convincingly isolate the benefit of robustness to attribute noise, and the three-dataset evaluation with ablations supports the claims. The few-shot extension is natural and empirically useful; the shift in optimal λ as data increases is sensible.
- Weaknesses: 
  - Limited qualitative analysis. The paper would benefit from illustrating which attributes and classes drive the gains (e.g., per-attribute reliability vs. utility, examples of learned splits, or per-class error breakdowns).
  - Hyperparameter choices are under-explained. For example, the rationale for the 80/20 split for ROC estimation, maximum depths, and the number of (attribute, threshold) tests per node should be justified beyond stating cross-validation. How sensitive are results to these settings? 
  - Clarify data usage. There appears to be tension between “reserving 20% for ROC validation” and statements about training the final attribute classifiers; a precise accounting of what portion of data is used for attribute SVM training vs. ROC estimation vs. model selection would improve reproducibility.
  - Assumptions about ROC stationarity under domain shift (seen attribute data vs. unseen classes) are acknowledged but not deeply analyzed; discussing failure modes or diagnostics when the validation distribution mismatches the unseen classes would be helpful.
  - Scalability: Some discussion of training/test-time complexity (trees per class, depth, and number of K unseen classes) and memory requirements would help assess practicality for larger K.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The step-by-step exposition—from basic signature forests to ROC-aware splitting, signature uncertainty, and few-shot extension—is effective. Providing pseudocode and a schematic (as referenced) is helpful; including at least a compact version in the main paper would aid readability.

Originality:
Focusing on attribute reliability rather than strength, and baking ROC-driven uncertainty into the split criterion for zero-shot tree learning from signatures, is a novel and useful perspective. While the random forest machinery is standard, the adaptation to zero-shot training with node-specific ROC-driven fractional propagation is, to my knowledge, new and distinct from prior attribute-based zero-shot methods.

Significance:
The improvements over DAP and signature-only forests across three datasets indicate practical impact for zero-shot and few-shot learning with unreliable attributes—a common real-world setting. The approach is sufficiently general to apply to discovered “pseudo-attributes” and other semantic mid-level cues. That said, the gains will likely depend on the availability and representativeness of attribute-labeled validation data for estimating ROC curves; providing guidance on obtaining such data or making the method robust to limited validation would increase impact.

Pros:
- Principled handling of attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC-based fractional propagation and information gain.
- Consistent empirical gains over strong baselines across multiple datasets; informative ablations.
- Natural few-shot extension with interpretable mixing of priors and data.
- Applicable to both named and discovered attributes.

Cons:
- Limited qualitative and diagnostic analysis; unclear which attributes/classes benefit most and why.
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection and data splits; sensitivity analysis is light.
- Assumes ROC generalizes from seen to unseen classes; robustness to mismatch is not deeply explored.
- Scalability and computational costs are not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add qualitative examples of learned trees/splits and per-attribute impact analyses (e.g., gains vs. TPR/FPR, calibration).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for: validation split ratio, tree depth, number of candidate thresholds, and λ selection.
- Clarify the exact protocol for using data for SVM training vs. ROC estimation vs. model selection, and ensure fairness across baselines.
- Include runtime/complexity analysis and discuss extensions to handle many unseen classes (e.g., shared forests or multi-label trees).
- Explore techniques to mitigate domain shift in ROC estimation (e.g., reweighting validation data, meta-modeling TPR/FPR as functions of context).

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed paper with a solid, novel idea that meaningfully improves zero-shot and few-shot recognition with attributes. While the core machinery is not new, the explicit reliability modeling and its demonstrated benefits make this a valuable contribution.
