This paper proposes a novel random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that effectively accommodates the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. The uncertainty is quantified by measuring the performance of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set, using metrics such as true positive rate and false positive rate. The authors incorporate this performance information into the information gain computation during the learning of the random forest, allowing the model to account for the uncertainty of attribute predictions when pursuing a given category-attribute signature along multiple paths of a decision tree.

Quality: The proposed method is well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper would benefit from additional qualitative analysis, such as an examination of the types of attributes and categories that are most affected or improved by this method. Furthermore, the authors could provide more insight into the selection of parameters, including the choice of an 80%-20% split for training-validation, to enhance the paper's overall rigor.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Originality: While previous works have primarily focused on addressing the problem of "attribute strength," this paper makes a novel contribution by exploring the lesser-studied issue of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is distinct in its explicit handling of this problem.

Significance: Although the proposed random forest method is not new, the idea of modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning performance is interesting and thought-provoking for researchers working on attribute-based methods. While the idea and method introduced in this paper may not be significantly novel, their application to improving zero-shot learning is revealing and warrants further exploration.
