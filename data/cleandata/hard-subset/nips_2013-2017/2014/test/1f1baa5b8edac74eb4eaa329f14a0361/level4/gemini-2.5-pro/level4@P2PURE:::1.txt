This paper addresses the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While theory suggests that novel classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors, the practical challenge is that learning these attribute classifiers can be as difficult, or more so, than learning the object classes directly.

The proposed method trains random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors. Crucially, the training procedure incorporates the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree at training time. The authors also show how the method can be extended to the few-shot learning setting, where a small number of training examples from the test categories are available. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several standard attribute datasets.

**QUALITY:** The authors effectively address a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning using a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound. The empirical results on three standard datasets are strong. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, featuring ablation studies, experiments with artificial noise, an analysis of both zero-shot and few-shot settings, and thorough comparisons with the literature.

**CLARITY:** The paper is very well written. One minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 with the introduction of the threshold `t`. Since the attribute signatures are binary at this point, any value of `t` where 0 < `t` < 1 does not change the outcome of equation (2). This makes it initially unclear how a novel test example can be propagated down the tree (lines 207-212), as `t` seems ill-defined in this context. This point is, however, clarified in subsequent sections.

**ORIGINALITY:** The paper presents a novel and effective application of the random forest framework to an important problem.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, making a strong, interesting, and sound contribution. The results, demonstrated on three datasets under a variety of settings, are convincing. This paper merits publication.
