- Proposes zero-shot method that builds class classifiers from attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute predictor unreliability via node-specific ROC estimates in a random forest.
- Trains attribute SVMs with Platt scaling, then trains per-class random forests using class–attribute signatures as exemplars.
- During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated down branches according to node-specific TPR/FPR estimates from a held-out validation set.
- Extends the approach to model uncertainty in class signatures and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven and image-driven information gains with a weighting λ.
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN with controlled-noise studies, ablations, and comparisons to DAP and signature-only baselines, showing consistent improvements.
- Differs from prior work (e.g., DAP) by using ROC-based calibration and implicit modeling of correlated attribute errors via validation-set propagation.
- Relates to methods handling missing/uncertain features in trees, but treats signatures as training data and uses validation-learned uncertainties to guide splits.
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation with a thorough related work section.
- Strength: conceptually appealing use of node-specific ROC estimates to favor splits robust to realistic attribute errors.
- Strength: addresses both attribute unreliability and signature uncertainty and naturally extends to few-shot.
- Strength: solid experimental design with multiple datasets, controlled-noise experiments, and ablation studies.
- Empirical finding: modeling attribute error tendencies yields the largest gains where attributes are least reliable.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental; prior methods already perform calibration and the claim of being the first to “handle unreliability” is overstated.
- Weakness: reliance on node-specific ROC estimates raises sample-efficiency concerns as validation data get partitioned; fallback to global stats is heuristic and underanalyzed.
- Weakness: few-shot extension is underdeveloped; weighted information gain is plausible but lacks a principled probabilistic derivation.
- Weakness: reported gains are modest in some settings and need more analysis explaining when the method excels.
- Suggestion: temper novelty claims and cite related calibration/fusion work (e.g., Scheirer et al.).
- Suggestion: analyze sensitivity to validation set size, tree depth, node thresholds, and quantify fallback heuristic effects.
- Suggestion: compare ROC-based calibration to alternative calibrations (Platt/temperature scaling) and to an independent-attribute variant.
- Suggestion: strengthen few-shot section with probabilistic/Bayesian derivation linking λ to data size and provide more λ ablations.
- Suggestion: discuss computational cost and scalability with many attributes/classes and whether shallow trees/pruning suffice.
- Pros for acceptance: practical mechanism improving robustness in zero-/few-shot settings, consistent empirical improvements, generalizable method.
- Cons against acceptance: incremental novelty, limited analysis of node-level sample complexity/stability, and a brief few-shot treatment.
- Assessment: quality good but needs deeper validation-analysis; clarity very good though few-shot is terse; originality moderate; significance moderate-to-good.
- Overall: well-executed paper recommended for acceptance if novelty claims are tempered and analyses of node-level calibration and few-shot formulation are strengthened.
