This paper introduces a random-forest-based approach to attribute-driven zero-shot learning. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is then learned over class–attribute signature vectors, while a held-out set of attribute-labeled images is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the trees. An extension to few-shot learning is also presented.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well written, with a solid summary of related work.

Originality: The contribution is of modest novelty, though not too low for acceptance. The claim that the method uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions, in contrast to prior work, feels overstated. For instance, direct attribute prediction [7] (DAP)—the baseline here and in many prior papers—has related mechanisms for handling attribute uncertainty. Both follow the same high-level recipe:
a) Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical in both lines of work).
b) Train a second-stage class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, using a validation set to calibrate attribute reliability ([6] employs Platt scaling and a probabilistic fusion model; the proposed method uses a new random-forest-based strategy).
Thus, I do not view the approach as a fundamentally new way to manage attribute uncertainty. Its main differences are a more expressive treatment of attribute noise: 1) it uses ROC-based calibration rather than a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) whereas [6] assumes independent attributes, the proposed method effectively captures joint statistics of attribute errors by propagating the validation set down the trees. In this sense, it may be more accurate to frame the contribution as improved modeling of attribute correlations (a potentially relevant citation is Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” though I am not deeply familiar with that area).

A potential drawback relative to methods that treat attributes independently is the likely need for a large validation set to obtain reliable probability estimates once the data are partitioned within decision trees. This concern is largely glossed over, and the proposed remedy (line 323) appears heuristic. Additional experiments analyzing sensitivity to training and validation set sizes would strengthen the paper.

Quality: Overall, the paper is of good quality, and the technical choices and descriptions are sensible. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the claims, though the quantitative gains are not substantial enough to justify acceptance on empirical results alone. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest: whereas earlier sections are thorough, this part is quite brief. Conceptually, a method that combines class–attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when modeling p(a|...) might be more intuitive than a weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6). In summary, this is a well written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experiments; however, the central claim of offering a new solution to unreliable attributes is somewhat debatable.
