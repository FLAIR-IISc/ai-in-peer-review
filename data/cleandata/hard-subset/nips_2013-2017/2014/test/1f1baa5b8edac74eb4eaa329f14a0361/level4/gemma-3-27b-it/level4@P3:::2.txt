This paper proposes a novel random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that explicitly addresses the unreliability of attribute classifier predictions. The core idea is to leverage performance metrics (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) of attribute classifiers, measured on a held-out validation set, during the random forest training process. This information is incorporated into the information gain computation, allowing the decision trees to account for attribute prediction uncertainty by pursuing multiple paths for a given category-attribute signature.

The paper begins by contextualizing the problem within the broader field of visual recognition, highlighting the limitations of traditional supervised learning and the promise of zero-shot learning. It clearly articulates the gap in existing zero-shot approaches – a lack of explicit modeling of attribute prediction unreliability – and positions the proposed method as a direct response to this challenge. The approach is well-motivated, building upon the observation that attributes are often correlated and difficult to predict accurately. Extending this to the few-shot setting and accounting for unreliable class-attribute associations further demonstrates the method's versatility.

**Quality:** The technical soundness of the paper is strong. The proposed method is well-defined and supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN). The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., removing signature uncertainty or ROC propagation) is particularly valuable for understanding the contribution of each component. The quantitative results demonstrate significant improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods, especially DAP [8, 9]. However, the paper would benefit from some qualitative analysis. Visualizing decision boundaries or providing examples of attributes where the method shows the most improvement could offer deeper insights. It’s also unclear how sensitive the results are to the 80%-20% split used for training and validation – a brief discussion of this parameter choice would strengthen the analysis. The paper would also benefit from discussing computational complexity. 

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The technical details are presented in a logical manner, with clear explanations of the key concepts and equations. The use of diagrams and pseudocode in the supplementary material is helpful. The authors effectively build intuition by explaining the rationale behind each design choice.

**Originality:** While random forests are a well-established technique, the application of this method to explicitly model attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning appears novel. The paper effectively distinguishes its work from previous approaches that have focused on “attribute strength” rather than “attribute reliability.” The idea of utilizing receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves to inform the tree-building process is a creative and insightful contribution. The connection to related work (fractional tuples, handling missing values) is appropriately acknowledged and contextualized.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and demonstrate a clear advancement over existing zero-shot learning methods. The ability to leverage unreliable attributes effectively addresses a critical limitation of current approaches. The extension to the few-shot setting further enhances the practical relevance of the work. The method is likely to be of interest to researchers working on attribute-based learning, transfer learning, and zero-shot recognition. The demonstrated improvement over DAP, a commonly used baseline, is significant. The findings suggest a promising direction for future research in visual recognition, particularly in scenarios where labeled data is scarce or unavailable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to address a key limitation of zero-shot learning.
*   Strong experimental results on three datasets, consistently outperforming state-of-the-art methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the technical details.
*   Thorough ablation studies provide insights into the contribution of each component.
*   Extension to the few-shot setting enhances practical relevance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of qualitative analysis to provide deeper insights into the method's behavior.
*   Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity (e.g., 80%-20% split).
*   No discussion of computational complexity.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, well-supported by experimental results, and clearly presented. While some minor improvements could be made (e.g., adding qualitative analysis and discussing parameter sensitivity), the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
