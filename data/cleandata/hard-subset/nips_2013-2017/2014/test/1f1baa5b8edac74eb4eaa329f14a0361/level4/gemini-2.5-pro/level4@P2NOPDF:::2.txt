This paper introduces a random forest-based method for zero-shot learning designed to better accommodate the uncertainty inherent in attribute classifier predictions. The core idea is to quantify this uncertainty by measuring the performance (e.g., true positive rate, false positive rate) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. This performance data is then integrated into the information gain computation during the training of the random forest. This novel approach allows the model to account for attribute unreliability, for instance, by pursuing a single category-attribute signature along multiple paths within a decision tree.

**Quality:** The paper's claims are well-supported by thorough quantitative analysis. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by including a qualitative analysis. For instance, an examination of the specific types of attributes and categories that are most affected or improved by this method would provide valuable insight into its practical behavior. Furthermore, the manuscript lacks justification for certain hyperparameter choices. For example, the rationale for selecting an 80%-20% training-validation split is not explained, leaving the reader to wonder about the sensitivity of the results to this parameter.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.

**Originality:** The authors effectively distinguish their work from previous efforts, which have largely focused on "attribute strength," by instead addressing the less-explored problem of "attribute reliability." While many learning algorithms may implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit approach to modeling and handling this uncertainty within the learning framework. This represents a distinct and original contribution.

**Significance:** Although the proposed machine learning model (the random forest) is not new, the central idea introduced in this paper is both insightful and thought-provoking for the attribute-based learning community. The concept of modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to directly improve zero-shot learning performance is a valuable contribution. The application of this idea demonstrates a promising new direction for making attribute-based models more robust.
