This paper tackles a central obstacle in attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are imperfect and their errors undermine downstream class recognition. The authors propose a random forest approach that trains directly on class–attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. During tree construction, they propagate class signatures fractionally through candidate splits according to attribute-specific ROC statistics estimated on a held-out, attribute-labeled validation set. This yields splits that are both discriminative and resilient to the expected error patterns of the attribute classifiers. They further (i) incorporate uncertainty in class signatures themselves and (ii) extend the method to few-shot learning by blending signature-driven information gain with standard supervised information gain using a weighting parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show strong gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest, with informative ablations and controlled-noise studies. The approach also works with discovered (pseudo)attributes.

Relation to prior work: Unlike DAP and two-stage similarity-to-signature methods, this model does not take attribute predictions at face value; it uses ROC-informed, node-specific error estimates to guide split selection. Compared to zero-shot methods based on label/feature embeddings or hierarchical adaptation, this work retains the advantage of human-interpretable, language-based category specification. While related to decision trees for missing/uncertain data, the zero-shot setting and the use of signatures as the sole training signal are novel.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant formulation that connects zero-shot learning with random forests via fractional propagation using ROC curves.
- Strong empirical results across three datasets, including substantial gains where attributes are especially noisy.
- Thorough evaluation: ablations (signature uncertainty, ROC propagation), controlled noise experiments, few-shot extension with sensible behavior as labeled data increases.
- Practical advantages: uses standard attribute SVMs, supports both named and discovered attributes, and offers interpretable decisions via split attributes and thresholds.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity around thresholds in Sec. 3.2.1: with binary signatures, any 0 < t < 1 yields identical splits under the basic formulation, making t appear ill-defined when describing test-time traversal. Consider stating explicitly that thresholds become meaningful once attribute unreliability is incorporated (Sec. 3.2.2), and move a brief preview of that mechanism earlier.
- Dependence on validation data: the method assumes ROC statistics from seen classes approximate errors on unseen combinations. It would help to analyze sensitivity to the amount and domain match of this validation set, and to report runtime/overhead from node-specific ROC estimation.
- Scalability and complexity: some discussion or measurements of training time/memory as a function of attributes, unseen classes, and tree depth would strengthen the paper, especially given per-node ROC computation and fractional propagation.
- Attribute correlations: although node-specific validation subsets partially capture dependencies, the model still tests one attribute at a time. A brief discussion of potential multi-attribute tests or decorrelation strategies (beyond future work) would be useful.
- Additional baselines: where feasible, include recent label/feature embedding zero-shot methods in the unified evaluation on the same splits, or clarify differences if results are not directly comparable. A calibrated-DAP baseline using ROC-informed thresholds could further contextualize gains.

Quality
- Technically sound and well-motivated. The fractional propagation and information-gain objective based on node-specific error rates are appropriate and carefully implemented. Experiments are solid, with statistically reported results, sensible hyperparameter choices, and informative ablations. Some additional sensitivity and complexity analyses would further bolster the claims.

Clarity
- Generally very well written and easy to follow. The main point of confusion is the role of t in the basic (binary-signature) formulation prior to introducing unreliability. Minor reorganization and an upfront note would resolve this.

Originality
- Applying random forests to zero-shot via ROC-driven fractional propagation of signatures is a novel and interesting contribution distinct from prior attribute-based and embedding approaches, and from trees for missing/uncertain data.

Significance
- The work addresses a key practical barrier to attribute-based zero-shot learning and shows consistent, meaningful improvements. The few-shot extension is natural and useful. The approach is likely to influence how attribute unreliability is handled in zero/few-shot settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general idea with clear practical impact.
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines on multiple datasets.
- Careful experimental design with insightful ablations and controlled noise tests.
- Interpretable and compatible with both named and discovered attributes.

Cons against acceptance
- Some clarity and completeness gaps (threshold interpretation; limited analysis of validation set dependence and computational costs).
- Limited discussion of attribute correlations and scalability.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper is well-executed, addresses an important problem with a clean and effective solution, and presents convincing evidence across multiple settings.
