This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is trained on class-attribute signature vectors, while a validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well-written, effectively summarizing related work.

Originality: The novelty of this paper is moderate. The claim that the proposed approach uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions is overstated, as existing work, such as Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7], incorporates mechanisms for handling attribute uncertainty. Both approaches involve training attribute classifiers and then estimating class probabilities based on attribute predictions, utilizing a validation set to assess attribute reliability (e.g., Platt scaling and probabilistic modeling in [7], versus the random forest-based method here). 

However, the proposed approach offers refinements in modeling attribute noise: it utilizes an ROC-based estimate instead of a sigmoidal model and, importantly, implicitly models joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set down the decision trees, addressing attribute correlations. Framing the contribution as a more sophisticated method for modeling attribute correlation may be more accurate; relevant work in this area includes Scheirer et al. ["Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search"].

A potential limitation is the need for a substantial validation set to obtain reliable probability estimates as it is subdivided during tree propagation. This concern, briefly addressed in the paper (line 323), would benefit from more thorough investigation, including experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size.

Quality: The paper’s technical quality is generally strong, with sound choices and clear descriptions. The experiments are appropriate and support the claims, though the magnitude of quantitative improvement may not be sufficient for acceptance based solely on empirical results. The few-shot learning extension (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the rest of the work. While the preceding sections are detailed, this extension is concise. A more intuitive approach might involve combining class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates directly in the probability estimation, rather than a weighted information gain combination (Eq. 6). Overall, this is a well-written paper with reasonable technical decisions and adequate experiments; however, the central claim of novelty in addressing attribute unreliability is debatable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to zero-shot learning.
*   Demonstrates improvements over strong baselines (DAP) on multiple datasets.
*   Offers a potentially valuable technique for modeling attribute correlations.
*   Provides a practical extension to the few-shot learning scenario.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of addressing attribute uncertainty is incremental, as existing methods already incorporate similar concepts.
*   The reliance on a large validation set for ROC estimation raises concerns about scalability and robustness.
*   The few-shot learning extension lacks the depth of explanation found in the rest of the paper.
*   The quantitative improvements, while statistically significant, are not substantial.
