The paper addresses the disconnect between the idealized promise and practical limitations of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While, in principle, new classes can be recognized solely from pre-trained attribute predictors, in practice the attribute classifiers themselves are often as hard (or harder) to learn than object categories. The authors propose training random forests on attribute vectors for unseen classes while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability by propagating a validation set through the trees during training. They also present an extension to incorporate a small number of labeled examples for the target classes (few-shot). Experiments on multiple attribute datasets show state-of-the-art performance.

QUALITY: The paper presents a well-conceived and technically sound random forest solution to a core zero-shot learning challenge. Empirical results on three standard benchmarks are strong. The experimental section is thorough: ablations, controlled noise studies, both zero- and few-shot regimes, and comprehensive comparisons to prior work are all included.

CLARITY: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. One point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: with binary attribute signatures, any threshold 0 < t < 1 leaves Eq. (2) unchanged, so it is initially unclear (lines 207–212) how a test instance is routed through the tree when t appears underdefined. This ambiguity is resolved in later sections when unreliability and ROC-driven thresholds are introduced.

ORIGINALITY: The work offers a thoughtful adaptation of random forests to zero-shot learning by integrating attribute error characteristics into tree construction, which is a novel and relevant contribution.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning and demonstrates clear, convincing gains. Given the breadth of settings (three datasets, zero- and few-shot) and the careful evaluation, the paper merits publication.
