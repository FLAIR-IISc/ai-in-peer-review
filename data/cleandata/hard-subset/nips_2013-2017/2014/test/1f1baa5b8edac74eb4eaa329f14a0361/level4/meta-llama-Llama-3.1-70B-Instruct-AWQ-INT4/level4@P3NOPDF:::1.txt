This paper bridges the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning, which aims to automatically recognize novel classes using pre-trained attribute predictors. However, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging or even more so than learning the object classes themselves.

The authors propose a method that uses random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors. Notably, the training procedure incorporates the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree at training time. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate how their method can be extended to handle training with a few training examples of test categories. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

The quality of the paper is high, as the authors provide an elegant and theoretically sound solution to the fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The results on three standard datasets are strong, and the authors conduct thorough ablation studies, introduce artificial noise, evaluate several settings (zero- versus few-shot learning), and compare their method with the literature.

The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand their proposed method. However, there is a minor point of confusion in Section 3.2.1, where the introduction of the threshold t is not entirely clear. Specifically, since the attribute signatures are binary, the value of t can be any value between 0 and 1 without affecting equation (2). This confusion is later resolved in subsequent sections.

The originality of the paper lies in its application of a random forest framework to an important problem in zero-shot learning. While the approach may not be entirely novel, the authors' contribution is significant, as they address a fundamental problem in the field.

The significance of the paper is substantial, as it tackles a critical challenge in zero-shot learning. The results on three datasets, under various settings, are convincing, and the paper is well-suited for publication. Overall, the paper is strong, interesting, and sound, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
