- Presents a zero-shot learning method: train SVM attribute classifiers, then train random forests on class-attribute signatures and use a validation set to estimate attribute reliability per tree node; also proposes a few-shot extension.  
- Clarity: paper is very clear, well-written, with a thorough related-work summary.  
- Originality: novelty is limited; the claim of a new way to handle unreliable attributes is overstated because prior work (e.g., DAP) uses a similar two-stage strategy.  
- Key technical difference: replaces DAP’s Platt-scaling/probabilistic independent-attribute model with a random-forest approach that models ROC-based attribute reliability.  
- Joint-error modeling: method implicitly captures joint attribute error statistics by propagating the validation set through trees, unlike independence-assuming methods.  
- Framing suggestion: contributions are better described as a more sophisticated model for attribute correlations rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.  
- Citation suggestion: consider citing related calibration work such as Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces.”  
- Validation-set dependency: approach may require a large validation set; progressive subdivision in trees can undermine probability estimates at deep nodes and the proposed remedy is heuristic.  
- Empirical gap: paper lacks sensitivity analysis to training/validation set size to assess robustness of probability estimates.  
- Quality: technical descriptions and experiments are strong and justified, but empirical gains over baselines are modest.  
- Few-shot extension: Section 3.3 is underdeveloped; a more intuitive alternative is to combine class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates rather than the proposed weighted information-gain mix.  
- Overall: well-written and technically sound, but the central novelty claim about handling unreliable attributes is debatable.
