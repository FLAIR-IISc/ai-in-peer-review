Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses zero-shot recognition using attributes, focusing on the practical challenge that attribute classifiers are imperfect. The core contribution is a random forest framework that trains directly on class–attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. During training, a separate attribute-labeled validation set is propagated through the tree at each node to estimate node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) for each attribute and threshold. Class signatures are then fractionally propagated down the tree according to these ROC-derived probabilities, so splits are chosen to be both discriminative across classes and robust to expected attribute errors at test time. The method also includes (i) a mechanism to handle uncertainty in class–attribute signatures themselves and (ii) a few-shot extension that combines signature-based information gain with standard image-based information gain via a weighting parameter.

Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN compare the approach to SIGNATURE-RF (forests on ideal signatures) and DAP (Direct Attribute Prediction), include controlled noise studies, ablations (removing signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation), and results with discovered attributes. The method typically outperforms DAP, with larger gains when attributes are less reliable, and shows benefits in the few-shot regime.

Relative to prior zero-shot work, the paper is positioned against two-stage attribute methods (e.g., Farhadi et al., Palatucci et al., Lampert et al.’s DAP) and alternative transfer strategies (embeddings, hierarchies). Unlike DAP, which calibrates attribute posteriors and typically treats attributes conditionally independently when combining them, the proposed trees use ROC curves and node-specific validation subsets to select operating points and implicitly capture dependencies among attribute errors. The approach is also related to decision trees for uncertain or missing features, though it adapts those ideas to a zero-shot setting where training “instances” are signatures and unreliability comes from attribute predictors.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: zero-shot learning is attractive but hampered by noisy attribute predictions.
- Methodologically sound and well-explained. The fractional propagation using node-specific ROC estimates is intuitive and technically reasonable.
- Good experimental design: three datasets, controlled noise experiments, ablations that isolate contributions (ROC propagation and signature uncertainty), and an evaluation with discovered attributes.
- Consistent improvements over DAP and SIGNATURE-RF, especially when attributes are less reliable, supporting the paper’s central claim.
- Few-shot extension is natural and empirically beneficial, with trends consistent with intuition as more labeled examples become available.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
Quality
- Dependence on validation data: Because ROC statistics are estimated per node, they are computed on progressively smaller validation subsets. The paper reverts to global statistics when too few positives/negatives reach a node, which is heuristic. A more systematic analysis of how validation set size affects performance would strengthen the claims (e.g., curves varying the validation fraction; sensitivity of accuracy to the minimum count threshold).
- Probabilistic grounding of the few-shot extension: Combining information gains via a mixing parameter (Eq. 6) is simple and effective, but it lacks a clear probabilistic interpretation. An alternative framing (e.g., a unified likelihood that fuses attribute-signature priors and image-based evidence) could be more principled, or at least a short justification of why IG interpolation is appropriate would help.
- Baselines: Since the central claim is about handling attribute unreliability, comparisons to additional calibrated fusion baselines (e.g., stacking with logistic regression over attribute posteriors or other calibration/fusion strategies) would help isolate the source of improvement beyond the tree structure.

Clarity
- The paper is generally very clear and well organized. The few-shot section is comparatively brief; expanding it slightly (intuition for λ selection, computational details, and how attribute and image evidence interact during training) would improve readability.
- A brief complexity discussion (cost of per-node ROC estimation, number of candidate splits, and impact on training time) would be useful.

Originality
- The core idea—training trees on signatures while using ROC-based fractional propagation to reflect attribute unreliability—is a meaningful extension of prior attribute-based zero-shot methods. However, the novelty is incremental rather than fundamental. DAP and related work already calibrate and combine uncertain attribute outputs; the primary advances here are (i) using ROC operating points within a discriminative tree and (ii) implicitly capturing attribute error dependencies by conditioning ROC estimates on node-specific subsets.

Significance
- The gains over strong baselines on standard datasets, particularly in lower-reliability regimes, suggest practical value. The method is simple to implement on top of existing attribute pipelines and can influence follow-on work on robust mid-level representations. That said, improvements are modest in some settings, and broader comparisons (e.g., to label/feature embedding approaches when applicable) could further demonstrate impact.

Suggestions for the authors
- Temper the claim that prior work does not handle attribute unreliability; clarify that your contribution is to integrate ROC-based robustness and node-conditional estimation into the training objective, thereby handling attribute error rates and their dependencies more explicitly than methods assuming independence.
- Analyze sensitivity to the size of the validation set and the minimum count threshold for reverting to global ROC estimates.
- Provide runtime/complexity metrics and a brief discussion of scalability with M attributes and K unseen classes.
- Expand the few-shot section with a short probabilistic rationale for Eq. 6 or an ablation showing that IG interpolation outperforms straightforward probabilistic fusion baselines.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a practical and important limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Technically sound, simple, and general approach; easy to reproduce and adapt.
- Solid empirical evidence, including ablations and controlled noise studies, with consistent gains across datasets.
- Clear writing and good positioning within the literature.

Arguments against acceptance
- Novelty is incremental; DAP and calibrated fusion methods already address uncertainty, albeit differently.
- Heavy reliance on a validation set, with limited analysis of the robustness of ROC estimates at deep nodes.
- Few-shot extension is less polished and not as rigorously justified as the main method.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a clear and practically useful advance for zero-/few-shot attribute-based recognition by bringing ROC-aware robustness into the training procedure. While the conceptual novelty is moderate, the method is well executed and supported by evidence. Strengthening claims about reliance on validation data and clarifying the few-shot formulation would further solidify the contribution.
