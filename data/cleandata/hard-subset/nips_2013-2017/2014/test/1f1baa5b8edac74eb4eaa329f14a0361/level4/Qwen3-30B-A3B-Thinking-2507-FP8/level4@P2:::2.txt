

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the assumption of perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic in practice. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers during training. Instead of treating attribute predictions as perfect, their method models the error tendencies of attribute classifiers (true positive rates, false positive rates, etc.) and uses this information to guide the construction of decision trees that better discriminate unseen classes despite attribute uncertainty.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on zero-shot learning, particularly the DAP (Direct Attribute Prediction) framework [8, 9, 18, 23], which has dominated the field. While existing methods focus on "attribute strength" (how strongly an attribute is present in an image), this paper tackles "attribute reliability" (how reliably an attribute can be predicted), a less explored but crucial aspect. The authors correctly note that most previous work assumes perfect attribute predictions, which is problematic given the well-documented difficulty of learning reliable visual attributes.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and well-supported by comprehensive experiments across three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The controlled noise experiments effectively demonstrate how the method handles increasing attribute prediction errors, with particularly strong results when noise levels vary per attribute. The empirical results consistently show significant improvements over DAP (the leading zero-shot method) and SIGNATURE RF (a baseline that ignores attribute unreliability). However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis that would help understand which specific attributes or categories benefit most from the approach. For instance, it would be valuable to see examples where the method successfully handles ambiguous attributes versus cases where it still struggles. Additionally, the 80%-20% split for attribute classifier training vs. validation is not adequately justified—was this chosen based on cross-validation or is it a standard practice? A more thorough explanation would strengthen the methodological soundness.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical approach is clearly explained with appropriate mathematical formalism, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts. The distinction between "attribute reliability" and "attribute strength" is particularly well-articulated, making the paper accessible even to readers less familiar with zero-shot learning.

Originality: While random forests themselves are not novel, the authors' approach to incorporating attribute reliability information into the decision tree construction process is genuinely original. They make a compelling case that this is a distinct problem from previous work focusing on attribute strength. The paper correctly positions itself as addressing a gap in the literature, and the method's novelty is well-justified through careful comparison with related work.

Significance: The work demonstrates that zero-shot learning can be significantly improved by addressing the practical limitation of unreliable attribute predictions. The method provides a simple yet effective solution that outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on standard benchmarks. While the random forest framework itself isn't new, the specific adaptation to handle attribute unreliability represents a meaningful contribution with practical implications for real-world zero-shot applications where attribute predictions are inevitably imperfect. The few-shot extension further enhances the method's applicability to scenarios with limited labeled data.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear contribution to a practical problem in zero-shot learning; the technical approach is well-motivated and sound; the empirical evaluation is comprehensive across multiple datasets; the method is simple yet effective; the few-shot extension adds practical value.

Weaknesses: The lack of qualitative analysis to understand which attributes or categories benefit most; insufficient justification for the 80%-20% training-validation split; the paper doesn't fully explore how the method might handle cases where attribute classifiers have very different error profiles across categories.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a practical limitation in the field. While the core method (random forests) isn't novel, the specific adaptation to handle attribute unreliability is both novel and effective. The empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear practical implications. With minor improvements to address the qualitative analysis and parameter justification concerns, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
