The paper aims to reconcile the theoretical and practical aspects of attribute-based zero-shot learning, where novel classes can be identified using pre-trained attribute predictors, despite the challenges in learning these attribute classifiers. 

The authors employ random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating the reliability of attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. Furthermore, the method is extended to accommodate training with limited examples of test categories. The proposed approach yields state-of-the-art results on multiple attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors provide an elegant and theoretically sound solution to the fundamental problem of zero-shot learning using a random forest framework. The results on three standard datasets are impressive, and the authors conduct thorough ablation studies, introducing artificial noise, and evaluating various settings, including zero-shot and few-shot learning, as well as comparisons with existing literature.

CLARITY: The paper is well-written, with one minor point of confusion in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold t. Since attribute signatures remain binary, any value of 0 < t < 1 does not affect equation (2). Additionally, the propagation of a novel test example down the tree is unclear in lines 207-212 due to the seemingly ill-defined threshold t; however, this is clarified in subsequent sections.

ORIGINALITY: The application of a random forest framework to this significant problem is noteworthy.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in zero-shot learning, presenting a strong, interesting, and sound contribution. The convincing results across three datasets in various settings warrant publication.
