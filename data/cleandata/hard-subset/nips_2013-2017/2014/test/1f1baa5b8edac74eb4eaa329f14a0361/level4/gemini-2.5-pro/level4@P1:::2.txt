This paper addresses a key challenge in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions from mid-level classifiers. The authors propose a novel random forest-based method that explicitly accounts for this uncertainty during training. The core idea is to model attribute classifier unreliability by measuring performance metrics (e.g., true and false positive rates) on a held-out validation set. This information is then incorporated directly into the information gain calculation used to build the decision trees. This allows a given class-attribute signature to be propagated fractionally down multiple paths, effectively teaching the model to select splits that are not only discriminative for ideal attribute signatures but also robust to the expected noise at test time. The paper also presents extensions for handling uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and for the few-shot learning scenario.

**Quality**
The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical analysis. The quantitative experiments, including the controlled noise study, effectively demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach across three challenging datasets. The results show significant improvements over the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) baseline.

However, the evaluation could be strengthened in two areas. First, the paper lacks qualitative analysis. For example, an examination of which specific attributes or categories benefit most from the proposed model would provide valuable insight into its mechanisms and limitations. Second, the selection of several key hyperparameters is not clearly justified. For instance, the rationale behind the 80%-20% split for training and validation data, or the chosen tree depths, is not explained, leaving some questions about the tuning process and its sensitivity.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and explaining the technical details of their method.

**Originality**
The paper makes a novel contribution by shifting the focus from the well-studied problem of "attribute strength" to the less-explored issue of "attribute reliability." While many machine learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier noise, this work is notable for its explicit modeling and handling of attribute unreliability within the zero-shot learning framework. This is a fresh perspective on a persistent problem in attribute-based recognition.

**Significance**
While the base method (random forests) is not new, the core idea of modeling attribute unreliability by incorporating classifier performance on validation data directly into the tree-building process is an interesting and thought-provoking contribution. This approach is likely to be valuable to researchers working on attribute-based recognition and could inspire further work on building more robust zero-shot learning systems. The application of established techniques in this novel way provides valuable insights for the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of unreliable attribute predictions in zero-shot learning.
*   Proposes a novel method for explicitly modeling this unreliability within a random forest framework, which is a clear and well-motivated idea.
*   The distinction between "attribute reliability" and "attribute strength" is insightful and represents a new direction for the field.
*   The method is demonstrated to be effective, significantly outperforming a strong and widely used baseline (DAP) on multiple datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The evaluation is purely quantitative; a lack of qualitative analysis makes it difficult to understand *why* and *where* the method succeeds.
*   The paper provides insufficient justification for the choice of hyperparameters, which may impact reproducibility and understanding of the model's sensitivity.

Overall, while the core algorithmic components are not fundamentally new, their application to improve zero-shot learning is interesting and revealing. The paper presents a solid contribution with a novel perspective on a challenging problem.
