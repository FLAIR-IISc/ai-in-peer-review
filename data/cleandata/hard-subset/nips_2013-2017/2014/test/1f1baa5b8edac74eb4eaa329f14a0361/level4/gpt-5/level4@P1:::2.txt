This paper proposes a random-forest-based approach for zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for uncertainty in attribute predictions. Uncertainty is quantified via each attribute classifier’s performance (e.g., true/false positive rates) measured on a held-out validation set. These ROC statistics are incorporated into the information gain calculation during tree construction, so that a class–attribute signature can be fractionally propagated along multiple branches according to expected errors, rather than routed deterministically. This yields splits that are both discriminative and predictable given the attributes’ error tendencies. The idea contrasts with prior work that focuses on modeling “attribute strength”; here the emphasis is on attribute “reliability.” While many learning techniques implicitly tolerate classifier unreliability, this work is novel in explicitly modeling it for zero-shot learning.

Quality: The empirical evaluation supports the main claims with relevant quantitative analysis. However, the paper would benefit from qualitative analysis. For example, it would be informative to see which attributes and which categories benefit most, and to provide concrete qualitative examples where unreliability modeling makes a difference. In addition, details on how parameters are chosen are unclear. For instance, why was the 80%/20% split between training and validation selected? A brief rationale and/or sensitivity analysis for such choices (e.g., tree depth, number of candidate splits per node, size of the validation set) would strengthen the experimental section.

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow. The problem setup and the algorithmic description are clear, and the main idea—using ROC-based fractional propagation in the information gain computation—is communicated effectively.

Originality: The paper shifts attention from attribute strength to attribute reliability, a comparatively less explored dimension in attribute-based recognition. Although random forests and the general idea of handling uncertainty are not new and many methods implicitly accommodate unreliability, explicitly injecting attribute error statistics into the split selection for zero-shot learning is, in my view, a novel and meaningful contribution.

Significance: While the classifier family (random forests) is standard and the methodological novelty is moderate, the central idea—measuring attribute unreliability on validation data and using it to guide tree construction for zero-shot recognition—is interesting and likely to be thought-provoking for researchers working with attributes. Even if the technique itself is not a major algorithmic advance, its application to zero-shot learning is revealing and suggests a practical path to making attribute-based zero-shot models more robust.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated idea: explicitly model attribute unreliability (via ROC statistics) during training, not just attribute strength at test time.
- Solid quantitative evaluation supporting the claim that accounting for unreliability improves zero-shot performance.
- Conceptually simple and compatible with common pipelines; likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Cons:
- Limited qualitative analysis. The paper does not illustrate which specific attributes or categories drive the gains, or provide qualitative examples where the proposed model succeeds or fails.
- Insufficient detail on parameter selection and design choices (e.g., justification for the 80%/20% train/validation split, sensitivity to validation set size, tree depth, or number of candidate splits).
- Methodological novelty is modest: the base learner is standard, and some aspects of handling unreliability are implicitly present in other techniques.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add qualitative results showing representative images and attribute predictions where the proposed method helps, and analyze which attributes (e.g., those with variable FPR/TPR) the trees prefer.
- Provide a brief sensitivity study or rationale for key hyperparameters, especially the training/validation split used to estimate ROC curves.
- If space permits, summarize failure cases to clarify the method’s limits and guide future work.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a clear and useful idea. Although the underlying learning machinery is not new, the explicit treatment of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning is novel and practically valuable.
