### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel random forest-based method for zero-shot learning (ZSL) that explicitly accommodates the uncertainty inherent in attribute classifier predictions. The core idea is to measure the performance of each attribute classifier (e.g., true positive and false positive rates) on a held-out validation set. This performance data, which serves as a proxy for attribute reliability, is then integrated directly into the information gain computation during the construction of the random forest's decision trees. By doing so, the model accounts for the varying reliability of different attributes, for instance, by allowing a given category-attribute signature to be pursued along multiple paths within a tree, effectively propagating the uncertainty through the learning process.

**Quality**

The paper's claims are well-supported by thorough quantitative experiments and empirical analysis. The proposed method is benchmarked against relevant baselines, and the results demonstrate its effectiveness.

However, the evaluation could be strengthened in two key areas:
1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper is missing a qualitative analysis that could provide deeper insight into the method's behavior. For example, an analysis of which specific attributes or categories benefit most from the proposed uncertainty modeling would be valuable. Visualizations or examples illustrating how the decision-making process changes for reliable versus unreliable attributes would greatly improve the reader's understanding.
2.  **Justification for Hyperparameters:** The manuscript lacks justification for several key hyperparameter choices. A prominent example is the 80%-20% training-validation split; the rationale behind this specific ratio and an analysis of the model's sensitivity to it should be discussed. Providing this information would improve the reproducibility and robustness of the work.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The authors effectively communicate the core concepts and the motivation behind their approach, making the manuscript easy to follow for readers familiar with zero-shot learning and random forests.

**Originality**

The work's primary contribution to originality lies in its explicit focus on "attribute reliability," a problem distinct from the more commonly addressed issue of "attribute strength." While many machine learning models implicitly account for classifier noise or unreliability as part of their learning process, this paper is novel in its direct and explicit modeling of this uncertainty. By formalizing reliability based on classifier performance metrics and integrating this information directly into the tree-building process, the authors provide a new and principled perspective on handling imperfect attributes in ZSL.

**Significance**

The significance of this work does not stem from the introduction of a fundamentally new learning algorithm, as it builds upon the well-established random forest framework. Instead, its main contribution is conceptual: it introduces a principled and explicit method for incorporating attribute classifier reliability into the ZSL pipeline. The approach of using validation set performance to directly modify the information gain criterion is an insightful technique. This idea is thought-provoking for researchers in the attribute-based learning community and could inspire similar explicit uncertainty modeling in other ZSL methods and related domains where models rely on intermediate, imperfect predictions.
