

This paper introduces a novel approach to zero-shot learning (ZSL) by integrating uncertainty estimation into random forest training. The core innovation lies in explicitly modeling attribute classifier unreliability using validation-set performance metrics (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) during information gain computation. This allows the random forest to better navigate category-attribute mappings by weighting predictions according to their empirical reliability, addressing a gap overlooked in prior work that primarily focused on attribute strength rather than reliability.

**Strengths:**  
The method is well-motivated and supported by robust quantitative results. The empirical evaluation demonstrates consistent performance gains over baselines across standard ZSL benchmarks (e.g., AwA, CUB), with statistically significant improvements in top-1 and top-5 accuracy. The technical exposition is clear, with sufficient detail on the algorithmic modifications to the standard random forest framework. The paper effectively situates its contribution within the ZSL literature, distinguishing its focus on *attribute reliability*—a less explored dimension compared to attribute strength—in a way that clarifies its novelty.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary limitation is the lack of qualitative analysis to contextualize the quantitative results. The paper would benefit from examining *which specific attributes* (e.g., "has spots," "has beak") and *which categories* (e.g., birds with rare features) most significantly benefit from the uncertainty-aware approach. Such analysis would provide deeper insights into the method’s mechanics and limitations, beyond the aggregate performance metrics. Additionally, key hyperparameters (e.g., the 80%-20% training-validation split for uncertainty estimation) are introduced without justification. The authors should clarify whether this split was tuned or fixed, and whether sensitivity analyses were performed across alternative splits (e.g., 70-30, 90-10). Without this, the reproducibility and robustness of the validation strategy remain questionable.

**Originality and Significance:**  
While the use of random forests for ZSL is not novel, the paper’s explicit incorporation of *attribute reliability*—via validation-set performance—represents a meaningful conceptual advance. Previous works implicitly account for classifier unreliability through ensemble averaging or regularization, but this paper formalizes the uncertainty modeling as a first-class component of the decision process. This approach is particularly valuable for ZSL, where attribute classifiers often exhibit high variance on unseen categories. The method offers a practical, interpretable pathway to improve ZSL robustness, making it a valuable contribution for researchers focused on attribute-based learning.

**Recommendation:**  
This work presents a well-executed, incremental yet insightful contribution to ZSL. The core idea is sound, the empirical validation is rigorous, and the focus on attribute reliability is timely and well-justified. The weaknesses identified—primarily the absence of qualitative insights and hyperparameter justification—are addressable in a revision. Given the clarity of presentation and the significance of the reliability modeling angle, this paper is **strongly recommended for acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:  
1) Add qualitative analysis (e.g., attribute-category case studies showing where uncertainty modeling improves predictions),  
2) Justify key hyperparameters (e.g., validation split) and report sensitivity results,  
3) Clarify the distinction between "attribute strength" (prior work) and "attribute reliability" (this work) in the introduction.  

The method is not transformative, but its practical utility and conceptual clarity make it a worthwhile addition to the ZSL literature. It thoughtfully bridges a gap in how uncertainty is handled in attribute-based learning, offering a straightforward yet effective refinement to existing frameworks. For a conference like NIPS, this work aligns well with the trend toward more robust and interpretable machine learning systems.
