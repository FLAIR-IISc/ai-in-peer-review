- Tackles unreliability of attribute classifiers in attribute-based zero-shot learning by training on class attribute signatures rather than images.  
- Proposes a random forest with fractional propagation of signatures down multiple paths using attribute ROC statistics from a validation set.  
- Fractional propagation bakes expected classifier error into the model, favoring attributes that are both discriminative and reliably predictable.  
- Framework is extended to few-shot learning and to handle uncertainty in class-attribute signatures.  
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets, showing significant improvements over DAP and other baselines.  
- Controlled noise experiments and ablation studies effectively demonstrate robustness and isolate contributions.  
- Well-motivated practical problem: addresses unrealistic perfect-attribute assumption in ZSL.  
- Novel and elegant method: explicit use of ROC-derived fractional propagation is clever and principled.  
- Strong empirical validation across datasets with clear presentation and organization.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis: no examples of which attributes are down-weighted, tree-level splits, or per-category explanations (e.g., giant panda).  
- Hyperparameter choices (80/20 split, tree depth, few-shot λ) need more justification and sensitivity analysis, especially validation set size.  
- Does not explicitly model attribute correlations; split criterion considers one attribute at a time, which may be limiting.  
- Useful distinctions and extras: separates attribute strength vs reliability, includes an upper-bound comparison (OURS+TRUE ROC), and shows signatures as priors in few-shot.  
- Overall conclusion: strong, novel, and effective work; weaknesses are mainly limited qualitative analysis; recommendation to accept.
