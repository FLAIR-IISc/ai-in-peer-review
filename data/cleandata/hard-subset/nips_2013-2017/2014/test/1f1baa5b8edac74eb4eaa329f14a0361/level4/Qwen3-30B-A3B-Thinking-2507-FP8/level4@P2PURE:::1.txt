

The paper addresses the gap between theory and practice in attribute-based zero-shot learning. While theory posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, practice reveals that learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging or more so than learning object classes directly. The authors train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating attribute detector reliability into the training procedure by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They demonstrate how the method extends to few-shot learning scenarios with minimal examples of test categories and achieve state-of-the-art results across multiple attribute datasets.  

QUALITY: The authors well-executed a fundamental zero-shot learning challenge using a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically grounded. Results on three standard datasets are strong, supported by thorough ablation studies, artificial noise experiments, evaluations across zero-shot and few-shot settings, and comprehensive literature comparisons.  

CLARITY: The paper is well-written. A point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: the introduction of threshold $t$ occurs while attribute signatures remain binary, meaning $t$ can take any value in $(0,1)$ without altering Equation (2). Consequently, it remains unclear in lines 207–212 how a novel test example propagates through the tree, as $t$ appears ill-defined at that stage. This ambiguity is resolved in subsequent sections.  

ORIGINALITY: The work presents a novel application of random forests to a significant problem in zero-shot learning.  

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in zero-shot learning.  

The paper is strong, compelling, and rigorously sound. The results across three datasets under diverse settings are convincing. It should be accepted.
