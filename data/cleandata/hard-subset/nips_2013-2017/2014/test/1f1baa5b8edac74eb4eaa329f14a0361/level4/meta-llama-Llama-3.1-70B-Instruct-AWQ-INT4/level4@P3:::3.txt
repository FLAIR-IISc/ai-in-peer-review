This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is trained on class-attribute signature vectors, while using a validation set of images with attribute-labels to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed.

The paper is well-written and does a good job summarizing related work. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its advantages over existing methods. However, the novelty of this paper is somewhat low, but not too low to be accepted. One issue is that the claim that the proposed approach deals with unreliable attribute predictions while earlier work does not is a bit overstated or misleading. For example, direct attribute prediction [7] (DAP)--the baseline method used in this paper and many others--has some similar abilities to deal with attribute uncertainty.

The proposed approach uses a more complex model for modeling attribute noise, which is a key contribution of the paper. It uses an ROC-based estimate instead of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and effectively models joint statistics in attribute errors, since the validation set is propagated down trees. This is a valuable contribution, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on three challenging datasets.

However, one possible weakness of the proposed approach in comparison to methods that treat attributes independently is that it seems like one would need a large validation set to obtain valid probability estimates as it gets subdivided when propagated down decision trees. The authors propose a heuristic approach to dealing with this issue, but it would have been nice to see more experiments evaluating the effect of training and validation set size.

The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) felt less polished than the rest of the paper. The authors propose a weighted combination of information gains, but it is not entirely clear why this approach is chosen over other possible methods. A more intuitive approach might be to combine class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates when estimating probabilities p(a|...).

Overall, this is a well-written paper with technical decisions that make sense, and adequate experiments. While the main selling point of the paper as a new way of handling unreliable attributes is somewhat questionable, the authors demonstrate a valuable contribution to the field of attribute-based zero-shot learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a clear and well-written explanation of the proposed approach.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on three challenging datasets.
* The proposed approach uses a more complex model for modeling attribute noise, which is a key contribution of the paper.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the paper is somewhat low, and the claim that the proposed approach deals with unreliable attribute predictions while earlier work does not is a bit overstated or misleading.
* The extension to few-shot learning feels less polished than the rest of the paper.
* The authors do not provide enough experiments evaluating the effect of training and validation set size.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the issues mentioned above, particularly the need for more experiments evaluating the effect of training and validation set size, and the clarity of the extension to few-shot learning.
