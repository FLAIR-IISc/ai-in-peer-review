This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning that uses random forests. The approach first trains SVM-based attribute classifiers. It then trains a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors, leveraging a validation set of images with attribute labels to estimate attribute reliability at each node. The authors also propose an extension for few-shot learning.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clear and well-written. It does a good job summarizing related work.

**Originality:** The novelty of this work is limited, though potentially sufficient for acceptance. A key issue is that the claim to be a new approach for handling unreliable attribute predictions, unlike prior work, is overstated. For instance, the baseline method of Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7] employs a similar high-level strategy for managing attribute uncertainty. Both methods follow a two-stage process:
a) training attribute classifiers on image features;
b) training a second-stage class probability estimator on the attribute predictions, using a validation set to gauge the reliability of these classifiers.

The primary difference lies in the second stage: while [6] uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model that assumes attribute independence, this work employs a random forest. Consequently, the proposed method is not a fundamentally new paradigm for handling attribute uncertainty.

The main distinctions are that this approach 1) uses a more complex, ROC-based estimate of attribute noise instead of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) implicitly models joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set through the decision trees, whereas [6] treats attributes as independent. Given this, it would be more accurate to frame the contribution as a more sophisticated method for modeling attribute correlation. (While I am not an expert in this specific area, Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search" may be a relevant citation).

**Quality:** The paper is generally of good quality, with sound technical choices and clear descriptions. The experiments are appropriate and support the paper's claims. However, the quantitative improvements are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance on empirical merit alone.

A potential weakness of this approach, compared to methods that assume attribute independence, is its data requirement. As the validation set is recursively partitioned when propagated down the decision trees, a large set seems necessary to obtain reliable probability estimates. This issue is not adequately addressed, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. An experimental analysis of the method's sensitivity to training and validation set sizes would have strengthened the paper.

The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest of the manuscript. This section is notably brief in its explanation compared to the detailed treatment of the main method. An alternative, and perhaps more intuitive, approach than the weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6) might involve directly combining class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when modeling the probability p(a|...).

In summary, this is a well-written paper with sensible technical decisions and adequate experiments. However, its primary framing as a novel method for handling unreliable attributes is questionable; its main contribution is more accurately described as a better model for attribute correlations.
