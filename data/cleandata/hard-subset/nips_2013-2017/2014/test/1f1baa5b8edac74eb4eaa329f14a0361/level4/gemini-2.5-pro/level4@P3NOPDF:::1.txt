### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses a fundamental gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL). While ZSL theory posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, the practical difficulty of learning reliable attribute classifiers often undermines performance. The authors propose a novel approach using random forests to predict unseen classes directly from attribute vectors. The core contribution is a training procedure that explicitly accounts for the reliability of attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during its construction. This allows the model to learn which attributes are trustworthy and to down-weight the influence of unreliable ones. The framework is elegantly extended to the few-shot learning setting, where a small number of training examples from test categories are available. The method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on several standard attribute datasets.

**Strengths**

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant and effective solution to a significant, practical problem in zero-shot learning. The proposed random forest framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and directly tackles the critical issue of noisy and unreliable attribute predictions.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors validate their method on three standard datasets (AwA, CUB, aP&Y) and demonstrate strong results. The experiments are comprehensive, including:
*   Detailed ablation studies that justify the components of the proposed model.
*   Robustness tests using artificially introduced noise, which confirm the model's ability to handle unreliable attributes.
*   Evaluation in both zero-shot and few-shot learning settings.
*   A rigorous comparison against a wide range of competing methods from the literature.

The application of a random forest framework to this problem is a novel and insightful contribution. The paper is well-structured and clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. However, there was a minor point of confusion in Section 3.2.1 that could be clarified to improve the reader's experience. When the threshold `t` is introduced for splitting nodes, the attribute signatures are described as binary. For binary attributes, any threshold `t` within the range (0, 1) would produce the exact same split, making its specific value in Equation (2) seem arbitrary. Consequently, it was initially unclear how a novel test example with a non-binary, probabilistic attribute prediction could be propagated down the tree, as the role and definition of `t` seemed ambiguous at this stage.

While this point is implicitly resolved in later sections where the use of continuous attribute predictions becomes clear, addressing it at the point of introduction would prevent potential confusion. A brief sentence in Section 3.2.1 explaining that `t` is learned with respect to the continuous-valued attribute predictions from the validation set would significantly improve the clarity of the exposition.

**Recommendation**

This is a strong, interesting, and well-executed paper that addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning with a novel and effective method. The contributions are significant, the model is sound, and the experimental results across multiple datasets and settings are convincing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
