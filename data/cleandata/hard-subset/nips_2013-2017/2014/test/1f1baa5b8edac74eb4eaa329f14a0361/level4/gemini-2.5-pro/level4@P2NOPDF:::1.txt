This paper addresses the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While theory suggests that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging as learning the object classes themselves. To tackle this, the authors propose a random forest framework to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors. A key aspect of their method is a training procedure that accounts for the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during its construction. The paper also demonstrates how the method can be extended to the few-shot learning setting, where a small number of training examples of test categories are available. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several standard attribute datasets.

**Quality:**
The authors present a high-quality study of a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The proposed model, which uses a random forest framework to handle attribute uncertainty, is both elegant and theoretically sound. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, with strong results on three standard datasets. The authors have done an excellent job of evaluating their model through extensive ablation studies, robustness tests with artificial noise, and direct comparisons to the literature in both zero-shot and few-shot learning settings.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. A minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold `t`. When this threshold is first defined, the attribute signatures are still binary. Consequently, any threshold `t` between 0 and 1 would yield the same result in Equation (2), making its role initially unclear. This ambiguity extends to lines 207-212, where it is not immediately obvious how a novel test example with continuous attribute predictions would be propagated down the tree. This point is sufficiently clarified in later sections, but an earlier explanation would improve the reader's comprehension.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel and insightful application of the random forest framework to an important problem. While random forests are a well-established method, the specific formulation for incorporating attribute reliability directly into the tree-building process is a novel contribution.

**Significance:**
This work addresses a fundamental and practical problem in zero-shot learning. The proposed solution is strong, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling across multiple datasets and experimental settings. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is of high interest to the community.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that presents an interesting and well-validated approach to a significant problem. It deserves to be published. I recommend acceptance.
