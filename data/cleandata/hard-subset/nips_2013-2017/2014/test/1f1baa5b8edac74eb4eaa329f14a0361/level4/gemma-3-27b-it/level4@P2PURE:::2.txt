This paper introduces a novel random forest approach to zero-shot learning designed to better handle uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. The method quantifies uncertainty by evaluating the performance (specifically, true positive and false positive rates) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set, and integrates this performance information into the information gain calculation during random forest training. This allows the decision tree to account for the reliability of attribute predictions when pursuing category-attribute signatures. 

Quality: The proposed approach is well-supported by comprehensive empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper would benefit from qualitative analysis; for example, examining which attributes and categories are most significantly impacted by this method. Furthermore, the rationale for certain parameter choices (e.g., the 80%-20% training-validation split) remains unclear.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easily understood.

Originality: Previous work has largely focused on “attribute strength,” whereas this paper addresses the comparatively underexplored challenge of “attribute reliability.” While classifier unreliability is often implicitly addressed in learning algorithms, this work is novel in its explicit handling of this factor.

Significance: Although the use of random forests is not new, the concept of modeling attribute unreliability through classifier performance on validation data to enhance zero-shot learning is both interesting and potentially impactful for researchers in this area. While the core idea and method are not groundbreaking, their application to zero-shot learning offers revealing and interesting results.
