- Proposes a random-forest zero-shot learning method that explicitly models attribute-classifier unreliability using ROC statistics from a validation set.  
- During tree construction, class-attribute signatures are fractionally propagated to account for expected attribute prediction noise at test time.  
- Contrasts with DAP by explicitly modeling error tendencies of attribute classifiers rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions.  
- Extends the approach to handle uncertainty in class-attribute signatures and to the few-shot learning setting.  
- Strength: Addresses an important practical gap by distinguishing attribute presence from classifier reliability.  
- Strength: Technically sound—uses ROC-derived true/false positive rates to guide information gain and enable soft propagation.  
- Strength: Thorough empirical evaluation with controlled noise experiments, significant gains over DAP on AwA, aPY, SUN, and informative ablation studies.  
- Strength/Novelty: Explicit modeling of attribute classifier unreliability within zero-shot learning is a novel and useful perspective.  
- Weakness: Lacks qualitative analysis showing which attributes are considered unreliable and examples of top splits/thresholds.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameter choices (e.g., 80/20 attribute train/validation split, tree depth) are not justified and need sensitivity analysis.  
- Summary: A novel, effective enhancement of random-forest-based zero-shot learning that meaningfully improves robustness, though more qualitative insight and hyperparameter justification would strengthen the work.
