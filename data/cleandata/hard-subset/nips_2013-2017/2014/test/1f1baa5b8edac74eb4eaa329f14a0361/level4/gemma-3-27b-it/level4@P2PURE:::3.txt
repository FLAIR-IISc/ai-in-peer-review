This paper introduces a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning leveraging random forests. Initially, attribute classifiers are trained using SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors, with attribute reliability at each tree node estimated using a validation set of images possessing attribute labels. An extension to the few-shot learning setting is also presented.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and provides a strong summary of related work.

Originality: While the novelty of this work is somewhat incremental, it is sufficient for consideration. The authors’ claim that their approach uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions is overstated, as existing methods exhibit similar capabilities. For instance, direct attribute prediction (DAP) – the baseline employed in this paper and widely used in the field – already incorporates mechanisms to handle attribute uncertainty. Both approaches follow a similar paradigm: a) training attribute classifiers on image features (a common step), and b) training a second-stage classifier to estimate class probabilities based on attribute predictions, using a validation set to assess attribute classifier reliability ([6] utilizes Platt scaling and a probabilistic model for attribute combination, while this work proposes a novel random forest-based method). Therefore, the contribution does not represent a fundamentally new approach to handling attribute uncertainty. Instead, the work offers a more complex model for attribute noise: 1) employing an ROC-based estimate rather than a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) effectively modeling joint statistics in attribute errors through propagation of the validation set down the decision trees – a feature absent in methods like [6] that treat attributes as independent. In this regard, characterizing the approach as an improved method for modeling attribute correlation may be more accurate. The authors might consider citing Scheirer et al. ("Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search") as potentially related work. 

A potential weakness of the proposed approach, compared to methods assuming attribute independence, is the need for a substantial validation set to obtain robust probability estimates as the data is partitioned during tree propagation. This issue receives insufficient attention in the paper, and the proposed heuristic solution (line 323) lacks thorough justification. Additional experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size would be beneficial.

Quality: Overall, the paper is of good quality, with sensible technical choices and clear descriptions. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the claims, though the quantitative improvements are modest and do not independently warrant acceptance. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) appears less developed than the rest of the paper; while the remainder of the work is detailed and well-explained, this section is comparatively brief. A method that combines class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when calculating probabilities p(a|...) might offer a more intuitive solution than the weighted combination of information gains presented in Eq. 6. This is a well-written paper, grounded in sound technical decisions and supported by adequate experiments; however, the central argument regarding a novel approach to handling unreliable attributes is questionable.
