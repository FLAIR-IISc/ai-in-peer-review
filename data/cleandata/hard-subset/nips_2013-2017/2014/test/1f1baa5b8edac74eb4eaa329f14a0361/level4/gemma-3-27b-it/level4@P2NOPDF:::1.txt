This paper presents a compelling approach to attribute-based zero-shot learning, aiming to reconcile the theoretical promise of the method with the practical challenges of training reliable attribute predictors. The core idea is to leverage random forests to map attribute vectors to unseen classes, while explicitly accounting for the uncertainty inherent in the attribute detectors themselves. This uncertainty is cleverly incorporated during training by propagating a validation set through each decision tree, effectively calibrating the forest to the reliability of its inputs. The authors further demonstrate the adaptability of their method to few-shot learning scenarios, where limited training data is available for the novel classes. Importantly, the proposed technique achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple benchmark datasets.

**Quality:** This work offers a well-considered solution to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The proposed model is both elegant in its design and grounded in sound theoretical principles. The experimental results are strong, consistently demonstrating superior performance across three standard datasets. The authors have invested substantial effort in a thorough evaluation, including insightful ablation studies, the introduction of artificial noise to assess robustness, examination of both zero- and few-shot learning settings, and comprehensive comparisons to existing literature. These evaluations provide convincing evidence of the method's efficacy.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. A minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 concerning the introduction of the threshold *t*. As initially presented, the attribute signatures remain binary, suggesting that *t* could take any value between 0 and 1 without affecting equation (2). Consequently, the method of propagating a novel test example down the tree (lines 207-212) felt initially unclear, as the role of *t* appeared undefined. However, this ambiguity is resolved through clarification in subsequent sections of the paper. Overall, the writing is clear and concise, making the technical details readily accessible.

**Originality:** This paper represents a novel and effective application of a random forest framework to the important problem of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While random forests are a well-established technique, their application within this specific context, coupled with the incorporation of attribute detector reliability, offers a significant contribution.

**Significance:** The work addresses a critical challenge in zero-shot learning: the difficulty of accurately predicting attributes for unseen classes. By providing a robust and effective method for attribute-based classification, the authors advance the field and broaden the applicability of zero-shot learning techniques. The strong results obtained across diverse datasets and settings underscore the significance of this contribution. 

In conclusion, this is a strong, interesting, and technically sound paper. The comprehensive experiments and convincing results strongly support the authors’ claims, and I recommend this paper for publication.
