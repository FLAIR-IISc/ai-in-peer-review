This paper proposes a novel random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that effectively accommodates the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. By measuring the performance of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set, the authors introduce a new approach to incorporate this uncertainty into the information gain computation during random forest learning. This allows for the pursuit of a given category-attribute signature along multiple paths of a decision tree, thereby accommodating the uncertainty of attribute predictions.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper could benefit from additional qualitative analysis, such as an examination of the types of attributes and categories that are most affected or improved by this method. Furthermore, it is unclear how some of the parameters involved were selected, such as the choice of an 80%-20% split for training-validation.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method and its underlying principles.

Originality: While the problem of attribute strength has been extensively explored in previous works, this paper focuses on the lesser-explored problem of attribute reliability. Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of unreliability.

Significance: The proposed method, while not entirely new, introduces an interesting and thought-provoking idea to researchers working on attributes. The application of this idea to improve zero-shot learning is revealing and has the potential to contribute significantly to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling attribute classifier uncertainty, which is a critical aspect of zero-shot learning.
* The method is well-supported by empirical and quantitative analysis, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving zero-shot learning performance.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from additional qualitative analysis to provide a deeper understanding of the method's strengths and limitations.
* Some parameters involved in the method, such as the training-validation split, could be better justified or explored.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning, and its novel approach to modeling attribute classifier uncertainty makes it a strong candidate for acceptance.
