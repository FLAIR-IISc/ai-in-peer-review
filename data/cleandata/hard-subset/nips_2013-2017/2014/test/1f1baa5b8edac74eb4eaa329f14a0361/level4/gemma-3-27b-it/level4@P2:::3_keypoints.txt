- Introduces a random-forest-based attribute zero-shot learning method using SVM-trained attribute classifiers and a validation set to estimate attribute reliability via ROC statistics, with a few-shot extension.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written and situates the contribution within existing literature.  
- Explicitly modeling attribute unreliability within the random forest is a valuable contribution, but claims of strong novelty should be nuanced.  
- The method’s key distinction is ROC-based reliability estimation and propagation of validation data down trees to capture joint attribute-error statistics.  
- Should cite and discuss related work on attribute correlations (e.g., Scheirer et al.) to clarify differences.  
- Reliance on a sufficiently large validation set to get robust node-wise probability estimates is a potential limitation; current heuristic treatment is brief.  
- Technical approach is sound and experiments are appropriate; results show consistent but modest improvements across datasets.  
- Few-shot extension (weighted information gain) feels underdeveloped and needs clearer rationale or alternative probabilistic formulations.  
- Suggest investigating the impact of training/validation set sizes more thoroughly with additional experiments.  
- Work is significant because it addresses practical attribute unreliability in ZSL and is particularly helpful when attribute classifiers are weak.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear writing, tackles an important problem, consistent improvements over strong baselines (including DAP), novel modeling of attribute unreliability, and a few-shot extension.  
- Arguments against acceptance: novelty claim somewhat overstated, potential need for large validation set, few-shot part less polished, and quantitative gains are not large.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—clarify novelty relative to prior work, emphasize ROC-reliability and attribute-correlation modeling, expand discussion/experiments on validation set requirements, and strengthen the few-shot explanation.
