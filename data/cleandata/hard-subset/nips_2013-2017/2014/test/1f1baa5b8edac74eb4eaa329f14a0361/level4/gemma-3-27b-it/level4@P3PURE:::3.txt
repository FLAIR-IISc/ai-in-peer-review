This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors, leveraging a validation set of images with attribute labels to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well-written, and provides a good summary of related work.

Originality: The novelty of this paper is somewhat limited, though not to a degree that precludes acceptance. The claim that the proposed approach uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions is overstated and potentially misleading. For example, direct attribute prediction (DAP) [7] – the baseline method used here and in many other works – possesses comparable abilities to handle attribute uncertainty. Both approaches follow a similar framework: (a) Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical in both cases), and (b) Train a second-stage class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, utilizing a validation set to estimate the reliability of the attribute classifiers. While [6] employs Platt scaling and a probabilistic model to combine attributes, the proposed approach introduces a novel random forest-based method. Therefore, I don’t believe the presented work represents a fundamentally new approach to handling attribute uncertainty. Rather, it offers a more complex model for representing attribute noise: first, it utilizes an ROC-based estimate in place of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and second, unlike [6], which treats attributes as independent, the proposed approach effectively models joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set down the decision trees. In this context, it would be more accurate to characterize the approach as a refined method for modeling attribute correlations. Scheirer et al. ("Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search") might be a relevant related work to cite.

At the same time, a potential weakness of this approach, compared to methods treating attributes independently, is the need for a substantial validation set to obtain reliable probability estimates as the data is subdivided during tree propagation. This issue is glossed over in the paper, and the proposed heuristic for addressing it (line 323) requires further justification. More experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size would be valuable.

Quality: Overall, the paper is of good quality, with sensible technical choices and descriptions. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the claims made, although the degree of quantitative improvement is not substantial enough to justify acceptance based on empirical results alone. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the rest of the paper. While the main body of the paper is clear and thoroughly explained, this section is comparatively brief. Intuitively, a method that combines class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates when estimating probabilities p(a|…) might be more effective than the weighted combination of information gains presented in Equation 6. 

In summary, this is a well-written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experimental evaluation; however, the central argument for the novelty of the approach – as a new way to handle unreliable attributes – is questionable.
