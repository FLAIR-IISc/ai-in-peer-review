- Goal: Bridge theory and practice in attribute-based zero-shot learning by enabling recognition of novel classes via pre-trained attribute predictors.
- Problem: Training attribute classifiers can be as difficult or harder than learning object classes directly, limiting practical zero-shot performance.
- Method: Train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors.
- Key training trick: Account for attribute detector reliability by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training.
- Extension: Method naturally extends to few-shot learning when a small number of labeled examples from test categories are available.
- Results: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple standard attribute datasets.
- Quality: Model is elegant, theoretically sound, and evaluated thoroughly with ablation studies, artificial-noise experiments, and detailed comparisons.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written overall but has a minor presentation confusion in Section 3.2.1 regarding the threshold t and how test examples propagate down the tree.
- Originality: Novel and effective application of the random forest framework to zero-shot learning.
- Significance: Addresses a fundamental ZSL problem with convincing empirical evidence and merits publication.
