This paper proposes a framework for attribute-based zero-shot learning that utilizes random forests. The method begins by training attribute classifiers using SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors. A key aspect of this approach is the use of a validation set of images with attribute labels to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the trees, which in turn guides the tree construction process. The paper also presents an extension of the method to the few-shot learning scenario.

**Clarity and Quality**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with a comprehensive summary of related work. The technical descriptions are sound, and the design choices are well-justified. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the paper's claims.

However, the extension to few-shot learning in Section 3.3 feels less polished and is described too briefly compared to the detailed explanations in the rest of the paper. An alternative approach, such as a probabilistic method that combines class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates to model p(a|...), might be more intuitive than the proposed weighted combination of information gains in Equation 6. While the paper is technically solid, the level of quantitative improvement over baselines is not substantial enough to warrant acceptance based on empirical performance alone.

**Originality and Novelty**

The primary concern with this paper lies in its claim of novelty. The authors position the work as a new way to handle unreliable attribute predictions, but this claim seems overstated when compared to prior art. For instance, the Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method, which is used as a baseline, employs a conceptually similar strategy to manage attribute uncertainty. Both the proposed method and DAP follow a two-stage process:

1.  Train primary attribute classifiers on image features.
2.  Train a second-stage model that estimates class probabilities as a function of the primary classifiers' predictions. This stage leverages a validation set to account for the reliability (or uncertainty) of the attribute predictions.

While DAP uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model that assumes attribute independence, the proposed method uses a random forest. Therefore, the fundamental idea of using a validation set to model attribute classifier reliability is not new.

The true novelty of this work lies in *how* it models this uncertainty, which is more sophisticated than in prior methods. Specifically, the contributions can be better framed as:
1.  Using an ROC-based estimate of attribute reliability, which may be more robust than the sigmoidal models used in methods like Platt scaling.
2.  Implicitly modeling the joint statistics and correlations in attribute prediction errors. Unlike methods that treat attributes independently, the proposed random forest propagates the validation set through the trees, allowing splits to be made based on the joint performance of multiple attributes.

Framing the contribution as a more effective way to model attribute correlations would be more accurate. In this context, citing related work on multi-attribute spaces, such as Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search," could be beneficial.

A potential weakness of this approach, which arises from its novelty, is its dependence on a sufficiently large validation set. As the validation set is partitioned and propagated down the decision trees, the number of samples available to estimate reliability at deeper nodes can become very small, potentially leading to unreliable probability estimates. The paper does not adequately address this issue, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study evaluating the impact of the training and validation set sizes on model performance.

**Conclusion**

This is a well-written paper with sound technical contributions. However, its main selling point—a novel method for handling unreliable attributes—is questionable, as the core concept exists in prior work. The true contribution is a more sophisticated method for modeling attribute noise and correlations. While this is a valuable contribution, the paper would be stronger if it were positioned as such and included a more thorough analysis of its potential limitations, particularly regarding the required size of the validation set.
