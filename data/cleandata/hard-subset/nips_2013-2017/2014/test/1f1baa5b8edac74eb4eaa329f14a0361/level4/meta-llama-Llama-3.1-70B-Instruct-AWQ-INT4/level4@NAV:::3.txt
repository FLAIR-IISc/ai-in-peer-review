This paper introduces an attribute-based zero-shot learning method using random forests. Initially, attribute classifiers are trained using SVMs, followed by the training of a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors. A validation set of images with attribute labels is utilized to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. Furthermore, an extension to few-shot learning is proposed.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, effectively summarizing related work.

Originality: Although the paper's novelty is somewhat limited, it is not low enough to warrant rejection. However, the claim that the proposed approach uniquely handles unreliable attribute predictions is slightly exaggerated. For instance, direct attribute prediction (DAP) [7], the baseline method employed in this paper and others, shares similarities with the proposed approach in addressing attribute uncertainty. Both methods follow a similar framework:
a) Training attribute classifiers on image features (identical for both papers)
b) Training a second class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, utilizing a validation set to estimate attribute classifier reliability ([6] employs Platt scaling and a probabilistic model to combine attributes, whereas the proposed approach utilizes a novel random forest-based method)

In this context, the proposed approach does not fundamentally revolutionize the handling of attribute uncertainty. Instead, it introduces a more complex model for attribute noise, primarily through the use of ROC-based estimation and the effective modeling of joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set down trees. A more accurate description of the approach might be that it offers an improved method for modeling attribute correlation (a related paper that could be cited is Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search"). However, one potential weakness of the proposed approach, compared to methods that treat attributes independently, is the need for a large validation set to obtain valid probability estimates as it gets subdivided when propagated down decision trees. This issue was not thoroughly addressed in the paper, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. Additional experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size would have been beneficial.

Quality: The paper's overall quality is good, with sound technical choices and descriptions. The experiments are suitable and support the paper's claims, although the level of quantitative improvement is not substantial enough to accept the paper solely based on empirical results. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) appears less refined than the rest of the paper. A method that combines class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates when estimating probabilities p(a|...) might be more intuitive than the weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6). Overall, this is a well-written paper with logical technical decisions and adequate experiments; however, the primary contribution of the paper as a novel approach to handling unreliable attributes is somewhat debatable.
