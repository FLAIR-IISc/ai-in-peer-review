

This paper introduces a random forest-based approach for attribute-driven zero-shot learning, where attribute classifiers are initially trained via SVMs. A random forest is then constructed using class-attribute signature vectors, with attribute reliability estimated at each tree node using a validation set of image-attribute pairs. The work extends this framework to few-shot learning.  

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. It effectively summarizes related work and presents technical concepts with precision.  

Originality: The core contribution—addressing unreliable attribute predictions—is overstated. While the paper claims prior work (e.g., Direct Attribute Prediction [7]) fails to handle attribute uncertainty, both methods share a fundamental two-step strategy: (1) training attribute classifiers on image features (identical across approaches), and (2) modeling class probabilities via a second-stage estimator using validation data to calibrate attribute reliability. Specifically, [6] employs Platt scaling and probabilistic attribute fusion, while the proposed method uses a random forest with ROC-based reliability estimation and joint error modeling. The key distinction lies not in "handling uncertainty" per se, but in *how* it models it: (1) replacing sigmoidal calibration (Platt scaling) with ROC-based estimates, and (2) capturing correlated attribute errors through tree-structured validation propagation (unlike [6]'s independence assumption). This suggests a more accurate framing as an improved method for *modeling attribute correlation*—a point supported by related work like Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces." The paper’s emphasis on "dealing with unreliable predictions" as novel misrepresents the incremental nature of the contribution.  

Quality: The technical execution is sound, with well-justified methodological choices. Experiments appropriately support the claims, though quantitative gains are modest. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is notably underdeveloped compared to the rest of the paper: it lacks the depth of explanation and intuition provided elsewhere. For instance, Equation 6’s weighted combination of information gains feels arbitrary; a probabilistic fusion of class-attribute priors and image-based attribute estimates (e.g., p(a|class, image)) would be more intuitive. Crucially, the approach’s reliance on large validation sets for reliable node-level reliability estimation is inadequately addressed. The heuristic solution proposed (Line 323) glosses over the risk of overfitting due to validation set partitioning in deep trees. The paper would benefit from experiments demonstrating sensitivity to training/validation set sizes—a gap that undermines confidence in the method’s practicality.  

Overall, the paper presents a technically competent method with clear writing, but the novelty is overstated. The core advance is a refined technique for modeling attribute correlation, not a paradigm shift in uncertainty handling. While the empirical results are adequate, they do not sufficiently justify acceptance given the modest performance gains and unresolved concerns about validation set requirements. The few-shot component further weakens the contribution’s completeness.  

In summary: The work offers a plausible refinement to attribute-based zero-shot learning, but its framing as a novel solution to attribute uncertainty misrepresents the literature. The method’s value lies in its specific modeling of attribute correlations, not in a purportedly new approach to uncertainty. The paper would strengthen its contribution by reframing its claim, addressing validation set scalability, and expanding the few-shot extension. As written, the incremental nature of the advance and the lack of compelling empirical justification make it difficult to recommend acceptance over existing methods.
