This paper proposes a technique for attribute-based zero-shot learning that utilizes random forests. The methodology begins by training SVM-based attribute classifiers. Subsequently, a random forest is trained on class-attribute signature vectors. A validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate the reliability of attributes at each node within the forest's trees. The authors also present an extension of their method to the few-shot learning setting.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-articulated. It provides a thorough summary of prior art and situates the work effectively within the existing literature.

**Originality:** The novelty of this work is marginal, though sufficient for acceptance. A key concern is that the authors' assertion that their approach uniquely handles unreliable attribute predictions—a claimed limitation of prior work—is overstated. For instance, the baseline method of Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7] exhibits a similar capacity for managing attribute uncertainty. Both the proposed method and DAP employ a comparable two-stage process:
a) Train attribute classifiers using image features (this step is identical in both approaches).
b) Train a second-stage class probability estimator that takes attribute predictions as input. This stage leverages a validation set to gauge the reliability of the attribute classifiers. While [6] uses Platt scaling within a probabilistic model to combine attributes, the proposed work introduces a random forest for this purpose.

Consequently, the proposed method does not represent a fundamentally new paradigm for handling attribute uncertainty. The primary distinction lies in the sophistication of the model used for attribute noise. Specifically, the authors' contribution is twofold: 1) it uses an ROC-based estimate of reliability instead of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) it implicitly models joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set down the decision trees, whereas methods like [6] treat attributes as independent. Therefore, it would be more accurate to frame the contribution as an improved method for modeling attribute correlation. In this context, citing related work like Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces" could be beneficial.

A potential weakness of this approach, when compared to methods that assume attribute independence, is its apparent need for a large validation set to ensure valid probability estimates, as this set is repeatedly subdivided down the trees. The paper does not sufficiently address this issue, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. The inclusion of experiments analyzing the model's sensitivity to training and validation set sizes would have significantly strengthened the paper.

**Quality:** The overall quality of the paper is high; the technical decisions are sound and the descriptions are coherent. The experiments are well-designed and support the paper's claims, although the performance improvements are not substantial enough to justify acceptance based on empirical results alone. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the core contribution. This section is concise to the point of being underdeveloped, lacking the detailed explanation characteristic of the rest of the manuscript. An alternative, perhaps more intuitive, approach might involve directly combining class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates to compute probabilities like p(a|...), rather than using a weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6).

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper with sound technical choices and adequate experimental validation. However, its primary selling point—as a novel method for handling unreliable attributes—is questionable when viewed in the context of prior art.
