- Method models attribute-classifier unreliability by fractional propagation of class-attribute signatures in random forests using node-specific ROC (TPR/FPR) statistics.
- Split criterion uses an information gain adjusted for expected attribute prediction noise to favor robust splits.
- Extends to uncertain class signatures and a few-shot regime by mixing signature-based and data-based information gains with a λ parameter.
- Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only random forest baseline, with ablation studies.
- Relates to prior zero-shot methods (DAP, label embeddings) but is distinct in explicitly incorporating attribute unreliability into training.
- Connects to decision trees that handle missing/uncertain data, but the zero-shot training-from-signatures setting is novel.
- Strength: principled, carefully implemented use of node-specific ROC estimates aligning training to test-time uncertainties.
- Strength: controlled-noise experiments convincingly isolate robustness benefits; few-shot extension is natural and empirically useful.
- Weakness: limited qualitative/diagnostic analysis—lacks per-attribute utility, learned-split examples, and per-class error breakdowns.
- Weakness: hyperparameter choices (80/20 ROC split, tree depth, candidate tests) are under-explained and sensitivity is unclear.
- Weakness: data usage protocol is ambiguous—need precise accounting for data used for attribute SVM training, ROC estimation, and model selection.
- Weakness: assumption that ROC statistics generalize from seen to unseen classes is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed for domain shift failures.
- Weakness: scalability and computational/memory complexity (trees per class, depths, large K) are not discussed.
- Clarity: paper is clearly written with effective step-by-step exposition and helpful pseudocode/schematics; main-paper compact pseudocode recommended.
- Originality: novel perspective in focusing on attribute reliability and integrating ROC-driven fractional propagation into zero-shot RF training.
- Significance: practical impact demonstrated across datasets, but reliance on representative attribute-labeled validation data may limit applicability.
- Pros summary: principled ROC-based propagation, consistent empirical gains, natural few-shot extension, applicable to named or discovered attributes.
- Cons summary: lacks qualitative diagnostics, hyperparameter/data-split detail, robustness to ROC distribution mismatch, and scalability discussion.
- Suggested improvements: add qualitative tree/split examples and per-attribute analyses relating gains to TPR/FPR and calibration.
- Suggested improvements: provide sensitivity analyses for validation split ratio, tree depth, candidate thresholds, and λ selection.
- Suggested improvements: clarify exact data-use protocol and ensure fairness across baselines; include runtime/complexity analysis and scalability strategies.
- Suggested improvements: explore methods to mitigate ROC domain shift (reweighting, meta-modeling of TPR/FPR) and diagnostics for mismatch.
