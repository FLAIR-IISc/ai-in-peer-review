

The paper addresses a critical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the gap between theoretical promise and practical implementation. While theory posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, practice reveals that learning these attribute classifiers often proves as challenging or more so than learning object classes directly. The authors propose a novel solution using random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, with a training procedure that incorporates attribute detector reliability by propagating a validation set through each decision tree. They further demonstrate how the method extends to few-shot learning scenarios with limited test-category examples, achieving state-of-the-art performance across three standard attribute-based benchmarks.

**Quality:** The authors provide an elegant and theoretically grounded approach to a core zero-shot learning challenge. The model’s design effectively leverages random forests’ strengths while addressing the reliability of attribute predictions—a key practical hurdle. Results are compelling across three major datasets, supported by thorough ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, evaluations of zero-shot versus few-shot settings, and comprehensive comparisons with prior work. The method’s robustness and performance gains are convincingly demonstrated.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible. A minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1 regarding the threshold parameter *t*. At this stage, attribute signatures are binary (Eq. 2), making *t* (0 < *t* < 1) irrelevant to the formulation. The subsequent explanation of test example propagation (lines 207–212) becomes clearer only after later sections introduce continuous attribute outputs, where *t* serves as a meaningful threshold. Clarifying this transition would enhance readability.

**Originality:** The application of random forests to attribute-based zero-shot learning is a fresh and well-motivated contribution. While random forests are common in machine learning, their systematic integration with attribute reliability and few-shot adaptation for zero-shot recognition represents a novel technical direction.

**Significance:** The work tackles a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning—bridging the theory-practice divide—where existing methods often fail to leverage attribute predictors effectively in real-world scenarios. By providing a reliable, extendable framework that handles both zero-shot and few-shot settings, the paper offers a practical advance with clear implications for broader applications in recognition systems.

The paper is strong, well-executed, and significant. The results across multiple datasets and settings are robust and convincing, demonstrating clear methodological and empirical contributions. The authors successfully address a persistent challenge in the field, and the proposed approach is both principled and effective. This work merits publication at NIPS.
