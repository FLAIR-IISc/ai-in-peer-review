Summary
The paper proposes a zero-shot learning method that builds class classifiers from attribute signatures while explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute predictors. First, attribute classifiers are trained (SVMs with Platt scaling). Then, for each unseen class, a random forest is trained using class–attribute signatures as “exemplars.” Unlike standard approaches that treat attribute predictions at face value, the method uses a held-out validation set with attribute labels to estimate ROC-based error tendencies (TPR/FPR) for each attribute at each node. During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated down both branches in proportion to these node-specific ROC estimates, encouraging splits that are both discriminative and robust to expected attribute errors. The approach is extended to model uncertainty in class signatures and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven information gain with image-driven information gain via a weighting parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show improvements over DAP and a signature-only random forest, including controlled-noise studies illustrating robustness to heterogeneous attribute reliability.

Relation to prior work
The approach follows the common two-stage attribute-based framework: learn attribute detectors, then infer unseen class labels from attribute evidence. It differs from DAP (Lampert et al.) and related work by (i) using ROC-based calibration rather than assuming a fixed sigmoidal calibration, and (ii) capturing dependencies among attribute errors through node-specific validation data propagation, rather than assuming independent attributes. The paper also compares to methods that discover mid-level features, emphasizing that such features may lack semantic interpretability required for zero-shot definition via attributes. The framing as robust decision-tree training is reminiscent of handling missing/uncertain features in trees, but here the signatures are the “training data,” and attribute prediction uncertainties learned on validation data guide split selection.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized presentation with a thorough related work section.
- Conceptually appealing use of node-specific ROC estimates to prefer splits that remain discriminative under realistic attribute error profiles.
- Addresses both classifier unreliability and signature uncertainty; extends naturally to few-shot.
- Solid experimental design: three datasets, controlled-noise analysis, ablations isolating contributions, and comparisons to strong baselines (DAP).
- Empirical results support the central claim that modeling attribute error tendencies matters, with the largest gains where attributes are least reliable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is incremental. DAP already calibrates attribute uncertainty and combines them probabilistically; the claim that prior work “does not handle unreliability” overstates the contrast. The main novelty is ROC-based calibration and implicit modeling of correlated attribute errors via validation-set propagation.
- The method’s reliance on node-specific ROC estimates raises sample-efficiency concerns. As the tree deepens, validation data are partitioned, potentially leading to brittle estimates; the fallback to global statistics when counts are low feels heuristic, and its effect is not deeply analyzed.
- The few-shot extension is comparatively underdeveloped. A weighted sum of information gains (Eq. 6) is plausible, but a probabilistic fusion that directly combines class–attribute priors with observed attribute predictions might be more principled and interpretable.
- Reported gains, though consistent, are modest on some settings; additional analysis could better explain when and why the method excels.

Suggestions for improvement
- Calibrate claims: position the contribution as improved modeling of attribute reliability and inter-attribute error dependencies, rather than the first to “handle unreliability.” Consider citing related calibration/fusion work (e.g., Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search”).
- Analyze sensitivity to validation set size, tree depth, and node thresholds. Show learning curves varying the fraction of validation data and quantify the impact of the fallback heuristic.
- Compare ROC-based calibration to alternative calibrations (e.g., Platt/temperature scaling) within the same framework, and to a variant that assumes independent attributes (no node-specific propagation).
- Strengthen the few-shot section: provide a probabilistic derivation or a Bayesian view connecting λ to data size, and include more ablations on λ selection and robustness.
- Discuss computational cost and scalability with many attributes/classes, and whether shallow trees or pruning suffice without sacrificing accuracy.

Pro and con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear idea with a practical mechanism to improve robustness in zero- and few-shot settings.
- Empirical improvements over widely used DAP across multiple datasets; controlled-noise experiments convincingly support the motivation.
- Method is general and could transfer to other semantic mid-level representations.

Cons:
- Incremental novelty relative to existing calibrated attribute models; some claims are overstated.
- Limited analysis of the sample complexity and stability of node-specific ROC estimates.
- Few-shot extension is brief and could use stronger theoretical and empirical grounding.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Good. Method is sound; the fractional propagation guided by ROC statistics is well-motivated. Would benefit from deeper analysis of validation data requirements and the fallback heuristic.
- Clarity: Very good. The paper is easy to follow, with helpful intuition and a solid related work discussion. The few-shot section is comparatively terse.
- Originality: Moderate. The key ideas refine known attribute-calibration/fusion approaches with a random forest formulation that captures error dependencies.
- Significance: Moderate to good. Results suggest meaningful practical gains in scenarios where attribute reliability is limited, a common case in zero-shot learning.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a sensible and useful enhancement to attribute-based zero-shot learning. I recommend acceptance if the authors temper the novelty claims and add analyses addressing the robustness of node-level calibration and the few-shot formulation.
