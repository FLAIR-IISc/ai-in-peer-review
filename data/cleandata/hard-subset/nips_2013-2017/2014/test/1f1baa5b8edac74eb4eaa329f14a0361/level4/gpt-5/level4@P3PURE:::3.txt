This paper proposes an attribute-based zero-shot learning method built on random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class–attribute signature vectors, using a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also presented.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well written, with an effective summary of related work.

Originality: The novelty is somewhat limited, though not too low to merit rejection. The claim that the proposed approach uniquely handles unreliable attribute predictions is somewhat overstated. For example, direct attribute prediction [7] (DAP)—used here as a baseline and in many prior works—already incorporates mechanisms to address attribute uncertainty. Both the proposed method and DAP follow the same high-level recipe:
- Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical in both).
- Train a second-stage class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, using a validation set to calibrate attribute reliability (in [6] via Platt scaling and a probabilistic combination model; here via a new random-forest-based approach).

In this sense, the proposed method is not a fundamentally new way of handling attribute uncertainty. Its distinctiveness lies in adopting a more flexible model of attribute noise: (1) using an ROC-based estimate rather than a sigmoidal Platt-scaling model, and (2) effectively modeling joint statistics of attribute errors, since the validation set is propagated down the trees instead of assuming independent attributes as in [6]. It may be more accurate to frame the contribution as improved modeling of attribute correlations. While I do not know this area exhaustively, Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” may be a related reference worth citing.

A potential weakness relative to methods that treat attributes independently is the reliance on a sufficiently large validation set. Because the validation data are repeatedly subdivided along tree paths, accurate reliability estimates at deeper nodes may require substantial data. This issue is largely glossed over, and the proposed mitigation (line 323) seems heuristic. Additional experiments analyzing the sensitivity to training and validation set sizes would strengthen the paper.

Quality: Overall, the technical choices are sound and well explained. The experiments are appropriate and support the claims, although the quantitative gains are not large enough to justify acceptance solely on empirical performance. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest of the paper: the exposition is brief, and the chosen mechanism—combining information gains with a weighted sum (Eq. 6)—is less intuitive than an approach that would explicitly integrate class–attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when forming probabilities p(a|...). 

In summary, this is a well-written paper with sensible technical decisions and adequate experiments. However, the main selling point—that it introduces a new way to handle unreliable attributes—is somewhat questionable; the contribution is better characterized as offering a more expressive model of attribute correlation rather than a fundamentally new treatment of attribute uncertainty.
