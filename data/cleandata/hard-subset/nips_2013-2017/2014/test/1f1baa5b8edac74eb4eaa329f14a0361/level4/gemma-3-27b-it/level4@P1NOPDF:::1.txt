The paper aims to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theoretical premise is that novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors; however, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging, or even more so, than learning the object classes themselves. 

The authors train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and their training procedure incorporates the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They also demonstrate how the method can be extended to handle training with a few examples from the test categories. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

**QUALITY:** The authors present a strong and elegant solution to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, leveraging a random forest framework with a sound theoretical basis. Results on three standard datasets are compelling. The paper includes a thorough evaluation, with insightful ablation studies, the introduction of artificial noise, assessment across both zero-shot and few-shot learning settings, and comprehensive comparisons to existing literature.

**CLARITY:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. I encountered a minor point of confusion in Section 3.2.1. Given that the attribute signatures remain binary, the threshold *t* introduced appears to be arbitrarily set, as any value between 0 and 1 (0 < *t* < 1) would yield the same result in equation (2). Consequently, the explanation in lines 207-212 regarding how a novel test example is propagated down the tree feels unclear, as the definition of *t* seems ill-defined at that point. This is clarified in subsequent sections of the paper.

**ORIGINALITY:** This work represents a compelling application of a random forest framework to a significant problem in the field.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The paper addresses a core challenge in zero-shot learning. It is a strong, interesting, and technically sound contribution. The results, demonstrated across three datasets and a variety of settings, are convincing. I recommend this paper for publication.
