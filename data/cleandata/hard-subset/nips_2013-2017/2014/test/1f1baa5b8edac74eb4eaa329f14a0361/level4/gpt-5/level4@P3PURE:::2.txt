This paper proposes a random-forest-based method for zero-shot learning that explicitly models the uncertainty of attribute classifier predictions. Uncertainty is estimated from attribute classifier performance (e.g., true positive rate, false positive rate) on a held-out validation set. This performance information is incorporated into the information gain calculation during tree construction, allowing a given category–attribute signature to propagate along multiple paths in the decision tree to account for attribute prediction uncertainty.

Quality: The proposed ideas are supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis; for example, it would be helpful to see which types of attributes and which categories are most affected or improved by the method. It is also unclear how parameters were selected—for instance, why an 80/20 training–validation split was chosen.

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow.

Originality: While much prior work has focused on “attribute strength,” this paper targets the less explored issue of “attribute reliability.” Although many learning methods implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of unreliability.

Significance: The underlying method (random forests) is not new, but the idea of modeling unreliability via validation-set performance to improve zero-shot learning is interesting and thought-provoking for researchers working on attributes. While neither the idea nor the method is significantly novel, their application to zero-shot learning yields revealing and interesting insights.
