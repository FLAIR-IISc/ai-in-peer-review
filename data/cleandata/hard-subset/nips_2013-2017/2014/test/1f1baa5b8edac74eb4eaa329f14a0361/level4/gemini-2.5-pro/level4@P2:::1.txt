This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the discrepancy between the idealized concept of defining novel classes by their attributes and the practical difficulty of creating reliable attribute classifiers. The authors propose a novel method to train robust ZSL models that explicitly account for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core contribution is a random forest framework trained not on image data, but directly on the attribute "signatures" that define the unseen classes. To handle attribute unreliability, the training process is augmented with a validation set of attribute-labeled images. This set is used to estimate the ROC characteristics (e.g., true/false positive rates) of each attribute classifier. During the construction of the decision trees, these statistics guide a "fractional propagation" of the class signatures, effectively encoding the expected prediction errors into the model's structure. The paper further extends this framework to account for uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and provides a natural extension to the few-shot learning setting. The proposed method is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance, significantly outperforming influential baselines on three standard ZSL datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality & Significance:** The paper tackles a critical and widely recognized bottleneck in attribute-based ZSL. While the idea of recognizing objects by their attributes is elegant, its practical application has been hampered by the noise and error from underlying attribute classifiers. This work provides a principled and effective solution to mitigate this problem, making the entire attribute-based ZSL paradigm more robust and practical. The problem is significant, and the proposed solution is a clear advance for the field.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is elegant and technically sound. The core idea of propagating fractional signatures based on attribute classifier reliability is a clever way to inject knowledge about prediction uncertainty directly into the learning process. Instead of treating attribute predictions as ground truth at test time, the model is trained from the start to anticipate their error patterns. The formulation is well-grounded, and the extensions for signature uncertainty and few-shot learning are logical and well-integrated.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on three diverse and standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The inclusion of a controlled noise experiment (Figure 1) is particularly insightful, as it clearly illustrates how the method's advantage grows as attributes become less reliable, confirming the theoretical motivation. Furthermore, the detailed ablation studies effectively isolate the performance contributions of the key components of the model (signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation). Comparisons to strong baselines, especially the widely used DAP model, and state-of-the-art results solidify the paper's contributions.

4.  **Originality:** The work presents a novel application and formulation of random forests for the ZSL problem. While handling uncertainty in decision trees is not a new concept in itself (as the authors acknowledge by citing work on missing values), the specific mechanism of training on class signatures while using a separate validation set to model test-time uncertainty is a novel and powerful combination tailored for the unique constraints of ZSL.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is of high quality, and weaknesses are minor. The main suggestion relates to a small point of clarity.

1.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. However, there is a minor point of potential confusion for the reader in Section 3.2.1. When the split threshold `t` is introduced for the basic signature random forest, the attribute signatures `Ak(m)` are binary. Consequently, any choice of `t` within the range (0, 1) results in an identical split, making the selection of `t` seem arbitrary at this stage. The critical role of `t` as a continuous variable for selecting an operating point on the ROC curve only becomes apparent in the next section (3.2.2). The authors could enhance readability by adding a brief sentence in Section 3.2.1 to foreshadow this, for instance, by noting that while `t`'s value is not crucial for binary signatures, it will become essential for modeling classifier uncertainty.

Overall, this is an excellent paper. It identifies an important problem, proposes a novel and elegant solution, and validates it with strong, comprehensive experiments. The work is a significant contribution to making zero-shot learning more practical and robust. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
