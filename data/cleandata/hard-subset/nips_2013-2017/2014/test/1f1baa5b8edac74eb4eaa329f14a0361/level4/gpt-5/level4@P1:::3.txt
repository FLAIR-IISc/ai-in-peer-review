This paper proposes a random-forest-based approach to zero-shot recognition that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors first train attribute classifiers (SVMs with Platt scaling) on attribute-labeled images. For zero-shot learning, they train decision trees not on images but on class-attribute signature vectors, and crucially use a held-out validation set to estimate each attribute classifier’s ROC at each node. They propagate both signatures and validation samples down the tree so that splits are chosen to maximize information gain under the expected true/false positive rates, effectively favoring attributes and thresholds that remain discriminative despite noise. They also handle uncertainty in class signatures and extend the method to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and data-based information gains with a weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with controlled-noise studies illustrating robustness benefits.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and carefully executed. The core idea—using node-specific ROC estimates to fractionally propagate signature mass—is well motivated and clearly described. The controlled-noise experiments convincingly demonstrate the method’s intended robustness, and the results on three datasets support the claims, with especially notable gains when attributes are less reliable. The ablations (signature uncertainty; per-node vs. global ROC propagation) help isolate where improvements come from. The few-shot extension is reasonable in spirit, but Section 3.3 is brief compared to the rest of the paper, and the choice to combine information gains (Eq. 6) feels less principled than alternatives that would combine class-attribute priors and image-based attribute estimates at the probability level (e.g., fusing p(a|x) with p(a|class) to form p(class|x)). It would strengthen the paper to either justify Eq. 6 more thoroughly or provide empirical comparisons to a probabilistic fusion baseline.

Clarity: The manuscript is very clear, well organized, and does a nice job situating the work relative to prior zero-shot methods, discovered attributes, and related decision-tree techniques for uncertainty/missingness. The technical exposition is easy to follow.

Originality: The contribution is incremental but meaningful. The framing that prior zero-shot methods “do not handle unreliable attributes” feels overstated. DAP (Lampert et al.) explicitly models uncertainty in attribute predictions via calibrated probabilities, using a validation set to calibrate p(a|x), and then computes class posteriors from these uncertain attributes. The proposed method follows the same two-stage structure—(a) train attribute classifiers on image features; (b) learn a class predictor as a function of attribute predictions calibrated on validation data—but differs in two important ways: (1) it uses ROC-based operating-point selection rather than a sigmoidal calibration model; and (2) by propagating validation data down the trees, it implicitly captures joint dependencies in attribute errors, whereas DAP treats attributes as independent. Framing the advance as improved modeling of attribute reliability and correlation would be more precise. A potentially relevant related work to cite in this context is Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” which also addresses calibration and fusion of multiple attributes.

Significance: The method addresses a practical and important issue in zero-shot learning—noisy attribute predictions—and shows consistent improvements over strong baselines. The gains are not uniformly large, but they are robust and most pronounced where attribute classifiers are weak, which is arguably the regime where zero-shot methods matter most. The extension to discovered (pseudo-)attributes and few-shot learning broadens applicability, although the few-shot component could benefit from deeper analysis.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Data requirements for reliable per-node ROC estimates: Because validation samples are recursively partitioned, the effective sample size at deeper nodes can be small. The paper acknowledges this and falls back to global ROC estimates when counts are low (line 323), but this is heuristic. A more systematic treatment (e.g., Bayesian smoothing of TPR/FPR estimates, or sharing statistics across nearby nodes) would be valuable. Empirical analysis of sensitivity to validation set size—and the trade-off between reserving data for validation vs. training—would strengthen the claims.
- Novelty claims: Tone down the contrast with prior work like DAP; clarify that the main advance is ROC-based, dependency-aware split selection rather than introducing a fundamentally new way to handle attribute uncertainty.
- Few-shot section: Provide more intuition or derivation for Eq. 6, and consider a comparison to a probabilistic fusion approach that integrates class-attribute priors and image-based attribute estimates directly in p(class|x).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated method that addresses a real weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Simple, effective integration of ROC-based reliability into tree construction; captures attribute error dependencies.
- Consistent empirical improvements on three datasets; informative controlled-noise studies.
- Applicable to both named and discovered attributes; natural few-shot extension.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to DAP; some claims about prior work are overstated.
- Heuristic handling of small validation counts; no systematic study of validation/training size effects.
- Few-shot extension is underdeveloped relative to the core contribution; alternative fusion strategies not explored.
- Improvements, while consistent, are sometimes modest.

Overall, this is a well-written paper with sound technical choices and supportive experiments. I view the contribution as a solid refinement that improves robustness to attribute unreliability by modeling reliability and dependencies more explicitly, rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. With tempered claims and additional analysis of data requirements (and a more developed few-shot section), it would be a strong addition.
