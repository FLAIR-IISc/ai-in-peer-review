This paper aims to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theoretical premise is that novel classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors. In practice, however, training these attribute classifiers can be as challenging, or even more so, than learning the object classes directly.

To address this, the authors propose a method that trains random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors. A key aspect of their training procedure is that it accounts for the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree at training time. The authors also demonstrate how the method can be extended to the few-shot learning setting, where a small number of training examples from test categories are available. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on several standard attribute datasets.

**QUALITY:** The authors have done an excellent job of addressing this fundamental problem in zero-shot learning using a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound. The empirical evaluation is thorough and demonstrates strong results on three standard datasets. The authors provide a comprehensive analysis, including ablation studies, experiments with artificial noise, an evaluation of both zero-shot and few-shot learning settings, and detailed comparisons with the existing literature.

**CLARITY:** The paper is very well-written. A minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1: when the threshold `t` is introduced, the attribute signatures are still binary. This implies that any value for `t` in the range (0, 1) has an identical effect on equation (2). It is therefore initially unclear, particularly in lines 207-212, how a novel test example can be propagated down the tree, as `t` appears to be ill-defined. While this is clarified in subsequent sections, the initial presentation could be improved.

**ORIGINALITY:** This work is a novel and effective application of the random forest framework to an important problem.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. It is a strong, interesting, and methodologically sound contribution. The convincing results across three datasets in a variety of settings demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The paper merits publication.
