This paper presents a random-forest approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for uncertainty in attribute classifier outputs. Uncertainty is quantified using operating characteristics (e.g., true positive and false positive rates) measured on a held-out validation set. These reliability estimates are incorporated into the information-gain criterion during tree construction, allowing a category–attribute signature to traverse multiple branches to reflect ambiguity in attribute predictions.

Quality: The empirical results and quantitative analyses are convincing and well-aligned with the stated goals. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis. For instance, it would be helpful to see which attributes and categories benefit most from the proposed approach, along with illustrative examples. In addition, the methodology would be stronger with clearer justification of design choices and hyperparameters—e.g., why an 80/20 train–validation split was selected—and a sensitivity analysis to show robustness to these settings.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow.

Originality: Whereas much prior work targets “attribute strength,” this paper focuses on the less-explored notion of “attribute reliability.” Although many methods may implicitly accommodate classifier noise, the explicit integration of measured reliability into random-forest learning appears novel.

Significance: While the use of random forests itself is not new, the idea of leveraging validation-based reliability estimates to improve zero-shot learning is interesting and thought-provoking for researchers working with attributes. The methodological novelty is moderate, but its application to zero-shot recognition is insightful and potentially impactful.
