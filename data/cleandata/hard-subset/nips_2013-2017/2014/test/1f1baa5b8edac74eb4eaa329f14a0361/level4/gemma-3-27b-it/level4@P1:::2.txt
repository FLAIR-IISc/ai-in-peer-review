This paper proposes a new random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that better accommodates the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. The method explicitly models this uncertainty using the performance (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. This performance information is then incorporated into the information gain computation during random forest learning, allowing the model to explore multiple paths in the decision tree for a given category-attribute signature and, consequently, better handle unreliable attribute predictions.

**Quality:** The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper could benefit from qualitative analysis – for example, examining which types of attributes and categories are most affected and improved by the proposed method. Additionally, it is unclear how certain parameters were selected (e.g., the rationale behind the 80%-20% split for training and validation data). A more detailed discussion of parameter selection would strengthen the work. Despite these minor points, the technical approach appears sound and the results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanations are clear and the organization is logical, making the core contributions readily accessible.

**Originality:** While previous works have largely focused on modeling “attribute strength,” this paper addresses the comparatively less explored problem of “attribute reliability.” Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of this issue within the context of zero-shot learning. The authors effectively differentiate their approach from existing techniques.

**Significance:** While the underlying random forest method is not novel, the core idea – modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning performance – is interesting and potentially impactful for researchers working with attributes. The demonstrated performance gains, particularly against a strong baseline like DAP, suggest the proposed method could become a valuable tool in this area.

**Overall:** The idea and method introduced in this paper are valuable and their application towards improving zero-shot learning is revealing. While the method isn’t groundbreaking in terms of fundamental algorithmic innovation, the specific application to attribute reliability in zero-shot learning, along with the demonstrated improvements, makes this a worthwhile contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical and important problem in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions.
*   Presents a novel approach to explicitly model and mitigate attribute unreliability using receiver operating characteristic analysis.
*   Demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets.
*   The approach is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by thorough experiments.
*   Extends naturally to the few-shot learning setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks detailed qualitative analysis to understand *why* the method works and which attributes benefit the most.
*   Parameter selection is not fully justified; a more rigorous explanation would be beneficial.
*   The method relies on a separate validation set, adding a potential cost in terms of data requirements (though a reasonable trade-off given the performance gains).
