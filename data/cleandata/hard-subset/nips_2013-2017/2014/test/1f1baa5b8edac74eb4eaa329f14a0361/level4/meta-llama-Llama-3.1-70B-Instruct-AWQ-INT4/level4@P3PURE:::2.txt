This paper proposes a novel random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that effectively accommodates the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. The authors measure uncertainty via the performance of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set, using metrics such as true positive rate and false positive rate. They then incorporate this performance information into the information gain computation during the learning of the random forest, thereby enabling the pursuit of a given category-attribute signature along multiple paths of a decision tree and accounting for the "uncertainty" of attribute predictions.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper would benefit from more qualitative analysis, such as an examination of the types of attributes and categories that are most affected or improved by this method. Furthermore, it is unclear how many of the parameters involved were selected, and the authors could provide more insight into the choice of certain hyperparameters, such as the 80%-20% split for training-validation.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to read, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Originality: While most previous works have focused on addressing the problem of "attribute strength," this paper claims to tackle the lesser-explored problem of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms and techniques implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of unreliability.

Significance: Although the proposed method (random forest) is not new, the idea of modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning performance is interesting and thought-provoking for researchers working on attributes. While the idea and method introduced in this paper are not significantly novel, their application towards improving zero-shot learning is revealing and interesting.
