- Problem: Addresses the gap between theoretical promise and practical implementation in attribute-based zero-shot learning, where learning attribute classifiers is often as hard as learning object classes.
- Proposed method: Introduces a random-forest approach that predicts unseen classes from attribute vectors and incorporates attribute detector reliability by propagating a validation set through each tree.
- Few-shot extension: Demonstrates how the method naturally extends to few-shot learning with limited test-category examples.
- Results: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard attribute-based benchmarks.
- Experiments: Supported by thorough ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, zero-shot vs few-shot evaluations, and comprehensive comparisons to prior work.
- Quality: Elegant, theoretically grounded design that effectively leverages random forests and addresses practical attribute reliability issues.
- Clarity: Generally well-written; minor confusion in Section 3.2.1 about the threshold parameter t — needs clarification on transition from binary attribute signatures to continuous outputs.
- Originality: Novel application of random forests combined with modeling of attribute reliability and few-shot adaptation in zero-shot recognition.
- Significance: Tackles a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning and offers a practical, extendable framework with clear implications for recognition systems.
- Recommendation: Strong paper, well-executed and significant; merits publication at NIPS.
