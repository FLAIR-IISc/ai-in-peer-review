

This paper introduces a novel random forest approach for zero-shot learning that explicitly addresses the unreliability of attribute predictions, a critical limitation in existing methods. The authors model attribute classifier uncertainty using receiver operating characteristics (ROC) derived from validation data, incorporating these statistics into the information gain computation during tree construction. This enables fractional propagation of class signatures through the forest, accounting for false positive/negative rates during attribute prediction. The work extends to few-shot learning and demonstrates consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods (e.g., DAP) across three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), particularly benefiting categories with high attribute prediction error.  

Quality: The technical approach is well-supported by comprehensive experiments, including controlled noise tests and real-world attribute reliability analysis. The method significantly outperforms SIGNATURE RF and DAP (e.g., +10.2% on "giant panda" in AwA), validating the core claim that modeling attribute unreliability is more impactful than increasing attribute classifier quality. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis to explain *which attributes* or *categories* most benefit from the proposed uncertainty handling, limiting insights into the method’s practical applicability. The 80%-20% split for attribute classifier training/validation is standard in ML but should be explicitly justified (e.g., as a common practice to estimate ROC curves without overfitting).  

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the fractional propagation mechanism and thorough experimental setup. The pseudocode and schematic in the supplement (referenced appropriately) further enhance reproducibility.  

Originality: While random forests are not new, the explicit integration of attribute classifier reliability (via ROC statistics) during tree training represents a novel contribution distinct from prior work focused on *attribute strength* (e.g., [19, 8]). The paper effectively differentiates this from related concepts (e.g., fractional tuples for missing values [21]) by highlighting the zero-shot setting’s unique challenges (e.g., domain shift between training signatures and test attribute predictions).  

Significance: The work resolves a key practical barrier to zero-shot learning—attribute unreliability—demonstrating that reliable zero-shot models are achievable even with imperfect mid-level attributes. This advances the field beyond "proof-of-concept" status, as prior methods [23] fail when attributes are unreliable. The few-shot extension further broadens applicability, making the method valuable for real-world scenarios with scarce labeled data. While the random forest framework itself is established, its adaptation to explicitly model attribute uncertainty provides a compelling, practical solution that could influence future work on semantic-based learning.  

Pro: Novel handling of attribute unreliability via ROC statistics; strong empirical validation across three datasets; clear few-shot extension; addresses a critical limitation in prior zero-shot literature.  
Con: Limited qualitative analysis of attribute/category-specific improvements; parameter choice (80-20 split) lacks justification; minor redundancy in describing ROC versus attribute strength.
