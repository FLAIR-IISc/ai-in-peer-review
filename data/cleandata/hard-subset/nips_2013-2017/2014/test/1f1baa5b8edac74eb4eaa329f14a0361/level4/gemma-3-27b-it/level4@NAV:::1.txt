This paper investigates the challenge of translating attribute-based zero-shot learning theory into practical applications. While the premise – recognizing novel classes via pre-trained attribute predictors – is theoretically promising, training effective attribute classifiers often presents significant difficulties. 

The authors address this by employing random forests to predict unseen classes based on attribute vectors. Critically, their training procedure incorporates the reliability of individual attribute detectors by utilizing a validation set during the construction of each decision tree. They further demonstrate the method’s adaptability to few-shot learning scenarios with limited training examples from test categories. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple benchmark attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors present a well-conceived and theoretically grounded solution to a core problem in zero-shot learning, leveraging the elegance of a random forest framework. The reported results on three standard datasets are compelling, and the thoroughness of the evaluation – including ablation studies, noise analysis, and comparisons across zero- and few-shot learning settings – is commendable.

CLARITY: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. A minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 regarding the threshold *t*; given the continued use of binary attribute signatures, *t* appears to have no effect on equation (2) for values between 0 and 1. Consequently, the propagation of a novel test example down the tree as described in lines 207-212 is initially unclear due to the ill-defined nature of *t* at that point – this is clarified with further explanation later in the paper.

ORIGINALITY: This work represents a valuable application of random forest methods to a significant problem in the field. 

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental issue within zero-shot learning, and the strong, convincing results obtained across various datasets and experimental settings demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach. Overall, this is a strong and interesting contribution that merits publication.
