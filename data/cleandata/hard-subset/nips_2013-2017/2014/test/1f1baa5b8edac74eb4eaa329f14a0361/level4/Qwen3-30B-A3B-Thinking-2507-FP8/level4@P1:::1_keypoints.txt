- Bridges theory and practice in attribute-based zero-shot learning by explicitly addressing attribute classifier unreliability.  
- Proposes a novel random forest method that incorporates attribute classifier error statistics during training.  
- Propagates validation data through decision trees to model how attribute prediction errors affect class prediction.  
- Extends the framework to handle few-shot learning scenarios.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks (AwA, aPY, SUN).  
- Theoretical approach is sound and elegantly targets a largely overlooked practical problem.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive: multiple datasets, ablation studies, controlled noise tests, and comparisons in zero- and few-shot settings.  
- Technical details, pseudocode, and supplementary material provide sufficient detail for reproduction; paper is well-structured and mostly clear.  
- Minor clarity issue around threshold t and initial description of test example propagation in Section 3.2.1, but resolved later.  
- Original contribution: first to explicitly incorporate attribute classifier reliability into random forest tree-building; applicable to discovered attributes as well.  
- Significant practical impact: enables more reliable zero-shot recognition with imperfect attributes and shows large gains on datasets with unreliable attribute classifiers.  
- Small limitation: relies on the assumption that validation data from seen classes accurately reflects error patterns on unseen classes, which could use more discussion.
