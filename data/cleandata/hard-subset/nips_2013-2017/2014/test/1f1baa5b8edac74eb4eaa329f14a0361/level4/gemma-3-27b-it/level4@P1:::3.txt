This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is trained on class-attribute signature vectors, while a validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clear and well-written. It does a good job summarizing related work.

**Originality:** The novelty of this paper is moderate, but sufficient for consideration. The claim that the proposed approach fundamentally differs in its handling of unreliable attribute predictions compared to earlier work is perhaps overstated. For example, Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8]—the baseline method used in this paper and many others—already incorporates mechanisms to address attribute uncertainty. Both approaches share a common framework: 
a) Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical for both papers)
b) Train a second-stage classifier to estimate class probability as a function of attribute predictions, utilizing a validation set to assess the reliability of attribute classifiers ([8] employs Platt scaling and a probabilistic model to combine attributes, whereas the proposed approach uses a novel random forest-based method).

Therefore, the contribution does not lie in introducing the concept of accounting for attribute uncertainty, but rather in offering a distinct method for modeling it. Specifically, the proposed approach differentiates itself by employing an ROC-based estimate instead of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and by effectively modeling joint statistics in attribute errors, leveraging the validation set’s propagation through decision trees – a departure from methods treating attributes as independent. In this light, framing the approach as an improved method for modeling attribute correlations might be more accurate. A related work that could be considered is Scheirer et al. "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search."

A potential weakness compared to methods treating attributes independently is the requirement for a substantial validation set to obtain reliable probability estimates, particularly as the data is subdivided during propagation down decision trees. This issue is briefly addressed in the paper (line 323) with a heuristic solution. More experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size would strengthen the analysis.

**Quality:** The paper is generally of good quality, with sound technical choices and clear descriptions. The experiments are appropriate and support the claims, although the magnitude of quantitative improvement is not substantial enough for acceptance solely based on empirical results. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the rest of the paper. While the core of the paper provides detailed explanations, this section is comparatively brief. A method integrating class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates to directly estimate p(a|…) might be more intuitive than the proposed weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6). Overall, the paper is well-written with sensible technical decisions and adequate experiments; however, the central premise of presenting a fundamentally new approach to handling unreliable attributes is debatable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Solid technical foundation and logical flow of ideas.
*   Demonstrated improvement over strong baselines on multiple datasets.
*   Novel approach to modeling attribute uncertainty through ROC curves and joint error statistics.
*   Successful extension to the few-shot learning setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; the core idea builds upon existing attribute-based zero-shot learning techniques.
*   The claim of fundamentally addressing attribute unreliability is potentially overstated, as existing methods like DAP already consider uncertainty.
*   Limited discussion of the validation set size requirement and its impact on performance.
*   The few-shot learning extension feels less developed and lacks detailed justification for the chosen approach.
