

The paper proposes a novel approach to bridge the theoretical and practical gaps in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL). While theory posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, practice reveals that learning reliable attribute classifiers is often as challenging as learning object classes directly. The authors address this by training random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating attribute detector reliability through validation-set propagation during tree construction. The method further extends to few-shot learning scenarios with minimal examples per test category, achieving state-of-the-art results across three standard attribute datasets.

**Quality:** The authors present a well-motivated and theoretically grounded solution to a core ZSL challenge. The random forest framework elegantly handles attribute uncertainty by propagating validation examples through decision trees, avoiding overfitting to unreliable attributes. The experimental evaluation is thorough: ablation studies validate the necessity of key components, controlled noise injections demonstrate robustness, and comparisons across zero-shot and few-shot settings provide comprehensive insights. Results consistently outperform prior methods on the AWA, CUB, and SUN datasets, with statistically significant gains in several cases. The technical execution is sound and reproducible.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. A minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1 regarding the threshold parameter *t*. Equation (2) assumes binary attribute signatures, making *t* (a continuous value between 0 and 1) irrelevant to the formulation at that stage. The subsequent explanation of test example propagation (lines 207–212) initially appears inconsistent with this, as *t* is then treated as a continuous threshold for continuous attribute scores. This ambiguity is resolved later in Section 3.3, where the transition to continuous attribute scores is explicitly justified. Clarifying the role of *t* earlier—perhaps by noting its purpose only applies after attribute scores are made continuous—would enhance readability.

**Originality:** The application of random forests to attribute-based ZSL is highly original. While random forests are common in classification, their integration with validation-aware propagation for ZSL represents a fresh perspective. The authors effectively leverage the tree structure’s inherent ability to model attribute dependencies and uncertainty, a direction underexplored in prior ZSL work. This framework offers a principled alternative to common embedding-based or generative approaches.

**Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in ZSL: the disconnect between theoretical promise and practical performance due to unreliable attribute predictors. By directly modeling attribute reliability within the classifier training process, the method provides a practical path to more robust ZSL systems. The extension to few-shot learning further broadens applicability, addressing a critical real-world constraint where limited test-category data is common. This work advances both the methodology and empirical understanding of ZSL, with clear potential for impact in vision and NLP applications requiring recognition of unseen categories.

Overall, the paper is strong, well-executed, and significant. The core idea is novel, the experiments are rigorous and convincing, and the results represent a meaningful advancement in ZSL. The minor clarity issue in Section 3.2.1 is easily resolvable with a brief clarification in the final version. The work merits publication in NIPS.
