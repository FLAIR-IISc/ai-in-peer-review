This paper introduces a random forest–based approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly models uncertainty in attribute classifier outputs. Uncertainty is quantified using attribute-level performance statistics (e.g., true positive rate, false positive rate) computed on a held-out validation set. These reliability estimates are incorporated into the information gain computation during tree induction, allowing a given class–attribute signature to follow multiple paths within a decision tree and thereby accommodating uncertainty in attribute predictions.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well supported by relevant empirical/quantitative results. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis (e.g., which attributes and which categories are most affected or improved by the method). It is also unclear how several parameters were chosen (e.g., the rationale for the 80%-20% training–validation split).

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow.

Originality: While most prior work has focused on “attribute strength,” this paper targets the less explored problem of “attribute reliability.” Although many learning techniques implicitly handle classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit treatment of unreliability.

Significance: Although the random forest framework itself is not new, the idea of modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning is interesting and thought-provoking for researchers working on attributes. While neither the idea nor the method is strongly novel, their application to zero-shot learning is revealing and interesting.
