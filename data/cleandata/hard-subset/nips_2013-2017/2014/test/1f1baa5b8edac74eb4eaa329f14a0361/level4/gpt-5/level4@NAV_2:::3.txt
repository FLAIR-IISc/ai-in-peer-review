This paper proposes a random-forest approach to attribute-based zero-shot learning. Attribute classifiers are first trained with SVMs. A forest is then learned over class–attribute signature vectors, while a separate set of attribute-labeled images serves as validation data to estimate attribute reliability at each tree node. An extension to the few-shot setting is also presented.

Clarity: The manuscript is clear and well organized. The coverage of related work is thorough and easy to follow.

Originality: The paper’s novelty is moderate—not too low for acceptance, but the claim that prior work does not address unreliable attributes feels somewhat overstated. For instance, direct attribute prediction [7] (DAP)—the baseline here and in many prior works—already incorporates mechanisms to handle attribute uncertainty. Both approaches follow a similar two-step pattern:
a) Train attribute classifiers on image features (shared by both).
b) Learn a second-stage class probability estimator that consumes attribute predictions, using a validation set to calibrate attribute reliability ([6] employs Platt scaling and a probabilistic combination of attributes, whereas the proposed method uses a new random-forest-based scheme).
Thus, the contribution is not a fundamentally new paradigm for handling uncertainty so much as a richer noise model: (1) it leverages ROC-based calibration instead of a sigmoidal model, and (2) unlike [6], it effectively captures joint structure in attribute errors by propagating validation data down the trees. Framed this way, it may be more accurate to characterize the advance as improved modeling of attribute correlations. I’m not deeply familiar with this sub-area, but Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” might be a relevant citation.

A potential drawback relative to methods that treat attributes independently is the apparent need for a fairly large validation set, since it is progressively partitioned as it flows down the trees. This limitation is not examined in depth, and the proposed fallback (line 323) appears heuristic. Additional experiments exploring sensitivity to the sizes of the training and validation sets would strengthen the paper.

Quality: Overall, the technical content is sound, and the design choices are reasonable. The experiments are appropriate and support the claims, though the absolute gains are not so large as to justify acceptance purely on empirical improvement. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) appears less developed than the rest; that section is brief relative to earlier, more detailed expositions. Conceptually, a mechanism that fuses class–attribute priors with image-level attribute estimates directly in p(a|...) might be more intuitive than the weighted sum of information gains in Eq. 6. In sum, this is a well-written paper with sensible methodology and adequate experiments; however, the core selling point—that it introduces a fundamentally new way to handle unreliable attributes—seems debatable.
