

This paper addresses a critical gap in zero-shot learning: the practical challenge that attribute classifiers—central to the zero-shot paradigm—are often unreliable, undermining the approach's theoretical promise. The authors propose a novel random forest framework that explicitly models attribute prediction unreliability during training, using receiver operating characteristic (ROC) statistics from validation data to guide tree construction. Their method propagates training signatures probabilistically through the tree, accounting for false positive and true positive rates of attribute classifiers. The approach extends naturally to few-shot learning and achieves state-of-the-art results on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods like DAP and signature-based random forests.

**Quality.** The work is technically rigorous and well-executed. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for handling attribute unreliability, with a novel information gain formulation (Eq. 5) that integrates ROC statistics into tree splitting. Their ablation studies (Table 1) convincingly isolate the contributions of key components: (1) ROC-based propagation (vs. global statistics), and (2) modeling signature uncertainty. The controlled noise experiments (Fig. 1) elegantly demonstrate how the method adapts to varying attribute reliability, outperforming baselines by up to 10.2% on challenging categories (e.g., "giant panda") where DAP fails. The few-shot extension (Sec. 3.3) is logically motivated and empirically validated (Fig. 2a), showing robust gains even with minimal labeled data. All experiments use standard protocols and datasets, and the 20-trial averaging ensures statistical reliability. The method’s dependence on attribute classifier quality is well-acknowledged and mitigated via careful validation data usage, avoiding the common pitfall of assuming perfect attributes.

**Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear technical exposition. The core idea—probabilistic signature propagation based on attribute error statistics—is intuitively explained through the forest construction process (Sec. 3.2.2). Minor ambiguity in Sec. 3.2.1 regarding the threshold *t* (which is irrelevant for binary signatures) is resolved in later sections (3.2.2–3.2.3), and the authors wisely defer the threshold’s role until the unreliability-aware formulation. The figures (Fig. 1, 2a–b) effectively illustrate key insights, and the supplementary material provides necessary algorithmic details. The distinction between attribute unreliability (our focus) and attribute strength (e.g., [19]) is clearly articulated, avoiding confusion with prior work.

**Originality.** The paper makes a significant contribution by reframing zero-shot learning through the lens of *attribute classifier reliability*, a problem overlooked in prior work (e.g., [8, 18, 23]). While random forests have been used in vision, this is the first application to zero-shot learning that explicitly models attribute errors. The method’s conceptual link to fractional tuples for missing values ([21]) is insightful but distinct, as it handles *predictive uncertainty* (not missing data) in a zero-shot context. The few-shot extension (Sec. 3.3) further extends the framework, offering a new way to incorporate descriptive priors into random forests. Crucially, the authors validate that their approach works with *both* hand-designed and discovered attributes ([30]), broadening its applicability beyond semantic constraints.

**Significance.** This work resolves a major practical barrier to zero-shot learning: the fragility of attribute-based systems. By demonstrating consistent gains over DAP (the leading method) across three diverse datasets—even when using only 80% of attribute-labeled data—the paper proves that modeling attribute unreliability is more impactful than improving attribute classifiers alone. This advances the field from "proof of concept" to a viable tool for real-world applications (e.g., rare animal species identification, dynamic category definition in scientific imaging). The results on AwA using discovered attributes ([30]) further show compatibility with contemporary attribute discovery pipelines, suggesting broader adoption potential. The method’s simplicity and scalability (100 trees per forest) make it readily deployable, and the few-shot extension directly addresses a common practical need.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pro*: (1) Solves a fundamental, previously unaddressed flaw in zero-shot learning; (2) Theoretical contribution (ROC-based propagation) is elegant and sound; (3) Strong, reproducible results across multiple datasets and settings; (4) Clear extension to few-shot learning; (5) Well-motivated and well-executed.  
*Con*: The few-shot results (Fig. 2a) show diminishing returns as labeled examples increase, but this aligns with expectations and does not weaken the core contribution. The method’s reliance on validation data for ROC estimation is a minor but necessary dependency, acknowledged by the authors.

This paper makes a substantial, well-supported contribution to zero-shot learning. It successfully bridges the gap between theory and practice by addressing the core issue of attribute unreliability, providing a robust, scalable solution that outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The clarity, rigor, and significance of the work meet NIPS standards for publication. I strongly recommend acceptance.
