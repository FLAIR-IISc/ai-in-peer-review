This paper proposes a random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that is designed to better accommodate the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. This uncertainty is quantified by measuring the performance (e.g., true positive rate, false positive rate) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. The core of the proposed method is to use this performance information within the information gain computation when learning the random forest, thereby accounting for the unreliability of attribute predictions by allowing a given category-attribute signature to be pursued along multiple paths of a decision tree.

**Quality:** The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis; for instance, a discussion of the types of attributes and categories that are most affected or improved by this method would have provided valuable insight. Furthermore, it is unclear how several key parameters were selected, such as the choice of an 80%-20% split for the training and validation sets.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to read.

**Originality:** While most previous work has addressed the problem of "attribute strength," this paper claims to focus on the less explored problem of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of this issue.

**Significance:** While the proposed method (a random forest) is not new, the idea of modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning performance is an interesting and thought-provoking contribution for researchers working on attribute-based models. The application of these established techniques to improve zero-shot learning is both interesting and revealing.
