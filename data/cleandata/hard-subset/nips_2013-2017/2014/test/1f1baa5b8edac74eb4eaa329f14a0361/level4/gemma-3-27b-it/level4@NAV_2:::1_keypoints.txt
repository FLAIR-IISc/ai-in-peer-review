- Addresses a key problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning: unreliable attribute predictions cause a gap between theory and practice.  
- Core idea: explicitly model attribute prediction uncertainty during training instead of treating predictions as ground truth.  
- Uses a novel random forest framework with a training procedure that propagates validation data through trees to capture attribute classifier error tendencies.  
- Method naturally extends to the few-shot learning setting.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple benchmark datasets.  
- Technically sound, well-motivated approach with clear theoretical underpinnings.  
- Comprehensive experiments and well-designed ablation studies, including controlled noise tests and comparisons to prior methods.  
- Exceptionally well-written and organized paper with sufficient reproducibility details.  
- Minor clarity issue: the role of threshold t in Section 3.2.1 is initially confusing given binary attribute signatures and the entropy formulation.  
- Original contribution: novel use of random forests to model attribute unreliability during zero-shot learning.  
- Significant practical impact: improves learning of new categories with few or no labels, especially when attribute classifiers are unreliable.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technical quality, strong results, addresses fundamental limitation, clear presentation, thorough ablations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: minor clarification needed on threshold t and performance gains are not dramatic in all scenarios.  
- Overall recommendation: strong, interesting, and deserving of publication.
