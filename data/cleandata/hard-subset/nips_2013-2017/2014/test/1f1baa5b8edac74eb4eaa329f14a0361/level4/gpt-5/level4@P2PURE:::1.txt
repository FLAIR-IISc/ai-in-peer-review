The paper aims to narrow the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theoretical premise is that unseen classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors; in practice, however, training reliable attribute classifiers can be as hard as—or harder than—learning the object classes themselves. The authors train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and the training procedure accounts for the reliability of attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They also show how to extend the method to the few-shot setting with a small number of training examples from the test categories. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors effectively address this core zero-shot learning problem using a random-forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically well-grounded. Results on three standard datasets are strong. The experiments are thorough, including ablations, injected artificial noise, evaluations in both zero-shot and few-shot settings, and comprehensive comparisons to prior work.

CLARITY: The paper is very well written. One point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: when the threshold t is introduced, the attribute signatures are still binary, so any 0 < t < 1 leaves equation (2) unchanged. Consequently, it is unclear in lines 207–212 how a novel test example is propagated down the tree, since t appears ill-defined. This is clarified in later sections.

ORIGINALITY: This is a well-executed application of a random-forest framework to an important problem.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. It is strong, interesting, and sound. The results on three datasets across multiple settings are convincing. The paper deserves to be published.
