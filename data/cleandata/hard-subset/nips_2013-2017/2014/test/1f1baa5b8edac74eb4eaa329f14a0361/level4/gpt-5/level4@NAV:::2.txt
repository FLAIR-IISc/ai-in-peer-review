This paper presents a random-forest-based approach to zero-shot learning that more effectively accounts for uncertainty in attribute classifier outputs. Uncertainty is quantified using the classifiers’ performance (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) on a held-out validation set. This reliability information is incorporated into the information gain computation during tree training (allowing a given category–attribute signature to traverse multiple paths in a decision tree), thereby addressing the “uncertainty” of attribute predictions.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well supported by relevant empirical/quantitative evaluations. However, the qualitative analysis is limited (e.g., I would have liked to see which attribute types and categories are most affected/improved by this method). It is also unclear how several parameters were selected (e.g., why an 80%–20% train–validation split was chosen).

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Originality: While most prior work has focused on “attribute strength,” this paper emphasizes the less-explored issue of “attribute reliability.” Although many learning techniques implicitly handle classifier unreliability, this work appears novel in explicitly modeling unreliability.

Significance: Although the learning method (random forests) is not new, the idea of leveraging validation-set performance to model unreliability for improved zero-shot learning is interesting and thought-provoking for attribute research. While neither the idea nor the method is highly novel, their application to zero-shot learning is insightful and engaging.
