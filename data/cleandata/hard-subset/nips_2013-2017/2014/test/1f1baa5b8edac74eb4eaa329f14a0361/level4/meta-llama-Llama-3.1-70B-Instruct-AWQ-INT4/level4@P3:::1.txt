The paper "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes" strives to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theory is that novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors; in practice, however, learning these attribute classifiers can be as difficult or even more so than learning the object classes themselves.

The authors propose a novel random forest approach to train zero-shot models that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. By leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies, their method obtains more robust discriminative models for the unseen classes. The approach is extended to handle the few-shot scenario and unreliable attribute descriptions.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their method, including the key components and the reasoning behind their design choices. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on three standard datasets and comparisons to several state-of-the-art methods.

The authors do a nice job of handling this fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound. Results on 3 standard datasets are strong. The authors do a nice job of performing ablation studies, introducing artificial noise, evaluating several settings (zero- versus few-shot learning), and comparing with the literature.

One minor point of confusion is in Section 3.2.1, where the threshold t is introduced. The attribute signatures are still binary, so t can be any value 0 < t < 1 without changing anything in equation (2). This is clarified later in the paper, but it may cause some initial confusion.

The paper is a nice application of a random forest framework to an important problem. The authors address a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, and their results are convincing. The paper is strong, interesting, and sound, and it deserves to be published.
