

This paper proposes a novel random forest approach for zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea leverages the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers—quantified via true positive rates (TPR) and false positive rates (FPR) on a held-out validation set—to model uncertainty during random forest training. Specifically, rather than treating attribute predictions as perfect, the method fractionally propagates each unseen class’s attribute signature through decision trees based on the classifier’s error tendencies. This allows signatures to traverse multiple paths in the tree, with the fraction of propagation determined by the classifier’s reliability at each node. The authors further extend this to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning scenarios. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods like DAP and SIGNATURE RF, particularly when attribute classifiers are unreliable.

**Quality**  
The paper presents strong empirical validation, with comprehensive experiments across three diverse datasets and detailed ablation studies isolating the contributions of key components (e.g., ROC-based propagation vs. signature uncertainty modeling). The results convincingly show that explicitly modeling attribute unreliability—rather than assuming perfect predictions—yields significant gains, especially on datasets like SUN and aPY where attribute classifiers are less reliable due to sparse positive examples per attribute. For instance, the method outperforms DAP by 9.4% on "whale seal" (aPY) and 10.2% on "giant panda" (AwA), with gains widening when attribute training data is reduced to 500 images (28.0% vs. 20.4% accuracy). The ablation studies (Table 1) effectively demonstrate that both ROC-based propagation *and* signature uncertainty modeling contribute to the full method’s success, with the latter providing a modest but consistent boost. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis to contextualize *why* certain attributes or categories benefit more. For example, it would be valuable to identify specific attributes (e.g., "striped" vs. "furry") that drive performance gains or losses, or to analyze which unseen categories (e.g., "giant panda" vs. "persian cat") show the largest improvements. Additionally, while the 80%/20% split for attribute classifier training/validation is used consistently, the rationale for this specific split (rather than, say, 70/30) is not justified, leaving room for questions about sensitivity to this choice.

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. The problem statement, methodology, and experimental setup are clearly articulated, with sufficient technical detail to reproduce the approach (e.g., the fractional propagation mechanism in Section 3.2.2 is precisely defined). The figures (e.g., Figure 1’s noise experiments, Figure 2’s few-shot results) effectively complement the text and highlight key insights. The distinction between "attribute strength" (how strongly an attribute is present) and "attribute reliability" (how accurately it is predicted) is well-motivated and critical to the contribution, avoiding common confusion in the literature. Minor improvements could include a brief glossary of terms like "signature uncertainty" in the introduction to aid readers unfamiliar with zero-shot nuances.

**Originality**  
The paper makes a compelling case for focusing on *attribute reliability*—a neglected aspect compared to *attribute strength* (e.g., in DAP [8] or [19]). While prior work implicitly handles classifier noise (e.g., via probabilistic models), this is the first to explicitly incorporate ROC statistics *during the training of zero-shot classifiers* to account for error tendencies. Crucially, the authors clarify that their approach is distinct from handling missing values (e.g., [21]) because the training and test domains differ fundamentally: the method builds trees from attribute *signatures* (not image features), and propagates both signatures *and* validation data through the tree to model dependencies. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) further extends the idea to leverage small amounts of labeled data, which is novel in the context of uncertainty-aware zero-shot learning. This focus on *explicitly modeling the reliability of attribute predictors*—rather than just the attributes themselves—represents a meaningful conceptual advance over prior work.

**Significance**  
The work addresses a critical bottleneck in zero-shot learning: the practical unreliability of attribute classifiers. By demonstrating that modeling this unreliability yields substantial gains (e.g., 8+ points over DAP on SUN with limited attribute data), the paper shows that zero-shot learning need not be abandoned due to imperfect attributes. The results also suggest broader applicability: the method achieves state-of-the-art performance on AwA even with *discovered* attributes (Section 4.1), and the few-shot extension (Figure 2a) provides a practical path for scenarios with minimal labeled data. While the random forest itself is not new, the *adaptation* of this framework to explicitly handle attribute uncertainty in zero-shot settings is significant. It offers a simple yet effective solution that could be integrated into other zero-shot pipelines, particularly where attribute-based descriptions remain the most intuitive way to define novel classes (e.g., for human-in-the-loop systems). The paper’s emphasis on using *real-world attribute classifiers*—not idealized ones—makes the contribution immediately relevant to practitioners.

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper makes a strong technical contribution by rigorously addressing a practical limitation in zero-shot learning. The methodology is sound, experiments are thorough, and results clearly demonstrate the value of modeling attribute unreliability. The primary limitations—lack of qualitative insights into *which* attributes/categories benefit most and insufficient justification for the 80/20 data split—are minor compared to the paper’s strengths. Given the prevalence of attribute-based zero-shot approaches in the field (e.g., DAP is a de facto standard [8, 23, 19]), this work provides a valuable, actionable improvement. It advances the state of the art by showing how to make zero-shot learning robust to the very real imperfections in attribute prediction. For these reasons, I recommend acceptance. The paper’s clarity and technical depth align well with NIPS standards, and its insights will likely influence future work on zero-shot learning and semantic representations.
