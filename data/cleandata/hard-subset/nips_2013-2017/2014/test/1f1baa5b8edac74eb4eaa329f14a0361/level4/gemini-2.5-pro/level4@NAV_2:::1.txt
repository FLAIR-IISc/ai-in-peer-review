This paper addresses a key challenge in zero-shot learning: the discrepancy between the theoretical promise of attribute-based recognition and the practical difficulty of training reliable attribute classifiers. The authors propose a novel approach that trains random forests on the attribute signatures of unseen classes. The core idea is to explicitly account for attribute classifier unreliability during the training process. This is achieved by leveraging a set of attribute-labeled validation data to estimate the error characteristics (e.g., ROC) of each attribute predictor, which then guides the selection of splits in the decision trees. The framework is also extended to the few-shot learning setting. The method demonstrates significant performance gains and achieves state-of-the-art results on three widely-used attribute-based recognition datasets.

QUALITY: The proposed random forest framework is an elegant and theoretically well-motivated solution to the problem of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning. The paper's claims are strongly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation across three standard benchmarks. The authors have done an excellent job of validating their approach through comprehensive ablation studies, experiments with controlled noise to isolate the model's behavior, and direct comparisons to leading methods in both zero-shot and few-shot settings.

CLARITY: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. A minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 concerning the introduction of the threshold `t`. Given that the attribute signatures are binary in this initial formulation, any threshold `t` between 0 and 1 would yield the same split, making its role unclear for propagating a real-valued test vector. However, this point is effectively clarified in Section 3.2.2 when the model is extended to account for attribute unreliability, where the choice of `t` becomes meaningful.

ORIGINALITY: The work presents a novel adaptation of the random forest training algorithm to the zero-shot learning problem. While random forests are a known technique, the proposed method for constructing trees using class signatures while incorporating the error statistics of attribute predictors is new and effectively tailored to address a critical bottleneck in the field.

SIGNIFICANCE: This paper tackles a fundamental and practical issue that has limited the effectiveness of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The proposed solution is effective, and the strong empirical results demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in this area. This is a strong, well-executed, and interesting paper that makes a significant contribution and is well-suited for publication.
