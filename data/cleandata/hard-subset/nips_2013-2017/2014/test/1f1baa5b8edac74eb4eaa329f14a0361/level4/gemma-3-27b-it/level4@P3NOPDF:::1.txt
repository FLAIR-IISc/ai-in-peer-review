## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper addresses a critical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the difficulty of accurately learning attribute predictors, which often rivals or exceeds the complexity of learning the object classes themselves. The authors propose an elegant and theoretically grounded approach leveraging random forests to map attribute vectors to unseen classes. Their method cleverly incorporates the reliability of attribute detectors during training by propagating a validation set through each decision tree, effectively weighting splits based on attribute accuracy. Furthermore, the framework is extended to gracefully handle few-shot learning scenarios, demonstrating versatility and practical applicability. The results achieved on standard benchmark datasets are state-of-the-art, solidifying the contribution of this work.

**Quality:** This is a high-quality paper that tackles a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning with a well-designed and effective solution. The random forest framework is thoughtfully applied and the theoretical justification is sound. The empirical evaluation is particularly strong, with compelling results reported across three standard datasets. The authors demonstrate a commendable attention to detail through comprehensive ablation studies, the introduction of artificial noise for robustness testing, evaluation in both zero-shot and few-shot settings, and thorough comparisons with existing literature. These experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and generally easy to follow. However, a minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1 concerning the introduction of the threshold *t*. As initially presented, the attribute signatures remain binary despite the introduction of *t*, implying that any value between 0 and 1 would be functionally equivalent within equation (2). Consequently, the mechanism described in lines 207-212 for propagating a novel test example down the tree, reliant on *t*, appears initially ill-defined. While this point is clarified in subsequent sections, a more explicit explanation earlier in the section would improve readability and prevent initial confusion. Overall, however, the writing is clear and concise, making the core ideas readily accessible.

**Originality:** The application of a random forest framework to this specific problem in zero-shot learning represents a novel and insightful contribution. While random forests are a well-established technique, their adaptation to address the challenges of attribute-based prediction and the incorporation of attribute detector reliability during training is a creative and effective innovation.

**Significance:** This paper addresses a significant bottleneck in the field of zero-shot learning – the reliable prediction of attributes for novel classes. Improving attribute prediction is crucial for the successful application of zero-shot learning in real-world scenarios. The presented method demonstrates a substantial improvement over existing approaches, offering a practical and effective solution to this critical problem. 

**Overall:** This is a strong, interesting, and technically sound paper. The convincing results obtained on three datasets, across a variety of experimental settings, highlight the practical value and impact of the proposed method. I recommend this paper for publication at NIPS 2014.
