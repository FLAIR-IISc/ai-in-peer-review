This paper proposes a new random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that better accommodates the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. Uncertainty is measured via the performance (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. The paper proposes to use this performance information in the information gain computation during the learning of the random forest, thereby accommodating the "uncertainty" of attribute predictions.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well-supported with relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper could benefit from additional qualitative analysis to provide a deeper understanding of the method's strengths and limitations. For example, it would be helpful to see the types of attributes and categories that are most improved by this method. Furthermore, the authors could provide more insight into the selection of certain parameters, such as the 80%-20% split for training-validation, and how these choices impact the results.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Originality: While most previous works have addressed the problem of "attribute strength", this paper focuses on the lesser-explored problem of "attribute reliability". Although many learning algorithms/techniques implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of unreliability.

Significance: The proposed method, while not entirely new, is interesting and thought-provoking in its application towards improving zero-shot learning. The idea of modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data is a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to handling attribute classifier uncertainty in zero-shot learning.
* The method is well-supported by empirical and quantitative analysis.
* The idea of modeling unreliability is a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from additional qualitative analysis to provide a deeper understanding of the method's strengths and limitations.
* The selection of certain parameters, such as the 80%-20% split for training-validation, is not well-justified.
* The method, while novel in its application, is not entirely new and builds upon existing random forest techniques.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the mentioned concerns.
