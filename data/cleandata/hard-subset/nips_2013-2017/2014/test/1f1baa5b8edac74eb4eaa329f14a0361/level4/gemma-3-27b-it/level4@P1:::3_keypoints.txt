- Paper proposes attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests; SVM-trained attribute classifiers and a validation set estimate attribute reliability per tree node; includes a few-shot extension.
- Paper is very clear and well-written, with a good related work summary.
- Novelty is moderate; the claim of fundamentally different handling of unreliable attributes is likely overstated.
- The approach shares the common two-stage framework with prior work (e.g., DAP): train attribute classifiers, then a second-stage class estimator using a validation set.
- Main technical novelty is using ROC-based estimates and random-forest propagation to model joint attribute error statistics instead of sigmoidal Platt scaling and independent-attribute assumptions.
- A more accurate framing would be that the paper offers an improved method for modeling attribute correlations/errors rather than introducing the concept of uncertainty handling.
- The method may require a large validation set for reliable probability estimates, especially as data is split down trees; only a heuristic fix is discussed.
- More experiments on the effect of training/validation set sizes would strengthen the paper.
- Paper quality is good: sound technical choices, clear descriptions, and appropriate experiments.
- Quantitative improvements over baselines are demonstrated but not large enough to warrant acceptance on empirical gains alone.
- The few-shot learning extension is underdeveloped relative to the rest of the paper and lacks detailed justification.
- A potentially more intuitive few-shot alternative would integrate class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates to directly estimate p(a|...).
- Arguments for acceptance: clear writing, solid technical foundation, improvements over strong baselines, novel ROC/joint-error modeling, and a successful few-shot extension.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, overstated claim about addressing attribute unreliability, limited discussion of validation-set-size impact, and a weakly developed few-shot section.
