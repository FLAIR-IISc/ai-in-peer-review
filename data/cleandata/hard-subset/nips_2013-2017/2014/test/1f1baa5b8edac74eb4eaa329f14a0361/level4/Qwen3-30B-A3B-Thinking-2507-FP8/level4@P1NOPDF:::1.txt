

The paper addresses a critical gap between theory and practice in attribute-based zero-shot learning. While the theoretical premise posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, practical implementation often proves as challenging as learning the object classes themselves. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains attribute classifiers while accounting for detector reliability through validation-set propagation during tree construction. They further extend the method to handle few-shot learning scenarios with minimal examples from test categories, achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple attribute datasets.  

QUALITY: The authors present a compelling solution to a fundamental zero-shot learning challenge. The model is theoretically well-motivated and elegantly implemented. Results on three standard datasets are robust, supported by thorough ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, comprehensive zero- versus few-shot comparisons, and rigorous literature benchmarking. The method’s strength lies in its practical handling of attribute reliability—a key overlooked factor in prior work.  

CLARITY: The paper is clearly written overall. A minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1 regarding the threshold parameter *t*. At this stage, attribute signatures remain binary (0/1), so any *t* ∈ (0,1) leaves Equation (2) unchanged. This creates ambiguity in lines 207–212 about propagating novel test examples, as *t* appears undefined for binary inputs. This issue is resolved later in the paper, but clarifying the transition from binary attributes to continuous thresholds earlier would improve readability.  

ORIGINALITY: The application of random forests to zero-shot learning via reliability-aware attribute classification represents a novel and well-suited approach to a persistent problem in the field.  

SIGNIFICANCE: The work directly tackles a core theoretical-practical disconnect in zero-shot learning, offering a methodologically sound and empirically validated path toward more reliable recognition of unseen classes.  

The paper is strong, compelling, and methodologically sound. The results across three datasets and multiple settings—including rigorous few-shot evaluations—provide convincing evidence of the approach’s efficacy. The authors’ careful attention to practical implementation details (e.g., detector reliability, few-shot adaptation) elevates the contribution beyond incremental improvements. This work merits publication in NIPS.
