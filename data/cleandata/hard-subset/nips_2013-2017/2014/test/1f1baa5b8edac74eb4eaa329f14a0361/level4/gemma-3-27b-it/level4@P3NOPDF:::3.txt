This paper introduces a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning leveraging random forests. The approach first trains attribute classifiers using Support Vector Machines. A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors, with attribute reliability estimated at each node using a validation set of images with attribute labels. An extension to the few-shot learning setting is also presented.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, providing a strong summary of related work in the field. The novelty, while present, is incremental. The central claim that this work uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions is somewhat overstated, as existing methods exhibit similar capabilities. Specifically, direct attribute prediction (DAP) – the baseline used here and in many comparable studies – already incorporates mechanisms for handling attribute uncertainty. Both approaches share a common foundation: training attribute classifiers on image features and subsequently learning a second-stage probability estimator based on these attribute predictions. The distinction lies in the method for estimating attribute reliability: Platt scaling and probabilistic models (e.g., [6]) versus the proposed random forest-based approach. 

Therefore, the contribution is better characterized as a refined method for modeling attribute correlation rather than a fundamentally novel approach to handling attribute uncertainty. The proposed method improves upon previous work by employing an ROC-based reliability estimate instead of a sigmoidal model and, crucially, by implicitly modeling joint statistics in attribute errors through the propagation of the validation set down the decision trees.  Relevant related work in this area, such as Scheirer et al. ("Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search"), might benefit further discussion.

A potential weakness of the proposed approach, compared to methods treating attributes independently, is the requirement for a substantial validation set to ensure accurate probability estimates as data is subdivided during tree propagation. This issue receives insufficient attention in the paper, and the proposed heuristic for addressing it (line 323) lacks thorough justification.  Further experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size would significantly strengthen the work. 

Overall, the paper exhibits good technical quality, with sensible choices and clear descriptions. The experimental evaluation is appropriate and generally supports the claims, although the quantitative improvements are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance based on empirical results alone. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels comparatively underdeveloped.  While the main body of the paper benefits from detailed explanations, this section is notably concise. The proposed weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6) is less intuitively appealing than a method that would combine class-attribute priors with image-derived attribute estimates when estimating probabilities.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experimentation. However, the core novelty—the claim of a new approach to handling unreliable attributes—is debatable.  I recommend further investigation into the validation set size requirements and a more detailed rationale for the chosen few-shot learning methodology.
