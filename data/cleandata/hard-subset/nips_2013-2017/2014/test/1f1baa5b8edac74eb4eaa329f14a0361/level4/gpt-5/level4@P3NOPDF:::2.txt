Summary:
This paper proposes a zero-shot learning approach built on random forests that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The method estimates attribute reliability (e.g., true positive and false positive rates) on a held-out validation set and incorporates this information into the information gain calculation during tree construction. By allowing category–attribute signatures to propagate along multiple paths proportionally to attribute uncertainty, the model aims to make more robust splitting decisions and improve zero-shot recognition.

Quality:
The empirical evaluation is thorough on the quantitative side and generally supports the core claims. The idea of using validation-based estimates of attribute reliability to guide tree splits is clearly operationalized and appears effective. However, the paper would benefit from stronger qualitative analysis. For example:
- Which attributes (e.g., “striped,” “has wheels,” “metallic”) are most improved by the proposed reliability modeling?
- Which categories benefit most, and under what conditions (e.g., fine-grained vs. coarse categories, visually subtle attributes)?
- Are there representative success and failure cases that illuminate the behavior of the method?

In addition, several methodological choices are under-specified:
- Parameter selection: Why was an 80/20 train–validation split chosen? How sensitive are results to this split ratio and to the size of the validation set used for estimating reliability?
- Hyperparameters: How were the number of trees, maximum depth, and other forest settings selected? Is there a validation protocol to avoid overfitting these choices?
- Robustness: How stable are the reliability estimates across different random splits? Are the improvements statistically significant across seeds?

Finally, a brief discussion of computational cost (both training and inference) relative to a standard random forest baseline would be useful.

Clarity:
The paper is well written and easy to follow. The motivation, methodological description, and experimental setup are presented clearly. A concise schematic of how reliability-modified information gain alters the routing of signatures through the tree would make the intuition even clearer.

Originality:
While random forests and attribute-based zero-shot learning are established, the explicit modeling of attribute reliability—as opposed to attribute strength—is a thoughtful and comparatively underexplored angle. Many prior methods may implicitly account for classifier noise, but the explicit incorporation of measured TPR/FPR into the split criterion is a novel and principled contribution.

Significance:
The methodological novelty is moderate, but the central idea is compelling and practically relevant for attribute-based recognition and zero-shot learning. By directly leveraging validation-derived reliability, the approach offers a simple and broadly applicable mechanism for improving robustness without redesigning the entire model. The work is likely to stimulate further research on calibrated attribute use, uncertainty-aware decision criteria, and more nuanced diagnostic analyses of attribute predictors.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide qualitative illustrations and per-attribute/per-class breakdowns to show where the gains come from.
- Include ablations comparing: (a) standard random forests, (b) forests using only attribute strength, and (c) the proposed reliability-aware variant.
- Report sensitivity to the train–validation split and to the number of validation samples used to estimate reliability.
- Consider comparisons with calibrated attribute probabilities (e.g., Platt scaling, isotonic regression) to disentangle benefits from calibration versus the proposed split modification.
- Report variance across multiple data splits and statistical significance of improvements.
- Discuss computational overhead.

Overall assessment:
A clear, well-executed paper with a simple yet effective idea. The contribution is not a major algorithmic advance, but the explicit treatment of attribute unreliability is novel in this context and yields meaningful improvements. With added qualitative analysis and clearer justification of parameter choices, the paper would be stronger and more convincing. I view it as a solid, interesting contribution to zero-shot learning and attribute-based recognition.
