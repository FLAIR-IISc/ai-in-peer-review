This paper introduces a random-forest approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly incorporates uncertainty in attribute classifier outputs. The authors estimate each attribute’s error characteristics (e.g., true/false positive rates) on a held-out validation set and integrate these ROC-derived statistics into the information gain used during tree construction. As a result, a class’s attribute signature is fractionally propagated along multiple branches according to expected classifier errors, enabling the model to better tolerate unreliable attribute predictions.

Quality: The empirical evidence is solid and includes relevant quantitative comparisons. However, the paper would benefit from more qualitative analysis (e.g., concrete examples of which attributes and categories are most affected or improved). In addition, some design choices are insufficiently justified; for example, the rationale for the 80/20 train–validation split is not clearly explained, nor is the selection process for other hyperparameters.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized, making the approach and results easy to follow.

Originality: Prior work often emphasizes “attribute strength,” whereas this paper focuses on the less explored dimension of “attribute reliability.” While some learning methods implicitly handle classifier noise, the explicit modeling of unreliability within the tree-building process appears novel.

Significance: Although random forests themselves are standard, the idea of leveraging measured attribute reliability from validation data to improve zero-shot recognition is interesting and likely to stimulate further research on attribute-based models. While neither the idea nor the method is radically new in isolation, their application to zero-shot learning is insightful and revealing.
