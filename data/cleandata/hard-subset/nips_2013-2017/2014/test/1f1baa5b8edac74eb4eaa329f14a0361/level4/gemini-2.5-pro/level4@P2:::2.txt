### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses a key practical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The central idea is to train a model that explicitly accounts for the known error tendencies of these classifiers. The authors propose a novel random forest approach where the training data consists of the attribute "signatures" of the unseen classes, rather than image examples. The key contribution lies in modifying the tree-building process. Instead of making hard splits, each signature is fractionally propagated down multiple paths in a decision tree. The proportion sent down each path is determined by the attribute classifier's performance statistics (e.g., true/false positive rates) measured on a held-out validation set of attribute-labeled images. This process effectively bakes in an expectation of classifier error into the learned model, leading it to favor splits on attributes that are not only discriminative but also reliably predictable. The framework is also extended to the few-shot learning setting and to handle uncertainty in the provided class-attribute signatures. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating significant improvements over the widely-used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) model and other baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a critical and practical bottleneck in zero-shot learning. The assumption that attribute predictions are perfect is unrealistic, and this work provides a direct and principled way to address the resulting performance degradation.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** While many algorithms implicitly handle noise, the explicit modeling of attribute unreliability during the training of the zero-shot classifier is a novel contribution. Using classifier performance statistics (ROC) from a validation set to guide the fractional propagation of class signatures within a random forest is a clever and well-conceived mechanism.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The proposed method consistently and significantly outperforms strong baselines, including DAP, across three challenging datasets. The controlled noise experiments are particularly effective at illustrating why and how the method works, clearly showing its robustness to varying levels of attribute noise. The ablation studies in Table 1 effectively isolate the contribution of the key components of the model.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind their method before delving into the technical details.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The evaluation is entirely quantitative. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a qualitative analysis that provides insight into the model's behavior. For example:
    *   Which specific attributes does the model learn are unreliable and therefore down-weights?
    *   Could the authors show an example of a few levels of a learned decision tree to illustrate which attributes are chosen for splits near the root?
    *   The paper notes large gains for specific categories like "giant panda". A brief analysis of why this might be (e.g., which of its key attributes are hard to detect, and how did the model overcome this?) would make the results more insightful.

2.  **Hyperparameter Justification:** Several hyperparameters are introduced (e.g., the 80%-20% train/validation split, tree depth, λ for the few-shot extension). While some are set via cross-validation, the choice of the validation set size seems particularly important to the method's success. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with the size of the validation set would increase confidence in the method's robustness and provide practical guidance for its application.

3.  **Attribute Dependencies:** The method selects splits based on one attribute at a time, which does not explicitly model correlations between attribute classifiers (an issue the authors acknowledge in the conclusion). While the recursive partitioning of the validation data partially addresses this, the core assumption of the split criterion is on a single attribute's reliability. A brief discussion of the potential limitations of this choice in the main body of the paper would be welcome.

**Detailed Comments**

The paper makes a clear distinction between "attribute strength" (how strongly an attribute is present) and "attribute reliability" (how well a classifier can detect it). This is an important distinction, and the focus on the latter is a key contribution.

The experiments are thorough. The comparison to an "upper bound" (OURS+TRUE ROC) is a nice touch, as it contextualizes the performance and shows the potential for further improvement if error rates on the unseen classes could be better estimated.

The extension to few-shot learning is natural and demonstrates the flexibility of the framework. It effectively shows how the attribute signatures can serve as a powerful prior when only a few labeled examples are available.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, sound, and effective solution to a significant problem in zero-shot learning. The strengths—a clear and important problem statement, a technically sound and creative solution, and strong empirical results—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to a lack of deeper qualitative analysis rather than any fundamental flaws in the method. The work is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to be influential for researchers working on attribute-based recognition. I recommend acceptance.
