This paper introduces a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning leveraging random forests. The approach first trains attribute classifiers using Support Vector Machines. A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors, with attribute reliability estimated at each node using a validation set of images with attribute labels. An extension to the few-shot learning setting is also presented.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, offering a strong summary of related work. The novelty of the contribution, while present, is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The central claim that this work fundamentally improves upon existing methods in handling unreliable attribute predictions is overstated. The baseline method, Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7], and similar approaches already incorporate mechanisms to address attribute uncertainty. Both DAP and this paper share a common framework: training attribute classifiers on image features, followed by training a second-stage estimator to predict class probabilities based on these attribute predictions. The key difference lies in *how* the reliability of the attribute classifiers is estimated – Platt scaling and probabilistic models in previous work [6], versus a novel random forest-based method here. 

Therefore, the contribution isn't a fundamentally new approach to handling attribute uncertainty, but rather a refined method for modeling attribute noise. Specifically, the proposed method offers two distinctions: employing an ROC-based reliability estimate instead of a sigmoidal model, and implicitly modeling joint statistics in attribute errors through the propagation of the validation set down the decision trees – effectively capturing attribute correlations. It would be beneficial to acknowledge this nuance and potentially cite relevant work exploring multi-attribute spaces and calibration techniques, such as Scheirer et al. (“Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search”). 

A potential weakness of this approach, particularly compared to methods treating attributes independently, is the reliance on a substantial validation set to generate reliable probability estimates as the data is subdivided during tree construction. This issue receives insufficient attention in the paper, and the proposed heuristic to mitigate it (line 323) lacks thorough justification. Further experimentation evaluating the impact of training and validation set size on performance would strengthen the analysis.

Overall, the paper demonstrates good quality with sensible technical choices and clear descriptions. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the claims made, though the quantitative improvements are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance based on empirical results alone. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the rest of the paper; while the core work is detailed, this section is comparatively brief. The proposed method of combining information gains (Eq. 6) seems less intuitive than an approach that directly integrates class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when calculating probabilities p(a|...).

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper with sound technical foundations and adequate experimental validation. However, the central argument regarding a novel approach to handling unreliable attributes is not entirely convincing.
