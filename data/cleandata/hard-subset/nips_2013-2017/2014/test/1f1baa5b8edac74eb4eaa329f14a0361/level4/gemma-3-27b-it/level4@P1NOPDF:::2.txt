This paper proposes a novel random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that more effectively handles the uncertainty inherent in attribute classifier predictions. The authors measure this uncertainty by evaluating the performance (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. They then propose incorporating this performance information into the information gain computation during random forest learning, allowing the algorithm to account for attribute prediction uncertainty when pursuing a given category-attribute signature along multiple paths of a decision tree.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper would benefit from qualitative analysis. For example, it would be insightful to see which types of attributes and categories are most significantly affected by – or improved by – this method. Furthermore, the justification for several parameter choices is unclear. Specifically, the rationale behind the 80%-20% training-validation split should be explained.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

Originality: While prior work has largely focused on the problem of “attribute strength,” this paper claims to address the less explored issue of “attribute reliability.” Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit handling of it.

Significance: Although the proposed method leverages existing techniques (random forests), the core idea – modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning – is interesting and thought-provoking for researchers working with attribute-based learning. While the idea and method presented are not groundbreakingly novel, their application to improving zero-shot learning is revealing and potentially impactful.
