This paper presents a random forest-based method for zero-shot learning designed to better accommodate uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. This uncertainty is quantified by measuring the performance (e.g., true positive rate, false positive rate) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. The authors then incorporate this performance information into the information gain computation during the training of the random forest. This modification allows the model to account for attribute unreliability, for example, by pursuing a given category-attribute signature along multiple paths within a decision tree.

**Quality:** The proposed ideas are well-supported by quantitative empirical analysis. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis. For instance, an analysis of which attributes and categories are most affected or improved by this method would have been insightful. Furthermore, the justification for several parameter choices is unclear, such as the selection of the 80%-20% split for the training and validation sets.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to read.

**Originality:** While most previous work has addressed the problem of "attribute strength," this paper focuses on the less-explored problem of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel for its explicit handling of the issue.

**Significance:** While the core method (random forests) is not new, the central idea of modeling attribute unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data is an interesting and thought-provoking contribution for researchers working on attribute-based learning and zero-shot recognition.
