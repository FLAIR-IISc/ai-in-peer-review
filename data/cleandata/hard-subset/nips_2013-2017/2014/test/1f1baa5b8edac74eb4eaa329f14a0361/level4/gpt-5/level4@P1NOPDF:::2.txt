This paper proposes a random-forest-based approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for uncertainty in attribute classifier outputs. Uncertainty is quantified using performance metrics (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) measured on a held-out validation set. These reliability estimates are incorporated into the information gain computation during tree construction, allowing a category–attribute signature to propagate along multiple branches when attribute predictions are uncertain. This design aims to better accommodate noisy attribute predictions during learning.

Quality: The empirical evaluation is solid and supports the main claims with relevant quantitative results. However, the paper lacks qualitative analysis. For example, it would be helpful to see which types of attributes (e.g., global vs. local, visual vs. semantic) and which categories benefit most from the proposed method, as well as cases where it fails. In addition, the methodology would be clearer with more detail on parameter selection and sensitivity. It is not explained why an 80/20 training–validation split was chosen, whether alternative splits were considered, or how other hyperparameters were tuned. Some ablation or sensitivity analysis would strengthen the conclusions.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The motivation for modeling attribute unreliability is clear, and the integration into the forest training procedure is described coherently.

Originality: Prior work has largely focused on modeling “attribute strength,” whereas this paper emphasizes “attribute reliability.” While many learning methods may implicitly account for classifier unreliability, the explicit incorporation of reliability estimates into the splitting criterion of a random forest is, to my knowledge, novel in this zero-shot setting.

Significance: Although the base model (random forests) is standard, the idea of using validation-based reliability estimates to guide tree growth for zero-shot learning is interesting and likely to stimulate further work on attribute modeling. The approach offers a practical and intuitive way to exploit attribute performance statistics, and the reported gains suggest it is a useful direction. That said, the methodological novelty is incremental, and the contribution would be strengthened by deeper analysis of when and why the method helps, clearer justification of design choices (including the data split), and discussion of limitations.

Overall: A well-executed and clearly presented paper with a thoughtful idea—explicitly modeling attribute unreliability—to improve zero-shot learning. The contribution is more in the application and insight than in algorithmic novelty, but the results and framing are compelling. Additional qualitative examples and parameter justification would make the paper more complete.
