The paper aims to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. In theory, novel classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors; in practice, however, learning these attribute classifiers can be as difficult as, or even more difficult than, learning the object classes themselves. The authors train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and the training procedure explicitly accounts for the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They also show how to extend the method to incorporate a few training examples of the test categories. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors address a fundamental zero-shot learning problem with a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound. Results on three standard datasets are strong. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including ablation studies, the injection of artificial noise, comparisons between zero- and few-shot settings, and comprehensive baselines from the literature.

CLARITY: The paper is very well written. One point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: when the threshold t is introduced, the attribute signatures are still binary, so any t with 0 < t < 1 leaves equation (2) unchanged. Consequently, it is unclear in lines 207–212 how a novel test example would be propagated down the tree, since t appears ill-defined at that stage. This issue is clarified in later sections.

ORIGINALITY: This is a solid and well-motivated application of a random forest framework to an important problem.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. It is strong, interesting, and sound. The results on three datasets, across both zero- and few-shot settings, are convincing. The paper deserves to be published.
