

This paper addresses a critical challenge in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. While zero-shot learning theoretically enables recognition of novel classes through attribute descriptions (e.g., "zebra = striped, black and white"), practical implementation suffers because attribute classifiers themselves are often imperfect. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability during training, using receiver operating characteristic (ROC) statistics to guide tree construction. The method handles both pure zero-shot scenarios and few-shot extensions where limited category-labeled examples are available. Experiments on three standard datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in challenging scenarios with unreliable attribute classifiers.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong theoretical grounding. The authors carefully address a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning that has been overlooked in previous work. Their method is well-motivated and properly accounts for the uncertainty inherent in attribute prediction systems. The experimental design is thorough, including ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, and comparisons across multiple datasets. The results consistently demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods (DAP and SIGNATURE RF), with particularly strong gains on datasets where attribute classifiers are less reliable (SUN and aPY). The method's ability to outperform DAP despite using only 80% of the attribute-labeled data for training demonstrates that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than simply having more training data. The few-shot extension is logically developed and shows clear benefits when combining attribute signatures with limited labeled examples.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though some technical details in Section 3.2.1 could be clarified. The explanation of how thresholds function with binary attribute signatures (where any t between 0 and 1 would be equivalent) is initially confusing but is appropriately resolved in later sections. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific parameter choices and experimental setup. The presentation of results is clear and well-organized, with Figure 1 effectively demonstrating the method's robustness to attribute prediction errors. A minor suggestion would be to include a more detailed schematic of the training procedure in the main paper (currently only in supplementary material), which would help readers visualize the key innovation of propagating validation data through the tree structure.

**Originality**: This work makes a significant contribution by recognizing and addressing the critical issue of attribute prediction unreliability in zero-shot learning. While previous methods have treated attribute predictions as perfect, the authors demonstrate that this assumption is problematic and develop a principled approach to account for it. The random forest framework is well-suited to this problem, and the authors' adaptation of ROC statistics to guide tree construction represents a novel technical contribution. The work also extends naturally to few-shot learning, further broadening its applicability. This is a meaningful advance over the standard zero-shot paradigm, as it makes the approach more practical while maintaining the elegant theoretical foundation.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning that has hindered its practical adoption. By demonstrating that reliable zero-shot recognition is possible even when attribute classifiers are imperfect, the authors show that this approach can be valuable for real-world applications involving rare categories or dynamically defined classes. The consistent improvements across three diverse datasets (animals, objects, and scenes) suggest broad applicability. The method's ability to work with both hand-designed and discovered attributes further enhances its significance. The few-shot extension is particularly valuable for practical applications where some labeled examples might be available.

The paper is strong in all four criteria. It makes a clear technical contribution to a significant problem in computer vision, with thorough evaluation and well-executed experiments. The writing is generally clear, with minor room for improvement in explaining the threshold parameter. The approach is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates practical improvements over existing methods.

The primary weakness is the limited discussion of why attribute classifiers are unreliable in the first place, which would provide stronger context for the problem. Additionally, while the paper mentions the method's applicability beyond visual recognition, it would be strengthened by a brief discussion of potential limitations or challenges in applying this approach to other domains.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances zero-shot learning by addressing a critical practical limitation. The method is elegant, well-validated, and represents a significant step toward making zero-shot recognition a practical tool for real-world applications. The paper deserves publication at NIPS.
