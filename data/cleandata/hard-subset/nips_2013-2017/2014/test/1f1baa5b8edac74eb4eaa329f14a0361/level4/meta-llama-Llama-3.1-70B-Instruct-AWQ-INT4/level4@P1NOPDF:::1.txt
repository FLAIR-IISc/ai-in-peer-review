The paper aims to bridge the gap between the theoretical foundations and practical applications of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The underlying theory posits that novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors; however, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging or even more so than learning the object classes themselves.

The authors employ random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and their training procedure takes into account the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They also demonstrate how the method can be extended to handle training with a few training examples of test categories. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors provide a elegant and theoretically sound solution to this fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The model is well-motivated, and the results on three standard datasets are strong. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies, introduce artificial noise, evaluate several settings (zero-shot versus few-shot learning), and compare their approach with existing literature.

CLARITY: The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. However, there is a minor point of confusion in Section 3.2.1, where the threshold t is introduced. Since the attribute signatures are still binary, the value of t can be any number between 0 and 1 without affecting equation (2). Furthermore, it is unclear in lines 207-212 how a novel test example can be propagated down the tree, as the definition of t seems ambiguous. Fortunately, this confusion is clarified in later sections.

ORIGINALITY: This work represents a nice application of a random forest framework to an important problem in zero-shot learning.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, and its contributions are substantial. The paper is strong, interesting, and sound, with convincing results on three datasets across various settings. Overall, the paper deserves to be published.
