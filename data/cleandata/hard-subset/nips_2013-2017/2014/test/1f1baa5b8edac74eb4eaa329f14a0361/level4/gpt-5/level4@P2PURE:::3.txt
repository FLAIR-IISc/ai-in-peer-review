This paper proposes an attribute-based zero-shot learning method built on random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class–attribute signature vectors, and a validation set of attribute-labeled images is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node. An extension to few-shot learning is also presented.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well written, with a solid summary of related work.

Originality: The novelty is moderate—perhaps not high, but not too low for acceptance. The claim that the method uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions whereas earlier work does not feels overstated. For example, Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7]—the baseline used here and in many other papers—also accommodates attribute uncertainty. Both follow the same high-level pipeline:
a) Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical in both).
b) Learn a second-stage class probability estimator over attribute predictions, using a validation set to calibrate the reliability of attribute classifiers. In [6], this is done via Platt scaling and a probabilistic combination of attributes; here, it is done via a new random-forest-based approach.
Thus, the proposed method is not a fundamentally new way to handle attribute uncertainty. Its main differences are that it uses a richer model of attribute noise: (1) ROC-based calibration rather than a sigmoidal/Platt model, and (2) unlike [6], which treats attributes as independent, it effectively models joint statistics of attribute errors because the validation set is propagated down the trees. Framed this way, it seems more accurate to present the contribution as improved modeling of attribute correlations. I am not an expert in this subarea, but Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” may be a related citation.

A potential weakness relative to methods that treat attributes independently is the need for a large validation set: as examples are recursively partitioned during tree growth, reliable probability estimates may require substantial data. This issue is mostly glossed over, and the mitigation proposed on line 323 appears heuristic. It would be helpful to include experiments that vary the sizes of the training and validation sets.

Quality: Overall, the paper is of good quality. The technical choices are sensible and clearly described. The experiments are appropriate and support the paper’s claims, though the quantitative improvements are not strong enough to warrant acceptance based solely on empirical gains. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest: while the main method is explained thoroughly, this section is quite brief. Intuitively, a method that combines class–attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when computing p(a|...) might make more sense than a weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6). In sum, the paper is well written, technically sound, and empirically adequate, but the central selling point—that it offers a new way to handle unreliable attributes—remains somewhat questionable.
