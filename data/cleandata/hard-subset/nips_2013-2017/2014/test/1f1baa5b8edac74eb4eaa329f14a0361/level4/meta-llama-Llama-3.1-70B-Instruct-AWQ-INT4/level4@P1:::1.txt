The paper strives to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theory is that novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors; in practice, however, learning these attribute classifiers can be as difficult or even more so than learning the object classes themselves. The authors propose a novel approach to train zero-shot models that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions, leveraging statistics about each attribute's error tendencies to obtain more robust discriminative models for the unseen classes.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the need for a more robust approach. The proposed method, which uses a random forest framework to train zero-shot models with attribute signatures, is elegant and theoretically sound. The authors also provide a clear explanation of how the method can be extended to handle training with a few training examples of test categories.

The experimental results are strong, with the proposed method achieving state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets. The authors also perform ablation studies, introduce artificial noise, evaluate several settings (zero- versus few-shot learning), and compare with the literature, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

QUALITY: The authors do a really nice job of handling this fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound. Results on 3 standard datasets are strong. The authors do a nice job of performing ablation studies, introducing artificial noise, evaluating several setting (zero- versus few-shot learning), and comparing with the literature.

CLARITY: The paper is very well written. One minor point of confusion is in Section 3.2.1, where the threshold t is introduced. As the attribute signatures are still binary, t can be any value 0 < t < 1 without changing anything in equation (2). However, this is clarified in later sections.

ORIGINALITY: This is a nice application of a random forest framework to an important problem. While the idea of using random forests for zero-shot learning is not new, the authors' approach to handling attribute unreliability is novel and effective.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, providing a robust and effective solution that can be applied to a wide range of applications.

Overall, the paper is strong, interesting, and sound. The results on 3 datasets in a variety of settings are convincing, and the paper deserves to be published.

* The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain the limitations of existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
