- Proposes a novel attribute-based zero-shot method that trains random forests directly on class attribute signatures instead of images.  
- Accounts for attribute classifier unreliability by guiding tree splits with ROC statistics estimated on a held-out validation set.  
- Propagates the validation set down the tree during training to capture conditional (joint) error statistics for more robust split selection.  
- Includes extensions to handle uncertainty in class signatures and to incorporate a few labeled examples for few-shot learning.  
- Fits within the two-stage zero-shot paradigm, offering a new, robustness-focused approach for the second stage compared to DAP.  
- Strength: tackles the important problem of poor attribute classifier performance with a clear, well-motivated solution.  
- Strength: technically clever integration of ROC-based reliability into random forest training, especially the conditional propagation idea.  
- Strength: paper is very well written, organized, and positions related work effectively.  
- Strength: thorough experiments, including controlled noise simulations and evaluations on three standard datasets, showing consistent improvement over DAP.  
- Weakness: novelty/positioning overclaimed — prior work (e.g., DAP variants) also models attribute uncertainty; the real novelty is modeling joint attribute error statistics via propagation.  
- Suggestion: better position contribution and cite related work on attribute correlations (e.g., Scheirer et al.).  
- Weakness: potential practical issue with validation data requirements — ROC estimates may be unreliable in deep nodes; fallback heuristic needs more analysis.  
- Suggestion: include experiments showing sensitivity to validation set size and behavior of the fallback strategy.  
- Weakness: few-shot extension is underdeveloped; linear combination objective is weakly motivated versus a more integrated probabilistic approach.  
- Weakness: empirical gains over DAP are moderate; significance rests more on methodological elegance than major performance breakthroughs.
