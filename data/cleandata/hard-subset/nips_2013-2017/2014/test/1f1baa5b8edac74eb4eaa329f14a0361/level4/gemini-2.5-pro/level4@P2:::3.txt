This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning that is designed to be robust to the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The core of the approach is a novel random forest model. Unlike traditional random forests that are trained on data instances, this model is trained directly on the attribute "signatures" that define the unseen classes. The key innovation is a process where a validation set of attribute-labeled images is used during tree construction to estimate the reliability (specifically, the ROC characteristics) of each attribute classifier at each node. These reliability statistics are then used to fractionally propagate the class signatures down the tree, effectively building a model that anticipates and accounts for the expected prediction errors of the attribute classifiers. The authors also propose an extension for the few-shot learning setting.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation is well-established, the proposed method is described in sufficient detail, and the connection to related work is thoroughly explained. The overall organization is logical and easy to follow.

**Originality**
The paper's main contribution is a novel method for incorporating attribute classifier uncertainty into a zero-shot model. The authors frame this as a key distinction from prior work. However, this characterization could be more nuanced. For example, the Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method, used as the primary baseline, also models uncertainty by learning a probabilistic mapping from continuous attribute scores to class probabilities.

The key novelty of this work lies in *how* it models this uncertainty. Instead of using a simple sigmoidal confidence model for each attribute independently, the proposed approach has two distinct advantages: 1) it uses a more detailed noise model based on the full ROC curve, which allows it to select optimal operating points (thresholds) for each decision; and 2) by propagating the validation data down the tree, it implicitly models dependencies and joint statistics in attribute prediction errors. A more precise framing of the contribution would be as a superior way to model attribute correlations and noise, rather than being the first to handle uncertainty at all. In this context, related work such as Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search" might be relevant to cite.

**Quality**
The technical quality of the paper is generally high. The random forest formulation is well-reasoned, and the experimental evaluation is appropriate, showing consistent improvements over a strong baseline on three standard datasets. The claims are well-supported by the results.

There are, however, a few points that could be strengthened:

1.  **Validation Set Size:** A potential concern is the statistical reliability of the ROC estimates computed at deeper nodes in the tree. As the validation set is recursively subdivided, the number of samples available to estimate the true positive and false positive rates can become very small, leading to noisy estimates. The paper acknowledges this and proposes a heuristic solution (reverting to the global validation set when the local set is too small), but the paper would be stronger with a more detailed analysis of this issue. For instance, an experiment evaluating the model's sensitivity to the initial size of the validation set would be very informative.

2.  **Few-Shot Extension:** The extension to few-shot learning in Section 3.3 feels less developed than the core zero-shot contribution. The section is quite brief, and the proposed method of taking a weighted average of two information gain terms (Eq. 6) is presented with limited justification. It is not immediately obvious why this is the optimal way to combine a descriptive prior with empirical data. A more detailed explanation of the intuition or a comparison to alternative, perhaps more probabilistically grounded, formulations would improve this part of the paper.

**Significance**
The paper addresses an important and practical limitation of attribute-based recognition systems. By providing a method to make zero-shot models more robust to the inevitable errors of their underlying attribute classifiers, this work could increase the viability of such approaches in real-world applications. The consistent empirical improvements over the popular DAP model demonstrate the value of the proposed technique. The ideas presented are a solid contribution to the field and could be built upon by other researchers working on zero-shot learning or other problems involving learning from noisy, intermediate representations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known and significant problem in zero-shot learning.
*   The core technical idea of using ROC-guided fractional propagation to train the random forest is novel and clever.
*   The paper is very clearly written and easy to understand.
*   Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over a strong and widely-used baseline (DAP) on multiple challenging datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The framing of the novelty relative to prior work like DAP could be more precise.
*   A potential weakness regarding the statistical validity of ROC estimates on small, partitioned validation sets is not fully explored.
*   The few-shot learning extension is somewhat underdeveloped and briefly explained.

Overall, this is a good paper with a solid contribution. While there are some areas that could be improved, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.
