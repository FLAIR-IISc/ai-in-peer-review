- Proposes a random-forest zero-shot method that trains trees on class-attribute signature vectors and uses held-out validation data to estimate node-specific attribute ROC for split selection.  
- Propagates both signatures and validation samples fractionally down trees to choose splits that maximize information gain under expected TPR/FPR, improving robustness to noisy attribute predictions.  
- Trains attribute classifiers (SVMs with Platt scaling) separately and extends the approach to handle signature uncertainty and discovered (pseudo-)attributes.  
- Introduces a few-shot extension that mixes signature-based and data-based information gains via a weighting λ (Eq. 6).  
- Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with controlled-noise studies demonstrating robustness benefits.  
- Technical execution is sound, well-motivated, and clearly described; ablations and controlled-noise experiments convincingly support claims.  
- Per-node ROC estimation and fractional propagation effectively capture attribute error dependencies that DAP’s independence assumption misses.  
- The manuscript is clear, well-organized, and situates the work appropriately among prior zero-shot and decision-tree literature.  
- Contribution is incremental but meaningful; framing should avoid overstating novelty relative to DAP, which already models attribute uncertainty via calibrated probabilities.  
- Key novelty is ROC-based operating-point selection and dependency-aware split selection, not a fundamentally new uncertainty modeling paradigm.  
- Practical concern: per-node validation counts shrink with tree depth, making node-specific ROC estimates noisy; fallback to global ROC is heuristic.  
- Suggests more systematic treatments (e.g., Bayesian smoothing or sharing statistics across nodes) and empirical sensitivity analysis of validation set size vs. training.  
- Few-shot extension (Eq. 6) is underdeveloped and feels less principled; recommend deriving/justifying it or comparing to probabilistic fusion baselines that combine p(a|x) and p(a|class).  
- Overall: solid, well-executed refinement that improves robustness to unreliable attributes, but needs tempered novelty claims and deeper analysis of data requirements and few-shot fusion.
