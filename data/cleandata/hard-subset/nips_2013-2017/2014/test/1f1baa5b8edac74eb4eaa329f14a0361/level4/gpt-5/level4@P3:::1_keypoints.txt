- Proposes a random-forest method that models attribute unreliability using node-specific ROC statistics to fractionally propagate class signatures during training.  
- Trains one-vs-all forests on class attribute signatures and uses validation attribute-labeled data to estimate TPR/FPR per attribute and threshold.  
- Splits are chosen by expected information gain under estimated attribute error, favoring learnable and discriminative attributes/thresholds.  
- Incorporates signature uncertainty and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and image-based information gain with weight λ.  
- Fits within two-stage attribute-based zero-shot paradigms but is novel in modeling attribute error tendencies rather than trusting attribute predictions.  
- Relates to methods for missing/uncertain features in trees but is adapted specifically to training on signatures and testing on attribute predictions.  
- Empirically evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN with controlled noise studies, ablations, discovered attributes, and a TRUE ROC upper bound.  
- Results show consistent improvements over DAP and baselines, especially when attributes are noisy.  
- Method is soundly motivated and clearly formulated; fractional propagation via node-specific ROC is principled.  
- Experiments are comprehensive and include sanity checks and ablations isolating key components.  
- Reproducibility is supported by provided implementation details (trees, depth, validation protocol, ROC estimation, scarce-node handling).  
- Writing is clear and well organized, though Sec. 3.2.1 should foreshadow the practical role of thresholds and move leaf-posterior details into the main text.  
- Original application of node-specific ROC-guided fractional propagation to zero-shot learning is novel despite related prior techniques.  
- Significance: addresses a practical limitation of attribute-based ZSL, maintains human-specifiable class definitions, and offers a pragmatic few-shot extension.  
- Strengths: principled modeling of attribute unreliability, robust expected-IG objective, comprehensive experiments, and interpretability.  
- Weaknesses: reliance on seen-class validation for ROC estimation risks domain shift to unseen classes.  
- Weaknesses: method treats attributes independently; correlated attributes may cause redundant or misleading splits.  
- Weaknesses: lacking discussion/measurements of computational cost, training/testing time, and scaling with M, K, and tree depth.  
- Weaknesses: sensitivity to calibration of attribute scores (e.g., Platt scaling) needs clarification to avoid leakage.  
- Weaknesses: guidance for choosing few-shot weight λ and cross-validation protocol is insufficient, especially under class imbalance.  
- Weaknesses: would benefit from comparisons to label/semantic embedding zero-shot methods to contextualize performance.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled, effective solution to attribute unreliability with strong empirical gains and practical adoption potential.  
- Cons for acceptance: dependence on validation data for ROC estimation, limited scalability discussion, and incomplete treatment of attribute correlations.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — a clear, technically sound, and influential contribution to attribute-based zero/few-shot learning.
