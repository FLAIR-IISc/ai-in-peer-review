The paper aims to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theoretical premise is that novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors; however, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging or even more so than learning the object classes themselves.

The authors employ random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and the training procedure takes into account the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree at training time. Furthermore, they demonstrate how the method can be extended to handle training with a few training examples of test categories. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors provide an elegant and theoretically sound solution to the fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The results on three standard datasets are strong, and the authors conduct thorough ablation studies, introducing artificial noise, evaluating several settings (zero- versus few-shot learning), and comparing with existing literature.

CLARITY: The paper is well-written, with one minor point of confusion in Section 3.2.1. The introduction of the threshold t seems unnecessary, as the attribute signatures remain binary, allowing t to take any value between 0 and 1 without affecting equation (2). Additionally, the explanation of how a novel test example is propagated down the tree in lines 207-212 is unclear, as the definition of t appears to be ambiguous. However, this is clarified in later sections.

ORIGINALITY: This work represents a nice application of a random forest framework to an important problem, offering a unique perspective on attribute-based zero-shot learning.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, providing a significant contribution to the field. The paper is strong, interesting, and sound, with convincing results on three datasets in various settings. Overall, the paper deserves to be published.
