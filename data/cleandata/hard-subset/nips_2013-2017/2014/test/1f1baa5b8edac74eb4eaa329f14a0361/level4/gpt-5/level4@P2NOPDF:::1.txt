This paper aims to close the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. In theory, new classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors; in practice, training reliable attribute classifiers can be as hard as, or harder than, training object classifiers themselves. The authors train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors and account for attribute detector reliability by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They also extend the approach to a few-shot scenario with a small number of labeled examples for the target categories. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The paper provides a strong and elegant random-forest-based solution to a fundamental zero-shot learning problem. The model is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. Results on three standard datasets are compelling. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including ablation studies, robustness to synthetic noise, comparisons with prior work, and assessments of both zero-shot and few-shot settings.

CLARITY: The paper is clearly written overall. One confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: when introducing the threshold t, the attribute signatures are still binary, so any 0 < t < 1 leaves equation (2) unchanged. Consequently, lines 207–212 make it unclear how a novel test example is propagated down the tree, since t appears ill-defined at that point. This issue is clarified in later sections.

ORIGINALITY: The work is a thoughtful and principled application of random forests to zero-shot learning, with a careful treatment of attribute unreliability.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a central challenge in zero-shot learning. It is strong, interesting, and sound, with convincing results across three datasets and multiple settings. The paper merits publication.
