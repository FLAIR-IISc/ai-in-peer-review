The paper addresses a key challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: bridging the gap between theoretical promise and practical implementation. While the theory suggests novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, learning these predictors can be as challenging as learning the object classes themselves. The authors propose a method that trains random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating the reliability of attribute detectors during training by propagating a validation set through each decision tree. They further demonstrate extensions to few-shot learning scenarios. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple attribute datasets.

QUALITY: This work presents a strong and elegant solution to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, effectively leveraging a random forest framework. The model is theoretically sound and supported by strong results on three standard datasets. The authors perform a thorough evaluation, including insightful ablation studies, the introduction of artificial noise, analysis of zero- versus few-shot learning settings, and comprehensive comparisons to existing literature. 

CLARITY: The paper is exceptionally well-written. However, Section 3.2.1 introduces a minor point of confusion: given that the attribute signatures remain binary, the threshold *t* appears arbitrary, as any value between 0 and 1 would yield the same result in equation (2). Consequently, the method for propagating a novel test example down the tree (lines 207-212) is initially unclear, as *t* appears undefined. This is clarified in subsequent sections.

ORIGINALITY: This work constitutes a valuable application of a random forest framework to a significant problem in zero-shot learning.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a central problem within zero-shot learning. It is a strong, interesting, and technically sound contribution. The convincing results across three datasets and a variety of settings support the paper’s claims, and I recommend it for publication.
