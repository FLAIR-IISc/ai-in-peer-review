Summary
The paper proposes an attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL) approach that combines (i) SVM-trained attribute classifiers with (ii) a random forest trained on class–attribute signature vectors. A key idea is to use an attribute-labeled validation set to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree, thereby influencing splits and downstream decisions. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed by interpolating information gains from class-attribute priors and image-level attribute predictions.

Clarity and Presentation
The paper is clearly written and well organized, with a thorough and accurate discussion of related work. Technical descriptions are generally easy to follow. The few-shot section (Sec. 3.3), however, is notably brief relative to the rest of the paper and could use more explanation and justification.

Originality and Relation to Prior Work
The claimed novelty—robust handling of unreliable attribute predictions—seems somewhat overstated. Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7], used as a baseline here and widely adopted in the literature, also accounts for attribute uncertainty by calibrating attribute outputs and combining them probabilistically (e.g., Platt scaling in [6]). Conceptually, both approaches:
- Train attribute classifiers on image features.
- Learn a second-stage class probability estimator using a validation set to assess and calibrate attribute reliability.

What is new and valuable in this paper is not the high-level idea of handling unreliable attributes, but the sophistication of the model used to do so:
- Use of ROC-based calibration rather than a fixed sigmoidal model (Platt scaling).
- Effective modeling of joint dependencies in attribute errors by propagating validation examples down the trees, relaxing the independence assumptions made in [6].

Framing the contribution as improved modeling of correlated attribute noise would be more accurate and compelling. It may also be helpful to cite related work on attribute calibration and fusion, e.g., Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search.”

Quality and Technical Soundness
The approach is technically sensible and well grounded. The random forest framework creates a flexible way to incorporate node-specific reliability estimates and to capture interactions among attributes. Empirically, the experimental setup is appropriate and generally supports the claims; however, the quantitative gains, while consistent, are not so large that they alone justify acceptance without stronger analysis.

A potential weakness relative to methods that assume attribute independence is data efficiency. Because reliability is estimated at each node, the validation set is repeatedly partitioned, raising concerns about variance and the need for a large validation pool to obtain stable estimates. The paper briefly mentions a heuristic to address sparsity (line 323), but this is underexplained and not evaluated. A more thorough study of sample complexity and regularization at the node level would strengthen the paper.

Few-Shot Extension
The few-shot extension feels underdeveloped. The proposed interpolation of information gains (Eq. 6) is intuitive but ad hoc. A more principled alternative would combine class–attribute priors with image-level attribute evidence at the probability level, e.g., via calibrated likelihoods p(a|x) and priors p(a|class) to obtain class posteriors, or through a Bayesian update that smoothly transitions from prior-driven to data-driven as few-shot labels accumulate.

Empirical Evaluation
The current experiments are reasonable, but the following analyses would substantially improve the paper:
- Sample efficiency: Vary the size of the training and validation sets; report how performance and calibration quality change with tree depth and minimum samples per node/leaf.
- Ablations: Compare ROC-based calibration to Platt scaling within your framework; evaluate independent vs. joint modeling (e.g., by shuffling attribute co-occurrences in the validation set).
- Sensitivity: Analyze performance as a function of attribute classifier quality, number of attributes, and number of seen classes; include calibration plots and reliability diagrams.
- Regularization: Detail and test smoothing/back-off strategies for nodes with few validation samples; consider out-of-bag estimates to reduce overfitting.
- Few-shot: Provide a deeper comparison to probabilistic integration of priors and evidence; vary the number of labeled images per novel class and report the transition from zero- to few-shot.

Minor Comments
- Clarify how tree hyperparameters are selected (number/depth of trees, min samples per node, stopping criteria).
- Report computational cost relative to DAP.
- Ensure that calibration uses strictly held-out data to avoid leakage.
- Consider statistical tests where appropriate, and provide code or detailed implementation notes to aid reproducibility.

Overall Assessment
This is a clear, well-executed paper with a sensible and practically useful method. The main conceptual contribution lies in better modeling of attribute correlations and node-specific reliability, rather than in introducing a fundamentally new mechanism for handling attribute uncertainty. The empirical improvements are consistent but modest, and the claims should be toned down accordingly. With a more rigorous analysis of validation-set efficiency, clearer regularization strategies, and a firmer probabilistic treatment of the few-shot extension, the paper would be notably stronger. As it stands, I view it as a borderline submission: technically sound and well presented, with modest novelty and impact.
