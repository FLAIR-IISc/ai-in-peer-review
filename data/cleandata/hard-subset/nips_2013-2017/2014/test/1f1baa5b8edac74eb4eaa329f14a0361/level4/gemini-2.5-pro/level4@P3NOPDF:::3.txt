### Summary

This paper introduces a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL) that leverages random forests to handle unreliable attribute predictions. The proposed two-stage approach first trains individual attribute classifiers using SVMs. Subsequently, a random forest is trained on the class-attribute signature vectors. A key aspect of the method is the use of a validation set of images, with known attribute labels, to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the trees. The authors also present an extension of their method to the few-shot learning scenario.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and does an excellent job of situating the work within the relevant literature. The technical descriptions are sound, and the design choices are generally well-justified. However, I have several concerns regarding the novelty, methodological assumptions, and the completeness of the experimental analysis.

**1. Novelty and Contribution Framing**

The paper's central claim—that it presents a novel approach for handling unreliable attribute predictions where previous work has not—could be positioned more accurately. The proposed method shares a fundamental structure with established techniques like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7], which is used as a baseline. Both approaches follow a similar two-stage paradigm:

a) Train a set of attribute classifiers on image features.
b) Train a second-stage classifier to predict class probabilities from the outputs of the attribute classifiers, using a validation set to model their reliability.

The primary distinction lies in *how* this reliability is modeled. Whereas DAP [6] uses a probabilistic model with Platt scaling and assumes attribute independence, the proposed method uses a random forest with an ROC-based reliability estimate at each node. By propagating the validation set through the trees, the method implicitly captures dependencies and joint error statistics among attributes.

Therefore, the proposed method's true novelty seems to be its ability to model the *joint statistics of attribute errors*, as opposed to treating them independently. It would be more accurate to frame the contribution as a more sophisticated model for attribute correlations and noise, rather than as a fundamentally new way of handling attribute uncertainty altogether. In this context, citing and discussing related work on multi-attribute spaces, such as Scheirer et al. ("Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search"), could strengthen the paper's positioning.

**2. Methodological Concerns and Experimental Validation**

A potential weakness of the proposed approach, compared to methods that model attributes independently, is its reliance on a sufficiently large validation set. As the validation set is progressively subdivided when propagated down the decision trees, the reliability estimates at deeper nodes may be based on very few samples, leading to poor generalization. This important issue is not deeply explored in the paper, and the proposed solution for handling small sample sizes (line 323) appears heuristic. The analysis would be far more convincing with an ablation study evaluating the method's sensitivity to the size of both the training and validation sets.

**3. Empirical Performance**

While the experiments are appropriate and demonstrate an improvement over baselines, the performance gains are modest. The empirical results, on their own, do not make an overwhelmingly strong case for the method's superiority, which places more weight on the novelty of the technical contribution.

**4. Few-Shot Learning Extension**

The extension to few-shot learning in Section 3.3 feels less developed than the core ZSL method. The section is quite brief and lacks the detailed explanation provided elsewhere in the paper. Furthermore, the proposed weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6) is not fully motivated. An alternative approach, such as one that formally combines class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates to compute probabilities like `p(a|...)`, might be more intuitive and principled.

### Conclusion

In summary, this is a well-written paper presenting a technically sound approach to zero-shot learning. The main strengths are its clarity and the interesting idea of using random forests to model attribute correlations. However, the paper's primary weakness is the framing of its novelty, which appears to be an incremental, albeit sophisticated, improvement over existing paradigms for handling attribute uncertainty rather than a fundamentally new one. This, combined with modest empirical gains and an underdeveloped few-shot learning extension, tempers the overall contribution. Addressing the concerns about the validation set dependency and re-framing the core contribution would significantly strengthen the paper.
