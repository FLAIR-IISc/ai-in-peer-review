This paper tackles the gap between the ideal of attribute-based zero-shot learning and its practical limitations. In principle, new classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors and class-level attribute signatures. In practice, however, learning reliable attribute classifiers can be as hard—or harder—than learning object categories themselves. The authors propose a random forest framework trained on attribute signatures that explicitly models attribute unreliability by propagating a validation set through the trees during training to account for each attribute classifier’s error tendencies (via ROC statistics). They also extend the approach to a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven splits with limited class-labeled images. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple standard attribute datasets.

QUALITY: This is a thoughtful and well-executed approach to a core problem in zero-shot learning. The idea to incorporate attribute unreliability directly into the training of decision trees is elegant and well-justified. The methodology is sound, and the experimental evaluation is thorough: results on three standard datasets are strong; the ablation studies are informative; the controlled noise experiments clearly demonstrate robustness to attribute errors; and the comparisons to prior work are appropriate. The few-shot extension is natural and empirically effective.

CLARITY: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. One point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: when the threshold t is introduced, the attribute signatures are still binary, so any 0 < t < 1 yields the same split, making t effectively moot in equation (2). As a result, it is unclear in lines 207–212 how a novel test example will be propagated down the tree, since t seems ill-defined at that stage. This issue is clarified in the later sections when attribute unreliability and validation-based operating points are incorporated.

ORIGINALITY: The contribution is a novel and effective application of random forests to zero-shot learning that directly models attribute unreliability during training. While random forests and attributes are established tools, the way ROC-based uncertainty is integrated into the split selection and training process is a meaningful innovation for this problem.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in attribute-based zero-shot recognition and offers a practical, principled solution that improves state-of-the-art results. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method’s benefits in both zero-shot and few-shot regimes. Given the importance of learning with limited or no class-labeled data, this contribution is likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners.

Overall, this is a strong, interesting, and technically sound paper with convincing results across multiple datasets and settings. It merits publication.
