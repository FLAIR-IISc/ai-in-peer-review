This paper proposes a new random forest-based approach for zero-shot learning that can better handle the uncertainty of attribute classifier predictions. The uncertainty is measured by the performance (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) of attribute classifiers on a hold-out validation set. This paper proposes using this performance information in the computation of information gain during random forest learning (following a specific category-attribute signature on multiple paths of a decision tree), thereby handling the "uncertainty" of attribute predictions.

**Quality:** The proposed concepts are well supported by relevant empirical/quantitative analysis. However, there is a lack of qualitative analysis (for example, I would have liked to see what types and categories of attributes are affected/improved by this method). Also, it is unclear how many of the parameters involved were chosen (for example, why a 80%-20% split was chosen for training-validation, etc.).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to read.

**Originality:** Although most previous work has addressed the problem of "attribute strength," this paper claims to focus on the less discussed problem of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms/techniques implicitly account for classifier unreliability, I believe this work is new in terms of explicitly handling unreliability.

**Importance:** Although the proposed method (random forest) is not new, the concept introduced in this paper (modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning performance) is interesting/thought-provoking for researchers working with attributes.

Although the concept and method introduced in this paper are not significantly new, its application to improving zero-shot learning is revealing/interesting.
