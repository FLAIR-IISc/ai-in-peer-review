This paper proposes a new random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that better accommodates uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. Uncertainty is measured through the performance of attribute classifiers (true positive rate, false positive rate, etc.) on a hold-out validation set. The paper proposes using this performance information in the calculation of information gain during random forest learning (to propagate a given class-attribute signature along multiple paths of a decision tree), thereby adjusting the "uncertainty" of attribute predictions.

Quality: The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical/quantitative analysis. However, it lacks qualitative analysis (for example, I would like to see how this method affects/improves the types of attributes and which classes). Also, it is not clear how many parameters were selected (for example, why an 80%-20% split was chosen for training-validation, etc.).

Clarity: This paper is well-written and easy to read.

Novelty: While most previous work has addressed the problem of "attribute strength," this paper claims to focus on the lesser-explored problem of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms/techniques explicitly account for classifier unreliability, I believe this work is novel in terms of explicit unreliability management.

Significance: While the proposed method (random forest) is not new, the idea presented in this paper (modeling unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning performance) is interesting/thought-provoking for researchers working on attributes.

While the idea and method presented in this paper are not significantly new, their application towards improving zero-shot learning is revealing/interesting.
