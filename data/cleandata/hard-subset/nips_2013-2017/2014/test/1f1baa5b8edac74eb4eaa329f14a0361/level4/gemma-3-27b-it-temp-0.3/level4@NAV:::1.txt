This paper investigates the challenge of translating attribute-based zero-shot learning theory into practical application. While the premise – recognizing novel classes via pre-trained attribute predictors – is strong, training accurate attribute classifiers often presents significant difficulties. This work addresses this by employing random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating attribute detector reliability through validation set propagation during training. The authors further demonstrate the method’s adaptability to few-shot learning scenarios. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors present a well-conceived and theoretically grounded solution to a core problem in zero-shot learning, leveraging the elegance of a random forest framework. The reported results on three benchmark datasets are compelling, and the thoroughness of the evaluation – including ablation studies, noise injection, and comparisons to existing literature across zero- and few-shot settings – is commendable.

CLARITY: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. A point of initial confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold *t*; as the attribute signatures remain binary, *t* appears to have no effect on equation (2) for values between 0 and 1. Consequently, the propagation of novel test examples down the tree (lines 207-212) initially lacked clarity due to the seemingly undefined nature of *t*. This is clarified in subsequent sections of the paper.

ORIGINALITY: This work represents a valuable application of random forest methodology to a significant problem in the field.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental issue within zero-shot learning. Overall, this is a strong, insightful, and technically sound contribution. The convincing results obtained across multiple datasets and experimental settings strongly support the proposed approach. I recommend this paper for publication.
