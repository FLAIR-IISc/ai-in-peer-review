The paper presents a novel approach to zero-shot recognition that addresses the issue of unreliable attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest framework that takes into account the reliability of attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree at training time. This approach is extended to handle training with a few training examples of test categories.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their method, including the key components of the random forest framework and how it is adapted to handle attribute unreliability. The experiments are thorough and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach on several standard datasets.

The authors do a nice job of performing ablation studies, introducing artificial noise, evaluating several settings (zero- versus few-shot learning), and comparing with the literature. The results show that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

One minor point of confusion is in Section 3.2.1, where the threshold t is introduced. The attribute signatures are still binary, so t can be any value 0 < t < 1 without changing anything in equation (2). However, this is cleared up in later sections.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, which is the unreliability of attribute predictions. The proposed method is elegant and theoretically sound, and the results are convincing. The paper is strong, interesting, and sound, and it deserves to be published.
