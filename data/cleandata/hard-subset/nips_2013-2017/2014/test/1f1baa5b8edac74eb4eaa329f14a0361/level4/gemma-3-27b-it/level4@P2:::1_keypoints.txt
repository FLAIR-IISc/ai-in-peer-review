- Problem: Addresses mismatch between theoretical promise and practical performance in attribute-based zero-shot learning due to unreliable attribute predictions.  
- Core idea: Explicitly model attribute prediction unreliability during training rather than just using noisy attributes.  
- Method: Novel random forest framework that propagates a validation set through trees to learn attribute error statistics.  
- Extensions: Framework extended to few-shot learning and to handle unreliable class-attribute associations.  
- Results: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on three standard datasets.  
- Technical quality: Method is theoretically sound, elegant, and well-suited to the problem.  
- Evaluation: Thorough experiments including ablation studies, noise injection, and comparisons to prior work.  
- Reproducibility: Paper provides sufficient detail to reproduce results.  
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized.  
- Clarity issue: Explanation of threshold t in Section 3.2.1 and its role in test-time propagation is initially confusing and could be improved.  
- Originality: Novel application of random forests and propagation of validation data to model attribute error characteristics.  
- Significance: Tackles a core limitation of zero-shot learning and is likely to be influential and widely adopted.  
- Recommendation: Strong paper with convincing results — reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
