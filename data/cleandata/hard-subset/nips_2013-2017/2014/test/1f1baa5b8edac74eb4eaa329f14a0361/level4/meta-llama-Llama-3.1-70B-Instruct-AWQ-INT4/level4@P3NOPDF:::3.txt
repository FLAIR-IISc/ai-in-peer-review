This paper proposes a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. The approach first trains attribute classifiers using Support Vector Machines (SVMs). A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors, while utilizing a validation set of images with attribute labels to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also presented.

The paper is well-written and clear, effectively summarizing related work. However, the originality of this paper is somewhat limited. The claim that the proposed approach deals with unreliable attribute predictions, while earlier work does not, is overstated. For instance, Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7], the baseline method used in this paper and many others, has similar abilities to handle attribute uncertainty. Both approaches follow a basic two-step process: (a) training attribute classifiers on image features, and (b) training a second class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, using a validation set to estimate the reliability of attribute classifiers. While [6] uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model to combine attributes, the proposed approach employs a new random forest-based method.

Upon closer examination, it becomes apparent that the proposed approach is not fundamentally novel in handling attribute uncertainty. Instead, it uses a more complex model for attribute noise, incorporating an ROC-based estimate rather than a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling. Furthermore, whereas [6] treats attributes as independent, the proposed approach effectively models joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set down trees. A more accurate description of the approach might be as a method for better modeling attribute correlation. A related paper that could be cited to support this is Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search".

One potential weakness of the proposed approach, compared to methods that treat attributes independently, is the need for a large validation set to obtain valid probability estimates, which becomes subdivided when propagated down decision trees. This issue is glossed over in the paper, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. Additional experiments evaluating the effect of training and validation set size would have strengthened the paper.

The overall quality of the paper is good, with sound technical choices and descriptions. The experiments are well-suited to support the claims made in the paper, although the level of quantitative improvement is not substantial enough to justify acceptance solely based on empirical results. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest of the paper. In contrast to the clear and detailed explanations provided elsewhere, this section is brief. A method that combines class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates when estimating probabilities p(a|...) might be more intuitive than the weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6).

In conclusion, while the paper is well-written, with technical decisions that make sense, and adequate experiments, the main selling point of the paper as a new way of handling unreliable attributes is somewhat questionable.
