This work introduces a novel random forest approach for zero-shot learning, designed to better account for uncertainty in the predictions of attribute classifiers. The authors quantify this uncertainty by evaluating the performance (e.g., true and false positive rates) of each attribute classifier on a held-out validation dataset. This performance data is then integrated into the information gain calculation during the construction of the random forest. This modification allows a single category-attribute signature to be explored along multiple decision tree paths, effectively incorporating the measured uncertainty of attribute predictions into the learning process.

**Quality:** The claims are well-substantiated with thorough quantitative and empirical results. However, the paper would be strengthened by the inclusion of qualitative analysis. For instance, an analysis of which specific attributes and categories see the most improvement from this method would provide valuable insight. Additionally, the manuscript lacks justification for the selection of several key parameters; a notable example is the rationale behind choosing an 80%-20% split for the training and validation sets.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is clearly written and straightforward to follow.

**Originality:** The authors position their work as addressing the less-explored issue of "attribute reliability," in contrast to the more commonly studied problem of "attribute strength" in prior literature. While many machine learning models implicitly handle classifier unreliability, the explicit mechanism for modeling and incorporating it presented in this paper is a novel contribution.

**Significance:** While the underlying method (random forests) is well-established, the core idea presented—modeling attribute unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to enhance zero-shot learning—is an interesting and thought-provoking contribution for researchers in attribute-based learning. The application of these concepts to improve zero-shot learning is both revealing and valuable to the field.
