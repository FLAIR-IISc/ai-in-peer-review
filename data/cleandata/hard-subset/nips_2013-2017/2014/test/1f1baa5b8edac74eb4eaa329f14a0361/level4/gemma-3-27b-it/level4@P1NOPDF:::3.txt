This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained using SVMs, and a random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors. Crucially, a validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well-written, and it provides a good summary of related work.

Originality: The novelty of this paper is somewhat limited, though not to the extent that it warrants rejection. The claim that the proposed approach uniquely addresses unreliable attribute predictions is overstated and potentially misleading. For example, direct attribute prediction (DAP) [7]—the baseline method used here and in many other works—already possesses some ability to handle attribute uncertainty. Both approaches share a fundamental structure: 
a) Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical in both cases).
b) Train a second-stage classifier to estimate class probability as a function of attribute predictions, using a validation set to estimate the reliability of the attribute classifiers ([6] uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model for attribute combination, while this paper proposes a new random forest-based method).

Therefore, I don’t believe the proposed approach represents a fundamentally new way of handling attribute uncertainty. Instead, it utilizes a more complex model for representing attribute noise: 1) it employs an ROC-based estimate rather than a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) unlike [6], which treats attributes as independent, this approach effectively models joint statistics in attribute errors by propagating the validation set down the decision trees. In this respect, it might be more accurate to characterize the contribution as a refined method for modeling attribute correlations. (I am not an expert in this area, but Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” might be a relevant related work to cite).

A potential weakness of this approach, compared to methods that treat attributes independently, is that it likely requires a substantial validation set to obtain reliable probability estimates as the data is subdivided during propagation down the decision trees. The paper glosses over this issue, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. It would have been beneficial to see more experiments evaluating the impact of training and validation set size.

Quality: Overall, the paper is of good quality, and the technical choices and descriptions are sensible. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the paper’s claims, although the quantitative improvements aren't substantial enough to justify acceptance based on empirical results alone. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) felt less developed than the rest of the paper. While the main body of the paper offers clear and detailed explanations, this section is quite brief. Intuitively, a method that could combine class-attribute priors and image attribute estimates when estimating probabilities p(a|…) might be more effective than the weighted combination of information gains used in Equation 6. 

This is a well-written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experiments; however, the central argument – that it presents a novel way to handle unreliable attributes – is somewhat questionable.
