This work addresses the discrepancy between the theoretical promise and practical challenges of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While theory suggests that unseen classes can be identified via pre-trained attribute detectors, the practical difficulty is that training these detectors is often as challenging, if not more so, than training classifiers for the object categories directly. The proposed method employs random forests to predict unseen classes from their corresponding attribute vectors. To handle the varying reliability of attribute detectors, the training procedure incorporates a validation set, propagating it through each decision tree during learning. The authors also demonstrate an extension of the approach to the few-shot learning scenario, where a small number of training examples from the test categories are available. The experiments demonstrate that the method achieves state-of-the-art performance across several benchmark attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors present a compelling solution to a core problem in zero-shot learning by effectively adapting a random forest framework. The proposed model is both elegant in its formulation and well-grounded in theory. The empirical results on three standard benchmarks are impressive. The evaluation is thorough, including comprehensive ablation studies, experiments with artificial noise, analysis in both zero-shot and few-shot settings, and a detailed comparison to existing methods.

CLARITY: The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear. One point of minor confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold t. Given that the attribute signatures are binary at this stage, any threshold t within the range (0, 1) would yield the same outcome in Equation (2). This makes its role unclear in lines 207-212, specifically concerning how a new test sample is propagated through the tree, as t appears to be ambiguously defined. However, this point is clarified satisfactorily in subsequent sections of the paper.

ORIGINALITY: The work represents a novel and effective application of the random forest framework to a significant and challenging problem.

SIGNIFICANCE: This paper makes a valuable contribution by tackling a foundational issue in the field of zero-shot learning. Overall, the work is robust, engaging, and methodologically sound. The convincing results, demonstrated across three datasets and under various experimental conditions, strongly support its publication.
