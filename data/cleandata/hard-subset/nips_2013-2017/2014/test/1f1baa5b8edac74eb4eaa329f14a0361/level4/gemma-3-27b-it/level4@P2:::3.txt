This paper introduces a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning leveraging random forests. The approach first trains attribute classifiers using SVMs, then constructs a random forest trained on class-attribute signature vectors. Critically, a validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree, enabling the model to account for inaccuracies in attribute predictions. An extension to the few-shot learning scenario is also presented.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, providing a comprehensive and accessible overview of the proposed method and related work. The authors effectively contextualize their contribution within the existing literature.

**Originality:** While the core idea builds on established techniques in zero-shot learning and random forests, the approach of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability within the random forest framework is a valuable contribution. However, the claim of substantial novelty regarding handling attribute uncertainty requires nuance. Existing methods, such as Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8], already incorporate mechanisms to address attribute uncertainty, albeit through different means (e.g., probabilistic modeling). The key difference lies in *how* this uncertainty is modeled. This work distinguishes itself by employing an ROC-based estimate of attribute reliability and, importantly, by effectively modeling dependencies between attribute errors through the propagation of validation data down the decision trees—potentially capturing joint statistics in attribute errors, rather than assuming independence. This aspect warrants further emphasis. It might be beneficial for the authors to acknowledge and cite related work exploring attribute correlations, such as Scheirer et al. "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search."

A potential limitation is the reliance on a sufficiently large validation set to obtain robust probability estimates as the data is subdivided during tree construction. The paper briefly addresses this (line 323) with a heuristic approach, but more thorough investigation and experimentation regarding the impact of training and validation set size would strengthen the work.

**Quality:** The technical aspects of the paper are sound and well-described. The experimental setup is appropriate, and the results generally support the authors’ claims. While the quantitative improvements are not dramatic, they are consistent across multiple datasets and demonstrate the benefit of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less developed compared to the rest of the paper; a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the weighted information gain combination (Equation 6) would be helpful. An alternative approach – combining class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates in a more direct probabilistic manner – might be worth considering and discussing. Overall, this is a well-executed paper with thoughtful technical choices and adequate empirical validation. However, the core claim of presenting a fundamentally new approach to handling attribute uncertainty requires careful framing.

**Significance:** The work addresses a crucial challenge in zero-shot learning – the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. By improving the robustness of zero-shot models to attribute noise, the authors contribute to making this technique more practical for real-world applications where attribute predictions are often imperfect. The extension to few-shot learning further enhances the practical utility of the proposed method. The results suggest that the approach is particularly beneficial when attribute classifiers are less reliable, as is often the case with automatically discovered attributes or datasets with limited training data.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Clear and well-written paper with a logical presentation of ideas.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in zero-shot learning.
*   Demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines (including DAP) across multiple datasets.
*   Introduces a novel approach to modeling attribute unreliability within a random forest framework.
*   Provides a valuable extension to the few-shot learning scenario.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claim of novelty regarding handling attribute uncertainty is somewhat overstated; existing methods already address this issue.
*   The potential need for a large validation set to estimate attribute reliability requires further investigation.
*   The few-shot learning extension feels less polished and could benefit from a more detailed explanation.
*   The quantitative improvements, while consistent, are not overwhelmingly large.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should clarify the novelty of their approach in relation to existing methods like DAP, emphasizing the specific contributions of ROC-based reliability estimation and the modeling of attribute correlations. They should also expand on the discussion of the validation set size requirements and potentially provide additional experiments to address this concern. A more detailed explanation of the few-shot learning extension would also be beneficial.
