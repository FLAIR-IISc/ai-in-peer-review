This paper addresses the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While theory suggests that novel classes can be recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging, or even more so, than learning the object classes themselves.

To tackle this, the authors propose a method where random forests are trained to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors. The training procedure accounts for the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree at training time. The authors also show how the method can be extended to the few-shot learning scenario, where a small number of training examples from the test categories are available. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on several standard attribute datasets.

**QUALITY**: The authors effectively address a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning by framing it within a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound. The results on three standard datasets are strong. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including ablation studies, tests with artificial noise, an analysis of both zero-shot and few-shot learning settings, and thorough comparisons with the literature.

**CLARITY**: The paper is very well written. One minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: when the threshold *t* is introduced, the attribute signatures are still binary, which implies that any value for *t* within the range (0, 1) has no effect on Equation (2). Consequently, it is initially unclear how a novel test example can be propagated down the tree, as *t* seems ill-defined at this stage. However, this is clarified in subsequent sections.

**ORIGINALITY**: The paper presents a novel and effective application of the random forest framework to an important problem.

**SIGNIFICANCE**: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. It is a strong, interesting, and sound contribution. The results, demonstrated on three datasets across a variety of settings, are convincing. This paper deserves to be published.
