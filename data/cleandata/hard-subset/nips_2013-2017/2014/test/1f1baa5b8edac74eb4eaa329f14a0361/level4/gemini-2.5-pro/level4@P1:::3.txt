This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning that uses random forests to explicitly model the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The core idea is to train a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors, but to inform the tree-building process using a validation set of attribute-labeled images. This validation set is used to estimate the reliability (i.e., the ROC statistics) of each attribute classifier at every node, allowing the model to favor splits that are both discriminative and robust to prediction errors. The paper also proposes an extension to the few-shot learning scenario. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets, where it demonstrates improved performance over existing methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP).

**Clarity**
The paper is very clear and well-written. The motivation is well-established, the proposed method is described in sufficient detail, and the related work is summarized effectively.

**Originality and Significance**
The primary concern with this paper lies in its framing and the extent of its novelty. The authors' central claim—that their approach is novel in dealing with unreliable attribute predictions while previous work is not—feels somewhat overstated. For instance, the main baseline, Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8], also accounts for attribute uncertainty. Both this paper and DAP follow a similar two-stage process:
1.  Train attribute classifiers on image features (this step is identical).
2.  Train a second-stage classifier to predict class probabilities as a function of the attribute predictions from the first stage. This stage explicitly uses information about attribute classifier reliability; DAP uses a probabilistic model informed by Platt scaling, whereas this work introduces a novel random forest-based method.

Consequently, the proposed method does not appear to be a fundamentally new paradigm for handling attribute uncertainty, but rather a more sophisticated implementation of an existing two-stage approach. The key technical distinctions, which are valuable, are that the proposed model:
1.  Uses a more detailed, ROC-based estimate of attribute noise instead of a simpler sigmoidal model.
2.  Effectively models some joint statistics of attribute errors by propagating the validation set down the decision trees, whereas methods like DAP typically treat attributes as independent.

Given this, it might be more accurate to frame the contribution not as the first method to handle unreliability, but as a better way to model attribute noise and correlations. The authors might also consider citing related work on attribute correlation, such as Scheirer et al., "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search."

**Quality**
Overall, the technical quality of the paper is high. The modeling choices are well-justified and the descriptions are sound. However, there are a few points that could be strengthened.

A potential weakness of the proposed approach is its reliance on the validation set. As this set is subdivided and propagated down the decision trees, the number of samples available to estimate ROC statistics at deeper nodes could become very small, leading to unreliable estimates. This issue is not fully addressed in the paper, and the proposed solution (reverting to the full validation set when sample sizes are small) seems heuristic. It would strengthen the paper to include an analysis of the method's sensitivity to the size of the validation set.

The experiments are appropriate and generally support the paper's claims. However, the quantitative improvements over DAP, while consistent, are modest. This suggests the paper's acceptance should hinge more on its conceptual contribution than on breakthrough empirical performance.

Finally, the extension to few-shot learning in Section 3.3 feels less developed than the rest of the paper. This section is very brief, and the proposed linear combination of information gains (Eq. 6) is not deeply motivated. An alternative approach that more directly combines the class-attribute priors with the image-based evidence when estimating node posteriors might be more intuitive and principled.

**Summary**
This is a well-written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experiments. Its main strength lies in the novel random forest formulation that explicitly incorporates detailed attribute error statistics into the training process. The primary weakness is that its central claim of novelty in handling attribute unreliability is somewhat overstated, as it builds directly on the conceptual foundation of prior work like DAP. While the paper presents a more sophisticated model for attribute noise, the empirical gains are not dramatic.
