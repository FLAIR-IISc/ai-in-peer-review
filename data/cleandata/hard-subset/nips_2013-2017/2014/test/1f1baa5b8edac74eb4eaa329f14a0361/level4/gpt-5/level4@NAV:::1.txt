The paper aims to narrow the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The theory posits that novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors; in practice, however, training these attribute classifiers can be as difficult as, or even more difficult than, learning the object categories directly.
Random forests are trained to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and the training procedure accounts for the reliability of attribute detectors by passing a validation set through each decision tree during training. The authors demonstrate how the method can be extended to handle scenarios with a few training examples of the test categories. The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on several attribute datasets.
QUALITY: The authors address this fundamental zero-shot learning problem effectively within a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically well-grounded. Results on 3 standard datasets are strong. The experiments include ablations, injected artificial noise, evaluations of both zero- and few-shot settings, and thorough comparisons to prior work.
CLARITY: The paper is very well written. One point of confusion occurs in Section 3.2.1: when the threshold t is introduced, the attribute signatures remain binary, so any value 0 < t < 1 leaves equation (2) unchanged. It is therefore unclear in lines 207–212 how a novel test example is propagated down the tree, since t appears ill-defined. This is clarified in later sections.
ORIGINALITY: This is a thoughtful application of a random forest framework to an important problem.
SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The paper is strong, interesting, and sound. The results on 3 datasets across multiple settings are convincing. The paper deserves to be published.
