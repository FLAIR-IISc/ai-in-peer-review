

This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for unreliable attribute predictions during model training. The authors introduce a random forest-based method that leverages statistics about each attribute's error tendencies to construct more robust discriminative models for unseen classes. The approach is grounded in the insight that standard zero-shot learning suffers from unreliable attribute predictions, and the authors demonstrate how their method can better handle this challenge compared to existing approaches. The paper further extends their method to handle few-shot scenarios and unreliable attribute descriptions.

The paper makes a strong connection to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors correctly identify that most zero-shot learning methods (including the widely used DAP [8]) treat attribute predictions as perfect, ignoring their inherent unreliability. The authors properly situate their work relative to the attribute-based zero-shot learning literature, noting that while some methods like [6] incorporate attribute confidence, they do so through simpler probabilistic models without accounting for inter-attribute dependencies. The authors also appropriately distinguish their approach from alternative zero-shot strategies that exploit class relationships rather than semantic attributes.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear technical contribution: a random forest training procedure that incorporates attribute classifier reliability through ROC-based error statistics. The authors correctly demonstrate that their method outperforms DAP on three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN), with particularly strong gains on classes that differ significantly from training classes. The results are well-supported, with careful ablation studies showing the importance of both the ROC propagation mechanism and the signature uncertainty modeling components. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is a valuable contribution that demonstrates the flexibility of their framework.

Regarding clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors provide thorough technical explanations of their method, with clear mathematical formulations and intuitive examples (e.g., the elephant example illustrating fractional propagation). The experimental setup is well-described, with appropriate dataset choices and careful attention to the details of attribute classifier training. The paper would benefit from slightly more discussion of the validation set size requirements (as noted in the draft review), but overall the writing is exemplary.

The originality of the work is somewhat nuanced. While the core idea of handling attribute unreliability is not entirely new (DAP and others incorporate some form of attribute confidence), the authors' specific implementation using ROC-based error statistics propagated through decision trees to model inter-attribute dependencies represents a meaningful advancement. The paper correctly identifies that previous work treats attributes as independent, whereas their approach captures correlations in attribute errors through the tree structure. This is a significant technical improvement over existing methods. The claim that "existing methods take attribute predictions at face value" is slightly overstated, but the paper's contribution is substantial in how it models and mitigates attribute unreliability.

The significance of the work is strong. Zero-shot learning remains a critical problem for rare or dynamically emerging categories, and the authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach can significantly improve performance in this setting. The fact that their method outperforms the widely adopted DAP framework—especially when attribute classifiers are less reliable—highlights its practical value. The extension to few-shot learning further broadens the applicability of their framework, addressing a common scenario where a small number of examples are available.

The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of the validation set requirements. While the authors mention reserving 20% of attribute-labeled images for ROC validation, they do not thoroughly address how the size of this validation set impacts performance, particularly as it gets subdivided through the tree structure. The paper's approach to handling small validation sets (line 323) is somewhat heuristic, and it would have strengthened the paper to include experiments showing the impact of varying validation set sizes. Additionally, the few-shot extension (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the main contribution, with the weighted information gain formulation (Equation 6) appearing somewhat ad hoc compared to the elegant tree-based approach for the zero-shot case.

The paper's quality is high, with sound technical choices, appropriate experimental design, and well-supported claims. The improvements over baselines are consistent across datasets, though the absolute gains (around 5-10% on AwA) are not massive. However, these gains are significant given the challenging nature of zero-shot learning and the fact that DAP is the current standard approach.

Pro:
- Clear, well-written presentation of a meaningful technical contribution
- Strong experimental results demonstrating the importance of modeling attribute unreliability
- Novel approach to modeling inter-attribute dependencies through ROC propagation
- Valuable extension to few-shot learning
- Good connection to prior work and clear distinction from existing methods

Con:
- Overstated claim about previous work ignoring attribute unreliability (DAP and others incorporate some form of attribute confidence)
- Insufficient discussion of validation set size requirements and impact on performance
- Few-shot extension feels less developed than the main contribution
- Limited exploration of the trade-offs between model complexity and performance

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to zero-shot learning. While the novelty is not revolutionary, the authors present a well-motivated, technically sound approach that demonstrates significant practical improvements over the current state of the art. The paper's clarity and strong experimental validation make it a worthy contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the validation set size discussion and strengthen the few-shot extension.
