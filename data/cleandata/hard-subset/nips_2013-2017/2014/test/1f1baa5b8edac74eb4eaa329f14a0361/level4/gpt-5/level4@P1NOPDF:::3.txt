This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. Attribute classifiers are first trained with SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class–attribute signature vectors, while a validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate attribute reliability at each tree node. An extension to few-shot learning is also proposed.

Clarity: The paper is very clear and well written, with a solid summary of related work.

Originality: The novelty is somewhat limited, though not too low for acceptance. The claim that the proposed approach uniquely handles unreliable attribute predictions, whereas prior work does not, feels overstated or misleading. For example, direct attribute prediction [7] (DAP)—the baseline used here and in many other papers—already provides a way to address attribute uncertainty. Both approaches follow the same basic recipe:
- Train attribute classifiers on image features (identical in both papers).
- Train a second-stage class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, using a validation set to estimate attribute reliability ([6] uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model to combine attributes; the proposed method replaces this with a random-forest-based estimator).

In this sense, the method is not a fundamentally new way to handle attribute uncertainty. Its main differences from earlier work are that it uses a more expressive model for attribute noise: (1) it relies on ROC-based estimates rather than a sigmoidal calibration such as Platt scaling, and (2) whereas [6] treats attributes as independent, the proposed approach effectively captures joint statistics in attribute errors because the validation set is propagated down the trees. It would be more accurate to describe the contribution as improved modeling of attribute correlations. While I am not deeply familiar with this area, Scheirer et al., “Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search,” may be a relevant reference to cite.

A potential weakness compared with methods that treat attributes independently is the likely need for a large validation set: estimates become less reliable as the validation data are subdivided down the decision trees. The paper largely glosses over this issue, and the proposed mitigation (line 323) seems heuristic. It would have been helpful to include experiments assessing sensitivity to the sizes of the training and validation sets.

Quality: Overall, the technical choices are sensible and clearly presented. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the claims, though the quantitative gains are not, by themselves, strong enough to carry the paper on purely empirical grounds. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest: while earlier sections provide detailed explanations, this section is quite brief. Intuitively, a method that combines class–attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when computing probabilities p(a|...) would make more sense than a weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6).

In summary, this is a well-written paper with reasonable technical decisions and adequate experiments. However, its primary claim—to provide a fundamentally new mechanism for handling unreliable attributes—is questionable; the contribution is better characterized as offering a richer model that captures attribute correlation and non-sigmoidal calibration.
