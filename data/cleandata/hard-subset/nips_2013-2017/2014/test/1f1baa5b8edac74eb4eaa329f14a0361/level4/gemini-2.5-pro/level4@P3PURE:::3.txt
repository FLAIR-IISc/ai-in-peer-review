This paper presents a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning using random forests. The proposed approach first trains attribute classifiers using SVMs. Subsequently, a random forest is trained on class-attribute signature vectors, while a validation set of images with attribute labels is used to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the tree. The paper also proposes an extension to the few-shot learning setting.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very clear, well-written, and provides a thorough summary of related work.

**Originality:**
The novelty of this work is somewhat limited, though not necessarily below the threshold for acceptance. A central claim of the paper—that it introduces a new way to handle unreliable attribute predictions while earlier work does not—appears overstated. For instance, the baseline method, Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [7], shares a similar high-level strategy for managing attribute uncertainty. Both approaches employ a two-stage process:
a) Train attribute classifiers on image features.
b) Train a second-stage class probability estimator as a function of attribute predictions, using a validation set to gauge the reliability of these classifiers.

The key difference lies in the second-stage model: while DAP [6] uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model that assumes attribute independence, this work employs a novel random forest-based method. As such, the proposed method is not a fundamentally new paradigm for handling attribute uncertainty.

Rather, its primary contributions are more nuanced. The proposed method introduces a more complex model for attribute noise by: 1) using an ROC-based reliability estimate instead of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) implicitly modeling the joint statistics of attribute errors by propagating the validation set through the decision trees, whereas methods like [6] treat attributes as independent. It would be more accurate, therefore, to frame the contribution as a more sophisticated method for modeling attribute correlations. In this context, citing related work such as Scheirer et al., "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search," could be beneficial.

A potential weakness of this approach, compared to methods that assume attribute independence, is its dependency on a large validation set. As the validation set is propagated down the decision trees, it is progressively subdivided, which could compromise the validity of probability estimates in deeper nodes. This issue is glossed over, and the proposed solution (line 323) appears heuristic. The paper would be strengthened by an empirical analysis of the method's sensitivity to the size of the training and validation sets.

**Quality:**
Overall, the quality of the paper is high. The technical descriptions are clear and the design choices are well-justified. The experiments are appropriate and support the paper's claims, although the quantitative improvements over baselines are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance on empirical performance alone.

The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less polished than the rest of the manuscript. In contrast to the lengthy explanations elsewhere, this section is notably brief. A more intuitive alternative might involve a method that combines class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates when modeling the probability `p(a|...)`, rather than the weighted combination of information gains proposed in Eq. 6.

In summary, while this is a well-written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experiments, its central claim regarding the novelty of handling unreliable attributes is debatable.
