This paper tackles a critical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the discrepancy between theoretical promise and practical performance stemming from unreliable attribute predictions. The core idea is to move beyond simply *using* attribute predictions and instead explicitly model their unreliability during training. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that leverages statistics about attribute error tendencies, achieved by propagating a validation set through the decision trees during learning. They further extend this framework to handle both few-shot learning and unreliable class-attribute associations. The method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on three standard datasets.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and elegant solution to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The random forest framework is well-suited to the task, and the method for incorporating attribute reliability is both theoretically grounded and practically effective. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods across three datasets. The ablation studies, noise injection experiments, and comparisons to relevant literature provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. The extensions to few-shot learning further enhance the method’s versatility.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally very well written and clearly organized. However, the introduction of the threshold *t* in Section 3.2.1 initially caused some confusion. While the attribute signatures are initially binary, the explanation of how *t* influences the process isn’t immediately apparent, as any value between 0 and 1 initially appears equivalent. The propagation of a novel test example through the tree, referencing *t*, is also unclear at first. This is subsequently clarified in later sections, but improving the explanation in 3.2.1 would enhance clarity. Overall, the paper is well-structured and provides sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results.

**Originality:** The application of a random forest framework to explicitly address attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning is a novel contribution. While random forests have been used in other contexts within computer vision, the specific approach of propagating validation data to model attribute error characteristics is a unique and valuable contribution.

**Significance:** This paper addresses a crucial limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods. By acknowledging and mitigating the impact of unreliable attribute predictions, the authors significantly improve the practical applicability of this promising learning paradigm. The demonstrated performance gains on standard datasets, coupled with the theoretical justification and clear explanation, suggest this work will be highly influential and likely adopted by other researchers in the field. The ability to extend the method to few-shot scenarios further enhances its impact.

**Summary of Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Technical Foundation:** The method is theoretically sound and well-motivated.
*   **Significant Performance Improvements:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** Comprehensive ablation studies and comparisons to existing methods demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Addresses a Core Challenge:** The work tackles a fundamental limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally easy to understand and follow.

**Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Initial Clarity Issue:** The explanation surrounding the threshold *t* in Section 3.2.1 could be improved for immediate understanding. (Minor concern)

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong, interesting, and technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The results are convincing, and the approach addresses a critical challenge in practical applications of this learning paradigm. I strongly recommend acceptance.
