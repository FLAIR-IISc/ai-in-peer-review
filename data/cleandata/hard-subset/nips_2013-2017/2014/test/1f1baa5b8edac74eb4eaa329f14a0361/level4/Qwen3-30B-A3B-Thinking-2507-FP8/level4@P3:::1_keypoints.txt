- Addresses practical gap in zero-shot learning: attribute classifiers are unreliable and undermine standard approaches.  
- Proposes a novel random forest framework that models attribute prediction unreliability during training.  
- Uses ROC statistics from validation data to guide probabilistic propagation of class signatures through trees.  
- Introduces an ROC-integrated information gain formulation (Eq. 5) for unreliability-aware splitting.  
- Extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining labeled examples with descriptive priors.  
- Ablation studies isolate gains from ROC-based propagation and modeling signature uncertainty.  
- Controlled noise experiments show robust adaptation to varying attribute reliability, outperforming baselines by up to 10.2%.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on AwA, aPY, and SUN, including with discovered attributes.  
- Experiments use standard protocols, 20-trial averaging, and are statistically reliable.  
- Paper is technically rigorous, well-executed, and clearly written with helpful figures and supplementary details.  
- Original contribution: first application of random forests to zero-shot that explicitly models attribute errors, distinct from missing-value approaches.  
- Significant practical impact: makes zero-shot learning robust and deployable, reducing reliance on improving attribute classifiers.  
- Minor cons: diminishing few-shot returns as labeled data grows and dependence on validation data for ROC estimation.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — substantial, well-supported contribution that meets conference standards.
