

This paper introduces a novel random forest-based approach for zero-shot learning that explicitly incorporates uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. The key innovation lies in leveraging the performance metrics (e.g., true positive rate, false positive rate) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set to inform the information gain calculation during random forest construction. By weighting attribute contributions based on their validated reliability, the method dynamically adjusts for prediction uncertainty along decision paths, thereby improving category-attribute mapping.  

**Quality:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-supported by quantitative results, demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods. However, the analysis lacks qualitative insights; for instance, the paper does not examine which specific attributes or categories benefit most from the uncertainty-aware approach (e.g., whether attributes like "has stripes" or categories like "zebra" show pronounced gains). Additionally, the rationale for key hyperparameters (notably the 80-20 train-validation split ratio) remains unexplained, raising questions about the sensitivity of the method to this choice.  

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear technical explanations and logical flow. The methodology is accessible to readers familiar with random forests and zero-shot learning.  

**Originality:** While prior work often focuses on *attribute strength* (e.g., how strongly an attribute predicts a category), this paper addresses the underexplored concept of *attribute reliability*—explicitly modeling how consistently attribute classifiers perform. Although some algorithms implicitly mitigate classifier unreliability, this work’s direct integration of validation-set performance into the learning process represents a distinct contribution. The framing of "reliability" as a measurable, context-dependent property (rather than a fixed attribute property) is particularly novel.  

**Significance:** Though the random forest framework itself is not new, the paper’s core contribution—using validated classifier performance to refine zero-shot learning—offers a valuable and thought-provoking perspective. It bridges a critical gap between standard attribute-based zero-shot learning and real-world classifier uncertainty. This approach provides a practical pathway to enhance robustness in attribute prediction systems, making it a meaningful contribution for researchers focused on scalable zero-shot recognition. While the methodological novelty is incremental, its application to a well-known problem with clear empirical validation elevates its impact.  

**Overall Recommendation:** The paper presents a compelling, well-executed adaptation of random forests for zero-shot learning. Its strength lies in the principled handling of uncertainty through measurable reliability metrics, supported by strong quantitative results. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should address the missing qualitative analysis and justify key hyperparameters. Despite the method not introducing a fundamentally new algorithm, the explicit treatment of attribute reliability offers a significant refinement to existing paradigms, making it a worthy addition to the field. The work is suitable for acceptance with minor revisions.
