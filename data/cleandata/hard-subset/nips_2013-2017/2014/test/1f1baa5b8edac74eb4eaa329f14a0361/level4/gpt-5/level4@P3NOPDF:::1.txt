Summary: 
This paper tackles the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While the theory suggests that unseen classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, in practice those attribute classifiers are often unreliable and as hard to learn as object classifiers. The authors propose a random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability by propagating a validation set through decision trees during training. The method naturally extends to few-shot learning by incorporating a small number of labeled examples from test categories. Experiments on several attribute datasets show strong, state-of-the-art performance, with comprehensive ablations.

QUALITY:
- The approach is elegant and well-motivated. Modeling attribute unreliability within the training of random forests addresses a key practical bottleneck in zero-shot learning.
- The methodology appears theoretically sound, and the use of a validation set to calibrate splits is a pragmatic way to reflect attribute detector noise in the classifier learning process.
- Empirical results on three standard datasets are strong. The paper presents careful ablations, including analyses under artificial noise, comparisons between zero-shot and few-shot settings, and thorough baselines from the literature.
- The extension to few-shot learning is a natural and useful addition, showing the method’s flexibility as soon as a handful of labeled examples become available.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Please report or discuss computational cost: how much overhead does propagating the validation set through each split introduce, and how does training time scale with the number of attributes, trees, and classes?
- Consider adding sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters (e.g., tree depth, number of trees, validation set size), and discuss any overfitting controls.
- If available, provide confidence intervals or variance across runs to quantify the robustness of the gains.

CLARITY:
- The paper is generally very well written and easy to follow. The problem setting, the limitations of existing attribute-based methods, and the proposed solution are clearly articulated.
- One point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1. When the threshold t is introduced, the attribute signatures at that stage are still binary; thus any choice of 0 < t < 1 would not alter Equation (2). As a result, it is unclear in lines 207–212 how a novel test example is propagated down the tree, since t appears ill-defined at that point. This ambiguity is resolved in later sections once probabilistic attribute outputs are incorporated. To improve readability, I recommend moving the relevant explanation earlier, or explicitly stating at the introduction of t that it will govern probabilistic attribute scores at test time (and during validation-set propagation) rather than binary signatures. A small illustrative example or figure could also help clarify how t affects split decisions when attributes are noisy.
- Minor editorial suggestions: briefly define any nonstandard notation when first used, and ensure consistent terminology for “attribute signatures,” “detector reliability,” and “validation propagation.”

ORIGINALITY:
- Applying random forests with an explicit mechanism to handle attribute unreliability is a novel and well-executed idea for zero-shot learning. While random forests themselves are standard, the way the validation set is used to calibrate splits for noisy attribute predictions is a distinctive contribution.
- The extension to few-shot learning within the same framework further enhances the paper’s novelty and practical relevance.

SIGNIFICANCE:
- The paper addresses a fundamental and timely problem in zero-shot learning: turning theoretically appealing attribute-based recognition into a practical system robust to imperfect attribute detectors.
- The state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets and the careful experimental design suggest the approach will be impactful and of interest to the community.
- By bridging zero-shot and few-shot regimes, the method offers a practical pathway for deploying recognition systems that gracefully improve as limited labeled data becomes available.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong, interesting, and sound paper. It proposes a clear and effective method that directly tackles a key practical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning, supports it with solid experiments and ablations, and achieves state-of-the-art results. With minor clarifications—especially regarding the role and interpretation of the threshold t in Section 3.2.1—and a brief discussion of computational cost and parameter sensitivity, the paper would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance.
