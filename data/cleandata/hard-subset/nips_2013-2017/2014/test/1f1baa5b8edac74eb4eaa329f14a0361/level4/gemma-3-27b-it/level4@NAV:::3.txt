This paper introduces a method for attribute-based zero-shot learning leveraging random forests. The approach involves initially training attribute classifiers using SVMs, followed by training a random forest on class-attribute signature vectors. Importantly, a validation set of images with attribute labels is utilized to estimate attribute reliability at each node of the decision tree. An extension to the few-shot learning setting is also presented.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and provides a strong summary of relevant prior work. The novelty of the work is moderate; while sufficient for consideration, the claim of fundamentally addressing unreliable attribute predictions in a way that differentiates it from existing methods is potentially overstated.  Specifically, the baseline method DAP [7], used in this work and others, already incorporates mechanisms for handling attribute uncertainty. Both approaches share a common structure: training attribute classifiers on image features and then learning a second-stage class probability estimator based on attribute predictions, using a validation set for reliability estimation.  Existing methods like [6] employ Platt scaling and probabilistic models, while this work proposes a random forest-based alternative. 

Therefore, the contribution appears to be a more sophisticated model for attribute noise, characterized by the use of an ROC-based reliability estimate instead of a sigmoidal model and, critically, the implicit modeling of joint statistics in attribute errors through propagation of the validation set down the decision trees.  Framing the work as a more effective method for modeling attribute correlations might be a more accurate description.  Relevant work, such as Scheirer et al. "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search," could be considered for citation.

A potential limitation of this approach, compared to those treating attributes independently, is the substantial validation set potentially required to obtain reliable probability estimates as the data is subdivided during tree construction.  The paper glosses over this issue, and the proposed heuristic (line 323) warrants further investigation. Additional experiments exploring the impact of training and validation set size would be valuable.

Overall, the paper demonstrates good quality and sound technical choices. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the claims, although the magnitude of quantitative improvement is not substantial enough for acceptance based on empirical results alone. The extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels comparatively underdeveloped, lacking the detailed explanations found elsewhere. A method that integrates class-attribute priors with image attribute estimates for estimating probabilities p(a|...) might be more intuitive than the current weighted information gain approach (Eq. 6).  In conclusion, this is a well-written paper with sensible technical decisions and adequate experimentation; however, the central argument regarding a novel approach to handling unreliable attributes requires further substantiation.
