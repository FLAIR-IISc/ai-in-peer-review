- Proposes a random-forest-based attribute-driven zero-shot learning method: SVM-trained attribute classifiers, random forests over class–attribute signatures, and a held-out attribute-labeled set to estimate attribute reliability per tree node; includes a few-shot extension.
- Paper is very clear and well written with a solid related-work summary.
- Novelty is modest; the claim of uniquely addressing unreliable attribute predictions is overstated.
- Method is similar in high-level recipe to prior work (e.g., DAP): train attribute classifiers, then a second-stage class estimator calibrated on a validation set.
- Key differences from prior work: uses ROC-based calibration rather than Platt scaling, and captures joint attribute-error statistics by propagating validation examples down trees.
- Framing the contribution as improved modeling of attribute correlations (rather than a fundamentally new uncertainty solution) is more accurate.
- Potential drawback: accurately estimating attribute reliabilities in tree nodes likely requires a large validation set once data are partitioned, which is glossed over.
- Proposed remedy for sparse validation statistics (line 323) appears heuristic and under-analyzed.
- Experiments are appropriate and support claims qualitatively, but quantitative gains are small and may not justify acceptance on empirical improvements alone.
- Few-shot extension is brief and less polished; the weighted information-gain formulation (Eq. 6) is conceptually less intuitive than combining class–attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates.
- Overall: technically sound and well written, but central claim about addressing unreliable attributes is debatable and some experimental/validation analyses are missing.
