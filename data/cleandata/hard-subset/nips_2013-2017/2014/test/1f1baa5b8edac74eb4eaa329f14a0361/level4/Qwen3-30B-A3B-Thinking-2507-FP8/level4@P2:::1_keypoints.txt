- Addresses a critical zero-shot learning problem: attribute predictions are often unreliable in practice.  
- Proposes a novel random forest method that explicitly models attribute prediction unreliability during training.  
- Uses ROC statistics to guide tree construction and account for classifier errors.  
- Handles pure zero-shot and few-shot settings, naturally extending to cases with limited labeled examples.  
- Experiments on three datasets (animals, objects, scenes) show consistent improvements over state-of-the-art, especially when attributes are unreliable.  
- Outperforms DAP even while using only 80% of attribute-labeled data, showing modeling unreliability matters more than more data.  
- Experimental design is thorough: includes ablation studies, controlled noise experiments, and clear comparisons.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.  
- Generally clear writing, though Section 3.2.1’s threshold explanation is initially confusing; a main-paper schematic of training would help.  
- Original contribution: recognizing and principled handling of attribute-classifier unreliability via ROC-guided random forests.  
- Significant practical impact: makes zero-shot recognition more viable for real-world and few-shot applications; works with hand-designed and discovered attributes.  
- Main weaknesses: limited discussion of why attribute classifiers are unreliable and limited treatment of challenges/applicability outside visual recognition.  
- Overall judgment: strong, well-validated contribution that merits publication.
