- Tackles gap between theory and practice in attribute-based zero-shot learning by proposing a random forest method that models attribute unreliability via validation-set propagation and extends naturally to few-shot learning.  
- Elegant and well-motivated approach that explicitly accounts for noisy attribute detectors during training.  
- Method appears theoretically sound; using a validation set to calibrate splits is a pragmatic way to reflect detector noise.  
- Strong empirical results on three standard datasets with comprehensive ablations, noise analyses, and comparisons to baselines.  
- Few-shot extension is a natural, useful addition demonstrating flexibility when a few labeled examples are available.  
- Suggest reporting computational cost and how training time scales with number of attributes, trees, and classes.  
- Suggest sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters (tree depth, number of trees, validation set size) and discussion of overfitting controls.  
- Suggest reporting confidence intervals or variance across runs to quantify robustness of gains.  
- Paper is generally well written and clear about problem setting, limitations, and proposed solution.  
- Clarity issue: ambiguity in Section 3.2.1 about threshold t and how novel test examples are propagated; recommend moving or clarifying the explanation and adding a small example/figure.  
- Minor editorial fixes: define nonstandard notation when first used and use consistent terminology for “attribute signatures,” “detector reliability,” and “validation propagation.”  
- Originality: novel application of random forests calibrated with validation propagation to handle noisy attributes; few-shot integration increases novelty.  
- Significance: addresses a fundamental practical problem in zero-shot learning, achieves state-of-the-art results, and offers an impactful bridge between zero-shot and few-shot regimes.  
- Overall assessment: strong, sound paper recommended for acceptance with minor clarifications on t, computational cost, and parameter sensitivity.
