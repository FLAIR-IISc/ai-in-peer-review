

The paper addresses a critical gap between theory and practice in attribute-based zero-shot learning. While the theoretical premise posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, the practical challenge lies in the difficulty of learning these attribute classifiers—often comparable to or exceeding the complexity of learning object classes directly. To bridge this gap, the authors propose a random forest framework that predicts unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating attribute detector reliability through validation set propagation during tree construction. The method further extends to few-shot learning scenarios with limited examples from test categories and achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple standard attribute datasets.

QUALITY: The authors provide an elegant and theoretically grounded solution to a fundamental zero-shot learning challenge. The model’s design is sound, and the empirical results on three benchmark datasets are compelling. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies, introduce controlled noise, evaluate both zero-shot and few-shot settings, and provide comprehensive comparisons with prior work.

CLARITY: The paper is well-written overall. A minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: the threshold $t$ is introduced when attribute signatures remain binary, making $t$ (where $0 < t < 1$) redundant for Equation (2). The description of test example propagation through the tree (lines 207–212) is initially unclear due to this redundancy, though the issue is resolved in subsequent sections.

ORIGINALITY: This work presents a novel application of random forests to zero-shot learning, offering a fresh perspective on leveraging attribute detectors.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a core challenge in zero-shot learning with practical implications for real-world deployment.

The paper is strong, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution. The results across three datasets and multiple settings are convincing and robust. It deserves publication.
