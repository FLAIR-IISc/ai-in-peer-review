This paper presents a random forest-based method for attribute-based zero-shot learning. The approach first trains attribute classifiers using SVMs. A random forest is then trained on class-attribute signature vectors, using a validation set of images with attribute labels to estimate the reliability of each attribute at every node in the trees. The authors also propose an extension for the few-shot learning setting.

**Clarity:** The paper is clear, well-written, and provides a good summary of related work.

**Originality:** The novelty of this work is somewhat limited, though not necessarily below the bar for acceptance. A central claim of the paper is that it introduces a novel way to handle unreliable attribute predictions, which previous methods supposedly do not. I find this claim to be overstated. For instance, the Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method [7]—a common baseline used in this paper and others—already incorporates mechanisms to manage attribute uncertainty.

At a high level, both DAP and the proposed method follow a similar two-stage pipeline:
1.  Training attribute classifiers on image features (this stage is identical in both papers).
2.  Training a second-stage estimator that predicts class probabilities from the attribute predictions.

Both methods leverage a validation set to gauge the reliability of the attribute classifiers. The primary difference lies in the second stage: while DAP [7] uses Platt scaling and a probabilistic model to combine attributes, the proposed approach introduces a novel random forest-based method for this task.

Therefore, the proposed approach does not seem to represent a fundamentally new paradigm for handling attribute uncertainty. Rather, its contribution is a more complex and potentially more powerful model for attribute noise. Specifically, its key differences from prior work are: 1) it employs an ROC-based reliability estimate instead of a sigmoidal model like Platt scaling, and 2) it implicitly models joint statistics of attribute errors by propagating the validation set through the decision trees, whereas methods like DAP [7] typically assume attribute independence. Given this, it might be more accurate to frame the contribution as an improved method for modeling attribute correlations. The authors might also consider citing related work on attribute fusion, such as Scheirer et al.'s "Multi-Attribute Spaces: Calibration for Attribute Fusion and Similarity Search."

A potential weakness of this approach, compared to methods that treat attributes independently, is its demand for a large validation set. As the validation data is propagated down the decision trees, it is repeatedly subdivided, which could lead to unreliable probability estimates in the deeper nodes. The paper does not adequately address this issue; the proposed solution on line 323 appears heuristic. A more thorough experimental evaluation, analyzing the method's sensitivity to the size of the training and validation sets, would have strengthened the paper.

**Quality:** Overall, the paper is of good quality. The technical details are well-described and the methodological choices are sound. The experiments are appropriate and generally support the paper's claims. However, the quantitative improvements over baselines are modest, meaning the paper cannot be accepted on the strength of its empirical results alone.

The proposed extension to few-shot learning (Section 3.3) feels less developed than the main contribution. This section is notably brief and lacks the detailed explanation found elsewhere in the paper. The proposed weighted combination of information gains (Eq. 6) is not well-motivated. An alternative approach that combines class-attribute priors with image-based attribute estimates to compute p(a|...) might be more intuitive and principled.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with sound technical decisions and adequate experiments. Its primary weakness is that its central claim—offering a fundamentally new way to handle attribute unreliability—is overstated. The contribution is better characterized as a more sophisticated model for attribute correlations and noise.
