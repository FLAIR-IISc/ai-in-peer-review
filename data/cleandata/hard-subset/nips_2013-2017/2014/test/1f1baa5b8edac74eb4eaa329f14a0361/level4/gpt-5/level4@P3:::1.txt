Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a central practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute classifiers are often unreliable, which degrades performance when unseen classes are defined by attribute signatures. The authors propose a random-forest method that explicitly models attribute unreliability during training. Each unseen class is represented by its attribute signature; a forest is trained one-vs-all using these signatures as training exemplars. Crucially, the method injects attribute-labeled validation data to estimate node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) for each attribute and threshold. Signatures are then fractionally propagated down the tree according to these ROC-derived probabilities, yielding splits that are discriminative yet robust to expected attribute errors. The framework also incorporates uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with image-based information gain via a tunable weight λ.

The approach fits squarely within the two-stage attribute-based zero-shot paradigm (e.g., SIGNATURE matching and DAP), but differs by modeling attribute error tendencies rather than taking attribute predictions at face value. It contrasts with transfer via hierarchical or label embeddings (e.g., ImageNet/semantic embeddings) that do not allow a human to specify new classes by attributes directly. The fractional propagation idea relates to handling missing/uncertain features in decision trees, but is adapted to the distinctive zero-shot setup: training on class signatures and testing on attribute predictions. The paper also demonstrates compatibility with discovered mid-level “pseudo-attributes.”

Empirical evaluation on AwA, aPY, and SUN shows improvements over DAP and baselines, with insightful ablations: controlled noise studies, removal of signature uncertainty, and removal of node-specific ROC propagation. The few-shot extension consistently benefits from the attribute prior and smoothly reduces reliance on it as labeled samples increase.

Quality
- Soundness: The core idea—select splits using expected information gain computed under estimated attribute error—is well motivated and clearly formulated. Fractional propagation using node-specific ROC statistics is a principled way to bias trees toward learnable and discriminative attributes/thresholds.
- Empirics: Results are comprehensive across three datasets and include controlled noise analyses, ablations isolating key components, comparisons with DAP and prior reported numbers, and experiments with discovered attributes. The “upper bound” with TRUE ROC is a useful sanity check.
- Reproducibility: Important implementation details (number/depth of trees, validation protocol, ROC estimation, handling of scarce validation examples at a node) are provided.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. One point of confusion arises in Sec. 3.2.1: with binary signatures, any 0 < t < 1 yields the same split, which makes t appear ill-defined for test-time traversal. This is resolved in Sec. 3.2.2 when attribute predictions are real-valued and ROC-driven thresholds matter. It would help to foreshadow this in Sec. 3.2.1 by noting explicitly that binary-signature splits are a conceptual scaffold, and that operational thresholds are determined once unreliability is introduced. It may also help to move a brief description of how leaf posteriors are computed under fractional propagation from the supplement into the main text.

Originality
While random forests and handling uncertain features are established, applying node-specific ROC-guided fractional propagation to zero-shot learning with class signatures is, to my knowledge, novel. The extensions to signature uncertainty and few-shot learning via a combined information gain are natural and useful.

Significance
Addressing attribute unreliability is critical for making attribute-based zero-shot learning practical. The method delivers consistent improvements, particularly when attributes are noisier (e.g., aPY and SUN), and retains the advantage of human-interpretable class definition. The few-shot extension is pragmatic and likely to be adopted.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling of attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC statistics within a random forest.
- Robust training objective (expected IG) that prefers learnable, discriminative attributes and thresholds.
- Comprehensive experiments: three datasets, controlled noise, ablations, discovered attributes, few-shot extension.
- Practical and interpretable; maintains the human-in-the-loop attribute specification.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarify Sec. 3.2.1 regarding thresholds with binary signatures; add a short bridge to Sec. 3.2.2 explaining how real-valued attribute predictions at test time make thresholds meaningful.
- Validation data dependence: Since ROC statistics are estimated from seen classes, there is a risk of domain shift if unseen classes exhibit different attribute manifestations. The paper briefly explores “TRUE ROC” and a nearest-signature heuristic; consider adding a short analysis of sensitivity to the size/composition of DV or a stratified selection strategy tied to signature similarity.
- Attribute correlations: The method treats attributes independently in splits; correlated attributes could lead to redundant or misleading splits. A brief discussion (or a simple de-correlation/whitening ablation) would be valuable, especially since attribute correlation is a known challenge.
- Computational aspects: Please report training/testing time and memory compared to DAP, and how costs scale with M, K, and tree depth, given node-wise ROC estimation and fractional propagation.
- Calibration: Since ROCs depend on calibrated scores, note whether Platt scaling is cross-validated without leakage and whether performance is sensitive to calibration quality.
- Few-shot weighting: Provide a brief guideline for choosing λ or a description of the cross-validation protocol (e.g., per-class vs. global) and discuss robustness to class imbalance.
- Baselines: If feasible, include a comparison to label/semantic embedding methods (e.g., Akata et al.) on at least AwA, to position the attribute-centric approach against that family of zero-shot techniques.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a principled, effective solution.
- Strong empirical gains and insightful analyses.
- Method is simple to implement on top of standard RFs and integrates naturally with few-shot training.
- Maintains semantic interpretability and human-specifiability of unseen classes.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on seen-class validation data for ROC estimation may underperform when attribute realizations shift substantially to unseen classes.
- Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability.
- Does not fully address inter-attribute correlations.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a well-executed and timely contribution that materially advances attribute-based zero-shot learning by confronting attribute unreliability head-on, with solid empirical evidence and practical extensions to few-shot settings. The paper is clear, technically sound, and likely to influence subsequent work in zero/few-shot recognition.
