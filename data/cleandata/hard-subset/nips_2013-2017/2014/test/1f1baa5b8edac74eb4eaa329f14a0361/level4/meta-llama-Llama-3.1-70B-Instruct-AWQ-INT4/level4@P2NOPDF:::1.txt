The paper presents a novel approach to bridge the gap between the theoretical foundations and practical applications of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The underlying theory suggests that novel classes can be automatically recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors. However, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging or even more so than learning the object classes themselves.

The authors employ random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating the reliability of attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They further demonstrate how the method can be extended to handle training with a limited number of training examples from test categories. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

**Quality:** The authors provide a theoretically sound and elegant solution to the fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The results on three standard datasets are impressive, showcasing the effectiveness of the approach. The authors also conduct thorough ablation studies, introduce artificial noise, evaluate multiple settings (zero-shot versus few-shot learning), and compare their method with existing literature, demonstrating a comprehensive understanding of the problem.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and methodology. However, a minor clarification is needed in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold t. Since the attribute signatures remain binary, the value of t can be any number between 0 and 1 without affecting equation (2). Furthermore, the explanation of propagating a novel test example down the tree in lines 207-212 is unclear due to the apparent ill-definition of t. Fortunately, this confusion is resolved in later sections.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel application of a random forest framework to a significant problem in zero-shot learning, demonstrating the authors' creativity in tackling this challenge.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning, providing a strong, interesting, and sound contribution to the field. The results on three datasets across various settings are convincing, and the paper deserves publication.
