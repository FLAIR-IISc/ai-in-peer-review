This paper proposes a random forest-based method for zero-shot learning that better accounts for the uncertainty in attribute classifier predictions. This uncertainty is quantified by measuring the performance (e.g., true and false positive rates) of attribute classifiers on a held-out validation set. The paper's core contribution is to incorporate this performance information directly into the information gain computation when learning the random forest. This allows the model to accommodate noisy attribute predictions, for example, by pursuing a given category-attribute signature along multiple paths within a decision tree.

**Quality:** The proposed ideas are well-supported by relevant empirical and quantitative analysis. However, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis. For instance, I would have liked to see which types of attributes and categories are most affected or improved by this method. Additionally, it is unclear how key parameters were selected; for example, the paper does not justify the choice of an 80%-20% split for the training and validation sets.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to read.

**Originality:** While most previous work has addressed the problem of "attribute strength," this paper focuses on the less-explored problem of "attribute reliability." Although many learning algorithms implicitly account for classifier unreliability, this work is novel in its explicit approach to modeling and handling this uncertainty.

**Significance:** The proposed method itself (the random forest) is not new. However, the idea of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability by measuring classifier performance on validation data to improve zero-shot learning is an interesting and thought-provoking contribution for researchers working on attribute-based learning. While the individual components may not be entirely novel, their application and integration to address this specific problem in zero-shot learning are significant.
