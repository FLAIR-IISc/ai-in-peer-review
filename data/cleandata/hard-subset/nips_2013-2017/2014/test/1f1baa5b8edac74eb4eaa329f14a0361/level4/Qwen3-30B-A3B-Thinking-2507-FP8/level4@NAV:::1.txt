

The paper aims to bridge the theoretical and practical aspects of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While theory posits that novel classes can be recognized via pre-trained attribute predictors, practice reveals that training these attribute classifiers can be equally or more challenging than learning object classes directly. The authors employ random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, incorporating attribute detector reliability during training by propagating a validation set through each decision tree. They further demonstrate how the method extends to few-shot learning scenarios with minimal examples from test categories, achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple attribute datasets.  

QUALITY: The authors present a well-executed approach to a core zero-shot learning challenge using random forests. The model is elegant and theoretically grounded. Results on three standard datasets are robust, supported by thorough ablation studies, controlled noise injection, comprehensive evaluation of zero- versus few-shot settings, and rigorous comparisons with prior work.  

CLARITY: The paper is exceptionally well-written. A minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1: the threshold parameter *t* is introduced while attribute signatures remain binary, rendering *t* (0 < *t* < 1) irrelevant to Equation (2). The subsequent explanation of test example propagation (lines 207–212) becomes clearer in later sections, resolving this ambiguity.  

ORIGINALITY: The application of random forests to attribute-based zero-shot learning represents a novel and well-motivated contribution.  

SIGNIFICANCE: The work addresses a foundational challenge in zero-shot learning with significant practical implications.  

The paper is strong, methodologically sound, and compelling. Results across three datasets under diverse settings are convincing. It merits publication.
