The paper addresses a key challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: bridging the gap between theoretical promise and practical performance. While the theory suggests novel classes can be recognized automatically using pre-trained attribute predictors, learning these attribute classifiers can be as difficult, or even more so, than learning the object classes themselves. 

The authors propose a novel random forest approach to train zero-shot models that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. Random forests are trained to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and the training procedure leverages a validation set to model attribute detector reliability, propagating it through each decision tree during training. They further demonstrate extensions to handle few-shot learning scenarios and unreliable class-attribute associations. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

**Quality:** The authors present a well-conceived and technically sound approach to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The random forest framework is elegant and the method is thoroughly evaluated. Results on three standard datasets are strong, and the ablation studies – including the introduction of artificial noise and evaluation across zero- and few-shot settings – are particularly valuable. The comparisons with existing literature are comprehensive.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and clearly organized. A minor point of confusion arose in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold *t*. While the attribute signatures are initially binary, the significance of *t* is not immediately apparent in the context of equation (2). The subsequent sections clarify its role in handling test examples. Overall, the paper is readily understandable and provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** This work represents a novel application of a random forest framework to an important problem in zero-shot learning. The explicit modeling of attribute unreliability and its integration into the training process is a significant contribution.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning, namely the inherent difficulty of learning reliable attribute predictors. The strong results on three datasets, in diverse settings, demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach and suggest it could have a significant impact on the field. The method has the potential to enable more robust and effective zero-shot learning systems for real-world applications.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong, interesting, and technically sound paper. The results are convincing, and the proposed method offers a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. I recommend this paper for publication.
