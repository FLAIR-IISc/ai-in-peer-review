- Proposes a random forest-based zero-shot learning method that explicitly models unreliable attribute predictions using attribute error statistics.  
- Trains trees on class-attribute signature vectors and uses a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate attribute reliability at each node.  
- Extends the approach to few-shot learning and to handle unreliable class attribute descriptions.  
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN, showing consistent improvements over baselines like DAP.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and technically well-explained.  
- Novelty lies in propagating validation data through the tree to model joint attribute error statistics, not just independent attribute probabilities.  
- The claim that prior work ignores attribute unreliability is overstated; methods like DAP address reliability probabilistically (e.g., Platt scaling).  
- Experimental design and ablation studies are appropriate and thorough.  
- Quantitative improvements are modest (roughly 2–10% absolute gains).  
- Few-shot extension is underdeveloped and needs more detail and justification.  
- Handling of validation set size is heuristic and lacks sufficient empirical support.  
- Requirement for additional attribute-labeled validation data reduces practical applicability.  
- Strengths: clear exposition, meaningful improvements, solid ablations, and good related-work context.  
- Weaknesses: overstated novelty, modest gains, heuristic validation handling, and insufficient few-shot development.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — technically sound and a meaningful contribution, but should better contextualize novelty and strengthen few-shot/validation analyses.
