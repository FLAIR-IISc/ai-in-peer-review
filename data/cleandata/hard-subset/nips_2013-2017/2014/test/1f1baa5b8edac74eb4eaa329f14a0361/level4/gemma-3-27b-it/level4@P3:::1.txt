This paper addresses a critical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. While the theoretical promise of zero-shot learning hinges on accurately predicting attributes for novel categories, in practice, learning these attributes can be as difficult as, or even more difficult than, learning the categories themselves. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for this unreliability during training, leveraging statistics about each attribute’s error tendencies to build more robust discriminative models for unseen classes. They further extend the method to handle few-shot learning scenarios and unreliable class-attribute associations, demonstrating strong performance on three standard datasets.

The paper presents a technically sound and elegant solution to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The core idea of incorporating attribute reliability into the random forest training process is well-motivated and effectively implemented. The results on three datasets – AwA, aPY, and SUN – are compelling, demonstrating state-of-the-art or competitive performance. The ablation studies, including experiments with artificially introduced noise and comparisons between zero- and few-shot learning, are well-designed and insightful. The authors thoughtfully compare their method to existing approaches, particularly the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method, and convincingly demonstrate its advantages.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the proposed solution within the broader context of zero-shot learning. The method is explained in a logical and accessible manner, with sufficient detail to allow for replication. While Section 3.2.1 initially introduced a minor point of confusion regarding the threshold *t* and its relevance with binary signatures (where any value between 0 and 1 yields the same result), this was clarified later in the paper. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to include a visual illustration of the fractional propagation of signatures through the decision tree – this could further enhance understanding. 

The application of a random forest framework to this problem is a novel and valuable contribution. While random forests are a well-established technique, adapting them to the specific challenges of zero-shot learning, particularly the need to account for attribute unreliability, demonstrates originality. Furthermore, the extension to few-shot learning and the handling of unreliable class-attribute associations add to the novelty of the approach. The method builds upon relevant work in attribute-based learning [8, 3, 18, 23, 19] and related fields like handling missing data in decision trees [21, 28, 16], while clearly differentiating itself and advancing the state-of-the-art.

The significance of this work is substantial. By addressing the practical limitations of attribute-based zero-shot learning, the authors contribute to the development of more robust and reliable methods for recognizing novel categories with limited or no labeled training data. This is particularly important for applications involving rare categories or rapidly evolving datasets, where collecting labeled data is expensive or impractical. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods suggest that this work is likely to be influential and will encourage further research in this area. The ability to leverage both attribute signatures and limited labeled data (in the few-shot setting) expands the applicability of the method and increases its potential impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong technical contribution:** The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and effectively addresses a core challenge in zero-shot learning.
*   **Compelling experimental results:** The results on three datasets demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive performance, with convincing ablation studies and comparisons to existing methods.
*   **Excellent clarity and presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to understand and appreciate the technical details.
*   **Significant impact:** The work addresses a fundamental problem with real-world implications and has the potential to advance the field of zero-shot learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   While the explanation of the threshold *t* could be slightly improved for initial clarity, this is a minor issue that does not detract significantly from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The reliance on pre-trained attribute classifiers means the performance is still ultimately limited by the quality of these classifiers, although the proposed method mitigates this limitation effectively.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors have successfully addressed a challenging problem with a novel and effective solution, supported by compelling experimental results and a clear, well-written presentation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
