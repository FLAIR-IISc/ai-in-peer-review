Summary
The paper addresses a central limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictions at test time are noisy and biased. The authors propose a random forest framework that explicitly models attribute unreliability when training classifiers for unseen classes. Each unseen class is represented by an attribute signature. Instead of taking these signatures at face value, the method uses attribute receiver operating characteristics (TPR/FPR at varying thresholds) estimated from held-out validation data to fractionally propagate class signatures down decision trees. This results in splits that are both discriminative across unseen classes and robust to expected attribute errors. The approach also accounts for uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with the standard data-driven gain.

Relative to prior work, the method builds on the two-stage attribute paradigm (e.g., DAP [8], signature matching [3,18,30]) but differs by integrating attribute error statistics into the training objective. It is related to decision trees for missing/uncertain features [21,28,16], but adapts the idea to the zero-shot setting where training uses only signatures and validation data, and test-time inputs are attribute predictions. The paper compares against DAP and ablations on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), shows favorable performance under controlled noise and real-world attribute predictors, and demonstrates a few-shot extension that leverages signatures as a prior.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core idea—fractional propagation based on node-specific ROC estimates and corresponding information gain—is well-motivated and carefully formulated. Using node-specific validation subsets to reflect attribute dependencies across splits is a strong design choice. The few-shot extension is coherent and simple, with an interpretable trade-off parameter λ.
- Empirical support: Experiments cover three datasets, controlled-noise studies, ablations (removing signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation), and comparison to DAP and prior published results. Gains are largest where attributes are less reliable, aligning with the motivation.
- Missing analysis/details: 
  - Hyperparameter selection (tree depth, number of candidate splits, λ) is only briefly discussed; more detail on how these were tuned and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the claims.
  - The 80/20 split of attribute-labeled data into validation vs. training is fixed; it would be useful to see performance as a function of this split and the absolute size of validation data.
  - Computational cost and scalability (tree building with per-node ROC estimation; runtime at test) are not reported.
  - Qualitative analysis is limited: examples of learned decision paths, the attributes favored by the method, and representative success/failure cases would improve interpretability.
  - While the paper cites alternatives that do not rely on attributes (e.g., label and feature embeddings [1,4,14,23]), direct empirical comparisons beyond DAP are not always clear in the main text.

Clarity
- The manuscript is well-written, with a clear narrative from motivation to method to extensions. The notation for the soft indicators and ROC-conditioned routing is clear, and the distinction between signatures, validation data, and test inputs is explicit. A minor suggestion is to clarify threshold selection for binary signatures in Sec. 3.2.1 vs. Sec. 3.2.2 (the role of t becomes substantive once unreliability is modeled) and to consolidate references to the supplement where core algorithmic details (pseudocode, signature uncertainty model) are needed to reproduce results.

Originality
- The key contribution—explicitly injecting attribute error statistics into zero-shot classifier training via node-specific fractional routing in random forests—is novel relative to standard attribute-based zero-shot methods (DAP and variants) and to prior work on uncertain feature trees, given the zero-shot setup and ROC-driven split selection. The extension to few-shot learning as a descriptive prior for forests is a neat, low-complexity addition.

Significance
- The method advances the state of the art for attribute-based zero-shot learning on multiple benchmarks, particularly in regimes with noisier attribute classifiers. It also provides a practical path to make attribute-based zero-shot learning more robust rather than abandoning semantic attributes for discovered features. The ideas are likely to be of interest to researchers working on zero-/few-shot learning, attributes, and robust decision trees. Broader impact may extend to other semantic mid-level representations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report sensitivity to the amount and composition of validation data (e.g., 10/90, 30/70; using seen classes most similar to the unseen ones).
- Provide qualitative insights: attribute importance, exemplar decision paths, and confusion analyses highlighting which unseen classes benefit most.
- Include runtime and complexity figures, and discuss scalability to larger attribute vocabularies and more unseen classes.
- Clarify the signature uncertainty model in the main text, not only in the supplement.
- Compare empirically (or discuss more thoroughly) against label/feature embedding baselines that do not require attributes at test time, to contextualize the trade-offs.
- Discuss calibration of posterior outputs and whether the forests yield well-calibrated class probabilities.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that directly addresses a known limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Solid empirical gains across datasets, with informative ablations and controlled-noise studies.
- Principled few-shot extension that yields additional improvements.
- Method is simple to implement on top of standard random forests and uses only attribute-labeled data plus signatures.

Cons
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and validation split design.
- Sparse qualitative analysis; interpretability of learned models could be improved.
- Computational considerations and scalability not discussed.
- Empirical comparisons beyond DAP are not consistently presented in the main text.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong and timely contribution that offers a principled and effective way to make attribute-based zero-shot learning robust to real attribute prediction errors. Despite some missing analyses and limited qualitative insight, the methodological clarity and empirical gains justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance, with the above suggestions to improve clarity, analysis, and contextualization.
