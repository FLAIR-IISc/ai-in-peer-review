The paper strives to bridge the gap between the theory and practice of attribute-based zero-shot learning. While the theory suggests novel classes can be automatically recognized using pre-trained attribute predictors, in practice, learning these attribute classifiers can be as challenging – or even more so – than learning the object classes themselves. 
The authors train random forests to predict unseen classes from attribute vectors, and their training procedure incorporates the reliability of the attribute detectors by propagating a validation set through each decision tree during training. They further demonstrate how the method can be extended to handle training with a few examples from test categories. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on several attribute datasets.

**QUALITY:** The authors effectively address this fundamental zero-shot learning problem using a random forest framework. The model is elegant and theoretically sound, and the results on three standard datasets are strong. The paper includes a thorough evaluation, with well-designed ablation studies, the introduction of artificial noise, assessment across zero- versus few-shot learning settings, and clear comparisons to existing literature.

**CLARITY:** The paper is very well written. However, Section 3.2.1 introduces a minor point of confusion: given that the attribute signatures remain binary, the threshold *t* can take on any value between 0 and 1 without affecting equation (2). Consequently, the method by which a novel test example is propagated down the tree in lines 207-212 is initially unclear, as *t* appears to be ill-defined at that stage. This is clarified in subsequent sections.

**ORIGINALITY:** This work represents a compelling application of a random forest framework to an important problem.

**SIGNIFICANCE:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The strength, interest, and soundness of the approach are demonstrated by convincing results obtained on three datasets in a variety of settings. This paper deserves to be published.
