The paper addresses a critical challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the discrepancy between theoretical promise and practical performance stemming from unreliable attribute predictions. The core idea is to move beyond treating attribute predictions as ground truth, and instead explicitly model their uncertainty during training. A random forest framework is employed, with a novel training procedure that propagates validation data through decision trees to account for attribute classifier error tendencies. The method extends naturally to the few-shot setting, and demonstrates strong performance on multiple benchmark datasets.

QUALITY: The authors present a technically sound and elegant approach to a fundamental problem in zero-shot learning. The proposed random forest framework is well-motivated and the theoretical underpinnings are clear. Comprehensive experimental results on three standard datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the method. The ablation studies, including the controlled noise experiments and comparisons to existing techniques in both zero-shot and few-shot scenarios, are well-designed and insightful. 

CLARITY: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. A minor point of confusion arises in Section 3.2.1 regarding the introduction of the threshold *t* when the attribute signatures are initially binary; the implication is that *t* has no effect given the binary representation and the entropy calculation in Equation (2). The subsequent explanation of how test examples are propagated clarifies this point. Overall, the paper adequately informs the reader and provides sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results.

ORIGINALITY: This work represents a novel application of random forests to address a key limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning. The approach of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during training, and propagating validation data to inform the decision tree construction, is a significant contribution.

SIGNIFICANCE: The paper tackles a highly relevant and important problem in the field of visual recognition. The ability to learn new categories with minimal or no labeled examples is crucial for applications involving rare or dynamically defined classes. The demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly on datasets with less reliable attribute classifiers, highlight the practical significance of this work. The method has the potential to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers in the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
*   Addresses a fundamental limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Comprehensive ablation studies and comparisons to existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The initial explanation of the threshold *t* in Section 3.2.1 could be clarified.
*   While strong, the performance gains are not dramatic in all scenarios.



This paper is strong, interesting, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. The results are convincing and the work is clearly deserving of publication.
