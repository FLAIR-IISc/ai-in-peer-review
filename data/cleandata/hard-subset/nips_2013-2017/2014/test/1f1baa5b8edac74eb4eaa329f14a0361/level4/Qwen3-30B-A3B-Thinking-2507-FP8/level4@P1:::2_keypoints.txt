- Proposes a novel random-forest zero-shot method that models attribute-prediction unreliability using ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) from a validation set.  
- Key mechanism: fractionally propagate unseen-class attribute signatures through trees according to classifier error tendencies, allowing signatures to follow multiple paths.  
- Extends the approach to model signature uncertainty and to few-shot learning settings.  
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN, showing consistent improvements over DAP and SIGNATURE RF, especially when attribute classifiers are unreliable.  
- Strong empirical validation with comprehensive experiments and detailed ablation studies isolating ROC-based propagation and signature-uncertainty contributions.  
- Demonstrates large gains on specific categories (e.g., +9.4% on whale seal aPY, +10.2% on giant panda AwA) and robustness when attribute training data is reduced.  
- Ablations show both ROC-based propagation and signature uncertainty contribute, with the latter giving a modest consistent boost.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis identifying which attributes or categories drive gains or failures (e.g., "striped" vs "furry").  
- Uses an 80/20 train/validation split for attribute classifiers but provides no justification or sensitivity analysis for this choice.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly structured, and reproducible; figures effectively support claims.  
- Clearly distinguishes attribute strength from attribute reliability; suggests adding a brief glossary for terms like "signature uncertainty."  
- Original contribution: first to explicitly incorporate ROC statistics during zero-shot classifier training to model attribute reliability.  
- Clarifies distinction from missing-value approaches and innovates by building trees from attribute signatures while propagating validation data.  
- Significant practical impact: makes zero-shot learning robust to real-world imperfect attribute predictors and attains state-of-the-art on discovered attributes.  
- Overall assessment: sound methodology, thorough experiments, minor limitations (qualitative analysis, split justification), recommended for acceptance.
