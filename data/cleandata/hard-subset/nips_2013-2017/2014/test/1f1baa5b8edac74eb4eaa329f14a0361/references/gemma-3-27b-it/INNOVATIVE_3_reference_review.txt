This paper tackles a critical, and often overlooked, issue in zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. While attribute-based ZSL is theoretically elegant, its practical performance is hampered by noisy attributes. The authors boldly propose to *explicitly* model this unreliability within a random forest framework, propagating uncertainty during training and leveraging ROC curves to guide split selection. This is a genuinely novel idea, moving beyond simply assuming perfect attribute predictions, and represents a significant departure from the typical two-stage ZSL pipeline. The extension to few-shot learning is a natural and well-motivated addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of incorporating attribute unreliability into the learning process is highly original. Most ZSL work implicitly assumes perfect attribute predictions, making this a significant contribution.
*   **Bold Approach:** The authors aren't afraid to challenge the conventional wisdom in ZSL and propose a fundamentally different approach.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The use of ROC curves and fractional tuple propagation within the random forest framework is a clever and theoretically sound way to model uncertainty.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The results on three datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines (including the widely-used DAP method), showcasing the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation studies clearly highlight the contributions of each component of the proposed method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The explanation of how validation data is used is particularly strong.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:**  While not a major concern, the process of estimating ROC curves and propagating validation data adds computational overhead. The paper doesn't delve deeply into the scalability of this approach, though the experiments suggest it's manageable.
*   **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:**  While the ablation studies are informative, a deeper analysis of *why* the method succeeds or fails in specific scenarios would be valuable. Are there specific attributes or classes where the approach struggles?
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The choice of the weighting parameter λ in the few-shot extension may require careful tuning.  More discussion on the robustness of the method to different hyperparameter settings would be useful.
*   **Validation Data Assumption:** The method relies on having access to attribute-labeled validation data. While this is a reasonable assumption, it's worth acknowledging that obtaining such data might be challenging in some scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a truly novel and promising approach to address a fundamental limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The methodology is well-motivated, sound, and clearly explained.
*   The extension to few-shot learning further strengthens the contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity could be a concern for very large-scale applications.
*   The lack of a detailed analysis of failure cases leaves some questions unanswered.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a significant new idea to the field of zero-shot learning. While not without minor flaws, the novelty, boldness, and empirical validation outweigh any weaknesses. The authors have convincingly demonstrated that explicitly modeling attribute unreliability is crucial for achieving practical zero-shot learning performance. I believe this work has the potential to significantly impact the field, and I **strongly recommend acceptance**.  I am willing to overlook the minor methodological limitations given the substantial conceptual advance. The work is a clear step forward and deserves to be presented at NIPS.
