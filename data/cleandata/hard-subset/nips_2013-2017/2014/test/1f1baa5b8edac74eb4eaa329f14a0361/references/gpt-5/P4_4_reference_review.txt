Summary
This paper addresses zero-shot recognition using attributes when attribute classifiers are unreliable. The core idea is to train random forests directly on class-attribute signatures (one-vs-all), but to account for the true/false positive behavior of attribute classifiers during training. Specifically, it uses attribute-labeled validation data to estimate per-attribute ROC statistics locally at each tree node; class signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes according to these ROC-derived probabilities. Information gain is computed on fractional counts, encouraging splits that are both discriminative and predictable given attribute noise. The method is further extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations (signature noise) and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-based and data-based information gains with a tunable weight λ.

The work relates to two-stage attribute-based zero-shot methods such as DAP [8], similarity-to-signature approaches [3, 18, 30], and to methods that exploit hierarchies/embeddings [1, 4, 13, 14, 23]. Unlike prior attribute-based zero-shot methods, it explicitly models attribute unreliability when learning the unseen-class classifiers. It is also related to decision-tree methods for missing/probabilistic features [21, 28, 16], but differs in that training uses class signatures while test-time inputs are attribute predictions, and it propagates both signatures and attribute-labeled data to capture dependencies and enable few-shot learning.

Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline. Controlled noise studies demonstrate robustness improvements when attribute noise differs across attributes. Ablations indicate contributions from both ROC propagation and signature-uncertainty modeling. A few-shot extension yields further improvements with appropriate λ.

Quality
- Soundness: The fractional propagation based on node-specific ROC estimates is a principled way to align training with expected test-time noise. The redefined information gain on fractional counts is appropriate. Using per-node validation subsets to compute ROC ties the learned operating points to the decision context and captures some attribute dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Assumptions: The main assumption is that ROC statistics estimated on seen classes transfer to unseen classes. This is reasonable in many cases but can fail under domain shift of attribute appearance. The paper acknowledges this and shows an “upper bound” using true unseen ROC.
- Empirical support: Results cover three datasets with many attributes, include ablations, and a controlled-noise study. Few-shot experiments are sensible and show how λ trades off prior vs data. However, significance testing is not reported, and generalized zero-shot (seen+unseen at test) is not evaluated.
- Clarity issue: There is an inconsistency about whether attribute classifiers are trained with 80% or 100% of attribute-labeled data. Section 4 states 20% is reserved for ROC validation but then “pooled” to train the final attribute classifiers (implying 100%); later it claims the method uses only 80%. This should be clarified.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The training and inference procedures are described clearly, and connections to related work are appropriate. Suggestions for clarity:
- Provide explicit pseudocode (currently relegated to supp.) and a brief complexity analysis (cost per node to compute ROC over attributes and thresholds).
- Clarify threshold sampling (#m, #t), and how posteriors from one-vs-all forests are calibrated when comparing across K classes.
- Move more detail on signature-uncertainty modeling from supp. into the main text (the noise model and its effect).

Originality
Modeling attribute unreliability during training via node-specific ROC-guided fractional propagation within random forests is novel relative to prior zero-shot methods that take attribute predictions at face value. Drawing on ideas from decision trees with uncertain/missing values in this zero-shot setup is an original and well-motivated adaptation. The few-shot combination via a signature-informed prior for split selection is a straightforward but useful extension.

Significance
The gains over DAP across three datasets and under controlled noise support practical impact for attribute-based zero-shot learning, especially where attribute detectors are weak. The approach is simple to implement, compatible with nameable and discovered attributes, and extends to few-shot, which increases potential adoption. Limitations—dependence on attribute-labeled validation data and closed-world evaluation—temper scope but do not negate the contribution.

Pros
- Addresses a central bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning: unreliable attributes.
- Principled use of ROC statistics to align training with test-time noise; per-node estimation captures dependency structure induced by splits.
- Strong empirical results with ablations and noise analyses across three datasets.
- Works with both named and discovered attributes; extends cleanly to few-shot with a tunable prior.
- Method is modular and likely efficient in practice.

Cons
- Assumes ROC statistics from seen classes transfer to unseen classes; no analysis of failure modes under domain shift or attribute co-occurrence shifts.
- Closed-world zero-shot only; no generalized zero-shot evaluation.
- Calibration across one-vs-all forests is not discussed; potential bias without normalization.
- Computational and memory complexity not analyzed; cost of per-node ROC estimation may grow with M and tree depth.
- Inconsistency about training data split for attribute classifiers; needs clarification.
- Signature-uncertainty model is under-specified in the main paper.
- Comparisons focus on DAP and ablative baselines; more recent embedding approaches are cited but not compared experimentally.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the attribute training/validation split and ensure fair, consistent use across methods.
- Report runtime and complexity; discuss scalability in M, K, and depth.
- Evaluate generalized zero-shot (seen+unseen at test) and report calibration strategy for combining one-vs-all posteriors.
- Analyze sensitivity to mismatch between DV and unseen-class attribute distributions; e.g., stratify DV by proximity of signatures to unseen classes or report robustness under synthetic domain shifts.
- Bring key details of signature-uncertainty modeling into the main text; ablate its parameters.
- Add significance testing and per-class breakdowns to complement means and SEs.

Overall, this is a solid, technically sound contribution that advances attribute-based zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability and demonstrates clear empirical benefits.
