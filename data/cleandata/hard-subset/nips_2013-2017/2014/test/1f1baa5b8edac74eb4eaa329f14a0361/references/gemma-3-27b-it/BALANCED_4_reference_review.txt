This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning, specifically focusing on the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest-based approach that incorporates the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers during training, allowing the model to account for and mitigate the impact of inaccurate attribute predictions. The method is further extended to the few-shot learning setting. The paper is well-motivated, presenting a clear articulation of the challenges with existing attribute-based zero-shot learning approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles a crucial limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: the inherent noise in attribute predictions. This is a significant practical issue often overlooked in prior work.
* **Novelty:** The proposed method of incorporating attribute classifier ROCs into the random forest training process is a novel and insightful approach.  The idea of propagating training instances proportionally to uncertainty is particularly clever.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), comparing against strong baselines, including DAP and ablated versions of their own method. The controlled noise experiments are particularly convincing.
* **Few-Shot Extension:** The extension to the few-shot setting provides further practical relevance and demonstrates the flexibility of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The connection to related work is appropriate and helps contextualize the contribution.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The results demonstrate a consistent improvement over existing methods, particularly in scenarios with unreliable attributes.  The gains on SUN and aPY, where attributes are less reliable, are particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the core idea is clear, the implementation details (especially regarding the recursive propagation of validation data and handling of signature uncertainty) can be a bit dense and require careful reading.  More illustrative diagrams would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions cross-validation for choosing λ in the few-shot setting, but doesn’t provide extensive details about the parameter tuning process for other hyperparameters (e.g., tree depth). More discussion of sensitivity to these parameters would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:**  The method appears computationally more expensive than simpler baselines like DAP, due to the need for ROC estimation and the more complex training process. A discussion of the computational overhead would be valuable.
* **Validation Data Dependence:** The performance seems reliant on having a representative validation dataset for accurately estimating ROC curves. The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate the impact of the size and quality of this validation set.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, a more detailed error analysis could provide further insights into *where* the method still struggles and *why*.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in zero-shot learning.
* The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates compelling results on multiple datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology.
* The few-shot extension adds to the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method is relatively complex and may be difficult to implement and scale.
* The dependence on a representative validation dataset for ROC estimation could be a limitation.
* While the results are strong, a more detailed error analysis could provide further insights.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors identify a key limitation of existing methods and propose a novel and effective solution. The thorough experimental evaluation and compelling results support their claims. While the method is somewhat complex and has a dependence on validation data, the benefits outweigh these drawbacks.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Clarify Implementation Details:**  Provide more illustrative diagrams and detailed explanations of the recursive propagation of validation data and the handling of signature uncertainty.
* **Discuss Parameter Sensitivity:**  Provide more details about the parameter tuning process and discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.
* **Analyze Computational Cost:**  Discuss the computational overhead of the proposed method compared to simpler baselines.
* **Investigate Validation Data Impact:**  Conduct experiments to assess the impact of the size and quality of the validation dataset on performance.
* **Perform Detailed Error Analysis:**  Analyze the types of errors the method makes and identify potential areas for further improvement.
