Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition via attributes, focusing on the key practical obstacle: attribute predictors are unreliable. The authors propose a zero-shot random forest that is trained on class-attribute signatures (not images). Crucially, during tree construction, each class signature is fractionally propagated down candidate splits according to attribute ROC statistics estimated from attribute-labeled validation data. This selects node tests that are both discriminative among classes and likely to be robust to attribute prediction errors at test time. The method also models uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends naturally to a few-shot setting by combining signature-based and data-based information gains with a weighting parameter.

This departs from two-stage approaches like DAP (Direct Attribute Prediction) that take attribute predictions at face value, and from methods discovering non-semantic mid-level features. It is related to decision trees for missing/uncertain attributes (fractional tuples, fuzzy decision trees), but adapts these ideas to the zero-shot scenario where training instances are signatures and the test-time inputs are attribute predictions, using node-specific ROC estimates to account for attribute dependencies.

Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show sizable accuracy gains over DAP and a signature-only forest baseline, with controlled noise studies, ablations (signature uncertainty and ROC propagation), and a few-shot extension demonstrating benefits of the attribute prior.

Strengths
- Novelty and idea: Training forests on signatures while explicitly injecting attribute ROC statistics into split selection is a simple, elegant, and genuinely new take on zero-shot learning. It targets the central impediment (unreliable attributes) rather than adding more features or post-hoc calibration.
- Empirical evidence: Consistent gains over DAP across three datasets, larger improvements where attributes are less reliable. Controlled noise experiments convincingly isolate the effect. Ablations illuminate which components matter. Few-shot extension is effective and intuitive.
- Generality: Works with both named and discovered (pseudo-)attributes. The framework is modular (can plug in any attribute predictor).
- Interpretability and practicality: Forests select reliable attributes/thresholds; the method requires no images from unseen classes and uses only a held-out attribute-labeled set.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data: Performance hinges on how well DV’s error statistics transfer to unseen classes. The paper acknowledges this and shows an upper bound with “true” ROC, but does not quantify sensitivity to domain shift or size/quality of DV.
- Comparisons: While DAP is a strong baseline in the attribute camp, the paper would be stronger with comparisons to zero-shot label embeddings and deep visual-semantic methods (e.g., DeViSE) on overlapping splits, as well as hierarchy/metric-transfer baselines.
- Limited theory: No guarantee or analysis of when node-specific ROC propagation improves over global estimates, or bounds on degradation under calibration shift.
- Scalability and complexity: Some discussion is warranted on computational cost (evaluating many (m, t) per node with node-specific ROC), memory/speed vs. DAP, and behavior for very large M, K.
- Attribute correlation: Although node-specific DV(n) partly captures dependencies, splits are axis-aligned and single-attribute. Oblique or joint tests could better exploit correlated attributes.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The fractional propagation using ROC is well-motivated, and the information gain modifications are appropriate. The experimental protocol is fair and includes ablations and noise analyses. However, the approach assumes calibrated attribute probabilities (Platt scaling) and transferability of ROC curves; more diagnostics (e.g., calibration plots, DV size/shift studies) would strengthen the case. Details of the signature uncertainty model are deferred to the supplement; a concise description in the main paper would help.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The build-up from basic signature RF to ROC-aware propagation and few-shot is easy to follow. Equations are explicit, and datasets/splits are described. It would help to move key pseudocode and the signature uncertainty term from the supplement into the main text and to clarify candidate threshold sampling and stopping criteria. A brief complexity analysis would aid readers.

Originality
High. While related to decision trees with uncertain inputs, the idea of training zero-shot forests on class signatures and using node-specific attribute ROC to shape split decisions is new and insightful in this context. The few-shot “descriptive prior” view of random forests is also a nice conceptual contribution.

Significance
Moderate to high. Addressing attribute unreliability is central to making attribute-based zero-shot practical. The method yields tangible improvements on standard datasets and should influence future zero/few-shot designs that explicitly model mid-level prediction errors. The idea is broadly applicable beyond vision wherever semantic attributes are used.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and addresses a fundamental pain point in zero-shot learning.
- Strong empirical gains over a widely used baseline across multiple datasets.
- Clear ablations and controlled noise studies support the claims.
- Simple, modular, and practical; extends naturally to few-shot.

Cons for acceptance
- Relies on attribute-labeled validation data with potentially mismatched distribution to unseen classes; limited analysis of robustness to such shift.
- Missing comparisons to recent embedding-based zero-shot methods.
- Limited discussion of scalability and computational overhead.
- Some important details relegated to the supplement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to label/deep embedding zero-shot methods on AwA/aPY where possible.
- Analyze sensitivity to DV size/composition and to calibration shift between seen and unseen classes; consider lightweight domain adaptation of ROC.
- Report runtime and memory vs. DAP and basic RF; discuss scaling with M and K.
- Explore oblique or multi-attribute node tests to better handle correlated attributes.
- Include the signature uncertainty formulation and pseudocode in the main paper.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a fresh, principled, and effective way to make attribute-based zero-shot robust, with clear empirical benefits and potential impact.

Confidence
High (knowledgeable in zero/few-shot and tree-based models).
