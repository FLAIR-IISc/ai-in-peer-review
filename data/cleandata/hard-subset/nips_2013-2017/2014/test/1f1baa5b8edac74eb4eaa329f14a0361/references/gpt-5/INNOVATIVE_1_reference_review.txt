Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot learning with semantic attributes by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute predictors. Rather than treating attribute predictions as ground truth at test time, the authors train a random forest on class-attribute signatures while injecting attribute classifier ROC statistics estimated on held-out attribute-labeled data. During training, each class signature is fractionally propagated down the tree according to the true/false positive rates at candidate thresholds, leading the split selection to favor attributes and operating points that will be both discriminative and reliably predicted. The method also accounts for uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-driven information gain with standard example-driven gain via a tunable prior weight. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with controlled-noise studies illustrating robustness and an extension to discovered pseudo-attributes.

Relation to prior work: It builds on attribute-based zero-shot methods (DAP; Farhadi et al.; Palatucci et al.) and connects to decision tree handling of missing/uncertain features (fractional tuples, fuzzy trees). It differs by using ROCs to shape tree training with fractional propagation of signatures, and by offering a simple few-shot prior within forests.

Strengths
- Novel and elegant idea: using per-attribute ROC curves to probabilistically route class signatures during tree construction directly addresses attribute unreliability.
- Clear conceptual link between training-time choices and test-time error characteristics; the method prefers learnable attributes and appropriate operating points.
- Simple, practical, and modular; compatible with both named and discovered attributes, and extends cleanly to few-shot learning.
- Empirical gains across three datasets and in controlled-noise regimes; especially strong where attributes are harder to learn (aPY, SUN).
- Good discussion of related work and distinctions; acknowledges limitations and future directions (correlations, multi-label trees).

Weaknesses
- Potential leakage/ambiguity around ROC estimation: the paper says 20% is reserved as validation and then “pooled” to train final attribute classifiers; it is unclear whether ROC is computed on data also used to fit the final attribute SVMs, which could bias the ROC estimates.
- The signature uncertainty component is deferred to the supplement; more detail in the main paper would aid reproducibility and understanding.
- Scalability concerns: one-versus-all forests per unseen class and per-node ROC estimation may be costly for large K; runtime/memory complexity is not reported.
- Dependency modeling is limited to node-conditional ROCs; no explicit modeling of attribute correlations or calibration shift between seen and unseen classes.
- Comparisons could include more zero-shot baselines beyond DAP (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE/ALE/SJE) to situate gains more broadly.

Quality
- Technically sound and carefully motivated. The fractional propagation via node-specific ROC estimates is coherent, and the information gain derivations are appropriate. However, stronger evidence of strict separation between ROC estimation and attribute SVM training would solidify validity. Reporting sensitivity to the amount of ROC data, and confidence intervals on TP/FP estimates at deeper nodes, would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The core idea, training and testing protocols, and ablations are clear. Including pseudocode in the main text and a more explicit description of the signature uncertainty term would help. Clarify the handling of validation data, exact training pipeline for attribute SVMs vs. ROC estimation, and smoothing used when node-level positives/negatives are sparse.

Originality
- High. While related to handling uncertainty in trees and zero-shot via attributes, the ROC-driven fractional propagation of class signatures in a zero-shot RF is, to my knowledge, novel. The few-shot integration as a prior in the forest is also a neat, low-friction contribution.

Significance
- High practical significance for attribute-based zero-shot learning, a setting where attribute unreliability is a central pain point. The approach is general and could be used in other mid-level semantic settings. The measured improvements over strong baselines suggest that the idea will be useful to practitioners.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the training/validation protocol: Are ROCs computed using attribute SVMs trained without the validation split? If not, please re-run with a strict hold-out or cross-fitting to avoid optimistic ROCs.
- Report computational cost (training and inference) and how it scales with M and K. Any impact of tree depth and (#m, #t) candidates on runtime vs. accuracy?
- Provide sensitivity analysis to the size/composition of the ROC validation set; explore domain shift mitigation (e.g., importance weighting) when unseen classes differ from seen.
- Add comparisons to label embedding approaches (e.g., ALE, SJE, DeViSE) on at least AwA to contextualize gains beyond DAP.
- Include per-attribute analyses showing which attributes and thresholds are selected, and how node-specific conditioning affects TP/FP estimates.
- Consider simple smoothing (e.g., Laplace) for node-level ROC estimates when counts are small, and report the effect.
- Discuss a multiclass forest variant (single forest across K) to improve scalability and possibly capture inter-class structure.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a core limitation of attribute-based zero-shot with a principled, practical method.
- Clear, substantial empirical gains and robust behavior under controlled noise.
- Conceptually novel and likely to inspire follow-up work; broadly applicable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguity around ROC estimation protocol and potential optimistic bias.
- Limited comparisons to recent embedding-based zero-shot methods.
- Scalability/runtime analysis is missing; some technical details deferred to the supplement.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The paper proposes a simple, novel, and effective idea that advances zero-shot learning in a meaningful way. Minor experimental clarifications and broader baselines would strengthen it further, but the conceptual contribution and empirical evidence meet the bar. Confidence: medium-high.
