Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition when mid-level semantic attributes are predicted unreliably. The core contribution is a zero-shot random forest (RF) that is trained not on image features but on class attribute signatures, while explicitly integrating the empirical receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers. At each node, the method fractionally propagates each class signature to children based on the attribute classifier’s TP/FP rates estimated from held-out, attribute-labeled validation data, and selects splits by an information gain defined over these fractional counts. This biases the trees toward splits that remain discriminative under realistic attribute noise. The framework is extended to handle uncertainty in class–attribute associations (signature noise) and to the few-shot regime by combining signature-based information gain with example-based gain via a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with larger gains when attributes are less reliable. Ablations demonstrate the roles of ROC-aware propagation and signature uncertainty.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated with respect to attribute-based zero-shot methods (DAP, attribute signature matching, topic models) and alternative transfer strategies (hierarchical/embedding-based approaches like DeViSE/label embeddings). Unlike most attribute-based methods that assume calibrated attribute posteriors and ignore error structure, this work directly incorporates attribute unreliability into training. The use of fractional propagation is related to decision trees for missing or uncertain inputs, but adapted to a distinct training/testing mismatch: training on signatures and testing on attribute predictions. The paper also considers discovered (pseudo)attributes and shows competitive performance, indicating generality beyond hand-designed vocabularies.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and practicality: A simple, general mechanism to integrate attribute ROC statistics into the training objective, addressing a core weakness of attribute-based zero-shot models.
- Methodological clarity: The fractional propagation and modified information gain are well-defined, with per-node ROC estimation to capture dependency induced by upstream splits.
- Empirical validation: Strong and consistent gains over DAP and a strong RF baseline across three datasets; controlled noise experiments substantiate the intended behavior; ablations isolate components; few-shot extension shows meaningful benefits and sensible dependence on λ.
- Generality: Applicability to continuous/discovered attributes and few-shot settings; the idea is not tied to specific features or SVMs.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Distribution shift and ROC estimation: Attribute ROC is estimated on seen classes and assumed to transfer to unseen classes. The paper acknowledges this with an upper bound using “true ROC” and notes the gap. While per-node DV propagation captures some conditional dependencies, the core assumption that P(âm > t | y(m), context) transfers across classes is untested; additional analysis on sensitivity to mismatch would strengthen the claims.
- Independence and calibration: The approach relies on Platt-calibrated SVM scores and thresholds. Miscalibration and inter-attribute correlations may undermine ROC transfer; the method partially addresses correlations via node-specific DV, but residual correlations remain unmodeled. A comparison with methods that jointly re-calibrate attributes (e.g., isotonic regression, temperature scaling) would be informative.
- Evaluation breadth: Baselines omit modern embedding-based zero-shot methods that can be instantiated with attributes (e.g., SJE/ALE/ESZSL-style label embeddings) and could incorporate attribute reliability via learned per-attribute weights. Since DeViSE and label embedding are cited, a comparison would better position the contribution.
- Scalability: Training one RF per unseen class (one-vs-all) may not scale to large K; the paper does not discuss computational or memory complexity, nor alternatives (e.g., K-way forests, shared trees with multi-label leaves).
- Validation set usage: It is unclear whether the DV used for ROC estimation is also used to train the attribute classifiers (the text suggests pooling). If so, ROC estimates may be optimistic. Please clarify whether ROC is computed on data strictly held out from attribute classifier training.
- Signature uncertainty details are deferred to the supplement; the main paper would benefit from at least a concise description of the noise model and hyperparameters.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the fractional split criterion is principled and aligns with the test-time noise model. Claims are supported by experiments, though broader baselines and clarifications on ROC estimation are needed. Few-shot linear combination is heuristic but effective.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. The training/testing mismatch is explained well. Provide more detail in the main text on signature uncertainty and the exact procedure for ROC estimation/calibration. Add pseudo-code or a brief algorithm box in the main paper if space allows.

Originality
- The core idea—training zero-shot RFs on signatures while injecting attribute ROC—is novel relative to prior attribute-based zero-shot and decision tree literature, which typically does not handle this specific training/testing mismatch.

Significance
- Modeling attribute unreliability directly improves a widely used zero-shot paradigm. Results suggest practitioners can leverage attributes even when detectors are weak. The idea could influence other semantic mid-level pipelines.

Questions for the authors
- Is DV disjoint from the data used to fit the attribute classifiers? If not, how much optimism does this introduce? Can you report results with strict hold-out ROC estimation?
- How sensitive are results to miscalibration of âm? Have you tried per-attribute calibration on DV or reweighting via ROC convex hull?
- Can you report training and inference time vs. K and M? Would a single multi-class forest be competitive?
- How is signature uncertainty modeled (distributional form, parameters)? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Could you add a label-embedding baseline using the same attributes?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a central, practical weakness in attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Simple, elegant modification to RF training with clear empirical benefits.
- Solid experimental methodology with meaningful ablations and few-shot extension.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited comparison to strong non-attribute zero-shot baselines that could be adapted to attributes.
- Some methodological ambiguity regarding ROC estimation and potential optimism.
- Scalability and calibration issues not thoroughly analyzed.

Recommendation
Lean towards accept. The paper introduces a principled and practically useful enhancement to attribute-based zero-shot learning, with solid empirical evidence. Addressing the clarity and evaluation concerns would further strengthen the submission.
