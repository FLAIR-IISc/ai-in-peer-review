Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition with attribute-based descriptions by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. It proposes a random forest trained not on images but on class attribute signatures, with a key innovation: at each node, class signatures are propagated fractionally to children according to node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) of the attribute classifiers estimated from attribute-labeled validation data. This biases split selection toward attributes and thresholds that are both discriminative and reliably predictable. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to few-shot learning via a convex combination of signature-driven information gain and standard data-driven information gain. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show substantial gains over DAP and a signature-only forest; controlled-noise studies and ablations support the design. The method also performs competitively with discovered attributes.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on attribute-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DAP, signature matching) but differs by integrating attribute reliability into the learning objective. It connects to decision trees with missing/uncertain values (fractional tuples, fuzzy trees) yet adapts them to the zero-shot setting where training instances are class signatures and test-time inputs are attribute predictions. It complements non-attribute zero-shot approaches (hierarchies, embeddings) by preserving semantic controllability via attributes. The paper cites relevant lines of work and explains distinctions.

Quality
Technical soundness: The training objective is well defined. Fractional propagation using ROC-derived probabilities is principled and leads to a clear, optimized information gain criterion that downweights unlearnable attributes and favors robust operating points. The node-specific ROC estimation using recursively filtered validation data is an important detail that captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by prior splits.

Empirical support: Results cover three datasets with many attributes, include controlled-noise experiments, multiple ablations (removing signature uncertainty and node-wise ROC propagation), and comparisons to standard baselines (DAP, signature RF). Gains are largest where attribute classifiers are weaker (SUN, aPY), aligning with the method’s intent. Few-shot results show the prior’s utility diminishes sensibly as labeled data increases.

Concerns:
- Domain mismatch in ROC estimation: The method assumes attribute error statistics on seen classes approximate those on unseen classes. The authors acknowledge this and show an upper bound with “true ROC.” It would help to quantify the sensitivity to validation set composition and size.
- Potential optimism in ROC estimates: The pipeline description suggests using 20% data for ROC estimation and then pooling it back to train final attribute classifiers. This introduces a mismatch between the ROC-estimated operating characteristics and the final classifier actually used at test time; clarifying the precise sequence (train attribute SVMs on 80% vs. 100% when computing ROCs and training forests) would improve rigor.
- Baselines: While DAP is a strong baseline, additional comparisons to more recent label-embedding methods (e.g., ALE) or DeViSE-like approaches (not necessarily with deep features if unavailable) would strengthen the claim of state of the art on AwA, aPY, and SUN.
- Scalability: The one-vs-all forest per unseen class may be costly when K is large. Complexity and memory/runtime analysis would be helpful.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well organized, and careful with notation. The role of DV and node-specific ROC estimations is explained well, and the intuition behind fractional propagation is compelling. Pseudocode and some derivations are deferred to the supplement; given the centrality of the training algorithm, including a concise algorithm box in the main text would aid reproducibility. Clarify the threshold sampling strategy and how (#m,#t) are chosen, as well as the stopping criteria and their impact.

Originality
The core idea—training zero-shot classifiers via random forests on signatures with node-wise ROC-driven fractional propagation—is novel in this domain. While related to decision trees with uncertain inputs, the zero-shot formulation and the way validation data is used to model test-time attribute prediction behavior are new and non-trivial. The few-shot extension as a descriptive prior in a combined information gain is natural but still a useful contribution.

Significance
Zero-shot learning with attributes remains important, yet fragile due to attribute noise. The method directly targets this pain point and delivers measurable, consistent improvements across multiple datasets and conditions. The idea is likely to be adopted or adapted by others working with semantic mid-level representations (attributes, poselets, mid-level patches). The demonstrated gains over a widely used baseline (DAP) suggest practical impact. The approach also integrates seamlessly into few-shot settings, increasing its scope.

Pros
- Clear, principled treatment of attribute unreliability via ROC-based fractional propagation.
- Strong empirical gains across datasets, with ablations and controlled-noise studies supporting the design.
- Captures inter-attribute dependencies via node-specific ROC estimation.
- Natural extension to few-shot learning; applicable to both named and discovered attributes.

Cons
- Assumes transferability of attribute ROC statistics from seen to unseen classes; sensitivity analysis could be deeper.
- Some ambiguity in how validation data is used vis-à-vis final attribute classifier training.
- Limited baseline coverage beyond DAP; scaling behavior and computational cost are not fully analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the exact training pipeline for attribute SVMs and ROC estimation to eliminate potential circularity or mismatch.
- Add comparisons to more recent embedding-based zero-shot methods, even with non-deep features if needed.
- Provide runtime/memory complexity and scaling results for larger K and M.
- Explore robustness of ROC estimates with smaller DV or with domain-shifted DV; consider Bayesian smoothing of ROC estimates at deeper nodes.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is technically sound, addresses a well-known practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a novel and well-motivated method, and demonstrates clear empirical benefits with solid ablations. My confidence is high.
