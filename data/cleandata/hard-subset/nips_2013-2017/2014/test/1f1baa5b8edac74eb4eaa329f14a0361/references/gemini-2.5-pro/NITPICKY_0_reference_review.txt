**Summary**

This paper addresses a critical and well-known problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The authors argue that existing methods, which typically take attribute predictions at face value, are brittle. To overcome this, they propose a novel random forest (RF) framework. Instead of training on image data, the RFs are trained directly on the attribute signatures of the unseen classes. The key contribution is to augment this training process by explicitly modeling the error characteristics (i.e., ROC statistics) of the attribute classifiers. During tree construction, idealized class signatures are fractionally propagated down the tree according to the true/false positive rates of the attribute classifiers, which are estimated on a held-out validation set. This encourages the trees to select splits on attributes that are not only discriminative for the signatures but also reliably predictable at test time. The authors further extend this idea to account for uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning setting. Experiments on three benchmark datasets show significant improvements over the popular Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) method and other baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of integrating attribute classifier reliability directly into the training of the zero-shot model is novel and addresses a fundamental weakness of prior work. By moving beyond simply using the output of attribute classifiers, the model learns to be robust to their inherent imperfections. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed mechanism of using ROC statistics to guide the fractional propagation of training signatures through a decision tree is elegant and technically sound. The formulation of the information gain criterion (Eq. 5) correctly reflects the goal of finding splits that are both discriminative and reliable.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results, demonstrating substantial improvements over the widely-used DAP baseline across three different datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The controlled noise experiments in Figure 1 provide a clear and intuitive validation of the method's design, showing that its advantage grows as attribute reliability decreases, particularly when noise varies across attributes.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, the paper is marred by a substantial number of issues related to clarity, methodological detail, and experimental rigor, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected of a top-tier conference paper.
    *   **Formatting:** There are numerous formatting inconsistencies. Footnote 1 is syntactically misplaced, breaking a sentence. The references are poorly formatted, with missing conference years and inconsistent styles (e.g., "[2]...Machine learning, 45(1):5–32, ,", "[3]...CVPR, ,"). A URL is provided as a footnote (footnote 5) rather than a proper citation.
    *   **Writing Style:** The tone is at times informal, with repeated use of scare quotes (e.g., “exemplar”, “cushion”). Phrases like "status quo models" could be more precise.
    *   **Equations:** The use of non-standard angle brackets (` ` and `«`) in Equation 2 is unnecessary and reduces clarity.

2.  **Methodological Ambiguity:** Several critical aspects of the methodology are either unclear or omitted.
    *   **Validation Data Handling:** The description of how the validation data `DV` is used is ambiguous. The text states: "Our method reserves 20% of the attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers." This could imply that the validation data is also used for training the classifiers whose ROCs are being estimated, which would be a form of data leakage. This needs precise clarification.
    *   **Omission of Key Details:** Two extensions are claimed as contributions but are entirely deferred to the supplementary material: accounting for class signature uncertainty (Sec 3.2.3) and handling continuous attributes from discovered attribute methods (Sec 4.1). Without these details, the paper is incomplete, and the corresponding results (OURS vs. OURS W/O SIG UNCERTAINTY, and the state-of-the-art claim with discovered attributes) are not fully reproducible or verifiable from the main text.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:**
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper states hyperparameters were set "Based on cross-validation," but provides no details on how this is possible in a zero-shot setting where there is no labeled data for the unseen classes. This is a critical omission that questions the validity of the chosen parameters and the reproducibility of the results.
    *   **Recursive ROC Estimation:** The benefit of recursively propagating `DV(n)` is supported by an ablation, but its stability is questionable. The paper notes that for small `DV(n)` (<10 samples), it reverts to global statistics. How frequently does this occur? This detail is important for understanding whether this component is truly effective or if the model often defaults to a simpler global error model.
    *   **Scalability:** The proposed one-vs-all training scheme requires training `K` separate random forests for `K` unseen classes. This approach does not scale well to scenarios with a large number of unseen classes, a significant limitation that is understated by being mentioned only briefly as future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to handle a key problem in ZSL.
*   The method is intuitive and demonstrates strong empirical performance against an important baseline.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a lack of rigor in its presentation, with numerous formatting and clarity issues.
*   Critical methodological details regarding data handling, hyperparameter tuning, and model extensions are either ambiguous or missing entirely from the main text.
*   The accumulation of these "minor" flaws makes the work feel premature and prevents a full and confident assessment of its contributions.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The central idea of this paper is strong and potentially impactful. However, a strong idea is not sufficient. The execution and presentation fall well short of the standards for NIPS. The manuscript is replete with formatting errors, unclear methodological descriptions, and missing experimental details that leave the reader with too many unanswered questions about the work's validity and reproducibility. While the empirical results are impressive, they cannot be fully trusted without a clear explanation of, for example, the hyperparameter tuning protocol in a zero-shot context. The paper requires a major revision to address these significant shortcomings before it is ready for publication.
