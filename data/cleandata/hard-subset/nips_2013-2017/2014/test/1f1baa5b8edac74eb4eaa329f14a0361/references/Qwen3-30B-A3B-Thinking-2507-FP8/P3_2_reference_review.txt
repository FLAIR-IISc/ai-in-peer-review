

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL) literature: the assumption that attribute classifiers can perfectly predict semantic attributes. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability during training. Their method uses the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers to fractionally propagate class signatures through decision trees, rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions. The paper also extends the approach to few-shot learning and demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). This work is particularly relevant as it acknowledges a practical reality in ZSL that most existing methods ignore, making it more applicable to real-world scenarios.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Identification**: The paper effectively identifies a crucial gap in the ZSL literature—most methods assume perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic in practice. The authors provide strong motivation for why this assumption is problematic, citing both practical challenges (attribute correlations, diverse visual instantiations) and empirical evidence (Section 1).

2. **Novel Technical Contribution**: The fractional propagation of signatures based on ROC curves is a clever and well-motivated extension of random forests to the ZSL setting. This is a significant technical contribution that directly addresses the problem statement.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The authors evaluate their method on three standard datasets across different domains (animals, objects, scenes) and compare against multiple baselines, including the widely used DAP method. The controlled noise experiments (Fig 1) provide valuable insights into how the method handles attribute unreliability.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The method's ability to work with real-world attribute classifiers (not perfect ones) makes it more applicable to practical systems. The few-shot extension further enhances its practical utility.

5. **Clear Ablation Studies**: The paper includes thorough ablation studies (Table 1) that isolate the contributions of different components of the proposed method, demonstrating the importance of both the ROC propagation and signature uncertainty modeling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the method is well-motivated, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of why the fractional propagation approach is optimal or how it relates to statistical learning theory. The authors provide empirical evidence of effectiveness but don't establish theoretical guarantees.

2. **Validation Data Dependency**: The method relies on having a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves. The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 3.2.2) but doesn't sufficiently address how well this generalizes to unseen classes. The validation data is drawn from "seen" classes, which may not accurately represent the error patterns on unseen classes.

3. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it doesn't adequately compare with recent advances in ZSL that use deep learning approaches (e.g., recent transformer-based methods). The paper's focus on attribute-based methods is valid, but it would strengthen the contribution to engage more deeply with the broader ZSL literature.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The results are reported as mean and standard error, but the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance improvements over baselines. This is a common oversight in ML papers that weakens the empirical claims.

5. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the method scales to very large numbers of attributes or classes. With M=102 attributes in SUN, the method's computational complexity could become prohibitive for larger attribute sets.

## Critical Analysis

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and the experiments are well-designed. The authors demonstrate that their method significantly outperforms DAP (the current state-of-the-art for attribute-based ZSL) on all three datasets, with particularly large gains on SUN and aPY where attribute classifiers are less reliable. The ablation studies provide strong evidence that both components of the method (ROC propagation and signature uncertainty modeling) contribute to performance. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and statistical significance testing are notable weaknesses in an otherwise strong technical contribution.

### Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear figures that effectively illustrate key concepts (Figs 1, 2a, 2b). The description of the fractional propagation method (Section 3.2.2) is logically presented, though the mathematical formulation could be simplified for better readability. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details (pseudocode, schematic), which is helpful for reproducibility. The paper would benefit from more detailed pseudocode for the main algorithm in the main text.

### Originality
The core idea of accounting for attribute unreliability in ZSL is novel in the context of the literature. While the concept of using ROC curves in decision trees is not new (citing [21, 28]), the application to the specific problem of ZSL with unreliable attributes is a fresh perspective. The fractional propagation approach cleverly adapts existing techniques to a new problem domain. However, the paper could better distinguish itself from recent work on attribute learning (e.g., [30]) that also deals with unreliable attributes.

### Significance
This work addresses a real-world limitation in ZSL that has been largely ignored in the literature. The fact that the method outperforms DAP despite using only 80% of the attribute-labeled data (instead of 100%) is particularly significant—it demonstrates that modeling attribute unreliability is more important than having better attribute classifiers. The few-shot extension further increases the practical significance. The results suggest that ZSL can be made more practical by acknowledging the reality of unreliable attribute predictions, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Statistical Significance Testing**: Include p-values or confidence intervals for the performance comparisons to strengthen the empirical claims.

2. **Address Validation Data Generalization**: Provide more thorough analysis of how well the validation data (from seen classes) generalizes to unseen classes. Consider experiments where the validation data is selected based on similarity to unseen classes.

3. **Expand Discussion of Attribute Correlations**: The introduction mentions attribute correlations as a problem, but the paper doesn't address how the method handles correlated attributes. This is a significant gap, as correlated attributes can greatly impact the reliability of attribute predictions.

4. **Compare with Recent Deep Learning Approaches**: Engage more deeply with recent ZSL methods that use deep learning (e.g., [1, 4, 12] in the references) to better position the contribution within the broader ZSL literature.

5. **Include More Detailed Pseudocode**: Provide a more detailed pseudocode for the main algorithm in the main text to improve reproducibility.

## Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by addressing a critical practical limitation in existing methods. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant improvements over the state-of-the-art. While there are some weaknesses in the theoretical analysis and experimental design (particularly regarding validation data generalization and statistical significance), these do not undermine the core contribution.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions (addressing the statistical significance testing and validation data generalization issues). The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution that addresses a real problem in the field, and the results are compelling. The method has clear potential for practical application in real-world ZSL systems.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Addresses a critical, overlooked limitation in ZSL literature (attribute unreliability)
- Method is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound
- Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Includes thorough ablation studies and controlled experiments
- Has clear practical relevance for real-world applications
- Few-shot extension enhances practical utility

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of the proposed method
- Validation data dependency may limit real-world applicability
- Insufficient comparison with recent deep learning-based ZSL methods
- No statistical significance testing for performance comparisons
- Limited discussion of attribute correlation effects

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a meaningful contribution to the field of zero-shot learning.
