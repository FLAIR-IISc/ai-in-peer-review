Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a key practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): mid-level attribute classifiers are noisy and often poorly calibrated, which degrades two-stage ZSL that treats attribute predictions as ground truth. The authors propose a random forest (RF) approach that trains only on class-attribute signatures (one-vs-all), but explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability by (1) injecting attribute-labeled validation data at training time to estimate node-specific ROC statistics for each attribute and threshold, and (2) fractionally propagating class signatures down both branches of a split according to the estimated TP/FP rates at that node. This biases split selection toward attributes and operating points that are both discriminative and reliably predictable. They further model uncertainty in class signatures (class-attribute associations) and extend the idea to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with the standard RF information gain computed on a small labeled set.

The work is well positioned relative to ZSL by attributes (DAP [Lampert et al.], attribute matching [Farhadi et al.], topic-model variants), and to alternatives that avoid semantic attributes in favor of discovered mid-levels or semantic embeddings (e.g., label/feature embeddings, hierarchical classifier transfer, DeViSE). Unlike those, this paper keeps the human-in-the-loop, language-based specification while directly modeling attribute unreliability. The method is also related to decision trees for missing/uncertain inputs (fractional propagation), but the zero-shot setting here is novel: trees are built from class signatures, ROC statistics are node-specific, and test-time inputs are attribute predictions.

On AwA, aPY, and SUN attributes, the method outperforms DAP and a signature-only RF baseline; controlled-noise experiments illustrate robustness; and a few-shot extension shows improvements over RFs trained purely on few labeled images.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, under-explored failure mode of attribute-based ZSL: unreliability and miscalibration of attribute classifiers. The idea of using ROC-driven fractional propagation to align tree training with expected attribute errors is elegant and intuitive.
- Node-specific ROC estimation via recursive filtering of validation data is a thoughtful design that captures conditional dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- The framework naturally extends to class-signature uncertainty and to few-shot learning with a principled prior (mixture of information gains).
- Empirical results are consistent across three datasets and over both controlled-noise and real attribute predictions, with clear gains over strong baselines (DAP) where attribute detectors are weak—precisely the regime of interest.
- The approach is simple to implement with public datasets and standard features; hyperparameters are reasonable, and ablations isolate key components.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Comparisons to more recent/alternative ZSL paradigms are limited. Although the paper cites label embeddings (e.g., ALE) and DeViSE, it does not provide a controlled comparison under identical features/splits. Since these methods also mitigate attribute unreliability by bypassing explicit attribute prediction, it would strengthen the case to report head-to-head results, especially on AwA (where several numbers are available).
- Domain shift in ROC estimation: ROC statistics are estimated from seen classes (attribute validation set) and assumed to transfer to unseen classes. The paper acknowledges this and presents an upper bound using “true ROC,” but it remains a potential source of bias, particularly when unseen classes combine attributes in novel ways. Some analysis of how ROC stability affects performance (e.g., stratifying by signature novelty) would be valuable.
- Calibration issues: Platt probabilities for different attributes can be differently calibrated. While ROC-based decisions circumvent needing calibrated scores, test-time traversal still depends on thresholds chosen during training; a brief discussion or empirical check of calibration sensitivity would help.
- Computational aspects: Training one RF per class scales linearly in K, but node-specific ROC computation involves maintaining DV(n) and estimating TP/FP per candidate split. Complexity/runtime and memory costs are not reported; a brief analysis or timing would aid practitioners.
- Details in supplementary: Important training choices (e.g., candidate threshold sampling strategy, signature uncertainty model) are deferred to the supplement. Some of these could be summarized in the main text.

Quality
- Technically sound and internally consistent. The information gain criterion under fractional indicators is appropriate, and the ablations and controlled-noise experiments validate design choices. The few-shot formulation is well motivated.
- Some additional baselines and analyses (above) would further solidify claims.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized, with a helpful walk-through of the training/testing pipeline. The intuition behind fractional propagation is well illustrated. Moving key implementation details (threshold sampling, λ selection policy) from supplement would improve readability.

Originality
- Novel application of node-specific ROC-weighted fractional propagation to zero-shot trees built from signatures, and a clean integration into RFs. The prior-guided few-shot extension via mixed information gains is a nice addition.

Significance
- Practically significant for attribute-based ZSL, showing that modeling unreliability yields robust gains without abandoning semantic interpretability. The method is likely to influence subsequent work that blends semantic supervision with learned detectors.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and often ignored pain point in ZSL.
- Simple, principled method with consistent empirical gains on multiple datasets.
- Generalizes to few-shot learning; interpretable and adaptable.
- Clear ablations and controlled-noise studies support the central claim.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing controlled comparisons to state-of-the-art embedding-based ZSL (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) under shared settings.
- Limited analysis of domain shift between validation ROC and unseen classes.
- Some key implementation details and complexity analysis missing from main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to label embedding/ALE and DeViSE (AwA, aPY), using identical features/splits if possible.
- Report training/inference time and memory, and how complexity scales with K, M, and tree depth.
- Analyze sensitivity to the size/composition of DV, including cross-class ROC stability and the impact of selecting validation images whose signatures are closer to unseen classes.
- Clarify the pipeline for training attribute classifiers vs. using DV for ROC estimation to avoid potential leakage or optimistic ROC estimates.
- Provide a short summary of the signature uncertainty model and threshold sampling in the main paper.
- Explore simple correlation-aware tests (e.g., joint splits on two attributes) or report empirical inter-attribute dependence captured implicitly by node-specific ROC.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper proposes a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated approach to a consequential problem in attribute-based ZSL. While broader comparisons and a bit more analysis would strengthen the case, the contribution is clear and of interest to the NeurIPS community.
