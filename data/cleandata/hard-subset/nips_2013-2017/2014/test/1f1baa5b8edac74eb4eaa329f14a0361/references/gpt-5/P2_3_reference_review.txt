Summary
The paper tackles a central practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute classifiers are unreliable, and existing methods typically take their outputs at face value. The authors propose a random forest model trained not on images but on class attribute signatures, augmented with a principled mechanism to account for attribute unreliability. The key idea is to inject attribute validation data to estimate per-attribute ROC curves and to propagate class signatures fractionally through the tree according to node-specific TP/FP rates at chosen thresholds. This yields a modified information gain that prefers splits that are both discriminative among unseen classes and robust to expected attribute errors. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-based and data-based information gains. Experiments on AWA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations illustrating the contributions of ROC-based propagation and signature uncertainty. The approach also works with discovered attributes and shows benefits in few-shot regimes.

Relation to prior work
The work is well-situated within attribute-based ZSL (DAP; Farhadi et al.; Lampert et al.), and distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during training. The fractional propagation idea relates to handling missing/uncertain features in decision trees, but the authors adapt it to the zero-shot setting with node-specific ROC estimates and soft counts. Comparisons to non-attribute ZSL approaches (label/feature embeddings, hierarchical adaptation) are discussed conceptually; empirical comparisons focus mainly on DAP and an RF baseline, with additional results using discovered attributes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of soft counts via node-conditional ROC estimates and the modified information gain is principled and internally consistent. The argument that, with perfectly matched validation statistics, the learned forest approximates training on test-time attribute outputs is reasonable.
- Empirical support: Results are thorough across three datasets, include controlled noise studies, ablations (no ROC propagation, no signature uncertainty), and few-shot extensions. Improvements over DAP are meaningful, especially when attributes are less reliable.
- Caveats:
  - Validation data usage needs clarification. The paper states it reserves 20% for ROC estimation but then “pools it” with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. If the same images used to estimate ROC are also used to fit the attribute SVMs, ROC estimates may be optimistic; if ROCs are computed with models trained without those images, there is a mismatch between the ROC-estimated behavior and the final classifiers used at test time. Please clarify and, ideally, report results with a strict hold-out for ROC estimation.
  - The signature uncertainty model is deferred to the supplement; more details and sensitivity analyses in the main paper would strengthen the case.
  - Scalability analysis is limited. One-vs-all forests per unseen class and per-node ROC computations could be costly with large K or M. A brief complexity/runtime analysis would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The intuition behind fractional propagation and why node-specific ROCs matter is well explained. Some implementation details that affect reproducibility (exact threshold sampling strategy, handling of small DV(n), and the signature uncertainty term) are partly in the supplement; surfacing more of them would help. A schematic or pseudocode in the main text would be useful.

Originality
Modeling attribute unreliability via ROC-aware, node-conditional fractional propagation within trees for zero-shot training is novel and non-trivial. While related to prior work on trees with uncertain or missing features, the zero-shot setting, per-node ROC conditioning, and combination with signature priors and few-shot data appear original.

Significance
Addressing attribute unreliability is important for making attribute-based ZSL practical. The demonstrated gains over a strong and widely used baseline (DAP) on multiple datasets, plus benefits in few-shot regimes and with discovered attributes, suggest real impact. The method is conceptually simple, leverages readily available validation data, and could be adapted beyond vision wherever mid-level semantic predictors are used.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated problem and an elegant, principled solution.
- Strong empirical gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline across three datasets.
- Insightful controlled noise experiments that diagnose when and why the method helps.
- Ablations isolate contributions (ROC propagation, signature uncertainty).
- Natural extension to few-shot learning with a tunable prior strength.
- Works with both named and discovered attributes.

Cons / Questions
- Potential leakage or mismatch in using DV both for ROC estimation and SVM training; needs clarification and, ideally, a stricter protocol.
- Limited comparisons to other ZSL paradigms (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE) in experiments.
- Scalability/runtime not analyzed; one-vs-all forests may become heavy with many unseen classes.
- Details of the class signature uncertainty model relegated to supplement; limited empirical sensitivity analysis.
- Assumes a single signature per class (multi-modality mentioned but not evaluated).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the data splits for training attribute classifiers vs. estimating ROCs; provide results with a true held-out DV.
- Add runtime/complexity analysis and discuss scaling to larger K and M.
- Include empirical comparisons to a representative label-embedding method on at least one dataset.
- Move key details of the signature uncertainty model into the main text and add sensitivity studies.
- Report robustness to the size/composition of DV and to the choice of (#m, #t) per node.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, technically sound, and impactful contribution that addresses a real limitation in attribute-based ZSL with a novel and practical method, backed by strong experiments. Pending clarification on validation protocol and some added comparisons, I lean toward acceptance.
