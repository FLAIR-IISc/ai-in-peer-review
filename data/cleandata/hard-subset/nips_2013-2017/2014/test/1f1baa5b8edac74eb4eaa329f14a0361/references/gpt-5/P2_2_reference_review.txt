Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets zero-shot recognition when attribute predictors are unreliable. It proposes training one-vs-all random forests whose “training data” are class-level attribute signatures rather than images. The core idea is to incorporate attribute unreliability during tree construction by using ROC statistics (TPR/FPR as a function of thresholds) estimated from attribute-labeled validation data. At each split on attribute m with threshold t, each class signature is fractionally propagated left/right according to TP/FP rates observed on validation images that reach that node, yielding an information gain criterion that favors splits both discriminative among class signatures and robust to expected attribute errors. The method also handles uncertainty in class-attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”) and extends to few-shot learning by mixing signature-based gain with standard gain computed on a small labeled set via a parameter λ.

This directly addresses a known limitation of attribute-based zero-shot methods like DAP (Lampert et al.), which take predicted attributes at face value. The approach relates to trees for missing/uncertain inputs (e.g., fractional tuples, fuzzy trees) but adapts them to the zero-shot setting where training inputs are signatures and testing inputs are attribute predictions. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest baseline, plus ablations and controlled-noise studies. Results with discovered attributes (Rastegari et al.) and the few-shot extension further support the approach.

Strengths
- Addresses a real, well-documented pain point: attribute unreliability. The model explicitly optimizes for robustness to anticipated errors rather than hoping calibrated probabilities suffice.
- Technically neat idea: fractional propagation of signature mass based on node-specific ROC estimates; redefining information gain accordingly is simple yet effective.
- Recursive propagation of validation data to estimate node-conditional ROC captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits, which prior “uncertain feature” tree methods typically ignore.
- Few-shot extension is principled and practical: signatures act as a prior via λ, with sensible behavior as labeled data increases.
- Solid empirical validation: three datasets, controlled-noise experiments that diagnose where gains come from, ablations disentangling signature uncertainty vs. ROC propagation, and comparison to both hand-designed and discovered attributes.
- Implementation appears straightforward; interpretable splits/operating points.

Weaknesses
- Potential optimism in ROC estimation: the paper states it “reserves 20% of attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” If the SVMs are trained on all images, and ROC is computed on images also used for training those SVMs, ROC estimates may be optimistic, favoring the proposed method. A strict separation between data used to train attribute classifiers and data used to estimate ROC is important.
- Limited theoretical analysis: the approach is heuristic (though well-motivated). There are no bounds or guarantees relating ROC-estimate error to zero-shot performance.
- Scalability: training K one-vs-all forests and computing M attribute predictions per test image could be heavy for large K, and the method doesn’t include a multi-class tree or shared forest; authors mention this as future work.
- Dependence on the match between seen-class validation data and unseen classes: if attribute classifiers’ error profiles shift for unseen classes, ROC-guided choices may be suboptimal. The “TRUE ROC” upper bound underscores this gap; more analysis of robustness to validation-set mismatch would help.
- Comparisons: DAP and a signature-RF are appropriate; but label-embedding/neural embedding methods (e.g., DeViSE, Akata et al.) are cited but not compared experimentally. Even if attribute-centric focus justifies this, a brief discussion of why those methods are not directly comparable on the chosen splits would strengthen the paper.
- Some details are deferred to the supplement (signature uncertainty formulation, full pseudocode), which slightly hinders reproducibility from the main text.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the use of node-conditional ROC and fractional propagation is well specified. Claims are supported by experiments with multiple datasets, ablations, and stress tests. The most significant concern is the possible ROC estimation bias; clarifying the data splits or providing results with strictly disjoint ROC-estimation sets would address this.

Clarity
- Clear, well-organized, with thorough related work. The method is explained at the right level, though the reader must consult the supplement for signature uncertainty details and pseudocode. Notation is consistent and the training/testing domains are clearly distinguished.

Originality
- Novel adaptation of decision forests to zero-shot learning with unreliable attributes: training on signatures, node-specific ROC-guided fractional propagation, and an IG criterion that reflects expected test-time noise. While related ideas exist for uncertain inputs in trees, the zero-shot formulation and ROC-based split selection conditioned on upstream splits are new contributions. The few-shot “descriptive prior” via IG mixing is a clean, original extension.

Significance
- Advances state of the art on standard zero-shot benchmarks (especially where attributes are less reliable), and offers a simple, general recipe for injecting attribute-error models into category learning. Likely to influence subsequent work on robust zero-shot/few-shot learning and mid-level semantics.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify data splits: Is the ROC validation set disjoint from the training set of attribute SVMs? If not, please provide results where ROC is estimated on a held-out set not used to train attribute predictors.
- Sensitivity analyses: (1) size of ROC validation set; (2) mismatch between seen vs. unseen domains; (3) number of thresholds (#t) sampled per node; (4) λ selection robustness in few-shot.
- Runtime and memory: report training/test time as K and M grow; discuss a shared multi-class forest or candidate-pruning to scale.
- Comparisons to embedding-based zero-shot methods on at least one dataset, or justify incompatibility with the attribute setting used.
- Expand on the “signature uncertainty” formulation in the main text, including the noise model and parameter selection.

Pro arguments for acceptance
- Clear and meaningful problem formulation; method directly tackles a critical bottleneck.
- Technically elegant and easy to implement; interpretable and extensible.
- Strong empirical gains and careful ablations; controlled-noise analysis is insightful.
- Few-shot extension is useful in practice.

Con arguments for acceptance
- Possible optimistic ROC estimation if validation data are not disjoint from attribute training.
- Limited theoretical grounding; scalability to many unseen classes not fully addressed.
- Missing comparisons to some contemporary zero-shot baselines outside DAP.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper proposes a simple, principled, and effective way to account for attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning, with solid empirical support and a useful few-shot extension. Addressing the ROC data-split concern and adding a brief scalability analysis would further strengthen the work.
