

# Review: Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL) - the reliance on perfect attribute predictions. The authors propose a novel approach that explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers into the training of a random forest. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods, particularly when attribute classifiers are unreliable.

## Strengths

**Originality (4/4):** The paper identifies and addresses a significant gap in ZSL literature that has been overlooked in previous work. While most ZSL methods assume reliable attribute predictions, the authors correctly point out that visual attributes are difficult to predict reliably, and propose a novel solution to account for this unreliability. This represents a meaningful contribution that advances the field beyond the current "proof of concept" stage of attribute-based ZSL.

**Quality (4/4):** The paper is technically sound with a clear methodological contribution. The authors provide a thorough ablation study (Table 1) that isolates the contribution of different components of their approach. The experiments on three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) are comprehensive, with detailed analysis of how the method performs under controlled noise and with real unreliable attributes. The method is well-justified through both theoretical reasoning and empirical results.

**Significance (4/4):** The results are impressive, with the proposed method significantly outperforming DAP (a widely used baseline) and SIGNATURE RF across all datasets. The paper demonstrates that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than having more attribute-labeled data (as evidenced by their 8-point gain over DAP when using only 500 attribute-labeled images). This has practical implications for real-world ZSL applications where attribute classifiers are inherently unreliable.

**Clarity (4/4):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The figures and explanations of the method (particularly the fractional propagation of signatures) are clear and helpful. The pseudocode and supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproduction. The authors effectively relate their work to previous contributions while clearly articulating their novel contributions.

## Weaknesses

**Validation Data Requirement (2/4):** The method requires a separate validation set (20% of attribute-labeled data) to estimate ROC curves. While the authors mention this, they don't adequately address how this affects the data efficiency of the overall approach. In many real-world scenarios, obtaining sufficient validation data for attribute classifiers might be challenging, which could limit the practical applicability of the method.

**Comparison to Recent Methods (3/4):** The paper compares to DAP and SIGNATURE RF, but doesn't compare to some of the most recent state-of-the-art ZSL methods (e.g., DEVISE, [31], or other deep learning-based approaches). The authors should include these comparisons to better position their work within the current literature.

**Theoretical Analysis (3/4):** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a formal theoretical analysis of why the proposed method should work better than alternatives. A more rigorous analysis of the error bounds or convergence properties would strengthen the contribution.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include comparisons to recent deep learning-based ZSL methods (e.g., DEVISE, [31]) to better position the work within the current literature.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the attribute classifier performance (e.g., TPR/FPR values for different attributes across datasets) to better understand the scope of the problem.

3. Address the scalability of the method to larger numbers of attributes and unseen classes.

4. Add a more formal theoretical analysis of the proposed approach.

5. Discuss potential limitations of the validation data requirement in real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical limitation that has prevented attribute-based methods from reaching their full potential. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages. While there are some concerns about the validation data requirement and limited comparison to recent methods, these can be addressed with minor revisions.

The paper advances the state of the art in zero-shot recognition and demonstrates that the elegance of attribute-based approaches need not be abandoned despite the inherent difficulty in learning visual attributes reliably. The authors have provided a compelling solution that could have significant practical impact in real-world applications.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions as noted above)
