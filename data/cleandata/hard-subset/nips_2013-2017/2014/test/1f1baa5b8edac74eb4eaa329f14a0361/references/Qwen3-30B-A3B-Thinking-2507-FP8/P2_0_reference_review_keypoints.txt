- Addresses unrealistic assumption of perfect attribute predictions in attribute-based zero-shot learning by modeling attribute classifier unreliability.  
- Proposes incorporating ROC statistics (false positive/true positive rates) into random forest training to account for attribute unreliability.  
- Constructs decision trees using class attribute signatures and propagates signatures fractionally according to estimated ROC rates.  
- Extends the approach to handle signature uncertainty and to few-shot learning scenarios.  
- Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art on AwA, aPY, and SUN, with strong gains on classes with unreliable attributes.  
- Method is technically sound with a rigorous formulation of information gain that supports fractional signature propagation.  
- Experimental design is thorough, including controlled noise experiments and real-world benchmarks across three datasets.  
- Ablation studies effectively isolate contributions of different components.  
- Authors honestly acknowledge limitations, including reliance on validation data to estimate ROC curves and upper-bound performance when ROC is perfectly known.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and explains key concepts (e.g., fractional propagation) and figures effectively.  
- The ROC-based approach to handle attribute unreliability is a novel and meaningful contribution to zero-shot learning.  
- Work is well-situated in related literature and distinguishes itself from prior methods like fractional tuples for missing values.  
- Significant practical impact: outperforms the widely used DAP model and shows benefits even with fewer attribute-labeled images.  
- Extension to few-shot learning and success with discovered attributes broaden applicability beyond hand-designed attribute sets.  
- Weakness: reliance on validation data for ROC estimation, with limited exploration of cases lacking representative validation data.  
- Weakness: lacks deeper analysis of why it outperforms DAP (e.g., decision boundary or attribute usage comparison).  
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability are not discussed.  
- Recommendation: accept — significant, well-supported contribution likely to influence the zero-shot learning community.
