- Summary: Proposes a random forest method for zero-shot learning that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability using ROC-informed fractional propagation and extends to few-shot learning, showing strong empirical gains.

- Strength: Addresses a critical limitation of ZSL by modeling imperfect attribute predictions rather than treating them as ground truth.

- Strength: Novel ROC-informed propagation within random forests and fractional signature propagation to inject attribute uncertainty.

- Strength: Strong empirical validation across three datasets with ablation studies demonstrating component contributions and improvements over DAP and other baselines.

- Strength: Provides clear mathematical formulation and connects to theory on decision trees with uncertain data.

- Strength: Natural extension to the few-shot setting, demonstrating method adaptability.

- Strength: Paper is well-written and clearly presented.

- Weakness: Computational complexity concerns from building/traversing ROC curves per attribute are not discussed.

- Weakness: Dependence on representative validation data for ROC estimation; sensitivity to dataset shift is unexplored.

- Weakness: New hyperparameters (e.g., lambda) require tuning; parameter sensitivity analysis is limited.

- Weakness: Limited error analysis—lack of insight into remaining failure modes, specific problematic attributes or classes.

- Weakness: Comparisons to recent attribute-discovery methods are limited; more direct benchmarks would help.

- Originality: Applying ROC-based attribute unreliability modeling within random forests for ZSL (and few-shot extension) is an original and meaningful contribution.

- Significance: Addresses a fundamental ZSL challenge with practical implications and potential to advance robust attribute learning.

- Overall assessment: Strong, technically sound paper with solid results; minor issues do not undermine the main contribution.

- Arguments for acceptance: Tackles a key limitation, introduces a novel approach, shows significant performance gains, offers theoretical grounding, and extends to few-shot learning.

- Arguments against acceptance: Potential computational/scalability issues, reliance on validation data quality, and limited error analysis.

- Recommendation: Strong Accept.
