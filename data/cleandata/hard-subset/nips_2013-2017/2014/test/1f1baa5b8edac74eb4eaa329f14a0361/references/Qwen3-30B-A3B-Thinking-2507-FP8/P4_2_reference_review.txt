

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. While previous work has assumed perfect attribute predictions, the authors correctly identify that visual attributes are difficult to predict reliably, which hinders practical zero-shot recognition. The paper proposes a novel approach that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers into the random forest training process. The authors further extend their method to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning.

The paper builds upon the two-stage attribute-based zero-shot learning framework established in prior work [8, 3, 18, 23, 19, 29], but makes a key distinction by recognizing that attribute unreliability (not just attribute strength) is a fundamental challenge. The authors correctly cite related work on handling missing features in decision trees [21, 28, 16] but note that their zero-shot scenario presents unique challenges that require different handling.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their approach, including detailed equations for information gain calculations with and without accounting for attribute unreliability. The methodology section (Section 3) is well-structured, with clear explanations of both the basic signature random forest and the extensions to handle attribute unreliability and signature uncertainty. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using three standard benchmark datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) and comparing against multiple baselines including DAP, which is the leading attribute-based zero-shot method. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different components of the approach. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need for attribute-labeled validation data, and conduct controlled noise experiments to validate their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem and motivation. The related work section provides a comprehensive comparison with previous approaches, highlighting how this work differs. The methodology section is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, with clear mathematical notation and pseudocode provided in the supplementary material. The experimental results are presented clearly with well-labeled tables and figures. The paper could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of why their approach works better than alternatives, but the technical details are sufficient for experts.

**Originality:** The core contribution of explicitly accounting for attribute prediction unreliability during training is novel and addresses a significant gap in the zero-shot learning literature. Previous work has focused on the two-stage classification approach but has not considered how to handle the unreliability of the attribute predictions themselves. The authors' use of ROC characteristics to guide tree construction represents a novel technical contribution. The extension to few-shot learning is also a valuable addition. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from prior work, particularly in the related work section, where they explicitly contrast their approach with methods that have attempted to handle attribute uncertainty.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate substantial improvements over the state-of-the-art DAP method (e.g., 10.2% improvement on "giant panda" in AwA). The authors show that their approach significantly outperforms DAP despite using only 80% of the attribute-labeled images for training, demonstrating that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than having more attribute-labeled data. The method also generalizes to discovered attributes, achieving state-of-the-art results on AwA when using discovered attributes. The few-shot extension provides a practical solution for scenarios where a small number of labeled examples are available, making the approach more broadly applicable.

**Strengths:**
1. Addresses a critical, previously overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning (attribute unreliability)
2. Theoretical approach is sound and well-motivated
3. Strong empirical results across three standard datasets
4. Thorough ablation studies to isolate contributions
5. Practical extension to few-shot learning
6. Clear comparison with relevant prior work

**Weaknesses:**
1. The method requires attribute-labeled validation data, which might not always be available
2. The paper doesn't fully address how to handle cases where attribute classifier error characteristics differ between seen and unseen classes
3. The computational complexity of the approach isn't explicitly discussed

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong submission that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The paper identifies a critical limitation in existing approaches, proposes a novel and technically sound solution, and demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over the state of the art. The writing is clear, the methodology is well-explained, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The work addresses a problem of practical importance and has the potential to influence future zero-shot learning research.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. It is technically sound, clearly written, makes a novel contribution, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods. The authors have addressed a critical gap in the literature with a well-motivated approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in visual recognition with a method that leverages machine learning techniques.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated, with results that significantly advance the state of the art.
