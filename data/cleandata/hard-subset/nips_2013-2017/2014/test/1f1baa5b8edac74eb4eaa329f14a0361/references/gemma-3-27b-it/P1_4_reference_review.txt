## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of zero-shot learning (ZSL) by acknowledging and explicitly modeling the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. Existing ZSL methods typically treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is unrealistic given the difficulty of learning robust attribute classifiers. The authors propose a random forest framework that incorporates attribute classifier ROC curves during training, enabling the selection of discriminative and predictable decision nodes, and generalizing to handle unreliable class-attribute associations. They further extend this approach to the few-shot setting.  The method is evaluated on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) and demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, including the widely used DAP method.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established two-stage attribute-based ZSL paradigm [Lampert et al., Farhadi et al., etc.]. It distinguishes itself from previous work by *explicitly* addressing attribute unreliability, rather than focusing on attribute strength [e.g., attribute regularization in Pasquale et al.].  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of related approaches, including those leveraging external knowledge [Rohrbach et al.] and discovering mid-level features [Rastegari et al., Singh et al.], while clearly articulating the advantages of their language-based description approach. The connection to handling missing/uncertain data in decision trees [Tsang et al.,  Olaru et al.] is also well-established. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The explicit modeling of attribute unreliability is a significant and novel contribution. It directly addresses a core limitation of existing attribute-based ZSL methods.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach, based on modifying the random forest learning process, is well-motivated and appears technically sound. The use of ROC curves to inform split selection is a clever and effective way to incorporate uncertainty. The extension to few-shot learning is a valuable addition.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper presents strong empirical results on three datasets, consistently outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods like DAP. The controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate the benefit of modeling attribute unreliability.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The organization is logical, and the mathematical notation is reasonably clear.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors perform extensive ablation studies, demonstrating the contribution of each component of their approach.  They also appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their method and suggest directions for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the method performs well, the training process, especially with the ROC propagation, could be computationally expensive, particularly for a large number of attributes and classes.  The paper doesn't thoroughly address the scalability of the approach.
* **Validation Data Dependence:** The method relies on a validation dataset to estimate attribute classifier ROCs.  The performance is potentially sensitive to the representativeness of this validation data, and a careful selection strategy might be required.  This is acknowledged, but could benefit from further discussion.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:**  While the quantitative results are strong, a more in-depth analysis of *where* the method fails would be beneficial. Understanding the types of attribute prediction errors that still cause problems could guide future improvements.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions cross-validation for λ in the few-shot extension, but doesn't elaborate on the sensitivity of the method to other hyperparameters (e.g., tree depth, number of trees).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem in zero-shot learning.
* The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and demonstrably effective.
* The experimental results are compelling and consistently outperform existing state-of-the-art methods.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear and thorough presentation of the method and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the method could limit its scalability.
* The reliance on validation data introduces a potential sensitivity issue.
* A more detailed analysis of failure cases would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors have identified a critical limitation of existing methods and proposed a novel and effective solution. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance.



**Confidence:** 9/10

**Recommendation:** Accept.
