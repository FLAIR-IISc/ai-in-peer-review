Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a central weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictors are unreliable. The authors propose a zero-shot random forest trained not on images but on class-attribute signatures. The key idea is to account for attribute unreliability during tree construction by fractionally propagating signature “instances” down the tree according to node-conditional ROC statistics (TPR/FPR at chosen thresholds) estimated from held-out attribute-labeled validation images DV. This yields a split selection criterion (information gain on soft counts) that prefers attributes and thresholds that are both discriminative and reliably predictable. The method also incorporates uncertainty in class-attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”) and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and data-based information gains via a mixing parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show substantial gains over DAP and over a naive signature-only RF; controlled-noise experiments further diagnose robustness. The paper builds on and contrasts with two-stage ZSL (DAP, attribute decoding), embedding-based approaches (Akata, DeViSE), and work on learned mid-level features, while drawing technical inspiration from decision trees with soft/fractional routing.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The ROC-aware fractional propagation is a principled way to compute expected impurity reductions given test-time attribute noise. Computing node-specific ROC estimates by propagating DV through the tree is a thoughtful design to partially account for inter-attribute dependencies conditioned on previous splits. The few-shot extension is straightforward and reasonable.
- Empirical support: Results on three datasets with >250 attributes, ablations (no ROC propagation, no signature uncertainty), and controlled noise studies support the main claims. The method consistently outperforms DAP, especially where attribute predictors are weaker (aPY, SUN).
- Limitations and concerns:
  - Dependence on DV representativeness: The approach assumes the attribute error characteristics on DV approximate those on unseen classes; the authors acknowledge this and show an upper bound with “true ROC.” It would help to quantify sensitivity to DV size/domain shift and to show results when unseen classes are deliberately out-of-domain relative to the seen ones.
  - Potential mismatch between ROC estimation and final attribute classifiers: The text states that 20% is held out for DV and then “pooled” with the remaining 80% to train final attribute classifiers. If ROC statistics are computed using a model trained on 80% but test-time uses a model trained on 100%, there is a distribution shift in scores/thresholds. Please clarify the training protocol and ensure ROCs are estimated for the same model used at test time (e.g., via cross-validation or nested training).
  - Hard vs soft routing at test time: Training uses fractional propagation; inference routes deterministically by âm(x) > t. Soft test-time routing (as in fuzzy trees) might better align with the training objective and could be compared empirically.
  - Scalability and complexity: One-vs-all forests (J=100 trees per unseen class) and per-node ROC estimation by propagating DV increase cost as K grows. An analysis of training/inference time and memory, and experiments with larger K, would strengthen the paper.
  - Modeling attribute dependencies: While node-conditional ROC captures some conditional effects, the method still treats attributes marginally at each split. Explicit modeling of attribute correlations (e.g., via multivariate tests or learned oblique splits) could improve robustness; at minimum, a discussion of this limitation would help.
  - Baselines: DAP and a signature-RF are appropriate, and Fig. 2b suggests competitiveness with discovered-attribute methods. However, a more complete comparison to label/feature embedding methods (Akata et al., DeViSE, Mensink et al.) on all datasets—when feasible given their settings—would situate the gains more broadly. Also consider comparing to augmented attribute representations (Sharmanska et al.) which explicitly address attribute noise.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The main algorithm is described with sufficient intuition and equations. A few points could be improved:
- Include the pseudocode for training and inference in the main paper (or at least a concise schematic), not only in the supplement.
- Provide more detail on threshold sampling (#t), how candidate thresholds are chosen, and whether thresholds are adaptive to DV(n) distributions.
- Move the signature uncertainty formulation from the supplement to the main text, as it contributes to gains.
- Clarify the attribute classifier training/validation protocol to address the potential mismatch noted above.

Originality
The work is original in bringing ROC-aware, fractional propagation to zero-shot random forest training on attribute signatures, and in using node-conditional DV to capture context-dependent error rates. While soft/fuzzy decision trees and handling of missing/probabilistic features are known, the adaptation to the ZSL setting (where “training data” are class signatures and “validation data” provide error models) is novel and well-motivated. The few-shot combination is a sensible extension.

Significance
Zero-shot learning via attributes remains relevant, and modeling attribute unreliability is a key practical issue. The method is simple, interpretable, and shows consistent improvements on standard datasets. The idea could transfer to other mid-level semantic representations. The work advances the practical state of the art for attribute-based ZSL and offers a tool others can build upon.

Specific suggestions
- Add experiments analyzing sensitivity to DV size/composition and to calibration (Platt scaling vs raw scores).
- Report computational costs and scalability with M, K, tree depth, and DV size.
- Evaluate soft test-time routing as an ablation.
- Provide cross-dataset comparisons to embedding-based ZSL baselines where possible.
- Discuss handling of continuous signatures in the main text.

Pros
- Well-motivated, principled treatment of attribute unreliability.
- Clever use of node-conditional ROC; solid ablations and diagnostics.
- Consistent gains over strong baselines on multiple datasets.
- Natural few-shot extension with demonstrable benefit.

Cons
- Reliance on DV representativeness; unclear training/validation protocol consistency.
- Limited analysis of scalability and computational cost.
- Some methodological details deferred to supplement.
- Baseline coverage could be broader.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning). The paper presents a clear, novel, and useful contribution to attribute-based ZSL with strong empirical support and good insights into when and why it helps. Addressing the noted clarifications and additional comparisons would further strengthen it.
