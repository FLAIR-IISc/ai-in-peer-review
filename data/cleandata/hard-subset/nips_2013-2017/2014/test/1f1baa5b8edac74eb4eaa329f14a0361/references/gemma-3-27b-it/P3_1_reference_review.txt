## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the inherent unreliability of attribute classifiers. Existing ZSL methods treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is unrealistic given the difficulty of learning robust attribute detectors. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly models attribute uncertainty during training, leveraging ROC curves to propagate information effectively. They further extend this to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute associations and generalize to the few-shot setting. Empirical results on three datasets demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, including the widely used DAP.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The paper directly confronts a fundamental weakness of current ZSL methods – the reliance on perfect attribute predictions. This is a highly relevant and impactful issue.
* **Novel approach:** The idea of incorporating attribute reliability into the training process, specifically through the ROC-informed propagation within a random forest, is genuinely novel and well-motivated.  The fractional propagation of signatures is a clever way to inject uncertainty.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents compelling experimental results across three datasets, consistently demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over strong baselines (including DAP). The ablation studies convincingly show the contribution of each component (signature uncertainty, ROC propagation).
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the approach and a reasonable justification for the design choices, referencing relevant work on decision trees with uncertain data.
* **Few-shot extension:** Extending the method to the few-shot scenario adds further practical value and demonstrates the adaptability of the approach.
* **Well-written & clear:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanations are clear, and the method is presented in a digestible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:**  While not explicitly discussed, building and traversing ROC curves for each attribute during training could introduce significant computational overhead, especially with a large number of attributes.  A discussion of the computational cost and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Validation Data Dependence:**  The performance relies on having a representative validation dataset to estimate the ROC curves accurately.  The sensitivity to the size and quality of the validation data is not fully explored.  What happens if the validation data distribution shifts from the test distribution?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces new hyperparameters (e.g., lambda for the few-shot extension) that require tuning. A more detailed analysis of the parameter sensitivity and a justification for the chosen values would be helpful.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper shows performance improvements, a deeper error analysis would be valuable.  What types of errors does the proposed method still make?  Are there specific attributes or classes where it struggles?
* **Comparison to Recent Advances:** The comparison to recent advances in attribute discovery (e.g., methods that learn better attribute representations directly from data) could be strengthened. While the authors acknowledge these methods, a more direct comparison on a common benchmark would be informative.



**Originality:**

The core idea of explicitly modelling attribute unreliability within a random forest framework for ZSL is a significant contribution. While the use of random forests and ROC curves are not novel in themselves, their application in this specific context, tailored to address the challenges of ZSL with unreliable attributes, is original. The extension to few-shot learning adds further novelty.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in ZSL, paving the way for more robust and practical applications. The ability to handle unreliable attribute predictions is crucial for deploying ZSL systems in real-world scenarios where attribute classifiers are often imperfect. The demonstrated improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods suggest that this approach has the potential to significantly advance the field. Furthermore, the insights gained from this work could inform the development of more robust attribute learning algorithms.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors identify a critical problem, propose a novel and well-motivated solution, and demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive experiments. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation of current ZSL methods.
* Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Achieves significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
* Provides a clear and well-supported theoretical framework.
* Offers a valuable extension to the few-shot setting.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity of ROC-based propagation could be a limitation.
* Dependence on the quality and representativeness of the validation data.
* Limited error analysis.



**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**. I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS. The technical soundness, originality, and significance of the work are compelling. Addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above in a future revision would further strengthen the contribution.
