Summary
The paper addresses a central practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute classifiers are imperfect and their errors are not accounted for when training unseen category models. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains directly on class-level attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability via ROC statistics estimated on attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes according to node-specific true/false positive rates of the candidate attribute and threshold, and the split is chosen to maximize an information gain defined on these fractional counts. The method also accounts for uncertainty in class-attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”) and extends naturally to the few-shot setting by combining signature-based information gain with that obtained from a small set of class-labeled images using a mixing parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, ablation studies quantify contributions, and controlled noise experiments substantiate the robustness argument. The approach can also leverage discovered mid-level attributes.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against mainstream two-stage zero-shot methods (DAP and variants) and alternatives leveraging label/feature embeddings or hierarchical relations. Its key novelty is not abandoning semantic attributes but learning to respect their reliability during training, in contrast to most prior attribute-based zero-shot methods that treat attribute predictions as trustworthy at decision time only. The fractional propagation is related to decision trees handling missing/probabilistic features, but the zero-shot setting differs materially: training inputs are class signatures, test inputs are attribute predictions. The paper also connects to works that forgo semantic attributes in favor of discriminative mid-level features; here the method remains applicable and interpretable.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Tackles a real impediment to deploying attribute-based zero-shot models, where attribute predictors are noisy, correlated, and often data-poor.
- Methodological contribution: Elegant integration of attribute ROC curves into the split selection of random forests; node-specific ROC estimation via recursively filtered validation sets is a thoughtful way to capture context-dependent operating characteristics and some inter-attribute dependencies.
- Few-shot extension: The λ-weighted joint information gain provides a simple and effective way to inject a semantic prior into RF training with scarce labeled data; the observed shift of optimal λ with sample size is sensible.
- Empirical validation: Clear gains over DAP and a strong signature-only RF across three datasets; controlled noise studies compellingly illustrate when and why the method helps. Ablations isolating signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation are informative.
- Practicality: Works with both named and discovered attributes; compatible with standard attribute training pipelines.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on validation ROC and potential mismatch: The method reserves 20% of attribute-labeled data to estimate ROC per node, but then “pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” This may induce a mismatch between the ROC used to build the forest (estimated on SVMs trained on 80%) and the attribute predictors used at test time (trained on 100%). Please clarify the sequence and whether ROC is recomputed for the final attribute models; otherwise, either bias or mismatch could affect performance comparisons.
- Scalability: Training one RF per unseen class scales linearly in K and may be heavy if K is large or changes dynamically. How expensive is training/inference in practice, and can a single multi-class forest be used without loss? Also, does adding new unseen classes require retraining forests for all existing classes?
- Statistical reliability of node-wise ROC: Filtering DV to DV(n) can quickly reduce positive/negative counts, making ROC estimates noisy; the heuristic fallback to global DV when class counts < 10 helps but is ad hoc. Sensitivity analyses on this threshold and the number of candidate thresholds (#t) would strengthen the case. Consider Bayesian estimation or shrinkage across nodes to stabilize ROC estimates.
- Attribute dependence: While restricting DV to DV(n) captures some context, the method still assumes separable per-attribute ROC behavior conditioned on earlier splits. Explicit modeling of inter-attribute correlations (as suggested in the conclusion) would likely help when attributes are strongly dependent.
- Baseline coverage: On AwA, the comparison to published results is convincing; on aPY and SUN, comparisons appear limited primarily to DAP and your ablations. It would be valuable to include recent semantic/label embedding baselines (e.g., ALE/WSABIE, DeViSE, ConSE/metric-transfer) for completeness, even if they do not use attributes, since they target the same task.
- Evaluation details: Please specify whether accuracy is per-class (mean over classes) or per-image, and whether hyperparameters (tree depth, #m, #t, λ) are tuned exclusively on seen classes without peeking at unseen-class test distributions. Report computational costs and variance across random splits.
- Theoretical grounding: The intuition that the criterion “prefers learnable attributes” is sound, and the zero information gain when TPR=FPR is noted, but a more formal analysis of consistency/robustness (or bounds tying IGzero to expected test risk) would improve the paper’s theoretical depth.

Suggestions
- Clarify and, if necessary, correct the ROC estimation/training pipeline to avoid mismatch; if ROC is estimated on a model different from the final attribute classifier, quantify the impact.
- Add comparisons to representative embedding-based zero-shot methods on aPY/SUN; include top-5 accuracy where K is large.
- Provide an ablation on the amount of attribute-labeled validation data to show robustness to scarce DV.
- Explore calibration improvements for attribute scores (e.g., isotonic regression) and report whether better calibration helps your method more than DAP.
- Discuss how to incorporate new unseen classes without retraining everything, e.g., via shared multi-class trees or incremental forests.

Quality: Technically sound, with careful experimental support and sensible ablations; a few methodological details and evaluation choices merit clarification.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Including pseudocode for training/testing in the main paper (not only supplement) and explicitly stating metrics would help reproducibility.

Originality: The idea of using node-specific ROC-driven fractional propagation of class signatures in RFs for zero/few-shot learning is novel and well-motivated.

Significance: Advances practical zero-shot learning with semantic attributes and is applicable beyond vision wherever mid-level semantic predictors exist. The approach is likely to influence follow-up work on modeling intermediate classifier uncertainty.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning accept). The paper addresses an important problem with a simple, effective, and well-validated method, despite some clarifications needed on ROC estimation, scalability, and baseline breadth.
