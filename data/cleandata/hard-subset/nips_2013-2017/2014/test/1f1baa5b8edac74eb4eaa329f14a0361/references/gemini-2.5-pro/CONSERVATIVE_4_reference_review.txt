**Summary:**

This paper addresses a well-known limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of the underlying attribute classifiers. The authors propose a novel method that explicitly models this unreliability during the training of the zero-shot classifier. The core idea is to use a Random Forest (RF) trained not on image data, but on the attribute "signatures" that define the unseen classes. The key contribution is a modified training procedure for this RF. Instead of making hard splits, the method uses a validation set of attribute-labeled images to estimate the ROC characteristics (TP/FP rates) of each attribute classifier. During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated down both sides of a split, weighted by these estimated error rates. This process builds trees that are robust to the expected noise from the attribute classifiers at test time. The approach is extended to handle uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to a few-shot learning scenario. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) and shows significant improvements over the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) baseline.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a practical and critical problem in attribute-based ZSL. The assumption that attribute classifiers are perfect, which is implicit in many prior methods, is demonstrably false and a key bottleneck for performance. The motivation to directly model this imperfection is strong and logical.
2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed method of using ROC statistics to guide the fractional propagation of signatures through a decision tree is a technically sound and clever way to inject knowledge of classifier unreliability into the learning process. It effectively translates an ideal class signature into a distribution over the noisy, predicted attribute space.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is a significant strength.
    *   The use of controlled noise experiments (Figure 1) provides clear and convincing evidence that the mechanism works as intended, showing graceful degradation compared to a naive RF baseline.
    *   The comparison against DAP is the correct and most relevant baseline for this line of work. The substantial performance gains on three different datasets demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
    *   The ablation studies (Table 1) effectively dissect the contributions of the different components of the model (signature uncertainty, ROC propagation), strengthening the paper's claims.
    *   Demonstrating the method's effectiveness with both hand-defined and discovered attributes shows its versatility.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core idea is explained clearly. The motivation, methodology, and experimental results are presented in a logical and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty of the Core Mechanism:** While the application to ZSL is novel, the underlying idea of propagating fractional instances or distributions through a decision tree has precedents in the literature for handling missing values or uncertain data (as the authors themselves cite, e.g., [21, 28]). The contribution is therefore more of a clever and effective adaptation of established concepts to a new problem domain rather than a fundamentally new learning algorithm.
2.  **Dependence on Validation Data:** The method's performance relies on having a validation set (DV) that can provide reliable estimates of the attribute classifiers' ROC curves. The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the performance to the size or composition of this validation set. It is plausible that if the validation set's distribution differs significantly from the unseen test data, the estimated error rates could be misleading, potentially harming performance. The "upper bound" experiment (OURS+TRUE ROC) highlights this gap but does not resolve the dependency.
3.  **Limited Scope of Baselines:** The paper focuses exclusively on attribute-based ZSL methods, with DAP as the primary baseline. However, in recent years, the field has seen significant progress from methods based on semantic embeddings (e.g., from word vectors) that map visual features directly to a semantic space (e.g., DeViSE, Frome et al., NIPS 2013). While the authors argue for the value of explicit, human-interpretable attributes, a comparison to these alternative state-of-the-art ZSL paradigms would be necessary to fully contextualize the significance of the results. The current evaluation demonstrates an advance within the attribute-based sub-field, but its standing in the broader ZSL landscape is less clear.
4.  **Scalability Concerns:** The one-vs-all framework for training the random forests may not scale well to scenarios with a very large number of unseen classes. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but for a NIPS paper, scalability is an important consideration.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a well-executed and effective solution to an important problem. The strengths—a sound methodology and thorough empirical validation against the most relevant baseline—are significant. The reported performance gains over a widely used method like DAP are compelling.

The weaknesses, particularly the incremental nature of the core algorithmic machinery and the lack of comparison to embedding-based ZSL methods, temper the excitement somewhat. The work represents a strong step forward for attribute-based ZSL, but perhaps not a paradigm shift for ZSL as a whole.

Nevertheless, the paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported by evidence, and the results are significant enough to be of interest to the community. It is a complete piece of work that advances the state of the art in its targeted sub-domain. I lean towards acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known, practical bottleneck in attribute-based ZSL.
*   The proposed solution is intuitive, technically sound, and well-executed.
*   Experimental validation is thorough, with controlled experiments, strong baselines (within its sub-field), and ablation studies.
*   The performance gains over the widely-used DAP method are substantial and meaningful.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The core mechanism of fractional propagation in trees is not fundamentally new.
*   The evaluation lacks comparison to the current state-of-the-art in non-attribute-based ZSL (i.e., embedding models).
*   Potential issues with scalability and dependency on the validation set are not fully addressed.
