Summary
This paper tackles a central, longstanding limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers at test time. Instead of taking attribute predictions at face value, the authors propose training random forests directly on class attribute signatures while explicitly integrating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers. The key idea is to fractionally propagate class signatures through the tree according to attribute true/false positive rates estimated on attribute-labeled validation data DV, redefining information gain in terms of these fractional counts. This encourages splits that remain discriminative under realistic attribute noise. The framework further accounts for uncertainty in class-attribute associations (signature noise) and extends to few-shot learning via a convex combination of signature-driven and data-driven information gains. Experiments on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) show clear improvements over standard methods (e.g., DAP) and over a signature-only RF baseline, with supportive controlled noise studies and results with discovered attributes.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: the paper contrasts two-stage attribute-based ZSL (DAP and variants), external-knowledge/embedding ZSL (hierarchies, label/feature embeddings), and discovered mid-level features. The contribution is orthogonal: it preserves the semantic attribute interface while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during classifier induction.

Strengths
- Clear, novel idea: Inject attribute ROC into the training of zero-shot classifiers via fractional propagation in a random forest. This is a new way to bridge the gap between ideal attribute signatures and noisy attribute predictions.
- Principled criterion: Redefined information gain that naturally downweights unlearnable attributes and favors robust operating points, with node-specific ROC estimation that partially captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Practical and general: Works with named or discovered attributes; simple few-shot extension via a descriptive prior; easy to implement on top of standard RFs and Platt-scaled SVMs.
- Solid empirical evidence: Consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF across three datasets, plus controlled noise experiments that diagnose when and why it helps (heterogeneous attribute noise).
- Sensible analysis: Ablations (no signature uncertainty; global vs node-specific ROC) show each component matters. Few-shot plots illustrate how the optimal prior weight evolves with more labeled data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Domain shift and ROC estimation: DV is drawn from “seen” classes; unseen classes may manifest attributes differently. While node-specific ROC helps, there remains a gap between estimated and true test-time error rates. The “upper bound” using true ROC is informative; more discussion on mitigating domain shift (e.g., reweighting DV towards unseen-like signatures, calibration under shift) would be valuable.
- Data usage/validation leakage: The setup states that 20% of attribute-labeled images are reserved for ROC estimation and then pooled with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. If the ROC is computed on a classifier trained without that 20% but then a final classifier is retrained on 100%, ROC and deployed classifier may mismatch; if ROC is recalculated after retraining, the validation set is no longer held out. Please clarify the sequence to avoid optimistic ROC estimates.
- Scalability: One-vs-all forests per unseen class may become costly as K grows. How does training/testing time scale with K, M, depth, and (#m,#t) candidates? Any sharing across classes (e.g., a single multi-class forest)?
- Comparisons breadth: The paper claims superiority to published methods on AwA and uses discovered attributes, which is great. It would strengthen the case to include direct, same-split comparisons to hierarchy/embedding ZSL baselines (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE, Costa/metric learning) when feasible, since they are prominent alternatives that do not rely on named attributes at test time.
- Modeling attribute correlations: While ROC propagation with DV(n) captures some conditional effects, splits remain univariate. Exploring oblique/multi-attribute tests or correlation-aware split selection (as noted in the conclusion) may further boost robustness, especially with entangled attributes.
- Signature uncertainty: Details are deferred to the supplement; a brief exposition in the main text would help assess how annotation noise is modeled and estimated in practice.

Evaluation
- Quality: Technically sound with a coherent learning objective and careful treatment of uncertainty through fractional propagation and entropy-based gains. The few-shot extension is straightforward yet useful. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though I would like clarity on validation usage and more timing/complexity reporting.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. The training/testing pipeline and the modified IG are clearly explained. Some implementation details (signature uncertainty, discovered-attribute handling) are deferred; including concise summaries in the main paper would improve readability.
- Originality: High. Integrating attribute ROC into tree construction for zero-shot classification is, to my knowledge, new. The idea of training forests on class signatures rather than images, with uncertainty-aware propagation, is a bold and elegant twist on attribute-based ZSL.
- Significance: High. Attribute unreliability has been a major practical blocker for ZSL; this work directly addresses it and shows substantial gains across diverse datasets. The approach preserves the semantic interface, making it appealing for human-in-the-loop category creation. The few-shot extension adds practical impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the use of DV vis-à-vis attribute classifier training to avoid leakage and ensure ROC reflects the deployed classifiers.
- Provide computational cost analysis and memory/runtime comparisons to DAP and SIGNATURE-RF.
- Add direct comparisons to strong embedding-based ZSL baselines on at least one dataset or discuss why such comparisons are infeasible under the given splits.
- Include a brief description (in the main text) of the signature uncertainty model and how its parameters are set.
- Report per-attribute reliability statistics and an analysis of which attributes the trees select; this could give insight into interpretability and the method’s preference for learnable attributes.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled treatment of attribute unreliability in ZSL
- Strong empirical improvements on multiple datasets and under controlled noise
- Maintains semantic interpretability; practical few-shot extension
- Methodologically simple and likely to influence subsequent ZSL work

Cons against acceptance
- Potential validation/training mismatch and limited discussion of domain shift
- Limited scalability discussion and breadth of baselines
- Some important details deferred to the supplement

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces an original, impactful idea that advances zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. Minor concerns around validation protocol and breadth of baselines are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution.
