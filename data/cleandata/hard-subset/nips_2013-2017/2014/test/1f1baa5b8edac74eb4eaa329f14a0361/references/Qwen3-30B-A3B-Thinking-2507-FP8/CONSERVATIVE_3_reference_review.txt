

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper proposes a zero-shot learning approach that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions during model training. The authors argue that existing zero-shot methods treat attribute predictions as perfect, while in reality, attribute classifiers are often unreliable. Their solution involves modifying random forests to propagate training signatures according to the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers, rather than assuming perfect predictions.

## Strengths
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The methodology is technically sound and builds on established random forest techniques, which is reassuring. The authors conduct experiments on three standard zero-shot datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) and provide thorough comparisons to relevant baselines, including DAP (the current state-of-the-art for attribute-based zero-shot recognition). The paper demonstrates clear improvements over DAP in the zero-shot setting, with the authors attributing these gains to their method's ability to model attribute classifier unreliability.

## Weaknesses

### Insufficient Theoretical Justification
While the authors present a compelling experimental narrative, they provide minimal theoretical justification for why their approach should work better than existing methods. The key insight—accounting for attribute unreliability through ROC characteristics during training—is intuitively reasonable, but the paper lacks a formal analysis showing why this should lead to improved generalization. The information gain formulation (Eq. 5) is presented without justification for why it should outperform standard information gain measures. Without theoretical grounding, the claimed advantages over DAP remain largely empirical.

### Limited Evidence for Core Claim
The authors claim their method "accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions," but the experimental evidence for this claim is insufficient. The controlled noise experiments (Figure 1) show improvements when noise is introduced, but this is a simplified scenario. The real test would be demonstrating that their method outperforms DAP on the same datasets using the *actual* attribute classifiers (which are known to be unreliable), not on artificially noisy versions of perfect attribute predictions. The paper acknowledges that DAP is the current state-of-the-art, but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that their method is truly addressing the *real* unreliability problem in zero-shot learning.

### Questionable Baseline Comparisons
The comparison to SIGNATURE RF (a random forest trained on signatures without uncertainty modeling) is appropriate, but the comparison to DAP is problematic. DAP is a probabilistic method that uses attribute predictions to compute a maximum a posteriori class prediction, while the authors' method is a non-probabilistic random forest approach. These are fundamentally different approaches, and the paper does not adequately explain why the random forest approach should outperform the probabilistic method in this context. The authors claim their method "beats DAP despite using only 80% of the attribute-labeled images," but this is misleading—they state they use 80% of the data to train the attribute classifiers, not that they use less data overall.

### Overstated Contributions
The paper claims their method "explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions," but the technique is essentially a modification of standard decision trees to handle uncertain features, which has been explored in other contexts (e.g., [21, 28] cited in the paper). The authors don't clearly distinguish their contribution from these prior works, and the paper's title and narrative overstate the novelty of the approach.

### Limited Validation
The paper demonstrates improvements on three datasets, but these datasets are standard benchmarks for zero-shot learning. The authors don't provide evidence that their method will generalize to other domains or more challenging zero-shot settings. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is interesting but lacks sufficient evaluation, with results shown only for one dataset (AwA) in Figure 2a.

## Recommendation

This paper addresses a relevant problem in zero-shot learning, but the evidence supporting its claims is insufficient to justify acceptance at NIPS. While the approach is technically sound and builds on established techniques, the paper overstates its novelty and lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the claimed improvements. The experiments demonstrate improvements over DAP, but don't convincingly show that these improvements are due to the authors' core innovation (accounting for attribute unreliability) rather than other factors. The paper would benefit from more thorough theoretical analysis, stronger experimental validation with the actual unreliable attribute classifiers, and clearer distinction from related work on handling uncertain features in decision trees.

I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting modification to zero-shot learning, but the evidence is not strong enough to justify the claims of significant advancement over the state-of-the-art. The authors should provide more rigorous theoretical justification and deeper experimental analysis before considering this work for publication at NIPS.
