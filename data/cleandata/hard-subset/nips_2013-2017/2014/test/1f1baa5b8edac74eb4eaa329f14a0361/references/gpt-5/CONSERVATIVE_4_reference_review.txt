Summary
The paper addresses zero-shot recognition via attributes when attribute classifiers are unreliable. The core idea is to train class-specific random forests over class–attribute signatures, but to account for attribute prediction errors during training by (i) propagating attribute-labeled validation data down the tree to estimate node-specific ROC operating characteristics for each attribute, and (ii) fractionally propagating class signatures at each node based on the estimated TP/FP/TN/FN rates for the split attribute and threshold. This leads to an information gain objective defined on soft counts that favors both discriminative and reliably predictable attributes/thresholds. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in class–attribute signatures and to a few-shot setting by mixing signature-based and data-based information gains with a weighting parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN report improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, plus ablations.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on attribute-based zero-shot methods (DAP, signature matching) and on decision-tree techniques for missing/uncertain features (fractional propagation, soft node memberships). Compared to label/feature embedding approaches (e.g., ALE, DeViSE, ESZSL/SJE), the focus remains on human-interpretable attributes and explicit modeling of attribute unreliability.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus: attribute unreliability is a known bottleneck; explicitly modeling ROC characteristics is sensible and practical.
- Methodologically coherent adaptation of random forests to zero-shot training on signatures with soft propagation; the node-specific ROC estimation is a thoughtful way to respect attribute dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Empirical gains over DAP across three datasets, with sensible ablations (no signature uncertainty, no recursive ROC).
- Few-shot extension that uses signatures as a prior is natural and shows consistent improvements over image-only training at low data regimes.
- Reasonably detailed experimental protocol (20 trials, cross-validated depth/parameters).

Weaknesses
- Potential leakage/bias in ROC estimation: experiments state the 20% validation subset is “pooled” back with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. If the same images used to compute ROC are also used to train the attribute SVMs, ROC estimates are optimistic and coupled to the trained classifier, risking overfitting the forest’s split selection. A strictly held-out calibration set is needed.
- Baselines are limited. DAP and signature-RF are weak by today’s standards. Missing comparisons to competitive zero-shot embedding methods (ALE/Label-Embedding, DeViSE, ESZSL/SJE), hierarchical or co-occurrence transfer (Mensink et al.), and “attribute decoding” alternatives beyond one cited instance on AwA limit the strength of claims.
- No comparison to a straightforward “ROC-aware DAP” (e.g., incorporating attribute confusion matrices into a probabilistic model or cost-sensitive thresholds). Without this, it is unclear whether the gains are due to RF structure or simply better accounting for attribute errors.
- Scalability and complexity are not analyzed: the method trains K forests (100 trees each), requires per-node ROC estimation, and relies on attribute-labeled data. It is unclear how this scales with larger M, K, or when unseen classes are numerous.
- Statistical rigor: while means and standard errors are reported, statistical significance testing is absent; SUN split selection is random (10 unseen classes) but not standardized; generalization to the generalized zero-shot setting (seen+unseen at test) is not evaluated.
- Theoretical support is minimal. Claims of robustness are purely empirical; no bounds or conditions under which fractional propagation improves over naïve use of signatures are provided.

Quality
Technically plausible and generally sound, but the ROC calibration procedure raises concerns about data reuse. The ablations help, yet stronger, modern baselines and a ROC-aware probabilistic baseline are needed to support the central claim. Few-shot integration is reasonable but fairly standard in spirit.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, the motivation is well explained, and the method is described in sufficient detail to reproduce, assuming the supplementary provides pseudocode and noise model specifics. However, the description of the validation split/training of attribute classifiers is ambiguous and should be clarified to avoid confusion about leakage.

Originality
Adapting fractional propagation and ROC-aware splitting to the zero-shot setting trained on signatures is a novel combination, though each component (handling uncertainty in trees; attribute-based zero-shot) is established. The contribution is incremental but focused.

Significance
If validated against stronger baselines with careful calibration, the idea could be useful for practitioners who must rely on attributes. As presented, the impact is limited by the evaluation scope and potential calibration bias.

Suggestions for improvement
- Use a strictly held-out ROC calibration set not used to train attribute classifiers; report results under this protocol.
- Add strong baselines: ALE/DeViSE/ESZSL/SJE, hierarchical transfer, and a “confusion-matrix-aware DAP.”
- Evaluate the generalized zero-shot setting (seen and unseen at test) and report harmonic mean.
- Provide computational complexity and memory analysis; discuss scaling to larger K and M.
- Perform sensitivity analyses: size of DV, quality of attribute classifiers, calibration drift across nodes.
- Include significance tests and standardized splits for SUN (or multiple random unseen sets with per-split results in supp).

Pros
- Addresses a real, often-ignored issue (attribute unreliability) with a concrete method.
- Clear, intuitive mechanism (node-specific ROC, fractional propagation).
- Consistent empirical improvements over DAP and ablations; useful few-shot extension.

Cons
- Ambiguous and potentially biased ROC calibration; risk of overfitting.
- Missing strong baselines and a fair “ROC-aware DAP” comparator.
- Limited analysis of scalability and no generalized ZSL evaluation.
- Lacks theoretical grounding; impact may be incremental.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the experimental validation is not rigorous enough relative to established alternatives, and the calibration protocol needs to be made clearly unbiased before strong claims can be supported.
