- Overall assessment: valuable, well-motivated contribution and a strong accept for NIPS.  
- Core problem: existing attribute-based ZSL assumes reliable attribute predictions, which is unrealistic.  
- Proposed method: random forest that incorporates attribute uncertainty via ROC curves and fractional signature propagation.  
- Extension: method naturally extends to the few-shot setting.  
- Positioning: clearly differentiated from hierarchical/latent-transfer methods and related probabilistic/missing-data tree work.  
- Key strength: explicitly models attribute unreliability, addressing a major practical ZSL bottleneck.  
- Key strength: principled use of ROC weighting lets the model learn to rely on more reliable attributes.  
- Key strength: strong empirical gains over DAP on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets.  
- Key strength: ablation and controlled-noise experiments convincingly show the method’s benefits and robustness.  
- Key strength: well-written, clear methodology, thorough related work, and well-designed experiments.  
- Weakness: likely higher computational cost (ROC computation and fractional propagation) with no complexity/scale discussion.  
- Weakness: depends on a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves; sensitivity to its size/representativeness is not explored.  
- Weakness: few-shot lambda parameter is tuned by cross-validation but lacks deeper analysis or principled selection guidance.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of failure cases — lacks detail on which classes/attributes perform poorly.  
- Pro-accept arguments: solves an important ZSL problem, is theoretically sound, empirically validated, extends to few-shot, and is well-presented.  
- Con-accept arguments: computational complexity, validation-data dependence, and need for more parameter/failure-case analysis.  
- Specific suggestions: add a visual example of fractional propagation, plot ROC curves used, discuss computational complexity/scalability, and include analysis of failure cases.
