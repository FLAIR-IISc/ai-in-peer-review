Summary
The paper addresses zero-shot recognition via attributes, focusing on the practical problem that attribute detectors are unreliable. The main contribution is a random forest framework that trains on class-level attribute signatures but explicitly models attribute classifier error using ROC statistics measured on attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes according to the estimated TP/FP rates at each node/threshold, and information gain is computed on these soft counts. The method can also incorporate uncertainty in class-attribute associations and is extended to few-shot by linearly combining information gains from signature-based splitting and standard splitting on few labeled examples. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline; controlled noise experiments further illustrate robustness. There is also a demonstration with discovered “pseudo-attributes” and a few-shot study.

Strengths
- Addresses a real, well-known pain point of attribute-based zero-shot methods: detector unreliability.
- Methodologically simple, leveraging ROC statistics in a way that is intuitive and compatible with decision-tree learning.
- The node-specific propagation of validation data to compute local ROC seems a sensible way to partially account for attribute dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Clear ablations (w/o signature uncertainty, w/o node-specific ROC propagation) and controlled noise studies help isolate where gains come from.
- Empirical gains over a standard DAP baseline on three datasets; the approach appears generally applicable (named or discovered attributes).
- Few-shot extension is straightforward and shows improvements over using few labeled images alone.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baselines feel limited for a NeurIPS contribution. DAP is a dated and relatively weak baseline by current standards. While label/feature embedding approaches (e.g., Akata et al., Frome et al.) are cited, direct comparisons are missing. Without stronger baselines, it is hard to judge significance beyond the attribute pipeline community.
- The approach critically depends on ROC estimation on “seen-class” validation data. The paper acknowledges the distribution shift but does not thoroughly assess sensitivity: How stable are TPR/FPR across seen/unseen attribute combinations? The “upper bound” using test-class validation is informative but unrealistic; more analysis on how validation composition affects performance is needed.
- Potential methodological leakage/clarity issue: the paper “reserves 20% as ROC validation, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” If ROC is computed with an SVM trained on all data (including the ROC set), the ROC may be optimistic. If ROC is computed on a holdout trained on 80%, while the final test-time SVM is retrained on 100%, there is a train/validation mismatch. This should be clarified and ideally fixed via a clean nested procedure.
- The few-shot extension is essentially a linear combination of two information-gain criteria with a tuned λ. It’s plausible but somewhat ad hoc; comparisons to strong few-shot baselines (metric learning/prototypical networks or semantic-embedding few-shot methods) are absent. Sensitivity to λ and its cross-validation protocol deserves more detail.
- Scalability: The one-vs-all forest per unseen class is O(K) in training/inference. For large K (common in zero-shot settings), complexity and memory may be problematic. No complexity analysis or empirical runtime/memory reporting is provided.
- The reliance on Platt-calibrated SVM scores is assumed; calibration quality affects ROC stability. No calibration sanity checks or alternative calibrations are discussed.
- The method assumes access to ample attribute-labeled images; this dependence should be discussed as a practical limitation relative to embedding-based methods that can leverage text corpora.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the fractional propagation using ROCs is reasonable and well-motivated. However, the evaluation would be stronger with additional modern baselines, a rigorous treatment of the ROC estimation protocol, and sensitivity analyses (validation set size/composition, calibration). Statistical testing beyond mean±SE is not reported; effect sizes are moderate in some settings.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable. Some crucial implementation details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, noise model, signature uncertainty formulation). The data usage for ROC/training needs clarification to avoid ambiguity. Complexity/runtime reporting would improve clarity for practitioners.

Originality
- The high-level idea—accounting for attribute unreliability—is not new, but the specific integration of ROC-based fractional propagation into zero-shot decision forests appears novel in this context. The link to fractional tuples/missing values in trees is appropriate; the zero-shot adaptation is the main novelty.

Significance
- Within attribute-based zero-shot recognition, the paper advances robustness in a practical way and shows consistent gains over DAP. However, without comparisons to stronger contemporary zero-shot methods that do not rely on attributes, the broader impact for NeurIPS is less clear. The few-shot extension is incremental.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the exact protocol for ROC estimation vs. final attribute classifier training. Provide results with a clean split (no pooling into final SVMs), and/or show that results are stable.
- Compare against semantic embedding baselines (e.g., label embedding, DeViSE) under identical features to contextualize gains.
- Provide sensitivity to the amount and composition of ROC validation data, and to calibration (Platt vs. isotonic).
- Report computational cost and memory as K scales; consider multiclass forests to avoid one-vs-all.
- Analyze per-attribute reliability: do trees consistently select the same “learnable” attributes? Are node-specific ROC estimates stable given limited data?
- For few-shot, compare to metric/embedding-based few-shot methods and clarify λ selection and generalization.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, intuitive method improves robustness of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Clear ablations and controlled experiments; consistent gains on multiple datasets.
- Applicable to both named and discovered attributes; simple to implement.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to stronger modern baselines; unclear broader significance.
- Ambiguity in ROC/training protocol; potential optimistic bias.
- Few-shot extension is modest and not rigorously benchmarked.
- Scalability and calibration issues not fully addressed.

Recommendation
Leaning toward reject in its current form. The idea is sensible and likely useful to practitioners using attributes, but the empirical validation is not yet convincing against stronger baselines, and certain methodological details need tightening. Strengthening comparisons, clarifying the ROC protocol, and adding sensitivity/complexity analyses could make this a solid contribution.
