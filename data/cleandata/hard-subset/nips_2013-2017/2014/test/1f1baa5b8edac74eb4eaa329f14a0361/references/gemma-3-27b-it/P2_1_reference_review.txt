## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot recognition, specifically tackling the practical issue of unreliable attribute predictions. Existing zero-shot methods typically assume accurate attribute information, which is often not the case in real-world scenarios. The authors propose a random forest-based approach that explicitly models and accounts for attribute unreliability during training. They achieve this by incorporating attribute classifier ROC curves into the decision-making process, effectively weighting decisions based on attribute prediction confidence.  The method is further extended to handle signature uncertainty and adapted to a few-shot learning setting.  Experiments on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly when attribute predictions are noisy.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Critical Practical Limitation:** The paper directly tackles a major roadblock in practical zero-shot learning – the inherent unreliability of attribute classifiers. This is a significant contribution as it moves the field beyond idealized assumptions.
* **Novel Approach:**  The proposed method of incorporating ROC curves into the random forest training process is a clever and effective way to model attribute unreliability. The propagation of fractional signatures is a well-reasoned and innovative idea.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, outperforming existing approaches (including the widely-used DAP) on multiple datasets.  The controlled noise experiments convincingly showcase the benefits of handling attribute unreliability.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The connection to existing work on handling uncertainty in decision trees (fractional tuples, etc.) is clearly explained, providing a strong foundation for the proposed approach.
* **Few-Shot Extension:** The extension to few-shot learning adds further practical value and demonstrates the adaptability of the proposed framework.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While not explicitly discussed, incorporating ROC curves and propagating fractional signatures likely adds significant computational overhead compared to traditional random forest training.  A discussion of the computational cost and potential scalability issues would be valuable.
* **ROC Validation Data Requirement:** The method requires a separate validation dataset for estimating ROC curves. While the authors use a subset of the attribute-labeled data for this purpose, it would be beneficial to analyze the sensitivity of the performance to the size of this validation set.
* **Limited Ablation on Few-Shot:** The few-shot extension is presented with a relatively limited set of experiments. A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different λ values and varying numbers of training examples would strengthen the results.
* **Dependence on Attribute Quality:** While the method *handles* unreliable attributes, it still fundamentally relies on having a reasonably good set of attributes to begin with. The paper doesn’t address the challenges of attribute discovery or selection.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The proposed method is a complete and well-defined contribution. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clearly written and well-organized. The technical details are explained adequately, though some sections could benefit from further elaboration (e.g., the details of the fractional signature propagation). **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The approach of incorporating ROC curves into random forest training for zero-shot learning is novel and represents a significant improvement over existing methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:** The results are important and address a significant practical challenge in zero-shot learning. The method has the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners working in this area. It advances the state-of-the-art and provides a new perspective on how to handle uncertainty in attribute-based learning. **(Strong)**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods.
* Proposes a novel and effective approach to handle attribute unreliability.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Well-written and technically sound.
* Significant potential impact on the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity is not thoroughly discussed.
* The validation data requirement could be a limitation in some scenarios.
* Ablation study for the few-shot extension is limited.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall impact and value of the work. I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
