

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses an important challenge in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest-based approach that accounts for attribute classifier unreliability by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) during tree construction. The core idea is to propagate training signatures through the tree according to the estimated false positive/true positive rates of attribute classifiers, rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions.

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to address a genuine problem in zero-shot learning, as acknowledged by the authors: "attribute-based zero-shot recognition remains in the 'proof of concept' realm, in practice falling short of alternate transfer methods." The proposed method is technically sound, with clear mathematical formulations for the modified information gain and signature propagation. The authors conduct experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) and demonstrate improvements over DAP (the leading attribute-based zero-shot method) and their own signature-based random forest baseline.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a real limitation in existing zero-shot learning approaches that has been noted in prior work (e.g., [23, 18]).
2. The method is well-motivated and the technical details are clearly presented.
3. The experimental results are generally convincing, showing consistent improvements over baselines across multiple datasets.
4. The few-shot extension is a reasonable and useful addition to the core contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited novelty:** The paper claims to be the first to account for attribute unreliability, but this is not entirely accurate. The authors cite [18] which provides PAC bounds on tolerable error for mid-level classifiers, though it doesn't propose a solution. More significantly, the core idea of incorporating uncertainty into decision trees has precedents in the literature (e.g., [21, 28]). The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing work on handling missing or probabilistic features.

2. **Questionable necessity:** The authors claim their method "significantly outperforms" DAP, but the gains (typically 2-5% absolute improvement) are modest. Given that DAP is a simple, well-established method that requires no additional validation data, it's questionable whether the added complexity of their approach is justified for such marginal improvements. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the attribute unreliability they address is the primary bottleneck in existing approaches.

3. **Insufficient comparison to relevant baselines:** The authors compare to DAP and a signature-based random forest, but omit several relevant zero-shot learning approaches that have emerged since DAP's introduction. For instance, methods like [1, 4, 13] that exploit semantic relationships rather than attributes could provide a more meaningful comparison to demonstrate the value of their approach.

4. **Validation data dependency:** The method requires a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves, which the authors reserve 20% of attribute-labeled data for. This is a significant constraint in practical applications where labeled data is scarce. The paper doesn't adequately address how this dependency affects the method's applicability.

5. **Lack of theoretical justification:** While the paper provides a clear algorithmic description, it lacks theoretical analysis explaining why their approach should work better than existing methods. The experimental results alone don't sufficiently establish the theoretical underpinnings of the improvement.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound approach to a recognized problem in zero-shot learning, but the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The marginal improvements over established baselines (DAP) are not sufficiently compelling to justify the added complexity of the proposed method, especially given the practical constraints of requiring additional validation data. While the paper is well-written and the experiments are generally well-executed, the significance of the contribution falls short of the bar for NIPS, which typically expects more substantial advances.

The authors have made a reasonable attempt to address an important limitation in zero-shot learning, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that their approach represents a meaningful step forward compared to existing methods. The modest gains (typically 2-5% absolute improvement) don't seem to justify the added complexity of the proposed framework, particularly when compared to simpler methods like DAP that require no additional validation data.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper addresses a genuine problem and presents a technically sound method, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and significance to warrant publication at NIPS. The improvements over existing methods are marginal, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their approach represents a substantial advancement over the state of the art. A more compelling case would require either significantly larger performance gains, theoretical justification for the approach, or demonstration of applicability in more challenging settings where existing methods fail.
