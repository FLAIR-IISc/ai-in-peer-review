### Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning (ZSL) in the context of attribute-based classification. The authors identify a critical weakness in existing attribute-based ZSL methods: the unreliability of the underlying attribute classifiers. Standard methods, such as Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP), typically train a model on idealized, ground-truth attribute signatures but apply it at test time to noisy, predicted attribute vectors, ignoring the known error characteristics of the classifiers.

The central contribution of this work is a novel zero-shot random forest (RF) algorithm that explicitly incorporates knowledge of attribute classifier unreliability into its training process. Instead of training on image data, the RF is trained discriminatively on the attribute signatures of the unseen classes. The key mechanism involves propagating these signatures fractionally down the decision trees based on the attribute classifiers' Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves, which are estimated on a held-out validation set. This procedure encourages the forest to select splits based on attributes that are not only discriminative for the ideal signatures but are also reliably predictable in practice. The authors also present extensions to account for uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning setting. The method is evaluated on three standard ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), where it demonstrates improved performance over the established DAP baseline.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies and directly addresses a significant and practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning. The discrepancy between noise-free training assumptions (using class signatures) and noisy test-time application (using classifier predictions) is a well-known but often overlooked issue. The motivation to create a model that is "aware" of its own input uncertainty is well-founded.

2.  **Methodological Idea:** The core idea of using classifier performance statistics (the ROC curve) to guide the construction of a discriminative model is interesting. The fractional propagation of signatures based on true/false positive rates provides a principled way to embed expected error into the learning process. This is a more sophisticated approach than simply ignoring the issue or using post-hoc corrections.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The controlled noise experiments (Fig 1) provide a clear and intuitive validation of the method's core hypothesis. The main results on three standard benchmarks show consistent, albeit moderate, improvements over the widely-used DAP baseline. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of the different components of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Increased Complexity for Modest Gains:** The proposed method introduces considerable complexity compared to established baselines like DAP. It requires a custom random forest training procedure, estimation of ROC curves for every attribute, management of a separate validation set (`DV`), and tuning of additional hyperparameters (tree depth, λ, etc.). While the results show an improvement, the margin over DAP (e.g., ~4% on AwA, from 40.5% to 44.1%) raises the question of whether this added machinery provides a sufficient return on complexity. An alternative and perhaps simpler direction would be to focus on improving the attribute classifiers themselves or incorporating uncertainty directly into the probabilistic framework of DAP, rather than building a new classification architecture.

2.  **Dependence on the Validation Set:** The method's performance is critically dependent on the representativeness of the validation set `DV` used to estimate the ROC curves. This set is drawn from the "seen" classes. However, the visual appearance of attributes and the correlations between classifier errors may differ significantly between the seen and unseen classes. The "OURS+TRUE ROC" experiment, which uses unseen class images for validation, shows a very large performance gap (e.g., 44.1% vs. 59.8% on AwA). This suggests that the ROCs estimated from seen classes are a poor proxy for the true error characteristics on the target domain, which is a fundamental limitation of the proposed approach that is not fully resolved.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed method relies on a one-versus-all training scheme, where a separate random forest is trained for each unseen class. This approach is computationally intensive and does not scale gracefully to settings with a large number of unseen classes, which is a common scenario in real-world ZSL. Established methods like DAP are inherently more scalable as they do not require retraining for each class. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, it remains a practical weakness of the current formulation.

4.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The primary baseline is DAP. While DAP is a foundational method, the comparison could be strengthened by considering other ways to handle attribute uncertainty. For example, one could simply weight attributes in the DAP similarity computation based on their validation accuracy, which would be a much simpler heuristic. It is not entirely clear from the experiments whether the performance gain comes from the specific fractional propagation mechanism or simply from the fact that a random forest is a more powerful non-linear classifier than the Bayesian inference used in DAP.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a novel and technically interesting approach to a real problem in zero-shot learning. The idea of making the model aware of attribute unreliability during training is sound. However, the methodological complexity, strong dependence on a potentially mismatched validation set, and concerns about scalability temper the significance of the contribution. The empirical gains over a standard, simpler baseline are consistent but not transformative. The work is a solid piece of engineering, but it feels more like an intricate patch for the problem of poor attributes rather than a fundamental advance. While the ideas are worth considering, the current approach may not be practical or compelling enough to replace established methods given its overhead.

**Recommendation:** Borderline Accept.
