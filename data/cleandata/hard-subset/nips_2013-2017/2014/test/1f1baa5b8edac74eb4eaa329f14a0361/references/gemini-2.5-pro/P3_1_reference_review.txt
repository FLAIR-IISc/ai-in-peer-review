This paper addresses the critical and often-overlooked problem of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The standard ZSL pipeline relies on first predicting a set of semantic attributes for a test image and then mapping these predictions to an unseen class label. The authors correctly argue that this pipeline is brittle because the intermediate attribute classifiers are imperfect, a fact that most existing methods ignore. The core contribution is a novel random forest framework that explicitly models the unreliability of attribute classifiers during the training of the zero-shot model. Instead of training on images, the forests are trained on the idealized attribute signatures of the unseen classes. The key innovation is to use the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers, estimated on a validation set, to fractionally propagate these signatures down the decision trees. This allows the model to learn splits that are not only discriminative in the ideal attribute space but are also robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The authors further extend this model to account for uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and demonstrate a natural extension to the few-shot learning setting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly practical problem in attribute-based ZSL. While the imperfection of attribute classifiers is widely acknowledged, few methods have proposed a principled way to directly incorporate this uncertainty into the ZSL model itself. The proposed solution of using classifier ROCs to guide the training of a random forest on class signatures is novel, elegant, and intuitive. It moves beyond simply using the probabilistic *output* of an attribute classifier (like DAP) to modeling the classifier's inherent *error characteristics*. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of fractional propagation of signatures based on TP/FP rates (Eq. 4) is a clever way to simulate the distribution of noisy predictions for an ideal signature. The decision to recursively propagate the validation data to compute node-specific ROCs is a particularly strong point, as it allows the model to capture dependencies between attributes (e.g., the performance of a "furry" classifier might change for a subset of data that has already been classified as "brown"). The information gain formulation (Eq. 5) naturally follows from this, leading to the selection of splits that are both discriminative and reliably predictable.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The controlled noise experiment (Figure 1) is excellent. It provides clear and interpretable evidence that the proposed model works as intended, showing robustness precisely where the baselines fail. The comparison between uniform and attribute-variant noise effectively highlights the model's ability to favor more "learnable" attributes.
    *   Results on three standard and diverse datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrate the method's general applicability. The significant improvements over DAP, a very strong and widely-used baseline, underscore the practical value of the contribution. The ablation studies effectively tease apart the contributions of the core idea, the signature uncertainty model, and the ROC propagation.
    *   Showing that the method achieves state-of-the-art results with both named and discovered attributes demonstrates its flexibility and relevance to different attribute-based ZSL paradigms.
    *   The few-shot learning extension is well-motivated and the results clearly show the benefit of using signatures as a prior, with performance gracefully improving as more data becomes available.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Scalability:** The framework is presented using a one-versus-all approach, where one random forest is trained for each unseen class. While feasible for the K=10 or K=12 classes in the experiments, this approach may not scale well to scenarios with hundreds or thousands of unseen classes, which is a target for large-scale ZSL. The authors mention this as future work, but a brief discussion of potential multi-class tree formulations in the main paper would be beneficial.

2.  **Validation Data Dependency:** The method's performance hinges on the assumption that the ROC statistics estimated on the validation set (drawn from *seen* classes) are representative of the classifier's performance on images of *unseen* classes. This is a potential point of failure due to domain shift. For instance, the visual appearance of the attribute "striped" might be different on a seen "shirt" class versus an unseen "zebra" class, leading to different classifier error profiles. The "OURS+TRUE ROC" experiment provides a useful upper bound, but could the authors elaborate on the sensitivity to this domain shift? Have they considered any strategies to mitigate this, such as re-weighting the validation set based on signature similarity to the unseen classes?

3.  **Clarity on Validation Data Propagation:** A minor point on clarity: the mechanism for propagating the validation set `DV(n)` could be made more explicit. At a given node `n`, a split `(m, t)` is chosen based on the information gain from the fractionally-propagated signatures. The text states `DV` is then "recursively propagated down the tree following each split". Does this mean `DV(n)` is split according to the ground truth attribute labels `y(m)` for the chosen attribute `m`? This seems to be the most logical interpretation, but clarifying this point would help reproducibility.

4.  **Comparison to Embedding Methods:** The related work section is thorough on attribute-based methods. However, a brief discussion contextualizing the work with respect to the other major ZSL paradigm—direct visual-to-semantic embedding (e.g., DeViSE, Socher et al.)—would strengthen the paper. These methods bypass the brittle intermediate attribute classification step entirely. While the authors' focus on language-based description is a valid and distinct goal, explicitly stating the trade-offs (e.g., interpretability and direct human control vs. end-to-end optimization) would better position the paper within the broader ZSL landscape.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective solution to a significant problem in zero-shot learning. The methodology is sound, and the extensive experiments provide strong support for the authors' claims. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to limitations and potential future work rather than fundamental flaws. The work is a clear advancement for attribute-based recognition and is very likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
