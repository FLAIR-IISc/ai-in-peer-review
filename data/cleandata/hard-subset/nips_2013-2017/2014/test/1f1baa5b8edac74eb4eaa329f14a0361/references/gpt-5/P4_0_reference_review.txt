Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses zero-shot learning with semantic attributes when attribute classifiers are unreliable. Prior zero-shot approaches typically follow a two-stage pipeline: predict attributes for a test image, then infer the class by comparing predicted attributes to class signatures [3, 8, 18, 30]. Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8, 9] is a representative probabilistic method. Other lines exploit semantic relationships or embeddings to transfer to unseen classes [1, 4, 13, 14, 23]. Acknowledging that learned attributes are error-prone, some work discovers discriminative mid-level features instead of nameable attributes [11, 22, 26, 27, 30], but these are less applicable to human-defined, attribute-based zero-shot description.

The paper’s main idea is a random forest that is trained not on images but on class attribute signatures, explicitly modeling attribute unreliability via ROC statistics computed from attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, class signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes according to estimated TP/FP rates at candidate thresholds (node-specific ROCs computed on the subset of validation data reaching that node). The split is selected by maximizing information gain with respect to these fractional counts. The approach also incorporates uncertainty in class signatures (annotation noise) and extends naturally to few-shot learning by interpolating information gain computed on signatures with gain computed on the small set of class-labeled images. Experiments on AwA [8], aPY [3], and SUN attributes [20] demonstrate consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only random forest baseline, with controlled-noise experiments supporting the mechanism’s robustness. The method performs competitively using discovered attributes [30] as well.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated treatment of a practical issue: attribute unreliability. Unlike prior two-stage methods that take attribute predictions at face value, the proposed training criterion explicitly anticipates false positives/negatives.
- Technically sound integration of ROC-based uncertainty into decision trees via fractional propagation and information gain on soft counts. The node-specific ROCs capture conditional dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Simple, general framework: the same machinery handles signature uncertainty and few-shot learning (via a convex combination of signature-based and data-based gains).
- Empirical validation across three datasets with >250 attributes, including ablations (no signature uncertainty; no node-specific ROC propagation), controlled-noise stress tests, and comparisons to published results (including discovered attributes [30]).
- Practical details (e.g., handling low sample counts at nodes, hyperparameters, cross-validation) are provided, and results are averaged over multiple trials with standard errors.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Data usage for ROC estimation vs. attribute classifier training is not perfectly clear. The paper states that 20% of attribute-labeled images are reserved for ROC validation and then pooled with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. If the ROC is estimated using models trained on only 80% while test-time uses models trained on 100%, there is a mismatch; if ROC is estimated on data also used to fit the final attribute classifiers, there is optimistic bias. Clarifying the exact sequence (and whether ROC is recomputed after retraining) would strengthen the methodological soundness.
- Scalability to many unseen classes: the approach trains a one-vs-all forest per unseen class and uses per-node ROC estimates. Experiments use K≈10–12. Complexity and memory usage for K in the hundreds are not analyzed.
- Signature uncertainty is relegated to the supplement; the main text would benefit from a concise description and a quantitative ablation isolating its contribution (the table mentions it, but details of the noise model are missing).
- While comparisons to DAP and a discovered-attribute method [30] are provided (and a figure compares to multiple published results on AwA), more systematic comparisons to embedding-based zero-shot methods (e.g., [1, 4, 13, 14]) across all datasets would better position the contribution. The field has multiple families of approaches with different assumptions; including them clarifies scope and strengths.
- The method assumes availability of attribute-labeled data whose error profiles are representative for unseen classes. The paper acknowledges domain shift (and shows an “upper bound” with “true ROC” using unseen-class images), and an initial attempt to select more relevant validation data had negligible impact. Further analysis of when validation ROCs generalize (e.g., by measuring calibration shift) would be useful.
- The controlled-noise experiment starts from perfect attribute scores and adds synthetic noise. This is informative but somewhat idealized; additional analyses using real attribute score distributions (e.g., varying per-attribute calibration or AUC) could add robustness.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The fractional propagation and entropy-based split selection are standard in spirit (cf. missing/probabilistic features in trees [16, 21, 28]) but novelly adapted to zero-shot training on signatures with ROC-informed propagation. The node-specific ROC computation is a reasonable way to capture conditional dependencies induced by splits. The few-shot extension is straightforward and coherent. Key concerns are the ROC/attribute-training data handling and scalability analysis.
- Empirical support: Results show clear gains over DAP and the signature-only RF, with ablations supporting design choices. Reporting mean±SE over 20 trials is good practice.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The methodology is clearly explained with equations, and implementation details are given. Some important pieces (signature uncertainty, pseudocode) are in the supplement; a brief in-paper presentation of signature uncertainty would aid readability. Clarify the data protocol for ROC estimation vs. attribute training, and briefly discuss computational complexity.

Originality
- The central idea—training zero-shot classifiers via forests over signatures that account for attribute unreliability with ROC-based fractional propagation—is novel relative to DAP-like methods and prior work on missing/probabilistic features in trees. The few-shot extension as a descriptive prior in forests is a reasonable, if incremental, addition.

Significance
- The work addresses a major barrier to attribute-based zero-shot learning and shows consistent improvements on standard datasets. It is likely to influence subsequent work that uses semantic mid-level representations and could generalize beyond attributes (e.g., poselets, mid-level patches). Practical impact depends on availability and representativeness of attribute-labeled data and scalability to many unseen classes.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real, underexplored limitation (attribute unreliability) with a principled, effective method.
- Strong empirical gains over widely used baselines across datasets; robust ablations and controlled experiments.
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable; few-shot extension is natural and useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity and potential bias in ROC estimation protocol relative to attribute classifier training.
- Limited scalability analysis and evaluation for large numbers of unseen classes.
- Comparisons to alternative zero-shot families (label/feature embeddings) could be more comprehensive across datasets.
- Some methodological details (signature uncertainty) deferred to the supplement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and, if needed, revise the ROC estimation protocol to avoid mismatch or optimism; consider using a held-out validation set not used for final attribute training, or recomputing ROC with cross-validated predictions.
- Provide complexity analysis and experiments with larger K to assess scalability (e.g., multi-label/multi-class forests rather than one-vs-all).
- Summarize the signature uncertainty model in the main paper and quantify its impact more explicitly.
- Broaden empirical comparisons to embedding-based zero-shot methods on all three datasets.
- Analyze sensitivity to calibration shift between seen and unseen classes (e.g., via temperature scaling or per-attribute calibration diagnostics).
