- Addresses unreliable attribute predictions in zero-shot learning by integrating attribute ROC curves into random forest training.
- Also models signature uncertainty and naturally extends to few-shot learning.
- Shows significant improvements over state-of-the-art on AwA, aPY, and SUN, especially when attribute classifiers are less reliable.
- Technically sound with clear mathematical formulation, theoretical justification, and thorough experimental design (20 trials).
- Ablation studies confirm both attribute unreliability modeling and signature uncertainty are necessary for best performance.
- Requires validation data to estimate ROC curves, which may be impractical in some settings.
- Does not fully explore performance when attribute classifiers are near-random (extremely unreliable).
- Computational complexity is not explicitly discussed.
- Well-written, clearly organized, and includes reproducibility details, good figures, and helpful supplementary material.
- Original contribution: first to explicitly account for attribute unreliability during training; novel use of fractional-tuple ideas in zero-shot context.
- Significant impact: makes attribute-based zero-shot learning more practical and works with hand-designed and discovered attributes.
- Empirically shows modeling unreliability can be more valuable than simply having more attribute-labeled data (outperforms DAP with 80% data).
- Positions itself well relative to related work and clarifies distinction between attribute unreliability and attribute strength.
- Overall assessment: strong, meaningful contribution that is theoretically sound and empirically effective.
- Recommendation: accept (NIPS).
- Specific suggestion 1: analyze performance when TPR = FPR for attributes to clarify method limits.
- Specific suggestion 2: include more comparisons to existing few-shot methods.
- Specific suggestion 3: discuss extensions to multi-label attributes.
- Specific suggestion 4: propose strategies to estimate ROC curves when validation data is unavailable (e.g., using seen-class data).
