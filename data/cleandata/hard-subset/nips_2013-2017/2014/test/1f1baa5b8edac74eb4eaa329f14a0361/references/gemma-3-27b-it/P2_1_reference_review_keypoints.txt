- Tackles zero-shot recognition under realistic conditions where attribute predictions are unreliable.  
- Proposes a random forest method that models attribute unreliability by incorporating attribute classifier ROC curves.  
- Uses propagation of fractional class signatures through trees to weight decisions by attribute confidence.  
- Extends the approach to handle signature uncertainty and a few-shot learning setting.  
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets, showing significant improvements over prior methods, especially with noisy attributes.  
- Addresses a key practical limitation of zero-shot learning by moving beyond the assumption of perfect attributes.  
- Novel integration of ROC curves into random forest training is a clever and effective idea.  
- Fractional signature propagation is an innovative mechanism for handling uncertainty in tree splits.  
- Strong empirical results and controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate benefits.  
- Theoretical connection to prior work on uncertainty in decision trees is well-explained.  
- The few-shot extension increases practical applicability of the method.  
- Paper is generally clear, well-written, and organized, though some technical details could use more elaboration.  
- Potential computational overhead from incorporating ROC curves and fractional propagation is not discussed.  
- Method requires a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves; sensitivity to validation size is not analyzed.  
- Ablation experiments for the few-shot extension (e.g., varying λ and sample counts) are limited.  
- Still depends on having a reasonably good initial attribute set; does not address attribute discovery/selection.  
- Quality: technically sound and claims well-supported.  
- Clarity: mostly clear, with some sections needing more detail.  
- Originality: novel and differentiates from previous work.  
- Significance: addresses an important practical problem and advances the field.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a key limitation, proposes a novel effective method, shows strong results, and is well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks discussion of computational cost, depends on validation data, and has limited few-shot ablation.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong Accept.
