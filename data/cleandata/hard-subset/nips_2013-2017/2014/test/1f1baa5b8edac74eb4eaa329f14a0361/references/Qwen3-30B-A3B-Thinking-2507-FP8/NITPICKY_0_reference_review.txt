

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for unreliable attribute predictions by developing a random forest algorithm that leverages receiver operating characteristics of attribute classifiers. The main contribution is a method that propagates training instances in proportion to attribute classifier uncertainty, which the authors claim significantly outperforms existing methods.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a genuine problem in zero-shot learning: the assumption of perfect attribute prediction is unrealistic. The proposed approach to account for attribute unreliability is conceptually interesting and potentially valuable. The experimental results on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines like DAP and SIGNATURE-RF, particularly when attribute classifiers are unreliable. The few-shot extension is well-motivated and provides a useful bridge between zero-shot and few-shot learning.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Inconsistent Notation and Formulation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., "A_k" vs "A(m)"), which creates confusion. In Equation (5), the authors use "H(pI'_n)" without defining pI'_n, while in Equation (3), pIn is defined as a length-2 vector, creating a notational inconsistency. The paper states "With binary A_i(m), all 0 < t < 1 are equivalent in Sec 3.2.1," which is factually incorrect - for binary attributes, the threshold t should be set to 0.5, and values outside [0,1] are meaningless.

2. **Data Usage Discrepancy**: The paper claims to use "the same amount of attribute-labeled data" for all methods, but this is not true. The authors reserve 20% of attribute-labeled data for ROC validation, meaning their method trains attribute classifiers on 80% of the data, while DAP and SIGNATURE-RF presumably use 100% of the data. This is a significant disadvantage for the proposed method that the authors don't adequately address.

3. **Missing Details**: The paper states they "use the features provided with the datasets" but doesn't specify which features were used for each dataset, hindering reproducibility. The choice of tree depths (AwA-9, aPY-6, SUN-8) is mentioned but not justified - how were these depths determined? Cross-validation is mentioned but not explained.

4. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper claims "our method can benefit zero-shot learning with such discovered (pseudo)attributes as well," but provides no experimental results to support this claim. The "OURS+TRUE ROC" upper bound is mentioned but its value isn't reported, making it impossible to assess how much room for improvement exists.

### Presentation and Clarity Issues
1. **Formatting and Typos**: The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies, including inconsistent section numbering (e.g., "4.1 Zero-shot object and scene recognition" with "Controlled noise experiments" not numbered as 4.1.1). There's a typo in "unreliability" (appearing as "unreliability" in the abstract).

2. **Incomplete Explanations**: The distinction between "attribute unreliability" and "attribute strength" is mentioned but not clearly explained. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is presented as a natural extension but lacks a clear explanation of how it differs from the zero-shot approach beyond the parameter λ.

3. **Reference Inconsistency**: The paper references [18] for PAC bounds but doesn't cite the specific work where these bounds were established, which is a significant omission for a technical paper.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper identifies a real problem in zero-shot learning and presents an interesting approach, the technical inconsistencies, unclear methodology, and presentation issues significantly undermine its contribution. The data usage discrepancy (80% vs 100% of attribute-labeled data) is particularly problematic as it creates an unfair comparison with the baselines. The paper's claims about the method's superiority over DAP are weakened by this unaddressed disadvantage.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to fix the notation inconsistencies, clarify the data usage, provide the missing experimental details, and properly justify the choice of hyperparameters. Without these corrections, the paper's technical claims cannot be verified.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the problem addressed is important and the core idea has merit, the technical flaws and presentation issues are too severe to accept. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims, and the methodological inconsistencies prevent a fair evaluation of its contribution. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting.
