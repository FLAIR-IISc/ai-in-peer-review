The paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning with unreliable attribute predictions. The core idea is to incorporate the uncertainty of attribute classifiers into a random forest framework, improving generalization performance. The approach is extended to the few-shot setting by combining signature-based priors with limited labeled data. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation.

**Summary of Related Work:** The authors accurately position their work within the existing literature on zero-shot learning, highlighting the limitations of methods that assume perfect attribute predictions. They correctly identify the distinction between attribute unreliability and attribute strength, referencing relevant work such as [18] and [8]. The discussion of alternative approaches like knowledge transfer via hierarchies [23, 14] and embeddings [1, 4] is appropriate, effectively framing the novelty of their attribute-focused approach. The connections to methods dealing with missing or distributed features [21, 28, 16] are noted, with a clear explanation of how their scenario differs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound. The incorporation of ROC curves and validation data to account for attribute classifier errors is a well-reasoned approach. The extension to few-shot learning with a weighted combination of signature-based prior and data-driven learning is also sensible.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The equations are well-explained, and the method is described in a step-by-step manner, making it easy to follow. The motivations behind each design choice are clearly articulated.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The authors evaluate their method on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), and the results consistently demonstrate improvements over existing baselines, including the widely used DAP method [8, 9]. The controlled noise experiments provide valuable insight into the effectiveness of the proposed uncertainty modeling. The ablation studies (OURS W/O SIG UNCERTAINTY and OURS W/O ROC PROP, SIG UNCERTAINTY) further strengthen the analysis.
*   **Novelty:** While building on existing zero-shot learning techniques, the explicit modeling of attribute classifier unreliability is a novel contribution. The approach of propagating uncertainty through the random forest, informed by ROC curves and validation data, is a clever and effective way to address a key limitation of previous methods.
*   **Significance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed approach can significantly improve zero-shot learning performance, particularly when attribute classifiers are unreliable. This is a practical concern in many real-world applications, making the work relevant and potentially impactful. The extension to the few-shot setting further enhances the significance of the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The method introduces additional computational overhead due to the ROC calculation and validation data propagation. While not explicitly discussed, this could be a limiting factor for very large-scale attribute vocabularies or datasets.
*   **Sensitivity to Validation Data:** The performance of the method relies on the quality and representativeness of the validation data used to estimate ROC curves. The authors address this partially by reserving 20% of attribute training data, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of validation data size and composition would be valuable.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The method introduces additional parameters (e.g., λ in the few-shot extension) that require careful tuning. The authors mention using cross-validation for parameter selection, but further discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be helpful.
*   **Limited Discussion of Attribute Selection:** The paper assumes a given attribute vocabulary. While acknowledging the importance of expressive attributes, it does not explore how attribute selection impacts performance. Investigating the interplay between attribute selection and the proposed uncertainty modeling could be a fruitful area for future work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods (attribute unreliability).
*   Presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on multiple datasets.
*   Clear and concise writing, making the paper easy to understand and reproduce.
*   Strong experimental validation, including ablation studies and controlled noise experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Introduces computational overhead that could be limiting for large-scale applications.
*   Relies on the quality of validation data, potentially impacting robustness.
*   Requires careful parameter tuning.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and well-supported by experimental results. The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the evaluation further strengthen the contribution. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational cost and parameter tuning, these do not outweigh the significant benefits of the proposed method. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section.
