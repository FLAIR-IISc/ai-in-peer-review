

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors recognize that while attribute-based zero-shot learning is theoretically appealing (where new classes are defined by their attribute signatures), in practice, attribute classifiers are often unreliable due to correlated attributes and diverse visual instantiations. The paper proposes a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly accounts for this unreliability during training by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers. This is a significant departure from existing methods that treat attribute predictions as perfect.

The paper builds upon the well-established two-stage framework for zero-shot learning (first predict attributes, then predict class from attributes) pioneered by works like [8, 3, 18]. It also relates to recent work on attribute learning [11, 22, 26, 15, 30] that seeks to discover more discriminative mid-level features. However, unlike those works that abandon semantic attributes for discovered features, this paper argues for retaining semantic attributes while accounting for their unreliability.

The authors' approach is conceptually distinct from prior work that attempts to handle uncertainty in zero-shot learning, such as [18] which provides PAC bounds on tolerable error but doesn't offer a practical solution. The paper also differs from methods that use external knowledge about class relationships [23, 14, 13] by maintaining the language-based description framework that gives human supervisors direct control over class definitions.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach**: The paper identifies a critical flaw in current zero-shot learning methods—treating attribute predictions as perfect—and proposes a principled solution to address this. The fractional propagation of signatures based on ROC characteristics is both theoretically sound and practically effective.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments across three diverse datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over state-of-the-art baselines. The controlled noise experiments provide compelling evidence for the method's core insight—that accounting for attribute unreliability matters.

3. **Practical Significance**: The paper demonstrates that their method outperforms DAP (the most widely used zero-shot method) even when using only 80% of the attribute-labeled data for training, suggesting that modeling attribute reliability is more important than having more attribute data.

4. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for their approach, showing how the information gain formulation accounts for attribute unreliability. The connection between their method and the ROC characteristics of attribute classifiers is well-motivated.

5. **Few-Shot Extension**: The extension to few-shot learning is natural and well-justified, with experiments showing clear benefits of incorporating attribute signatures as a prior even with limited labeled data.

## Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for ROC Propagation**: While the paper provides a clear algorithmic description of their fractional propagation approach, it lacks a theoretical justification for why using ROC characteristics (as opposed to other error metrics) is optimal. The authors should discuss whether this approach is provably optimal under certain assumptions, or provide a more formal argument for why ROC is the right metric.

2. **Validation Data Dependency**: The method's performance critically depends on the quality of the validation data used to estimate ROC characteristics. The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate how the method performs when the validation data doesn't perfectly represent the unseen classes' attribute distributions. This is a significant limitation in real-world applications where unseen classes may have very different attribute distributions.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of their method compared to baselines. The recursive propagation of validation data through the tree structure could significantly increase training time, especially with large attribute vocabularies. The authors should provide runtime comparisons.

4. **Limited Analysis of Attribute Correlations**: The paper acknowledges that attributes are often correlated but doesn't explicitly address how their method handles these correlations. This is a significant oversight since attribute correlation is one of the main reasons attribute classifiers are unreliable in the first place. The authors should explore how their method performs when attributes are highly correlated.

5. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare against some of the most recent zero-shot learning methods, such as the work by [29] on attribute-based classification or the recent approaches using graph neural networks for zero-shot learning [32]. This omission weakens the significance claim.

6. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions using "some notation shortcuts" for the few-shot extension but doesn't provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The exact implementation of the combined information gain formula (Equation 6) could benefit from more detail.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical but overlooked challenge: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The proposed method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art baselines. The controlled noise experiments provide particularly compelling evidence for the core insight of the paper.

However, the paper has some limitations in its theoretical justification, dependency on validation data quality, and lack of thorough comparison with recent work. These are important but not fatal flaws for a NIPS paper.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of zero-shot learning that addresses a fundamental challenge. The methodology is novel, the experiments are thorough and convincing, and the results represent a significant improvement over the current state of the art. While there are some areas for improvement in the theoretical justification and implementation details, these do not undermine the overall contribution of the paper.

The paper's significance is particularly strong because it demonstrates that zero-shot learning with attributes can be practical and effective, which is crucial for real-world applications where new categories emerge dynamically. The few-shot extension further enhances the practical applicability of the method.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a theoretical analysis of why ROC characteristics are the appropriate metric for uncertainty propagation, and discuss alternatives.

2. Include a more thorough analysis of how the method performs when attribute correlations are high.

3. Compare against more recent zero-shot learning methods in the experiments.

4. Provide more detailed implementation information for reproducibility, especially regarding the few-shot extension.

5. Discuss the limitations of the validation data dependency more thoroughly, and suggest potential ways to mitigate this in future work.

This paper represents a significant step forward in making zero-shot learning with attributes practical and reliable. The insights provided have clear potential to impact both research and applications in visual recognition.
