### **Summary**

This paper addresses a critical and practical problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of the underlying attribute classifiers. While the ZSL paradigm of defining new classes via semantic attribute signatures is conceptually appealing, its real-world performance is often hampered by the fact that attribute classifiers are imperfect. Existing methods, such as the widely-used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) model, typically treat the outputs of these classifiers as reliable, which can lead to poor generalization.

The authors propose a novel and elegant solution framed within a random forest (RF) classifier. The key idea is to train the RF not on image data, but on the "ideal" attribute signatures of the unseen classes, while explicitly accounting for the expected errors of the attribute classifiers at test time. During tree construction, the algorithm uses a set of attribute-labeled validation images to estimate the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) for each attribute classifier. When considering a split, a class signature is not sent deterministically down a single path, but is propagated *fractionally* to both children nodes based on the attribute classifier's true positive and false positive rates. This novel training procedure results in decision trees that learn to prioritize splits on attributes that are not only discriminative for the ideal signatures but are also reliably predictable in practice. The framework is further extended to handle uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to the "few-shot" learning setting.

The proposed method is evaluated on three standard ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), where it significantly outperforms the DAP baseline and achieves state-of-the-art results on the AwA benchmark.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly significant problem in attribute-based learning. While the difficulty of learning robust attribute classifiers is well-known, most ZSL literature has focused on downstream modeling, often ignoring the noise in this crucial intermediate representation. This work's core contribution—a training procedure that explicitly models and mitigates the impact of attribute classifier unreliability—is both novel and important. The connection to prior work on decision trees with uncertain data is well-made, but the specific formulation for the ZSL problem is original and insightful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound, principled, and elegant. The idea of using ROC statistics from validation data to guide the fractional propagation of "ideal" training signatures is a clever mechanism to inject knowledge about test-time uncertainty into the training process. The re-formulation of the information gain criterion (Eq. 5) is a direct and appropriate consequence of this mechanism. The careful design of the controlled noise experiments provides strong, direct evidence for the method's effectiveness and its ability to favor more "learnable" attributes.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The paper shows strong performance gains over a very competitive and widely used baseline (DAP) across three diverse datasets. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of the key components of the model (signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation). Furthermore, showing that the method achieves state-of-the-art performance on AwA with both standard named attributes and discovered "pseudo-attributes" demonstrates its versatility. The few-shot learning extension is a valuable addition and is shown to be effective.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, the related work is appropriately situated, and the method is built up logically from a basic formulation to the full model. The figures and tables are clear and effectively support the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a very strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or addressed to make it even stronger.

1.  **Representativeness of the Validation Set:** The method's performance hinges on the assumption that the error statistics (ROCs) computed on the validation set `DV` (drawn from seen classes) are representative of the errors that will be made on images of unseen classes. The authors acknowledge this and provide a useful "upper bound" analysis. However, it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the method to this assumption. For instance, how does performance change if `DV` is drawn from classes that are semantically very distant from the unseen classes? While the current validation strategy is standard practice, a deeper analysis of this point could provide valuable insights for future work.

2.  **Selection of λ in Few-Shot Learning:** In the few-shot experiments (Sec 4.2), the paper states that the optimal value for the weighting parameter λ is selected via cross-validation. This is slightly confusing, as cross-validation typically requires a labeled validation set for the *target* classes. In a true few-shot scenario, one would only have the small training set `DT` for these classes. Please clarify how λ was chosen. Was it on a hold-out portion of `DT`? If so, performance might be sensitive to this split when `DT` is very small. Suggesting a heuristic or a more robust method for setting λ would strengthen this part of the work.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The proposed training algorithm appears more computationally intensive than a standard random forest, as it requires propagating the validation dataset `DV` down the tree and re-computing ROC statistics at each node. A brief comment on the computational overhead compared to the baselines would be helpful for practitioners considering adopting the method, especially in scenarios with very large validation sets or deep trees.

### **Decision**

This paper presents a novel, elegant, and highly effective solution to a critical problem in zero-shot learning. The quality of the technical contribution is high, the writing is clear, and the experimental results are strong and convincing. The strengths of this work far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a significant advance in making attribute-based recognition more practical and robust. I strongly recommend acceptance.
