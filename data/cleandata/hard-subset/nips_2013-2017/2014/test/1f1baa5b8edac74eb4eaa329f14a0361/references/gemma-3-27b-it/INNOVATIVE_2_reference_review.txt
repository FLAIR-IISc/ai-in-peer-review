This paper tackles a critical challenge in zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. While attribute-based zero-shot learning is conceptually elegant, its practical success has been limited by noisy attributes. This work directly addresses this limitation, offering a novel approach to train models that explicitly account for attribute uncertainty. The core idea – leveraging ROC curves of attribute classifiers during random forest training to select discriminative and predictable splits – is clever and well-motivated. The extension to few-shot learning is a natural and valuable addition. 

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors thoroughly explain their approach and provide a good connection to related work, correctly positioning their contribution as distinct from existing methods that focus on attribute strength or discover latent features. The experimental setup is robust, utilizing three standard datasets and comparing against strong baselines, including the widely-used DAP method. The results are compelling, consistently demonstrating the superiority of their approach, particularly in scenarios with unreliable attributes. The controlled noise experiments are particularly insightful, illustrating *why* their method works.  The ablation studies convincingly show the contributions of the different components of their method. 

The novelty of this work is high. While handling uncertainty in machine learning is not new, applying it to the specific problem of attribute-based zero-shot learning, and *during the training process* of the zero-shot model itself, is a genuinely new idea.  The insight that leveraging the ROC curves during the tree construction is a smart way to implicitly weight more reliable attributes and avoid splits based on noisy ones is a significant contribution. The method is not simply a refinement of existing techniques; it represents a conceptual shift in how we approach zero-shot learning with attributes.

The significance of this work is substantial. Improving the robustness of attribute-based zero-shot learning has the potential to unlock its full potential for real-world applications. This work provides a promising path towards that goal. The demonstrated performance gains, particularly on datasets with less reliable attributes, are significant. The extension to few-shot learning further broadens the applicability of the method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The approach of incorporating attribute reliability directly into the training process of a zero-shot model is a novel and impactful idea.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method consistently outperforms strong baselines on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Clear and Well-Motivated:** The paper is well-written, clearly explains the approach, and provides a strong motivation for the work.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The use of ROC curves and the information gain calculation are well-grounded in decision tree theory.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method addresses a critical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning, improving its practical viability.

**Arguments for Rejection (Minor):**

*   **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on a validation dataset to estimate attribute reliability.  While the authors address this, the dependence on a representative validation set could be a limitation in some scenarios. (However, this is a reasonable trade-off for the gains achieved.)
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The authors don’t extensively discuss the computational cost of the ROC estimation and propagation steps, though they do mention the method scales to the datasets used. A more thorough discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
*   **Further Exploration of Attribute Correlation:** While acknowledged, a more in-depth investigation of explicitly modeling attribute correlations within the framework could be a valuable future direction.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to address a critical challenge in zero-shot learning. The combination of theoretical justification, strong empirical results, and clear writing makes this a highly valuable contribution. I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS and recommend acceptance despite the minor weaknesses mentioned above.  The novelty and potential impact of the work significantly outweigh any minor concerns. It’s a refreshing and impactful take on zero-shot learning.
