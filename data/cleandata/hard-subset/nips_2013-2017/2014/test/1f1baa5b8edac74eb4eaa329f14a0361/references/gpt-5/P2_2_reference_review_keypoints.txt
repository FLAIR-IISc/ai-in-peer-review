- Summary: Proposes training one-vs-all random forests on class-level attribute signatures and incorporates attribute unreliability via node-specific ROC (TPR/FPR) statistics from validation data to fractionally propagate signatures during splits.

- Relation to prior work: Addresses limitations of attribute-based zero-shot methods like DAP by explicitly modeling attribute prediction errors; relates to uncertain-input trees but adapts them to signature-based zero-shot learning.

- Core technical idea: Use ROC-derived fractional propagation of class signatures at each attribute-threshold split and redefine information gain to favor discriminative, error-robust splits.

- Signature uncertainty: Method also models uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends to few-shot by mixing signature-based and labeled-data-based gains with parameter λ.

- Empirical evaluation: Shows consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline on AwA, aPY, and SUN, with ablations, controlled-noise studies, discovered-attribute experiments, and few-shot results.

- Strength — problem relevance: Explicitly targets the well-documented issue of unreliable attribute predictors rather than assuming calibrated probabilities suffice.

- Strength — technical novelty: Fractional propagation using node-conditional ROC statistics and an information gain criterion is conceptually simple and effective.

- Strength — accounting for dependencies: Recursive propagation of validation data captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits, improving split decisions.

- Strength — few-shot extension: Principled incorporation of signatures as a prior (via λ) that naturally diminishes as labeled data grows.

- Strength — empirical rigor: Strong experimental design including multiple datasets, stress tests, ablations, and analyses that pinpoint where improvements arise.

- Strength — practicality: Implementation is straightforward and splits/operating points are interpretable.

- Weakness — ROC estimation optimism: Concern that ROC may be estimated on data also used to train attribute SVMs, producing optimistic ROC and biased gains for the method.

- Weakness — limited theory: No formal bounds or guarantees linking ROC-estimate error to zero-shot performance; approach remains heuristic.

- Weakness — scalability: Training K one-vs-all forests and computing M attribute predictions per test image may not scale to large K; no multi-class/shared forest presented.

- Weakness — validation/test mismatch risk: If attribute error profiles differ between seen (validation) and unseen classes, ROC-guided splits may be suboptimal; TRUE ROC upper bound highlights this gap.

- Weakness — comparisons: Lacks experimental comparisons to embedding-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DeViSE, Akata), which are cited but not evaluated.

- Weakness — reproducibility details: Some important details (signature uncertainty, full pseudocode) are in the supplement, slightly hindering standalone reproducibility.

- Quality assessment: Technically sound with well-specified node-conditional ROC propagation and supportive experiments; main concern is potential ROC-estimation bias.

- Clarity assessment: Clear, well-organized exposition with consistent notation; supplement needed for some implementation details.

- Originality assessment: Novel adaptation of decision forests to zero-shot learning with ROC-guided fractional propagation and signature-based training; extends prior uncertain-input tree ideas to this new setting.

- Significance assessment: Advances state of the art on standard zero-shot benchmarks and offers a general recipe for incorporating attribute-error models into category learning.

- Key question/suggestion: Clarify whether ROC validation data are disjoint from attribute-SVM training and provide results with strictly held-out ROC estimation.

- Key question/suggestion: Provide sensitivity analyses for ROC-validation set size, seen-vs-unseen mismatch, number of thresholds per node, and λ robustness in few-shot.

- Key question/suggestion: Report runtime/memory scaling with K and M and discuss shared multi-class forests or pruning strategies for scalability.

- Acceptance pros: Clear problem, elegant/practical method, strong empirical gains, insightful controlled analyses, and useful few-shot extension.

- Acceptance cons: Potential optimistic ROC estimation, limited theoretical grounding, and missing comparisons to some contemporary zero-shot baselines.

- Overall recommendation: Lean accept, contingent on addressing ROC data-split concern and adding brief scalability analysis.
