This paper addresses the crucial problem of zero-shot learning, specifically tackling the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest-based approach that incorporates attribute classifier ROC characteristics to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes, and extend this to handle signature uncertainty and even a few-shot setting. While the core idea is promising and addresses a well-recognized limitation of existing attribute-based zero-shot learning methods, the presentation and execution are riddled with minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws that detract from the overall quality of the work.

The introduction effectively motivates the problem and connects it to relevant cognitive science literature. However, the phrasing "attribute predictions at face value" feels unnecessarily dismissive of prior work, which often *acknowledges* attribute noise through probabilistic models (e.g., DAP) – the issue is not simply *taking* predictions, but *modeling* the uncertainty adequately. The claim of a “clear advantage” in the introduction feels premature, given the intricacies of the experimental setup and nuances in the comparisons.

The related work section is comprehensive, but also suffers from a degree of imprecision. Describing methods that use external knowledge (e.g., ImageNet hierarchy) as fundamentally different from attribute-based approaches is a simplification; they all fall under the umbrella of leveraging prior knowledge for zero-shot transfer. The discussion of discovered features lacks depth; the authors briefly mention their limitations regarding semantic interpretability but don’t fully explore the trade-offs. The connection to fractional tuples and related methods feels tangential and lacks sufficient justification for its inclusion.

The approach section is dense and technically intricate. While the core idea of incorporating ROC curves into the split selection is clever, the notation is often cumbersome and the explanations lack sufficient clarity. The justification for choosing specific tree depths and (m, t) test combinations feels arbitrary; a more thorough exploration of these hyperparameters would have strengthened the analysis. The description of signature uncertainty is particularly convoluted and difficult to follow. The transition from attribute-level to signature-level uncertainty lacks a clear rationale. The mathematical formalism, while present, isn’t always paired with intuitive explanations, making it hard to assess the method’s practical implications.

The experimental setup is reasonably well-defined, but several aspects raise concerns. The choice of validation data proportion (20%) feels somewhat arbitrary and isn't thoroughly justified. The use of pre-existing features raises questions about the method’s generality; would it perform as well with features learned specifically for this task? The comparison to DAP is appropriate, but the authors should have also included comparisons to more recent, state-of-the-art zero-shot learning methods beyond those listed, particularly those employing embedding-based approaches. The “controlled noise experiments” are valuable, but the noise model itself is not fully described. The statement that OURS+TRUE ROC provides an “upper bound” is misleading; it's an *oracle* bound, as it relies on perfect knowledge of unseen class images – a scenario unrealistic in a true zero-shot setting. The analysis of few-shot results lacks sufficient detail; the impact of the λ parameter isn't fully explored, and the automatic selection process is presented without sufficient explanation.

Throughout the paper, minor formatting inconsistencies detract from its professionalism. Figures lack informative captions, and the text contains several grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. References are not consistently formatted. Table and figure references in the text are occasionally incorrect.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to addressing attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing methods (DAP) on several datasets.
*   Extension to the few-shot setting.
*   Insightful analysis of attribute classifier errors.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation, notation, and experimental setup.
*   Lack of clarity in explanations and justifications.
*   Overstated claims of significance.
*   Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Cumbersome mathematical formalism without sufficient intuitive explanation.
*   Arbitrary hyperparameter choices without thorough exploration.
*   Formatting inconsistencies and grammatical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach and the demonstrated improvements over baseline methods, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to its numerous minor flaws. The lack of clarity, the questionable experimental choices, and the pervasive formatting issues detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. While the core idea has merit, the paper requires substantial revision and polishing before it could be considered acceptable for publication. I recommend **rejection** at this time. A revised version addressing these concerns could be reconsidered in the future.
