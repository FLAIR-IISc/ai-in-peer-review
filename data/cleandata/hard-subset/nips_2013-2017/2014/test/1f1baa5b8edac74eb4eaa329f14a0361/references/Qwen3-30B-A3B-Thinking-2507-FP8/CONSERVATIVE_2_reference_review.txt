

**Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"**

Summary: The paper proposes a method for zero-shot learning that accounts for unreliable attribute predictions by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers into random forest training. The authors argue that existing zero-shot methods treat attribute predictions as reliable, which is problematic given the inherent unreliability of visual attribute classifiers. Their approach trains a random forest using class attribute signatures but propagates signatures through the tree according to estimated ROC curves, thereby accounting for prediction errors.

Strengths:
1. The paper correctly identifies a genuine limitation of current zero-shot learning approaches: the assumption that attribute predictions are reliable.
2. The method is theoretically sound, building on established techniques (random forests, ROC analysis) in a principled way.
3. The experimental results on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) show consistent improvements over DAP, a strong baseline widely used in the field.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method provides meaningful improvements over existing approaches. The reported gains (e.g., 10.2% on "giant panda" for AwA) are modest, and the paper lacks convincing justification for why these improvements are practically significant. The authors should provide context on what constitutes a meaningful improvement in the zero-shot learning literature.

2. The method requires a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves, which is an additional requirement not needed by simpler methods like DAP. This is a significant practical limitation that the paper doesn't adequately address. In real-world applications where labeled data is scarce, this requirement could make the method infeasible. The authors should demonstrate how this validation set requirement impacts performance when data is limited.

3. The core claim that "modeling how good/bad the attribute classifiers are is even more important than having better attribute classifiers" is not supported by evidence. The authors don't show that their method would outperform DAP when using the same attribute classifiers. A more convincing experiment would be to use identical attribute classifiers as DAP but apply the proposed method, and show it outperforms DAP with the same attribute classifiers.

4. The few-shot extension lacks proper evaluation. The paper doesn't compare the few-shot extension to state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods, making it impossible to assess its contribution. It's unclear whether the proposed extension provides any advantage over standard few-shot learning approaches.

5. The paper doesn't analyze failure cases or limitations of the proposed method. For example, how does the method perform when attribute classifiers have very high error rates? How does it handle cases where the attribute vocabulary is not expressive enough? Without this analysis, it's hard to understand the method's practical applicability.

6. The approach to handling signature uncertainty (3.2.3) is mentioned but not thoroughly explained, and it's unclear how much this contributes to the overall performance. The paper should provide a clearer explanation of this component and its impact on results.

Overall Assessment:
While the problem addressed is important, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the proposed method is substantially better than existing approaches. The improvements over DAP are incremental at best, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why these modest gains are meaningful in practical applications. The additional requirement of a validation set for ROC estimation is a significant practical limitation that isn't adequately addressed.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it provided:
1) A clear demonstration of when and why the method outperforms existing approaches
2) A thorough analysis of the method's limitations and failure cases
3) Comparison to methods that might handle attribute unreliability differently
4) Evidence that the proposed method is more practical than existing approaches

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper makes a modest contribution to the field but doesn't provide enough evidence to demonstrate that its approach is substantially better than existing methods. The reviewers should be concerned about the lack of compelling evidence for the practical significance of the proposed method. While the problem is important, the paper's solution doesn't convincingly address it with sufficient evidence.
