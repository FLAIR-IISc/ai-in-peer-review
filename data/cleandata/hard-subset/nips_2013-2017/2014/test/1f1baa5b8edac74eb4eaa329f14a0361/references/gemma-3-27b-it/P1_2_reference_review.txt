## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of zero-shot learning (ZSL) where novel classes are recognized based on semantic attributes. The core idea is to move beyond the assumption of perfect attribute prediction, a key limitation of existing ZSL methods. The authors propose a random forest framework that explicitly models and accounts for attribute prediction unreliability during training. This is achieved by propagating training instances fractionally based on the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers, effectively “softening” the attribute signatures. The method is extended to a few-shot learning setting and demonstrated on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), showing improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly DAP.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the existing body of work in attribute-based ZSL (e.g., [8, 3, 18, 23, 19]). It distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the inherent unreliability of attribute classifiers, a limitation acknowledged but not tackled by prior approaches. The work also connects to broader concepts of handling uncertainty in decision trees, citing related work on fractional tuples [21] and soft node memberships [28], however highlights the novelty of applying these concepts to the specific zero-shot learning setting. The authors correctly position their work alongside alternative ZSL strategies like knowledge transfer based on class relationships [23, 14], and recent methods focusing on discovering discriminative mid-level features [11, 22], while emphasizing the advantage of retaining human-interpretable attribute descriptions.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical limitation:** The paper directly tackles the real-world problem of unreliable attribute predictions, which significantly hampers the performance of existing ZSL methods.  The assumption of perfect attributes is rarely met in practice, making this a valuable contribution.
* **Novel and well-motivated approach:** The proposed method of propagating signatures based on ROC curves is a clever way to incorporate attribute uncertainty into the learning process. It leverages information about *how* the attribute classifiers fail, rather than simply treating them as noisy.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines (including DAP) across three datasets. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different components of the proposed method.
* **Extension to Few-Shot Learning:** The extension to the few-shot setting, elegantly combining attribute signatures with limited labeled data, showcases the versatility of the approach.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making the technical details accessible.  The inclusion of supporting information and pseudocode enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The validation data propagation and ROC computation add computational overhead. While not explicitly addressed in the paper, the scalability to very large attribute vocabularies or datasets could be a concern.  A discussion of this would be valuable.
* **Sensitivity to Validation Data:** The performance is reliant on the quality and representativeness of the validation data used to estimate ROC curves.  The paper acknowledges this, but further investigation into the robustness of the approach to variations in validation data would be beneficial.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the controlled noise experiments provide insights, a more detailed analysis of failure cases in the real-world experiments would be useful. Understanding *which* attributes are most problematic and *why* could guide further improvements.
* **Choice of hyperparameters:** The paper mentions the hyperparameters used, but doesn't discuss a comprehensive sensitivity analysis or justification for the specific values chosen. A more in-depth discussion of parameter tuning would strengthen the results.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Addresses a crucial, often overlooked, issue in ZSL: attribute reliability.
*  Offers a novel and well-motivated technical solution.
*  Demonstrates strong empirical performance on multiple datasets.
*  Provides a valuable extension to the few-shot learning setting.
*  Clear writing and organization.

**Con:**

*  Potential computational cost and scalability concerns.
*  Reliance on the quality of validation data.
*  Limited analysis of failure cases.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors identify a key limitation of existing approaches and propose a principled and effective solution. The experimental results are compelling, and the extension to few-shot learning demonstrates the broader applicability of the method. While there are minor weaknesses related to computational cost, validation data dependence, and analysis of failure cases, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions. 

I recommend the authors address the scalability concerns and provide a more thorough analysis of failure cases.  Discussing the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters would also be beneficial.
