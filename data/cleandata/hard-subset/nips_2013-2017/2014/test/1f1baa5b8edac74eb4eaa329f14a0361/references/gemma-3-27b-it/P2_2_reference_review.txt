## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of zero-shot recognition, specifically addressing the practical issue of unreliable attribute predictions. Existing zero-shot methods often assume perfect attribute information, which is unrealistic. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly models attribute unreliability during training by propagating signatures through the tree based on the ROC characteristics of the attribute classifiers. They further extend this to handle signature uncertainty and demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on three datasets, outperforming existing baselines, including the widely used DAP.  A few-shot extension is also presented.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical limitation of existing ZSL methods:**  The paper directly confronts the issue of unreliable attribute predictions, a major bottleneck in the practical application of attribute-based ZSL. This is a very important contribution.
* **Novel and well-motivated approach:** The use of ROC curves to inform the training of the random forest, allowing for probabilistic propagation of signatures, is a clever and well-justified idea. It's a sophisticated way to account for classifier uncertainty.
* **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over strong baselines (DAP, SIGNATURE-RF) across multiple datasets. The gains are particularly pronounced in scenarios with noisy attributes and less reliable classifiers. The controlled noise experiments are particularly convincing.
* **Thorough experimentation:** The authors explore ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of their method (signature uncertainty, ROC propagation), providing valuable insights. The inclusion of a few-shot extension also broadens the applicability of the work.
* **Clear writing and organization:**  The paper is well-written and logically organized.  The explanation of the method is relatively easy to follow, given the complexity of the technique.
* **Connection to cognitive science:** The introduction effectively grounds the work in the context of human attribute-based categorization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The method is relatively complex, with multiple components and parameters. While the authors provide a detailed explanation, the implementation details might be challenging to reproduce without significant effort.
* **Computational cost:**  Training random forests and computing ROC curves can be computationally expensive, especially for large attribute vocabularies and datasets. The paper doesn’t delve into the computational scalability of the approach.
* **Validation data dependence:** The method relies on a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves. The performance of the method is thus dependent on the representativeness of this validation data.  While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of potential biases and sensitivity to validation set selection would be helpful.
* **Limited discussion of attribute quality:**  While the method *handles* unreliable attributes, it doesn’t address the fundamental issue of *acquiring* high-quality attributes.  A brief discussion of how attribute selection or refinement could be incorporated into the framework would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors identify a critical limitation of existing methods and propose a novel and effective solution. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and organized. While the method is complex and computationally demanding, the benefits in terms of accuracy and robustness appear to outweigh these drawbacks. The paper clearly meets the NIPS reviewing criteria.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound, well-motivated, and supported by thorough experimentation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Originality:** The approach of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability in a random forest framework is novel and represents a significant advancement over existing ZSL methods.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of ZSL, making it more practical and applicable to real-world scenarios.  The gains over DAP are particularly noteworthy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the method and potential computational cost could be a barrier to adoption.
*   Dependence on a validation dataset for ROC estimation introduces a potential source of bias.



**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I believe this paper is a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference and deserves to be published.  I would encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses identified above in a revised version of the paper, particularly regarding computational scalability and the validation data dependence.
