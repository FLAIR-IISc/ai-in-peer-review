

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: the assumption that attribute predictions are reliable. While existing methods treat attribute predictions as perfect, the authors correctly identify that visual attributes are difficult to learn accurately, leading to poor performance in practice. They propose a novel approach that explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability during training by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers into the random forest training process. The method also handles signature uncertainty and extends naturally to few-shot learning. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), with the most notable gains occurring when attribute classifiers are less reliable.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their approach, with appropriate theoretical justification for why their method should outperform existing ones. The experimental design is thorough, with consistent results across three challenging datasets and 20 random trials to account for variability. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of the key components (accounting for attribute unreliability vs. signature uncertainty), demonstrating that both aspects are necessary for optimal performance.

The paper acknowledges important limitations, such as the need for validation data to estimate ROC curves, and carefully discusses the trade-offs between different components of their method. The comparison to DAP (a leading zero-shot method) is particularly compelling, showing that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than having more attribute-labeled data.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the paper within the context of zero-shot learning literature. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of the field, clearly distinguishing this work from previous approaches. The methodology section (Section 3) is particularly strong, with clear mathematical formulations and intuitive explanations of the key innovations. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including dataset specifications, training parameters, and statistical analysis.

The paper's clarity is further enhanced by the well-designed figures and tables that present the results in an accessible manner. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would be helpful for reproduction.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by addressing a fundamental problem that has been overlooked in previous work. While the idea of using attributes for zero-shot learning is not new, the authors are the first to explicitly account for attribute unreliability during training. The connection to fractional tuples for handling missing values in decision trees (from [21]) is insightful, but the application to the zero-shot setting with its unique challenges (different training and testing domains) represents a novel contribution.

The few-shot extension is also a meaningful contribution that broadens the applicability of their method. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from related work, particularly in how they handle attribute unreliability versus attribute strength, which is a key distinction the field has previously overlooked.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors successfully demonstrate that attribute-based zero-shot learning can be practical and effective when properly accounting for attribute unreliability, which has been a major barrier to adoption in real-world applications. Their results on standard datasets show consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art, particularly in settings where attribute classifiers are less reliable (e.g., SUN and aPY datasets).

The paper's impact is further enhanced by showing that their method can be used with both hand-designed attributes and discovered attributes, making it more broadly applicable. The fact that their approach outperforms DAP (a leading method) despite using only 80% of the attribute-labeled images is particularly compelling, suggesting that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more valuable than having more training data for attributes.

## Strengths

1. Addresses a critical, previously overlooked problem in zero-shot learning (attribute unreliability)
2. Proposes a well-motivated and technically sound solution
3. Provides comprehensive experiments showing clear improvements over strong baselines
4. Includes thorough ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components
5. Extends the method to few-shot learning, broadening its applicability
6. Well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound

## Weaknesses

1. The need for validation data to estimate ROC curves is a practical limitation that might not be feasible in all settings, though the authors acknowledge this
2. The paper doesn't fully explore the method's performance when attribute classifiers are extremely unreliable (e.g., near-random performance)
3. The computational complexity of the method isn't explicitly discussed (though it's probably not a major issue given the use of random forests)

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself relative to related work. It clearly distinguishes their approach from existing methods by noting that none of them account for attribute unreliability during training. The authors also thoughtfully address the distinction between attribute unreliability and attribute strength, which is a subtle but important point that previous work has conflated.

The paper's connection to attribute discovery work (e.g., [30]) is particularly valuable, showing that their method can be applied to a wider range of attribute representations, not just hand-designed ones. This makes their approach more practical and broadly applicable.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear, significant contribution to the zero-shot learning literature. It addresses a fundamental limitation in the field that has hindered practical adoption, and proposes a well-motivated solution that is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and makes a compelling case for the value of accounting for attribute unreliability.

The authors have successfully demonstrated that the elegance of zero-shot learning need not be abandoned due to the difficulty of learning reliable attributes. Their method provides a practical path forward for attribute-based zero-shot learning that can be applied to a wide range of datasets and attribute representations.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field with a well-executed approach that addresses a real problem. The results are strong and the methodology is sound, making this a valuable addition to the zero-shot learning literature.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method performs when attribute classifiers are extremely unreliable (e.g., when TPR = FPR for all attributes). This would help readers understand the method's limits.

2. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is well-motivated, but it would be helpful to include a more detailed comparison to existing few-shot methods (not just zero-shot baselines).

3. The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of how their method might be extended to handle multi-label attributes, which is a common scenario in zero-shot learning.

4. While the paper acknowledges the need for validation data to estimate ROC curves, it would be useful to discuss potential strategies for estimating these curves when such data is unavailable (e.g., using data from seen classes with similar attributes).

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It addresses a critical problem in zero-shot learning with a well-motivated and effective solution, and the results clearly demonstrate its value.
