- Summary: Tackles ZSL's flawed assumption of perfect attribute predictors by modeling attribute classifier unreliability with ROC-guided random forests, extending to few-shot and improving results on AwA, aPY, SUN.

- Novel, well-motivated approach: Using ROC characteristics for tree splitting directly addresses attribute predictor unreliability.

- Rigorous experimental evaluation: Consistent improvements over DAP across three datasets, with controlled noise experiments and ablation studies supporting the claims.

- Practical significance: Modeling attribute unreliability yields larger gains than more attribute training data and works with modest data (e.g., 500 images).

- Clear technical contribution: Natural, well-executed few-shot extension that integrates attribute signatures with limited labeled examples.

- Reliance on attribute validation data: Requires separate validation set to estimate ROC curves; OURS+TRUE ROC is unrealistic and handling no-validation cases isn’t addressed.

- Limited treatment of attribute correlations: Does not explicitly model attribute dependencies nor evaluate their impact on performance.

- Computational complexity concerns: Propagating validation data through trees increases training cost, with no efficiency comparison to prior methods.

- Lack of theoretical guarantees: No formal analysis explaining why ROC-based information gain improves generalization or relates to PAC-style bounds.

- Comparison to related work: Correctly positioned against DAP and recent discovered-feature approaches; method can complement discovered features.

- Significance and impact: Substantial contribution showing attribute-based ZSL remains viable when accounting for unreliability, with broader applicability beyond ZSL.

- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated paper with convincing empirical results but practically constrained by the need for validation data.

- Recommendation: Accept.
