Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition with attribute-based descriptions by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during the construction of classifiers for unseen classes. The main idea is to train one-vs-all random forests not on image features but on class attribute signatures, while using attribute ROC statistics estimated from held-out attribute-labeled “validation” data to fractionally propagate class signatures down the tree. The information gain is redefined to use soft counts (fractional presence of class signatures) so that splits prefer attributes and operating points that are both discriminative and predictable. The method is further extended to handle uncertainty/noise in the class-attribute signatures and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven information gain with standard gain on a small labeled training set. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, as well as controlled-noise analyses and some results with discovered pseudo-attributes.

Strengths
- Addresses a recognized limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: imperfect attribute predictors. The ROC-aware fractional propagation is conceptually clean and well-motivated.
- The node-specific ROC estimation using recursively filtered validation data is a nice touch to capture context-dependent operating points and some inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Simple few-shot extension via a convex combination of information gains is practical and yields sensible behavior as λ varies.
- Evaluations on three datasets with >250 attribute classifiers, controlled-noise experiments, and comparisons to DAP and an ablated RF baseline support the claims.
- The method is modular and can, in principle, work with discovered attributes.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality (technical soundness and empirical support)
- Assumption/stability of ROC transfer: The approach implicitly assumes ROCs measured on seen-class attribute-labeled data transfer to the unseen classes. Domain shift in attribute appearance (e.g., “striped” on zebras vs. shirts) can alter TPR/FPR. The paper acknowledges this and shows an “upper bound” using true ROC on unseen data, but provides no mitigation beyond node-specific filtering. A sensitivity analysis to signature out-of-distribution attributes or a class-conditioning strategy would strengthen the case.
- Signature uncertainty is critical yet relegated to the supplement. The main text does not specify the form of the appended term in Eq. 4, the prior/noise model, or how its hyperparameters are set. This limits reproducibility of a key component that contributes to gains.
- Candidate threshold selection for splits is underspecified. The paper mentions sampling (#m,#t) but does not describe how t is drawn (uniform over [0,1], quantiles, ROC operating points, or per-node histogram bins). This choice affects both accuracy and fairness of comparisons.
- Validation data reuse: The method “reserves 20%” for ROC estimation but then “pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” It is unclear at what stage the ROC is computed (before pooling?) and whether any leakage occurs. Please clarify the precise sequence (train attribute SVMs on 80%, compute ROC on 20%, then retrain SVMs on 100% and use the previously computed ROC?).
- Few-shot cross-validation: λ is selected by cross-validation. With very few labeled images, this may be high-variance, and the protocol is not specified (number of folds, held-out strategy, whether validation images overlap with those used to learn trees). Also, the few-shot baseline is weak (a forest purely on the tiny DT); stronger baselines (e.g., fine-tuning DAP or label-embedding methods with few-shot data) are needed.
- Comparisons: DAP and the signature RF baseline are informative, but the paper cites richer zero-shot baselines (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE, hierarchical/neighborhood classifier transfer) and shows only one consolidated comparison for AwA. It is unclear if all methods use the same splits and attribute sets. A fair, unified comparison across all three datasets would be more convincing.
- No complexity analysis or scalability discussion. Propagating fractional signatures and recomputing node-specific ROC over DV(n) at each candidate split may be costly for large M, K, and J. Empirical training/test time and memory would be useful.
- The controlled noise experiments lack procedural detail (how noise is injected per-attribute, how ROC is estimated in this synthetic setup, whether the same noise model is used to compute the ROC fed to the method, risk of “knowing the noise” it will face).

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but key methodological details are deferred to the supplement (signature uncertainty, pseudocode). As written, an expert reader could not fully reproduce results.
- Notational issues and footnotes: several footnote markers (1,2,3,4,6) and references to supp. occur mid-flow; some definitions (e.g., exactly how t is sampled) are missing. The phrase “ground truth negatives—i.e., true negatives and false positives” is confusing; “signatures with Ak(m)=0” is clearer.
- Hyperparameter selection (tree depth, (#m,#t) per dataset) is said to be from cross-validation; the protocol is not described for the zero-shot setting.
- Reference formatting is inconsistent (missing years/venues, duplicates). Some entries lack complete bibliographic details.

Originality
- The central idea—using validation-derived attribute error statistics to guide zero-shot classifier learning—is a meaningful and non-trivial extension of two-stage attribute methods. The connection to fractional propagation/missing values in decision trees is apt but the application and training regime (using class signatures as training instances, node-specific ROCs) appear novel.

Significance
- Improving zero-shot performance when attributes are unreliable is important. The reported gains over DAP on three datasets suggest practical value. However, the breadth of baselines and some missing details limit the strength of the empirical claim. If clarified and reinforced, this could be a useful contribution for attribute-based zero-/few-shot methods and potentially other semantic mid-level pipelines.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a principled, implementable method.
- Empirical improvements on multiple datasets; controlled noise study is insightful.
- Few-shot extension is simple and useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on supplement; insufficient methodological detail (signature uncertainty, thresholding, CV protocols) to reproduce.
- Limited and uneven comparisons to stronger contemporary zero-shot methods across datasets.
- Unclear robustness to domain shift in attribute ROCs; no mitigation strategy beyond acknowledgment.
- Missing complexity/scalability analysis; some experimental protocols underspecified.

Questions for the authors
1) Precisely describe how candidate thresholds t are sampled per attribute and per node.
2) Detail the signature uncertainty model and its parameters in the main text.
3) Clarify the training/validation pipeline for attribute SVMs and ROC estimation to rule out leakage.
4) Provide the cross-validation protocol for tree depths, (#m,#t), and λ in zero-/few-shot settings.
5) Report training/test time and memory and discuss scalability in M, K, and J.
6) Add comparisons to label-embedding/DeViSE-style methods on all datasets with matched splits.

Overall rating: Weak reject
Confidence: High

While the idea is promising and results are encouraging, the paper’s current form has too many missing details/ambiguities and limited comparisons. Tightening the exposition, expanding baselines, and clarifying protocols could elevate this to a solid contribution.
