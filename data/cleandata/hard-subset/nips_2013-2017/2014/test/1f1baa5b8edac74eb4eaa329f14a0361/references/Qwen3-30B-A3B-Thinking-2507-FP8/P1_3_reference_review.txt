

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL) literature: the assumption that attribute predictions are perfect. While existing ZSL methods define novel classes through attribute signatures (e.g., "zebras are striped, black and white"), they treat attribute predictions as accurate, ignoring the reality that attribute classifiers are often unreliable. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability during training by leveraging the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers. Their method propagates signatures probabilistically through decision trees based on estimated false positive/true positive rates, rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions. The paper also extends the approach to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning. Evaluated on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), the method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines like DAP (Direct Attribute Prediction), demonstrating the value of accounting for attribute unreliability in ZSL.

This work builds upon and addresses a key gap in previous ZSL literature, which has largely ignored the practical limitations of attribute prediction. While related work (e.g., [21, 28]) has explored handling missing values in decision trees, the authors' zero-shot setting with distinct training and testing domains requires a novel adaptation of these concepts.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a crucial, often overlooked issue in ZSL—attribute unreliability. The authors convincingly demonstrate that existing methods (including the widely used DAP) fail when attribute predictions are imperfect, which is the practical reality.

2. **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach**: The core idea of using ROC curves to account for attribute classifier unreliability during random forest training is both novel and well-justified. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their information gain criterion (Equation 5) and demonstrate its effectiveness through controlled noise experiments (Figure 1).

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method achieves substantial improvements over DAP (the leading attribute-based ZSL method) across three challenging datasets. The results are statistically significant (20 random trials), and the authors provide thorough ablation studies showing the contributions of each component (OURS W/O ROC PROP, etc.).

4. **Practical Extensions**: The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is natural and well-motivated, showing clear benefits even with limited labeled data. The results in Figure 2a demonstrate the value of using attribute signatures as priors.

5. **Rigorous Evaluation**: The paper carefully evaluates the method's performance under controlled noise scenarios and with real-world unreliable attributes. The comparison with both standard named attributes and discovered attributes (Figure 2b) demonstrates the approach's versatility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Validation Data Requirement**: The method requires a separate validation set to estimate ROCs, which may not always be available in practical applications. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't explore how to handle cases where such validation data is unavailable or limited.

2. **Dependency on Attribute Classifier Quality**: The method's performance depends on the quality of the underlying attribute classifiers. If attribute classifiers are extremely poor (e.g., near-random), the method may not provide significant benefits. The paper doesn't fully explore this boundary case.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the method scales to very large numbers of classes or attributes. While the datasets tested have moderate class counts (K=10-12), ZSL applications often require handling hundreds or thousands of classes.

4. **Limited Analysis of Attribute Vocabulary**: The paper assumes the attribute vocabulary is expressive enough to discriminate between categories. It doesn't explore cases where the vocabulary is insufficient, which is a common challenge in attribute-based approaches.

5. **Few-Shot Analysis**: While the few-shot extension is promising, the paper provides limited analysis of how the method performs across different numbers of training examples beyond the 200-image case shown in Figure 2a.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The work addresses a significant, practical limitation in zero-shot learning that has been largely overlooked in previous research. The proposed method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates substantial improvements over state-of-the-art baselines on multiple challenging datasets. The paper is well-written, clear, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that the elegance of attribute-based zero-shot learning need not be abandoned due to attribute unreliability. The authors have thoroughly evaluated their approach and provided a compelling case for its superiority over existing methods. The few-shot extension further broadens the applicability of their method.

While the dependency on validation data for ROC estimation is a limitation, this is a practical concern that the authors acknowledge, and the method's performance gains in realistic settings justify this requirement. The paper's contributions are significant, well-supported, and likely to influence future work in zero-shot learning and related areas.

## Final Assessment

*Quality*: 9/10 - The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundation and thorough experimental validation.

*Clarity*: 8/10 - Well-written with clear explanations, though some technical details could be made more accessible.

*Originality*: 9/10 - Addresses a novel aspect of ZSL with a creative solution that builds on but significantly extends prior work.

*Significance*: 9/10 - The problem addressed is important for practical ZSL applications, and the results demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to zero-shot learning that is both theoretically sound and practically valuable. I believe it would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
