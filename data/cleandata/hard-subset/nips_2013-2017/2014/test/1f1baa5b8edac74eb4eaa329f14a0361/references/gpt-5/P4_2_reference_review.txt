Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses zero-shot learning for visual recognition when attribute predictors are unreliable. Standard attribute-based zero-shot methods train attribute classifiers, then map test images to unseen classes defined by attribute “signatures” (e.g., DAP; Farhadi et al.; Lampert et al.). This work proposes a random forest approach that, during training, explicitly accounts for the error characteristics (ROC curves) of attribute classifiers. The key ideas are:
- Build one-versus-all random forests for unseen classes using only their attribute signatures as “training instances.”
- Use a held-out set of attribute-labeled images (no class labels) to estimate per-attribute TP/FP rates at candidate thresholds. During tree construction, signatures are fractionally propagated to child nodes in proportion to these ROC-derived probabilities. This yields node-level soft counts and an information gain objective that prefers splits that are both discriminative and predictable given attribute unreliability.
- Extend to uncertain class–attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”) and to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with that from a small set of class-labeled images using a mixing parameter λ.

The approach is related to decision-tree methods that handle missing or uncertain features via fractional instance propagation, but differs in that trees are constructed over class signatures (not data) and use attribute validation sets to estimate error tendencies at each node. It contrasts with two-stage zero-shot methods (DAP and variants), as well as classifier/label-embedding approaches and knowledge-graph/hierarchy-based methods, by directly modeling attribute unreliability during the construction of the unseen-class classifiers.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a zero-shot random forest that integrates attribute ROC statistics, yielding an information gain criterion that favors splits robust to attribute prediction errors.
- Use of node-specific validation subsets DV(n) to estimate ROC captures some inter-attribute dependencies induced by prior splits.
- Practical few-shot extension via a convex combination of signature- and data-driven information gains, with empirical evidence that the prior remains beneficial even with non-trivial labeled data.
- Extensive experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), including controlled noise studies, ablations (with/without signature uncertainty, with/without node-specific ROC propagation), and comparisons to DAP and other published results. Reported improvements are especially notable when attributes are less reliable.
- Applicability to both semantic attributes and discovered mid-level “pseudo-attributes,” suggesting utility beyond hand-defined vocabularies.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- ROC estimation uses “validation” images that are also pooled back into the attribute classifier training set. This violates standard separation between training and validation and risks optimistic ROC estimates. Since the proposed trees directly exploit these ROCs for split selection, an independent validation split (or nested cross-validation) would better isolate the gains attributable to modeling unreliability.
- The treatment of class signature uncertainty is deferred to the supplement. Because it is central to robustness, more detail in the main paper (functional form, parameters, and sensitivity) would improve reproducibility.
- The few-shot baselines are limited. Comparisons to strong few-shot/zero-shot hybrids (e.g., label embeddings, semantic embeddings, or adaptation from related seen-class classifiers) would strengthen claims.
- While node-specific DV(n) partially captures dependencies, the method still treats attributes conditionally independently given the split, and does not explicitly model attribute correlations beyond this recursive conditioning. Discussion or experiments on correlated attributes would be valuable.
- Scalability: training one forest per unseen class scales linearly in K. For large K or dynamic open-world settings, multi-label forests or shared-tree strategies (mentioned in conclusion) warrant at least a preliminary analysis.
- Calibration dependence: the approach uses Platt-scaled SVM scores and thresholding; performance may be sensitive to calibration quality. An analysis of calibration effects or non-parametric thresholding would be helpful.

Quality
- The core technical contribution—fractional propagation of class signatures based on empirically estimated ROC statistics and corresponding information gain—is sound and aligns with established probabilistic decision-tree practices. Breadth-first construction, stopping criteria, and test-time posterior aggregation are standard.
- The node-specific use of DV(n) to derive ROC entries is a reasonable way to reflect the conditional distributions after prior splits, though data fragmentation can lead to sparse estimates (the paper falls back to global DV when few positives/negatives reach a node).
- Experimental design covers multiple datasets, ablations, and controlled noise, supporting the main claims. However, the non-independent ROC estimation and relatively narrow set of strong baselines limit the conclusiveness of the empirical evidence.

Clarity
- The method is generally clearly explained with precise notation for signature indicators, fractional propagation, and information gain. The flow from basic signature RF to unreliability-aware RF is easy to follow.
- Some essential details are deferred to the supplement (signature uncertainty formulation, pseudocode). Including a concise main-text description of signature noise modeling would improve clarity.

Originality
- Incorporating attribute ROC statistics into zero-shot classifier construction via fractional propagation and information gain is a novel and specific contribution in the attribute-based zero-shot literature.
- The connection to decision-tree methods for uncertain features is acknowledged, with a distinct adaptation to the zero-shot scenario using class signatures and attribute-only validation.

Significance
- Addressing attribute unreliability is central to making attribute-based zero-shot learning practical. The reported gains over DAP and on challenging datasets suggest the method is impactful for low-data category learning.
- The few-shot extension with descriptive priors is simple and potentially useful more broadly for tree-based learners.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a well-motivated limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a technically sound, interpretable method.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over widely used baselines across multiple datasets, with ablation studies and controlled noise analysis.
- Method is simple to implement on top of standard random forests and attribute classifiers.

Cons for acceptance
- Validation protocol blends training and validation for ROC estimation; independent validation would strengthen claims.
- Limited comparison to alternative contemporary zero-/few-shot methods beyond DAP and selected published results.
- Key component (signature uncertainty) lacks sufficient detail in main text; scalability analysis is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Recompute ROCs on an independent validation split or via nested CV; report sensitivity to validation size/domain shift.
- Provide main-text details on signature uncertainty modeling and its hyperparameters.
- Add comparisons to embedding-based zero-shot/few-shot methods and report computational cost.
- Analyze sensitivity to attribute calibration, correlation, and number of unseen classes.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically plausible approach with empirical gains, and addresses an important practical gap in zero-shot learning. The main concerns are the validation protocol and breadth of baselines.
