## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical issue of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). While ZSL offers a promising solution for recognizing novel classes with limited data, its performance heavily relies on the accuracy of attribute predictions, which are often noisy and imperfect. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly models and accounts for this unreliability during training, leveraging the Receiver Operating Characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers. They further extend this approach to handle signature uncertainty and adapt it to the few-shot learning setting.  Experiments on three datasets demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, including the widely used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical limitation of ZSL:** The paper directly tackles a well-known, yet often overlooked, problem in ZSL – the unreliability of attribute predictions. This is a significant contribution as it acknowledges the practical challenges of obtaining perfect attribute information.
* **Novel Approach:**  The proposed method of incorporating attribute classifier ROCs into the random forest training process is a novel and insightful idea.  It moves beyond simply using attribute predictions and instead leverages information about *how confident* those predictions are.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates compelling experimental results on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN), consistently outperforming the strong baseline DAP and the naive SIGNATURE-RF. The gains are particularly pronounced in scenarios with less reliable attributes, validating the core premise of the work.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear rationale for the approach and connects it to existing work on decision trees with missing or uncertain data (fractional tuples).
* **Extension to Few-Shot Learning:** The extension to the few-shot setting is a logical and valuable addition, broadening the applicability of the proposed method.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the proposed methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:**  The method introduces additional complexity compared to simpler ZSL approaches (like DAP). While the gains justify this complexity, it might pose a barrier to adoption for some practitioners.  A more detailed discussion of the computational cost would be helpful.
* **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on a validation set to estimate attribute classifier ROCs.  The performance could be sensitive to the size and representativeness of this validation set. The paper acknowledges this, but a more thorough analysis of this sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Limited Ablation Study Depth:** While ablation studies are included, expanding them could be beneficial. For example, exploring the impact of different ROC estimation methods or alternative ways to incorporate attribute uncertainty could provide further insights.
* **Assumptions about Attribute Vocabulary:**  The method assumes a predefined attribute vocabulary, either hand-designed or discovered. The performance could be impacted if the vocabulary is poorly chosen or incomplete. While acknowledged, a more in-depth discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-motivated and the experimental results strongly support the claims. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.  It is a complete piece of work.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear and well-organized, though the details of the ROC integration into the random forest can be a bit dense. More illustrative examples or diagrams could further enhance clarity.
* **Originality:** The approach of incorporating attribute classifier ROCs into a random forest framework for ZSL is novel. It builds upon existing work but provides a unique and valuable contribution. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the paper within the field.
* **Significance:** The results are significant. Addressing attribute unreliability is a crucial step towards making ZSL a more practical and reliable technique.  The improvements over existing methods demonstrate the potential impact of this work.  Other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant challenge in ZSL.
* Presents a novel and effective solution.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results.
* Offers theoretical justification.
* Extends to the few-shot learning setting.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Method introduces additional complexity.
* Performance relies on a validation dataset.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The authors have identified a critical limitation of ZSL and proposed a novel and effective solution. The experimental results are compelling and the paper is well-written and organized. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent on minor revisions to address the points raised above, such as a more detailed discussion of computational cost, a deeper analysis of validation data sensitivity, and potentially expanding the ablation study.
