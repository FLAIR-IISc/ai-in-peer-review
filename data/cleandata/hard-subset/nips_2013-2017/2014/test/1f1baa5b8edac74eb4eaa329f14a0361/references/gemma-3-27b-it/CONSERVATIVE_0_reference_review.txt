This paper addresses the challenging problem of zero-shot learning, specifically acknowledging the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions – a crucial point often overlooked. The authors propose a random forest-based approach that incorporates attribute classifier ROC characteristics during training to mitigate the impact of unreliable attributes, and extend this to a few-shot setting. While the motivation is sound and the problem important, I find the paper’s contributions incremental and the experimental validation, while extensive, not entirely convincing given the complexity added.

The core idea – adapting decision trees to account for prediction uncertainty – is not entirely novel (citations [21, 28, 16] are acknowledged, albeit briefly). The key contribution lies in adapting this concept specifically to the zero-shot learning setting with attribute-based signatures, and demonstrating its effectiveness. However, the gains over a strong baseline like DAP [8, 9], while statistically significant, are not dramatic, and the added complexity of ROC-aware training feels disproportionate to the improvement achieved. The theoretical justification for the approach, while present, could be strengthened. The authors state it "preemptively builds in the appropriate 'cushion' of expected errors," but a more formal analysis linking ROC curves directly to decision boundary optimization would be beneficial.

The experimental setup is comprehensive, utilizing three datasets and numerous ablations. However, the results are presented in a somewhat fragmented manner. Tables are dense and require careful parsing. While the controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate the benefit of accounting for attribute unreliability, the real-world experiments show improvements that, while statistically significant, appear modest. The performance on AwA, in particular, feels limited. The authors highlight gains on specific categories, but a more holistic view of the overall performance difference is warranted. The discussion of parameter tuning (λ in the few-shot setting) feels cursory. The claim that the method selects λ automatically with cross-validation, while positive, needs more detail – what cross-validation scheme was used, and how stable were the selected λ values across different random seeds?

The writing is generally clear, though occasionally dense. The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the ROC-aware splitting process, perhaps with a simplified illustrative example. The organization is logical, but the connection between related work and the proposed approach could be strengthened. For instance, the discussion of discovered attributes feels somewhat tacked on; a more integrated discussion of how the method handles both hand-crafted and discovered attributes would be helpful.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a crucial issue in zero-shot learning – attribute unreliability.
*   Proposes a technically sound approach based on random forests and ROC analysis.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple datasets.
*   Demonstrates a few-shot learning extension.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental contribution; the core idea isn’t entirely novel.
*   Modest performance gains compared to existing methods like DAP.
*   Theoretical justification could be strengthened.
*   Experimental presentation is somewhat fragmented.
*   Discussion of parameter tuning is limited.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem that has been largely overlooked in the zero-shot learning literature.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and empirically validated.
*   The experimental results, while not dramatic, are consistently positive.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough in zero-shot learning.
*   The performance gains do not justify the added complexity of the approach.
*   The theoretical analysis could be more rigorous.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject with encouragement to revise and strengthen the theoretical justification, and explore more substantial performance gains through architectural improvements or alternative attribute learning strategies. The paper presents a valid contribution but currently falls short of the bar for NIPS acceptance given its incremental nature and modest results. I encourage the authors to address the points raised above and resubmit in the future.
