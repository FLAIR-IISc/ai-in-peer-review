This paper tackles a critical, and often overlooked, issue in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. While the field frequently *assumes* perfect attribute information, the authors correctly point out that this is a strong and often unrealistic assumption. The core idea – to explicitly model and account for attribute uncertainty during training – is both novel and potentially impactful. The proposed random forest approach, incorporating ROC curves to guide split selection, is a clever way to inject this uncertainty into the learning process. The extension to the few-shot setting is a natural and valuable addition.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-established, and the technical details are presented in a digestible manner. The experimental setup appears thorough, utilizing standard datasets and baselines. The ablation studies are particularly insightful, demonstrating the importance of both modeling attribute uncertainty and propagating validation data. The results are compelling, consistently outperforming the strong DAP baseline, particularly in scenarios with noisy attributes. The inclusion of results using discovered attributes further strengthens the applicability of the proposed method. 

The primary strength of this work lies in its originality.  While previous work has acknowledged attribute ambiguity or weakness, this paper takes the bold step of *directly* modeling its impact on learning. This is a significant departure from the typical two-stage pipeline and opens up new avenues for research in zero-shot learning. The idea of leveraging ROC curves during tree construction is particularly inventive and demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying challenges. The clear empirical improvements across multiple datasets solidify the impact of this work.

However, the paper isn't without minor weaknesses. The ROC validation data is a crucial component, but the justification for reserving 20% of the attribute-labeled data feels somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough exploration of the optimal data split, or an investigation into alternative validation techniques, would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge inter-attribute correlations, the current approach doesn’t explicitly model them. Exploring ways to incorporate these dependencies into the split selection process could potentially lead to further improvements. Additionally, the discussion of extending the method to multi-modal attribute signatures feels a bit brief; a more detailed explanation or experimentation would be appreciated. The reliance on SVMs for attribute prediction is standard, but exploring other attribute prediction methods might reveal further opportunities for improvement.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability is genuinely new and represents a significant departure from existing methods.
*   **Impact:** The results demonstrate a clear and consistent improvement over strong baselines, indicating the potential for real-world impact.
*   **Soundness:** The technical approach is well-motivated and the experimental validation is thorough.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Boldness:** The paper tackles a difficult problem head-on and proposes a creative solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The choice of 20% for ROC validation data seems somewhat arbitrary.
*   The method doesn't explicitly model inter-attribute correlations.
*   Limited discussion/experimentation regarding multi-modal signatures.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and impactful approach to zero-shot learning. The core idea is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results. While minor improvements could be made, the originality and potential significance of this work far outweigh its weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance, prioritizing a novel direction over minor methodological refinements. This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to inspire further research in this area.
