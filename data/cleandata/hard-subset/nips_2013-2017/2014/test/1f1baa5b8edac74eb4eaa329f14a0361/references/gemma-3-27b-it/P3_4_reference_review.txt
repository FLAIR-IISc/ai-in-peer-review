## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the challenging field of zero-shot learning. The authors directly address a critical limitation of existing attribute-based ZSL methods – the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. Their proposed approach, which incorporates attribute uncertainty into the training process of a random forest classifier, is both conceptually sound and empirically effective. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and demonstrates strong results on multiple datasets. I recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of zero-shot learning, where new classes are recognized based on semantic descriptions (attributes) rather than labeled training examples. The core idea is to move beyond simply *using* attribute predictions and instead *account for their inherent unreliability* during model training. Existing methods assume perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic.  The authors propose a random forest approach that leverages the ROC curves of the attribute classifiers to select informative splits, effectively handling both unreliable attribute predictions and uncertain class-attribute associations. They also extend this to a few-shot learning setting.

The related work section is thorough and accurately positions the paper within the ZSL landscape. It correctly highlights the two-stage attribute prediction approach, contrasting it with methods exploiting external knowledge and feature discovery techniques.  The authors effectively differentiate their work from prior approaches that focus on attribute *strength* versus their *reliability*, and correctly cite relevant work on handling missing or probabilistic features in decision trees.  The inclusion of cognitive science literature supporting the attribute-based approach is a nice touch, adding context and justification.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The unreliability of attribute predictions is a well-known bottleneck in ZSL, and this paper directly addresses it. This is a highly impactful and practical consideration.
* **Novel Approach:**  Leveraging ROC curves to inform the decision tree splitting process is a clever and effective way to incorporate attribute uncertainty. It's a logical extension of standard decision tree learning and well-suited to the ZSL context.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods (including DAP) on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The ablation studies clearly illustrate the benefits of incorporating both signature uncertainty and ROC propagation.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is easy to follow and clearly explains the methodology. The introduction provides excellent motivation, and the experimental setup is detailed enough for reproducibility.
* **Few-Shot Extension:** The extension to the few-shot setting is a valuable addition, demonstrating the flexibility and robustness of the approach.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The inclusion of controlled noise experiments, real attribute unreliability tests, and comparison with several baselines provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the method shows performance improvements, the computational complexity of the ROC-aware splitting process isn’t explicitly discussed.  Training multiple random forests, especially with the added complexity of the ROC estimation and propagation, could be computationally expensive.  A discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on reserving 20% of the attribute labeled data for validation. While explained, the sensitivity to this percentage is not thoroughly explored. How does performance degrade with less validation data?
* **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the quantitative results are strong, a more detailed qualitative analysis of the error cases could provide further insights. For example, identifying specific attributes that remain challenging for the model, or visualizing the decision boundaries learned by the random forests.
* **Choice of Random Forest:** The authors chose random forests. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss why this particular model was selected, and whether other ensemble methods (e.g., gradient boosting) might be applicable and potentially offer further improvements.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The methodology is clearly explained, and the ablation studies provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear and concise, and the use of diagrams and tables is effective.
* **Originality:**  The idea of incorporating attribute uncertainty into the training process of a random forest for ZSL is novel and represents a significant contribution. While building on existing techniques, the combination and application are highly original.
* **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to significantly impact the field of ZSL. By addressing the problem of attribute unreliability, the authors are paving the way for more robust and practical ZSL systems.  The ability to leverage attribute descriptions for new category recognition is valuable in scenarios with limited labeled data.

**5. Arguments for & against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a significant challenge in ZSL.
* Novel and effective methodology.
* Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Potential for real-world impact.

**Against:**

* Potential computational cost could be a limitation.
* Dependency on validation data requires careful consideration.
* Limited analysis of error cases.




**Conclusion & Recommendation:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh them. The authors present a compelling solution to a critical problem in zero-shot learning, and the empirical results are highly encouraging.  I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.  I encourage the authors to address the concerns regarding computational cost and validation data dependency in the camera-ready version.
