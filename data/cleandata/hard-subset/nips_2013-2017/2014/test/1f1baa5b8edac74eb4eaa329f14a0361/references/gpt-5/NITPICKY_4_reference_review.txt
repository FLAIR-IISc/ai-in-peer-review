Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition with attributes by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. It proposes a random forest (RF) framework that trains trees on class-level attribute signatures (not images), and, crucially, injects attribute validation data to estimate node-specific ROC operating characteristics for each attribute classifier. During training, class signatures are fractionally propagated down the tree according to attribute TP/FP rates at a chosen threshold, yielding an information gain criterion (IGzero) that prefers discriminative yet reliably predictable attributes and thresholds. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in class–attribute associations (details deferred to the supplement) and to a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven IG with data-driven IG via a tunable mixing parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a “signature RF” baseline, as well as robustness under synthetic attribute noise.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The fractional propagation using node-conditional ROC estimates is a sensible and novel way to fold attribute unreliability into split selection. The basic formulation is clear, and the IGzero objective is coherent with the intended behavior (e.g., degenerates when TPR=FPR).
- However, several methodological details are underspecified or contradictory, risking leakage or miscalibration:
  - Data usage ambiguity: Sec. 4 states “reserve 20% as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers,” yet later claims superiority “despite using only 80% to train attribute classifiers.” These statements are incompatible. If DV is also used to train the final attribute SVMs, ROC estimates computed on DV are not out-of-sample, biasing split selection. Please clarify whether DV is strictly held out from training attribute classifiers used at test time.
  - Threshold sampling: The paper does not specify how thresholds t are sampled for ROC/IG search (uniform over [0,1]? quantiles of DV(n) scores? fixed grid?), nor how (#m, #t) are selected per node. This affects reproducibility and could materially change performance.
  - Normalization/mixing in few-shot: IGzero and IGbasic may be on different scales (due to different numbers of instances and fractional vs. hard counts). Eq. (6) uses a convex combination but lacks normalization details. Is entropy computed with the same class priors? Are IG terms re-scaled to prevent the larger pool from dominating? How is λ cross-validated given tiny DT?
  - Small-sample ROC estimation: When few positives/negatives reach a node, the method reverts to global DV. This undermines the claimed ability to capture inter-attribute dependencies. No ablation quantifies the impact of this fallback or calibrates variance of ROC estimates (confidence intervals, smoothing).
  - Overfitting risk: Split selection repeatedly queries DV(n) to pick (m, t). No mechanism (e.g., nested validation or regularization on ROC) is used to prevent overfitting to DV. Yet test-time uses attribute classifiers trained on potentially different data. An analysis of variance or a simple sanity check (DV vs. disjoint DV’) is missing.
  - Class signature uncertainty: The key extension is deferred to the supplement; no explicit form is provided. Without it, the reader cannot assess how annotation noise interacts with fractional propagation (e.g., is it a convolution with a Bernoulli flip model?).
- Evaluation: Results claim significant gains, but tables/figures are referenced and not shown here; crucial numeric details (acc, variance) are absent. The “OURS+TRUE ROC” upper bound uses unseen test images for ROC, which is not a valid training protocol; it is more of an oracle and should be clearly labeled as such (the paper does so, but also uses it to argue headroom without quantifying the gap).

Clarity
- The exposition of the core idea is clear, but critical details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, signature uncertainty, selected unseen SUN classes, noise model), limiting reproducibility.
- Notation: Using I and I’ for hard and soft indicators is fine; Eq. (3) and (5) are consistent. However, Eq. (1)–(2) mix probabilities and counts; Eq. (1) uses P(Ai(m)>t | In(i)=1) but reduces to counts in Eq. (2) without stating the assumption (binary signatures, fixed t). The footnote about t for binary Ai(m) should be integrated in the main text for clarity.
- The procedure to propagate DV and signatures “simultaneously” should be precisely defined; at present, DV(n) is updated only after a split is chosen, but for evaluating candidate splits you need ROC conditioned on the current node’s previous splits, which is consistent; spelling this out would avoid confusion.

Originality
- Explicitly modeling attribute unreliability via node-conditional ROC-driven fractional propagation within a RF trained on class signatures is, to my knowledge, novel relative to DAP and prior zero-shot with attributes. The connection to decision trees with missing/uncertain features is appropriate, and the zero-shot-specific adaptation is distinct (training on signatures, ROC estimated from attribute-labeled data).
- Few-shot extension as a descriptive prior for forests is a reasonable combination of known ingredients; not groundbreaking but useful.

Significance
- If validated rigorously, robustness to attribute unreliability is important for zero-shot methods relying on mid-level semantics. The gains over DAP on three datasets suggest practical impact. The idea may generalize beyond attributes (e.g., other noisy mid-level detectors).
- That said, the empirical section lacks comprehensive, controlled comparisons to stronger contemporary zero-shot baselines that do not require attributes (e.g., DeViSE, label embeddings) under matched supervision budgets. The AwA “all published results” comparison is mentioned but details are missing; supervision types differ. The SUN/aPY choices (10 unseen classes on SUN chosen at random) should be reported and repeated across multiple random splits.

Pros
- Clear, intuitive idea; principled use of ROC to choose reliable attributes/thresholds.
- Simple to implement atop RFs; computationally modest.
- Improves robustness under synthetic and real attribute noise; few-shot extension is natural.

Cons
- Ambiguity/leakage in validation/training split for attribute classifiers.
- Many implementation details missing (threshold sampling, normalization in few-shot).
- Several key components deferred to supplement; limited reproducibility from main text.
- Limited comparisons to non-attribute zero-shot baselines; potential selection bias on SUN split.
- No analysis of sensitivity to DV size/quality or to domain shift between seen/unseen classes.

Questions for authors
1. Is DV strictly disjoint from the data used to train the attribute SVMs ultimately deployed at test time? If not, please rerun with a true hold-out DV.
2. How are thresholds t sampled per node? Is there any calibration of Platt scores across attributes?
3. How are IG terms normalized in Eq. (6)? Do you weight by the number of (fractional) samples to ensure comparable scales?
4. How sensitive is performance to the size of DV and to miscalibrated Platt probabilities? Any benefit from isotonic regression?
5. Please provide full numeric tables for all datasets and list the 10 unseen SUN classes and random seeds.

Minor issues and formatting
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues). Please fix all citations.
- Typographical issues: inconsistent spacing around equations; “whale seal” class name looks suspect; clarify.
- Notation “<M” should be R^M. Use consistent math fonts.
- Excessive reliance on “supp.” for core algorithmic details; move essentials to main paper.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper, as written, has too many methodological ambiguities, missing details, and reliance on supplemental material. Clarifying data usage to avoid leakage, providing full experimental details and stronger comparisons, and moving key technical content into the main text would significantly strengthen the submission. My decision is driven less by the main idea (which I like) and more by concerns over rigor and reproducibility.
