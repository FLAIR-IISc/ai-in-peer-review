Summary
The paper tackles a central bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are often unreliable, and most existing methods take their outputs at face value. The authors propose a zero-shot random forest that explicitly models attribute unreliability during training. Key ideas:
- Train one-vs-all forests over class attribute signatures (no class-labeled images), where splits are chosen on attribute dimensions and thresholds.
- Inject a held-out, attribute-labeled validation set DV to estimate node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) for each attribute/threshold.
- Fractionally propagate class signatures down the tree according to those ROC rates, redefining information gain to account for partial mass at each node. This favors splits that are both discriminative and reliably predictable.
- Extend the framework to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to few-shot learning by combining signature-driven IG with standard IG on a small set of labeled images via a mixing parameter λ.

Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline; controlled noise studies illuminate the benefits when attributes vary in reliability; ablations isolate contributions (signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation). A few-shot extension further improves performance and behaves sensibly as the number of labeled examples grows.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within attribute-based zero-shot learning (DAP and variants), and contrasts with transfer/embedding methods (label/feature embeddings, hierarchy-based, co-occurrence). It relates to decision trees with missing/uncertain features (fractional tuples, fuzzy trees), but adapts the idea to the zero-shot regime with training on class signatures and node-specific ROCs gleaned from attribute-labeled validation data. The semantic vs. discovered attribute discussion is appropriate; the method also works with discovered attributes.

Strengths
- Novelty: The core contribution—training zero-shot classifiers that explicitly integrate attribute unreliability via ROC-driven, node-specific fractional propagation—is conceptually clean, original, and addresses a known pain point that has limited attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Practicality and generality: Requires only a modest attribute-labeled validation set; integrates naturally with random forests; extends to continuous signatures and to few-shot training with an interpretable λ.
- Soundness and insight: The node-specific ROC conditioning is a thoughtful design that captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits. The information gain reformulation is appropriate and yields intuitive behavior (e.g., unlearnable attributes get zero gain).
- Empirical evidence: Consistent improvements over DAP and a strong signature RF baseline across three datasets, with ablations clarifying which parts matter. Controlled noise experiments convincingly demonstrate why accounting for attribute-specific reliability helps, especially when reliabilities differ across attributes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Distribution shift in DV: The approach hinges on ROC estimates from attribute-labeled images of seen classes. If unseen classes exhibit different attribute statistics or visual manifestations, TP/FP estimates may be biased. The authors acknowledge this and even report an upper bound using “true” ROCs, but more analysis on sensitivity to DV composition would help (e.g., varying DV size; using more/less similar seen classes; cross-dataset DV).
- Data efficiency at nodes: Node-specific ROC estimation can be data-hungry; the fallback to global DV when few positives/negatives reach a node is reasonable but may reduce the claimed dependency modeling. Some quantitative analysis of how often the fallback triggers, and its impact on performance/variance, would be helpful.
- Baselines breadth: DAP is a canonical baseline, and published numbers for other methods are reported for AwA, but a more systematic head-to-head comparison against contemporary embedding/transfer methods on all three datasets would strengthen the case. Also, a simple calibrated stacking/threshold-optimization baseline over attributes could test whether ROC-aware thresholding alone (without trees) explains gains.
- Theoretical guarantees: The method is largely empirical; while that’s fine here, even a simple consistency argument or generalization bound under ROC estimation error would add depth.
- Clarity details deferred to supplement: Some important pieces (signature uncertainty term, pseudocode, noise models) are in the supp. A brief in-paper summary of the signature uncertainty model would aid readability.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to the calibration of attribute scores (e.g., Platt scaling quality)? Would using isotonic regression or temperature scaling change results?
- Did you try precision-recall curves for attributes with heavy imbalance? Does optimizing AP-aware thresholds at nodes help?
- Can you quantify the fraction of nodes where DV(n) had sufficient positives/negatives vs. fallback to DV, and the observed impact?
- How does training/inference time scale with M and K? Any memory or latency concerns when M,K are large?
- Can the approach directly incorporate attribute correlation models (e.g., via multivariate splits or group tests)? Any preliminary results?

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear exposition of the core idea and its intuition. Adding a brief in-paper description of the signature-uncertainty formulation and some complexity notes would improve completeness.

Reproducibility
Reasonable: datasets are standard; features and SVMs are described; key hyperparameters and ablations are reported. Including pseudocode and more implementation details in the main text would help, as would code release.

Pros
- Addresses an important failure mode of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a simple, elegant, and novel idea.
- Strong empirical gains and informative controlled-noise studies.
- Clean extension to few-shot with interpretable mixing.

Cons
- Reliance on DV representativeness; limited analysis of sensitivity to DV composition and node data scarcity.
- Baseline coverage could be broader across datasets; some details deferred to supplement.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a fresh, impactful direction—explicitly baking attribute unreliability into zero-shot training via ROC-aware random forests—that is likely to influence how the community builds semantic zero-shot models and other mid-level concept pipelines. Despite some limitations and deferred details, the novelty and demonstrated gains outweigh the concerns.

Confidence
High.
