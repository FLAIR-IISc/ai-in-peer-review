This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning with unreliable attribute predictions. The core idea is to build a random forest classifier that explicitly accounts for the uncertainty of attribute predictions during training, rather than treating them as ground truth. The approach propagates attribute predictions through the forest, weighting branches based on the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers. The method is further extended to the few-shot setting.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing attribute-based zero-shot learning methods that assume perfect attribute predictions. The proposed solution is conceptually sound and builds upon existing techniques like random forests and ROC analysis in a novel way. The extension to the few-shot setting is also a valuable contribution.  The experimental evaluation on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) is thorough, comparing the proposed method against several baselines, including the widely used DAP method. The results demonstrate clear improvements over existing approaches, particularly in scenarios with unreliable attribute predictions. The ablation studies further validate the effectiveness of the proposed components, specifically accounting for signature uncertainty and ROC propagation. The presentation of results, with controlled noise experiments and analysis of gains per category, is convincing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-defined and appears technically sound. The use of ROC curves to weight branches in the random forest is a clever way to account for attribute unreliability. The mathematical formulation is clear and concise.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The ideas are presented in a logical order, and the figures and tables are helpful.
*   **Originality:** The approach of explicitly accounting for attribute unreliability during training is novel. While random forests and ROC analysis are well-established techniques, their application to zero-shot learning in this manner is a significant contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed method can significantly improve the performance of zero-shot learning, especially when attribute predictions are unreliable. This is a practical limitation of current zero-shot learning methods, and the proposed solution addresses it effectively. The gains on datasets like SUN and aPY, which have fewer positive training samples per attribute, highlight the importance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The use of validation data to estimate ROC curves adds to the computational complexity of the method. While not explicitly discussed, the cost of training and evaluating the random forest with this additional step could be a concern for very large datasets or a large number of attributes.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces parameters like λ (in the few-shot extension) and potentially the size of the validation set, which may require careful tuning. The sensitivity of the method to these parameters is not extensively explored.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the results are generally positive, the paper does not delve deeply into the scenarios where the method fails or performs poorly. Identifying the limitations of the approach would strengthen the paper.
*   **Connection to Existing Work on Uncertainty:** While the authors acknowledge related work on handling missing values and probabilistic features (e.g., fractional tuples, [21, 28]), the discussion could be expanded to include more recent advances in modeling uncertainty in machine learning.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods.
*   Proposes a novel and technically sound approach to account for attribute unreliability.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Contributions are aligned with the NIPS focus on machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential computational cost.
*   Parameter sensitivity.
*   Limited discussion of failure cases.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and well-supported by experimental results. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational cost, parameter sensitivity, and a more detailed discussion of failure cases.
