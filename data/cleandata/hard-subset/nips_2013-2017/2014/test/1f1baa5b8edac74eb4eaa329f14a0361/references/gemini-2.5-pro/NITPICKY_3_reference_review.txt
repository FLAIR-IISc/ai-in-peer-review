This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning (ZSL) in the context of unreliable attribute classifiers. The core premise is that standard attribute-based ZSL models, such as Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP), are handicapped because they treat the outputs of mid-level attribute classifiers as veridical, despite their known high error rates. The authors propose a novel random forest-based ZSL framework that explicitly models this unreliability during training. Instead of training on attribute signatures directly, the proposed method propagates "fractional" signatures through the decision trees, where the splits are determined by the expected true/false positive rates of the attribute classifiers, estimated on a validation set. The information gain criterion is adapted to account for this fractional propagation, leading the forest to select splits that are not only discriminative in the ideal signature space but also robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning setting. The approach is evaluated on three standard ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating significant improvements over the DAP baseline and showing strong performance compared to other published methods.

**Strengths:**

*   The central idea of explicitly modeling attribute classifier unreliability within the ZSL training process is novel and compelling. It addresses a well-known, critical bottleneck in attribute-based recognition that is often acknowledged but rarely tackled directly in the learning model.
*   The proposed mechanism, using ROC statistics from a validation set to guide the fractional propagation of training signatures, is an elegant way to inject knowledge about classifier performance into the model.
*   The experimental results are strong. The method consistently and significantly outperforms the widely used DAP baseline across three different datasets. The controlled noise experiments provide clear intuition for why and how the method works.
*   The extension to the few-shot setting is natural and demonstrates the utility of the attribute signatures as a strong prior for learning from limited data.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is riddled with methodological ambiguities, experimental gaps, and formatting inconsistencies that, in aggregate, undermine the work's contribution.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Dependence on Validation Data (`DV`):** The entire method hinges on the quality of the ROC statistics estimated from the validation set `DV`. The paper states this set is a 20% holdout from the attribute-labeled "seen" class images. However, there is no analysis of the sensitivity of the method to the size or composition of `DV`. More critically, the attribute distributions in the seen classes may not be representative of the unseen classes, potentially leading to biased ROC estimates and suboptimal performance. The paper acknowledges this by testing an "upper bound" using unseen class images for `DV`, but it fails to adequately address the potential mismatch in its primary setup. The claim that focusing `DV` on similar seen classes had "negligible" impact feels like an important detail that is dismissed too quickly.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The primary baseline is DAP (Lampert et al., 2009). While foundational, the ZSL field has advanced significantly. The paper mentions but does not compare against more contemporary ZSL methods in its main results table (Table 1), including embedding-based approaches (e.g., DeViSE, ALE) which are also cited. While Figure 2b provides a qualitative comparison on AwA, a direct quantitative comparison is necessary to properly situate the work and substantiate claims of state-of-the-art performance.
3.  **Unreported Methodological Details:** The paper claims state-of-the-art results on AwA by using "discovered attributes" from [30]. It mentions this required a "generalization of our method to handle the continuous attribute strength signatures". This is a non-trivial extension, yet it is mentioned only in passing with no technical details provided in the main text. This is a significant omission that makes a key result unreproducible and difficult to evaluate. Similarly, the method for handling class signature uncertainty (Section 3.2.3) is deferred entirely to the supplement.

**Minor Methodological and Experimental Concerns:**

1.  **Attribute Independence:** The model splits on one attribute at a time. While the paper argues that propagating `DV(n)` captures some dependencies, the splitting criterion itself still treats attributes as independent at each node. This ignores known strong correlations between attributes (e.g., "furry" and "brown") and is a clear limitation.
2.  **Scalability:** The one-vs-all training scheme for the random forests does not scale well to scenarios with a large number of unseen classes. This is mentioned as future work but is a practical limitation of the current method.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several hyperparameters (tree depth, number of splits to test, `λ` for few-shot learning). The paper states they are set via cross-validation but provides no details on this process or any analysis of the model's sensitivity to these choices. For the few-shot experiment, it is unclear how CV can be reliably performed on the very small training sets (`DT`).
4.  **Few-shot Baseline:** The baseline for the few-shot experiment is a standard random forest trained only on the few-shot examples. This is a weak baseline. A stronger comparison would be to methods that fine-tune a model pre-trained on the seen classes, or other established few-shot learning techniques.

**Clarity and Formatting Issues:**

The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness in its presentation.
*   There is a dangling footnote marker `1` in the first paragraph of the introduction.
*   Footnote 2 is placed awkwardly in the middle of a sentence.
*   The references are poorly and inconsistently formatted, with extraneous commas and missing information (e.g., [2], [5], [16]).
*   There are minor typos/unclear phrases (e.g., "whale seal" instead of "whale, seal").
*   The caption for Figure 1 is missing, forcing the reader to hunt for it in the main text. The reference is to "See supp. for details on the noise model," but the figure caption itself should be present.

**Summary:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   A novel and well-motivated approach to a critical problem in ZSL.
*   Strong empirical performance against a key baseline (DAP).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Significant questions about the reliance on the validation set (`DV`) and its representativeness.
*   Lack of comparison to more recent and relevant ZSL baselines.
*   Crucial methodological details (handling of continuous attributes for the SOTA claim) are completely omitted from the paper.
*   A general lack of rigor in the experimental analysis (hyperparameter sensitivity, few-shot baseline).
*   Numerous formatting errors and a general lack of polish suggest a rushed submission.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the core idea is valuable, the paper in its current form is not a complete piece of work. The combination of methodological ambiguity, an outdated primary baseline, and a general lack of attention to detail prevents me from recommending acceptance. The authors should address the concerns about the validation data, perform a more thorough comparison with contemporary methods, and include the crucial missing details about their model's extensions before the work is ready for publication.
