

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical but overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL): the assumption that attribute predictions are reliable. While existing methods (e.g., DAP [8]) treat attribute predictions as perfect, the authors correctly identify that attribute classifiers are often unreliable due to correlated attributes and diverse visual instantiations. Their solution uses random forests that incorporate receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers during training, propagating signatures through trees based on actual false positive/true positive rates rather than assuming perfect predictions. The paper also extends to handle class signature uncertainty and provides a natural few-shot extension.

The work properly differentiates itself from related approaches that abandon semantic attributes for discovered features [11, 22] and from decision tree methods handling missing values [21]. It makes a clear distinction between attribute unreliability (the focus) and attribute strength, a nuance often overlooked in prior work.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear formulation of their method with detailed pseudocode and thorough ablation studies. Their experiments on three standard ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art, particularly DAP. The controlled noise experiments provide valuable insight into how the method handles attribute unreliability.

However, the theoretical justification for using ROC in this specific context is somewhat underdeveloped. While the authors provide intuition, a more rigorous analysis of why ROC-based propagation is optimal for the zero-shot setting would strengthen the paper. The requirement for 20% of attribute-labeled data as validation (necessary for ROC estimation) is a practical limitation that needs more thorough discussion regarding scalability and real-world applicability.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The main ideas are clearly presented, with effective use of figures and tables to illustrate the method and results. The ablation studies help isolate the contribution of different components of the method. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The paper could be strengthened by more explicit discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the assumptions about validation data. The statement that "the attribute-labeled validation data is important to our solution's robustness" needs elaboration—how robust is it to distribution shifts between validation and test data? What happens if the validation data doesn't match the unseen classes?

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a key limitation in ZSL that has been largely overlooked. While related work exists on handling uncertainty in decision trees [21, 28], the authors clearly differentiate their approach as specifically designed for the zero-shot scenario with attribute signatures. The insight that attribute unreliability is distinct from attribute strength is particularly valuable and well-justified.

The paper's most compelling original contribution is demonstrating that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than having better attribute classifiers (shown by their 8-point gain over DAP when using only 80% of attribute-labeled data).

## Significance
This work is highly significant as it makes zero-shot learning more practical by acknowledging and addressing a key limitation. The results show that the method substantially improves performance over state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks, even with unreliable attributes. The few-shot extension is particularly promising for real-world applications where small amounts of labeled data may be available.

The paper's insights about the importance of modeling attribute classifier unreliability rather than just relying on better attribute classifiers have broader implications for transfer learning research. It suggests that more attention should be paid to the reliability of intermediate representations in transfer learning.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a critical, overlooked limitation in ZSL literature
- Simple yet effective solution with clear theoretical motivation
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets
- Comprehensive ablation studies demonstrating component contributions
- Natural and useful few-shot extension

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical justification for ROC-based approach
- Validation data requirement (20% of attribute-labeled data) not thoroughly discussed
- Few-shot extension not deeply analyzed
- Limited discussion of scalability to larger attribute/class sets

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to zero-shot learning. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and well-executed, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art. While there are some limitations regarding theoretical justification and practical constraints, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

I recommend acceptance for NeurIPS. This paper advances the state of the art in zero-shot learning in a practical and meaningful way, and its insights about attribute unreliability will likely influence future research in the field. The work is particularly relevant to NeurIPS as it addresses a key challenge in visual recognition that has practical implications for real-world applications.

**Final Recommendation:** Accept
