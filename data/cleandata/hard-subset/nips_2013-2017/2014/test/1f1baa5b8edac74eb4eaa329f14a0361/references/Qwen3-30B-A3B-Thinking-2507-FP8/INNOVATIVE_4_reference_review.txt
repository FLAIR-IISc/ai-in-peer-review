

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper tackles a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning: the assumption of perfect attribute predictions. While attribute-based zero-shot learning has been a popular approach, the authors rightly point out that visual attributes are often unreliable predictors, yet existing methods treat attribute predictions as perfect. The paper proposes a novel solution using random forests that explicitly account for attribute unreliability by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers during tree construction.

The core innovation lies in their random forest approach that uses attribute signatures (not image features) as training examples, and then modifies the tree-building process to account for the false positive/true positive rates of each attribute classifier. This is a significant departure from standard approaches like DAP and SIGNATURE-RF, which treat attribute predictions as perfect. The authors further extend their method to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of this approach through comprehensive experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). Their method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods like DAP by significant margins (e.g., 10.2% gain on "giant panda" in AwA), demonstrating that modeling attribute unreliability is more valuable than simply having more attribute-labeled data. The ablation studies clearly show the contribution of each component of their method.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The paper identifies a critical oversight in the zero-shot learning literature (treating attributes as perfect) and proposes a fundamentally new approach to address it. The use of ROC characteristics to guide tree construction is a fresh perspective that hasn't been explored in this context.

2. **Significant Impact:** The results are impressive and demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The method works particularly well where attribute classifiers are most unreliable (e.g., SUN and aPY), exactly where the approach is needed most.

3. **Clear Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a well-structured method with solid theoretical foundations, including clear mathematical formulations and thorough experimental validation.

4. **Practical Relevance:** The approach has clear implications for real-world applications where attribute predictions are imperfect, and the few-shot extension broadens its applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The method relies on having validation data to estimate ROC characteristics. While the authors address this by reserving 20% of attribute-labeled data, it's worth noting that in some real-world scenarios, obtaining such validation data might be challenging.

2. The paper could provide more discussion on how to handle attribute correlations (mentioned briefly in the conclusion as future work), as correlated attributes might affect the performance of the random forest approach.

3. The few-shot extension is promising but not deeply explored beyond the presented results. More analysis of how the method scales with larger few-shot datasets would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by addressing a fundamental limitation that has been overlooked in most previous work. The authors present a novel, well-motivated approach that demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods, and the paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated.

While the method has some limitations (primarily the reliance on validation data for ROC estimation), these are minor compared to the paper's substantial contribution. The novelty and significance of the approach far outweigh the technical limitations, especially given that the authors are addressing a real problem that has hampered the practical application of attribute-based zero-shot learning.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. It introduces a bold new direction for zero-shot learning that has demonstrable impact, and the authors have provided thorough experimental validation to support their claims.

**Recommendation:** Accept
