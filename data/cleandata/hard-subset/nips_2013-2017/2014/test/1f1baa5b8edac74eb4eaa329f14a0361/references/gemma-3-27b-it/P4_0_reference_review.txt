The paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning with unreliable attribute predictions. The core idea is to account for the known biases of mid-level attribute models during training by leveraging the attribute classifiers’ receiver operating characteristics within a random forest algorithm. The approach is further generalized to account for unreliable class-attribute associations and extended to the few-shot setting. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN), showcasing clear advantages over existing models, including the widely used DAP method.

This paper tackles a crucial limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute classifiers. Existing methods largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is unrealistic. The proposed approach offers a principled way to mitigate this issue by explicitly modeling and incorporating attribute uncertainty into the learning process. The use of ROC curves to guide split selection in the random forest is a novel and well-motivated idea. The extension to the few-shot setting further enhances the practical applicability of the method. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing baselines, especially on datasets with less reliable attribute classifiers (aPY and SUN). The inclusion of controlled noise experiments provides valuable insight into the robustness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-defined and based on sound principles. The use of ROC curves and fractional propagation of signatures is a clever way to handle attribute uncertainty. The extension to few-shot learning is logically consistent and effectively integrated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the proposed approach is explained in a reasonably understandable manner. The notation is clear, and the algorithmic steps are well-defined.
*   **Originality:** The idea of incorporating attribute unreliability into a random forest framework for zero-shot learning is novel. While related work explores handling missing or probabilistic features, this paper specifically addresses the unique challenges of attribute-based zero-shot learning, where the uncertainty stems from the mid-level attribute classifiers themselves.
*   **Significance:** The results demonstrate a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art DAP method, particularly on challenging datasets. Addressing the issue of attribute unreliability is a crucial step towards making attribute-based zero-shot learning a more practical and reliable technique. The extension to few-shot learning further enhances the method's potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, the use of ROC curves and validation data adds computational overhead compared to simpler methods. The impact of this overhead on scalability is not fully addressed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method may be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the tree depth and the weighting parameter λ in the few-shot extension. Further analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Validation Data Requirement:** The method requires a separate set of validation data to estimate the ROC curves. While the authors use 20% of the attribute-labeled data for this purpose, the performance implications of varying the size of the validation set are not explored.
*   **Limited Discussion of Attribute Discovery:** The paper briefly mentions the applicability of the method to discovered attributes. A more in-depth analysis of the performance with discovered attributes, along with a comparison to methods specifically designed for discovered attributes, would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the method is not fully addressed.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and validation data requirements could limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The discussion of attribute discovery is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to address a crucial challenge in zero-shot learning. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
