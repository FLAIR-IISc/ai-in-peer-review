Summary
This paper tackles zero-shot learning (ZSL) with attributes by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. Rather than taking predicted attributes at face value (as in DAP or similarity-to-signature methods), the authors train one-vs-all random forests directly on class-attribute signatures, but augment the tree-building process with (i) ROC-derived statistics of each attribute classifier’s error rates estimated from attribute-labeled validation data and (ii) fractional propagation of class signatures down the tree to reflect expected TP/FP/FN/TN at a chosen threshold. This encourages splits that are both discriminative and learnable. They further account for uncertainty in the class signatures themselves, and extend the approach to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and data-based information gains. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations and controlled noise studies that isolate the value of modeling attribute unreliability.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-positioned relative to attribute-based ZSL (e.g., Farhadi et al., Lampert et al. DAP, Parikh & Grauman), and distinguishes attribute unreliability from attribute strength. It contrasts with alternatives that bypass semantic attributes (discovered mid-level features) or leverage label embeddings/hierarchies (Mensink et al., Frome et al., Akata et al.). The closest technical relatives are decision trees with missing/uncertain features (fractional propagation, fuzzy trees), but the authors argue—and implement—key differences for the signature-training/attribute-prediction-testing regime.

Strengths
- Clear, simple idea with sound intuition: maximize expected information gain under a test-time attribute noise model derived from ROC curves; avoids unlearnable splits.
- Addresses a real impediment in attribute-based ZSL; controlled-noise experiments convincingly demonstrate robustness benefits.
- Solid empirical evaluation across three datasets with >250 real attribute classifiers; consistent improvements over DAP and a naive signature-based RF; useful ablations (no signature uncertainty, global vs node-specific ROC).
- Few-shot extension is natural and effective; the λ tradeoff behaves sensibly as data increases; cross-validation strategy described.
- Can operate with discovered (pseudo)attributes as well as named ones.

Weaknesses
- Baseline coverage is limited. DAP is a strong historical baseline, but more recent contemporaries (label/feature embeddings like DeViSE, ALE; class-combination approaches) are not compared on the same splits, making it harder to gauge SOTA impact beyond DAP.
- The method hinges on ROC statistics estimated on seen classes; domain shift between seen-attribute distributions and unseen classes’ attribute realizations is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed. The “upper bound” experiment with true ROC is informative, but it underscores sensitivity to this assumption.
- One-vs-all forests may not scale optimally with many unseen classes; a multiclass forest or shared trees could reduce computation and capture inter-class structure.
- Inter-attribute correlations are only partially addressed via node-conditional ROC estimation; no explicit modeling of correlations or joint tests in splits.
- Some implementation details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, signature-uncertainty formulation, continuous attributes), which slightly hampers reproducibility from the main text.

Quality
- Technically sound and careful. The fractional propagation guided by node-conditional ROCs is a principled way to optimize for test-time conditions. The fallback when DV(n) is small is pragmatic.
- Experimental design is thoughtful: controlled noise studies, cross-validation of λ, ablations. However, runtime/complexity and sensitivity to validation set size/composition are not reported and would be helpful.
- The few-shot combination of information gains is reasonable; a short analysis of λ sensitivity and its interaction with class imbalance would add depth.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The motivation and differences from related work are clear. Figures/tables (as described) support claims. Some details (exact signature-uncertainty model, pseudocode) are in the supplement; a brief in-paper equation or intuition would improve self-containment.

Originality
- The core contribution—embedding attribute unreliability via ROC-based fractional propagation into the training of zero-shot trees on signatures—is novel and distinct from prior tree work on missing/uncertain features. The few-shot “descriptive prior” via signatures is also a neat twist.

Significance
- The paper addresses a barrier that has constrained practical ZSL with attributes and shows consistent improvements on standard datasets. Given the enduring interest in ZSL and the simplicity/practicality of the approach, the contribution is likely to be used and extended (e.g., to generalized ZSL, other semantic mid-levels). Broader impact could be stronger with comparisons to additional modern baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled method that directly addresses a key failure mode in attribute-based ZSL.
- Strong empirical gains and useful ablations across multiple datasets.
- Extensible to few-shot and to discovered attributes.
- Clear positioning relative to prior art.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to several influential contemporary ZSL baselines (e.g., label embeddings).
- Sensitivity to the quality and domain match of ROC estimates not thoroughly probed; no analysis of runtime or scalability to many unseen classes.
- Some reliance on supplementary materials for core components.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines such as ALE/DeViSE/metric/compositional approaches on the same splits, or provide a reasoned discussion if infeasible.
- Analyze sensitivity to size/composition of DV, and to ROC estimation noise; include per-attribute reliability vs. model gains correlations.
- Report training/inference time and memory vs. K and M; consider a multiclass RF variant.
- Provide a brief mathematical description of the signature-uncertainty term in the main paper and an experiment where class signatures are intentionally noisy/multi-modal.
- Explore generalized ZSL (seen+unseen at test time) and/or calibration for open-set settings.
- Consider explicit joint tests or oblique splits to better handle correlated attributes.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clear, effective, and novel approach to a well-motivated problem, with convincing evidence against strong baselines in the attribute-based ZSL family. Addressing the suggested additional baselines and analyses would strengthen the case further.
