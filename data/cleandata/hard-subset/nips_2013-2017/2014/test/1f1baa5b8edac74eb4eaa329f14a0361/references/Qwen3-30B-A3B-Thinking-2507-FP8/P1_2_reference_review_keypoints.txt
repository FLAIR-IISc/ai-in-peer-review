- Paper tackles zero-shot learning failure due to unreliable attribute classifiers by using ROC statistics to build random forests that train on attribute signatures and account for prediction errors.  
- Method includes fractional propagation of signatures, extensions for signature uncertainty and few-shot learning, and shows strong gains on AwA, aPY, and SUN.

- Strength: Clearly motivated identification of a practical, overlooked limitation of attribute-based ZSL and convincing analysis of failure modes.  
- Strength: Novel technical contribution—using ROC-guided random forest construction and fractional signature propagation to handle attribute unreliability.  
- Strength: Thorough experimental evaluation with controlled-noise and real-world experiments, plus useful ablation studies.  
- Strength: Practical, simple-to-implement method and valuable few-shot extension bridging zero- and few-shot regimes.  
- Strength: Authors honestly evaluate limitations and provide an upper-bound variant (OURS+TRUE ROC) to show theoretical potential.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical analysis or formal guarantees explaining why the method reduces error.  
- Weakness: Insufficient implementation details about how ROC statistics and thresholds are computed and how validation data is partitioned.  
- Weakness: Scalability concerns not addressed—computational complexity as classes/attributes grow is unclear.  
- Weakness: Limited comparison to more recent ZSL methods beyond the cited 2014–2015 work.  
- Weakness: Assumes attribute vocabulary is sufficiently expressive and does not discuss failure when attributes are insufficient.

- Specific: Controlled-noise experiments are excellent and should be emphasized more.  
- Specific: Clarify how much attribute classifier training data is reserved for ROC estimation and how thresholds are selected.  
- Specific: Better justify the combined information gain formulation used in the few-shot extension.  
- Specific: Discuss handling of attribute correlations, a known issue for attribute-based methods.  
- Specific: Report computational cost relative to standard SIGNATURE RF training.

- Overall assessment: Strong empirical contribution, novel and well-motivated; minor theoretical and scalability gaps do not preclude acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
