Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot learning with attributes by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. Instead of taking predicted attributes at face value (as in DAP and many two-stage zero-shot methods), the authors train a random forest over class attribute signatures where, during tree construction, each split is chosen using estimated ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) of the attribute classifiers on a validation set. Signatures are fractionally propagated down both children in proportion to these error rates, yielding split criteria that are both discriminative and likely to be preserved under noisy attribute predictions. The method also (i) handles uncertainty in class-attribute associations (signature noise) and (ii) extends to few-shot by combining signature-based and data-based information gain with a weighting parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a “signature-only RF” baseline, with the largest benefits when attributes are less reliable. The approach also performs competitively with discovered pseudo-attributes.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on attribute-based zero-shot recognition (e.g., Farhadi et al., Lampert et al. DAP, topic/embedding variants) by retaining the semantically interpretable attribute interface but addressing a key practical limitation: imperfect attribute predictors. It is also related to random forests and decision trees with soft/missing inputs (e.g., fuzzy trees, fractional tuples), but applies these ideas in a distinct regime: training trees on idealized signatures while using validation ROC statistics to model test-time attribute noise. Compared to alternative zero-shot strategies via external label embeddings or classifier transfer (e.g., hierarchy or co-occurrences), the contribution is orthogonal: robustifying semantic-attribute-based zero-shot with explicit error modeling.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The fractional propagation based on node-specific ROC estimates is well-motivated and yields a clear information gain objective. The few-shot extension as a convex combination of signature- and image-based IG is coherent.
- Empirical evaluation: The paper evaluates on three standard datasets with many attributes, includes controlled-noise diagnostics, ablations (no signature uncertainty, no node-specific ROC propagation), and compares to DAP and a signature-only RF. The observed trends match the hypothesis: larger gains when attributes are noisier.
- Concerns:
  - Potential leakage/optimism in ROC estimation: It is stated that 20% of attribute-labeled data is reserved to compute ROC, then “pooled” with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. Please clarify the chronological order. If the final attribute SVMs are trained on 100%, but ROC is estimated on a subset of that same data, the ROC may be optimistic. Ideally ROC should be computed on data not used to fit the attribute models used at test time (or via nested cross-validation).
  - Baselines: Beyond DAP and signature-RF, a stronger set of baselines would include: (i) a DAP variant that reweights attributes by reliability (e.g., incorporate per-attribute TPR/FPR into priors/likelihoods), (ii) nearest-signature with learned per-attribute weights from ROC, (iii) label embedding/metric-transfer baselines (if applicable to these datasets).
  - Scalability/complexity: Some discussion of computational costs (tree depth, candidate split sampling, number of attributes M) and memory would help. How does performance scale with M and K?
  - Assumptions: The method assumes that attribute error statistics estimated on seen classes transfer to unseen classes. Node-specific ROC helps with conditional dependence induced by earlier splits, but inter-attribute correlations and distribution shift across classes remain potential failure modes.
  - Details deferred to supplement: Signature-uncertainty modeling is important; at least a brief description in the main paper would improve self-containment.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The intuition behind fractional propagation and node-specific ROC is nicely presented. Minor issues:
- Be explicit about the ROC estimation protocol to avoid confusion about data reuse.
- Provide pseudocode (or a compact algorithm sketch) for tree training in the main paper.
- Clarify candidate threshold sampling strategy and how continuous-valued signatures are handled.
- Add a brief complexity analysis.

Originality
Modeling attribute unreliability within the split-selection of a random forest trained on signatures, via node-specific ROC-based fractional propagation, is novel in the zero-shot attribute literature. While soft decision trees and handling missing/probabilistic features exist, their adaptation to a zero-shot regime (training on signatures, testing on predicted attributes) and the few-shot integration are original and insightful.

Significance
The work addresses a practical and often-overlooked bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning and demonstrates consistent gains on multiple datasets. The approach is simple, efficient at test time, and broadly applicable wherever mid-level semantic predictors are noisy. The contribution should be of interest to the zero/few-shot, transfer learning, and recognition communities.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated idea that directly tackles a key limitation of attribute-based zero-shot.
- Solid empirical gains over strong and widely used baselines (DAP).
- Insightful controlled-noise experiments; ablations support claims.
- Natural few-shot extension demonstrating benefit of semantic priors.

Cons
- Potential ROC estimation leakage; protocol needs clarification or correction.
- Missing comparisons to additional baselines that account for attribute reliability within non-RF frameworks.
- Limited analysis of scalability and attribute correlation effects.
- Important details (signature uncertainty) pushed to supplement.

Questions for authors
- How exactly is ROC estimated relative to training the final attribute classifiers? Can you report results with ROC computed on a held-out fold not used to train the attribute SVMs?
- How sensitive is performance to the amount of validation data used for ROC estimation?
- Can you report training/testing time and memory as functions of M and K?
- How many candidate thresholds per attribute are sampled, and how are they chosen?

Suggestions
- Add a reliability-aware DAP or weighted-similarity baseline to strengthen the empirical case.
- Provide a short subsection on computational complexity and practical guidance for hyperparameters.
- Move a concise description of the signature-uncertainty model into the main text.
- Consider analyzing learned split usage/feature importance to illustrate the method’s preference for reliable attributes.
- Release code and splits for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a principled and effective way to incorporate attribute unreliability into zero- and few-shot learning with clear improvements over standard baselines. Addressing the ROC estimation protocol and adding one or two stronger baselines would further strengthen the submission.
