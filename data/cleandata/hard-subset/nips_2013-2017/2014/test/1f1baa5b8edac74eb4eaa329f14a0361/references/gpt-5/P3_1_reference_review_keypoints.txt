- Proposes training zero-shot random forests on class-attribute signatures and accounting for attribute unreliability via ROC-aware fractional propagation using node-conditional TPR/FPR estimated from held-out attribute-labeled validation images (DV).
- Fractional propagation yields an information-gain split criterion that prefers attributes and thresholds that are both discriminative and reliably predictable.
- Incorporates signature-attribute association uncertainty and extends naturally to few-shot learning by mixing signature-based and data-based information gains with a parameter λ.
- Empirical results on AwA, aPY, and SUN (>250 attributes) show substantial gains over DAP and a naive signature-only RF, with ablations and controlled-noise diagnostics supporting claims.
- ROC-aware fractional propagation is technically principled and node-specific ROC estimation helps capture some context-dependent attribute error characteristics.
- Few-shot extension is straightforward and empirically reasonable.
- Main limitation: method depends on DV being representative of unseen-class attribute error characteristics; sensitivity to DV size/domain shift is not quantified.
- Potential protocol mismatch: ROCs may be computed from a model trained on 80% DV while final attribute classifiers use 100% — needs clarification or cross-validation to match training and test-time models.
- Training uses fractional propagation but inference uses hard routing (âm(x) > t); soft test-time routing might better align with the training objective and should be evaluated.
- Scalability concerns: one-vs-all forests (J≈100 per unseen class) and per-node DV propagation increase cost as number of classes K grows; runtime/memory analysis and larger-K experiments are missing.
- Method treats attributes marginally at each split despite node-conditional ROC partially capturing dependencies; explicit multivariate modeling or oblique splits could improve robustness.
- Baseline coverage could be broader: compare more extensively to embedding-based ZSL methods (Akata, DeViSE, Mensink) and augmented attribute approaches that address attribute noise.
- Clarity improvements needed: include pseudocode for training/inference in main text, detail threshold sampling (#t and adaptivity), and move signature uncertainty formulation from supplement to main.
- Suggest experiments on sensitivity to DV size/composition, score calibration (Platt scaling), soft test-time routing, and computational cost reporting as functions of M, K, depth, and DV size.
- Originality: novel adaptation of soft/fractional tree routing to ZSL by training on signatures and using DV for node-conditional error modeling.
- Significance: practical, interpretable method that advances attribute-based ZSL and could transfer to other mid-level semantic representations.
- Pros: principled treatment of attribute unreliability, clever node-conditional ROC use, solid ablations, consistent multi-dataset gains, natural few-shot extension.
- Cons: reliance on DV representativeness, unclear training/validation protocol consistency, limited scalability analysis, some methods/details relegated to supplement, narrower baseline comparisons.
- Overall recommendation: Accept (leaning) — clear, novel, useful contribution with strong empirical support but would benefit from the noted clarifications and additional comparisons.
