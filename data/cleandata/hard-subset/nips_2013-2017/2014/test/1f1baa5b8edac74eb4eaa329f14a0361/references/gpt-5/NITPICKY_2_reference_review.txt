Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition via attributes, focusing on the practical problem that attribute classifiers are unreliable. It proposes a random forest that is trained not on images but on class attribute signatures, and explicitly incorporates the attribute classifiers’ ROC curves to account for false positives/negatives when selecting splits. The key idea is fractional propagation of class signatures through the tree according to node-specific TP/FP estimates computed from a held-out attribute-labeled validation set; the information gain is redefined to reflect fractional counts. The method is extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to few-shot learning by interpolating between signature-driven information gain and standard data-driven gain (parameter λ). Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations and a controlled-noise study.

Relation to prior work
This fits the line of attribute-based zero-shot learning (DAP, Farhadi et al., Palatucci et al.), but differs by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during the training of the zero-shot classifier. Related ideas on handling missing/uncertain features in trees (fractional tuples, fuzzy/soft trees) are cited, as are non-attribute zero-shot strategies (hierarchies, label/feature embeddings). The work is orthogonal to attribute-discovery methods; authors claim compatibility and present a result with discovered attributes.

Strengths
- Addresses a real and often ignored issue: attribute unreliability. The ROC-driven split selection and fractional propagation are well-motivated and intuitive.
- Simple, practical, and likely easy to implement; random forests are efficient at test time.
- Ablations (no signature uncertainty; no node-specific ROC propagation) and controlled-noise experiments support the main claims.
- Few-shot extension is natural; the prior-like use of signatures is attractive and seems to help across training set sizes.
- Empirical results show notable gains over DAP on multiple datasets, especially when attributes are weak, aligning with the method’s motivation.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- Lack of theoretical analysis: no guarantees on consistency, bias introduced by ROC-based fractional propagation, or sample complexity effects when DV(n) becomes small. Smoothing/regularization of TP/FP estimates is not described.
- Node-specific ROC estimation can be very noisy with small DV(n). The fallback to global DV when <10 pos/neg contradicts the stated benefit of capturing attribute dependencies at deeper nodes. No principled smoothing (e.g., hierarchical Bayes or shrinkage) is used.
- Calibration: the approach relies on thresholding Platt-scaled scores; no analysis of calibration quality or cross-attribute comparability. The choice/distribution of candidate thresholds (#t) is unspecified and could materially affect results.
- Few-shot objective IGfew mixes signature-based and data-based gains; the exact definition of IGbasic for image data (are features â(x)? what distributions?) is not fully clear. It would help to formalize the training objective and clarify multi-class vs one-vs-all training in the few-shot case.
- The claim “equivalent to building a RF on test samples’ outputs” if validation perfectly represents test is strong; at minimum, clarify assumptions (matching class-conditional attribute distributions and inter-attribute dependencies).
- Scalability: training one forest per class may be fine for K≈10, but the method’s cost and memory footprint with hundreds of unseen classes is not discussed.

Experimental clarity/reproducibility
- Data usage ambiguity: you “reserve 20% of attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data, then pool it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” Do you retrain attributes on 100% only after using the 20% for ROC? Do baselines use the same final attribute classifiers? This needs to be explicit to avoid data leakage concerns.
- Hyperparameters (tree depth, #m, #t per node, λ) are chosen via cross-validation—on what supervision? In zero-shot, how are labels for CV obtained without peeking at unseen classes? Please detail the protocol.
- Evaluation metric: Is accuracy per-class or per-image? This matters for class imbalance (e.g., AwA typically reports per-class accuracy).
- Additional baselines: an informative baseline would be DAP or signature-RF with per-attribute reliability weighting (e.g., learned attribute-wise weights or thresholds set by ROC), and more recent embedding-based zero-shot methods where applicable. The paper compares to published AwA numbers, but comparable baselines for aPY and SUN are thin.
- Controlled-noise study: the noise model is in the supp; please summarize it in the main text to judge realism.

Presentation/formatting
- Several crucial details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, signature uncertainty, noise model), making the main paper hard to fully assess.
- Notation: “Ak ∈ <M” should be R^M; superscripts/subscripts and footnotes are uneven (e.g., “attribute presence1” has missing footnote in the main text).
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (years/pages missing, duplicates; URLs inline). Please clean per conference style.
- Minor typos/wording: “whale seal,” “We use the authors’ code: http://…” should be in a footnote; ensure consistent dataset naming and capitalization.

Pros
- Clear motivation; method directly targets a practical bottleneck.
- Solid empirical gains with ablations; method seems robust in low-attribute-quality regimes.
- Simple, extensible framework; few-shot extension is sensible.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on supplemental material; core components under-specified in the main text.
- No theoretical grounding; potential overfitting/instability of node-specific ROC estimates unaddressed.
- Experimental protocol details (data usage, CV) are ambiguous; missing baselines weaken claims.
- Scalability and computation not analyzed; per-class forests may not scale.

Questions for authors
1) Precisely detail the data flow: how is DV selected, when are attributes retrained on 100%, and do all methods share the same final attribute classifiers?  
2) How are (#t) thresholds sampled per attribute per node? Uniform over [0,1], quantiles of DV(n), or unique score values?  
3) How is IGbasic computed for images in few-shot? Are the inputs â(x) and splits over attributes/thresholds identical to zero-shot?  
4) Do you apply any smoothing for ROC estimates at nodes (e.g., Beta priors)?  
5) Report per-class vs per-image accuracy, and the exact CV protocol without unseen-class leakage.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and likely useful, and the empirical results are promising; however, the paper contains too many underspecified methodological details, relies critically on the supplement, lacks theoretical or stability analysis for its core mechanism, and has several ambiguities in the experimental protocol and data usage. With clearer exposition, stronger baselines, and a more rigorous treatment of ROC estimation and calibration, this work could be much stronger. Confidence: high.
