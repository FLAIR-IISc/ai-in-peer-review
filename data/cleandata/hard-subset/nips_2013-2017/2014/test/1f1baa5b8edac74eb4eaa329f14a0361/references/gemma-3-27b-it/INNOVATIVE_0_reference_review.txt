This paper tackles a critical, and often overlooked, challenge in zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. While zero-shot learning elegantly addresses the problem of classifying novel categories, its performance is fundamentally limited by the quality of the intermediate attribute representations. This work moves beyond simply *using* attributes and instead focuses on explicitly modeling and mitigating the uncertainty associated with their prediction. This is a bold and insightful direction, and a significant departure from most existing literature. The approach, leveraging random forests and incorporating ROC curves to account for prediction errors, is well-motivated and shows promising results.

The core idea of propagating signatures through the random forest proportionally to the attribute classifiers’ confidence—informed by validation data—is particularly clever. It elegantly avoids the pitfalls of treating unreliable attribute predictions as ground truth and proactively builds robustness into the learning process. The extension to few-shot learning, adding a prior weighting based on attribute signatures, is a sensible and natural extension. The experimental validation on three datasets, including comparisons against established baselines like DAP, demonstrates a clear advantage, particularly in scenarios with less reliable attribute predictions.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. While the results are compelling, the experimental setup feels somewhat limited. The choice of datasets and feature representations is standard, which is fine, but more extensive ablation studies could strengthen the analysis. For example, a deeper dive into the impact of varying the amount of validation data, or the sensitivity of the method to different attribute noise levels, would be valuable. The discussion around the choice of hyperparameters (tree depth, number of tests per split) is brief, and more justification for these settings would be helpful. The implementation details, while present, could be expanded, particularly regarding the efficient computation of ROC curves and signature propagation.

Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of using pre-trained attribute classifiers, the potential for jointly learning attributes and the zero-shot model is not explored. This could be a promising avenue for future work. Also, the claim that the method “inherently prefers more learnable attributes” could be more rigorously demonstrated, potentially through analysis of the learned tree structures.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability is a significant contribution, moving beyond the standard assumption of perfect attribute predictions.
*   **Significance:** Addressing attribute reliability is a fundamental bottleneck in zero-shot learning, and this work offers a promising solution.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical motivation for the approach is strong, and the experimental results provide compelling evidence of its effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, clearly articulating the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited experimental analysis:** More extensive ablation studies and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Implementation details could be more comprehensive:** Providing more details regarding the computational aspects would facilitate reproducibility.
*   **Potential for further exploration:** The paper doesn't explore joint learning of attributes and the zero-shot model.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant approach to zero-shot learning. The problem addressed is important, the proposed solution is well-motivated and effective, and the experimental results are promising. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh the limitations. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions addressing the suggested areas for further analysis and detail. The authors have identified a crucial weakness of current zero-shot learning methods and provided a smart and practical solution.
