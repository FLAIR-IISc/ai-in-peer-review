Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition when attribute predictors are unreliable. The core idea is to train a class-specific random forest that operates on class attribute signatures rather than images. During training, the method explicitly models attribute-prediction noise by (i) introducing a held-out attribute-labeled validation set DV to estimate node-specific ROC statistics for each attribute classifier and threshold, and (ii) fractionally propagating class signatures through the decision tree according to those ROC-derived TP/FP/TN/FN probabilities. The information gain is redefined to account for fractional counts. The approach is extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to a few-shot regime by combining signature-based information gain with standard image-based information gain weighted by λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest, plus controlled-noise studies and results with discovered attributes.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned with respect to attribute-based zero-shot methods (DAP and variants) and alternative knowledge-transfer/embedding approaches. It also relates to decision trees with missing/uncertain features and fuzzy trees. The novelty lies in using class signatures as training “instances” and integrating ROC-aware fractional propagation per node, as well as combining this with few-shot training. However, comparisons to newer embedding-based zero-shot baselines (e.g., ALE/SJE/ESZSL/DeViSE) are missing from the main evaluation.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound and intuitive modeling of attribute unreliability; the fractional propagation using node-specific ROC is a principled way to prefer learnable attributes and operating points.
- The extension to few-shot learning via combined information gain is simple and useful.
- Controlled-noise experiments are informative; ablations (no signature uncertainty, no ROC propagation) support the design.

Concerns and weaknesses:
- Potential data leakage in ROC estimation: “Our method reserves 20% of the attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” If DV is used to estimate ROC for the attribute classifiers and also used to train those same classifiers, the ROC is not an unbiased estimate. This could favor the proposed method in split selection and inflate gains. Please clarify the exact chronology: are attribute SVMs trained without DV when computing ROC? If not, re-run with a strictly held-out DV not used for attribute training.
- Calibration and class imbalance: ROC/thresholding depends on well-calibrated Platt scores. Platt scaling on heavily imbalanced attribute data can be brittle. No details on balancing, calibration assessment, or alternative calibration (e.g., isotonic) are provided.
- Threshold selection: Candidate thresholds per attribute are sampled ((#m, #t) per node), but it’s unclear how thresholds are chosen (e.g., quantiles from DV(n), uniform over score ranges). This detail is crucial for reproducibility and performance.
- Statistical and computational stability: Fractional counts I′ at deeper nodes can become very small. There is no mention of smoothing (e.g., Laplace) in entropy calculation. The fallback “<10 positives or negatives” applies to ROC estimation, not I′. Please clarify smoothing/regularization of entropies and the minimal I′ mass to continue splitting.
- Handling TPR < FPR regimes: If an attribute performs worse than random at some thresholds, the method could still select it. Is there any constraint to avoid such operating points (or to flip the inequality)?
- Few-shot λ selection: λ is cross-validated, but the protocol with very few labeled samples can overfit. Please specify the CV scheme, partitions, and variance across runs; a sensitivity plot vs λ is provided, but details on how the chosen λ generalizes are sparse.
- Generalization to continuous signatures and inter-attribute dependencies is deferred to the supplement; the main paper claims dependency handling via recursive DV(n), but splits remain univariate and may not capture strong interactions. Some analysis or examples would help.
- Scope of comparisons: The main table compares to DAP and a signature-RF baseline. While later figures suggest comparisons on AwA with discovered attributes, stronger baselines (e.g., ALE/SJE/DeViSE/ESZSL) are not reported. Given current standards, this weakens the empirical case.

Clarity
Generally clear, but some important implementation details are omitted or deferred:
- Exact threshold sampling strategy, complexity per node, and memory/time costs.
- The contradictory phrasing around “reserving” versus “pooling” DV needs correction.
- Definition of “signature uncertainty” handling is in the supplement; a brief formula would aid understanding.
- Minor typos and inconsistencies: duplicate/ambiguous citations (e.g., [8] appears multiple times), many references lack years/pages; “whale seal” likely “harp seal” or “seal”; inconsistent notation spacing.

Originality
Moderately novel. While fractional propagation and uncertain trees exist, applying ROC-aware fractional propagation to class signatures in zero-shot forests and combining with few-shot IG is a meaningful and original twist.

Significance
The problem is important. Demonstrated improvements over DAP are valuable, especially under noisy attributes. However, without comparisons to stronger contemporary baselines and without removing possible ROC leakage, the strength of the claims is limited.

Pros
- Addresses a real pain point in attribute-based zero-shot learning: unreliable attributes.
- Method is conceptually simple and extensible (few-shot prior).
- Controlled noise studies and ablations support the approach.

Cons
- Possible data leakage undermines the key component (ROC-based training).
- Missing crucial implementation details (thresholding, smoothing, calibration).
- Limited baselines relative to current zero-shot literature.
- Some clarity and formatting issues.

Questions for the authors
1) Is DV used to train the attribute SVMs whose ROC is measured? If yes, please re-run with a disjoint held-out DV and report the impact.  
2) How are thresholds sampled per node? Are they attribute-specific quantiles from DV(n)?  
3) Do you apply any entropy smoothing or minimum-mass criteria on I′ to curb overfitting at deep nodes?  
4) How do you handle attributes whose TPR < FPR at candidate thresholds?  
5) Please report training/inference times and complexity as a function of M, K, J, depth.  
6) Can you add comparisons to ALE/SJE/ESZSL/DeViSE on AwA/aPY/SUN?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the cumulative methodological and reporting concerns (especially ROC leakage and missing stronger baselines) prevent a confident acceptance at this stage. With a clean held-out ROC protocol, added implementation detail, and broader comparisons, this could become a strong submission.

Confidence
High on methodological concerns; medium on empirical significance pending clarified baselines and leakage.
