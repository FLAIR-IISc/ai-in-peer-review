Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition when attribute classifiers are unreliable. The key idea is to train random forests directly on class-level attribute signatures but to explicitly account for attribute prediction errors by using per-attribute ROC estimates measured on attribute-labeled validation images. During training, each class signature is fractionally propagated down the tree according to TP/FP rates of the attribute at a given threshold at that node, yielding an information gain criterion that prefers splits that will be both discriminative and learnable at test time. The method also handles uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining the signature-based information gain with the standard data-based information gain via a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations validating contributions. Controlled noise experiments further support the robustness claims.

Strengths
- Addresses a central practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning—unreliable attribute predictors—head-on, with a clean, intuitive mechanism grounded in ROC analysis.
- Methodologically novel for zero-shot: training a one-vs-all random forest over signatures with fractional propagation driven by validation-set ROC estimates; conceptually related to fuzzy/missing-value trees but adapted to the specific zero-shot setting and the test-time attribute prediction pipeline.
- Accounts for both attribute classifier unreliability and class-signature uncertainty; provides a principled few-shot extension (signature prior for RF split selection).
- Solid empirical evaluation on three datasets with >250 attributes, including ablations (no signature uncertainty, global vs node-specific ROC), controlled noise studies, and comparison with DAP; gains are strongest when attributes are less reliable, matching the method’s intent.
- The node-specific ROC computation using recursively filtered validation sets is a thoughtful design that partially captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Potential leakage/mismatch in ROC estimation: The paper states it “reserves 20% … as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” If DV is used both to train attribute SVMs and to estimate ROC, the ROC may be optimistic. If instead ROC is computed using classifiers trained without DV but test-time uses classifiers retrained on all data, ROC and test-time classifiers are mismatched. Please clarify the exact training sequence and ensure ROC is measured on held-out data from the attribute classifier used at test time (or justify the approximation).
- Scalability: The approach trains a one-vs-all forest per unseen class. Complexity and memory scale with K. With many unseen classes, this could be costly. A single multiclass forest over signatures might be more scalable; a discussion or empirical runtime/memory analysis would help.
- Limited baselines beyond DAP and signature RF in the main tables. While the AwA figure includes discovered-attribute baselines, comparisons on aPY/SUN to label embedding or semantic embedding methods (e.g., DeViSE, ALE/label embedding) would strengthen the case.
- The method models per-attribute error rates but still assumes per-attribute ROC suffices. While the node-specific DV(n) partially captures dependencies, joint attribute correlations beyond the split history are not modeled. This is acknowledged as future work; some analysis of when this approximation breaks (e.g., highly co-linear attributes) would be useful.
- Statistical robustness of node-specific ROC: For deeper nodes, DV(n) can be small; the fallback to global DV mitigates this, but some smoothing/calibration (e.g., Bayesian estimators for rates) could reduce variance. Did you observe instability across runs attributable to ROC variance?
- Calibration and metric: Accuracy is reported, but per-class vs overall accuracy, and confidence calibration of the posterior P(k|â) are not discussed. Given class imbalance in attributes and datasets, per-class accuracy is important.
- Hyperparameters (#m, #t per node, depth, λ): These were cross-validated, but on which data? Since unseen class labels are not available, specify the protocol (e.g., using seen-class held-out) to avoid optimistic selection.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. The fractional-propagation training objective is a coherent approximation to expected test-time discriminativeness under attribute noise. The experiments are carefully executed with multiple datasets, ablations, and a few-shot extension. The main concern is the ROC estimation protocol and potential mismatch/leakage, which could inflate gains; this should be clarified and, if needed, corrected.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The training/testing flow, fractional propagation, and information gain are explained crisply. Important implementation details are deferred to the supplement (signature uncertainty model, continuous attributes, pseudocode), which slightly hinders reproducibility from the main text. The description of the ROC validation data usage needs clarification as noted.

Originality
Moderate to high. While related to fuzzy/missing-value decision trees, applying ROC-driven fractional propagation to zero-shot learning with attribute signatures, and integrating it as a prior for few-shot forests, is novel. The emphasis on modeling attribute unreliability at training time is a valuable and under-explored angle in zero-shot.

Significance
Good practical significance. Attribute unreliability is a key blocker for real zero-shot deployment; the proposed approach offers consistent gains over a widely used baseline (DAP) and is likely to influence subsequent work. The few-shot extension is attractive and pragmatic.

Questions for the authors
- Please detail the exact pipeline for training attribute classifiers and computing ROC on DV. Is DV excluded from training the attribute SVMs used to compute ROC? If not, can you re-run with a true hold-out to quantify any inflation?
- What is the computational cost (training/test time, memory) as a function of K, M, J, depth? Any comparison to DAP on speed?
- How sensitive are results to Platt scaling (calibration) and to the number of thresholds sampled per attribute? Did you try quantile-based thresholds?
- What metric is reported (per-class vs overall accuracy)? Provide both if possible.
- In few-shot, how is λ selected without unseen-class labels? What is the validation protocol?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an impactful problem with a principled, effective solution.
- Strong empirical gains across datasets; insightful controlled-noise analysis.
- Simple, general method that integrates cleanly with existing attribute pipelines and extends to few-shot.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity around ROC estimation/possible leakage; limited baselines on some datasets.
- Scalability to many unseen classes not analyzed; theoretical understanding limited to intuition.

Overall, I lean toward acceptance pending clarification of the ROC protocol and, ideally, an additional experiment with a strict hold-out for ROC to confirm robustness of the reported gains.
