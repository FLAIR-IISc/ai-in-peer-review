Summary
This paper addresses a central challenge in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictors are imperfect and their errors can strongly degrade downstream class inference. The authors propose a random forest framework that trains directly on class attribute signatures (one per unseen class), while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. Key ideas:
- At training time, instead of taking attribute signatures at face value, the method uses validation data to estimate ROC curves per attribute and per node, and fractionally propagates each class signature through both children according to the estimated TPR/FPR at the chosen threshold. This yields a modified information gain criterion that prefers splits that are both discriminative and learnable given expected attribute errors.
- The approach also handles uncertainty in class-attribute associations (signature noise), and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with data-based information gain via a mixing parameter.
- Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN demonstrate improvements over DAP and a signature-only random forest baseline, controlled-noise studies, ablations, and few-shot gains. The method also works with discovered “pseudo-attributes.”

Relation to prior work is well covered: the approach differs from prior ZSL methods (e.g., DAP, label embeddings, hierarchical adaptations) by treating attributes as unreliable and building that into training; it is related to fractional propagation and fuzzy trees but distinct in that training “instances” are class signatures, and the fractional propagation is driven by empirically estimated ROC statistics at each node.

Quality
- Soundness: The fractional propagation mechanism and IGzero are well-motivated. Using node-specific ROC estimates is an important detail to capture conditional dependencies induced by upstream splits. The few-shot extension is straightforward and reasonable.
- Empirical support: Results on three datasets, controlled-noise experiments, ablations (no signature uncertainty, global vs node-specific ROC), and a few-shot study collectively support the claims. The observation that gains are larger when attributes are less reliable is convincing.
- Limitations: No theoretical guarantees (reasonable for a practical method). Some details critical to reproducibility are deferred to the supplement (e.g., exact modeling of signature uncertainty; continuous-signature handling). Runtime/scalability are not reported; one-vs-all forests with J=100 trees per class could be costly for large K. The text has a potential inconsistency regarding whether attribute classifiers are trained on 80% or 100% of the data (the methods section says the 20% validation split is later pooled back, while a later sentence suggests only 80% was used). Please clarify.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The description of ROC-driven propagation and its effect on IG is easy to follow. Suggestions:
  - Move the signature uncertainty model from the supplement into the main text, or summarize it more concretely.
  - Explicitly describe how thresholds t are sampled and how many candidates are considered; add sensitivity to (#m, #t), tree depth, and J.
  - Clarify the earlier point about the training data split for attribute classifiers.
  - Provide complexity or timing to help assess scalability.

Originality
- Novelty lies in training zero-shot forests on signatures with node-specific, ROC-driven fractional propagation to account for attribute unreliability, and in combining that signature prior with few-shot data via dual information gains. This is a non-trivial and useful contribution beyond standard ZSL pipelines and beyond prior fractional/missing-feature trees.

Significance
- Modeling attribute unreliability is practically important; the demonstrated gains over DAP on three datasets indicate the approach substantially improves a widely used ZSL paradigm. The idea is general and could transfer to other “semantic mid-level” settings and domains. Potential impact is moderated by reliance on attribute signatures and attribute-labeled data, and by scalability concerns for very large K.

Strengths
- Simple, principled modification to tree training that directly addresses a known pain point in attribute-based ZSL.
- Careful design (node-specific ROC, fractional propagation) and ablations that illuminate where gains come from.
- Strong empirical results across datasets, plus few-shot gains.
- Works with both named and discovered attributes.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Missing details in main text (signature uncertainty; continuous signatures).
- Scalability and runtime not discussed; OVA forests may be expensive.
- Comparisons: While DAP is a strong and appropriate baseline, results against embedding-based methods that can use attribute signatures (e.g., ALE/label embeddings, DeViSE) would strengthen the case.
- Potential domain shift: ROC estimated on seen classes may not match unseen classes; authors’ “true ROC” upper bound highlights this gap. More analysis of robustness to this shift would be valuable.
- Minor inconsistency regarding attribute training data fraction.

Suggestions for improvement and questions
- Add comparisons to label embedding approaches that can ingest class-attribute vectors.
- Report training/inference time and memory, and discuss scalability for large K and M.
- Provide sensitivity to the amount of validation data used to estimate ROC and to λ in few-shot beyond the plotted cases.
- Consider a single multi-class forest rather than OVA, or justify OVA empirically.
- Explore simple ways to mitigate seen→unseen ROC shift (e.g., weighting validation data by signature similarity; calibration techniques).
- Clarify the discrepancy about using 80% vs 100% of attribute-labeled data to train attribute classifiers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-explored failure mode in ZSL with a principled, effective method.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains with solid ablations.
- Clear idea that is likely to be used and extended by others (e.g., to other mid-level semantics).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited set of baselines; missing some prominent embedding-based ZSL comparisons.
- Some implementation details deferred; no runtime/scalability analysis.
- Dependence on attribute-labeled data and curated signatures may limit applicability in settings without such supervision.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a neat and impactful solution to a real problem in attribute-based ZSL, with convincing results and a method that is both conceptually clean and practically useful. Addressing the clarity and comparison gaps would further strengthen it.
