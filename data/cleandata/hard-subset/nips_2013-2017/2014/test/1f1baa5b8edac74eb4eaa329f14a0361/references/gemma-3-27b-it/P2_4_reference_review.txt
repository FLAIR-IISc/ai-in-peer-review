## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical challenge of attribute-based zero-shot learning, acknowledging the inherent unreliability of attribute predictors. The authors propose a novel random forest approach that incorporates attribute prediction uncertainty during training, specifically leveraging ROC curves to guide split selection and account for signature uncertainty. They extend this to a few-shot setting, demonstrating improvements over existing methods on three datasets. The core idea is well-motivated, building upon the established DAP framework but crucially addressing its limitation of assuming perfect attribute predictions.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Limitation:** The paper directly tackles a major practical problem in attribute-based ZSL: the inaccuracy of attribute classifiers. This is a significant and well-recognized weakness of existing approaches.
* **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The proposed method, utilizing ROC curves and fractional signature propagation, is a clever way to incorporate attribute uncertainty into the learning process. The connection to related work on handling missing/uncertain data (fractional tuples, etc.) is clearly articulated.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines (DAP, Signature RF) on multiple datasets. The controlled experiments with injected noise convincingly illustrate the benefits of the proposed uncertainty modeling.  The few-shot extension is also a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and experimental setup.  The authors provide sufficient detail to understand and potentially reproduce the results.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate the method across three datasets, with ablation studies exploring the contributions of different components (ROC propagation, signature uncertainty). They also compare against a strong baseline (DAP) and consider both named and discovered attributes.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, incorporating ROC analysis and fractional signature propagation likely increases the computational cost compared to simpler methods like DAP.  A discussion of the computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters (e.g., λ for few-shot learning, tree depth, sampling parameters for split selection).  The sensitivity of the results to these parameters and the strategies used for their selection could be further elaborated.
* **Validation Data Requirement:** The approach requires a separate validation set to estimate ROC curves.  While reasonable, the impact of the size and representativeness of this validation set on performance could be further investigated.
* **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the controlled noise experiments are informative, a more detailed error analysis of the results on the real datasets could provide further insights into the types of errors the method still makes and potential directions for improvement.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The method appears complete and the authors are careful about evaluating both strengths and weaknesses. The use of ROC curves and fractional signature propagation represents a significant technical contribution. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The explanations of the method and experiments are easy to follow.  **(Strong)**
* **Originality:** The core idea of incorporating attribute uncertainty into the ZSL training process is novel and represents a significant advancement over existing methods. The specific implementation using ROC curves and fractional signatures is also original. **(Strong)**
* **Significance:**  The results are important and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods. The ability to effectively handle unreliable attributes is a crucial step towards making attribute-based ZSL more practical. This work is likely to be used by other researchers in the field and could potentially influence the development of more robust ZSL systems. **(Strong)**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant limitation of existing ZSL methods.
* Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Clearly written and well-organized.
* Significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential computational cost concerns.
* Parameter sensitivity could be an issue.
* Requirement for validation data.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The authors effectively address a critical limitation of existing methods and propose a novel and well-supported solution. The empirical results are compelling and the paper is clearly written and organized.  While there are minor areas for improvement (e.g., computational cost discussion, parameter sensitivity analysis), these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept**
