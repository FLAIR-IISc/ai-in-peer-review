Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a core limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): mid-level attribute classifiers are often unreliable, yet most ZSL methods (e.g., DAP and signature-matching approaches) treat attribute predictions as if they were accurate. The authors propose a random forest framework trained not on images but on class-attribute signatures. Crucially, they incorporate attribute unreliability by: (1) estimating per-attribute ROC statistics on held-out attribute-labeled data; (2) fractionally propagating class signatures through candidate splits according to node-specific TP/FP rates; and (3) redefining information gain to reflect these fractional memberships. They also model uncertainty in the class-attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”), and extend the framework to a few-shot setting by combining signature-based and data-based information gains with a weighting parameter λ.

The work situates well in ZSL literature: it contrasts with DAP and signature-similarity methods [3,8,18,30], knowledge- and label-embedding approaches [1,4,13,14,23], and methods that learn discriminative mid-level features rather than semantic attributes [11,15,22,26,27,30]. It also relates to decision trees with missing/uncertain features [16,21,28] but adapts them to the ZSL regime where training instances are class signatures and test-time inputs are attribute predictions. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show clear gains over DAP and ablations, with further evidence from controlled noise studies and a few-shot extension.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated contribution: explicitly modeling attribute unreliability at training time—via node-specific ROC-aware fractional propagation—addresses a widely acknowledged bottleneck in attribute-based ZSL.
- Principled and intuitive: the information gain becomes zero if an attribute is unlearnable (TPR=FPR); node-specific DV(n) captures dependencies introduced by earlier splits.
- Practical extensions: signature uncertainty and a few-shot formulation that uses signatures as a prior (λ-weighted IG) are natural and useful.
- Solid empirical validation: three datasets, controlled-noise analysis, ablations (no signature uncertainty; no node-wise ROC propagation), and results with discovered attributes. Improvements over DAP are consistent and larger when attributes are noisier (aPY, SUN), aligning with the paper’s premise.
- Clarity: the main ideas are clearly presented; training/testing protocols are described and sensitivity to λ is discussed.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data: the method assumes ROC estimated on “seen” classes generalizes to “unseen” classes and to unseen attribute combinations. While acknowledged, it remains a potential domain-shift failure mode. The “upper bound” experiment (using true unseen-class ROC) suggests room for better generalization strategies.
- Scalability: training one forest per unseen class (one-vs-all) could be costly for large K; test-time complexity scales with K×J. A multi-class/multi-label forest or shared-tree variant might be more scalable.
- Baselines: comparisons focus on DAP and signature-RF. Stronger baselines that also downweight unreliable attributes (e.g., reliability-weighted matching, calibrated-threshold variants of DAP) or label-embedding methods (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) are not fully reported in the main results. Given the related work cited, numerical comparisons would strengthen claims.
- ROC estimation/training pipeline clarity: the paper reserves 20% DV to estimate ROC, then pools DV with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. This creates a potential mismatch: ROC used to build forests is derived from classifiers trained on a subset, while test-time predictions come from classifiers trained on all data. Clarify whether ROC is recomputed with cross-validation or held fixed; assess impact.
- Sensitivity and robustness: while cross-validation for depths and λ is used, sensitivity to (#m, #t), DV size, and the fallback when DV(n) is small (<10 positives/negatives) is not explored. Some guidance on stability would be helpful.
- Inter-attribute correlations: partially addressed via node-specific DV(n), but no explicit modeling. This could be problematic when attributes co-occur strongly or are mutually exclusive.

Quality
- Methodologically sound, with a coherent objective and careful handling of uncertainty. The information gain derivation and the extreme-case behavior (TPR=FPR) are correct. Few-shot extension is reasonable.
- Experimental design is appropriate: three datasets, ablations, and noise studies support the claims. More comprehensive baselines and scalability analysis would further bolster quality.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized; the core ideas are easy to follow. Minor places to clarify: training/validation pipeline for ROC vs final attribute models; the exact few-shot baseline used; details for continuous signatures (deferred to supp.).

Originality
- The key novelty is training forests on class signatures using node-specific ROC-aware fractional propagation. While related to decision trees for uncertain features, the adaptation to zero-shot settings and the dual propagation of signatures and validation data are original and insightful.

Significance
- Addresses a central pain point in attribute-based ZSL and shows convincing empirical impact, suggesting that attributes remain viable for zero-/few-shot learning when unreliability is explicitly modeled. Potentially influential for other mid-level concept pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines that explicitly calibrate or weight attributes by reliability (e.g., per-attribute weights or thresholds tuned on DV) and representative label-embedding methods (ALE, DeViSE) on at least AwA.
- Provide a multi-class/shared forest variant and report computational complexity vs K.
- Clarify and, if possible, align ROC estimation with the final attribute classifiers (e.g., nested CV or calibration transfer).
- Analyze sensitivity to DV size/choice and to hyperparameters (#m, #t, depth); report runtime and memory costs.
- Explore simple correlation-aware tests (e.g., conjunctions of attributes) or oblique splits to better handle co-dependencies.
- Release code/splits; for SUN, fix unseen class splits for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled treatment of attribute unreliability.
- Strong and consistent empirical gains with informative ablations.
- Practical extension to few-shot learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing comparisons to some strong contemporary ZSL baselines.
- Scalability and pipeline clarity issues need addressing.
- Generalization of ROC from seen to unseen remains an open risk.

Recommendation: Accept (clear contribution with solid evidence; suggested additions would further strengthen the paper but are not strictly required).

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high.
