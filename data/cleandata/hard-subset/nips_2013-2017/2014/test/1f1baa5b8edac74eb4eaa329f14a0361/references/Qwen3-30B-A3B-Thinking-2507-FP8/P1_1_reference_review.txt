

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning: the assumption that visual attribute predictions are perfect. The authors recognize that while attribute-based zero-shot learning offers an elegant framework for recognizing novel categories without labeled training examples, the practical performance is severely hampered by the unreliability of attribute classifiers. They propose a novel approach that explicitly accounts for this unreliability during training by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of the attribute classifiers.

The core contribution is a zero-shot random forest method that builds on attribute signatures (rather than image features) to train classifiers for unseen classes. The key innovation is a modified training procedure that propagates training signatures through the tree according to the ROC characteristics of each attribute classifier, rather than assuming perfect predictions. The authors further extend their approach to account for uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to handle the few-shot setting where a small number of labeled examples are available.

The paper is evaluated on three standard zero-shot learning datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), and the proposed method demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches, particularly in scenarios where attribute classifiers are less reliable. The results are compelling, showing that modeling attribute classifier unreliability is more important than having more attribute training data.

This work relates to previous zero-shot learning methods like DAP [8] and SIGNATURE RF [3], but makes a key distinction by explicitly accounting for the unreliability of attribute predictions rather than assuming they are perfect.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Contribution:** The paper identifies and addresses a fundamental limitation of current zero-shot learning approaches that has been largely ignored in the literature. The authors demonstrate that the unreliability of attribute predictions can severely degrade performance, and their method provides a principled solution to this problem.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The approach is well-motivated and technically rigorous. The extension to account for attribute unreliability through ROC characteristics is elegant and well-justified. The information gain formulation (equation 5) provides a clear theoretical basis for the modified tree construction.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough and well-designed, including both controlled noise experiments and real-world experiments on three challenging datasets. The ablation studies (Table 1) clearly isolate the contributions of the different components of the proposed method.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, and the supplementary material appears to contain additional helpful details. The key ideas are presented in a logical flow that builds from the basic concept to the more sophisticated extensions.

5. **Strong Results:** The results are impressive, with consistent improvements over existing methods across multiple datasets. The performance gains are particularly significant on datasets where attribute classifiers are less reliable (aPY and SUN), which is exactly where the method is designed to help. The comparison to DAP (Table 1) is particularly convincing, showing that modeling attribute unreliability is more important than having more attribute training data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on having a separate validation set to estimate the ROC characteristics of attribute classifiers. While the paper reserves 20% of attribute-labeled images for this purpose, it doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the method is to the choice of validation data. In practice, the validation set must be representative of the test data, which may be challenging in some applications. The authors could strengthen their case by analyzing this sensitivity.

2. **Limited Analysis of Attribute Reliability:** While the paper acknowledges that attribute classifiers are unreliable, it doesn't deeply analyze why certain attributes are more reliable than others. A deeper analysis of which attributes are most reliable (e.g., based on their visual distinctiveness or correlation with class categories) would provide additional insights into the limitations and potential of the approach.

3. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't address how the method scales to very large numbers of attributes or unseen classes. With thousands of attributes and hundreds of unseen classes, the computational complexity of the proposed method might become prohibitive. The authors could provide some discussion or preliminary experiments on scalability.

4. **Comparison to Other Uncertainty-Aware Methods:** While the paper compares to DAP and SIGNATURE RF, it doesn't compare to other methods that have attempted to handle unreliable attributes, such as those that use uncertainty estimates from the attribute classifiers (e.g., [11, 22]). Including such comparisons would further strengthen the paper's claim that their approach is the most effective for handling attribute unreliability.

5. **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is well-supported by experiments but lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the guarantees provided by the proposed method. While not strictly necessary, some theoretical bounds on how much the method improves over existing approaches would be valuable.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims backed by thorough experimental evaluation. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, as evidenced by the controlled noise experiments and ablation studies. The method is presented as a complete piece of work, not just a position paper. The experimental setup is standard and appropriate for the problem domain, and the results are statistically significant.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The key ideas are presented in a logical flow that builds from the basic concept to the more sophisticated extensions. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would be helpful for reproduction. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, and the figures effectively illustrate the key points.

## Originality

This work is novel in its explicit modeling of attribute prediction unreliability during zero-shot learning. While random forests have been used in zero-shot learning before, the specific way the authors incorporate ROC characteristics to account for attribute unreliability is a significant contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly DAP, which assumes perfect attribute predictions. The authors also make a clear connection to related work on handling missing values in decision trees [21, 28], but extend it to the zero-shot setting with important distinctions.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. Zero-shot learning has the potential to be extremely useful in real-world applications where new categories emerge dynamically (e.g., scientific discovery), but the practical limitations due to unreliable attribute predictions have hindered its adoption. This paper provides a practical solution to this problem, and the results demonstrate that the method can significantly outperform existing approaches. The authors also show that their method can be extended to the few-shot setting, further increasing its applicability. The approach is general enough to be applied to other domains that use semantic mid-level concepts for higher-level tasks.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work addresses a significant practical limitation of zero-shot learning, presents a technically sound solution, and provides strong experimental evidence of its effectiveness. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The results are compelling, and the method has clear potential for practical application.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Addresses a critical, previously overlooked limitation of zero-shot learning
- Provides a principled method to account for attribute prediction unreliability
- Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods across multiple datasets
- Well-written and well-organized
- Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies
- Clear connection to related work and theoretical foundation

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Limited analysis of why certain attributes are more reliable than others
- Validation data dependency could be a concern in some applications
- Scalability to very large numbers of attributes/classes is not addressed
- Some comparison to other methods that handle unreliable attributes could be added

## Final Recommendation

Accept. This is a strong contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation in zero-shot learning and provides a practical solution with significant empirical validation. The work is technically sound, well-presented, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.
