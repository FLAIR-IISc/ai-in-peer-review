This paper addresses the challenging problem of zero-shot learning, specifically tackling the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea – to incorporate attribute prediction uncertainty into the training of a random forest classifier – is a valuable contribution. The authors convincingly argue that existing methods treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is often unrealistic. The proposed approach, which modifies the random forest training process to account for ROC characteristics and signature uncertainty, demonstrates improved performance on multiple datasets, outperforming the widely-used DAP baseline. The extension to the few-shot setting is also a nice addition and logically follows from the core idea.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical limitation of existing ZSL methods:** The paper directly confronts the issue of unreliable attributes, a well-known weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   **Novel and well-motivated approach:** The use of ROC curves and fractional propagation of training signatures is a clever way to incorporate uncertainty into the learning process.
*   **Strong experimental validation:** The paper presents comprehensive experiments on three datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines, including DAP. The controlled noise experiments are particularly insightful, validating the core premise of the work.
*   **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The approach is explained in a clear and logical manner.
*   **Extension to Few-Shot Learning:** Extending the method to the few-shot setting is a natural and valuable extension, demonstrating the versatility of the proposed approach.
*   **Comparison with discovered attributes:** Showing results using discovered attributes demonstrates applicability beyond hand-crafted attributes.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, the process of computing ROC curves for each attribute at each node of the random forest could be computationally expensive, especially for datasets with a large number of attributes. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions cross-validation for selecting λ in the few-shot setting, but doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of other hyperparameters (e.g., tree depth, number of trees) to performance. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the results.
*   **Validation Data Requirement:** While the use of validation data to estimate ROC curves is a clever idea, it requires reserving a portion of the attribute-labeled data. This could be problematic in scenarios where labeled data is scarce. Exploring alternative methods for estimating ROC curves (e.g., using prior knowledge or regularization) could be valuable.
*   **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases. Understanding *why* the method fails in certain scenarios could provide valuable insights for future work.
*   **Connection to Existing Work on Uncertainty:** While the paper cites related work on handling missing values and probabilistic features, it could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the connection to broader research on uncertainty quantification and robust learning. For example, exploring connections to conformal prediction or Bayesian methods could be fruitful.
*   **Ablation study detail:** While ablation studies are present, the impact of each modification isn’t always clearly articulated in the text.  More explicit statements tying the changes to performance would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in zero-shot learning with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The experimental results are strong and consistent, demonstrating clear improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The extension to few-shot learning adds to the value of the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the approach is not fully addressed.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is limited.
*   The reliance on validation data could be a limitation in some scenarios.
*   The analysis of failure cases is lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and failure case analysis. Specifically, including a section detailing the computational complexity of the approach and a more in-depth discussion of the hyperparameter selection process would significantly strengthen the paper.  Adding a short qualitative analysis of failure cases would also be valuable.
