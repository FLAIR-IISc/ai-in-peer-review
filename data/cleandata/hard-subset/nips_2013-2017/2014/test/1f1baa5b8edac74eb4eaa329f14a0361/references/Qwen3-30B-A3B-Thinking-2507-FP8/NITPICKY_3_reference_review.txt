

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to improve zero-shot learning by explicitly accounting for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors develop a random forest algorithm that uses attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristics to select discriminative decision nodes, and extend the approach to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), with results showing improvements over baseline methods like DAP and SIGNATURE RF.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important practical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea of incorporating ROC curves into the tree-building process is novel and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both controlled noise experiments and real-world attribute predictions. The method shows consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in scenarios where attribute classifiers are less reliable.

## Weaknesses and Minor Flaws

### Methodological Details
1. **Threshold Confusion**: In Section 3.2.2, the paper states "With binary Ai(m), all 0 < t < 1 are equivalent in Sec 3.2.1." This is factually incorrect. For binary attributes, the threshold t matters significantly in how the attribute is split (e.g., t=0.5 vs t=0.9 would produce different splits). The paper later acknowledges this in Section 3.2.2, but the initial statement creates confusion.

2. **Fractional Propagation Logic**: The paper defines I'_r(k) = I'_n(k) * P_n(âm(x) > t | y(m) = Ak(m)), but the notation is inconsistent. The conditional probability should be based on the validation data at node n, not on the specific class k's attribute value. The notation suggests a dependency on k that doesn't exist in the validation data.

3. **Validation Data Representation**: The paper states "we reserve 20% of the attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data," but doesn't clarify how this validation set is distributed across attributes or whether it's representative of the unseen classes (which are not available during training). This is a critical detail for reproducibility.

4. **Missing Parameter Details**: The paper mentions "tree depths of (AwA-9, aPY-6, SUN-8)" and " (#m,#t) tests per node," but doesn't specify how these parameters were chosen (e.g., via cross-validation). This is important for understanding the experimental setup.

### Technical Nuances
1. **Class vs. Category Terminology**: The paper inconsistently uses "class" and "category" to refer to the same concept (e.g., "zebra" is referred to as both a class and a category). This creates confusion in the method description.

2. **Reference Inconsistency**: The paper cites "the unseen class splits specified in [9]" for AwA and aPY, but [9] (Lampert et al., "Attribute-based classification for zero-shot visual object categorization") is not properly introduced in the text. The paper should explicitly state what [9] refers to.

3. **Missing Code Link**: The paper states "We use the authors' code: http://attributes.kyb.tuebingen.mpg.de/" but this link is not provided in the paper itself, making reproducibility difficult.

### Formatting and Clarity
1. **Typo in Abstract**: The abstract states "attribute signatures" but later refers to "attributed signatures" (Section 1), creating a minor inconsistency.

2. **Reference Format**: The reference "Label-Embedding for Attribute-Based Classification, Z. Akata, F. Perronnin, Z. Harchaoui, C. Schmid, CVPR, ," has a missing year, which is unprofessional.

3. **Table Presentation**: Table 1 reports mean accuracy but doesn't provide standard deviations for all results, making it difficult to assess statistical significance.

4. **Confusing Statement**: In Section 1, the paper states "Supposing the system can predict the presence of any such attribute in novel images, then adding a new category model amounts to defining its attribute 'signature'." This contradicts their main premise that attribute predictions are unreliable, creating a logical inconsistency.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated approach to a meaningful problem in zero-shot learning. While the core idea is novel and the experimental results are promising, several methodological inconsistencies and minor technical flaws undermine the paper's rigor. The threshold confusion in Section 3.2.2 is particularly concerning as it suggests a lack of precision in the technical description. The inconsistent use of "class" and "category" terminology further reduces clarity.

The improvements over baselines are modest (typically 2-10% accuracy gains), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method fundamentally advances the state of the art beyond incremental improvements. While the approach is interesting, the technical inconsistencies and lack of precision in describing the method make it difficult to evaluate the contribution's true significance.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper addresses an important problem and has a novel idea, the methodological inconsistencies and technical inaccuracies (particularly regarding threshold handling and validation data representation) are significant enough to warrant rejection. The paper would benefit from a thorough technical revision to resolve these inconsistencies before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. The minor flaws are substantial enough to overshadow the paper's potential contribution.
