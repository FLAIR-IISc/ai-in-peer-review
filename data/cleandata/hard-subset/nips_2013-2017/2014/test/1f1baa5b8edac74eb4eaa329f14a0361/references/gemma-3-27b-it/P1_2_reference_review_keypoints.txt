- Problem: Zero-shot learning assumes perfect attribute prediction; this paper tackles attribute prediction unreliability.  
- Method: Proposes a random forest that models attribute unreliability by fractionally propagating training instances using attribute classifier ROC curves.  
- Extension: Method naturally extends to few-shot learning by combining attribute signatures with limited labeled data.  
- Evaluation: Tested on AwA, aPY, and SUN datasets; shows consistent improvements over baselines (notably DAP).  
- Ablations: Ablation studies isolate the contributions of different components and validate the approach.  
- Novelty: First to explicitly model attribute classifier unreliability in ZSL using ROC-based fractional propagation.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on attribute-based ZSL and uncertainty-handling in trees, while keeping interpretable attribute descriptions versus alternative ZSL strategies.  
- Strength — real-world relevance: Directly addresses a critical, practical limitation of existing ZSL methods.  
- Strength — technical idea: ROC-based softening of attribute signatures is well-motivated and leverages error modes of classifiers.  
- Strength — empirical support: Strong empirical results across multiple datasets and clear ablation evidence.  
- Strength — versatility: Effective extension to few-shot scenarios.  
- Strength — clarity: Well-written, organized, and includes pseudocode/supporting details for reproducibility.  
- Weakness — computational cost: Validation propagation and ROC computation add overhead and may not scale to large attribute vocabularies/datasets.  
- Weakness — validation dependence: Performance depends on the quality and representativeness of validation data used to estimate ROC curves.  
- Weakness — limited failure analysis: Lacks detailed analysis of real-world failure cases and which attributes cause problems.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter treatment: No comprehensive sensitivity analysis or strong justification for chosen hyperparameters.  
- Pro arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important issue, offers a novel solution, strong empirical results, useful few-shot extension, and clear presentation.  
- Con arguments against acceptance: Potential scalability issues, reliance on validation data quality, and limited failure-case analysis.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution to ZSL; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: Authors should address scalability concerns, provide deeper failure-case analysis, and discuss hyperparameter sensitivity.
