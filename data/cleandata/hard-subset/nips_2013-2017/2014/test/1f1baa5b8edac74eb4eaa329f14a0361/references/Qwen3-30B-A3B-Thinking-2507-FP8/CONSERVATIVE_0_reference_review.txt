

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a genuine limitation in zero-shot learning: the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors propose a random forest approach that accounts for the error characteristics of attribute classifiers (via ROC curves) during training, rather than assuming perfect attribute predictions. The method extends to handling signature uncertainty and few-shot learning. The paper is well-written, with clear problem formulation, methodological details, and thorough experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN).

**Strengths:**
The problem is well-motivated, as attribute-based zero-shot learning has indeed been limited by unreliable attribute predictions. The method is theoretically sound, using ROC characteristics to propagate uncertainty through the random forest rather than treating attribute predictions as perfect. The experimental results are convincing: the method consistently outperforms DAP (a leading baseline) by 5-10% across datasets, with particularly strong gains on SUN and aPY where attribute classifiers are less reliable. The few-shot extension is a practical contribution that shows clear benefits even with modest amounts of labeled data.

**Weaknesses:**
While the paper addresses a real problem, the approach remains fundamentally limited by the same fundamental issue it aims to mitigate: the unreliability of attribute predictions themselves. The authors acknowledge this in the introduction ("attribute-based zero-shot recognition remains in the 'proof of concept' realm"), yet the paper does not demonstrate how their method overcomes the core problem of attribute learning difficulty. The improvement over DAP is significant, but DAP itself has been shown to be inferior to more recent approaches (e.g., [23, 14, 1]) that bypass attributes entirely by leveraging semantic relationships between classes. The paper does not compare against these stronger baselines, which weakens the claim that this is a meaningful advancement.

The method's dependency on having good attribute classifiers is not adequately addressed. The paper reserves 20% of attribute-labeled data for ROC validation, but this validation data comes from "seen" classes, not the unseen classes for which the method is intended. This creates a mismatch: the error statistics are estimated on seen classes, but the method is applied to unseen classes. The authors acknowledge this limitation but provide no convincing evidence that the error statistics from seen classes generalize to unseen classes (the "upper bound" result using unseen class images is hypothetical, as the authors cannot access these images during training).

Furthermore, the paper does not sufficiently justify why attribute-based approaches remain relevant in the face of stronger non-attribute-based methods. The authors mention that their method can work with "discovered (pseudo)attributes," but this is presented as an afterthought rather than a fundamental shift. The paper's focus on improving an inherently limited approach (attribute-based zero-shot learning) seems misguided given the field's direction toward more robust methods.

**Critical Evaluation:**
Quality: The paper is technically sound and experiments are well-designed, but the method does not address the root problem of attribute unreliability—it merely mitigates its effects. The paper lacks theoretical analysis of why this approach works better than alternatives, relying solely on empirical results.

Originality: The idea of accounting for attribute unreliability is novel, but it's an incremental improvement to an approach that has already been shown to be suboptimal. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on handling uncertainty in classification (e.g., [21, 28]).

Significance: The method provides a modest improvement over a specific baseline (DAP), but this is not sufficient to justify its significance given the existence of stronger zero-shot methods. The paper doesn't demonstrate that attribute-based zero-shot learning is a viable path forward, which is a key limitation.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to a research direction that has already been shown to have fundamental limitations. While the method is well-executed and shows consistent improvements over a specific baseline, it does not advance the field in a meaningful way. The paper would be stronger if it addressed why attribute-based approaches remain relevant compared to more successful methods that bypass attributes entirely. As it stands, the contribution appears to be a refinement of an approach that has already been superseded by more effective techniques.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly strengthened by (1) comparing against more recent zero-shot methods that do not rely on attributes, (2) providing evidence that the ROC statistics from seen classes generalize to unseen classes, and (3) demonstrating why attribute-based approaches remain a viable path forward despite the existence of stronger alternatives. Without these, the paper does not make a compelling case for the significance of its contribution.
