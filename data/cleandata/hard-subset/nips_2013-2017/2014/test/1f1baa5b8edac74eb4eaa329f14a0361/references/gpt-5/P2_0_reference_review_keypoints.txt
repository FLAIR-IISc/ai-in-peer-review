- Proposes zero-shot recognition via random forests trained on class attribute signatures with node-wise fractional propagation based on attribute classifier ROC (TPR/FPR).
- Fractional propagation biases splits toward attributes that are both discriminative and reliably predictable using node-specific ROC statistics from validation data.
- Extends the method to handle class-attribute association uncertainty and to few-shot learning by combining signature-driven and data-driven information gain.
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN with controlled-noise studies and ablations, showing substantial gains over DAP and a signature-only forest.
- Connects to prior attribute-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DAP) but uniquely integrates attribute reliability into the learning objective.
- Relates to decision trees handling uncertain/missing values (fractional/fuzzy trees) but adapts them to signature-based zero-shot training.
- Training objective and fractional propagation using ROC-derived probabilities are principled and technically sound.
- Node-specific ROC estimation using recursively filtered validation data captures inter-attribute dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Empirical support includes multiple datasets, controlled-noise experiments, ablations removing signature uncertainty and node-wise ROC propagation, and sensible few-shot behavior.
- Concern: assumes ROC statistics transfer from seen to unseen classes; sensitivity to validation composition and size isn’t fully quantified.
- Concern: potential optimism/circularity if validation data used for ROC estimation is pooled back to train final attribute classifiers; pipeline clarity needed.
- Concern: baseline set is limited (missing recent embedding methods like ALE or DeViSE), weakening claims of state-of-the-art.
- Concern: scalability and runtime/memory cost of training a one-vs-all forest per unseen class are not analyzed.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; inclusion of a concise algorithm box and clarification of threshold sampling (#m,#t) and stopping criteria would improve reproducibility.
- The core idea of ROC-driven fractional propagation in zero-shot random forests is novel and non-trivial for the domain.
- The method addresses a practical weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning (attribute noise) and has likely practical impact.
- Pros: principled treatment of attribute unreliability, strong empirical gains with ablations, models inter-attribute dependencies, and natural few-shot extension.
- Cons: relies on transferability of ROC stats, some ambiguity in validation usage, limited baseline coverage, and unclear scaling behavior.
- Suggestions: clarify attribute SVM/ROC training pipeline, add comparisons to embedding-based methods, provide runtime/memory scaling results, and test ROC robustness or use Bayesian smoothing.
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, novel, empirically effective, and well supported by ablations and analysis.
