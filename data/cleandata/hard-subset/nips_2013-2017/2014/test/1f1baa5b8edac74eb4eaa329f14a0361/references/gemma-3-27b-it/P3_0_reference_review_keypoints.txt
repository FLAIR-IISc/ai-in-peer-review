- Problem: Attribute-based zero-shot learning suffers because attribute predictors are unreliable yet often treated as ground truth.  
- Proposed method: A random forest approach that explicitly models attribute uncertainty during training using ROC curves to select discriminative, predictable splits.  
- Generalization: Method extended to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and to the few-shot setting.  
- Empirical results: Significant improvements over state-of-the-art across three datasets, with ablation studies validating component contributions.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on attribute-based ZSL literature; novelty lies in modeling attribute uncertainty and ROC-informed RF splits.  
- Comparison to alternatives: Contrasts with embedding and transfer methods, emphasizing interpretability and control of attribute-based approaches.  
- Connection to related methods: Acknowledges prior work on missing/uncertain data in decision trees but highlights distinct ZSL challenges and required adaptations.  
- Strength — practical impact: Addresses a major practical bottleneck, improving real-world applicability of attribute-based ZSL.  
- Strength — methodological novelty: ROC-based training that biases learning toward reliable attributes is innovative and intuitive.  
- Strength — empirical validation: Strong, consistent gains and robust controlled experiments (including injected noise).  
- Strength — presentation: Clear, comprehensive exposition and useful controlled experiments.  
- Strength — versatility: Few-shot extension and cognitive-science framing add conceptual and practical value.  
- Weakness — computation: ROC-informed training likely increases computational cost; no detailed complexity analysis provided.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: Performance appears sensitive to tree depth and tests per node; sensitivity analysis is limited.  
- Weakness — validation dependence: Relies on representative validation data for ROC estimation; impact of validation set size/composition not fully explored.  
- Weakness — failure analysis: Limited examination of remaining failure cases and error types.  
- For acceptance: Significant problem addressed, novel well-motivated method, strong empirical results, and clear writing argue for acceptance.  
- Against acceptance: Concerns about computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and limited failure analysis argue for caution.  
- Overall assessment: Strong paper, valuable contribution, recommended for acceptance (8/10).  
- Recommendations: Add computational complexity discussion, thorough hyperparameter sensitivity study, deeper failure-case analysis, and note adaptability to other mid-level representations.
