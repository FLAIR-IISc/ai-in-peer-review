- Summary: Addresses unreliable attribute predictions in zero-shot learning by using attribute signatures and a random forest that incorporates each attribute classifier's ROC; extends to uncertainty in class-attribute associations and few-shot, with strong evaluations on AwA, aPY, SUN.

- Strength: Identifies and addresses a fundamental practical limitation of zero-shot learning (unreliable attribute predictions).

- Strength: Technically sound and well-motivated; incorporates ROC-based unreliability via an information-gain formulation for tree construction.

- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation including controlled noise experiments, three datasets, and ablation studies isolating contributions.

- Strength: Clear presentation and reproducibility, with helpful supplementary material.

- Strength: Strong empirical results, especially on datasets with less reliable attributes, demonstrating gains over prior methods like DAP.

- Weakness: Method depends on a representative validation set to estimate attribute ROC; sensitivity to this choice is not thoroughly explored.

- Weakness: Limited analysis of why some attributes are more reliable than others and what drives attribute reliability.

- Weakness: Scalability concerns unaddressed — potential computational issues with many attributes or many unseen classes.

- Weakness: Lacks comparisons to other uncertainty-aware methods that handle unreliable attributes (e.g., methods using classifier uncertainty estimates).

- Weakness: Missing deeper theoretical analysis or bounds quantifying the method's improvements.

- Quality: Technically sound with thorough, statistically significant experimental support and a complete method beyond a position paper.

- Clarity: Well-written, well-organized, consistent notation, and sufficient detail for reproduction.

- Originality: Novel explicit modeling of attribute prediction unreliability using ROC within a zero-shot random forest framework.

- Significance: High practical significance; addresses a major obstacle to real-world zero-shot adoption and generalizes to few-shot and other domains.

- Overall recommendation: Accept.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a critical limitation, provides a principled and effective method, shows significant empirical improvements, is well-presented, and includes thorough evaluations.

- Arguments against acceptance: Needs deeper attribute reliability analysis, validation-dependency concerns, scalability discussion, and comparisons to other uncertainty-aware approaches.

- Final recommendation: Accept — strong contribution with practical impact and solid empirical validation.
