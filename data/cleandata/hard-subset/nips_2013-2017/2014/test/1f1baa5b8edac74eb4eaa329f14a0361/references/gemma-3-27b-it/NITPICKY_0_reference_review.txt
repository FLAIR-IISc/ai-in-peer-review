This paper proposes a method for zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions, leveraging receiver operating characteristics to select discriminative decision nodes in a random forest. The core idea – moving beyond treating attribute predictions as ground truth – is a valuable and timely contribution, addressing a known weakness in attribute-based zero-shot learning. The authors further extend the method to the few-shot setting. However, despite the conceptual novelty, the presentation suffers from numerous inconsistencies, a lack of sufficient detail in several crucial methodological choices, and a somewhat inflated assessment of the results’ impact.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical issue in zero-shot learning: the inherent noise and unreliability of attribute predictions.
* **Novel Idea:** The approach of incorporating ROC curves and validation data into the random forest training process to account for attribute uncertainty is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The method consistently outperforms existing baselines (including DAP) on the presented datasets, suggesting a practical benefit.
* **Extension to Few-Shot Learning:** The extension to few-shot learning is a natural and valuable addition.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Detail in ROC Implementation:** The description of how ROC curves are estimated and integrated into the tree splitting process is insufficiently detailed. Specifically, it’s unclear how the validation set is used to *dynamically* estimate ROC curves at *each node* during tree construction. The statement "we recursively propagate validation data" lacks precise explanation. What happens when validation data is limited at deeper nodes?
* **Ambiguous Parameter Choices:** Several crucial parameters are mentioned (e.g., tree depth, (#m,#t) tests per node, λ in the few-shot extension) without sufficient justification for their values. Cross-validation is mentioned, but the details of the cross-validation procedure (folds, metrics) are missing.
* **Inconsistent Notation & Definitions:** The paper suffers from several inconsistencies in notation. For example, `Ak(i)` is initially defined as the association of attribute *i* with class *k*, but later appears in equations as a vector. The definition of I’n is also ambiguous – is it a binary vector representing presence, or a continuous value reflecting probability?
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the method provides a “valuable role for attributes” feels overstated, given that it *corrects* for the inherent flaws of attribute-based approaches, rather than demonstrating their intrinsic strength. The concluding statement that “the elegance of zero-shot learning need not be abandoned” feels like framing a workaround as a fundamental advancement.
* **Minor Formatting Issues:** Numerous minor formatting errors exist throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent spacing, incorrect equation numbering, awkward phrasing). These detract from the paper’s overall professionalism.
* **Insufficient Ablation:** While ablation studies are present, they feel incomplete. For instance, the impact of the signature uncertainty component (OURS W/O SIG UNCERTAINTY) could be analyzed more thoroughly.

**Specific Comments:**

* **Section 3.2.2:** The explanation of how fractional propagation works is conceptually sound but lacks mathematical rigor. A more precise equation or algorithm would clarify the process.
* **Equation (4):** The explanation of I’r and I’l is confusing. The probability terms TP, FN, FP, TN are mentioned but not properly defined in relation to the class *k*.
* **Section 4:** The justification for choosing the specific unseen classes for SUN is weak ("randomly selected"). A more principled selection strategy would strengthen the results.
* **Table 1:** The standard errors reported are often very small, raising concerns about the statistical significance of the observed improvements.
* **Reference Formatting:** The reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., some include volume numbers, others don’t).

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The core idea is novel and addresses a significant limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over state-of-the-art methods.
* The extension to few-shot learning is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper lacks sufficient methodological detail and suffers from numerous inconsistencies.
* The claims are sometimes overstated, and the framing of the work feels overly optimistic.
* The presentation is marred by minor formatting errors and a lack of polish.
* The statistical significance of some results is questionable given the small reported standard errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a genuinely interesting idea with promising results, the numerous flaws in presentation, detail, and rigor significantly detract from its overall quality. I believe that, in its current form, the paper falls short of NIPS standards. While the idea is potentially significant, the lack of clarity and precision prevents a strong assessment of its true impact. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the concerns outlined above and resubmit a revised version. A significantly more polished and detailed manuscript would have a much stronger chance of acceptance. The method *has* potential, but the paper needs substantial work to demonstrate that potential convincingly.
