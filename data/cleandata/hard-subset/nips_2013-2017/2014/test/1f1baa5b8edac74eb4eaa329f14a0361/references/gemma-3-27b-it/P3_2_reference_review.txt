## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the challenging field of zero-shot learning. The authors correctly identify a critical weakness of existing attribute-based ZSL methods – the assumption of reliable attribute predictions – and propose a principled approach to address it. The proposed method, which leverages ROC curves to account for attribute classifier uncertainty within a random forest framework, demonstrates clear improvements over strong baselines on multiple datasets. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles zero-shot learning, a paradigm for recognizing novel classes without direct training examples, relying instead on semantic descriptions in the form of attributes. Previous work in this area (e.g., [8, 18, 23]) typically assumes accurate attribute prediction. The authors argue this is unrealistic, as attributes are often correlated and their visual instantiations are diverse. They propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly incorporates attribute prediction uncertainty during training.  This is achieved by propagating training instances through the trees weighted by the attribute classifier's ROC curves, effectively accounting for both false positives and false negatives. They also extend the method to the few-shot setting.

The authors correctly position their work within the existing landscape of ZSL. They clearly differentiate their approach from methods focusing on alternative knowledge transfer (e.g., hierarchical relationships [23, 14]) and those discovering latent, non-semantic features [11, 22]. They appropriately acknowledge related work on handling missing or probabilistic data in decision trees [21, 28, 16], highlighting the novel aspects of their approach specifically tailored to the zero-shot learning context.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical limitation:** The core idea of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability is a significant advancement. The assumption of perfect attribute prediction has been a major bottleneck in the practical application of attribute-based ZSL.
* **Principled approach:** Using ROC curves to weight the propagation of signatures through the random forest is a theoretically sound and practical way to incorporate uncertainty.  This allows the model to learn to rely on more reliable attributes.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods (DAP) on three diverse datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN).  The ablation studies convincingly show the contribution of both accounting for attribute unreliability and signature uncertainty.
* **Few-shot extension:** The extension to the few-shot setting is a valuable addition, demonstrating the versatility of the proposed approach.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly with sufficient detail, and the experiments are well-designed.
* **Thorough related work:** The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good context for the proposed approach.
* **Controlled Experiments:** The controlled noise experiments provide excellent insight into *how* the method improves performance with varying levels of attribute unreliability.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:**  While not explicitly stated, the computation of ROC curves and the propagation of fractional signatures likely introduces significant computational overhead compared to simpler ZSL methods. The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or scaling concerns.
* **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on a separate validation dataset to estimate ROC curves. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of how the performance is affected by the size and representativeness of this validation set would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of the parameter lambda in the few-shot setting, while effectively tuned via cross-validation, could benefit from further analysis.  What factors influence the optimal lambda? Could there be a more principled way to select it?
* **Limited analysis of failure cases:** While the overall performance is impressive, a deeper analysis of *which* classes or attributes the method struggles with would provide valuable insights.



**4. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Solves a significant problem in ZSL.
* Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
* Demonstrates strong performance improvements over state-of-the-art.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Extends to the few-shot setting.

**Con:**

* Computational complexity might be a concern.
* Dependence on validation data for ROC estimation.
* Further analysis of parameter tuning and failure cases could strengthen the paper.




**5. Specific Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Page 7 (Section 3.2.2):**  It would be helpful to visually illustrate the fractional signature propagation with a small example.
* **Page 9 (Section 4.1):**  Adding a plot showing the ROC curves used for validation would strengthen the presentation of the results.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Please add a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method and potential scaling issues.
* **Further Analysis of Failure Cases:**  Consider including a small table or analysis identifying classes or attributes where the method performs poorly and discussing potential reasons for this.




**Overall:**

This is a well-executed and impactful paper that addresses a critical challenge in zero-shot learning. The proposed method is novel, principled, and demonstrably effective. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.  I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
