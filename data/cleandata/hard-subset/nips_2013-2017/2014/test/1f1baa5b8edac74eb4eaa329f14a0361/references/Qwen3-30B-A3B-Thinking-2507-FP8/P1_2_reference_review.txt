

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL) where attribute-based approaches assume perfect attribute predictions, which is not the case in practice. The authors propose a novel approach that accounts for the unreliability of attribute classifiers during the training of zero-shot models. Their key insight is to use the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers to guide the construction of random forests that can handle imperfect attribute predictions. The method trains on attribute signatures rather than image features, and during training, it propagates signatures through the tree based on ROC statistics. The authors also extend their approach to handle signature uncertainty and to the few-shot setting. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), especially when attribute classifiers are unreliable.

The paper relates well to previous work in ZSL, particularly the DAP method [8] and other attribute-based approaches [3, 18, 30]. It clearly identifies the gap in the literature: existing methods treat attribute predictions as perfect, while in reality, attributes are difficult to predict reliably. The authors build on random forest techniques [2] and extend them to the zero-shot setting with a novel handling of uncertainty.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Identification**: The paper compellingly argues that attribute unreliability is a major limitation of existing ZSL methods, which has been overlooked in the literature. The analysis of why current methods fail (e.g., SIGNATURE RF breaks down with minor attribute errors) is particularly convincing. The authors provide a clear explanation of why attribute-based methods are "in the proof of concept realm" in practice.

2. **Novel Technical Contribution**: The core idea of using ROC statistics to guide random forest construction for ZSL is genuinely novel. The fractional propagation of signatures based on attribute classifier reliability is a clever solution to the problem of attribute unreliability. This represents a significant advancement over treating attribute predictions as perfect, which has been the standard assumption in the field.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The authors provide extensive experiments across three datasets with careful ablation studies. The controlled noise experiments (Fig 1) are particularly effective at demonstrating why their method works. The real-world experiments (Table 1) show consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, especially on datasets with less reliable attribute classifiers (aPY and SUN).

4. **Practical Significance**: The method is simple to implement and can be applied to existing ZSL pipelines with minimal changes. The few-shot extension (Fig 2a) is particularly valuable as it bridges the gap between zero-shot and few-shot learning, which is an important practical consideration.

5. **Rigorous Evaluation of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly the need for attribute validation data, and provide an "upper bound" (OURS+TRUE ROC) to show the theoretical potential of their method. This demonstrates scientific honesty and helps set realistic expectations for the method's performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides strong empirical results, it lacks a theoretical analysis of why the proposed method should work better than existing approaches. A formal analysis of the expected error reduction or a PAC-learning bound would significantly strengthen the paper.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper could provide more details about how the ROC statistics are computed and used during training. For example, the threshold selection process (Section 3.2.2) is not fully explained, and the paper mentions using "some" attribute classifier training data for validation but doesn't clarify how much data is used or how it's partitioned.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper mentions the potential for handling many unseen classes but doesn't address how computational complexity scales with the number of classes or attributes. For large-scale ZSL applications, this could be a significant limitation that should be discussed.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares to DAP and SIGNATURE RF, but doesn't compare to some very recent ZSL methods that have emerged since the cited papers (e.g., [23, 19, 29] are from 2014-2015; the field has moved forward significantly since then). The authors should at least acknowledge whether their method would work with more recent ZSL frameworks.

5. **Attribute Vocabulary Assumption**: The paper assumes the attribute vocabulary is expressive enough to discriminate between categories, but doesn't discuss what happens when it's not. How does the method handle cases where the attribute vocabulary is insufficient for the unseen classes?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical problem that has been overlooked in the literature. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the paper is generally well-written.

The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical justification and limited discussion of scalability. However, these are not fatal flaws for a paper that makes a strong empirical contribution. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes an important contribution to the field of zero-shot learning.

## Specific Comments

- The controlled noise experiments (Fig 1) are excellent and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed method. This should be highlighted more in the main text as a key strength.

- In Section 3.2.2, the authors should clarify how the validation data is used to compute the ROC statistics. The paper mentions "we reserve some attribute classifier training data for this purpose," but it's not clear how much data is used and how it's partitioned.

- The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) is interesting, but the paper could better explain why the combined information gain (Eq 6) is the right way to balance the signature prior and the training examples.

- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method handles attribute correlations. The authors mention this as future work, but it's a known issue in attribute-based methods that could impact the performance of their approach.

- The paper should be clearer about the computational cost of their method. How much more expensive is it to train the random forests with the ROC propagation compared to the standard SIGNATURE RF?

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical practical limitation. The method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches. The experimental results are thorough and convincing.

The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for its contribution. While there are some minor weaknesses (lack of theoretical analysis, limited discussion of scalability), these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would likely be cited and built upon by other researchers in the field.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Addresses a critical, overlooked problem in zero-shot learning
- Novel technical approach with clear motivation
- Thorough experimental evaluation with strong results
- Practical and simple to implement
- Few-shot extension adds significant value
- Rigorous evaluation of limitations

**Cons:**
- Limited theoretical justification
- Some implementation details could be better explained
- Potential scalability concerns not fully addressed
- Limited comparison to very recent ZSL methods

Overall, the pros outweigh the cons, and I believe this paper would make a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
