

# Review: Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to zero-shot learning that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute predictions. The authors introduce a random forest-based method that incorporates attribute classifiers' receiver operating characteristics during training, rather than treating attribute predictions as perfect. The method is extended to handle class signature uncertainty and few-shot learning scenarios. The approach is evaluated on three standard zero-shot datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) and shows improvements over existing methods, particularly when attribute classifiers are unreliable.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a significant practical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning that has been overlooked in previous work. The core idea of incorporating attribute classifier error statistics into tree training is well-motivated and potentially valuable for real-world applications. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly on datasets where attribute classifiers are less reliable (aPY and SUN). The few-shot extension is a natural and useful contribution.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Notational Inconsistencies
1. **Citation Errors**: The paper repeatedly cites [9] as a reference for key claims (e.g., "DAP method [9]" in Section 4.1, "unseen class splits specified in [9]"), but [9] is not listed in the reference section. This appears to be a formatting error that undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Notation Confusion**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For instance, in Section 3.2.1, the indicator vector is denoted as "In" (without subscript), but later as "I_n" (with subscript). Similarly, "pIn" is used for the probability distribution, but it should be "p_{I_n}" for clarity. These inconsistencies make the mathematical formulation difficult to follow.

3. **Inconsistent Threshold Treatment**: The paper states "With binary Ai(m), all 0 < t < 1 are equivalent in Sec 3.2.1. Selecting t becomes important in Sec 3.2.2." However, in the basic formulation (Sec 3.2.1), the threshold t is already used for splitting, making it equally important there. This is a confusing and contradictory statement.

4. **Undefined Notation**: In Equation (4), the term Pn(âm(x) > t | y(m) = Ak(m)) uses Pn without definition. Is this a probability, a fraction, or a function? The paper does not clarify this notation, making the equation difficult to interpret.

### Experimental and Methodological Concerns

5. **Validation Data Usage**: The paper claims that "our method reserves 20% of the attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers." However, in Section 3.2.2, they state that "the data in DV is labeled by attributes only; it has no unseen class labels and never features in the information gain computation." This is inconsistent with the training procedure described, as the validation data is used to estimate ROC curves but then discarded for final training.

6. **Inconsistent Validation Data Handling**: The paper claims that "the recursive sub-selection of the validation data is important to capture the dependency of TP/FP rates at higher level splits," but then in the ablation study (OURS W/O ROC PROP), they "compute TPR/FPRs globally on the full validation dataset DV rather than for node-specific subsets DV(n)." This contradicts their earlier claim about the importance of node-specific subsets.

7. **Reference Mismatch**: The paper cites [21] as "fractional tuples for handling missing values in decision trees," but the reference listed is "Decision Trees for Uncertain Data" by Tsang et al., which appears to be a different paper. The correct reference for fractional tuples should be a different work.

8. **Minor Notational Errors**: In Section 3.1, "Let âm(x) denote the Platt probability score" should use proper subscript notation (â_m(x)), not " âm(x)". Similarly, "In" should be "I_n" throughout.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to handling unreliable attributes in zero-shot learning, the technical execution is marred by multiple notational inconsistencies, citation errors, and methodological ambiguities. The paper's credibility is significantly undermined by the missing reference [9], which is cited multiple times in the main text. The inconsistent notation and undefined terms make it difficult to fully understand and evaluate the proposed method.

The experimental results are promising, but the paper fails to provide sufficient technical clarity to fully assess the contribution. The methodological details are presented with insufficient precision, which is particularly problematic for a technical paper at NIPS.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper's core idea is promising, but the numerous technical and presentation flaws make it difficult to evaluate the contribution with confidence. The citation errors and notational inconsistencies are particularly concerning for a venue like NIPS, which expects rigorous technical presentation. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before reconsidering for publication.
