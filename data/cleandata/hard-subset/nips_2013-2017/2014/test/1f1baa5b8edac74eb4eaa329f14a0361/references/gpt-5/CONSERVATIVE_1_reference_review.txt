Summary
The paper tackles a central weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are unreliable and their errors are not accounted for when building unseen-class classifiers. The core idea is a random forest trained not on images but on class–attribute signatures, while explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during tree construction. Using a held-out attribute-labeled validation set, the method estimates per-attribute ROC statistics locally at each node and fractionally propagates class signatures according to TP/FP rates for candidate splits. This yields splits that are both discriminative among unseen classes and robust to expected attribute errors. The framework is extended to handle uncertainty in class–attribute signatures and to a few-shot setting via a weighted combination of signature-based and data-based information gains. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN report improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations to isolate components, synthetic-noise studies, and a few-shot variant.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on two-stage attribute-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DAP, Farhadi et al.) and relates to decision trees with missing/uncertain features. It contrasts with zero-shot methods based on label/feature embeddings and hierarchical adaptation (Mensink, Akata, DeViSE), emphasizing language-defined attributes and interpretability.

Strengths
- Addresses a real and well-known limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: brittle reliance on noisy attribute predictions.
- Methodologically simple and plausible: node-local ROC estimates and fractional propagation are well-motivated and easy to implement on top of random forests.
- Sensible ablations (signature uncertainty; node-local vs global ROC) and synthetic-noise experiments that align with the stated motivation.
- Empirical gains over DAP across three datasets; especially larger when attributes are less reliable.
- Few-shot extension is straightforward and shows consistent benefits; the λ sensitivity plots are informative.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baselines are limited. While DAP is a canonical attribute-based baseline, the paper does not compare to contemporary zero-shot approaches that do not require attributes but are standard references (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE, hierarchy-based adaptation, metric learning). Even if the focus is attribute-defined classes, a modern practitioner would weigh these alternatives; at minimum, a comparison on AwA would contextualize the contribution.
- Fairness of DAP comparison is not fully clear. Was DAP thoroughly tuned (priors, calibration)? DAP’s performance is sensitive to calibration and independence assumptions; details of its hyperparameter selection and operating point should be made explicit.
- Potential data leakage/clarity issue: The text says 20% of attribute-labeled data is “reserved as ROC validation data, then pooled with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” It is unclear whether the same held-out subset used to estimate node-local ROCs is later reused to re-train attribute SVMs before testing. Please clarify the precise protocol and ensure no leakage between ROC estimation and final attribute score calibration for the test images.
- Assumptions about transferability of ROC curves from seen to unseen classes are strong. The paper offers an “upper bound” using unseen-class validation, but no analysis of domain shift. Sensitivity to the choice/size of DV, and to mismatches in attribute prevalence across seen vs unseen classes, should be quantified.
- Scalability: training one forest per unseen class may be problematic for large K, and fractional propagation at every node requires computing node-conditional ROC statistics. Complexity and memory costs are not discussed, nor is an analysis of how depth/number of thresholds (#m, #t) impact runtime and accuracy.
- Threshold selection: For binary signatures, t matters only once unreliability is modeled. How are candidate thresholds chosen (uniformly, quantiles of DV(n), or based on ROC convex hull)? The paper mentions (#m, #t) but lacks detail; results may depend on this choice.
- Limited analysis of attribute calibration. The method relies on Platt-scaled scores; poor calibration could distort ROC-based propagation. Some analysis of calibration quality and its effect on results would strengthen the claims.
- Few-shot comparisons are weak. The only baseline is an RF trained on the few labeled images. Prior hybrid zero/few-shot methods (e.g., topic/embedding-based, hierarchical) should be included, or at least discussed more critically.

Quality
- The method is technically sound and clearly defines the modified information gain with fractional counts. The node-local ROC estimation is a reasonable way to handle dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Proof-of-concept is convincing for attribute-based pipelines, but the empirical evaluation lacks stronger contemporary baselines and deeper stress tests (sensitivity to DV composition, calibration, and scalability).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Key equations and intuition are clear.
- However, some critical implementation details are deferred to the supplement (signature uncertainty term; continuous signatures; candidate threshold generation). Clarifying these in the main text would improve reproducibility.
- The handling of the 20% validation split and its reuse should be clarified to avoid ambiguity about leakage.

Originality
- The idea of incorporating attribute unreliability into RF training via node-local ROC-driven fractional propagation is novel in this specific zero-shot context and goes beyond standard “missing value” handling.
- Few-shot extension is straightforward but not novel per se.

Significance
- Within attribute-based zero-shot learning, the contribution is meaningful and likely to be adopted by researchers who continue to use attributes. The improvements over DAP on multiple datasets suggest practical value.
- Broader impact may be limited if the community moves away from attributes toward embedding-based zero-shot methods; comparisons to those would help establish significance.

Questions for the authors
1. Detail the threshold sampling strategy and its sensitivity. Did you explore ROC convex hull operating points?
2. Precisely describe the data split for DV and attribute SVM training. Is DV used only for ROC estimation, or also for final SVM training? Any cross-validation to avoid leakage?
3. How sensitive are results to DV size/composition? What happens if DV excludes classes most similar to the unseen ones?
4. Provide runtime/memory costs and scalability behavior with K, M, tree depth, and (#m, #t).
5. Can you include comparisons to at least one embedding-based zero-shot method on AwA (e.g., Akata, DeViSE) to contextualize performance?
6. How robust is the method to miscalibrated Platt probabilities? Did you try isotonic regression or temperature scaling?

Pros
- Addresses a real limitation; clear, principled method.
- Consistent gains over DAP and meaningful ablations.
- Applies to both named and discovered attributes; extends to few-shot.

Cons
- Missing comparisons to stronger non-attribute zero-shot baselines.
- Ambiguity around validation split and potential leakage.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity, calibration, and scalability.
- Few-shot comparisons are weak.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is well-motivated and technically sound, and I am sympathetic to the focus on attribute unreliability. However, stronger and fairer baselines, clearer experimental protocol, and additional analysis are needed to meet the bar. With those additions, I would lean toward acceptance. Confidence: medium.
