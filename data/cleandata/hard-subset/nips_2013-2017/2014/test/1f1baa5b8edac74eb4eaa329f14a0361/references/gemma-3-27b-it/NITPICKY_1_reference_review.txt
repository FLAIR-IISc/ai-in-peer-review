This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning with attribute-based classification, a field aiming to recognize novel classes without requiring labeled training data for those classes. The authors acknowledge the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions and propose a random forest-based approach that explicitly accounts for this unreliability during training. The core idea is to propagate attribute signature information through the forest in a manner proportional to the attribute classifiers’ ROC curves, thereby mitigating the impact of inaccurate attribute predictions. The approach is extended to the few-shot setting, and demonstrated on three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) with compelling results.

While the paper tackles a relevant and important problem—the fragility of attribute-based zero-shot learning—and proposes a novel solution, I find a substantial number of shortcomings that, collectively, lead me to recommend rejection. My concerns span technical details, presentation, and a perceived disconnect between the experimental setup and the claimed advantages.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The issue of unreliable attribute predictions is undeniably a major bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning, and addressing it is crucial for practical applicability.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of incorporating ROC curves into the random forest training process is a novel and conceptually sound way to handle attribute uncertainty.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate improvements over the baseline DAP method, especially on datasets with less reliable attribute classifiers (SUN and aPY). The few-shot extension is also a valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Detailed ROC Implementation:** The paper describes injecting ROC data into the process but lacks crucial implementation details. How precisely are ROC curves estimated and incorporated? Specifically, the "validation data" needs far more discussion. The statement that 20% of attribute data is reserved feels arbitrary. Why this percentage? How is it selected? The authors mention dependence on validation data subsets, but don’t explain how this impacts computational cost.
*   **Overly Complex Notation:** The introduction of numerous indicator variables (I<sub>n</sub>, I’<sub>n</sub>, I<sub>l</sub>, I<sub>r</sub>) and equations (particularly Eqn. 5) make the method difficult to follow. The justification for these formulations, and their connection to existing techniques like fractional tuples (mentioned in the related work), remains unclear.
*   **Ambiguous "Signature Uncertainty" Component:** The handling of class signature uncertainty (Section 3.2.3) is mentioned but receives insufficient detail. The impact of this component on the experimental results is not clearly delineated.
*   **Questionable Experimental Setup:** The claim of using the same amount of attribute-labeled data as baselines is misleading. While the *total* data used for attribute classification is the same, the authors explicitly *reserve* 20% for ROC estimation, effectively reducing the data available for *training* the attribute classifiers themselves. This introduces a potential bias. Moreover, performance gains against DAP are minimal (often < 2%), raising questions about practical significance given the increased complexity.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The ablation study (Table 1) is insufficient. The impact of signature uncertainty is not clearly isolated, and the effect of the ROC propagation strategy warrants more detailed analysis.
*   **Presentation Issues:** The paper suffers from several minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, missing periods). Although minor, these detract from the overall professionalism. The citations are also sometimes oddly placed within sentences.
*   **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** Incorporating ROC curves and recursively propagating validation data likely introduces significant computational overhead. The paper fails to address this concern.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a critical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   Presents a novel approach with a sound theoretical basis.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods.

**Con:**

*   Lack of implementation details and clarity in key aspects of the method.
*   Questionable experimental setup with potential bias.
*   Insufficient ablation study.
*   Limited discussion of computational cost.
*   Overall complexity may outweigh practical benefits.
*   Numerous minor formatting and presentation issues.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to a lack of technical rigor, clarity, and a questionable experimental setup. While the concept of incorporating ROC curves into the zero-shot learning process is novel, the insufficient details and the potential bias in the experimental design undermine the validity of the reported results. The added complexity of the method does not appear to translate into significant practical gains. A major revision addressing these concerns—specifically, providing comprehensive implementation details, a more robust experimental design, a thorough ablation study, and a discussion of computational cost—would be required for reconsideration. As it stands, I recommend rejection.
