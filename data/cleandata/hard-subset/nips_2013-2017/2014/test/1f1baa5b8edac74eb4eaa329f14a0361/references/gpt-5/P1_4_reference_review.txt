Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot learning with semantic attributes by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. Instead of taking predicted attributes at face value, the authors train a random forest directly on class-level attribute signatures, but, crucially, during training they propagate each class signature fractionally down the tree according to node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) of the attribute classifiers estimated on a held-out attribute-labeled validation set. Information gain is redefined to operate on fractional counts, encouraging splits that are both discriminative and reliably predictable given the attribute classifiers’ error tendencies. The method also incorporates uncertainty in class signatures (to account for intra-class variation) and extends to few-shot learning by combining information gain on signatures and on few labeled images with a tradeoff parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest, with ablations and controlled noise studies supporting the design. The approach also works with discovered (pseudo)attributes.

Strengths
- Addresses a central practical weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute unreliability. The fractional propagation using ROC at node level is a neat, principled idea.
- Careful algorithmic design: redefined information gain for fractional counts; node-specific ROC computed on recursively filtered validation data captures conditional dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Generality: handles binary/continuous signatures, accommodates noisy class-attribute associations, and extends naturally to few-shot learning via a prior on tree splits.
- Solid empirical evaluation: three datasets, multiple trials, controlled noise experiments, ablations isolating ROC propagation and signature uncertainty, and results with both named and discovered attributes.
- Clear performance gains over the widely used DAP baseline, especially when attributes are less reliable (e.g., SUN, aPY), which aligns with the stated motivation.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on validation ROC under domain shift: The approach assumes attribute error profiles learned from seen classes transfer to unseen classes. The sizable gap between OURS and OURS+TRUE ROC suggests residual domain shift. Suggest evaluating sensitivity to DV composition (e.g., importance-weighted ROC, per-attribute covariate shift correction, or conditioning DV on nearest seen signatures) and reporting how performance varies with DV size.
- Limited baselines: Beyond DAP and the signature-RF, comparisons to more recent zero-shot label/feature embedding methods (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE) would strengthen the case, especially on AwA where these are standard. Even if features differ, a representative embedding baseline would be informative.
- Estimation at deeper nodes: Node-specific ROC can be data-sparse; reverting to global DV when <10 positives/negatives may introduce abrupt shifts. Consider smoothing across nodes (e.g., hierarchical Bayesian shrinkage) or monotonic constraints on TPR/FPR as nodes specialize.
- Attribute correlation: While recursive DV propagation captures some conditional structure, tree tests remain axis-aligned on single attributes. Strongly correlated attributes may still lead to redundant or brittle splits. Discuss oblique/multivariate splits or feature grouping/whitening to mitigate correlation effects.
- Computational considerations and scalability: Training one forest per class can be heavy when K is large. Provide training/test time and memory footprint, and discuss multi-class forests or shared-tree strategies. Also clarify how many thresholds per attribute are evaluated and how candidate thresholds are chosen (uniform in [0,1], quantiles on DV, or score percentiles).
- Few-shot λ selection: Cross-validating λ with very few labeled images risks overfitting. A Bayesian or evidence-based scheme, or a heuristic tying λ to |DT|, could be more robust; please report variance across λ and a sensitivity plot.
- Clarity/details deferred to supplement: The signature uncertainty model (Sec. 3.2.3) is central but only referenced; include at least the main formula in the paper. Also clarify how continuous signatures and discovered attributes are handled in split tests and ROC computation.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. The training objective and the fractional propagation are coherent, and ablations support the design choices. Some assumptions (ROC transferability) are empirically plausible but not theoretically justified; adding an analysis or diagnostics on domain shift would improve rigor. Experimental protocol is generally careful (multiple trials, cross-validation), though broader baselines and runtime reporting would help.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The high-level narrative is easy to follow. A few crucial components are deferred to the supplement (signature uncertainty, pseudocode); condensing key formulas into the main text would aid reproducibility. Explicitly stating candidate threshold sampling and calibration details would improve clarity.

Originality
The idea of training trees on class signatures and using node-specific ROC for fractional propagation in zero-shot is novel and distinct from prior two-stage attribute matching (e.g., DAP) and from decision trees for missing/probabilistic features. The few-shot extension as a descriptive prior on splits is a clean contribution.

Significance
Practical and conceptual impact for zero-/few-shot learning with attributes. Demonstrated gains on standard benchmarks and robustness under attribute noise address a well-known pain point and are likely to influence subsequent work. The idea could generalize beyond attributes to other mid-level semantic detectors.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to the size and class composition of DV? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis?
- How are candidate thresholds t selected per attribute? Do you use probability quantiles or fixed grid? Are attribute scores calibrated per node or globally?
- What are training/testing runtimes and memory costs for each dataset and K? Could a single multi-class forest approximate the one-vs-all forests?
- How does performance degrade if ROC calibration is biased (e.g., using different feature backbones for DV vs. test)?
- Can you report per-class mean accuracy (to control for class imbalance) and confusion matrices?

Minor comments
- Specify random seed or release splits for SUN to ease reproducibility.
- Include a brief description of how continuous signatures are incorporated into split tests.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a clear, novel, and effective approach to an important problem, with solid empirical evidence and reasonable generality. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation breadth issues would further strengthen the submission.

Reviewer confidence: High.
