Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses zero-shot visual recognition when attribute classifiers are unreliable. Standard attribute-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DAP; Lampert et al.) typically treat attribute predictions at face value, mapping test-time attribute scores to class signatures. The authors propose a random forest approach that explicitly models attribute unreliability during training. They train one-vs-all forests over class attribute signatures (not images). Crucially, they incorporate attribute classifier ROC statistics, estimated on attribute-labeled validation data, to fractionally propagate each class signature down tree nodes: a signature splits to left/right children according to the TP/FP/TN/FN rates for the candidate attribute threshold at that node. Information gain is computed on fractional counts, favoring splits that remain discriminative given realistic attribute errors. They further (i) account for uncertainty in class-signature annotations and (ii) extend to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain with standard data-driven information gain via a weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF, including controlled noise studies and evaluations with discovered “pseudo-attributes.”

This work relates to: attribute-based zero-shot recognition (Farhadi et al., Lampert et al., Parikh & Grauman, Patterson & Hays), alternatives using label/feature embeddings (Akata et al., Frome et al., Mensink & Verbeek), and methods discovering mid-level features (e.g., Rastegari et al., Singh et al.). Methodologically it is connected to decision trees with missing/uncertain features and fractional propagation (Quinlan; Olaru & Wehenkel; Tsang et al.), but is distinct in using ROC-driven fractional propagation in a zero-shot setting where training units are class signatures rather than instances.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The core idea of using node-specific ROC estimates to fractionally propagate class signatures and compute information gain over fractional counts is well-motivated and sound. It provides a principled way to bias tree splits toward attributes and thresholds that are both discriminative and predictable at test time.
- The recursive restriction of the validation set DV(n) per node is important to capture dependencies induced by upstream splits; this is a thoughtful design choice.
- The few-shot extension (convex combination of signature-based and instance-based gains) is straightforward and reasonable.
- Experiments are thorough across three datasets, include ablations (no ROC propagation; no signature uncertainty), controlled noise analyses, and comparisons to DAP and a signature-only RF. Results align with the premise: larger gains where attributes are noisier.
Potential technical concerns:
- The paper states it “reserves 20% … as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” This sequence is ambiguous. If DV is also used to train the final attribute classifiers that produce test-time predictions, ROC estimates may be optimistic (no true holdout). Alternatively, if ROC is computed using models trained on 80% and test-time predictions come from retrained models using 100%, the ROC and deployed classifier may be mismatched. Clarifying this pipeline is important.
- Calibration/score monotonicity: the approach relies on thresholds over attribute scores; while ROC is threshold-insensitive in the ranking sense, the stability of rankings (and the distribution of thresholds t sampled) matters. Some discussion of calibration practices and sensitivity to Platt scaling would be useful.
- Scalability: training one forest per unseen class scales linearly with K; for large K, this could be costly. Runtime/memory analysis is not reported.
- Some details (signature uncertainty formulation, pseudocode, noise model) are deferred to the supplement; moving key formulas into the main text would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The problem setup, notation, and the key equations (fractional indicators and IGzero) are presented cleanly. The role of DV and node-specific ROC is explained well, with helpful intuition. However:
- The treatment of DV (holdout vs pooled training) needs clarification, as noted above.
- The description of signature uncertainty is referenced to the supplement; a concise equation and intuition should be included in the main paper.
- Reporting more implementation details (candidate threshold sampling strategy, computational cost) would improve completeness.

Originality
The work’s central contribution—integrating attribute unreliability via ROC-driven fractional propagation in forests trained on class signatures—is, to my knowledge, novel. While related ideas exist for handling missing/uncertain features in decision trees, the zero-shot setting, the use of ROC statistics, and the training on signatures rather than instances constitute a new combination. The few-shot extension with a signature prior is a natural but useful addition.

Significance
Empirically, the method yields consistent, and sometimes substantial, gains over DAP and a signature-only RF on three standard datasets. Given the persistent challenge of attribute unreliability in zero-shot recognition, a method that leverages known error characteristics to improve robustness is valuable. The approach is generic, compatible with both semantic and discovered attributes, and conceptually simple to implement with existing attribute classifiers. That said, comparison to stronger non-attribute zero-shot baselines (e.g., label/visual-semantic embeddings such as DeViSE or label-embedding methods) would better position the contribution within the broader zero-shot literature.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled modeling of attribute unreliability using ROC statistics within random forests.
- Strong empirical improvements over widely used DAP across multiple datasets, with thorough ablations and controlled noise studies.
- Natural extension to few-shot learning demonstrating benefits of the signature prior.
- Applicable to both semantic and discovered attributes.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Ambiguity regarding the use of the validation set DV and potential over-optimism or mismatch between ROC estimates and final attribute models.
- Lack of runtime/scalability analysis and discussion for large numbers of unseen classes.
- Key details (signature uncertainty) relegated to the supplement.
- Limited comparisons to recent non-attribute zero-shot approaches (e.g., label/semantic embeddings), which are relevant for NeurIPS readership.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the exact training/validation protocol for attribute classifiers and ROC estimation, ensuring no leakage and alignment between ROC and deployed classifiers.
- Include the signature uncertainty formulation in the main text, with a brief derivation and intuition.
- Report training and inference times, and discuss scalability to larger K and M.
- Add comparisons to representative embedding-based zero-shot methods, or justify their exclusion (different supervision assumptions).
- Discuss sensitivity to the quantity/composition of DV and to threshold sampling; provide guidance for choosing (#m, #t) and tree depths.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed contribution that advances attribute-based zero-shot learning by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability. With clarifications on validation protocol and broader comparisons, it would make a strong NeurIPS paper.
