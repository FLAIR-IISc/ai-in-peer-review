The paper addresses the problem of zero-shot recognition, specifically tackling the unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea is to build a random forest that explicitly accounts for the known biases of mid-level attribute models during training, propagating uncertainties through the decision tree. The approach is generalized to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and extended to a few-shot setting. The authors demonstrate improvements over existing methods on three datasets.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed approach of incorporating attribute uncertainty into a random forest framework is well-motivated and logically presented. The use of ROC curves to inform the tree splitting process is a reasonable and effective way to handle unreliable attributes. The extension to the few-shot setting appears sensible. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons to relevant baselines and ablation studies. However, the details of the validation data selection (20% of attribute labeled images) could be explained better. The effect of parameters like the depth of the trees and the number of trees is also not rigorously explored. While the authors acknowledge potential improvements (inter-attribute correlations, multi-label forests), the lack of a deeper discussion on these points weakens the paper slightly. The reliance on existing attribute classifiers trained with a fixed C value limits exploration of optimal attribute learning.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The approach section is detailed and easy to follow, although some mathematical notation could be simplified. The description of the information gain calculation and the validation data propagation is particularly clear. However, the connection between the fractional tuple approaches cited ([21, 28, 16]) and the current approach could be explained more explicitly, beyond stating a general similarity. More illustrative figures beyond the results would benefit the reader.

**Originality:** The idea of explicitly incorporating attribute prediction uncertainty into a zero-shot learning framework is novel. While random forests are a well-established technique, their application in this context, particularly the propagation of uncertainty based on ROC curves, is a significant contribution. The extension to few-shot learning builds naturally on the zero-shot framework and adds further value. The work adequately references related work, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature.  The use of the validation data for ROC estimation is a clever adaptation. The approach differs significantly from existing zero-shot methods that treat attribute predictions as ground truth or focus on discovering mid-level features without semantic interpretation.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods, especially in scenarios with unreliable attribute predictions. The approach addresses a critical limitation of current zero-shot learning techniques, which often struggle with noisy or inaccurate attribute information. The method’s ability to handle both attribute prediction uncertainty and class signature uncertainty is particularly valuable. The potential applications in scenarios where labeled data is scarce or where new categories emerge dynamically are significant. While the gains are not massive, they are consistent and demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. The positive results on discovered attributes demonstrate the method's versatility beyond hand-engineered attributes. The few-shot extension is also a positive contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to address a key limitation of zero-shot learning.
*   Clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods on multiple datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.
*   Demonstrates a practical solution to handling unreliable attribute predictions.
*   Extension to the few-shot setting.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The impact while positive is not transformative.
*   Limited exploration of hyperparameter space (tree depth, number of trees).
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the validation data selection strategy.
*   Could benefit from a discussion of inter-attribute correlations and more sophisticated handling of attribute dependencies.
*   The reliance on a fixed C parameter for training the base attribute classifiers.



Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is technically sound, novel, and addresses a significant practical challenge. While some aspects could be further explored, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
