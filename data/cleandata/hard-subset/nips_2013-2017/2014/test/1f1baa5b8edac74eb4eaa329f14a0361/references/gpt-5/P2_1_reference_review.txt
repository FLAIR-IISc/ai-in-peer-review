Summary
The paper addresses a core practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attributes are predicted unreliably. The key idea is to train random forests directly on unseen-class attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute classifier error via node-specific ROC estimates computed on held-out attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, each class signature is fractionally propagated down splits according to TP/FP/TN/FN rates at the chosen threshold, yielding an information gain criterion (IGzero) that favors splits that remain discriminative under expected attribute noise. The method is extended to: (i) uncertainty in class-attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”), and (ii) few-shot learning by blending signature-based IG with image-based IG via a weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, stronger gains when attributes are noisier, ablations of key components, and benefits in the few-shot regime. The approach also works with discovered attributes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent. Fractional propagation using node-specific ROC statistics is a principled way to inject attribute unreliability into split selection, and the revised information gain is well defined. Propagating validation data per-node captures conditional dependencies induced by previous splits—an important and often neglected detail.
- Empirical support: The paper provides controlled noise studies (diagnostic), three real datasets, comparisons to DAP and a signature-only baseline, ablations (without signature uncertainty; without node-specific ROC propagation), and an “upper bound” using true ROC from unseen classes. Few-shot experiments demonstrate the prior’s utility and sensible behavior of λ. These results substantiate the claims.
- Caveats: The method relies on validation ROC estimated on seen-class data, which may not match unseen-class statistics; the paper acknowledges this and reports an upper bound, but a deeper analysis of domain shift (e.g., varying the composition/size of DV) would strengthen the conclusions. One-vs-all forests for each unseen class may pose calibration and scalability issues when K grows; there is no analysis of computational or memory complexity. The signature uncertainty model is delegated to the supplement; some detail in the main paper would help assess its contribution more precisely.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The basic RF formulation, the fractional propagation, and the IGzero criterion are explained with equations and intuition. The role of DV and node-specific ROCs is described explicitly.
- Some important details are in the supplement (pseudocode, noise model, signature uncertainty), which slightly hinders reproducibility from the main text. A brief description of the signature uncertainty term and more concrete hyperparameter ranges (#m, #t sampling, λ selection) in the main paper would help.
- Notation is consistent; however, the statement that 20% of data is reserved as validation and then pooled with the remaining 80% to train attribute classifiers could be clarified to avoid confusion about data usage and leakage.

Originality
- Modeling attribute unreliability at training time for zero-shot category induction via ROC-aware random forests is novel. Prior attribute-based zero-shot methods (e.g., DAP and variants) treat attribute predictions at face value or model confidence only at test time. The connection to fractional/missing-value decision trees is appropriate, but the training-from-signatures with node-specific ROC-based fractional propagation is new in the zero-shot context.
- The few-shot extension that treats signatures as a descriptive prior and blends IGs is a sensible and, to my knowledge, novel instantiation for random forests.

Significance
- The problem is important: attribute unreliability is a major bottleneck for zero-shot learning. The method advances the state of the art on multiple datasets, with especially notable gains when attributes are noisier (aPY, SUN, reduced AwA). This directly improves the practical viability of attribute-based zero-shot recognition and suggests a path to leveraging discovered attributes as well.
- The approach is likely to influence subsequent work on zero/few-shot learning with semantic mid-levels and could be adapted to other domains with unreliable intermediate predictors.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and well-motivated treatment of attribute unreliability.
- Solid empirical gains over strong baselines, with ablations validating design choices.
- Node-specific ROC handling addresses attribute dependencies after earlier splits.
- Natural few-shot extension with demonstrated benefits.
- Applicable to both named and discovered attributes.

Cons
- Dependence on seen-class validation ROC; limited analysis of domain shift sensitivity.
- One-vs-all forests may not scale or calibrate well for large K; no complexity analysis.
- Some essential details (signature uncertainty, pseudocode, noise model) only in the supplement.
- Comparative scope: main comparisons focus on DAP and related attribute methods; limited benchmarking against non-attribute zero-shot methods (e.g., label/feature embeddings) in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Analyze sensitivity to DV size/composition and cross-domain mismatch; consider techniques to better estimate unseen-class ROCs (e.g., domain adaptation or importance weighting).
- Report training/inference time and memory as functions of M, K, tree depth, and (#m,#t).
- Discuss calibration across one-vs-all forests and potential multi-class tree variants.
- Include a concise description of the signature uncertainty term in the main paper and its quantitative impact.
- Broaden comparisons to embedding-based zero-shot baselines, even if semantically different, to contextualize performance.

Overall recommendation
- Verdict: Accept.
- Justification: Addresses an important limitation with a principled, novel method and thorough experiments showing meaningful gains. Remaining concerns are incremental and largely addressable; the contribution is both technically interesting and practically valuable.

Reviewer confidence: High.
