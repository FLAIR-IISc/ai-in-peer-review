Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition with semantic attributes when attribute predictors are unreliable. Instead of taking attribute predictions at face value (as in DAP and nearest-signature methods), the authors propose training random forests directly on class-level attribute signatures and explicitly modeling attribute unreliability using ROC statistics estimated on attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, each class signature is fractionally propagated to child nodes according to attribute TP/FP rates at a candidate threshold, redefining information gain to reflect the expected distribution of noisy attribute predictions at test time. The method naturally prefers reliable attributes or reliable operating points for unreliable attributes. The approach is extended to account for uncertainty/noise in class-attribute associations (signature uncertainty) and to a few-shot regime by combining signature-driven information gain with standard training-image-driven gain, controlled by a mixing parameter λ.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN with hundreds of real attribute classifiers. It includes controlled noise experiments, ablations (without signature uncertainty, without recursive ROC propagation), and shows substantial gains over DAP and a signature-only random forest. The few-shot extension provides consistent improvements over training from few labeled images alone.

Relative to prior zero-shot work: the two-stage attribute pipeline and DAP [Lampert et al.] are closest; unlike them, this method models attribute unreliability during training. Compared to approaches leveraging hierarchies, label/feature embeddings, or discovered attributes, this work retains the interpretability and human-in-the-loop definability of semantic attributes while explicitly handling their detection noise. The ROC-driven fractional propagation is related to handling missing/uncertain features in decision trees but adapted to the zero-shot setting where training instances are signatures and test inputs are attribute predictions.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem formulation: explicitly addressing attribute unreliability, which is widely acknowledged to limit attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Technically sound core idea: fractional propagation based on node-specific ROC estimates and an information gain criterion that anticipates test-time noise. The recursive use of DV(n) to capture conditional dependencies between splits is thoughtful.
- Practical impact: consistent improvements over DAP and signature-RF across three datasets; larger gains where attributes are less reliable (aPY, SUN). Controlled noise studies convincingly diagnose why the method helps.
- Few-shot extension is simple, principled, and effective; the signature prior remains useful even with hundreds of labeled images.
- Ablations isolate contributions (signature uncertainty, recursive ROC propagation); analysis of an “upper bound” with true ROC is informative.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
Quality
- The method relies on ROC estimates from attribute-labeled validation data drawn from seen classes. Distribution shift between seen and unseen classes may make these ROC estimates miscalibrated. The “upper bound” experiment underscores this gap, but the paper lacks a systematic analysis of sensitivity to the composition/size of DV and domain shift mitigation strategies.
- Some important modeling details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., the exact form of signature uncertainty), making it harder to assess reproducibility and the precise behavior of the method.
- Scalability is not fully discussed: one-vs-all forests (K forests) and per-node threshold search with ROC computation may become expensive for large M and K. Complexity/runtime numbers would help.

Clarity
- Overall well written, but certain notational choices and footnotes impede flow. Provide in-paper pseudocode for the training loop and the exact computation of IGzero with soft indicators. Clarify how candidate thresholds t are selected per attribute and how many are sampled.
- Define precisely how cross-validation selects λ in the few-shot setting and whether it uses only seen classes or a held-out subset that mimics the zero-shot condition.

Originality
- The key idea—injecting ROC-driven uncertainty into tree building on signatures—is novel in the zero-shot attribute literature and distinct from DAP and label/feature embedding approaches. The relation to decision trees for uncertain/missing data is acknowledged; the adaptation to zero-shot with signatures and node-conditional ROCs is original.

Significance
- Within semantic attribute-based zero-shot learning, the contribution is significant: it addresses a core Achilles’ heel and yields clear improvements. The few-shot extension is also useful.
- That said, the experimental comparisons omit some influential contemporaneous baselines that do not rely on attributes (e.g., label embedding/ALE, DeViSE, hierarchical/metric-based zero-shot). While these differ in supervision and features, a discussion and, where feasible, comparisons on shared datasets would help position the contribution’s impact relative to broader zero-shot directions.

Specific suggestions
- Add sensitivity analyses: (i) size/composition of DV, including selecting DV to be “closer” to unseen signatures; (ii) calibration method for attribute scores (Platt vs. isotonic); (iii) effect of threshold sampling strategy (#t, distribution).
- Report computational costs (training/test time) as functions of M, K, tree depth, and (#m, #t).
- Provide more detail on the signature uncertainty model in the main text and an ablation quantifying its contribution per dataset.
- Discuss or explore simple strategies to mitigate DV–test shift (e.g., domain adaptation of attribute calibrations, importance-weighted ROC estimates, or grouping validation examples by signature similarity to unseen classes).
- Where feasible, include comparisons to at least one embedding-based zero-shot baseline on AwA/aPY (e.g., ALE/label-embedding) using the same features to contextualize gains.
- Consider multi-class forests rather than one-vs-all, or clarify why one-vs-all is preferred here.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a central weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a principled, practical method.
- Demonstrated, consistent gains on three datasets; solid ablations and controlled noise studies.
- Method is conceptually clean and extensible (few-shot, discovered attributes).
- Maintains interpretability and human-defined category control.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Dependence on validation ROC from seen classes; limited treatment of domain shift and sensitivity to DV.
- Missing comparisons to strong non-attribute zero-shot baselines; limited discussion of scalability/runtime.
- Some crucial methodological details relegated to the supplement.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a novel and useful advance for attribute-based zero-shot learning with convincing empirical benefits and a sound formulation. Addressing the clarity/scalability concerns and broadening comparisons would strengthen it further.
