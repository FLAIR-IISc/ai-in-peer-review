This paper tackles the challenging problem of zero-shot learning, specifically addressing the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea – to explicitly model and account for attribute prediction errors during training – is a strong one, and the proposed random forest approach with ROC-informed splitting is a novel and well-motivated solution. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, presenting a clear progression of ideas from problem formulation to experimental validation. It situates itself well within the existing literature on zero-shot learning and attribute-based recognition, accurately identifying the limitations of prior work and highlighting the contributions of this paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The explicit modeling of attribute prediction unreliability is a significant contribution. Existing methods largely treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which is a strong and unrealistic assumption.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach, leveraging ROC curves and fractional signature propagation, is technically sound and appears well-motivated. The extension to few-shot learning is a logical and valuable addition.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods like DAP and the basic signature random forest. The controlled noise experiments effectively illustrate the benefit of accounting for attribute uncertainty.
* **Clarity:** The paper is very well written and easy to follow. The explanations of the method and experimental setup are clear and concise.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors have considered and addressed several important aspects of the problem, including signature uncertainty and the impact of validation data.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the method demonstrates improved accuracy, the computational complexity associated with ROC estimation and fractional propagation could be a concern, especially for datasets with a large number of attributes. This is not explicitly discussed in the paper. A comparison of training/inference time to baselines would be helpful.
* **Validation Data Dependence:** The performance of the method relies on having a representative validation dataset for estimating attribute classifier error rates. The impact of the size and quality of this validation data is not thoroughly investigated. Are the results sensitive to the split between training and validation data used for ROC estimation?
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While ablation studies are present, they could be more comprehensive. For example, the impact of different parameters (e.g., λ in the few-shot extension) could be explored in more detail.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal values for tree depth and the number of tests per split were determined via cross-validation. The sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters is not discussed.
* **ROC Estimation for Continuous Attributes:** The paper mentions handling continuous attribute strength signatures but lacks details on how ROC curves are estimated in this scenario.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing zero-shot learning methods (attribute unreliability).
* The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art baselines.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
* The extension to few-shot learning is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the method is a potential concern.
* The dependence on a representative validation dataset for ROC estimation could limit its applicability.
* The lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and experimentally validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*  Discuss the computational complexity of the method and compare the training/inference time to baselines.
*  Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the size and quality of the validation data used for ROC estimation.
*  Provide a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*  Elaborate on how ROC curves are estimated for continuous attribute strength signatures.
* Add a discussion on the limitations of the approach and potential future research directions.
