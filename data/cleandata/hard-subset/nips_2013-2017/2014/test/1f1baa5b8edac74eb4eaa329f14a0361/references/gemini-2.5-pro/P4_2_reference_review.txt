This paper addresses a critical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of the underlying attribute classifiers. The standard ZSL paradigm defines a novel, unseen class via a semantic attribute "signature" and then classifies test images by first predicting their attributes and matching them to the known signatures. The authors argue that this pipeline is flawed because existing methods, such as the widely-used Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) model, treat the (often noisy) attribute predictions at face value.

The central contribution is a novel random forest (RF) framework that is trained to be robust to this attribute unreliability. Instead of training on images, the RFs are trained directly on the attribute signatures of the K unseen classes. The key innovation lies in how the decision trees are constructed. During training, for a given split on an attribute, a class signature is not sent deterministically down a single path. Instead, it is fractionally propagated to both child nodes. This propagation is governed by the reliability of the attribute classifier, specifically its receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve (i.e., its true positive and false positive rates). These ROC statistics are estimated at each node using a validation set of attribute-labeled images that is also propagated down the tree. Consequently, the information gain criterion for selecting splits favors attributes that are not only discriminative for the idealized signatures but are also reliably predictable in practice. The framework is also extended to handle uncertainty in the class signatures themselves and to the "few-shot" learning setting.

This work builds upon the substantial body of literature in attribute-based ZSL [8, 18, 3] but departs from it by explicitly modeling classifier error during the training of the zero-shot model itself, rather than treating it as a separate, upstream problem. The mechanism of fractional propagation is related to methods for handling missing data in decision trees [21], but its application to the ZSL setting—training on ideal signatures while anticipating noisy predictions at test time—is novel.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is well-formulated, and the derivation of the modified information gain criterion (Eq. 5) is a principled way to incorporate attribute reliability into the tree-building process. The use of a recursively propagated validation set to estimate node-specific ROCs is a sophisticated detail that allows the model to capture dependencies between attribute tests in the tree. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
*   The controlled noise experiments provide clear insight into the method's robustness and its ability to correctly favor more reliable attributes.
*   The comparison against a strong and widely-used baseline (DAP) on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrates significant and consistent performance gains.
*   Ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of different model components (signature uncertainty, recursive ROC propagation).
*   The demonstration that the method also achieves state-of-the-art results with discovered (non-semantic) attributes from [30] shows its generality.
The claims made in the paper are well-supported by these comprehensive results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for addressing attribute unreliability is clearly established in the introduction. The approach is presented logically, beginning with a basic signature-based RF and incrementally adding the core components for handling unreliability. The distinction between the training phase (using signatures) and the testing phase (using attribute predictions on images) is made clear. The notation is consistent, and the figures and tables effectively communicate the main results. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics.

**Originality:** The core idea of training a zero-shot classifier to be explicitly aware of the error characteristics of its input (the attribute predictions) is novel. While previous work has acknowledged the problem of unreliable attributes, most solutions have focused on learning better attributes or transferring knowledge in other ways. This paper proposes a new direction: making the final classifier robust to the imperfections of the mid-level representation. The specific implementation within a random forest, using fractional propagation guided by node-local ROC estimation, is a novel combination of techniques applied to a distinct problem setting. The related work section is thorough and correctly positions the contribution with respect to prior art in ZSL and decision trees.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and likely to have an impact on the field. The paper tackles a well-known, practical bottleneck that has limited the real-world applicability of attribute-based ZSL. By demonstrating a method that yields substantial improvements over the de facto standard DAP model, the paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The problem of building robust high-level models from noisy mid-level predictions is general, and the ideas presented here could potentially be adapted to other areas beyond ZSL (e.g., action recognition from poselet detectors). The extension to the few-shot setting also provides a principled way to incorporate descriptive priors into traditional classifiers, which is of broad interest.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, practical, and well-motivated problem in zero-shot learning.
*   Proposes a novel and technically sound method that explicitly models attribute classifier unreliability during training.
*   The experimental evaluation is extensive, with strong results that significantly outperform a widely-used baseline (DAP) on multiple challenging benchmarks.
*   The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The one-versus-all training scheme, which requires one RF per unseen class, may face scalability challenges if the number of unseen classes is very large. The authors acknowledge this as future work.
*   The method's performance depends on having a validation set of attribute-labeled images that is representative of the unseen classes. The sensitivity to distribution shift between the validation and test sets is not fully explored, although the "true ROC" experiment provides a useful upper bound.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed method is elegant, technically sound, and empirically validated. The strengths clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are appropriately noted as directions for future work.
