Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition with attribute-based descriptions by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The core idea is a random forest trained not on images but on class-level attribute signatures, where at each split the propagation of signatures to children is fractional and governed by node-specific estimates of the attribute classifiers’ ROC (TP/FP) at the candidate threshold. This encourages splits that are both discriminative and “learnable” given expected attribute prediction errors. The authors also add a mechanism for uncertainty in class-attribute associations and a simple few-shot extension that interpolates information gain from signatures and image-labeled data. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN include controlled noise analyses, ablations, and comparisons primarily against DAP and a basic signature-only RF baseline, with additional comparisons to published results on AwA.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical and well-known weakness of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attributes are noisy and unevenly reliable.
- Methodologically clear and simple: integrating ROC-driven fractional propagation into decision-tree training is intuitive and easy to implement.
- Node-specific estimation of ROC using recursively filtered validation data is a nice touch to capture dependencies induced by earlier splits.
- Sensible ablations (without ROC propagation, without signature uncertainty) and controlled noise studies illuminate why the method helps.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over DAP across three datasets, with larger gains when attributes are less reliable; few-shot extension shows benefit from the attribute prior.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Potential leakage/optimism in ROC estimation: the paper states it “reserves 20% of the attribute-labeled images as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers.” If the ROC is computed on data used to train the attribute SVMs, the error estimates are biased and can be overly optimistic. Please clarify whether ROC is computed on a held-out set never used to fit Platt scaling or SVMs; otherwise, redo with a proper hold-out or cross-validated estimates.
- Limited baselines: DAP is a strong early baseline but there are other established zero-shot approaches that should be compared under the same splits and features, especially label-embedding methods (e.g., ALE/Akata, ESZSL, DeViSE) and hierarchy/metric transfer (Mensink et al.). The text claims superiority over published results on AwA; however, direct, controlled comparisons on the same protocol across all three datasets are needed to substantiate significance.
- Theoretical grounding is limited: while the fractional information gain is reasonable, there is no analysis of consistency or bias introduced by using estimated ROCs (especially at deep nodes with few samples) or of the error propagation through the tree. The fallback to global DV when <10 positives/negatives reach a node is heuristic and its effect is not quantified.
- Training on K signatures: forests are grown from very few “instances” (one signature per class). The method relies on ROC-driven fractional counts to emulate data. Some analysis of stability with respect to K (e.g., more unseen classes) and sensitivity to class-signature sparsity would strengthen the case.
- Scalability: training one forest per unseen class and maintaining DV(n) at every node may be fine at K≈10, but it is unclear how this scales to hundreds of unseen classes. Complexity and runtime are not reported; a discussion or empirical scaling study is warranted.
- Reproducibility gaps: important details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., signature uncertainty model, handling continuous/discovered attributes, exact sampling of thresholds). For a reader to reproduce results, more of these should be in the main paper. Also, calibration choices for Platt scaling (held-out vs in-sample) should be specified.
- Domain shift: the ROC is estimated from “seen” classes. The paper notes this and shows an “upper bound” using true ROC on unseen-class images, but does not attempt to mitigate the domain shift (e.g., by stratifying DV to match unseen signatures, or by reporting performance as a function of the validation–test attribute distribution mismatch). The gains may be sensitive to this shift.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with a coherent objective and sensible ablations. However, concerns about ROC estimation leakage and the narrow set of baselines weaken the empirical support. No theoretical guarantees are provided.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The main algorithmic idea is easy to follow. Some crucial implementation details are pushed to the supplement; bringing the signature-uncertainty formulation and threshold-sampling scheme into the main text would help reproducibility.

Originality
The idea of handling uncertainty in trees via fractional propagation relates to fuzzy decision trees and handling missing/uncertain features, but its application to zero-shot learning on class signatures with node-specific ROCs is novel and interesting. The few-shot interpolation via information gain is straightforward.

Significance
If validated against stronger baselines and with proper held-out ROC estimation, the approach could be a practical improvement for attribute-based zero-/few-shot recognition, a setting still relevant where human-interpretable descriptions are required. As presented, the significance is promising but not yet convincingly established beyond DAP.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify the ROC estimation protocol. Is DV excluded from fitting the SVMs/Platt scaling? If not, please redo with a proper hold-out or cross-validated ROC.
- Add comparisons to established zero-shot baselines beyond DAP on the same splits (e.g., ALE/ESZSL/DeViSE, and hierarchy/metric-transfer methods), and to a simple embedding method that uses attributes as label embeddings.
- Report computational cost and scaling with K and M; discuss or show results for larger K.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: effect of (#m,#t) per node, tree depth, and λ; show robustness to Platt calibration choices.
- Move the signature-uncertainty formulation and continuous-attribute handling into the main text.
- Report calibration metrics (e.g., Brier score) for class posteriors, and correlation between per-attribute AUC and attribute usage in learned trees.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
- Simple, general method; good ablations and controlled noise studies.
- Consistent improvements over DAP on three datasets; useful few-shot extension.

Cons
- Possible evaluation bias in ROC estimation; needs a clean protocol.
- Missing comparisons to stronger, established zero-shot baselines.
- Limited analysis of scalability, sensitivity, and theoretical properties; key details in supplement.
