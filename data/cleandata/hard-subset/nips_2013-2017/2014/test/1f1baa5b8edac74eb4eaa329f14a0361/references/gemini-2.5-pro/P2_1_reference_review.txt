### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a critical and often overlooked problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of the underlying attribute classifiers. The standard ZSL pipeline first predicts an image's attributes and then classifies the image into an unseen category based on the similarity between the predicted attributes and the predefined attribute signatures of the unseen classes. The authors rightly argue that this pipeline is flawed because it treats attribute predictions as ground truth, even though they are often very noisy.

The core contribution is a novel random forest framework for ZSL that explicitly models and accounts for attribute classifier uncertainty during training. Instead of training on images, the random forest is trained directly on the attribute signatures of the unseen classes. The key innovation is in the node splitting process. Rather than making a hard decision, each class signature is propagated *fractionally* down the tree. The proportion sent to each child node is determined by the attribute classifier's expected performance (i.e., its ROC curve, estimated on a held-out validation set of attribute-labeled images). This process effectively bakes robustness to attribute prediction errors directly into the structure of the decision trees. The framework is also naturally extended to the few-shot setting.

This work is well-situated within the ZSL literature. It directly confronts a major weakness of prominent attribute-based methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8, 9] and its variants [3, 18], which do not model classifier error. While some work has side-stepped this issue by learning non-semantic, "discoverable" attributes [22, 30], this paper's approach retains the key advantage of semantic attributes: the ability for a human to define new classes using language. The technique of fractional propagation in decision trees is related to methods for handling missing values [21] or probabilistic features [28], but the authors correctly distinguish their work by noting the unique ZSL setting where training is on clean signatures and testing is on noisy predictions, and by incorporating conditional ROC estimation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is sound, well-motivated, and the mathematical formulation for the fractional propagation and the modified information gain criterion (Eq. 5) is clear and correct. The experimental evaluation is particularly strong and serves as a model for thoroughness.
1.  **Controlled Experiments:** The synthetic noise experiments (Fig. 1) are an excellent inclusion. They provide clear, intuitive evidence that the proposed mechanism works as intended, showing that the performance gap widens as attribute reliability decreases, especially when noise levels vary across attributes. This directly validates the core hypothesis.
2.  **Thorough Ablation:** The comparison against "SIGNATURE-RF" effectively isolates the benefit of modeling unreliability. The further ablations ("OURS W/O ROC PROP") demonstrate the value of estimating ROC statistics locally within tree nodes, which helps account for attribute dependencies.
3.  **Strong Baselines and Benchmarks:** The method is evaluated on three standard and diverse datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) and shows significant improvements over the widely-used and strong DAP baseline.
4.  **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete piece of work, including a logical extension to the few-shot learning scenario and demonstrating the method's applicability to both semantic and discovered attributes.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of first establishing the problem, then building their solution from a simple base case (Signature RF) to the full, more complex model. The logic behind fractional propagation based on ROC statistics is explained with a clear example ("elephant" being "gray"). The structure is logical, and the figures and tables are informative and well-designed. The writing provides sufficient detail for an expert to grasp the core concepts and implementation details.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the problem of noisy mid-level representations is known, this paper is the first to propose a solution that integrates an explicit model of classifier error directly into the zero-shot training phase. The specific idea of training a random forest on class signatures while using validation data to guide fractional splits based on expected classifier performance is novel. It represents a clever and previously unexplored way to bridge the gap between the clean, idealized world of attribute signatures (training) and the noisy reality of attribute predictions (testing). This is a significant conceptual advance over existing ZSL methods.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is very significant. Attribute-based ZSL is an elegant paradigm, but its practical utility has been hampered by the poor performance of attribute classifiers. This work provides a direct and effective solution to this bottleneck.
1.  **Practical Impact:** The substantial performance gains over DAP on standard benchmarks demonstrate that this is not just an incremental improvement. It makes attribute-based ZSL a much more viable and robust approach. The authors make a powerful point that "modeling how good/bad the attribute classifiers are is even more important than having better attribute classifiers."
2.  **Broader Applicability:** The core idea of training a model that is robust to the errors of its constituent, pre-trained components could be influential beyond ZSL. As the authors suggest, this could be applied to other domains using noisy mid-level concepts, such as action recognition from poselets.
3.  **Advancing the State of the Art:** By achieving state-of-the-art results on AwA using both named and discovered attributes, the paper clearly demonstrates its ability to advance the field. Researchers are highly likely to build upon this work.

There are no significant weaknesses. One minor point is that the method requires a separate set of attribute-labeled data for validation to estimate the ROC curves. However, this is a reasonable requirement, and the paper shows that even when borrowing this data from the attribute training set (and thus slightly weakening the attribute classifiers), the net result is a large performance gain. This trade-off is clearly worthwhile.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-defined, important, and practical problem in zero-shot learning.
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and technically sound solution.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The experimental validation is comprehensive, rigorous, and convincing, demonstrating significant improvements over a strong and widely-used baseline (DAP).
*   The findings are significant and likely to have a strong impact on future research in attribute-based recognition and potentially other areas using mid-level representations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   I can find no compelling arguments against accepting this paper. It is a strong contribution in every respect.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that I would enthusiastically recommend for acceptance. It is a model of good empirical research: identifying a clear problem, proposing a creative and sound solution, and backing it up with thorough and convincing experiments.
