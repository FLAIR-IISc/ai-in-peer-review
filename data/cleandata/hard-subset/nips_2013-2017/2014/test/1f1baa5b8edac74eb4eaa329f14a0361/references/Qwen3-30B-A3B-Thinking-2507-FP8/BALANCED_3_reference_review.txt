

**Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"**

**Summary**  
This paper addresses a critical gap in zero-shot learning (ZSL): the assumption of perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic in practice. The authors propose a random forest-based ZSL method that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier unreliability by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) during tree training. The approach extends to handle class signature uncertainty and few-shot learning. It builds on prior work like DAP [8] and signature-based ZSL [3, 18], but differs by modeling attribute prediction errors rather than treating them as ideal. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines, including DAP (the leading ZSL method).

**Strengths**  
*Quality:* The technical contribution is sound and well-motivated. The ROC-guided tree training (Sec. 3.2.2) is a novel adaptation of random forests to ZSL’s uncertainty, with rigorous ablation studies (e.g., Table 1 isolates the impact of ROC propagation and signature uncertainty). Experiments are thorough: controlled noise tests validate the method’s core intuition (Fig. 1), and real-data results show significant gains over DAP (e.g., +10.2% on "giant panda" in AwA). The few-shot extension (Sec. 3.3) is practical and well-integrated.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized. Key concepts (e.g., fractional signature propagation) are explained step-by-step with clear equations and pseudocode. The ablation studies and dataset descriptions are detailed enough for reproducibility.  
*Originality:* The core idea—using attribute classifiers’ ROC curves to guide tree splits—has not been explored in ZSL. While related to fractional tuples for missing values [21], the zero-shot setting (where training uses signatures, not images) and simultaneous handling of attribute unreliability *and* signature uncertainty make this distinct. The method also generalizes to discovered attributes [30], broadening applicability.  
*Significance:* This work directly addresses a major barrier to practical ZSL: unreliable attributes. The results (Table 1, Fig. 2b) show that modeling uncertainty is more impactful than improving attribute classifiers alone. The few-shot extension further enhances real-world utility, as it leverages minimal labeled data. The method sets a new state-of-the-art on AwA (even with discovered attributes), advancing ZSL beyond "proof-of-concept" status.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality:* The method relies on reserved attribute-labeled validation data (20% of the attribute training set) to estimate ROC curves. However, this data is drawn from *seen* classes (not unseen), which may not generalize to unseen classes’ attribute distributions. The "upper bound" (OURS+TRUE ROC) uses unseen class images for validation—unrealistic in practice—and slightly overstates the method’s potential. The few-shot parameter λ is tuned via cross-validation (Fig. 2a), but the paper lacks guidance on selecting λ in deployment (e.g., how to estimate it without labeled unseen data).  
*Clarity:* The "signature uncertainty" component (Sec. 3.2.3) is mentioned but underdeveloped in the main text (details are in supp.). This risks confusing readers about its necessity relative to attribute unreliability. The noise model in Sec. 4.1 (Fig. 1) could be more explicit (e.g., how noise scales per attribute).  
*Originality:* The connection to [28] (handling probabilistic features) is acknowledged but not deeply contrasted. While the zero-shot setting is distinct, the paper could better clarify why prior work on probabilistic features (e.g., [28]) does not directly apply.  
*Significance:* The paper claims ZSL’s "elegance need not be abandoned," but the method’s complexity (requiring ROC estimation and tree retraining) may offset this benefit. Real-world impact could be strengthened by discussing deployment challenges (e.g., latency of ROC-based trees vs. DAP).

**Recommendation**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a strong, technically sound contribution to ZSL by addressing a fundamental practical limitation. The empirical results are convincing, and the method is both novel and impactful. The weaknesses are manageable with minor revisions (see suggestions below).

**Suggestions for Improvement**  
1. **Clarify validation data limitations:** Discuss how the *seen*-class validation data might misestimate unseen-class attribute errors (e.g., "We note that DV’s representativeness of unseen classes is a key assumption; future work could explore domain adaptation for DV").  
2. **Expand few-shot discussion:** Add a sentence on λ selection in practice (e.g., "For deployment, λ could be estimated via meta-learning across seen classes" or "We observe λ ≈ 0.3–0.5 is robust across datasets").  
3. **Strengthen signature uncertainty explanation:** Briefly justify why signature uncertainty (vs. attribute unreliability alone) is necessary in the main text (e.g., "Class-level attribute variations, e.g., 'whale' having gray *or* black variants, require explicit modeling").  
4. **Contextualize real-world impact:** Add a short paragraph on how this method enables applications (e.g., "For a wildlife monitoring system, this allows adding 'new species' classifiers via attribute descriptions without retraining from scratch").  

The paper’s core contribution—modeling attribute unreliability as a *training-time* constraint—is a significant step toward robust ZSL. With minor clarifications, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
