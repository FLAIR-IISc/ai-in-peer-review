

# Peer Review: "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical limitation in attribute-based zero-shot learning: the assumption that attribute predictions are reliable. While existing methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) [8] treat attribute classifiers as perfect, the authors demonstrate that this assumption is unrealistic and leads to poor performance in practice. They propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly accounts for attribute unreliability by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers during training. The key innovation is propagating training signatures through the tree according to the estimated true/false positive rates of attribute classifiers, rather than assuming perfect predictions. The method also handles signature uncertainty and extends naturally to few-shot learning. The authors validate their approach on three challenging datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

This work builds on the standard attribute-based zero-shot learning framework [3, 8, 18, 23] but directly addresses a major practical limitation that has prevented this approach from being widely adopted. Unlike related work on handling missing features in decision trees [21, 28], this paper applies the concept to the zero-shot learning domain where the training and test distributions differ fundamentally.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Significance**: The paper correctly identifies and addresses a crucial gap in zero-shot learning—attribute unreliability—which has been overlooked in prior work. The authors convincingly demonstrate that even minor attribute prediction errors severely degrade performance of existing methods.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The proposed method is theoretically well-founded and carefully implemented. The ROC-based propagation mechanism is elegant and directly addresses the core problem. The ablation studies (Table 1) clearly isolate the contributions of different components (ROC propagation vs. signature uncertainty).

3. **Experimental Rigor**: The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines (DAP, SIGNATURE-RF) across three diverse datasets. The controlled noise experiments (Figure 1) provide valuable insight into why the method works. The few-shot extension (Figure 2a) is particularly compelling as a practical application.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper shows that modeling attribute unreliability can significantly outperform methods that simply use more attribute-labeled data. The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on AwA using both named and discovered attributes.

## Weaknesses

1. **Validation Data Assumption**: The method relies on a separate validation set to estimate attribute classifier error characteristics. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't adequately address how to handle cases where validation data doesn't generalize to unseen classes. The "upper bound" (OURS+TRUE ROC) in Table 1 suggests this is a significant limitation.

2. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't discuss how the method scales to very large attribute vocabularies or large numbers of unseen classes. The experimental setup uses relatively modest numbers of attributes (M=65-102) and unseen classes (K=10-12), which may not reflect real-world zero-shot scenarios.

3. **Implementation Details**: Some technical aspects could be clarified, particularly regarding the validation data propagation and how the attribute classifiers' error characteristics are estimated at each node. The supplementary material mentions "pseudocode" but doesn't include it in the main paper.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with strong experimental validation. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and limitations (e.g., the validation data assumption, scalability concerns). The results are well-supported, and the ablation studies provide clear evidence for the method's components. The approach is a complete piece of work, not just a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The main idea is easy to follow, and the mathematical formulation is precise. However, some details about the validation data propagation could be clarified, and the supplementary material could be referenced more explicitly in the main text.

**Originality**: The core idea of explicitly modeling attribute unreliability through ROC-based propagation is novel in the context of zero-shot learning. While related to work on handling missing values in decision trees [21, 28], this application to zero-shot learning with fundamentally different training/test distributions represents a significant conceptual advance. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior contributions.

**Significance**: This is a significant contribution to zero-shot learning. The paper demonstrates that attribute-based zero-shot learning can be practical and effective when accounting for attribute unreliability—a key barrier to adoption. The results show clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods, and the few-shot extension provides a practical pathway to real-world applications. The work has the potential to revitalize interest in attribute-based approaches for zero-shot learning.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by addressing a critical practical limitation of existing methods. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results are compelling. While the validation data assumption is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and provide valuable insights into the method's behavior. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.

**Pro Acceptance**:
- Addresses a significant, overlooked problem in zero-shot learning
- Novel approach that directly handles attribute unreliability
- Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets
- Clear theoretical foundation with practical implications

**Con Acceptance**:
- Validation data assumption requires careful consideration
- Scalability to larger attribute/class spaces not thoroughly explored
- Some implementation details could be clearer

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The work advances the state of the art in zero-shot learning and provides a practical solution to a significant problem. I recommend acceptance.
