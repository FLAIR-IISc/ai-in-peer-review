- Paper addresses zero-shot recognition by training random forests over class attribute signatures while explicitly modeling attribute classifier errors.  
- Each tree split tests an attribute threshold and uses node-specific ROC estimates to fractionally propagate class signatures left/right.  
- Fractional propagation yields soft counts and an information gain that favors splits both discriminative and predictable given attribute noise.  
- Method extends to handle annotation/signature uncertainty by further softening propagation.  
- Few-shot extension blends signature-based and image-based information gain via a convex weight λ, treating signatures as a prior.  
- Evaluated on AwA, aPY, and SUN with controlled-noise experiments, showing improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline.  
- Ablation studies confirm benefits from ROC-based propagation and signature-uncertainty modeling; results competitive with discovered mid-level features.  
- Novelty lies in training on signatures with node-specific ROC conditioning, distinct from fuzzy trees and existing zero-shot methods.  
- Strength: principled, intuitive mechanism that down-weights unlearnable attributes and chooses informative operating points under expected errors.  
- Strength: clear extensions and solid empirical evaluation across datasets and noise settings.  
- Main weakness: central assumption that ROC statistics from seen classes transfer to unseen classes (domain shift risk).  
- Scalability concern: training and test-time cost scale linearly with number of unseen classes (one forest per class).  
- Attribute correlation not fully addressed since splits test single attributes; oblique or joint tests might be needed for entangled attributes.  
- Few-shot blending is heuristic; selection of λ lacks theoretical grounding or principled selection under no unseen-labels.  
- Potential data-leakage/clarity issue: ROC estimation protocol needs to avoid optimistic bias if SVMs are retrained on pooled data.  
- Evaluation breadth limited: main quantitative baselines are DAP and Signature-RF; comparisons to embedding/hierarchical methods under same features are missing.  
- Quality: technically sound derivation and consistent ablations, but lacks analysis of sensitivity to validation set size, attribute rarity, and complexity/memory costs.  
- Clarity: generally clear, but recommends detailing ROC estimation protocol, summarizing pseudo-code in main paper, and clarifying accuracy metrics and chance levels.  
- Significance: addresses practical barrier of unreliable attributes and likely to influence zero-/few-shot work reliant on mid-level semantics.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept (weak to moderate); promising contribution but needs clarifications on ROC estimation, broader baselines, and scalability.
