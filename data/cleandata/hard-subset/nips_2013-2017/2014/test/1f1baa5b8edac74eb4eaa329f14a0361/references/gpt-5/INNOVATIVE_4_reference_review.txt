Summary
The paper tackles a core limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): attribute predictors are unreliable. The authors propose a zero-shot random forest that is trained not on images but on unseen-class attribute signatures, while explicitly modeling attribute prediction error. The key idea is to use attribute-classifier ROC statistics, estimated from held-out attribute-labeled validation data, to fractionally propagate each class signature down decision trees, thereby computing node-dependent expected information gain under realistic test-time attribute noise. The method naturally prefers discriminative and learnable attributes and operating points, and avoids splits that would be brittle given the attribute errors. The approach is extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to few-shot learning via a combined information gain that treats signatures as a prior (weighted by λ) alongside limited labeled examples. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with controlled noise studies, ablations, and results using discovered pseudo-attributes.

Relation to prior work
Most attribute-based ZSL methods (DAP and variants, similarity-based matching to signatures, topic/embedding models) take attribute predictions at face value; this paper is the first to build the error model into the learning algorithm itself by optimizing tree splits w.r.t. ROC-aware fractional propagation. While fractional instance propagation resembles treatments of missing/uncertain features in decision trees, the adaptation to the zero-shot regime (training on class signatures, test-time traversal with attribute predictions, node-conditional ROC estimation) is novel and appropriate. The few-shot extension is a clean, principled way to inject descriptive priors into RF training.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Training forests on signatures while accounting for attribute unreliability via node-specific ROCs is an elegant and bold idea that directly targets a known failure mode of attribute-based ZSL.
- Soundness: The redefinition of information gain with fractional counts is coherent; the node-conditional ROC computation captures attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Practicality: No need for unseen-class images; can use any attribute vocabulary (named or discovered). The few-shot extension with a tunable prior is simple and useful.
- Empirical validation: Improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF across three datasets; controlled noise experiments convincingly isolate the benefit of modeling unreliability; ablations (signature uncertainty, node-conditional ROC) show additive gains. Using only 80% of attribute-labeled data yet outperforming DAP underscores robustness.
- Insight: The method prefers both reliable attributes and good thresholds, and effectively ignores unlearnable attributes (TPR≈FPR), matching intuition.

Weaknesses
- Baselines: The comparisons focus heavily on DAP and a signature RF. Given the topic, stronger baselines from semantic/label-embedding ZSL (e.g., ALE/Akata, DeViSE, ESZSL, ConSE) should be included on aPY/SUN as well as AwA for a fuller picture. The text mentions published results but detailed side-by-side numbers are limited.
- Scalability: One-vs-all forests imply training K ensembles; complexity and memory footprint vs. K, M, and tree depth are not reported. Discussion of training/inference wall-time would help.
- Dependence on validation distribution: The approach relies on ROCs estimated from seen-class attribute data; if unseen classes induce significant covariate shift in attribute appearance, node-conditional ROC estimates may miscalibrate. While authors show an “upper bound” with true ROCs, strategies to mitigate shift (e.g., domain-adaptive calibration, focusing DV by signature similarity) merit deeper exploration.
- Limited analysis of calibration: Results are reported in accuracy; assessing posterior calibration (e.g., ECE), top-k, and generalized ZSL (seen+unseen) would strengthen claims of practical utility.
- Some details deferred to supp.: Pseudocode, handling of signature uncertainty, continuous-valued signatures, and candidate split sampling could be summarized more fully in the main text.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. The fractional propagation and information gain derivations are correct and align with how RFs accumulate counts. The few-shot objective is straightforward and effective. Claims are supported by experiments and ablations, though broader baselines and runtime analysis would improve completeness.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The intuition behind fractional propagation is nicely explained. Minor suggestions: include a concise algorithm box; clarify the strategy when few positives/negatives reach a node (reverting to global DV) and its effect on bias; provide more detail on the signature uncertainty model in the main text.

Originality
High. Incorporating measured attribute unreliability into the training objective for zero-shot category models, with node-conditional ROC-driven fractional trees, is a fresh and impactful idea that goes beyond simple confidence weighting or post-hoc thresholding.

Significance
High. Attribute-based ZSL remains attractive due to human interpretability; this work directly addresses a key blocker, yielding sizable gains when attributes are noisy (the common case). The descriptive prior perspective for few-shot RFs is broadly applicable. I expect others to build on this, including extensions to generalized ZSL and other semantic mid-levels (poselets, parts).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Please add comparisons on aPY and SUN to stronger embedding baselines (ALE/DeViSE/ESZSL) if feasible.
- Report training/inference times and memory vs. K, M, tree depth; discuss prospects for multi-label forests to avoid K ensembles.
- Analyze sensitivity to the size/composition of DV and to Platt calibration quality; could isotonic regression or temperature scaling help?
- Evaluate generalized ZSL (seen+unseen) and top-5 metrics; discuss posterior calibration.
- Can you exploit uncertainty estimates of attribute correlations (beyond node-conditional conditioning) without exploding the split space?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a novel, well-executed idea that materially advances attribute-based zero-shot and few-shot learning by embracing attribute unreliability in the learning algorithm. Despite some missing baselines and runtime analysis, the conceptual contribution and empirical gains justify acceptance.

Confidence
High (4/5).
