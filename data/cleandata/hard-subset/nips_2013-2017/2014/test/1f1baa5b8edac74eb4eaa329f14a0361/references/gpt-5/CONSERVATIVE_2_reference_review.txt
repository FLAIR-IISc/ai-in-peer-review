Summary
The paper addresses zero-shot recognition when mid-level semantic attributes are predicted unreliably. It proposes training one-vs-all random forests directly on class-attribute signatures, but crucially modifies tree training to account for attribute classifier error characteristics. Using a held-out attribute-labeled validation set, the method estimates per-attribute ROC statistics and fractionally propagates class signatures down the tree according to node-specific TP/FP rates. This biases split selection toward attributes and thresholds that are both discriminative among unseen classes and reliably predictable. The approach is further extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations and to a few-shot regime by combining signature-driven information gain with standard data-driven gain via a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show gains over DAP and over a signature-only RF, with ablations and controlled-noise studies.

Relation to prior work
The paper is situated in attribute-based zero-shot recognition (Farhadi et al.; Lampert et al. DAP). It contrasts with work that learns semantic/feature embeddings (e.g., label embedding, DeViSE) or transfers along taxonomies, and with work discovering non-semantic mid-level features. The decision-tree fractional propagation idea relates to handling missing/uncertain features in trees (Quinlan; Olaru & Wehenkel; Tsang et al.). The main claimed novelty is incorporating attribute unreliability via ROC-aware soft propagation of signatures during tree construction and combining this with few-shot data.

Strengths
- Clearly motivated problem: attribute unreliability is a known Achilles’ heel of attribute-based zero-shot methods.
- Conceptually simple and implementable approach that leverages well-understood machinery (random forests, ROC curves).
- Sensible criterion: node-specific ROC-informed soft propagation captures test-time behavior better than assuming perfect attributes.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over DAP and a naïve signature RF on three datasets; controlled-noise experiments are informative.
- Few-shot extension is natural and practical; ablation studies help isolate contributions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited baselines. DAP and a signature RF are relatively weak comparators by current standards. Although the authors cite label/feature embedding methods and hierarchical transfer, they are not consistently compared across datasets (Figure 2b only for AwA; details sparse). Given the community’s move beyond DAP, stronger baselines (e.g., label embedding, DeViSE-like models, hierarchical/neighborhood transfer) are needed for a convincing case.
- Dependence on validation ROCs. The approach hinges on DV(n)-specific ROC estimates. These can be high-variance when few positives/negatives reach a node; the fallback to global DV partially addresses this but introduces instability and potential bias toward early splits. There is no analysis of sensitivity to the size/quality of DV, nor to calibration errors in Platt scaling.
- Potential mismatch/data reuse. The paper states that 20% is reserved for ROC estimation and then pooled with the remaining 80% to train final attribute classifiers. This implies the ROC estimates are obtained with attribute classifiers trained on only 80%, whereas test-time predictions come from classifiers trained on 100%. This distribution shift could invalidate the assumed error model. Clarify the sequence and ensure no leakage or mismatch.
- Scope and robustness. On SUN, unseen classes are “randomly selected”; this risks cherry-picking. Standardized splits or reporting over multiple disjoint splits would improve credibility. Also, the method is only evaluated on 10–12 unseen classes; scalability to larger K is untested.
- Theoretical grounding. The method is intuitive, but there is no formal analysis (e.g., expected risk reduction vs. naïve trees, consistency under certain assumptions). Some theoretical insight or bounds would strengthen the claim that the ROC-aware criterion is well-founded.
- Few-shot comparisons. The few-shot extension is reasonable, but comparisons are limited to a baseline trained only on DT. Missing are simple and competitive alternatives: (i) learning on attribute predictions with regularization, (ii) DAP or label embedding fine-tuned with few labeled images, (iii) transductive/self-training variants.
- Inter-attribute dependencies are not modeled beyond data subsetting; correlated attributes are common in practice, yet the paper defers this to future work.

Questions for the authors
- Clarify the training timeline: are ROCs computed using classifiers trained on 80% and then final attribute classifiers retrained on 100%? If so, how does this mismatch affect performance? If not, is there leakage from using DV both to calibrate ROC and to train attribute classifiers?
- How sensitive is performance to the size of DV and to Platt calibration quality? Any results with isotonic regression or uncalibrated margins?
- How are thresholds sampled per node (#t) and how is this parameter tuned? Is there a risk of overfitting via threshold search on DV(n)?
- For SUN and aPY, can you compare against at least one embedding-based zero-shot method under the same features to strengthen the case?
- What is the computational cost as K grows (100 trees per unseen class) and how does it compare to DAP/embeddings?

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound at a high level, with clear algorithmic steps and appropriate ablations. However, reliance on node-specific ROC with limited validation data raises concerns about variance and overfitting; stronger comparative baselines and sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Some key details (signature uncertainty model, pseudocode) are deferred to the supplement; bringing the essentials into the main paper would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The combination of signature-trained trees with ROC-aware soft propagation for zero-shot is novel relative to classical attribute-based ZSL. That said, it is an incremental adaptation of established tools rather than a fundamentally new framework.
- Significance: Improvements over DAP are encouraging, but the impact is tempered by limited comparisons to stronger, established zero-shot baselines and lack of large-scale/scalability evidence.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real limitation of attribute-based ZSL with a practical, interpretable method.
- Consistent gains over standard baselines across multiple datasets and useful ablations.
- Few-shot extension is straightforward and potentially useful.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong contemporary baselines; improvements may not hold against label/feature embedding methods.
- Potential methodological fragility due to ROC estimation and validation data choices.
- Limited analysis of scalability, sensitivity, and theoretical underpinnings.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and likely useful in practice, but the experimental evidence is not yet rigorous enough against established alternatives, and some methodological details need clarification. Strengthening the baseline comparisons, clarifying the ROC/training protocol, providing sensitivity analyses, and reporting results on standardized splits would substantially improve the paper.
