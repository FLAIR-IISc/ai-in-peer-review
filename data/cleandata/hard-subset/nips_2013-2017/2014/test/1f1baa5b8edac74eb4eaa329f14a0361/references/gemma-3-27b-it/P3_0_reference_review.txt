## Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: the inherent unreliability of attribute predictors. The authors argue that existing methods treat attribute predictions as ground truth, which hinders performance in practice. They propose a novel random forest approach that explicitly models attribute uncertainty during training, leveraging receiver operating characteristics to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. The method is further generalized to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and extended to the few-shot setting. Empirical results on three datasets demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, validating the benefits of accounting for attribute unreliability.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the established framework of attribute-based zero-shot learning (e.g., Lampert et al. '09, Farhadi et al. '09, Rohrbach et al. '13), which utilizes semantic attributes to define unseen classes.  It differentiates itself by explicitly addressing a known weakness of this approach – the noisiness of attribute predictions – a point also acknowledged by others (e.g., Jayaraman et al. '15, but not addressed with a solution).  The use of random forests is also not novel in ZSL (e.g., Palatucci et al. '09), but the proposed modifications for handling attribute uncertainty and incorporating ROC curves are a significant contribution. The work acknowledges and contrasts itself with alternative ZSL strategies like embedding-based approaches (Frome et al. '13) and knowledge transfer methods (Mensink et al. '14), highlighting the advantages of attribute-based methods for human interpretability and control. The connection to related work on handling missing/uncertain data in decision trees (Friedman & Popescu '08, Tsang et al. '01) is appropriately noted, but the authors clearly articulate the distinct challenges and adaptations required by the zero-shot setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper identifies and rigorously addresses a major practical bottleneck in attribute-based zero-shot learning – the inherent unreliability of attributes. This is a significant contribution, as it moves the field closer to real-world applicability.
* **Novel Methodology:** The proposed approach of incorporating ROC curves into the random forest training process is innovative and intuitively sound. It allows the model to learn to rely more on reliable attributes and avoid being misled by noisy predictions.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The results across three diverse datasets consistently demonstrate substantial improvements over strong baselines, including the widely used DAP method. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of the different components of the proposed method.
* **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup.  The inclusion of controlled experiments with injected noise is particularly valuable for demonstrating the robustness of the approach.
* **Few-Shot Extension:** The extension to the few-shot setting provides additional practical relevance and demonstrates the versatility of the proposed approach.
* **Connection to Cognitive Science:** The opening discussion linking attribute-based recognition to human cognitive processes grounds the work in a broader theoretical context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:**  Training the random forest with the ROC-informed splitting and validation data propagation likely introduces significant computational overhead compared to simpler ZSL methods. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to hyperparameters like tree depth and the number of tests per node (mentioned briefly, but more detailed analysis would be helpful).  A more thorough sensitivity analysis might be warranted.
* **Validation Data Dependence:** While the use of validation data to estimate ROC curves is clever, the performance seems to depend on having a representative validation set. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more in-depth discussion of the impact of the validation set size and composition would be beneficial.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on overall performance gains. A more detailed examination of the types of examples where the method *still* fails would provide valuable insights for future research.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practical limitation of existing ZSL methods.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated methodology.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple datasets.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Offers a promising direction for future research in ZSL.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Potential computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity require further investigation.
* Limited analysis of failure cases.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning.  The proposed approach is both innovative and practical, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10)

**Recommendations:**

* Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method.
* Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters.
* Provide a more in-depth analysis of failure cases to identify areas for future improvement.
* Briefly discuss the potential for adapting the method to other mid-level representations beyond attributes.
