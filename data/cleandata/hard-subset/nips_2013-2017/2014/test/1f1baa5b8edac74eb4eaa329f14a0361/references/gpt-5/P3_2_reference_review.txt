Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition via attributes when attribute predictors are unreliable. The core idea is to train one-vs-all random forests directly on class-level attribute signatures rather than on images, and to inject attribute unreliability into the tree-growing procedure. Specifically, at each node, the method uses ROC estimates for each attribute classifier (obtained from a held-out attribute-labeled validation set DV) to fractionally propagate class signatures to left/right child nodes according to the true/false positive rates at a chosen threshold. This yields an information gain objective that prefers splits that both discriminate classes and are predictable given expected attribute errors. The method also adds a term for class-signature uncertainty (annotation noise), and extends to few-shot learning by combining signature-based information gain and standard (image-based) information gain with a weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a “signature-only” RF, with ablations underscoring the importance of ROC-based propagation and signature-uncertainty modeling. Controlled-noise studies further illustrate the benefits when attribute reliability varies by attribute.

Quality
- Soundness: The fractional propagation using per-node ROC statistics is a principled way to fold test-time attribute noise into training. The reformulated information gain with soft counts is consistent with RF training under uncertainty. The few-shot extension is straightforward and sensible.
- Empirics: The evaluation spans three standard datasets, includes ablations, a controlled-noise study, and comparisons to strong baselines (DAP, signature RF). The reported gains are meaningful, especially in settings with fewer positive attribute examples.
- Caveats:
  - Domain shift and ROC estimation: DV contains seen-class images, while test images belong to unseen classes. Although you acknowledge this, the method’s effectiveness hinges on how well node-conditional ROC statistics transfer. The “OURS+TRUE ROC” upper bound is informative, but further analysis of how ROC quality degrades with mismatch would strengthen the claims (e.g., sensitivity to size/composition of DV, importance weighting toward seen classes with signatures closest to unseen).
  - Possible optimism in ROC estimation: Section 4 suggests reserving 20% for DV and then pooling DV with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. Please clarify whether ROC curves are computed using predictions from models trained without DV (to avoid using training predictions for ROC), or whether a separate calibration is used. Using training scores to estimate ROC can be optimistic.
  - Attribute dependence: While recursively subsetting DV captures dependencies induced by earlier splits, attribute predictors can be correlated even conditioned on earlier tests. The current node-wise ROC approach remains marginal per attribute. Empirical evidence is positive, but a discussion or experiment quantifying robustness to inter-attribute correlation would be useful.
  - Scalability: The one-vs-all forest per unseen class may be heavy when K is large. A single multi-class forest trained with the same fractional-propagation principle could reduce inference cost; a complexity/runtime report would help.
  - Threshold sampling: The choice of (#m, #t) and how thresholds t are sampled (e.g., quantiles of validation scores) is important for stability and speed; more detail and sensitivity plots would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The core mechanism (fractional propagation guided by node-conditional ROC) is well motivated. Some crucial details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode; signature-uncertainty model), which slightly harms reproducibility. Please bring the signature-uncertainty formulation into the main text succinctly, and clarify the exact protocol for ROC estimation and threshold sampling.

Originality
Training decision forests on class signatures with fractional propagation driven by ROC statistics is novel in the zero-shot setting. The connection to handling missing/probabilistic features in trees is apt, but your scenario differs materially (training on signatures; testing on attribute predictions). The few-shot extension as a descriptive prior in tree learning is a natural but useful contribution.

Significance
The work addresses a key pain point in attribute-based zero-shot learning—unreliable attribute predictors—and shows clear gains over widely used DAP-style methods on multiple datasets. The idea is practical and likely to influence follow-up work, including applications to other mid-level semantic cues (poselets, mid-level patches). The controlled-noise analysis is insightful.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean way to incorporate attribute unreliability via per-node ROCs and soft propagation.
- Strong empirical results and ablations; improvements largest when attributes are less reliable.
- Few-shot extension with an attribute-signature prior yields additional gains and behaves as expected with varying λ and data size.
- Discusses applicability to discovered (pseudo)attributes and continuous signatures.

Weaknesses and questions
- Reliance on DV from seen classes for ROC estimation: quantify the impact of DV composition and size; consider domain adaptation/calibration (e.g., Bayesian treatment of TPR/FPR with Beta priors, confidence intervals, or covariate shift correction).
- Clarify potential optimism in ROC estimation if DV is also used to train attribute classifiers; ideally use held-out predictions or cross-fitting for ROC.
- Attribute dependence is only partially handled; could oblique/multi-attribute splits or pairwise modeling further help?
- Scalability to many unseen classes; inference/training time not reported.
- Additional baselines: a DAP variant with per-attribute reliability weighting or calibrated thresholds; cost-sensitive or Bayesian DAP; a single multi-class forest trained on signatures; label-embedding or DeViSE-style methods adapted to attribute signatures where applicable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide details/sensitivity on threshold sampling, (#m,#t), tree depth, and runtime.
- Include the signature-uncertainty formulation in the main text and analyze its contribution more thoroughly.
- Add experiments varying DV size/composition; try cross-fitted ROC estimation to avoid optimism.
- Report per-class accuracies and confusion to illustrate which attributes/splits drive gains.
- Explore a multi-class forest alternative and/or report memory/inference cost.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a principled, effective method.
- Solid empirical gains with careful ablations and analysis.
- Method is simple to implement and broadly applicable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on seen-class ROC estimates with limited analysis of domain shift.
- Some important implementation details deferred to the supplement; limited scalability discussion.
- Missing comparisons to reliability-aware DAP variants and more recent embedding baselines (where applicable).

Recommendation: Weak accept to accept. The methodological idea is novel and practical, and the empirical evidence is compelling; tightening the analysis of ROC estimation, detailing implementation, and discussing scalability would further strengthen the paper. Confidence: high.
