Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses zero-shot recognition, where categories are specified by attribute signatures rather than class-labeled training images. The central challenge identified is attribute unreliability: attribute classifiers are noisy and can have correlated errors, which degrades zero-shot performance. The paper proposes a random forest-based method that explicitly incorporates attribute unreliability during training. Key ideas:
- Build one-vs-all random forests for unseen classes using only class-attribute signatures as “training instances.”
- At each tree node, estimate attribute classifier ROC statistics (TPR/FPR at threshold t) using attribute-labeled validation data from seen classes passed down to that node.
- Fractionally propagate class signatures to child nodes according to the estimated ROC at the candidate split, redefining information gain in terms of fractional counts. This biases split selection toward attributes and thresholds that will remain discriminative under expected prediction errors.
- Extend to uncertain class-attribute signatures (annotation noise) and a few-shot setting by interpolating signature-based information gain with standard data-driven information gain.

The work is squarely within the attribute-based zero-shot tradition (e.g., Farhadi et al., Palatucci et al., DAP [Lampert et al.]). Unlike DAP and related two-stage methods that take attribute predictions at face value, this method models attribute unreliability explicitly in the learner. It also relates to decision tree methods for missing/uncertain features (fractional propagation, fuzzy trees; e.g., Olaru & Wehenkel, Tsang et al.) but differs in that training instances are class signatures, while validation images supply ROC-based uncertainty, and node-specific validation subsets capture dependencies induced by prior splits. The paper contrasts with non-attribute zero-shot methods that use hierarchies or embeddings (Mensink et al., Akata et al., DeViSE) by preserving the human-interpretable, attribute-based interface.

Strengths
- Technical novelty in adapting random forests to zero-shot learning with unreliable attributes via ROC-informed, fractional propagation of signatures and redefined information gain. The per-node ROC estimation and validation-data propagation address attribute dependencies induced by the tree structure.
- Empirical validation is thorough: three datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), hundreds of attributes, controlled noise studies, ablations (without signature uncertainty; without recursive ROC propagation), and comparisons to a strong baseline (DAP) and a signature-only RF. Gains are largest when attribute classifiers are less reliable, aligning with the stated motivation.
- Clear demonstration that modeling classifier unreliability matters more than marginal gains in attribute classifier quality: the method outperforms DAP even when using less data for initial attribute training.
- Generality: the approach works with named and discovered attributes, and has a natural few-shot extension via a principled interpolation of information gains.
- Clarity of the main algorithm: definitions of fractional indicators, node-specific ROC, and the modified information gain are precise.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on attribute-labeled validation data from seen classes. The method assumes that ROC characteristics on seen classes transfer to unseen classes; distribution shift can degrade calibration. The “OURS+TRUE ROC” upper bound is informative, but the paper would benefit from techniques to mitigate shift (e.g., domain adaptation or calibration transfer) or analysis of sensitivity to choice/size of DV and to the proportion reserved.
- Potential mismatch between ROC used for split selection and the final attribute classifiers, since the paper indicates retraining attribute classifiers on all data after reserving validation. Clarify whether ROC statistics are recomputed or whether a consistent set of predictors is used throughout.
- Comparisons mostly focus on DAP and ablations. Given the breadth of zero-shot literature, additional baselines using semantic/label embeddings (e.g., Akata et al., DeViSE) would strengthen significance claims, even if they use different supervision.
- Scalability: training one forest per unseen class yields O(K) forests; discuss computational complexity, tree construction cost (number of candidate (m, t) pairs, per-node ROC computation), and test-time throughput, especially for large K.
- Signature uncertainty is deferred to the supplement. Since it contributes to performance, a concise description in the main text would improve clarity.
- Multi-modal class signatures are mentioned as straightforward; an empirical example would be helpful to show practical impact.
- Attribute correlation beyond those induced by the path constraints is not explicitly modeled. While node-wise ROC on DV(n) captures some dependencies, discussing limitations and possible extensions (as noted in conclusion) would improve transparency.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The method appears technically sound, with a coherent objective and reasonable assumptions. Theoretical properties are not derived, but the formulation is well-motivated and empirically supported. Ablations and controlled noise experiments substantiate the claims.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic core is clear. Suggested clarifications include: training/validation split protocol for attribute SVMs vs. ROC estimation, computational complexity, and a brief in-paper description of the signature uncertainty extension.
- Originality: The application of ROC-aware, fractional propagation of class signatures within random forests for zero-shot learning is novel relative to prior attribute-based zero-shot methods and to uncertain-feature decision trees. The few-shot interpolation via information gains is a natural but useful extension.
- Significance: The reported gains over widely used DAP across three datasets and the robustness under attribute noise indicate practical impact for attribute-based zero-shot. The approach maintains semantic interpretability while addressing a core limitation of attributes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a well-motivated and persistent limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning with a principled method.
- Consistent, substantial empirical improvements over strong baselines across diverse datasets, with informative ablations and stress tests.
- Method is general, interpretable, and extendable to few-shot settings and discovered attributes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on attribute-labeled validation data and potential distribution shift may limit robustness in practice; limited analysis of this sensitivity.
- Missing comparisons to more recent zero-shot embedding methods may understate the broader significance.
- Some implementation details (complexity, signature uncertainty mechanism) are relegated to the supplement.

Overall, the paper presents a clear and technically sound contribution that advances attribute-based zero-shot learning by explicitly handling attribute unreliability, with solid empirical evidence of its benefits.
