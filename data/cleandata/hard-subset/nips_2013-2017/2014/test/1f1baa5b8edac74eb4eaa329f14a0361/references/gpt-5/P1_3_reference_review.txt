Summary
The paper tackles a key limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning: attribute predictors are often unreliable, and standard approaches (e.g., DAP) take their outputs at face value. The authors propose a zero-shot random forest (RF) framework trained not on images but on class-level attribute signatures. Crucially, during training they inject attribute validation data to estimate per-attribute ROC curves at each node and fractionally propagate class signatures to children according to the estimated TP/FP rates at the chosen threshold. This lets the tree prefer splits that are simultaneously discriminative across unseen classes and robust to expected attribute prediction errors. They further model uncertainty in class-attribute associations (“signature uncertainty”), and extend the method to a few-shot setting by combining signature-driven information gain with standard supervised gain, governed by a mixing weight λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show strong gains over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, controlled-noise studies that isolate the contribution of robustness, ablations for signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC estimation, and a few-shot study that demonstrates the usefulness of the signature prior. They also show competitive performance with discovered (pseudo)attributes.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-positioned relative to two-stage attribute-based zero-shot methods (Farhadi et al., DAP; Parikh & Grauman, relative attributes; topic models), knowledge-transfer/embedding approaches (Mensink et al., Akata et al., DeViSE), and discriminative mid-level discovery (Rastegari, Singh et al.). The technical connection to decision trees with missing/uncertain features (fractional tuples, fuzzy trees) is noted, while emphasizing the distinctive zero-shot training setup: training on signatures, testing on attribute predictions, and propagating validation attribute data to capture node-dependent ROC behavior. The novelty lies in injecting calibrated attribute unreliability into the training objective that selects splits.

Quality
- Methodology: Sound and well-motivated. The fractional propagation based on ROC estimates is a principled way to incorporate expected test-time noise during training. Using node-specific validation subsets DV(n) is a nice touch to capture inter-attribute dependencies induced by upstream splits.
- Experimental support: Strong. Results on three datasets with >250 attributes, controlled-noise experiments, ablations disentangling signature uncertainty and node-specific ROC propagation, and a few-shot extension with cross-validation of λ. The method beats DAP by a comfortable margin, especially when attributes are weak (aPY, SUN), validating the central claim.
- Caveats: No formal guarantees; the approach hinges on validation attribute data being representative of test-time attribute error profiles for unseen classes. Some important details (exact modeling of signature uncertainty, candidate threshold sampling) are deferred to the supplement. Comparisons to more recent label/feature embedding methods are discussed but not always directly compared numerically in the main text.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The key idea—training RFs on signatures with fractional propagation determined by ROC—is explained intuitively and mathematically. Suggestions:
- Bring the “signature uncertainty” formulation from the supplement into the main paper, even briefly, as it contributes to gains.
- Clarify how thresholds t are sampled per attribute and how many per node; include sensitivity to (#m, #t).
- Add a brief complexity discussion: training J forests one-vs-all in K and the impact of ROC bookkeeping; comment on scalability to large K.

Originality
The combination of zero-shot training on signatures with node-specific ROC-driven fractional propagation in trees is novel and non-trivial. While related to decision trees with uncertain/missing inputs, the zero-shot twist (training on class signatures; using validation attribute distributions to emulate test-time prediction noise) is original. The few-shot extension as a “descriptive prior” for RF split selection is a natural and useful addition.

Significance
The work addresses a central blocker for attribute-based zero-shot learning—unreliable attributes—offering a simple, effective, and interpretable solution that advances state of the art on standard benchmarks. The approach is broadly applicable wherever mid-level semantic predictors are noisy (attributes, poselets, mid-level patches), and the gains are practical. This is likely to influence subsequent zero/few-shot and robust decision tree research.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation; elegant and practical solution.
- Principled use of validation ROC estimates with node-specific conditioning.
- Strong empirical gains over DAP and a signature-only RF, with informative ablations and controlled-noise analysis.
- Few-shot extension with a tunable (and cross-validated) prior.
- Applicability to discovered attributes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on the representativeness of DV: attribute error rates can shift for unseen classes, especially when their attribute combinations are out-of-distribution. Consider domain adaptation or importance weighting to better estimate ROC under distribution shift.
- One-vs-all forests may not scale to very large K; a shared multi-class forest or multi-label trees could be discussed beyond future work.
- Attribute correlations are only indirectly handled via node conditioning; explicit joint modeling (e.g., multivariate splits or oblique trees) might further help.
- Provide more direct comparisons to label/feature embedding baselines (ALE, DeViSE) where feasible, noting differences in supervision.
- Expand on calibration of attribute predictors: ROC-based propagation assumes reasonably calibrated scores and stable operating points across domains.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the size and composition of DV? Have you tried selecting DV to better match unseen signatures (you mention negligible impact)?
- What is the computational overhead of computing node-specific ROC vs. global ROC, and its impact on training time?
- Could a single multi-class forest replace K one-vs-all forests without hurting performance?
- How is signature uncertainty modeled quantitatively (noise model, priors)? Any empirical sensitivity analysis?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical limitation of a widely used paradigm.
- Method is novel, simple, and well-justified.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains; clear ablations.
- Useful few-shot extension.

Cons against acceptance
- Relies on representativeness of validation attribute data; limited analysis of distribution shift.
- Some methodological details in supplement; limited theoretical analysis.
- Limited comparisons to non-attribute zero-shot baselines in the main text.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a principled and effective approach to handling unreliable attributes in zero-shot learning, with strong empirical validation and useful extensions. It should be of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.

Reviewer confidence: High.
