This paper addresses the problem of zero-shot learning with unreliable attribute predictions, a critical limitation of existing attribute-based methods. The authors propose a random forest-based approach that explicitly models attribute uncertainty during training, leveraging receiver operating characteristics to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes. They extend this to the few-shot setting, demonstrating improvements over existing methods on three datasets.

The paper is well-motivated. The observation that attribute predictions are often unreliable and that existing methods treat them as ground truth is a significant one. The proposed approach, using ROC curves to inform the decision tree construction, is a sensible and novel way to address this issue. The extension to the few-shot setting is also a valuable contribution. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against strong baselines (including DAP) and ablation studies to demonstrate the effectiveness of different components of the proposed method. The controlled noise experiments are particularly insightful, showcasing the method’s robustness to varying levels of attribute prediction error.  The connection to existing work on handling missing data in decision trees (fractional tuples) is appropriately noted, and the authors clearly articulate the differences and contributions of their work.

The clarity of the paper is generally good. The introduction clearly lays out the problem and motivates the approach. The technical details are explained reasonably well, although the mathematical notation could be slightly more streamlined. The organization is logical, progressing from problem statement to related work, proposed method, experiments, and conclusion. However, the presentation of the equations (particularly in section 3.2.2) could benefit from more detailed explanations of the variables and their relationships.  The writing is generally concise and avoids unnecessary jargon. Providing pseudocode is beneficial.

The originality of the work lies in the explicit modeling of attribute unreliability within a random forest framework for zero-shot learning. While the use of random forests is not novel, the integration of ROC curves and the propagation of uncertainty through the tree structure represent a significant contribution. The extension to the few-shot setting further enhances the originality. The paper adequately references related work, including both attribute-based zero-shot learning and methods for handling uncertainty in decision trees. A search for recent NIPS proceedings did not reveal directly competing work on this specific combination of techniques.

The significance of this work is considerable. Attribute-based zero-shot learning has the potential to enable flexible and adaptable visual recognition systems, but its practical application is limited by the reliability of attribute predictions. By addressing this limitation, the authors make a significant step towards realizing the full potential of this approach. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods on multiple datasets suggest that the proposed method is likely to be adopted by researchers in this field.  The extension to the few-shot setting further broadens the applicability of the work.  The insights gained from the controlled noise experiments are also valuable, providing a better understanding of the factors that influence the performance of attribute-based zero-shot learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to address a key limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   Strong experimental results on multiple datasets, consistently outperforming existing methods.
*   Comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies and controlled noise experiments.
*   Clear and well-motivated presentation of the problem and proposed solution.
*   Extension to the few-shot setting, increasing the applicability of the work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The mathematical notation in section 3.2.2 could be more streamlined and explained more thoroughly.
*   The reliance on SVMs for attribute prediction might limit the scalability of the approach to very large attribute vocabularies.
*   The choice of parameters (tree depth, number of trees) seems to be empirically determined and could benefit from a more systematic investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is clearly written. I recommend acceptance.
