Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot learning with attributes by explicitly modeling attribute unreliability during training. The core idea is a random forest that is trained not on images but on class-level attribute signatures, while incorporating attribute classifiers’ ROC statistics estimated from attribute-labeled validation images. At each split, class signatures are fractionally propagated to children according to true/false positive rates at a chosen threshold, yielding information gains that reflect both discriminativeness and learnability. The method also accounts for uncertainty in class-attribute associations and extends naturally to few-shot learning by combining signature-based and example-based information gains via a mixing parameter. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent gains over DAP and a signature-only RF, robustness to controlled attribute noise, and benefits in the few-shot regime.

Relation to prior work
This work lies within attribute-based zero-shot recognition (DAP, attribute signatures) but differs by incorporating attribute prediction reliability into training, rather than treating predicted attributes as perfect or only using their confidences at test time. It is related to decision trees for uncertain/missing data, but the domain shift (training on signatures, testing on attribute predictions) and the use of node-conditional ROCs are novel. It also contrasts with label or feature embedding methods and with discovered mid-level features by preserving semantic control via attributes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The fractional propagation based on node-specific ROC estimates is well-motivated and consistent: it converts ideal signatures into distributions in predicted-attribute space, so splits optimize expected information gain under the attribute error model. The recursive conditioning of DV to compute node-specific TPR/FPR addresses dependencies induced by earlier splits. The information gain formulation with soft counts is standard and appropriate. The few-shot extension via a convex combination of two IG objectives is reasonable and easy to implement.
- Empirical support: The paper reports thorough experiments on three datasets with 250+ attributes, including controlled noise studies that isolate the effect of unreliability, ablations (without signature uncertainty, without node-conditional ROC), and comparisons to DAP and a signature-only RF baseline. The improvements are largest where attributes are less reliable, which matches the stated motivation. Few-shot experiments demonstrate consistent gains and sensible trends as λ varies.
- Caveats: The approach relies on DV representing attribute error statistics for unseen classes. The authors acknowledge this and show an “upper bound” with true ROC from unseen classes. While the recursive DV(n) mitigates some attribute dependence, the method still largely treats attributes independently at split time; correlations remain an open issue (noted in future work). One potential clarity/validity concern: the data protocol says 20% is reserved for ROC estimation and then “pooled” with the remaining 80% to train final attribute classifiers. If ROC is estimated with models trained on 80% but test-time models are retrained on 100%, there is a mismatch; if ROC is estimated on data also used to train the final SVMs, it may be optimistic. This should be clarified and, ideally, controlled.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with concrete notation for indicators, ROC-based propagation, and information gain. The conceptual explanation of fractional propagation is intuitive. Some details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, signature-uncertainty formulation), which slightly hampers reproducibility from the main text. The training protocol for attribute SVMs and ROC estimation needs clearer sequencing, as noted above. A brief complexity analysis (e.g., cost per node in terms of #m, #t, K) would help.

Originality
Modeling attribute unreliability via node-conditional ROC within a random forest trained on class signatures is, to my knowledge, novel in the zero-shot setting. While related to fuzzy/missing-feature decision trees, the adaptation to zero-shot with signature-based training, conditional propagation of validation data, and the combined IG for few-shot training are original and nontrivial.

Significance
Zero-shot learning remains important, and attribute unreliability is a central pain point. The method is practical, simple to implement once attribute classifiers exist, and yields consistent gains over widely used baselines (DAP) across multiple datasets. The approach preserves semantic interpretability while being robust to imperfect attributes, which is valuable both for research and potential applications. The few-shot extension is a useful byproduct. The main limitation is reliance on a good DV; nevertheless, the demonstrated improvements suggest impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled treatment of attribute unreliability via ROC-aware random forests.
- Simple, modular method that integrates easily with existing attribute pipelines.
- Strong empirical results with ablations and controlled-noise experiments supporting the claims.
- Natural and effective few-shot extension.
- Semantic interpretability preserved.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on the representativeness of DV for unseen classes; limited analysis of domain shift in attribute error.
- Potential ambiguity/optimism in ROC estimation protocol when also used to train attribute SVMs.
- Limited comparisons beyond DAP in the main experiments; although a figure compares to broader published results on AwA, inclusion of recent embedding-based zero-shot methods in the main tables would strengthen claims.
- Details of signature uncertainty modeling are deferred; main text could include at least the core formulation.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify the exact sequence for training attribute SVMs and estimating ROC: are ROCs computed with models trained without DV and then SVMs retrained on all data, or are ROCs in-sample? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- How robust are node-conditional ROC estimates at deeper nodes with few validation samples? Beyond a threshold of 10, did you try smoothing or Bayesian estimates?
- Can you report runtimes and tree depth sensitivity? Any overfitting observed with deeper trees?
- How does the method perform against label-embedding approaches (e.g., ALE, DeViSE) under the same feature set?

Reasons to accept
- Addresses a real and under-modeled issue in zero-shot learning with a principled, effective method.
- Solid empirical evidence with clear gains and diagnostic experiments.
- Method is general, interpretable, and extensible (few-shot, discovered attributes).

Reasons to reject
- Results rely on a specific validation protocol whose potential optimism is not fully addressed.
- Comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art zero-shot baselines are limited in the main tables.
- Some important details are deferred to the supplement, reducing immediate reproducibility.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological idea is sound and useful, empirically validated, and of interest to the NeurIPS community. Addressing the clarity and comparison concerns would further strengthen the paper.
