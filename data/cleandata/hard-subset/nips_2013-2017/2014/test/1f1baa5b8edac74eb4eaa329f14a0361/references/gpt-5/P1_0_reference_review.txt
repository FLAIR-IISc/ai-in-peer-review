Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot recognition when mid-level semantic attributes are predicted unreliably. Rather than taking attribute predictions at face value, the authors train a random forest whose split selection explicitly accounts for attribute classifier errors. Key ideas:
- Train one-vs-all random forests over class attribute signatures (not images). Each split tests an attribute m with a threshold t.
- Inject attribute-labeled validation data to estimate node-specific ROC statistics (TPR/FPR) for each attribute at threshold t. During training, each class signature is fractionally propagated left/right according to these ROCs, yielding soft counts and an information gain that prefers splits that are both discriminative and predictable given expected attribute errors.
- Extend to handle uncertainty in class signatures (annotation noise) by further softening propagation.
- Extend to few-shot by combining signature-based information gain with standard image-based gain via a convex weight λ, viewing signatures as a prior.
Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline; controlled-noise experiments illustrate robustness when attribute reliability varies by attribute. Ablations confirm contributions from ROC-based propagation and signature-uncertainty modeling. Results with discovered mid-level features are also competitive.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on attribute-based zero-shot learning (e.g., DAP) and contrasts with label/feature embedding approaches, hierarchical transfer, and discovered mid-level features. It is related to decision trees for missing/uncertain data and fuzzy trees, but differs by (i) training on signatures rather than data; (ii) using node-specific ROC estimates derived from attribute-labeled validation data; and (iii) its zero-/few-shot setting.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical, well-known limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning—unreliable attribute predictors—with a principled, simple mechanism.
- The node-specific ROC-driven fractional propagation is intuitive, technically sound, and well-motivated; it naturally down-weights unlearnable attributes and chooses operating points that are informative under expected errors.
- Clear extensions: handling signature uncertainty and a few-shot prior via blended information gain.
- Solid empirical evaluation: three datasets, controlled-noise analyses, ablations, comparisons to widely used baselines; gains are largest where attributes are less reliable, matching motivation.
- Uses the same attribute-labeled data as baselines; the method’s advantage comes from modeling unreliability rather than more training data.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Domain shift assumption: The method assumes ROC statistics estimated on seen-class images transfer to unseen-class images. The oracle “OURS+TRUE ROC” suggests headroom remains; attempts to focus validation on similar signatures had negligible effect. This is the central risk factor; consider domain-adaptive calibration or confidence adjustment for classes with novel attribute combinations.
- Scalability: Training one forest per unseen class scales linearly in K; test-time cost also scales with K. For many unseen classes (e.g., open-vocabulary settings) a multi-class or shared-forest formulation could help.
- Attribute correlation: While node-specific DV(n) partially captures dependencies, the split model tests a single attribute at a time. Strongly correlated or entangled attributes could still confound split selection; oblique splits or joint tests might help.
- Few-shot combination is a simple linear blend with λ tuned by cross-validation; some theoretical guidance or Bayesian formulation for setting λ would strengthen the section.
- Clarity on ROC estimation protocol: It appears 20% is held out to estimate ROCs, then pooled with the 80% to train the final attribute SVMs; ensure the ROC estimates are computed from models not trained on that 20% to avoid optimistic calibration. If retraining on all data for test-time SVMs, discuss mismatch between ROCs and final models.
- Evaluation breadth: The main quantitative baselines are DAP and Signature-RF. Although related works are cited (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE, hierarchical transfer), side-by-side comparisons (where features/attributes permit) would strengthen claims.

Quality
Technically sound with a clear derivation of the modified information gain and consistent ablations validating design choices. The use of node-specific validation subsets to compute ROC statistics is well-justified. Claims are supported by experiments; however, more analysis of sensitivity to validation set size, attribute rarity, and λ would be useful. Complexity and memory costs are not analyzed.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Equations define the core training objective; the narrative makes the intuition accessible. Suggestions:
- Explicitly detail the ROC estimation protocol to avoid data leakage and specify whether ROCs are cross-validated.
- Summarize pseudo-code in the main paper (currently in supplementary) and provide complexity per tree.
- Clarify the metric used (per-class vs per-image accuracy) and report chance levels.

Originality
The combination of signature-trained forests with ROC-based fractional propagation for zero-shot is, to my knowledge, novel. While related to fuzzy/uncertain decision trees, the zero-shot training on signatures and node-specific ROC conditioning is a fresh contribution. The few-shot prior via blended gains is a reasonable, if incremental, extension.

Significance
Improving robustness of attribute-based zero-shot learning addresses a core barrier to practical deployment. The empirical gains over a widely used baseline (DAP) on multiple datasets suggest impact. The idea is likely to influence subsequent work on zero-/few-shot methods that rely on mid-level semantics, and may transfer beyond vision.

Questions for the authors
- How are ROC curves estimated to avoid optimistic bias? Are the attribute SVMs used to produce ROCs trained without the validation split (e.g., via CV)? If you retrain SVMs on all data for testing, how sensitive are results to the mismatch?
- Sensitivity to validation set size/composition: What happens if DV is smaller or biased? Any analysis vs number of positives/negatives per attribute?
- How is λ selected in the few-shot setting given no labeled data for unseen classes? What validation protocol is used?
- Runtime and memory: training/testing cost vs number of attributes and unseen classes?
- Did you try shared multiclass trees rather than one-vs-all forests?

Pros
- Principled handling of attribute unreliability via ROC-aware training
- Consistent improvements on three datasets; informative ablations
- Simple, general, and compatible with discovered mid-level features
- Natural few-shot extension

Cons
- Assumes transferability of ROC statistics; domain shift may limit gains
- Limited baseline breadth (no direct comparisons to some embedding/hierarchy methods under same features)
- Potential scalability concerns and limited analysis of complexity
- Few-shot blending is heuristic; limited theory

Overall recommendation: Accept (weak to moderate). The paper offers a clear, novel idea that advances zero-/few-shot learning with semantic attributes and demonstrates solid empirical benefits. With clarifications on ROC estimation and broader baselines, it would be even stronger.
