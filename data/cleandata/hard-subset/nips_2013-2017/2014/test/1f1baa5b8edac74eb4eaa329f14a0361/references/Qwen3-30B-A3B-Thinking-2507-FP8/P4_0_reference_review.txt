

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL) approaches: the assumption of perfect attribute prediction. While existing ZSL methods (e.g., DAP [8]) use attribute signatures to define novel classes, they assume attribute classifiers are perfectly reliable, which is unrealistic. The authors propose a novel random forest-based method that explicitly accounts for attribute prediction unreliability by incorporating the receiver operating characteristics (ROC) of attribute classifiers into the training process. The approach trains decision trees using class attribute signatures rather than image descriptors, and propagates signatures through the tree according to the estimated false positive and true positive rates of attribute classifiers. The paper further extends the approach to handle signature uncertainty and to the few-shot setting.

This work builds upon recent NIPS papers on ZSL, particularly the influential DAP model [8] which has become a standard baseline in the field. It also relates to work on attribute-based recognition [3, 18, 19] and recent efforts to discover mid-level features [11, 22, 30]. Unlike these approaches, the authors specifically address the unreliability of attribute predictions, which has been largely overlooked in prior ZSL literature.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper presents a well-motivated solution to a genuine problem in ZSL. The mathematical formulation of the information gain criterion that accounts for attribute unreliability (Eq. 5) is sound and properly justified. The authors provide comprehensive experimental validation across three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines. Their controlled noise experiments (Fig. 1) clearly demonstrate how the method handles varying levels of attribute prediction error, with particularly strong results when attribute reliability varies. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of the key components (accounting for attribute unreliability and signature uncertainty).

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The methodology is clearly explained with precise mathematical formulations, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce their results, including dataset specifics, parameter choices, and implementation details. The figures effectively illustrate the key results.

**Originality:** The core idea—explicitly modeling attribute prediction unreliability in ZSL—represents a significant conceptual advance over existing work. While prior papers have acknowledged attribute unreliability [18], none have proposed a method to actively incorporate this uncertainty into the learning process. The authors' approach to propagate signatures according to ROC curves is novel and well-executed. The few-shot extension is a valuable practical contribution that naturally follows from the core idea.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in ZSL that has hindered practical deployment of these methods. The consistent improvements over DAP (the leading ZSL method) across multiple datasets demonstrate the practical value of the approach. The fact that their method outperforms DAP even when using only 80% of the attribute-labeled data (compared to DAP's full data usage) is particularly compelling evidence of the value of their approach. The few-shot extension further enhances the practical utility of the method.

## Weaknesses

**Assumption of Attribute Vocabulary Expressiveness:** The approach assumes the attribute vocabulary is expressive enough to discriminate between classes, which is a common assumption in attribute-based ZSL. However, the paper doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how this limitation might affect performance when the attribute vocabulary is insufficient.

**Validation Data Limitation:** The authors use validation data from seen classes to estimate ROC curves, which may not perfectly represent the error patterns on unseen classes. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, they don't explore how to mitigate it (e.g., by using seen classes with attribute signatures similar to unseen classes).

**Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their approach, particularly for large numbers of attributes or classes. This could be a concern for practical deployment.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong technical contribution to the field of zero-shot learning by addressing a critical practical limitation of existing methods. The authors present a well-motivated, technically sound approach that is clearly superior to existing baselines in experiments across multiple datasets. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of both the theoretical foundations and practical implementation.

The paper's main novelty lies in explicitly modeling attribute prediction unreliability during training, which has been overlooked in prior ZSL work. This is not merely a minor modification to existing methods but represents a conceptual advance that significantly improves performance. The few-shot extension further enhances the practical value of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Solves a genuine problem in ZSL that has been overlooked in prior work
- Technically sound with rigorous experimental validation
- Consistent improvements over strong baselines (DAP, SIGNATURE-RF)
- Clear and well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility
- Practical significance for real-world ZSL applications

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of scalability for large attribute sets
- Reliance on attribute vocabulary expressiveness assumption
- Validation data limitation (seen classes for ROC estimation)

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to zero-shot learning that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The approach is novel, technically sound, and has clear practical value. The paper meets all four criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) at a high level.
