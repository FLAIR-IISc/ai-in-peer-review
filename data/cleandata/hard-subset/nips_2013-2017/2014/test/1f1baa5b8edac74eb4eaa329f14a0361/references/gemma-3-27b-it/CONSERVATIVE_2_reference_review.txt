This paper addresses the well-known challenge of zero-shot learning with attributes, specifically acknowledging and attempting to mitigate the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea – to incorporate the ROC characteristics of attribute classifiers into a random forest framework – is reasonable and conceptually sound, building upon existing attribute-based zero-shot learning methods like DAP. The extension to the few-shot setting is also a logical progression. However, despite a clear presentation and a seemingly sensible approach, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advance over established baselines, and I have serious reservations about its claims of superiority.

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the authors test on three datasets, and perform ablation studies, the gains over the DAP baseline, while statistically significant, are often modest, particularly on AwA. The justification that modeling attribute reliability is *more* important than improving the attribute classifiers themselves feels unsupported. If the underlying attribute predictions are poor, any refinement of the decision process will be limited. The controlled noise experiments are useful for demonstrating the method’s sensitivity to attribute uncertainty, but don’t fully translate to real-world performance gains beyond what could likely be achieved with more robust attribute classifiers trained with more data or better features. 

Furthermore, the selection of the validation set for ROC estimation feels ad-hoc. While the authors acknowledge the importance of this data, the specifics of its selection, and the potential impact of its composition on the results, are not thoroughly discussed. The claim that the method avoids "unlearnable" attributes is intriguing, but requires more in-depth analysis. Which attributes are effectively discarded, and why? Are these attributes inherently problematic, or merely poorly modeled by the chosen classifiers?

The writing is generally clear, but the paper suffers from a degree of verbosity. The mathematical formulations, while correct, could be streamlined. The connection to existing work on handling uncertainty in decision trees (fractional tuples, etc.) feels superficial; the authors acknowledge the similarities but don’t delve into the nuances of how their approach differs in a meaningful way.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Attribute unreliability is a significant limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning.
*   **Sensible approach:** Incorporating ROC characteristics into the decision-making process is a logical way to address this problem.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Extension to few-shot learning:** The few-shot extension provides additional flexibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest performance gains:** The improvements over DAP are often small and may not justify the added complexity.
*   **Lack of strong justification:** The claim that modeling attribute reliability is more important than improving attribute classifiers is not adequately supported.
*   **Ad-hoc validation set selection:** The method for choosing the validation set for ROC estimation is not fully explained.
*   **Superficial connection to related work:** The discussion of related work on handling uncertainty in decision trees is limited.
*   **Verbosity:** The paper could be more concise.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a real problem in zero-shot learning.
*   The proposed method is novel and conceptually sound.
*   The experimental results, while modest, are positive.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gains are not substantial enough to warrant a strong recommendation.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more rigorous.
*   The justification for the method's superiority over simpler approaches is weak.
*   The connection to related work is superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject**. While the paper presents a technically sound and reasonably well-written approach, it does not demonstrate a significant enough advance over existing methods to justify acceptance to NIPS. The gains are modest, the experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing, and the justification for the method's superiority is weak. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial contribution to the field. I would be more receptive to a revised version with more compelling experimental results and a more thorough analysis of the method's strengths and limitations. Specifically, a comparison to a stronger attribute classifier (trained with more data, better regularization, or more informative features) would be essential.
