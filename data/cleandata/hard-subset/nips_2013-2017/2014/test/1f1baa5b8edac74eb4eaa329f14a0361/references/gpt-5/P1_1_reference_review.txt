Summary
The paper tackles zero-shot learning (ZSL) with semantic attributes by explicitly modeling the unreliability of attribute classifiers. The core contribution is a random forest (RF) training procedure that uses class attribute signatures as the only “training instances” and incorporates attribute classifier error statistics via ROC curves estimated on attribute-labeled validation data. During tree construction, each class signature is fractionally propagated to child nodes according to the estimated TPR/FPR at the candidate split (m, t), and information gain is computed with soft counts. This favors splits that are both discriminative among class signatures and predictable given known attribute error tendencies. The method is further extended to handle uncertainty in class-attribute associations (signature noise) and to the few-shot setting by combining signature-based and data-based information gains with a mixing parameter λ. Experiments on AwA, aPY, and SUN show consistent improvements over DAP and a signature-only RF baseline, with ablations validating the importance of ROC-based propagation and signature uncertainty modeling. Controlled noise studies corroborate robustness when attribute reliabilities vary. The few-shot extension yields additional gains and shows sensible behavior as λ varies.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on attribute-based ZSL (e.g., Farhadi et al., DAP, topic and embedding variants), but departs by explicitly accounting for attribute unreliability during the classifier learning stage, rather than treating attribute predictions at face value. It is related to handling missing/uncertain features in trees (fractional tuples, fuzzy trees), but is distinct in that training instances are class signatures and test-time inputs are attribute predictions. It contrasts with alternative ZSL approaches based on label or feature embeddings or classifier adaptation via external knowledge, emphasizing human-interpretable attributes. The authors also show applicability to discovered (pseudo-)attributes.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated problem: attribute unreliability is a central practical limitation in attribute-based ZSL; the proposed approach directly targets it.
- Technical novelty: fractional propagation driven by ROC estimates within a random forest trained on signatures is elegant and, to my knowledge, new in ZSL. The node-conditional ROC estimation DV(n) accounts for dependencies introduced by upstream splits.
- Sound criterion: the soft information gain appropriately down-weights unlearnable attributes (TPR≈FPR).
- Practicality and extensibility: straightforward to implement on top of standard RFs; natural few-shot extension with a signature prior; ability to handle multi-modal or continuous signatures.
- Empirical validation: thorough experiments on three datasets with many attributes, controlled noise studies, ablations isolating key components, comparison to widely used DAP, and evaluation with discovered attributes. Reported improvements are consistent and often substantial, particularly when attributes are less reliable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on ROC generalization: ROCs are estimated on seen-class attribute-labeled data; their generalization to unseen classes with potentially different attribute appearances is uncertain. The “OURS+TRUE ROC” upper bound suggests sensitivity to this assumption. Although acknowledged, it remains a core limitation.
- Limited baseline breadth: DAP is an important baseline, but additional comparisons to contemporaneous embedding-based ZSL methods (e.g., label embeddings, DeViSE) would strengthen claims, especially on AwA where such results exist.
- Details deferred to supplement: the signature uncertainty model is important; more detail in the main paper would help assess its effect and reproducibility. Similarly, explicit pseudocode would aid clarity.
- Potential data leakage in ROC estimation: the paper states reserving 20% DV for ROC estimation, then pooling it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers. If DV is also used to train the attribute SVMs, ROC estimates may be optimistic. Please clarify whether DV is excluded from SVM training when estimating ROC.
- Sample scarcity at deep nodes: when DV(n) has few positives/negatives, the method backs off to global DV. While pragmatic, this introduces a distribution mismatch; sensitivity analyses on the threshold (<10) and its effect on accuracy would be useful.
- Scalability: While RFs scale well, the number of candidate tests per node and the need to recompute node-conditional ROCs may grow with M and tree depth. A brief complexity analysis or runtime measurements would be helpful.

Quality
Technically sound with a coherent objective (IGzero), well-motivated design choices (ROC-based fractional propagation, node-conditional DV(n)), and careful ablations. Experiments are substantial and support the main claims. Some methodological details (signature uncertainty term) should be moved from the supplement.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The intuition behind fractional propagation is well-explained. Suggestions:
- Include the exact formulation for signature uncertainty in the main text.
- Clarify the treatment of DV vis-à-vis attribute SVM training to avoid confusion about dependence.
- Provide a brief algorithm box/pseudocode and complexity/running time stats.

Originality
Novel application of ROC-driven fractional propagation in RFs for ZSL with signatures, distinct from prior handling of missing/uncertain features, and a principled few-shot integration. Adequately references related work.

Significance
Addresses a practical bottleneck in attribute-based ZSL and demonstrates notable gains on standard benchmarks, especially under realistic attribute unreliability. The idea is likely to influence future ZSL methods that rely on semantic attributes and could inform robustness-aware training in other mid-level concept pipelines.

Questions for authors
- Is DV excluded from training the attribute SVMs when computing ROC, or is there any risk of optimistic ROC due to reuse?
- How sensitive are results to the size and composition of DV and to the <10 sample threshold for backing off to global DV?
- How are thresholds t sampled, and how many per node are needed for good performance? Any per-attribute calibration?
- Can you report runtime/training complexity vs. DAP and the signature-only RF?
- For SUN, are results averaged over multiple unseen-class splits, or do trials vary only in random seeds?
- Any empirical analysis of correlated attributes and whether node-conditional DV(n) meaningfully captures such dependencies?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical limitation in ZSL with a principled, novel approach.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements and informative ablations.
- Clear intuition and compatibility with both named and discovered attributes.
- Natural few-shot extension with demonstrated benefit.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on ROC generalization from seen to unseen classes; limited exploration of alternative ZSL baselines (embeddings).
- Some key details and analyses deferred to the supplement; limited scalability/runtime discussion.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel method that advances attribute-based zero-shot learning by directly modeling attribute unreliability, with solid empirical evidence and practical relevance. Addressing the clarity points and expanding baselines would further strengthen it.
