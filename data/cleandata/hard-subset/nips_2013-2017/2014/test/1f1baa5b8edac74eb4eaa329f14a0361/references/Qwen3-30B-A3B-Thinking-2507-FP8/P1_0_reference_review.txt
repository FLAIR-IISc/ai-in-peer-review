

# Peer Review for NIPS

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical but often overlooked limitation in zero-shot learning (ZSL): the assumption that attribute predictions are perfect. The authors propose a novel approach called "Zero-Shot Random Forest" that explicitly accounts for the unreliability of attribute classifiers by leveraging their receiver operating characteristics (ROC) during training. Unlike existing methods that treat attribute predictions as accurate, the proposed method models how attribute classifiers might fail, using validation data to estimate true/false positive rates at different operating points. The authors further extend their approach to handle signature uncertainty and develop a few-shot extension.

The work builds upon and significantly improves over the standard Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP) framework [8], which is the most widely used ZSL method. The paper demonstrates their approach on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN), showing substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The core innovation is the way they incorporate attribute classifier uncertainty into the tree construction process, rather than treating attribute predictions as perfect.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, with formal definitions of the information gain metrics under attribute unreliability. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using three standard datasets with multiple baselines, including the widely used DAP method. The ablation studies (Table 1) effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of their method.

The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss how their approach might be improved (e.g., better modeling of expected error rates on unseen attribute combinations). The evaluation methodology is sound, with 20 random trials to account for variance, and they carefully control the amount of attribute-labeled data used across all methods.

One minor limitation is that the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the computational complexity of their method compared to DAP, especially as the number of attributes grows. However, this doesn't undermine the technical soundness of the approach.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the outset, and the motivation for addressing attribute unreliability is compelling. The methodology is explained in a logical sequence, with sufficient mathematical formalism to understand the technical contributions. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the key insight about handling attribute uncertainty.

The paper would benefit from slightly more detail on how the validation data is selected and how it relates to the unseen classes, but this is a minor point. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would help with reproducibility.

## Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution to the ZSL literature. While prior work has recognized the importance of attributes in ZSL, none have explicitly modeled the unreliability of attribute predictions during training. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work, particularly noting that attribute unreliability is distinct from attribute strength (a key conceptual distinction).

The core idea of using ROC characteristics to guide tree construction is novel and directly addresses a practical limitation of existing methods. The few-shot extension further demonstrates the generality of their approach. The authors have thoroughly reviewed the literature and properly positioned their work against related contributions, particularly the DAP method and other attribute-based approaches.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors demonstrate that accounting for attribute unreliability leads to significant improvements (up to 10+ points on some categories) over the current state-of-the-art. This is particularly important because DAP is the most commonly used ZSL method in the literature.

The paper provides a practical solution to a real problem in ZSL, showing that the elegance of the attribute-based approach can be salvaged despite the inherent difficulty of learning reliable attributes. The results on the SUN dataset (with fewer positive training samples per attribute) are especially compelling, as this is where the method's benefits are most needed.

The approach is generalizable to other semantic-based recognition tasks beyond ZSL, and the authors rightly note potential applications in domains like poselets for action recognition. This work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with clear improvements over established baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a critical problem** in ZSL that has been overlooked in prior work.
2. **Novel theoretical contribution** of incorporating ROC characteristics into tree construction.
3. **Strong empirical results** showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
4. **Comprehensive evaluation** across three standard datasets with thorough ablation studies.
5. **Practical extension** to few-shot learning, demonstrating the method's versatility.
6. **Strong theoretical grounding** with clear mathematical formulation of the information gain metrics.
7. **Clear distinction** from related work, particularly the DAP method.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper could better explain why attribute classifiers are unreliable in practice beyond stating it as a fact. While they reference [6] on correlated attributes, a more detailed discussion of practical challenges would strengthen the motivation.

2. The computational complexity of the method, particularly as the number of attributes grows, is not thoroughly discussed. This is important for practical adoption, especially compared to simpler methods like DAP.

3. The validation data requirement (20% of attribute-labeled images) might be a limitation for applications with scarce attribute-labeled data. The paper doesn't explore how the method performs with smaller validation sets.

4. The few-shot extension (Section 3.3) could be more thoroughly explained, particularly how the parameter λ is selected in practice beyond mentioning cross-validation.

5. While the paper shows significant improvements over DAP, it could better articulate *why* their method works better beyond the ROC modeling aspect. A more detailed analysis of which attributes are most reliable and how they contribute to the decision process would add depth.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The problem they address is real and important, their solution is novel and well-executed, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over the current state-of-the-art. The paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated.

The minor weaknesses noted above are not significant enough to outweigh the paper's strengths and contributions. The work advances the field in a demonstrable way and provides a practical solution to a real problem in zero-shot learning.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the zero-shot learning literature by addressing a critical limitation in existing approaches—the assumption of perfect attribute prediction. The authors' solution is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated across multiple datasets. The results demonstrate substantial improvements over the widely used DAP method, and the few-shot extension further demonstrates the method's versatility. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I believe this work would be of great interest to the NIPS community and would make an excellent addition to the conference proceedings.
