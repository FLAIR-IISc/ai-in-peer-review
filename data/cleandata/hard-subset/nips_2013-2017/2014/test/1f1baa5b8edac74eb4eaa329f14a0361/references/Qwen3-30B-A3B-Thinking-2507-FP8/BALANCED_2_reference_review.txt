

# Review of "Zero-Shot Recognition with Unreliable Attributes"

This paper addresses a critical limitation in zero-shot learning: existing methods assume perfect attribute predictions, which is unrealistic given the difficulty of learning reliable attribute classifiers. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier unreliability by incorporating receiver operating characteristics (ROC) during tree construction. Their method trains on class attribute signatures rather than image features, and during training, it propagates signature fractions through the tree according to the estimated error tendencies of attribute classifiers. The paper further extends this to handle signature uncertainty and few-shot learning.

The paper builds upon the well-established two-stage attribute-based zero-shot learning framework (DAP, [8]), but makes a crucial contribution by modeling the unreliability of attribute predictions, a gap that has been noted in prior work [18]. It also relates to recent work on attribute discovery [30] and random forest extensions for missing data [21], but the authors' approach is distinct in its application to the zero-shot setting and its focus on propagating error statistics through the tree.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation in information theory and ROC analysis. The method is well-motivated and the experimental evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines (DAP and SIGNATURE-RF) across three diverse datasets. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of the key components (ROC propagation and signature uncertainty modeling).

2. **Originality:** The core idea of explicitly modeling attribute prediction unreliability during zero-shot training is novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The authors clearly distinguish their work from prior methods that treat attribute predictions as perfect. The few-shot extension is also a valuable contribution.

3. **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical zero-shot applications where attribute classifiers are inherently unreliable. The results show that accounting for this unreliability leads to significant performance gains (up to 10.2% on AwA), demonstrating that the elegance of attribute-based zero-shot learning can be preserved in practice.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The method is explained with sufficient detail, including pseudocode and a schematic illustration in the supplement. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Validation Data Representation:** The paper reserves 20% of attribute-labeled images for ROC estimation, but doesn't adequately address whether this validation data is representative of the unseen classes. Since unseen classes aren't available during training, it's unclear how well the validation data captures the error tendencies on unseen classes. This is a critical limitation as the method's performance depends on the accuracy of the ROC estimates.

2. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proposed method compared to baselines. The recursive propagation of validation data through the tree could significantly increase training time, which is an important consideration for practical applications.

3. **Few-Shot Extension Depth:** The few-shot extension is promising but not deeply explored. The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison to other few-shot learning methods or demonstrate concrete scenarios where the attribute signature prior provides the most benefit.

4. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't address how the method scales to very large numbers of classes or attributes, which is important for real-world applications where zero-shot recognition might need to handle thousands of classes.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:* The paper addresses a significant limitation in zero-shot learning with a theoretically sound and empirically effective approach. The results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The method is well-motivated, well-executed, and provides a principled solution to a real problem in the field.

*Con:* The validation data assumption is a potential weakness, as the method's performance depends on the quality of ROC estimates from the validation set. The computational complexity and scalability are not adequately addressed, which could limit practical adoption.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Address the validation data representativeness issue by experimenting with different validation data selection strategies (e.g., selecting images from seen classes with attribute signatures most similar to the unseen classes).

2. Include a computational complexity analysis and compare training times with baselines.

3. Expand the few-shot section to include comparisons with other few-shot learning methods and provide more detailed analysis of when the attribute signature prior provides the most benefit.

4. Discuss scalability considerations for large-scale applications and potential optimizations.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to zero-shot learning by addressing a critical practical limitation. While the validation data assumption requires careful consideration, the core idea is sound and well-supported by the experimental results. With the suggested improvements, this work has the potential to become a significant contribution to the field.

**Overall Recommendation:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and well-executed solution. The results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, and the approach has significant potential for practical applications. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the paper's core contribution.
