### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a critical and well-known problem in attribute-based zero-shot learning (ZSL): the unreliability of attribute classifiers. Most existing ZSL methods operate in two stages: first predicting attributes for a test image, then classifying the image based on these predictions. However, they typically assume perfect attribute predictions when learning the classifier for unseen classes, making them brittle to the inevitable errors from the first stage.

The authors propose a novel zero-shot random forest framework that explicitly accounts for attribute classifier uncertainty during the training phase. Instead of training on image data (which is unavailable for unseen classes), the random forests are trained directly on the semantic attribute signatures that define each class. The core contribution is a modified training procedure where class signatures are propagated fractionally down the decision trees. This fractional propagation is guided by the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves of the individual attribute classifiers, which are estimated on a held-out set of attribute-labeled validation images. By incorporating expected true positive and false positive rates into the information gain calculation, the algorithm learns to favor splits that are not only discriminative for the idealized signatures but also robust to prediction errors. The framework is further extended to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and is naturally adapted to the few-shot learning setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and practical limitation of attribute-based ZSL. While the unreliability of attribute classifiers is widely acknowledged, previous work has not offered a direct solution to mitigate its effect during the training of the zero-shot model itself. The proposed idea of integrating the attribute classifiers' error statistics (via ROC analysis) into the discriminative training process is novel, elegant, and directly addresses the core of the problem. This represents a significant conceptual advance for attribute-based recognition.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-realized within the random forest framework. The mechanism for fractional propagation of signatures based on attribute-specific TP/FP rates is a clever way to "bake in" robustness to expected errors. The formulation correctly captures dependencies between attributes by recursively partitioning the validation data used for ROC estimation, which is a subtle but important detail. The extensions to signature uncertainty and few-shot learning demonstrate the flexibility and coherence of the proposed framework.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem. The methodology is presented in a logical, incremental fashion, starting with a basic "Signature RF" and then layering on the key contributions. This greatly aids reader comprehension. The figures and tables are clear and effectively support the paper's claims.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The controlled noise experiments provide excellent intuition and a clear proof-of-concept, demonstrating that the method's advantage increases with attribute noise, particularly when noise levels vary across attributes (a realistic scenario).
    *   The method achieves state-of-the-art or significantly improved results over strong baselines like DAP on three diverse and standard ZSL datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN). The substantial gains, especially on aPY and SUN where attribute classifiers are less reliable, strongly validate the approach.
    *   The ablation studies in Table 1 effectively isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed method (modeling signature uncertainty and recursive ROC propagation).
    *   The successful application to the few-shot learning setting further highlights the method's utility and its potential as a general framework for learning with descriptive priors.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Scalability to Many Unseen Classes:** The current approach trains one-vs-all random forests, which requires training K separate forests for K unseen classes. This may not scale well to scenarios with a very large number of unseen classes. The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work in the conclusion. A brief discussion in the main paper about the computational complexity with respect to K and potential solutions (e.g., multi-class trees) would be beneficial.

2.  **Distribution Mismatch in Validation Data:** The method's effectiveness relies on accurately estimating attribute classifier ROC curves from a validation set (`DV`) composed of images from *seen* classes. There is a potential distribution mismatch, as the visual appearance of attributes in unseen classes might differ from that in seen classes. The "OURS+TRUE ROC" experiment hints at the performance gap caused by this mismatch. While solving this is a hard open problem in ZSL, the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of this limitation and its potential impact. For instance, could this gap be mitigated by re-weighting the validation data to better match the attribute statistics of the target unseen classes?

3.  **Clarification on Attribute Dependencies:** The paper makes the important point that "recursive sub-selection of the validation data is important to capture the dependency of TP/FP rates". While the high-level idea is clear, a small, concrete example would help readers fully grasp the mechanism. For instance, showing how a split on `has_stripes` might influence the estimated TP rate for `is_black` in a child node would make this powerful aspect of the model more explicit.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. It identifies a key weakness in a popular class of methods and proposes a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically successful solution. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and experimental validation are substantial. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to potential limitations and future extensions rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed work. The paper is well above the acceptance threshold for NeurIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
