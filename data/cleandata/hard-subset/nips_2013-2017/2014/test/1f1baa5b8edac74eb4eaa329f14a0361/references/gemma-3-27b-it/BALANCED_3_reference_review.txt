This paper addresses the challenging problem of zero-shot recognition, specifically focusing on the inherent unreliability of attribute predictions. The core idea—to explicitly account for attribute prediction errors during training—is both insightful and well-motivated. The authors propose a random forest approach that leverages receiver operating characteristics (ROC) to select discriminative and predictable decision nodes, and further generalize it to handle unreliable class-attribute associations and extend to the few-shot setting. The experimental results on three datasets (AwA, aPY, and SUN) demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, including the widely used DAP approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Importance:** The paper tackles a crucial limitation of attribute-based zero-shot learning—the reliance on potentially noisy attribute predictions. Addressing this issue is essential for bridging the gap between theoretical potential and practical performance.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach is technically well-grounded. The use of ROC curves to model attribute reliability is a clever and effective strategy. The extension to few-shot learning is also a natural and valuable addition.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, including controlled noise experiments to isolate the impact of attribute unreliability and comparisons with state-of-the-art baselines on standard datasets. The ablation studies further demonstrate the contribution of different components of the proposed method.
* **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach. The motivation is well-established, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of pseudocode is helpful.
* **Strong Results:** The consistent improvements over existing methods, particularly DAP, are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed approach.  The gains on datasets with less reliable attributes (SUN, aPY) specifically support the core claim.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the approach is sound, the implementation details and extensions (ROC propagation, signature uncertainty) add significant complexity. This complexity may make it difficult for others to reproduce or build upon the work. Further simplification could enhance accessibility.
* **Validation Data Dependency:** The method relies on a validation dataset to estimate attribute classifier errors.  While the authors acknowledge this, the performance is potentially sensitive to the quality and representativeness of this validation data, which isn't extensively explored. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of validation data size and composition would be beneficial.
* **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:**  The paper demonstrates overall performance gains, but a more detailed analysis of *where* the method succeeds and fails would be valuable. Are there specific attributes or classes where the proposed approach struggles? Understanding these limitations could guide future research.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The mention of cross-validation to determine optimal parameters (like λ) hints at potential sensitivity to hyperparameter settings. A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and strategies for robust parameter tuning would strengthen the paper.
* **ROC Computation Cost:** Computing and propagating ROC curves at each node in the random forest could be computationally expensive, especially for a large number of attributes.  A discussion of the computational cost and potential optimization strategies would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in zero-shot learning and proposes a novel and effective solution.
* The technical contributions are sound and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the approach might limit its accessibility and reproducibility.
* The reliance on validation data introduces a potential dependency that isn't fully explored.
* A deeper analysis of failure cases and hyperparameter sensitivity is needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of zero-shot learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are some weaknesses related to complexity and validation data dependency, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the paper.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.**  I believe this paper is suitable for publication at NeurIPS. I recommend the authors consider addressing the weaknesses mentioned above (specifically, providing a more detailed discussion of computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity) in a revised version. Additionally, expanding the failure case analysis could further strengthen the paper’s contribution.
