**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of attribute unreliability in zero-shot learning (ZSL). The standard ZSL pipeline relies on predicting attributes for a test image and then mapping these predictions to a class label. However, attribute classifiers are often noisy and unreliable, which degrades performance. The authors propose a novel random forest-based approach that explicitly models this unreliability during training. Instead of training on image data, the forests are trained on the idealized attribute "signatures" of the unseen classes. The key idea is to use a set of attribute-labeled validation images to estimate the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) of each attribute classifier. During tree construction, signatures are propagated down the tree fractionally, according to these estimated true/false positive rates. This process selects for splits that are not only discriminative for the idealized signatures but are also robust to the expected prediction errors at test time. The authors extend this framework to handle uncertainty in the class-attribute signatures themselves and to the few-shot learning setting. Experiments on three standard datasets (AwA, aPY, SUN) demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods like Direct Attribute Prediction (DAP).

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a well-known but often-ignored limitation of attribute-based ZSL. The assumption that attribute classifiers are reliable is a major bottleneck in practice, and this work provides a direct and principled approach to mitigate this issue. This is a significant contribution to making attribute-based methods more practical.
2.  **Novel and Intuitive Method:** The core idea of building decision trees on class signatures while propagating them fractionally based on pre-computed classifier ROCs is clever and elegant. It provides a clear mechanism to "bake in" knowledge about classifier deficiencies into the zero-shot model itself.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. It includes controlled noise experiments that provide clear insight into the method's behavior, extensive tests on three diverse and standard benchmarks, and a meaningful ablation study that isolates the contributions of different model components. The reported performance gains over a strong and widely-used baseline (DAP) are substantial.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the clear novelty and strong results, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws in its presentation, methodological clarity, and experimental reporting. These issues, in aggregate, detract from the quality of the submission and prevent an unreserved recommendation for acceptance.

1.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript lacks the level of polish expected for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Footnotes:** Footnote 1 is grammatically incomplete and placed awkwardly mid-sentence, disrupting readability. Footnotes 2 and 4 define standard concepts (class/attribute, ROC) that should be familiar to the NIPS audience and are thus superfluous. Footnote 5 contains only a URL, which should be a proper citation.
    *   **Mathematical Notation:** The large parentheses in Equations (1) and (2) (`´` and `„...«`) are non-standard and should be replaced with standard LaTeX delimiters.
    *   **Citations:** The ordering of citations appears inconsistent (e.g., `[10, 18, 8]` vs. `[3, 18, 30]`). The reference list contains several formatting errors (e.g., extra commas in references [2], [3], [16], [28]; missing information in [5]; missing year in [21]).
    *   **Clarity:** The description of the validation data usage is slightly confusing. The text states "reserves 20%...as ROC validation data, then pools it with the remaining 80% to train the final attribute classifiers." Later it says, "we use only 80% of the attribute-labelled images to train attribute classifiers." This is contradictory and needs to be clarified. Does the final SVM see 100% of the data or 80%? This is critical for assessing the fairness of the baseline comparisons.

2.  **Methodological Ambiguity:**
    *   **Attribute Dependencies:** The paper claims that "our formulation properly accounts for dependencies between attributes" because of the recursive propagation of validation data `DV(n)`. This claim is overstated. The model only accounts for dependencies *induced by the tree structure*. That is, the choice of a split at a parent node influences the data distribution (and thus ROC estimates) at its children. However, the split criterion at each node is still univariate, considering only one attribute at a time. It does not model correlations between attributes directly, for instance by using multivariate splits.
    *   **Signature Uncertainty:** Section 3.2.3, which describes a method for handling uncertainty in the class signatures, is a potentially important contribution. However, it is entirely relegated to the supplementary material with only a single sentence in the main paper. A core component of the proposed framework should be at least summarized in the main text.
    *   **Few-Shot Extension:** The combination of two information gain terms in Equation (6) via a weighted sum is heuristic. `IG_zero` is computed on abstract signatures, while `IG_basic` is computed on attribute predictions from real images. It is not clear if these two quantities are on a comparable scale, and a simple linear interpolation might not be optimal. The process for cross-validating `λ` in a true few-shot setting (where hold-out data is scarce) is also not detailed.

3.  **Experimental Details:**
    *   **DAP Comparison:** The paper highlights that its method outperforms DAP even when using only 80% of the data for attribute training. For a fair comparison, the DAP baseline should also be run with attribute classifiers trained on the same 80% partition. Was this done? If DAP was run with classifiers trained on 100% of the data, the comparison is not entirely fair, and the performance gap might be different under identical conditions.
    *   **State-of-the-Art Claim:** In Section 4.1, the paper claims its method with discovered attributes from [30] "achieving the state-of-the-art on AwA". However, the text qualifies this by saying it performs "comparably". Looking at Figure 2b, the bar for "Ours + [30]" appears to be slightly lower than the bar for "[30]". This claim should be phrased more precisely.

**Recommendation**

I am recommending **rejection** for this paper.

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper addresses a very important and practical problem in ZSL.
    *   The core idea is highly novel, intuitive, and effective.
    *   The empirical results show a clear and significant improvement over strong baselines on multiple datasets.

*   **Arguments for rejection:**
    *   The paper suffers from numerous distracting issues in formatting, clarity, and presentation that fall short of NIPS standards.
    *   There are several points of methodological ambiguity, including an overstated claim about handling attribute dependencies and insufficient detail on key components (signature uncertainty, few-shot CV).
    *   Critical details regarding the experimental comparison to the main baseline (DAP) are unclear, making it difficult to fully assess the reported gains.

While the central contribution is strong, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication. The large number of minor flaws and ambiguities collectively amount to a significant issue. I believe the paper has the potential to be an excellent contribution, but it requires a thorough revision to address the points raised above. I would be enthusiastic to see a revised version of this work in a future venue.
