This paper addresses the question of estimating dirty models, which are combinations of "simple parameters" whose complexities are individually regular. The paper proposes a subset selection strategy that makes quadratic approximation possible. The theoretical development is based on the notion of decomposition criteria.

I must acknowledge that I have not worked very closely in this area, so my comments are essentially those of someone generally knowledgeable in machine learning and model selection.

It seems that this work is a well-organized combination of ideas that have been published before, such as reducing atomic criteria through active subset selection (ICML 2014), and using quadratic approximation for sparse inverse covariance estimation (NIPS 2011), but this work has now been done in a more general setting of decomposition criteria. I find this elegant, but some questions remain.

The algorithmic achievement of 10x speed does not seem very impressive; such factors are usually easily achieved by optimizing the implementation, thus the question becomes how easy it is to optimize the most demanding parts of the algorithm (so-called parallelization, etc.). Very practically oriented for someone, this achievement may not seem great.

The presentation of the theory is generally quite clear, even if somewhat concise due to page limits. The writing is not flawless – some linguistic errors distract and hinder understanding. The use of quotations as part of sentences is generally discouraged and creates new grammatical problems: should we write "[7, 5] consider the estimate..." or "[7, 5] consider the estimate..."? Sometimes bad punctuation creates problems like "[14] use a superposition instead..." or "we consider a proximal instead..."

In the worst case, the sentences are invalid, incomprehensible, such as the sentence on page 2 "Overall, our algorithm ...."

Writing formulas inside the text creates a whole set of problems in itself, and sometimes the result is strange – very often we see a line ending with an equals sign (so-called 0 = \newline). On page 7, the formula violates the margin as does figure 1 and table 1 on page 8.

Generally, it is customary for a paper to have some kind of conclusion or discussion. Now there is nothing like that. For example, a discussion of future directions would be welcome, as it is not clear to me where to go from here.

In algorithm 1, line 7, the superscript (t) should probably be (r), and updating the sum on line 8 seems odd (variables should be updated instead of expressions).

The theoretical development seems interesting for using quadratic approximation for dirty models with decomposition criteria, but it is not clear to me how big a step this is in practice. To some extent, this work seems like the end of a branch of development, not an interesting new beginning. The editing is not very good.
