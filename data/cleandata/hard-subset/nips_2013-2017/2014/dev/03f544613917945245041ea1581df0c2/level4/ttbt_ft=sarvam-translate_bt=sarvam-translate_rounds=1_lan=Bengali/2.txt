This paper discusses the question of estimating the so-called "dirty models," where the parameter is a collection of "simple parameters" whose complexities are individually regularized. The paper proposes a subspace selection technique that makes quadratic approximation possible. The theoretical development is based on the concept of a decompोजable norm.

I must admit that I haven't paid much attention to this subject, so my comments are mainly the opinions of someone with general knowledge of machine learning and model selection.

It seems that the work is a neat combination of some concepts that have been published before, such as reducing nuclear norms through active subspace selection (ICML 2014), and using quadratic approximations for sparse inverse covariance estimation (NIPS 2011), but the work is now done in a more general context of decompोजable norms. It seems polished to me, but some questions remain.

The 10-fold speed increase in the algorithm doesn't seem very impressive; such things can usually be easily achieved by optimizing the implementation. So, the question is how easy it is to optimize the most difficult parts of the algorithm (such as paralizing, etc.). This achievement may not seem very good to a practical-minded person.

The presentation of the theory is generally quite clear, although it may be a bit concise due to the limitation of the number of pages. The writing is not flawless – some language errors are so confusing that they hinder comprehension. Using quotations as sentences is generally discouraged, and it creates new grammatical problems: should we write "[7, 5] consider..." or "[7, 5] considering..."? Sometimes weak punctuation creates problems like "[14] instead use a superposition..." or "we consider a proximal instead..."

Worst of all, the sentences are so grammatically incorrect that they go beyond comprehension, such as "Overall, our algorithm ...." on page 2.

Using formulas in the text creates some problems of its own, and sometimes the results are strange – often we see a line ending with an equal sign (e.g., 0 = \newline). On page 7, the formula violates the margin, as in Figure 1 and Table 1 on page 8.

In general, it is normal for a paper to have a conclusion or discussion. There is nothing there now. For example, future directions can be discussed, since it is not clear to me where to go from here.

In line 7 of algorithm 1, the superscript (t) should probably be (r), and the sum update in line 8 looks strange (the variable should be updated instead of the expression).

The theoretical development of using quadratic approximations for dirty models with decompोजable regulators seems interesting, but it is not clear to me how big a step it is in reality. In some cases, the work seems to me more like the end of a branch of development than an interesting new direction. The editing is not good enough.
