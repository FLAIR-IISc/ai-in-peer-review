This paper discusses the question of estimating the so-called "dirty models," where the parameters are a collection of "simple parameters" whose complexities are individually regular. The paper proposes a subspace selection technique that makes quadratic approximation possible. Theoretical development is based on the concept of a decomposable norm.

I must admit that I haven't paid much attention to this subject, so my comments are mainly the opinions of someone with a general knowledge of machine learning and model selection.

It seems the work is a neat combination of some concepts that have been published before, such as reducing nuclear rules through active subspace selection (ICML 2014), and using quadratic approximations for sparse inverse covariance estimation (NIPS 2011), but the work is now done in a more general context of decomposable norms. It seems polished to me, but some questions remain.

The algorithm's 10-fold speed increase doesn't seem very impressive; such things can usually be easily achieved by optimizing the implementation. So, the question is how easy it is to optimize the most difficult parts of the algorithm (such as parallelization, etc.). This achievement may not seem very good to a practically-minded person.

The presentation of the theory is generally quite clear, although it may be a bit concise due to the limitation of page numbers. The writing is not flawless - some language errors are so confusing that they hinder comprehension. Using quotations as sentences is generally discouraged and creates new grammatical problems: should we write "[7, 5] should be considered..." or "[7, 5] considering..."? Sometimes weak punctuation creates problems like "[14] instead use a superposition..." or "we consider a proximal instead of..."

Worst of all, the sentences are so grammatically incorrect that they go beyond comprehension, such as "Overall, our algorithm ...." on page 2.

Using formulas in the text creates some problems of its own and sometimes the results are strange - often we see a line ending with an equals sign (e.g., 0 = \newline). On page 7, the formula violates the margin, as in Figure 1 and Table 1 on page 8.

In general, it is normal for a paper to have a conclusion or discussion. There is nothing there now. For example, future aspects can be discussed, since it is not clear to me where to go from here.

In line 7 of algorithm 1, the superscript (t) should probably be (r), and the sum update in line 8 looks strange (the expression should be updated instead).

The theoretical development of using quadratic approximations for dirty models with decomposable regulators seems interesting, but in reality, it is not clear to me how big a step it is. In some cases, the work seems like the end of a branch of development rather than an interesting new direction. The editing is not good enough.
