The authors generalize the results published in [11] and [12] to a more general context. The proposed computational framework works for the following:

(a) Strongly convex and doubly differentiable loss functions.
(b) Partition regularization functions.

The theoretical analysis seems reasonable, and the numerical results are also credible. (b) is natural for sparse learning problems, but (a) seems somewhat limited, as many sparse learning problems are formulated as strongly non-convex.

Here are my two concerns:

(1) Equation (16) seems strong. Can the authors provide some examples?
(2) Are block coordinate descent algorithms also competitive for solving optimization problems of order 5.2? Can the authors provide a detailed comparison?

Response to the objection: Even if we consider statistical errors, we generally do not need "very accurate" solutions. To more broadly compare the proposed algorithm with first-order block coordinate descent algorithms, the authors may need to conduct more experiments, such as classification errors versus time in multi-task learning. My assumption is that local quadratic convergence may not be as competitive in the case of statistical errors. However, I agree that getting a "very accurate" solution will not hurt if the computational resources are affordable.

Overall, I am positive about the quality of this paper. I would like this paper to be published in NIPS.
