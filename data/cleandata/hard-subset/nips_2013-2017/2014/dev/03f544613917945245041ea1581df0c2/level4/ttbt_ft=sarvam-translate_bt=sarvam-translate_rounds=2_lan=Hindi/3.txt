The authors extended the results given in [11] and [12] to more general settings. The proposed computational framework works for the following:

(a) Strong convex and twice differentiable loss functions.
(b) Decentralized optimization tasks.

The theoretical analysis seems reasonable, and the numerical results are also reliable. (b) is natural for sparse learning problems, but (a) seems a bit restrictive, as many sparse learning problems are formulated as non-strong convex problems.

Here are my two concerns:

(1) Equation (16) seems strong. Can the authors provide some examples?
(2) The block coordinate descent algorithm for solving the optimization problem in Section 5.2 is also competitive. Can the authors provide some detailed comparisons?

Answer to the contradiction: Even if we consider statistical error, we usually do not need a "very accurate" solution. To more broadly compare the proposed algorithm with the first-order block coordinate descent algorithm, the authors may need to do more experimentation, for example, classification error versus time in multi-task learning. I think local quadratic convergence may not be as competitive in terms of statistical error. But I agree that there is no harm in obtaining a "very accurate" solution if the computational resources are affordable.

Overall, I am positive about the quality of this paper. I would like to see this paper published in the NIPS.
