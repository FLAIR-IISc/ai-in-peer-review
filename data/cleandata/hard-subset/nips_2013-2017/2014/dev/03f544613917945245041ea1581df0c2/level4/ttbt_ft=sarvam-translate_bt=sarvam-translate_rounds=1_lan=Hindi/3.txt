The authors extended the results given in [11] and [12] to more general settings. The proposed computational framework works for the following:

(a) Robustly convex and twice differentiable loss functions.
(b) Decentralized optimization functions.

The theoretical analysis seems reasonable, and the numerical results are also reliable. (b) is natural for sparse learning problems, but (a) seems a bit restrictive, as many sparse learning problems are formulated as non-robustly convex problems.

Here are my two concerns:

(1) Equation (16) seems robust. Can the authors provide some examples?
(2) The block coordinate descent algorithm for solving the optimization problem in Section 5.2 is also competitive. Can the authors provide some detailed comparisons?

Answer to the contradiction: Even if we consider the statistical error, we usually do not need a "very accurate" solution. To more broadly compare the proposed algorithm with the first-order block coordinate descent algorithm, the authors may need to do more experiments, for example, timing versus classification error in multi-task learning. I guess local quadratic convergence may not be as competitive in terms of statistical error. But I agree that there is no harm in obtaining a "very accurate" solution if the computational resources are affordable.

Overall, I am positive about the quality of this paper. I would like to see this paper published in NIPS.
