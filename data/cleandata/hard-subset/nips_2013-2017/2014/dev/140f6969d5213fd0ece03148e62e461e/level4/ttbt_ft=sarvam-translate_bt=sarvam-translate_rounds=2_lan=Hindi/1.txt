This paper presents a new approach to representing multiple facial scenes using a neural-net-like model, incorporating deterministic and probabilistic units. The network is trained to identify and retrieve the scene. It can also be used to synthesize a new scene. Generally, this paper does a good job of distinguishing the factors contributing to the success of the method, and the results seem quite good.

I wish the authors had presented more evidence as to why they chose to design their model in this way. For one, it's not clear whether a non-deterministic approach is really needed in this case – could they have built a model that is fully feed-forward with deterministic neurons? This would certainly have avoided the need for a variable/critical sample estimate.

**Quality:** Generally, a good paper with an interesting and well-established model is well-evaluated on a competitive benchmark.
**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to read, for the most part.
**Originality:** In some ways, this approach is similar to the work of Geoff Hinton and colleagues, who use a stack of restricted Boltzmann machines, where the top RBM can be labeled as one side.
**Importance:** This is likely to be of interest to the facial recognition community, but also to other researchers working on problems where a hybrid deterministic and generative approach might be a good solution.
**Notes and Questions:**
- How did the authors create the structure in Figure 2? What is the insight behind the design options?
- The authors should define q (h) at line 165.
- Is the MCEM method important for the presented model? More analysis is needed on why this particular optimization method was used, as MCEM is not a widely used method in the community.
- For the estimate: how large is S at line 235? How expensive is the estimate generally? Some numbers and O () notation results would be good.
- Is an LDA necessary to get the best results? What do the results look like without an LDA?
- I don't think the authors should use the tangent with the way the brain works, except very rarely. There is not much evidence that the presented method is particularly close to how humans recognize or see faces.
- Did the authors test their approach in the labeled wild benchmark? If so, do they have results to report?

An interesting new approach to solving the problem of facial recognition using a model like a graphical neural net trained to recognize input faces and retrieve their scenes. Competitive results on the MPIE benchmark and interesting concrete experiments.
