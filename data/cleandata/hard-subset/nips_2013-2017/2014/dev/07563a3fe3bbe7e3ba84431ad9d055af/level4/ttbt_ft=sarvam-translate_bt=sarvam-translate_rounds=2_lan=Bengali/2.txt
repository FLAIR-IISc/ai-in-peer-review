This paper presents a new approach to unsupervised feature learning with discriminatory supervision. Given a set of unlabeled images, 'seed' patches are extracted, which serve as alternative classes for training. For each of these seed images, various transformations are applied within the seed classes to create training examples. A convolutional network is trained on the patches to predict the seed classes. After training, a new image is passed through to extract features, extracting feature maps at each layer and combining them. This paper includes tests on several standard image classification benchmarks, including additional tests to determine the effectiveness of the learned feature transformations and the robustness of the unlearnable features.

The proposed method is simple and demonstrates strong performance on each classification task. Additional analysis, particularly the effects of removing the transformations, is highly welcomed. The paper is written with sufficient information to reproduce the results of most readers.

Detailed comments/questions:
- Could you please explain in more detail about patch extraction? Are you simply sampling a large number of patches, calculating gradients for each, and selecting the top-n?
- You should mention that the STL-10 dataset uses 96x96 image size, with a preference for 32x32 patch size to provide context.
- Was the 32x32 patch size intentionally chosen? Do you have any idea about selecting the patch size as a function of the image size?
- Section 4.1: It would be really helpful if one or two images, perhaps a convolutional network during surrogate training and another depicting feature extraction, were included. The detail is quite clear for a reader familiar with this work, but may be difficult for those unfamiliar to understand.
- From a practical standpoint, given a small number of labeled datasets, one of the easiest things to try is to feed the images through a pre-trained convolutional network in ImageNet and train a linear classifier on these features. I think it would be beneficial to include these results in Table 1, although they fall under a different section of the algorithm (you could include it in a separate block in the table). It would be interesting to see how your results compare (at least in Caltech 101, your results are equivalent to DeCAF).
- How much does the surrogate task's validity serve as a proxy for the classification task's validity? Does the network's classification task also have the best validity performance of the surrogate task? Figure 3 indicates that this may be true. Table 1 in the supplementary material presents classification accuracy with several networks. I would highly recommend that both the surrogate validity score and the classification validity score for each of these tasks be included (possibly in a separate table), to see how well they align.
- Supplementary, Table 2: How does the bias differ from the results of Table 1 with the same network?
- To my knowledge, the best published result on STL-10 is "Multi-Task Bayesian Optimization" 70.1% +- 0.6% (Swersky et al, NIPS 2013). They first extracted k-means feature maps and then achieved their results by training a convolutional network on top of it. By fixing the k-means features, the network is much less sensitive to over-fitting. A strong baseline that I would recommend the authors try, if they have the time, is to include every transformation they use in the paper. This gives a relatively easy way to run convolutional networks on small labeled datasets that I suspect will be very competitive with your method. In short, this is a nice paper that provides a simple algorithm that gets very good results on standard benchmarks. I suspect the generality of this method will lead to several interesting research directions. For this reason, I recommend adoption.
