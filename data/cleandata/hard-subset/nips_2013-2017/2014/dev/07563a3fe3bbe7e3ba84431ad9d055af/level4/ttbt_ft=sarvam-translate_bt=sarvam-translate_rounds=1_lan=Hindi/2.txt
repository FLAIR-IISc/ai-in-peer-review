This paper presents a new approach to differential unsupervised feature learning. Given an unlabeled set of images, 'seed' patches are extracted that act as surrogate classes for training. For each of these seed images, various types of transformations are applied to generate training examples within the seed classes. A convolutional network is trained on top of the patches to predict the seed classes. After training, features are extracted by forward propagating a new image, extracting feature maps at each layer, and pooling them. With additional experimentation to determine the effectiveness of the learned feature transformations and invariant properties, the paper is applied to several standard image classification benchmarks.

The proposed approach is simple and shows strong performance on each of the classification tasks. Additional analysis, particularly the effect of removing the transformations, was welcomed. The paper is clearly written for the most part, providing enough information for the reader to reproduce the results.

Detailed Comments / Questions:
- Could you elaborate more on the patch extraction? Are you simply sampling a large number of patches, computing gradients for each, and selecting the top-n?
- You should mention that the STL-10 dataset uses 96x96 images in the first paper to reference the choice of 32x32 patch size.
- Was the 32x32 patch size chosen arbitrarily? Do you have any intuition about selecting the patch size as a function of image sizes?
- Section 4.1: One or two figures, perhaps one depicting the convnet during surrogate training and the other depicting feature extraction, would be really helpful. The details are quite clear to a reader familiar with the work in this line, but may be challenging for those who are not.
- From a practical standpoint, given a dataset with a small amount of labeled data, one of the simplest things to try is feeding images through a pre-trained convolutional net on ImageNet and training a linear classifier on top of these features. I think it would be beneficial to include these results in Table 1, even if they fall under a different category of algorithms (you could include it in a separate block of the table). It would be interesting to see how your results compare (at least on Caltech 101, your results are equivalent to Decaf).
- How much does validation on the surrogate task serve as a proxy for validation on the classification task? Does the network with the best validation performance on the surrogate task also perform the best validation on the classification task? Figure 3 indicates that this may be true. Table 1 in the supplementary material presents classification accuracy with several networks. I would highly recommend including both the surrogate validation score as well as the classification validation score for each task, to see how well they match.
- Supplementary, Table 2: How does the diagonal differ from the results in Table 1 with the same network?
- To my knowledge, the best published result on STL-10 is 70.1% +- 0.6% from "Multi-Task Bayesian Optimization" (Swaricki et al, NIPS 2013). They obtained their results by first extracting feature maps with k-means and training a convolutional network on these. By fixing the k-means features, the network is much less prone to overtraining. A strong baseline that I would advise the authors to try, if time permits, is to use the same approach they do but include every transformation you use in the paper. This gives a relatively straightforward way to run the convnet on small labeled datasets, I suspect it would be very competitive with your approach. In summary, this is a good paper with a simple algorithm that achieves very good results on standard benchmarks. I suspect the generality of this method should lead to many interesting research directions. For these reasons, I recommend acceptance.
