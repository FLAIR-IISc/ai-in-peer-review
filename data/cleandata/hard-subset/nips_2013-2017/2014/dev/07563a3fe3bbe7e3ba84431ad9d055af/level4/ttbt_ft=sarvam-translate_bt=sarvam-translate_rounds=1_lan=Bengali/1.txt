This paper describes a new method for training convolutional neural networks (CNNs) unsupervised, so that the model learns to remain unchanged even when subjected to simple transformations.

The authors' proposed method is simple and somewhat elegant. The idea is to train the CNN to distinguish only patches of images and their transformations from other patches of images and their transformations.

This method teaches the model to remain unchanged even when subjected to simple transformations. However, as the authors note, this method is at risk of "collision," where the individual image patches – which the model will try to distinguish – share the same content.

Although the method is new and simple, it may not always be clear (at least not to me). It is an ingenious idea, and I believe, with some minor modifications (see below), it is worthy of publication.

The authors also present theoretical results that, according to their claim, support the argument that the model is learning unchanged features. I did not find this analysis particularly compelling, as it essentially states that if the model can find a perfect unchanged feature representation, it will achieve global optimization of the objective function. This is clear from the definition of the objective function. Perhaps I missed something more subtle.

The empirical investigation of the proposed method is relatively thorough and insightful, testing the potential weaknesses described above with increasing the number of substitute "classes" (or base training patches). The experimental results also include an evaluation of the unchanged features of the learned representations. Empirically, this work is relatively reliable and mature.

My only objection is that while presenting state-of-the-art results for STL-10, CIFAR-10, and Caltech-101, the authors failed to compare them with actual state-of-the-art results (especially for CIFAR-10). They mention that they are not comparing with standard discriminatively trained CNN models – the intention behind this distinction seems pointless and somewhat self-serving. The problem is that the paper's presentation clearly gives the reader the impression that this method is close to state-of-the-art for all datasets. This is clearly not true, and it is unacceptable to have this impression in the reader's mind. I only request that the authors provide actual state-of-the-art results for each of these datasets, with and without dataset transformations.

A good paper with a simple, but ingenious idea, well-investigated empirically.
