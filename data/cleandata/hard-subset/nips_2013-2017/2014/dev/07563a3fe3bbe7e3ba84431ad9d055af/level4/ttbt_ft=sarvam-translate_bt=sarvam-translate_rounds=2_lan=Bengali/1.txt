This paper describes a new method for training convolutional neural networks (CNNs) so that the model learns without changing itself.

The authors' proposed method is simple and somewhat elegant. The idea is to train the CNNs to distinguish only between patches of images and their transformations, and not between other images and their transformations.

This method teaches the model to remain unchanged even in the case of simple transformations. However, as the authors note, this method is at risk of "collision," where individual image patches – which the model will try to distinguish – share the same content.

Although the method is new and simple, it may not always be clear (at least not to me). It is an intelligent idea, and I believe, with some minor modifications (see below), it is worthy of publication.

The authors also present theoretical results that, according to their claim, support the argument that the model is learning unchanged features. I did not find this analysis particularly appealing, as it essentially states that if the model can find a perfect unchanged feature representation, it will achieve global optimization of the objective function. This is clear from the definition of the objective function. Perhaps I missed something more subtle.

The empirical investigation of the proposed method is relatively thorough and insightful, testing the potential weaknesses described above by increasing the number of alternative "classes" (or base training patches). The empirical results also include an evaluation of the unchanged features of the learned representations. Empirically, this work is relatively reliable and mature.

My only objection is that while presenting state-of-the-art results for STL-10, CIFAR-10, and Caltech-101, the authors failed to compare them with their actual state-of-the-art results (especially for CIFAR-10). They noted that they are not comparing with standard discriminatively trained CNN models – the purpose behind this difference seems meaningless and somewhat self-serving. The problem is that the paper's presentation clearly gives the reader the impression that this method is close to state-of-the-art for all datasets. This is clearly not true, and it is unacceptable for the reader to have this impression. I only request that the authors provide the actual state-of-the-art results for each of these datasets, with and without dataset transformations.

A good paper presents a simple, but intelligent idea, well-investigated empirically.
