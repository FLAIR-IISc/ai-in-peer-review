This paper presents a new approach to unsupervised feature learning with discriminatory supervision. Given a set of unlabeled images, 'seed' patches are extracted, which serve as alternative classes for training. For each of these seed images, various transformations are applied to create training examples within the seed classes. A convolutional network is trained on the patches to predict the seed classes. After training, features are extracted by forward propagating a new image, extracting feature maps at each layer and pooling them. The paper also includes experiments on several standard image classification benchmarks, including additional tests to determine the effectiveness of the learned feature transformations and the robustness of the unlearnable features.

The proposed method is simple and demonstrates strong performance on each classification task. Additional analysis, particularly the effects of removing the transformations, is highly welcomed. The paper is written with sufficient information for most readers to reproduce the results.

Detailed comments/questions:
- Could you please elaborate more on the patch extraction? Are you simply sampling a large number of patches, calculating gradients for each, and selecting the top-N?
- You should mention that the STL-10 dataset uses 96x96 images, with the choice of 32x32 patch sizes to provide context.
- Was the 32x32 patch size intentionally chosen? Do you have any idea about selecting the patch size as a function of the image size?
- Section 4.1: It would be really helpful if one or two images, perhaps one convolutional network during surrogate training and the other depicting feature extraction, were included. The description is quite clear for a reader familiar with this work, but may be difficult for those unfamiliar to understand.
- From a practical perspective, given a small number of labeled datasets, one of the easiest things to try is to feed images through a pre-trained convolutional network in ImageNet and train a linear classifier on these features. I think it would be beneficial to include these results in Table 1, even though they fall under a different section of the algorithm (you could include it in a separate block in the table). It would be interesting to see how your results compare (at least in Caltech 101, your results are equivalent to DeCAF).
- How much does the surrogate task's validation serve as a proxy for the classification task's validation? Does the surrogate task also have the best validation performance on the network's classification task? Figure 3 indicates that this may be true. Table 1 in the supplementary material presents classification accuracy with several networks. I would highly recommend that both the surrogate validation score and the classification validation score for each of these tasks be included (perhaps in a separate table), to see how well they align.
- Supplementary, Table 2: How does the diagonal differ from the results in Table 1 with the same network?
- To my knowledge, the best published result on STL-10 is "Multi-Task Bayesian Optimization" with 70.1% +- 0.6% (Swersky et al, NIPS 2013). They achieved their results by first extracting k-means feature maps and then training a convolutional network on top of it. By fixing the k-means features, the network is much less sensitive to over-fitting. A strong baseline that I would advise the authors to try, if they have the time, is to include every transformation they use in the paper. This gives a relatively simple way to run the convolutional network on small labeled datasets that I suspect will be very competitive with your method. In summary, this is a nice paper with a simple algorithm that gets very good results on standard benchmarks. I suspect the generality of this method will lead to several interesting research directions. For this reason, I recommend adoption.
