In this paper, the authors propose an interesting concept for learning invariance in unsupervised learning for image classification problems. The core idea is to create surrogate classes with randomly selected groups of patches that undergo transformations. These transformations include 2D functions such as rotation, scaling, translation, and color change. The formal analysis is interesting and sheds further light on why this approach might work.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The tests include several aspects of the problem, including the number of transformations and the size of the network.

One drawback of this approach in the paper is that the two randomly selected patches will be "forced to be different" with very similar content because they will be considered as different surrogate classes. The authors avoid this problem by using a maximum of 3200 patches from large datasets. However, using too few surrogate patches may hinder generalization in the case of larger datasets. Do the authors have any comments on this?

Another good test was to use the same surrogate classes for unsupervised learning and dataset-specific classifier training. For example, since STL-10 and CIFAR-10 are closely related, why did the authors want to retrain on each dataset?

Finally, it would have been interesting to experiment with changing the size of the patches.

The core idea is to create surrogate classes with randomly selected groups of patches that undergo transformations. The concept in the paper is novel and applicable to unsupervised learning.
