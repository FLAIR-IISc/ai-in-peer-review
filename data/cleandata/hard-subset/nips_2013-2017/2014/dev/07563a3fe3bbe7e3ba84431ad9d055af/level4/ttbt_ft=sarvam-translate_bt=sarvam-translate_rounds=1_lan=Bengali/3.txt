In this paper, the authors propose an interesting idea for learning invariance in unsupervised learning for image classification problems. The core concept is to create surrogate classes with groups of randomly selected patches undergoing transformations. These transformations are 2D functions, such as rotation, scaling, translation, and color change. The formal analysis is interesting and sheds further light on why this method might work.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The experiments include several aspects of the problem, including the number of transformations and the size of the network.

One drawback of this method in the paper is that two randomly selected patches with very similar content will be "forced apart" because they will be considered as different surrogate classes. The authors circumvent this problem by using a maximum of 3200 patches from large datasets. However, using too few surrogate patches can hinder generalization in the case of larger datasets. Do the authors have any comments on this?

Another good experiment would have been to use the same surrogate classes for unsupervised learning and dataset-specific classifier training. For example, since STL-10 and CIFAR-10 are closely related, why did the authors need to retrain on each dataset?

Finally, it would have been interesting to experiment with changing the size of the patches.

In this paper, the authors propose an interesting idea for learning invariance in unsupervised learning for image classification problems. The core concept is to create surrogate classes with groups of randomly selected patches undergoing transformations. The paper's concept is novel and appealing for unsupervised learning. It is well-written and easy to follow.
