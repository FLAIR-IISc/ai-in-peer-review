This research paper describes a new method for training convolutional neural networks (CNNs) without supervision, allowing the model to learn to remain invariant to common changes.

The method proposed by the authors is simple and somewhat elegant. The idea is simply to train the CNN to distinguish between image patches and their changes, and to distinguish between other image patches and their changes.

This approach allows the model to learn to remain invariant to common changes. However, as the authors note, this method is sensitive to "collisions," where different image patches – which the model will try to distinguish – share similar content.

Although this method is new and simple, it is not necessarily clear (at least to me). It is a clever idea and I think it should be published with some minor changes (see below).

The authors also present a theoretical result that they claim supports the argument that the model learns invariant features. I did not find this analysis particularly compelling because it basically states that if the model gets a correct invariant feature representation, it will achieve the global optimum of the objective function. This seems clear from the definition of the objective function. Perhaps I have missed something more subtle.

The empirical exploration of the proposed approach is relatively thorough and insightful, including experiments carefully exploring the potential vulnerability described above as the number of surrogate "classes" (or base training patches) increases. The experimental results also include an evaluation of the invariant properties of the learned representation. On the empirical side, this work is relatively solid and mature.

My only complaint is that while presenting state-of-the-art results for STL-10, CIFAR-10, and Caltech-101, the authors neglect to provide a comparison with the actual state-of-the-art (especially for CIFAR-10). They say that they are not comparing CNN models trained differentially by standard means – the motivation for this line of sand seems arbitrary and clearly a bit self-serving. The problem is that the presentation in the paper clearly leaves the reader with the impression that this method adopts a state-of-the-art approach for all these datasets. This is clearly not the case and leaving the reader with that impression is not acceptable. I only ask that the authors provide the actual state-of-the-art for all these datasets, with and without dataset changes.

A good paper about a simple, but clever idea, which is empirically well-researched.
