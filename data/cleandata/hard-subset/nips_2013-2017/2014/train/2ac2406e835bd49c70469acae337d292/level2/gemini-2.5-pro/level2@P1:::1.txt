This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the Method of Moments (MoM). The standard approach for learning MHMMs is the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be computationally expensive on large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. This work provides a computationally cheaper alternative. The key insight is to view an MHMM with K components, each having M states, as a single large HMM with KM states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. While existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs can be applied to this larger model, they recover the parameters only up to a permutation of the KM states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The central contribution of this paper is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The authors propose a spectral de-permutation technique that leverages the structure of the learned global transition matrix. By computing a low-rank reconstruction of this matrix corresponding to its top K eigenvalues, they can group the columns using a clustering algorithm to identify the states belonging to each component HMM, thereby recovering the permutation. The paper also proposes a heuristic based on the spectrum of the transition matrix to estimate the number of components K. The method is validated on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating significant speed advantages over EM and comparable clustering accuracy.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete, self-contained algorithm. The core idea of identifying and then resolving the permutation ambiguity is elegant. The theoretical development is divided into a noiseless and a noisy case. The analysis of the noiseless case is clear and correct. The analysis of the more practical noisy case relies on Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3. While the justification for Conjecture 1 is somewhat informal, citing a bound from related work without a full derivation, the intuition is plausible. Similarly, the "spectral longevity" criterion in Lemma 3 for determining the number of clusters K is an interesting heuristic, though its theoretical properties are not fully established. The experimental evaluation supports the main claims of the paper, particularly the computational advantages of the proposed spectral method. The authors are reasonably careful in their evaluation, comparing against EM and also showing the utility of their method as an initializer for EM.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The progression from the model definition, to the equivalence with a block-diagonal HMM, to the permutation problem, and finally to the spectral solution is logical and easy to follow. Lemma 1 provides a clear foundation for the approach. Figure 1 is particularly effective in visually explaining the core problem and the intuition behind the de-permutation algorithm. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the entire procedure. Some sections could benefit from minor improvements. For instance, the notation in Section 3.2.2 is quite dense, and a more intuitive explanation of the different eigenvalues (λG, λL) would be helpful. The description of the real data experiment could be more detailed, for example by specifying the accuracy metric used in Table 1.

**Originality:** The work is novel and builds upon the recent line of research on spectral learning for latent variable models. To my knowledge, this is the first application of a MoM-based spectral algorithm to the problem of learning MHMMs. While the base spectral HMM learning algorithm is from prior work, the identification of the permutation ambiguity as the key obstacle and the development of a spectral clustering method to resolve it are significant and original contributions. This is a clever and non-trivial extension of existing techniques to a more complex and widely used model.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem: the scalable learning of MHMMs. The proposed algorithm offers a substantial computational advantage over EM, which is highly relevant for applications involving large sequence datasets. The results suggest that the method provides a robust, non-random initialization for EM, which can help overcome one of EM's primary weaknesses. This alone is a valuable practical contribution that researchers and practitioners are likely to use. By extending the reach of spectral learning methods to hierarchical models like MHMMs, this work may also inspire similar approaches for other complex latent variable models.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Elegance:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant spectral algorithm for a classical model (MHMM), effectively solving the key challenge of permutation ambiguity.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed method is significantly faster than the standard EM algorithm, making MHMMs more tractable for large-scale problems.
*   **Good Initialization for EM:** The experiments demonstrate that the algorithm provides an excellent initialization for EM, leading to improved performance over random initializations. This is a very useful practical result.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and the core ideas are communicated effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical justification for the noisy case rests on a conjecture and a heuristic for model selection (finding K). While plausible and supported by experiments, more rigorous analysis would strengthen the paper.
*   **Dependence on Spectral Gap:** The method's success relies on a spectral gap condition (`|λGK | > max_k |λL2,k|`). The paper does not provide much discussion on when this condition is likely to hold in practice or how the algorithm behaves when it is violated.
*   **Accuracy:** Experimental results show that the accuracy of the spectral method can be slightly lower than EM, particularly on smaller datasets. Its primary strength may lie more in its speed and utility as an initializer than as a standalone replacement for EM in all scenarios.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, efficient, and well-motivated algorithm for learning MHMMs. The weaknesses, primarily concerning the rigor of the theoretical analysis for the noisy case, are outweighed by the originality of the core idea and its practical significance. The work is a valuable contribution to the literature on both spectral methods and mixture models.
