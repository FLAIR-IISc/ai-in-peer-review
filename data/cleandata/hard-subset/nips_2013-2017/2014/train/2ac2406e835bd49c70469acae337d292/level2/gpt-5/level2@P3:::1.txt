Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with one block per component HMM. Applying spectral learning for HMMs (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang; Anandkumar et al.) yields parameters up to a global permutation of latent states; for MHMMs this permutation tangles states across components and prevents component-wise recovery. The paper exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix to de-permute: in the noiseless case, Ā has K unit eigenvalues (one per component), and Āe reveals K column clusters corresponding to stationary distributions; in the noisy case, the paper proposes a rank-K reconstruction using the dominant eigenvalues and then clusters the columns to recover a mapping of states to components. A “spectral longevity” heuristic is introduced to estimate K by tracking the persistence of dominant eigenvalues under exponentiation. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwritten trajectory dataset show speed advantages over EM and that the spectral estimator can be a strong initializer for EM.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction of MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is correct and useful. The permutation issue is real in spectral learning, and the proposed de-permutation via spectral structure is a plausible solution. The assumptions that each Ak is ergodic/aperiodic with a unique stationary distribution and that Ā is diagonalizable are important and should be stated explicitly early. In the noisy case, the main guarantees are presented as Conjecture 1 with a justification sketch that reuses a sample complexity bound from a different mixture setting; this undercuts the theoretical claims. The “spectral longevity” criterion is an interesting heuristic but lacks formal analysis. Leveraging standard matrix perturbation results (Weyl’s inequality; Davis–Kahan sin-theta theorem) could turn conjectures into theorems under clear spectral gap conditions.
- Completeness: The algorithm description is clear. However, the conditions required by the underlying MoM HMM routine (full column rank of Ō, L ≥ MK, identifiability assumptions for continuous emissions) are only partially discussed, and the implications for practice (feature lifting, noise sensitivity) merit more detail.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments illustrate the de-permutation effect and speed/accuracy trade-offs versus EM; the real-data experiment demonstrates practical utility as an initializer. Missing are stress tests that vary spectral gaps, similarity of stationary distributions across components, and systematic evaluation of K-estimation accuracy. Timing results are helpful, but absolute runtime breakdowns (moment computation vs eigendecomposition vs clustering) would better support scalability claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The equivalence lemma and spectral intuition are clear. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., use consistent symbols for noisy vs noiseless quantities). The longevity definition would benefit from a more intuitive explanation and a pseudo-code snippet describing how K is selected. Explicitly stating all assumptions (ergodicity, diagonalizability, Ō full rank, L ≥ MK) up front would improve readability.

Originality:
The central novelty lies in using the spectral structure of the global transition matrix to resolve the permutation ambiguity for MHMMs and to estimate K. While spectral methods for HMMs and mixtures are established, applying them to MHMMs via block-diagonalization and proposing an eigenvalue “longevity” criterion appear new. Prior MHMM work (e.g., Smyth; Qi et al.) is EM-based; there does not appear to be a prior spectral solution for MHMM de-permutation.

Significance:
MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering; a scalable, initialization-free method is valuable. If robust, the de-permutation approach could have impact for other block-structured latent models. The requirement L ≥ MK is restrictive for many applications with low-dimensional observations and many states/components. The proposed feature lifting workaround is practical but may distort moments and weaken identifiability; discussing this trade-off would clarify applicability.

Constructive feedback and suggested revisions:
Essential:
- Assumptions and guarantees: Clearly state identifiability/regularity assumptions (ergodicity/aperiodicity of Ak, diagonalizability, distinct stationary distributions across components, full column rank of Ō, requirement L ≥ MK). Either prove Conjecture 1 under explicit spectral gap and perturbation conditions using standard matrix perturbation tools, or reframe the claims as heuristic with empirical support and adjust the language accordingly.
- K estimation: Provide a more thorough evaluation of the longevity criterion. Report precision/recall of K estimation across synthetic settings that vary spectral gaps, component similarity (e.g., stationary distributions close or identical), and noise levels (sample sizes, sequence lengths).
- Failure modes: Discuss scenarios where de-permutation may fail (e.g., identical or very similar stationary distributions across components; small spectral gaps; non-diagonalizable or nearly defective Ā; components with different M). Offer practical diagnostics (e.g., separation of column clusters; condition numbers) and mitigation strategies.
- Practical constraints: Detail the computational complexity of moment estimation and eigendecomposition as functions of N, T, L, M, K. Explicitly discuss continuous-emission assumptions and how feature mappings affect identifiability and sample complexity.

Optional:
- Numerical stability: Consider using real Schur decomposition or power iteration/subspace iteration to construct the rank-K approximation more stably than full eigendecomposition for near-defective matrices.
- Baselines: Add baselines such as (i) EM with k-means initialization on simple sequence features; (ii) running spectral HMM per pre-clustered sequences using a rough moment-based pre-clustering, to position the method among practical alternatives.
- Broader datasets: Include additional real-world sequence clustering tasks (e.g., speech, music, motion capture) to demonstrate generality. Release code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear and useful reduction of MHMM to block-diagonal HMM within the spectral learning framework.
- Novel and practical de-permutation strategy with intuitive spectral justification.
- Computational advantages over EM are demonstrated; method serves as a strong EM initializer.
- Topic is timely and relevant to NIPS.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Central guarantees are conjectural; theoretical development is incomplete.
- Applicability is limited by L ≥ MK and full-rank Ō; practical impact depends on feature lifting whose effects are not analyzed.
- Empirical evaluation of K estimation and robustness to challenging spectral regimes is limited.

Overall:
A promising and practically motivated contribution with a neat idea for resolving the permutation ambiguity in MHMM spectral learning. Strengthening the theoretical underpinnings and expanding robustness experiments would substantially improve the work.
