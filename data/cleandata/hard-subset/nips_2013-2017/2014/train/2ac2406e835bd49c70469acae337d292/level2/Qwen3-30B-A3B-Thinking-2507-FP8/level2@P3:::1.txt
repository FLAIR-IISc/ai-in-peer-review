

The paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using the Method of Moments (MoM), addressing the permutation ambiguity that arises when applying MoM to MHMM learning. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, and that spectral properties of the global transition matrix can resolve the permutation ambiguity. The authors demonstrate that the MHMM can be expressed as an HMM with specific global parameters, and they develop a method to recover the individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters using spectral decomposition.

The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with clear lemmas establishing the equivalence between MHMM and HMM with block-diagonal structure, and the spectral properties necessary to resolve permutation ambiguity. The paper provides a conjecture regarding the error bounds for the estimated transition matrix, which appears reasonable based on the cited literature, though a more rigorous proof would strengthen this aspect. The authors demonstrate how to estimate the number of clusters K using spectral longevity, a novel metric that measures the convergence rates of eigenvalue ratios.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. Synthetic experiments show the method correctly recovers the block-diagonal structure and identifies the correct number of clusters even with modest sequence lengths (T=20). The comparison with EM demonstrates that the spectral approach provides comparable accuracy with significantly reduced computational requirements, particularly for large datasets. The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories shows the method can serve as an effective initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with consistent mathematical notation that makes the theoretical contributions accessible. The connection to related work is thorough, with proper citation of Anandkumar et al. for MoM and Smyth for MHMM. The experimental setup is well-described, and results are presented with appropriate metrics and visualizations.

The main limitations concern the spectral conditions required for successful depermutation (|λGK| > maxk∈{1,...,K} |λL2,k|). The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not provide sufficient analysis of when these conditions fail or how to handle such cases. The conjecture about error bounds could be strengthened with a more rigorous proof or additional empirical validation. Additionally, the paper does not explore how the method performs when the number of states per HMM (M) is not uniform across clusters, which might be a common scenario in practice.

The significance of this work is substantial. MHMMs have important applications in sequence clustering, and the proposed method provides a computationally efficient alternative to EM that is particularly valuable for large datasets. The ability to use the method as an initialization for EM enhances its practical utility. The approach could potentially be extended to other hierarchical latent variable models, suggesting broader applicability.

The paper makes a clear contribution distinct from previous NIPS publications on related topics. While Anandkumar et al. have developed spectral learning for single HMMs, this work addresses the more complex MHMM case with a novel resolution of permutation ambiguity. The method significantly advances the state of the art in MHMM learning by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient approach.

Strengths include the novel resolution of permutation ambiguity using spectral properties, the strong theoretical foundation with clear lemmas, the demonstrated computational advantages over EM, and the practical utility as an initialization for EM. The work is well-written and properly contextualized within the literature.

Weaknesses involve the reliance on specific spectral conditions that may not always hold in practice, and the need for stronger theoretical justification of the error bound conjecture. The paper would benefit from additional analysis of failure cases for the spectral conditions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of latent variable models. The theoretical approach is novel, the experimental results are convincing, and the practical implications for large-scale sequence clustering are meaningful. The work addresses a meaningful problem with a well-motivated solution that advances the state of the art. The computational advantages over EM are particularly valuable for real-world applications with large datasets.
