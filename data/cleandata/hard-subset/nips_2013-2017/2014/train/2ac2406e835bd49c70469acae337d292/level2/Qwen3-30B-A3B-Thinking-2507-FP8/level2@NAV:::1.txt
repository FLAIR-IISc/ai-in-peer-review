

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using the Method of Moments (MoM), addressing the computational intractability of EM for large datasets. The core claim is that MHMMs can be viewed as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices, and permutation ambiguity in MoM estimation can be resolved via spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The authors demonstrate recovery of individual HMM parameters and cluster count K through eigenvalue analysis and clustering, with experiments on synthetic and real data.

Strengths include a theoretically sound foundation (Lemma 1 establishing the block-diagonal equivalence) and compelling computational advantages. The synthetic experiments (Fig. 3-4) convincingly show the method's speed (independent of data size) and accuracy comparable to EM, with the real data experiment (Table 1) highlighting its utility as an EM initialization. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented, and the spectral depermutation approach elegantly resolves a key challenge in MHMM learning.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The theoretical justification for noise handling relies on a conjecture (Conjecture 1) without rigorous proof or empirical validation of the error bound, which weakens the claim of robustness. The real data experiment uses a cubic polynomial transformation to satisfy MK < L (a MoM constraint), but this ad hoc step lacks justification and may limit generalizability. Crucially, the paper omits comparisons with recent MHMM methods (e.g., [10] on EM variants), making it difficult to assess novelty against state-of-the-art. The limitation of requiring MK < L is acknowledged but not thoroughly discussed in terms of practical impact.

The paper is well-written and addresses a meaningful problem, but the theoretical gaps and insufficient comparison with contemporary work reduce its significance. To strengthen it, the authors should: 1) replace Conjecture 1 with a proof sketch or empirical validation of the error bound, 2) directly compare against recent MHMM techniques, and 3) discuss the MK < L constraint more critically. Despite these issues, the computational benefits and initialization utility are promising. I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions addressing the theoretical and comparative shortcomings.
