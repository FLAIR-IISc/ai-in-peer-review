This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key idea is to leverage the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM’s global transition matrix to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of this approach both theoretically and empirically, showing promising results on synthetic and real data.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results. The motivation for using MoM, particularly for large datasets, is clearly articulated. The connection between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is a crucial observation, and the subsequent development of the de-permutation technique based on spectral properties is a significant contribution. The discussion of the spectral conditions required for successful de-permutation adds nuance and practical consideration to the algorithm. The experiments demonstrate the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to EM, and the real-data experiment, while somewhat limited by the feature transformation required, provides a plausible demonstration of the approach's utility.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. The theoretical justification, specifically the conjectures relating to the eigenvalue separation and spectral longevity (Lemmas 3 and Conjecture 1), requires more rigorous analysis. While the intuition is sound, the presented "justification" for Conjecture 1 relies on sample complexity results from a related paper without a clear adaptation to the MHMM context. A more detailed analysis of the noise sensitivity and its impact on the de-permutation accuracy would be valuable.  The experimental evaluation, while reasonable, could be expanded. For example, varying the number of hidden states (M) and the number of mixtures (K) could provide more insight into the algorithm’s performance. A more detailed comparison with existing MHMM learning techniques, beyond just EM, would also strengthen the results. Finally, the discussion of related work could be more comprehensive; for example, citing work on spectral methods for clustering and model selection might be beneficial.

Specifically, the paper mentions using a cubic polynomial feature transformation for the real data experiment, but doesn’t discuss the potential impact of this transformation on the results. How sensitive are the results to the degree of the polynomial? Did they try other feature transformations?  The explanation of the spectral longevity metric (Lλ̃K′ ) is also slightly unclear; a more intuitive explanation or illustration would improve understanding.

Arguments for acceptance:

*   Novel approach to MHMM learning based on MoM and spectral techniques.
*   Addresses a practical limitation of EM for large datasets.
*   Provides a theoretically motivated solution to the permutation ambiguity problem.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real data.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   Theoretical justification relies on conjectures with limited rigorous analysis.
*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The impact of the feature transformation on the real-data results is not fully addressed.



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a clear presentation and a novel approach to a challenging problem. Addressing the theoretical gaps and expanding the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper. While the current form is not groundbreaking, the originality and potential impact of the work warrant consideration for acceptance. A revision addressing the points above is recommended.
