**Summary**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the method of moments. The core contribution is an extension of spectral learning techniques, previously developed for single HMMs, to the more complex MHMM setting. The proposed approach is based on the key insight that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, can be viewed as a single large HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. While standard spectral HMM learning can be applied to this larger model, it recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main technical contribution of this work is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. This is achieved by analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. The paper demonstrates that the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of this matrix (using its top K eigenvectors) should cluster according to the K underlying HMMs, allowing for the recovery of the block-diagonal structure and, consequently, the parameters of the individual HMMs. The work also proposes a heuristic based on the eigenvalues to estimate the number of mixture components, K. The effectiveness of the proposed algorithm is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data, where it is shown to be significantly faster than the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**

The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The central idea of resolving the permutation ambiguity via spectral clustering of the transition matrix columns is elegant and well-motivated. The theoretical development, particularly Lemma 1 and Lemma 2, clearly establishes the foundation for the proposed approach in the noiseless case.

However, the theoretical analysis of the noisy case could be strengthened.
*   **Conjecture 1:** The sample complexity bound is presented as a conjecture, justified by analogy to prior work. While the justification is plausible, a more formal derivation, even under simplifying assumptions, would significantly bolster the paper's theoretical contributions.
*   **Lemma 3:** The method for determining the number of clusters K via "spectral longevity" (Equation 7) is an interesting heuristic, but its practical implementation and robustness are not fully elaborated. The definition involves a sum over an infinite range of exponents, and the algorithm description simply mentions using a "sufficiently large range." Further details on how this range is chosen and the sensitivity of the results to this choice would improve clarity and reproducibility. The underlying idea of using the eigengap is standard, and perhaps framing the model selection criterion more directly in terms of the ratio of consecutive eigenvalues could provide a more robust and well-understood alternative.

The experimental evaluation is solid, providing evidence for the algorithm's performance. The authors are honest about the results, showing cases where EM achieves higher accuracy, while rightly highlighting the proposed method's significant speed advantage and its value as an initialization strategy for EM.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The progression from the model definition to the proposed solution is logical and easy to follow. The problem of permutation ambiguity is clearly stated, and the proposed solution is well-motivated. Figure 1 provides an excellent visual illustration of the core idea behind the de-permutation algorithm.

For improved clarity and reproducibility, the following suggestions could be considered:
*   **Algorithm 1:** This high-level summary could benefit from more detail. For instance, the specific clustering algorithm used to group the columns of the reconstructed transition matrix (e.g., k-means, as mentioned in the experiments) should be specified within the algorithm block itself.
*   **Notation:** The distinction between the permuted estimate `ĀP` and the noisy estimate `Ā P` can be slightly confusing. Adopting a more distinct notation (e.g., using a hat `^` for estimated quantities) might improve readability.
*   **Real Data Preprocessing:** The feature transformation in the real data experiment is mentioned briefly. A more detailed description of the cubic polynomial transformation used to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint would be beneficial for readers wishing to reproduce or build upon this work.

**Originality**

The paper makes a clear and original contribution. While it builds upon the existing framework of spectral learning for HMMs [1, 3], it addresses a non-trivial extension to the MHMM model class. To this reviewer's knowledge, this is the first work to propose a computationally efficient, non-iterative learning algorithm for MHMMs based on the method of moments. The core contribution—the spectral de-permutation technique to recover the component HMMs—is novel and directly tackles the primary obstacle to applying spectral methods in this context. The work is well-situated within the literature and correctly references the foundational papers on which it builds.

**Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners. The EM algorithm, while widely used for MHMMs, is known to be slow on large datasets and susceptible to poor local optima. This work provides a valuable alternative that is not only orders of magnitude faster (as shown in Figure 4) but also comes with the consistency guarantees inherent to moment-based methods.

The finding that the proposed method serves as an excellent initializer for EM (Table 1) is also a highly significant practical contribution. This hybrid approach combines the speed and good basin of attraction of the spectral method with the fine-tuning capabilities of EM, potentially leading to a new best practice for training MHMMs. Given the wide applicability of MHMMs in sequence clustering tasks (e.g., in bioinformatics, speech, and motion analysis), the proposed algorithm is likely to be used and built upon by others in the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem: efficient learning of MHMMs.
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution to the permutation ambiguity problem that has hindered the application of spectral methods to this model class.
*   The proposed method is computationally much more efficient than the standard EM algorithm.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance and offers a high-quality initialization scheme for EM, which is a significant practical contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance (or for revision):**
*   The theoretical analysis for the noisy case rests on a conjecture and a somewhat heuristic model selection criterion.
*   The experimental validation on real-world data could be extended to a problem with more than two clusters (K>2) to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

Overall, the paper's strengths, particularly its novelty and practical significance, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a valuable contribution to the literature on learning latent variable models.
