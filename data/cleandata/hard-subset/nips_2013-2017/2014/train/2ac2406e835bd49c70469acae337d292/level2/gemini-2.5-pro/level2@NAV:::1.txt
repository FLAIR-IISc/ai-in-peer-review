This paper introduces a novel Method of Moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to treat an MHMM with K components as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Applying standard spectral learning for HMMs to this formulation results in a permutation ambiguity that mixes the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper's main contribution is an elegant de-permutation technique that resolves this ambiguity by leveraging the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By using a low-rank reconstruction based on the top K eigenvalues, the authors demonstrate how to recover the block-diagonal structure and the individual HMM parameters.

The primary strength of this work is its original and computationally efficient approach to the significant problem of scaling MHMM learning. The de-permutation strategy is the key technical insight and is well-motivated. The paper is clearly written, and the experimental results effectively demonstrate the substantial speed-up over the traditional EM algorithm. These aspects address the criteria of originality, clarity, and significance.

However, the paper's quality is affected by weaknesses in its theoretical underpinnings. The analysis hinges on Conjecture 1, which is presented without a formal proof, diminishing the paper's technical soundness. Additionally, the proposed method for identifying the number of clusters K relies on a spectral gap condition that may be fragile in practice. The empirical evaluation, while showing promise on synthetic data, is limited to a single real-world dataset where the method does not consistently outperform randomly initialized EM on its own. This suggests its primary value may be as an efficient and effective initialization scheme for EM, which is a useful but more modest contribution than a full replacement.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable idea for learning MHMMs. The novelty of the de-permutation method and the significant computational advantages are strong arguments for acceptance. However, the incomplete theoretical analysis and limited real-data experiments are notable drawbacks. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to strengthen the theoretical justification for Conjecture 1 and broaden the experimental validation in future work to better situate their method's practical performance against standard baselines.
