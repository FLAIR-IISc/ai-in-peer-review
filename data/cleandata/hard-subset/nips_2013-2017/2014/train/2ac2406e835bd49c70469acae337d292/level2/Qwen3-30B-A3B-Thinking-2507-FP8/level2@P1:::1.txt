

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using the Method of Moments (MoM). The authors make the key insight that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. The main challenge they address is the permutation ambiguity that arises during MoM estimation, which prevents recovery of the individual HMM parameters. They propose a novel method to resolve this ambiguity using the spectral properties of the global transition matrix, even in the presence of estimation noise. The authors also provide a procedure to estimate the number of HMM clusters K under certain spectral conditions.

The paper makes a significant contribution by extending spectral learning methods to MHMMs, which is an important class of models for sequence clustering. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, with well-motivated lemmas and corollaries establishing the mathematical foundation. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly described and includes a practical approach to the permutation problem through spectral clustering of the transition matrix.

The experimental section demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach on both synthetic and real data. The synthetic experiments (Section 4.1) show that the method correctly recovers the block-diagonal structure even with limited data. The comparison with EM (Section 4.2) demonstrates that the spectral method is significantly faster while maintaining comparable accuracy. The real data experiment (Section 4.3) on handwritten character trajectories shows that the proposed method can serve as an effective initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution.

The paper builds well on prior work in spectral learning for HMMs (e.g., Anandkumar et al. in COLT 2012 and Hsu et al. in Journal of Computer and System Sciences 2012) and clearly positions its contribution within the literature. The writing is generally clear, though some parts of the theoretical analysis could benefit from more intuitive explanation.

Strengths:
1. The core insight of viewing MHMM as a special HMM with block-diagonal structure is elegant and well-executed.
2. The method to resolve permutation ambiguity using spectral properties is novel and well-motivated.
3. The experimental results effectively demonstrate the computational advantages and practical utility of the approach.
4. The paper makes a clear connection to real-world applications (handwriting recognition).

Weaknesses:
1. Some theoretical claims are presented as conjectures rather than proven theorems (Conjecture 1), which weakens the theoretical foundation. The authors could strengthen this by providing a more rigorous analysis or better justification for the conjecture.
2. The real data experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation to satisfy the condition MK < L, which feels like a workaround rather than a natural solution to the problem. The authors could better justify why this transformation is appropriate for their application.
3. The paper doesn't compare with other recent approaches to MHMM learning beyond EM, which would help contextualize the significance of their contribution.
4. The explanation of why the spectral properties help resolve the permutation ambiguity could be more intuitive, particularly for readers unfamiliar with spectral methods.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of latent variable model learning. The spectral approach offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMM learning, which is valuable for large-scale applications. The ability to serve as a good initialization for EM is particularly practical.

The significance of this work is substantial, as MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering across various domains. The computational advantages demonstrated in the experiments could enable applications with much larger datasets than currently feasible with EM. The paper advances the state of the art in spectral learning methods and demonstrates how these methods can be extended to more complex models.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. While some theoretical aspects could be strengthened, the practical significance and clear presentation make this a compelling contribution to NIPS. The authors have demonstrated both the theoretical soundness and practical utility of their approach.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on spectral learning and latent variable models. The paper advances the state of the art in MHMM learning and provides a computationally efficient alternative to EM that could be widely adopted in practice.
