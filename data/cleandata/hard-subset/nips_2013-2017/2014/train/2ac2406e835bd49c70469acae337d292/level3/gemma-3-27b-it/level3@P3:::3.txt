This paper presents a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea is to leverage the block-diagonal structure inherent in an MHMM to apply existing MoM algorithms for HMMs, and then address the resulting permutation ambiguity using spectral properties of the global transition matrix. While the proposed de-permutation algorithm appears promising, the paper suffers from several weaknesses in clarity, originality, and presentation.

**Quality:** The technical approach seems sound, building on established work in spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu, Kakade & Zhang, 2014) and MoM. The theoretical justification for the de-permutation algorithm, relying on eigenvalue analysis, is a valuable contribution. However, the proof of Conjecture 1 feels hand-wavy and relies on citing a related result ([1]) without sufficient justification for its direct applicability to this MHMM context. More rigorous analysis would strengthen the paper considerably. The experiments demonstrate the method works, but the synthetic data experiments are somewhat limited in scope. The real data experiment is a positive step, but the feature transformation applied to the UCI dataset complicates the interpretation of the results. The authors are reasonably careful about highlighting limitations, acknowledging the need for further analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured, but suffers from occasional lapses in clarity. Several typos and grammatical errors ("For each e, identify of eigenvalues...") detract from readability and suggest a lack of careful proofreading. The notation is dense and could benefit from more explanation, particularly regarding the manipulation of matrices and the use of the MATLAB colon notation. The connection between Lemma 3 and the practical identification of K could be explained more clearly. The progression of ideas, particularly in Section 3.2.2, feels rushed and requires more careful unpacking for the reader.

**Originality:** The core algorithm is described as a "trivial variant" of Hsu, Kakade & Zhang (2014), which significantly impacts the assessment of originality. While resolving the permutation ambiguity in the MHMM context *is* a novel contribution, the authors downplay the reliance on existing work. A crucial omission is the failure to adequately situate the work within the context of Chaganty & Liang (2013), which also addresses spectral learning of mixture models and provides a potentially relevant comparison point. The paper would benefit significantly from a more thorough discussion of related work and a clearer delineation of its unique contributions.

**Significance:** The problem addressed – efficient learning of MHMMs – is important, as MHMMs are widely used in sequence modeling. The potential to circumvent the computational expense of EM, particularly for large datasets, is a significant advantage. However, the practical impact hinges on the robustness of the de-permutation algorithm and its scalability to more complex scenarios. The experiments, while encouraging, do not definitively establish a clear advantage over existing methods, especially given the limitations described above. If the conjecture concerning the error bound holds and the algorithm proves scalable, this work could be valuable. The potential for the approach to generalize to other hierarchical latent variable models is also promising.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem (efficient MHMM learning).
*   Presents a novel approach to de-permuting components in the spectral learning framework.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results, suggesting the feasibility of the approach.
*   Offers a potential alternative to computationally expensive EM algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies heavily on existing work (Hsu, Kakade & Zhang, 2014), diminishing novelty.
*   Fails to adequately address related work (Chaganty & Liang, 2013).
*   Suffers from clarity issues due to typos, dense notation, and rushed explanations.
*   Theoretical justification (Conjecture 1) lacks rigor and relies on unstated assumptions.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the core idea is interesting and the proposed algorithm shows promise, the significant weaknesses in originality, clarity, and theoretical rigor outweigh the strengths. Addressing the missing related work, strengthening the theoretical analysis, improving the clarity of the presentation (including careful proofreading), and conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation are crucial for improving the paper's quality. As it stands, the contribution is incremental and does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS.
