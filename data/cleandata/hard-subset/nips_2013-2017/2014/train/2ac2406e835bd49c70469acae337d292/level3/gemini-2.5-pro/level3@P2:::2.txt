This paper proposes a spectral, Method of Moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The core idea is to re-frame the MHMM as a single, larger HMM whose transition matrix has a special block-diagonal structure. This insight allows the authors to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, such as those developed by Hsu et al. (2012) and Anandkumar et al. (2012), to estimate the global model parameters.

The main technical challenge, and the paper's central contribution, is resolving the permutation ambiguity that arises from the spectral estimation. Because the states of the global HMM are recovered up to an unknown permutation, the parameters of the individual component HMMs are mixed together. The authors propose a novel and intuitive solution: they analyze the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By assuming each component HMM's transition matrix has a single eigenvalue of 1, they show that the global transition matrix will have K such eigenvalues. In the noiseless case, exponentiating this matrix reveals K distinct blocks of columns corresponding to the K HMMs. The authors extend this idea to the noisy setting by performing a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix using its top K eigenvalues. Clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix provides an estimate of the de-permutation mapping. The paper also provides a heuristic for estimating the number of clusters, K, from the data based on the "longevity" of eigenvalues under exponentiation. The method is evaluated on synthetic and real-world data.

### Strengths

*   **Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its clever solution to the permutation ambiguity problem when applying spectral methods to MHMMs. The idea of viewing the MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is a nice insight, and the subsequent use of matrix exponentiation and clustering to "de-permute" the estimated parameters is novel and elegant. This stabilization step is the key technical contribution.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses an important problem. Learning MHMMs with traditional EM can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. A fast, non-iterative MoM approach is a valuable contribution. If proven robust, this method could be highly impactful for sequence clustering tasks.
*   **Technical Approach:** The theoretical motivation for the de-permutation algorithm is intuitive and well-explained, particularly the connection between the block-diagonal structure of the true transition matrix and the clustering of columns in its exponentiated form.

### Weaknesses

The paper is promising, but it has several weaknesses in its current form that undermine the contribution, primarily concerning clarity, experimental rigor, and engagement with prior work.

*   **Clarity:** The paper's notation and presentation could be significantly improved.
    *   Several symbols and matrices are used without clear definitions or dimensions (e.g., `1_J` in Eq. 2, though `1_M` is defined later). It would be beneficial to define the dimensions of every vector and matrix when it is first introduced.
    *   The notation is not standard. Conforming to established notation from the HMM literature (e.g., using `T` for the transition matrix and `n, m` for observation/state dimensions, as in Hsu et al., 2009) would greatly improve readability for experts in the field.
    *   Corollary 1 is presented in a confusing way. The structure of `lim e→∞ Ā^e` as a block matrix where each block `k` is `v_k 1_M^T` should be stated more explicitly for clarity.

*   **Quality & Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation is not convincing and raises several questions.
    *   The presentation of results is poor. Figure 4, which compares accuracy and speed, is very difficult to read. Results of this nature should be reported in tables with means and standard deviations over multiple trials to allow for proper assessment.
    *   The EM baseline appears unusually weak. In Figure 4, EM's accuracy is surprisingly low, especially given sufficient data. The authors should provide more details about their EM implementation, initialization strategy, and convergence criteria. Reporting the best result from only four restarts may not represent a strong EM baseline. A more thorough comparison is needed to substantiate the claim of "comparable in accuracy."
    *   The real data experiment requires a feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint of the MoM algorithm. This is a significant practical limitation of the approach that should be discussed more transparently as a weakness of the method itself. The conclusion that the spectral method serves as a good initializer for EM is valuable but positions the algorithm as a pre-processing step rather than a standalone replacement.

*   **Originality & Related Work:** The paper needs to better contextualize its contribution. While it cites Chaganty and Liang (2013), it does so only in the context of the experiment. The idea of using spectral estimates to initialize EM is a major alternative to using the spectral method as a standalone algorithm. This line of work should be discussed more thoroughly in the introduction or a related work section to clarify how the proposed method differs from or complements these hybrid approaches.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and elegant algorithm for a non-trivial problem: permutation recovery in spectral learning of MHMMs.
2.  The method is computationally attractive compared to EM, which is a significant advantage for large-scale sequence clustering.
3.  The core idea is intuitive and theoretically well-motivated.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The experimental evaluation is weak and does not convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness compared to a properly tuned EM baseline.
2.  The clarity of the paper is hampered by confusing notation and poor presentation of results.
3.  The paper does not adequately discuss important related work on hybrid spectral-EM methods, and it is not fully transparent about the practical limitations of the proposed approach (e.g., the `MK < L` constraint).

In conclusion, the paper contains a strong core idea but is let down by its execution. The weaknesses in clarity and experimental validation are significant enough that I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. However, if the authors were to thoroughly revise the experimental section with stronger baselines and clearer presentation, improve the notation and clarity, and better contextualize their work, the paper could represent a solid contribution to the field.
