This paper proposes a method of moments based algorithm for learning mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The main idea is to use spectral learning to estimate a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) and then resolve the permutation ambiguity to recover a block-diagonal MHMM structure. The authors demonstrate the validity of their approach on synthetic and real data.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in the area of spectral learning for HMMs and mixture models. The authors build upon the work of Anandkumar et al. [1, 2] on method of moments based algorithms for learning latent variable models. The paper also relates to the work of Hsu et al. [3] on spectral algorithms for learning HMMs.

The strengths of the paper include its solid theoretical foundation and interesting ideas. The authors provide a clear and well-written presentation of their approach, including a detailed description of the algorithm and its underlying assumptions. The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the approach, showing that it can be computationally much cheaper than Expectation-Maximization (EM) while being comparable in accuracy.

However, there are several weaknesses and concerns that need to be addressed. One major concern is that estimating a full HMM first may be impractical for large, sparse mixtures. The authors should clarify when the method is appropriate versus when it is not. Additionally, there is no guarantee that the spectral-learned transition matrix will be (nearly) sparse, and many full matrices may be close to the desired sparse block structure under noise.

Another concern is that the experiments corrupt permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise rather than sampling observed sequences from a mixture of HMMs. The authors should provide experiments that learn from observations to reflect real noise and limited-data effects. Furthermore, Section 4.2 lacks specifics about the mixture HMM parameters (transition and observation matrices), and the authors should provide full parameter details (e.g., in an appendix).

It is also unclear why EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral alone, as EM should typically improve results. The authors should provide an explanation for this phenomenon.

In terms of presentation, there are a few minor issues. There is a typo in the proof, where the subscript of Λ should be K rather than 2. Additionally, the figures have text and numbers that are too small to read.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to learning MHMMs, but significant doubts remain about its practical performance on real datasets. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above to strengthen their paper.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to learning MHMMs using spectral learning and method of moments.
* The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach.
* The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the approach, showing that it can be computationally much cheaper than EM while being comparable in accuracy.

Arguments con acceptance:

* Estimating a full HMM first may be impractical for large, sparse mixtures.
* There is no guarantee that the spectral-learned transition matrix will be (nearly) sparse.
* The experiments do not reflect real noise and limited-data effects.
* Section 4.2 lacks specifics about the mixture HMM parameters.
* EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral alone.

Rating: 6/10

Recommendation: The paper has some interesting ideas and solid theory, but significant doubts remain about its practical performance on real datasets. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above to strengthen their paper. With some revisions to address these concerns, the paper could be a good fit for NIPS.
