This paper presents a novel spectral learning approach for Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), leveraging the Method of Moments (MoM) and addressing the inherent permutation ambiguity in MHMM parameter estimation. The core idea is to formulate an MHMM as a special case of an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling the application of existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs [Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012]. A key contribution lies in a stabilization method, exploiting the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to resolve the permutation issue, under the assumption of a dominant eigenvalue of 1 for each local transition matrix.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in MHMM learning – the computational cost of EM, particularly for large datasets. The connection to spectral HMM learning is clearly articulated, and the proposed approach offers a potentially efficient alternative. The authors acknowledge the impact of estimation noise and propose a novel analysis utilizing eigenvalues, culminating in Equation (7) which attempts to define a condition for accurate cluster number estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of MoM, spectral HMM learning, and the stabilization technique to address permutation ambiguity appears novel.  The approach provides a distinct alternative to EM-based methods.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The authors provide a theoretical justification for their method, including Lemma 1 and Lemma 2, establishing the relationship between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices. The analysis accounting for noise is a valuable contribution.
* **Potential for Scalability:** By leveraging MoM, the algorithm aims to reduce the computational burden compared to EM, making it suitable for large datasets.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of MHMM learning and positions its contribution within this context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Notation:** The notation is often confusing, hindering understanding. For example, the meaning of  `1J^T` isn’t immediately clear, and the description of the block-diagonal structure in the limit (Corollary 1) is difficult to follow. A consistent introduction of dimensions for all vectors and matrices would significantly improve readability. Conforming to standard HMM notation (e.g., using 'T' for transition matrix, 'n'/'m' for observation/hidden state counts) would enhance accessibility.
* **Related Work:** The paper lacks sufficient engagement with related work. While Anandkumar et al. (2012) is cited, critical related work is missing. Notably, the authors should discuss and contrast their approach with methods that initialize EM with spectral estimates, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013).  A more comprehensive literature review would strengthen the paper's position within the field.
* **Experimental Evaluation:**  The experiments are basic and the presentation of results is poor. Figure 4 is particularly difficult to interpret. Results should be presented in tables with clear metrics and statistical significance. The EM performance is surprisingly poor, and the paper should report the *best* EM accuracy (over multiple random initializations) for a fairer comparison. The choice of the UCI handwritten character trajectory dataset and the use of a cubic polynomial feature transformation seem somewhat arbitrary and require further justification.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption that each local transition matrix has only one eigenvalue equal to 1 is a strong one and its impact on the algorithm's performance in realistic scenarios should be discussed. The conditions on the eigenvalues presented in Conjecture 1 also require further scrutiny.
* **Conjecture vs. Proof:** The spectral longevity argument, culminating in Equation (7) and Lemma 3, is presented as a conjecture. While understandable, further investigation or a more rigorous proof would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The approach presents a novel and potentially efficient alternative to EM for MHMM learning.
* The theoretical foundation is reasonably sound, with a clear attempt to address the challenges posed by estimation noise.
* The proposed stabilization technique to resolve permutation ambiguity is a significant contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from clarity issues due to confusing notation and a lack of precise definitions.
* The experimental evaluation is weak and lacks thorough comparisons with existing methods.
* The strong assumptions underlying the algorithm limit its applicability.
* The related work section is incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea with the potential to advance the field of MHMM learning. However, the current version suffers from issues related to clarity, experimental evaluation, and a lack of engagement with the existing literature. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, they are overshadowed by these weaknesses.  I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the identified shortcomings and resubmit to a future NIPS conference.  Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation, and expanding the related work section.
