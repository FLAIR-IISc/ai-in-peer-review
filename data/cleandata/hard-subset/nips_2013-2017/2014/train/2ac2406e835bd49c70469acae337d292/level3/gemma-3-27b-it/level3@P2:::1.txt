This paper proposes a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM) and spectral learning techniques. The core idea is to leverage the equivalence between an MHMM and a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, learn the latter using MoM, and then resolve the inherent permutation ambiguity through spectral properties of the learned transition matrix to recover individual HMM parameters. This work builds upon existing MoM algorithms for latent variable models [1, 2, 3] and spectral learning for HMMs [3], addressing a key challenge in applying MoM to more complex models like MHMMs. The motivation is strong, given the computational limitations of traditional EM-based approaches for large datasets.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical justification. The lemmas and proofs, while concise, appear logically consistent. The connection between the MHMM and the block-diagonal HMM is well-established. However, a critical question regarding the practical applicability of learning a full HMM *before* attempting to decompose it remains. For large, sparse mixtures, the initial HMM learning phase could be computationally prohibitive. The authors need to more clearly delineate the scenarios where this method is advantageous compared to existing techniques. The conjecture about the noise tolerance and estimation error bound, while promising, lacks a rigorous proof and further analysis would strengthen the contribution. The experiments, while demonstrating feasibility, lack the depth required to convincingly establish practical effectiveness.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is largely clear, although a minor typo exists in the proof of Lemma 2 (subscript of Λ should be *k* rather than 2). The flow of ideas is logical, with a clear progression from model definitions to the proposed algorithm. However, the discussion surrounding the spectral properties and the conditions for de-permutation can be dense and potentially challenging for readers not intimately familiar with spectral graph theory. Expanding on the intuition behind these concepts would improve accessibility. The figures suffer from small text and numbers, hindering readability.

**Originality:** The combination of MoM and spectral learning for MHMMs represents a novel contribution. While spectral learning has been applied to HMMs previously [3], its application within the context of MoM and the specific approach to resolving permutation ambiguity in MHMMs are original. The proposed algorithm addresses a known limitation of applying MoM to more complex models. However, the degree of novelty is somewhat tempered by the reliance on established techniques. The authors adequately reference related work, including key papers in MoM and HMM learning.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work lies in offering a computationally efficient alternative to EM for learning MHMMs, particularly for large datasets. If the method proves robust and scalable, it could find applications in various domains like speech recognition, bioinformatics, and time series analysis. However, the current experimental results do not fully demonstrate this potential. The observation that EM initialized with the spectral learning approach sometimes performs *worse* than spectral learning alone is puzzling and requires further investigation. A successful explanation could reveal crucial limitations of the proposed method or point towards refined initialization strategies. The lack of detailed parameter specifications in the experiments also hinders assessing the broader impact of the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of techniques (MoM and spectral learning) to address a challenging problem in MHMM learning.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with clear mathematical derivations.
*   Demonstrated feasibility on synthetic data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of detailed parameter information.
*   Concerns about the scalability of the initial HMM learning step for large, sparse mixtures.
*   Unclear conditions for guaranteeing a (nearly) sparse spectral decomposition under noise.
*   Puzzling results where EM initialized with the proposed method performs worse than the method alone.
*   Experiments do not fully reflect real-world noise (using Dirichlet noise instead of learning from observed sequences).



**Overall Recommendation:** Reject. While the ideas presented are interesting and the theoretical analysis is sound, significant concerns remain regarding the practical performance and applicability of the proposed method. The experimental results are insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of this approach over existing techniques. Addressing the points raised above, particularly through more comprehensive and well-documented experiments, would significantly strengthen the paper. A clearer articulation of the method’s strengths and limitations, and the conditions under which it is most effective, is crucial.
