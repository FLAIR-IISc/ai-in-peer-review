

The paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM), addressing a key challenge in learning these models: the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral estimation. The authors cleverly recognize that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral HMM learning algorithms. The core contribution lies in their de-permutation algorithm that recovers the individual HMM components from the permuted global parameters, using spectral properties of the transition matrix.

This work builds upon the foundational spectral learning framework for HMMs established by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012) and further developed in Anandkumar et al. (2012), which the authors appropriately cite. However, the paper would benefit from stronger contextualization with respect to related work on mixture models, particularly Chaganty and Liang's (2013) spectral approach for mixtures of linear regressions, which shares conceptual similarities in handling component permutation. The authors acknowledge the permutation problem but don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing solutions for mixture models.

The technical quality of the paper is generally strong. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the spectral properties of the global transition matrix, establishing conditions under which the de-permutation algorithm succeeds (Lemmas 2, 3 and Conjecture 1). The algorithmic contribution is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate the method's computational advantages over EM, particularly on large datasets. The real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories shows practical utility, though the comparison with EM initialized randomly appears somewhat limited (the authors could have included more thorough comparisons with other initialization methods).

The clarity of the paper is good overall, though it suffers from occasional proofreading issues (e.g., "For each e, identify of eigenvalues..." in Section 3.2.2). The mathematical notation is well-defined, but some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the spectral decomposition process. The experimental section is well-organized and provides meaningful comparisons between spectral learning and EM, though the paper would be stronger with additional baselines or comparisons to other spectral mixture learning approaches.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the application to MHMMs is novel, the core spectral learning technique is largely a direct adaptation of existing HMM methods. The key innovation is the de-permutation algorithm, which effectively addresses a critical practical challenge in spectral learning of mixture models. This solution is both elegant and practical, though the paper doesn't fully articulate how it differs from similar approaches in the mixture learning literature.

The significance of the work is clear: MHMMs are important for sequence clustering applications, but EM-based learning becomes computationally prohibitive with large datasets. The proposed spectral method offers a computationally efficient alternative that achieves comparable accuracy. The real-data experiment demonstrates practical utility, and the paper's finding that spectral learning can serve as an effective initialization for EM is particularly valuable for practitioners.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
1. Solves a meaningful problem in sequence clustering with a well-motivated approach
2. Provides theoretically grounded solution to the permutation ambiguity challenge
3. Demonstrates significant computational advantages over EM
4. Shows practical utility through real-data experiments
5. Clear algorithmic contribution that can be applied beyond MHMMs

Con-acceptance arguments:
1. Limited differentiation from existing spectral learning literature
2. Some proofreading errors that undermine confidence in the technical content
3. Incomplete contextualization with respect to related work (e.g., Chaganty & Liang 2013)
4. Theoretical analysis of the de-permutation algorithm is not fully rigorous (Conjecture 1 is not proven)
5. Limited comparison with other spectral approaches for mixture models

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on spectral learning for latent variable models, particularly for addressing the permutation problem in mixture models. While the core spectral learning technique is not entirely novel, the specific adaptation to MHMMs and the de-permutation algorithm represent a useful practical advance. The computational advantages demonstrated in the experiments are significant, and the real-data results suggest immediate applicability.

The paper should be strengthened by:
1. More thorough comparison with related work on mixture models (especially Chaganty & Liang 2013)
2. Correction of proofreading errors
3. More rigorous theoretical justification of the de-permutation algorithm
4. Additional experimental comparisons with alternative spectral mixture learning approaches

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in spectral learning for sequence clustering models. The paper's practical significance is clear, and the technical approach is well-executed. With minor revisions to address the contextualization and theoretical gaps, this would be an excellent contribution to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
