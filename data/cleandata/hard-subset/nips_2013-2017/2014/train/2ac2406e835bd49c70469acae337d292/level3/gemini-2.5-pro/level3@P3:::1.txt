This paper presents a novel method-of-moments approach for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key insight is to view an MHMM with K components, each having M states, as a single large HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The proposed algorithm first leverages existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this large, aggregated model. This estimation, however, is only identifiable up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of the paper is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. By analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix—specifically by identifying the K dominant eigenvalues corresponding to the stationary distributions of the K components—the authors propose a procedure to cluster the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of this matrix, thereby recovering the block structure and identifying the parameters of the individual HMMs. This work builds upon the recent line of research in spectral learning for latent variable models, extending it from single HMMs to the more complex MHMM setting, which has traditionally been approached using the EM algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of learning MHMMs, which are widely used for clustering sequence data. The proposed approach offers a compelling alternative to the EM algorithm, which is known to be sensitive to initialization and computationally expensive on large datasets. The idea of learning a global model and then decomposing it by resolving a permutation ambiguity is novel and theoretically elegant.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the method is solid. The equivalence between an MHMM and a single HMM with a block-diagonal structure is well-established in Lemma 1. The core contribution—using the spectral properties of the transition matrix to perform the de-permutation—is clever and well-motivated, particularly in the noiseless case.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is described logically, and the theoretical arguments are presented in an accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Practicality of the Core Assumption:** The entire approach hinges on the ability to first estimate a single HMM with MK states. This initial step can be computationally and statistically demanding, especially as K (the number of mixture components) grows. The size of the moment matrices that need to be factorized in spectral HMM learning scales with the number of hidden states (MK). The authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the scalability of this step and clarify the practical limits of the method. It seems most applicable when K is small, which should be explicitly stated as a limitation.

2.  **Robustness of the De-permutation Step:** A major concern is the assumption that the estimated transition matrix, A_hat, will be close enough to the true permuted block-diagonal matrix for the spectral clustering to work. While spectral learning provides a consistent estimator, in the finite sample regime, A_hat will be a dense matrix. The method's success depends on a clear spectral gap being preserved between the K "global" eigenvalues and the remaining "local" eigenvalues under estimation noise. However, there is no guarantee that this will hold. Many dense matrices could be close in Frobenius norm to the desired sparse structure, and it is not obvious that the noise introduced by the spectral estimation procedure will not disrupt this structure sufficiently to make recovery impossible. Stronger justification, either theoretical or empirical, is needed for this crucial step.

3.  **Inadequate Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation is the most significant weakness of the paper and does not sufficiently support the claims.
    *   **Artificial Noise Model:** The experiment in Section 4.1, designed to test the de-permutation algorithm's robustness to noise, appears to be flawed. Rather than generating sequences from an MHMM and running the full learning pipeline, the authors seem to directly add noise to the true permuted transition matrix. This setup bypasses the critical and challenging step of estimating the matrix from finite observation data. A proper experiment must start from generated sequences to reflect the realistic noise and limited-data effects inherent in the initial spectral estimation step.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** The experimental descriptions are sparse. In Section 4.2, the parameters of the MHMM used to generate the data (e.g., specific transition and observation matrices) are not provided. This makes the results irreproducible and difficult to interpret. These details should be included, at least in an appendix.
    *   **Counter-intuitive EM Results:** In Table 1, EM initialized with the spectral method's result sometimes performs worse than the spectral method alone (e.g., 87.9% vs. 91.4% for 'u' vs 'o'). This is highly unexpected, as EM is a likelihood-maximization procedure and should, in principle, improve upon or at least maintain the quality of its initialization. This result requires a thorough explanation. It may point to an issue with the EM implementation, the evaluation metric, or a more fundamental problem.

**Minor Issues:**

1.  There appears to be a typo in the proof of Lemma 2. In the final eigendecomposition of A_bar, the subscript of the eigenvalue matrix should be K, not 2, to be consistent with the notation for K HMMs.
2.  The figures are difficult to read due to the extremely small font size for axis labels and numbers. This should be corrected to improve legibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper introduces an interesting and theoretically appealing approach to learning MHMMs. The core ideas are novel and the theoretical motivation is sound. However, there are significant doubts about the method's practical performance and robustness, which stem from strong assumptions about the initial estimation step and the preservation of spectral structure under noise. These doubts are amplified by an experimental section that is insufficient in detail, uses an artificial noise model, and contains unexplained results. While the paper presents a promising research direction, its current form lacks the rigorous empirical validation required for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The work could be significantly strengthened by addressing the concerns with the experimental setup and providing a more realistic and detailed evaluation of the proposed algorithm's performance and limitations.
