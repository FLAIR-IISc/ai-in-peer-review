Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) approach to learn mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The main observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with each block corresponding to a component HMM. Leveraging existing spectral learning for HMMs (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang, JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al.), the authors estimate the global HMM parameters from moments, which yields parameters only up to a permutation of the latent states. This permutation mixes states across the component HMMs and thus prevents recovering per-component parameters. The novel contribution is a “de-permutation” procedure that uses spectral properties of the learned transition matrix: specifically, reconstructing a rank-K approximation from the top eigenvalues and clustering its columns to recover blocks, plus a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K (the number of mixture components).

The work sits squarely in the spectral/moment-based literature for latent variable models and HMMs (Hsu et al.; Anandkumar et al.). Relative to prior work, the core reduction of an MHMM to a large HMM is straightforward; the key technical step here is to align the permuted spectral output with the actual components. The paper would benefit from a broader positioning within related spectral literature on mixtures and alignment/identifiability (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2013, “Spectral Experts,” and other tensor approaches), and a discussion of when tensor methods might avoid permutation/identifiability issues without post-hoc de-permutation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction and use of spectral HMM learning are sound and standard. The de-permutation idea is intuitive and practically useful: in the noiseless case, the block structure is revealed by the multiplicity of the eigenvalue 1; under noise, the authors propose to use a low-rank reconstruction and cluster columns. However, the most important claims about when this works are heuristic. “Conjecture 1” and Lemma 3’s selection method for K lack formal proofs with explicit conditions and explicit perturbation bounds. The reuse of sample-complexity bounds from related mixture models is not rigorous for MHMMs. The assumption that each Ak has a unique eigenvalue at 1 (ergodic, aperiodic chain with simple stationary eigenvalue) is reasonable, but the behavior under near-degenerate spectra (slow mixing, small spectral gaps) needs more careful treatment. The requirement MK < L for the MoM estimator is stated and leads to feature lifting in the real-data experiment; implications of this requirement should be discussed more fully.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments illustrate that the de-permutation works over a range of sequence lengths, and that the method is faster than EM while achieving competitive clustering accuracy when sufficient data is available. The real-data experiment (UCI character trajectories) shows that the spectral method is a good initializer for EM. However, empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope: the sensitivity to spectral gaps and noise levels is not systematically explored; there is no ablation of the de-permutation step (e.g., failure modes when K is underestimated/overestimated); and scalability to larger MK is not demonstrated. Reporting absolute runtimes and complexity of the moment computation stage would also help.
- Reproducibility: Many details are present, but some are missing (e.g., exact e-range for longevity computation, distance/normalization choices in clustering columns of Ar, handling of sequences of varying length, and how M is chosen). Providing code or pseudo-code for moment estimation and the de-permutation step would be valuable.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a helpful equivalence lemma and a concrete algorithm summary. The new “spectral longevity” measure is explained but could use more intuition and clearer guidance on hyperparameters (e.g., choice of γ and e-range). The writing has occasional typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “For each e, identify of eigenvalues...” and scattered minor errors); a careful proofreading pass is needed. The real-data section should explicitly discuss the implications of the polynomial feature lift (L increased to 10) and potential distortions it introduces.

Originality
The core reduction (MHMM as block-diagonal HMM) and the use of existing spectral HMM learning are not novel. The paper’s main original contribution is the de-permutation and K-estimation procedure based on spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix. While the idea is simple, it addresses a real and often glossed-over practical issue in spectral learning: aligning permuted latent states across components. The lack of formal guarantees diminishes the novelty’s technical depth.

Significance
The problem is important: MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering, and EM can be slow and sensitive to initialization. A fast method-of-moments approach with a practical de-permutation can be valuable both as a standalone approach and as an initializer for EM. That said, the significance is limited by the current theoretical gaps and by empirical scope. The need for MK < L (and corresponding feature lifting) can constrain applicability in low-dimensional observation settings. The contribution feels incremental but practically relevant.

Pros
- Addresses a concrete, under-discussed challenge in spectral learning for MHMMs: de-permutation/alignment of components.
- Simple, interpretable procedure using eigenstructure and column clustering of a rank-K reconstruction.
- Empirically faster than EM and competitive in accuracy when enough data is available; effective as an EM initializer.
- Clear exposition of the MHMM–HMM equivalence and a usable algorithmic recipe.

Cons
- Key claims are conjectural/heuristic; no rigorous perturbation analysis or finite-sample guarantees for de-permutation or K estimation.
- Limited empirical evaluation: no systematic stress-tests on spectral gaps/noise, no scalability results to larger MK, and few real datasets.
- Requires MK < L; the need for feature lifting and its effects are not fully discussed.
- Related work coverage is too narrow; insufficient positioning relative to tensor/spectral work on mixtures and to alignment strategies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal guarantees for the de-permutation step under perturbations, e.g., using Davis–Kahan or eigenvector subspace perturbation bounds, and conditions on spectral gaps within/between blocks that ensure correct clustering of columns of Ar.
- Replace “Conjecture 1” with a theorem and proof, or clearly delimit the regime where the method is provably consistent (including sample complexity and dependence on gaps).
- Analyze and justify the “spectral longevity” heuristic; consider simpler alternatives (e.g., eigengap statistics) and compare on synthetic data. Clarify how to choose γ and the e-range.
- Expand experiments: vary spectral gaps and mixing rates, explore mis-specified K and M, evaluate robustness to noise and short sequences, and report runtimes (including moment computation) vs EM on larger problems.
- Discuss the MK < L requirement and alternatives (e.g., multi-view/tensor methods using higher-order moments that may relax dimensionality constraints).
- Broaden related work: include Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013) and other tensor/spectral methods for mixtures; clarify how your alignment approach compares to possible tensor-based identification of components.
- Proofread carefully and fix typos/notation inconsistencies. Define all quantities when introduced (e.g., choice of clustering metric, normalization).

Minor comments/typos
- “For each e, identify of eigenvalues...” → “For each e, identify the eigenvalue with the largest longevity...”
- Ensure consistent notation for vectors/matrices and transposes; some lines switch between boldface and non-bold.
- Clarify whether columns of Ar are normalized before k-means, and whether clustering is done in Euclidean or another metric.
- In Algorithm 1, specify the range for e, γ, and the stopping criterion for longevity.

Overall assessment
A practically motivated and clearly described de-permutation strategy on top of standard spectral HMM learning. The contribution is useful but incremental, with theoretical claims left as conjectures and a relatively limited empirical evaluation. With strengthened analysis and broader experiments/positioning, this could become a solid contribution. As it stands, I view it as borderline.
