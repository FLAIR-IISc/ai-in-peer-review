- Proposes learning mixtures of HMMs by treating the mixture as a single HMM with block-diagonal transition and applying HKZ spectral estimation plus de-permutation.
- Core contribution is a de-permutation/aligning procedure using the global transition eigenstructure and a “spectral longevity” statistic to recover K and block permutation.
- Empirical evaluation uses synthetic and a small real dataset and claims computational advantages over EM.
- Related work: builds on HKZ and tensor/MoM methods, but insufficiently discusses broader spectral mixture literature (e.g., Chaganty & Liang).
- Reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is standard; main novelty is the de-permutation heuristic.
- Key theoretical claims are presented as conjectures rather than theorems and lack formal guarantees.
- Recommends providing perturbation guarantees (e.g., via Weyl/Davis–Kahan) for eigenvalue separation and k-means block recovery.
- Important assumptions (unique unit eigenvalue per Ak, mixing, distinct stationary distributions) should be explicitly stated.
- Needs analysis of sensitivity to small spectral gaps and estimation noise.
- Experiments are promising but limited in scope and missing comparisons to simple alignment baselines (e.g., Hungarian matching on emissions or stationary distributions).
- Clarity issues: typos, proofreading errors, inconsistent notation, and some incomplete references.
- The longevity statistic and the algorithmic complexity require a clearer presentation.
- Originality is modest: follows HKZ with an incremental de-permutation heuristic addressing a practical issue.
- Potential significance: useful for practitioners if made robust, but current theory and empirical breadth limit impact.
- Pros: addresses alignment gap in spectral MHMMs; simple and computationally attractive; clearly formulates the permutation problem.
- Cons: lacks formal guarantees; limited evaluation and missing baselines; insufficient related-work positioning; presentation polish is low.
- Main suggestions: add perturbation-based theoretical guarantees, include alignment baselines, expand experiments, deepen related-work discussion, and fix proofreading/notation.
