

# Review for "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) that addresses the computational limitations of the standard EM approach. The authors recognize that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral HMM learning methods (Anandkumar et al., 2012). The key contribution is a novel method to resolve the permutation ambiguity that arises when applying standard spectral learning to MHMMs, using spectral properties of the global transition matrix. This work extends the literature on spectral learning for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al., 2012, 2014; Hsu et al., 2009) to a more complex model structure.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, and the paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the permutation resolution method. The authors establish the equivalence between MHMM and HMM with block-diagonal structure (Lemma 1), and then develop a method to recover the individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters. The spectral analysis (particularly Lemma 3 and Definition 3) provides a theoretically grounded approach to determining the number of clusters, which is a significant contribution. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, showing that the spectral approach is computationally more efficient than EM while maintaining comparable accuracy on synthetic and real data. However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail regarding the EM implementation (e.g., how many restarts, stopping criteria), which makes it difficult to fully assess the comparison.

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from several clarity issues. The notation is inconsistent and confusing in places (e.g., Corollary 1's notation for the limit matrix, unclear definition of 1J^T), which makes it difficult to follow the theoretical developments. The authors should define all vectors and matrices with their dimensions when first introduced. The presentation of results (particularly Figure 4) is poor, with unclear axes and labels that make it hard to interpret the accuracy versus data size relationship. The real data experiment (Section 4.3) would benefit from a table presentation instead of the current textual description.

**Originality**: The core idea of viewing MHMM as a special HMM is not entirely novel (see Smyth, 1996), but the authors' contribution lies in the specific method to resolve the permutation ambiguity using spectral properties. This is a valuable extension to existing spectral learning methods for HMMs. However, the paper lacks sufficient engagement with related work, particularly with Chaganty and Liang's (2013) spectral initialization for mixture models, which is directly relevant to the proposed approach as an EM initialization method. The authors should explicitly compare and contrast their work with this prior research.

**Significance**: The computational advantage over EM is significant for large-scale sequence clustering applications, as the spectral approach's runtime is independent of the number of sequences. This could enable MHMM applications in domains with massive sequence datasets where EM is prohibitive. The demonstration that the spectral method can serve as a good initialization for EM (Section 4.3) further enhances the practical value. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the basic nature of the experiments and the lack of comparison to other state-of-the-art approaches for sequence clustering.

**Strengths**: 
- Novel solution to the permutation ambiguity problem in MHMM learning
- Theoretical analysis of spectral properties for cluster identification (Lemma 3)
- Demonstrated computational advantage over EM
- Practical utility as an EM initialization method

**Weaknesses**:
- Confusing notation and unclear definitions (e.g., Corollary 1)
- Poor experimental presentation (Figure 4 is hard to read)
- Insufficient comparison with related work (notably Chaganty & Liang 2013)
- EM implementation details not sufficiently described
- Lacks deeper analysis of when the spectral approach fails (e.g., when the noise assumption in Lemma 3 is violated)

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically valuable extension to spectral learning for latent variable models. The permutation resolution method is novel and well-motivated, and the computational benefits are significant for large-scale applications. The results on real data demonstrate practical utility as an EM initialization method.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The paper's clarity issues significantly hinder understanding of the technical contributions. The experimental section needs substantial improvement in presentation and depth, particularly regarding the EM comparison. The lack of engagement with relevant prior work (Chaganty & Liang 2013) weakens the contribution claim.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the spectral learning literature, but requires significant revisions to improve clarity, strengthen the experimental presentation, and better position the work within the existing literature. With these improvements, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the clarity issues and lack of engagement with related work prevent it from being a top-tier contribution. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
