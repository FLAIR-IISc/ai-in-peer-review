This paper proposes a spectral method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by observing that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Building on spectral HMM learning (e.g., Hsu, Kakade & Zhang, JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al.), the authors estimate global parameters up to a permutation and then introduce a de-permutation procedure that exploits the eigenstructure of the estimated global transition matrix. In the noiseless case, the K blocks yield K unit-magnitude eigenvalues; in the noisy case, they propose a rank-K reconstruction using the top “global” eigenvalues and cluster columns to recover block structure, together with a “spectral longevity” heuristic to estimate K. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwriting dataset compare favorably in speed to EM and show the spectral outputs can serve as a good initializer for EM.

Relation to prior work: The use of MoM/tensor methods for HMMs follows Hsu et al. (spectral HMM) and Anandkumar et al. (tensor decompositions). The main novelty here is the de-permutation/cluster-alignment step specific to mixtures of HMMs. The paper should better situate itself relative to concurrent spectral work on mixtures (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2013 “Spectral Experts”), which also confronts component permutation/alignment issues, and to earlier MHMM learning via EM (Smyth, NIPS 1997).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is correct and convenient. The de-permutation idea—using the rank-K part of the estimated transition matrix and clustering columns to recover blocks—is plausible and empirically effective. However, key parts are heuristic. Conjecture 1 (finite-sample closeness of the low-rank reconstruction) is unproven, and the longevity criterion for estimating K is justified by an argument about spectral gaps but lacks guarantees. The assumption that each Ak has exactly one eigenvalue equal to 1 (ergodic, aperiodic chain) is reasonable but should be made explicit with discussion of nearly periodic or reducible chains (multiple eigenvalues with magnitude near 1). Sensitivity to small spectral gaps (between |λGK| and maxk |λL2,k|) is not quantified.
- Experiments: Synthetic studies show the de-permutation works with moderate T and few sequences; the EM comparison highlights a favorable speed/accuracy tradeoff. The real-data experiment is limited (binary class pairs, M=4, K=2) and requires feature lifting to satisfy MK < L; more diverse datasets and larger K, M would strengthen claims. It would help to include ablations on eigen-gap size, noise level, mis-specified MK, and failure cases.
- Reproducibility: Algorithmic steps are stated clearly, but code and exact moment settings (order, regularization) are not provided.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized with helpful lemmas. Some notation is heavy; unifying matrix/vector notation and consistently using subscripts would aid readability. The spectral longevity definition is nonstandard; an intuitive explanation and a small illustrative example would help.
- Proofreading is needed (e.g., “For each e, identify of eigenvalues...”; inconsistent spacing in MK; occasional grammar issues). The requirement MK < L for the MoM HMM algorithm should be stated earlier, with guidance on when feature lifting is advisable and its impact on modeling assumptions.

Originality:
- Viewing an MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is not new, and the base MoM estimator follows prior spectral HMM work. The main contribution is the de-permutation/alignment strategy and the heuristic for estimating K from eigenvalue “longevity.” While conceptually simple, this addresses a real obstruction for applying spectral methods to mixtures of HMMs and appears novel in this specific context. The paper would benefit from comparing against alternative alignment strategies (e.g., Hungarian matching using emission similarity, joint diagonalization across moments, leveraging stationary distributions) and discussing connections to alignment in other spectral mixture models (e.g., Spectral Experts).

Significance:
- If robust, the approach could make MHMM learning practical at scale, with competitive accuracy and significantly reduced runtime relative to EM, and provide strong initialization for EM. The contribution is primarily algorithmic/engineering rather than theoretical; stronger guarantees or broader empirical validation would increase impact.

Specific suggestions:
- Provide at least partial finite-sample analysis of the de-permutation under perturbations (e.g., Davis–Kahan bounds for invariant subspaces) and conditions on eigen-gaps and noise.
- Compare de-permutation to baselines: Hungarian matching on emissions (columns of O), clustering of stationary distributions, and simple thresholding on A entries; report accuracy and runtime.
- Evaluate sensitivity to: (i) near-multiple unit eigenvalues (slow-mixing chains), (ii) mis-specified MK and K̂, (iii) nonuniform π and νk, and (iv) different observation models (multinomial/Poisson).
- Clarify computational complexity: cost to compute moments (in N, T, L) vs eigendecomposition and k-means on MK; report end-to-end wall-clock.
- Expand real-data evaluation beyond paired classes; include cases with K>2 and cross-validated K.
- Improve related work discussion, especially spectral methods for mixtures (Anandkumar et al.; Chaganty & Liang) and prior MHMM applications.
- Proofread carefully and fix typos.

Pros:
- Addresses a practical and important barrier (permutation/misalignment) to using spectral learning for MHMMs.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with clear intuition.
- Strong speed advantage; can serve as an effective EM initializer.
- Empirical results indicate the method works with moderate data.

Cons:
- Key steps are heuristic; theoretical guarantees are missing (Conjecture 1).
- Limited empirical scope (small K, M; one real dataset; feature lifting to satisfy MK < L).
- Sensitivity to spectral gaps and model misspecification not characterized.
- Related work and positioning could be strengthened; proofreading issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept if the venue values practical spectral algorithms and the authors can improve clarity, related work, and empirical breadth. Otherwise, I lean weak reject due to the lack of theoretical guarantees and limited evaluation of the de-permutation’s robustness.
