This paper presents a method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key insight is that an MHMM with K components, each with M hidden states, is equivalent to a single large HMM with KM states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose applying the spectral HMM learning algorithm of Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012) to learn the parameters of this large HMM. However, this direct application recovers the parameters only up to an unknown permutation of the KM hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs, rendering them unidentifiable.

The main contribution of this work is a "de-permutation" algorithm designed to solve this component-mixing problem. The proposed method exploits the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By computing a low-rank reconstruction of this matrix using its top K eigenvalues, the authors argue that the columns of the resulting matrix will form K distinct clusters, where each cluster corresponds to the hidden states of one of the original HMM components. Applying k-means clustering to these columns reveals the permutation, which can then be used to correctly partition the learned parameters into their respective component HMMs. The paper provides experimental validation on synthetic and real data, demonstrating the method's computational efficiency compared to EM and its effectiveness as an initialization scheme.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a critical and practical challenge in applying spectral methods to mixture models. The problem of aligning or "de-permutating" the learned parameters to their true components is a common bottleneck that prevents the direct application of many spectral techniques to more complex hierarchical models. The proposed solution is a valuable step towards making these efficient spectral methods more broadly applicable.

2.  **Elegant and Intuitive Solution:** The de-permutation algorithm is simple, intuitive, and well-motivated. The idea of using the spectral structure of the transition matrix (specifically, its top K eigenvectors) to cluster states is elegant and appears to work reasonably well in practice, as shown in the experiments (e.g., Figure 3).

3.  **Computational Efficiency:** As with other MoM-based approaches, the proposed algorithm offers a significant computational advantage over iterative methods like Expectation-Maximization (EM), particularly for large datasets. The experiments in Section 4.2 effectively demonstrate this speed-up, highlighting a key practical benefit.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** The paper's originality is somewhat overstated due to its failure to situate itself within the broader context of spectral learning for mixture models. The core problem of using spectral clustering to de-permute or align components has been addressed in prior work. Most notably, **Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013, "Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions")** tackle a very similar problem. They also use a tensor method to recover a permuted matrix of model parameters and then apply spectral clustering to its columns to recover the component memberships. The current paper should extensively discuss this and other related works to clarify its specific contribution. As it stands, the novelty of the central de-permutation idea is not clearly established.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:** The paper suffers from numerous typos and unclear statements that detract from its quality. For example:
    *   In Algorithm 1, the description "Identify K̂ as the eigenvalue with largest longevity" is confusing. K̂ is the *number* of clusters, not an eigenvalue itself.
    *   The justification for Conjecture 1 is difficult to follow and relies on loose notation (e.g., "A is used for ĀP to reduce the notation clutter"). A more careful and clear presentation of this argument is needed.
    *   The definition of "spectral longevity" (Definition 3) is convoluted. While the intuition is to find a significant spectral gap, the formulation is difficult to parse and seems overly complex for the stated goal. A simpler explanation or metric could improve clarity.

3.  **Limited Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis supporting the de-permutation algorithm is not fully developed. Conjecture 1 provides a high-level justification for why the low-rank approximation is close to the ideal one, but it is not a formal proof and the bound is simply reused from prior work on a different model without a new derivation. Furthermore, the analysis does not account for the performance of the k-means clustering step, which is crucial to the algorithm's success. The success of clustering depends on the separation of the column vectors, and a formal analysis would need to characterize this separation.

### **Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Improve Related Work Section:** The authors must add a thorough discussion of related work on component alignment in spectral methods, especially the Chaganty & Liang (2013) paper. This would involve explaining the similarities and highlighting the unique aspects of the proposed approach in the context of MHMMs.

2.  **Thorough Proofreading:** The manuscript requires a careful proofreading pass to correct typos, grammatical errors, and imprecise statements. Re-phrasing the description of the algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the justification for Conjecture 1 would significantly improve readability.

3.  **Refine Theoretical Claims:** While a full theoretical analysis might be extensive, the authors could strengthen the paper by either providing a more rigorous proof for their claims or by being more explicit about the assumptions and limitations of the current analysis (e.g., assumptions on the success of k-means).

### **Summary of Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound on an intuitive level, but the theoretical analysis is informal ("Conjecture") and the presentation has clarity issues.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is moderately clear but is hampered by typos, imprecise language, and some convoluted explanations, particularly in Section 3.2.2.
*   **Originality:** The base spectral algorithm is a direct application of prior work. The main contribution, the de-permutation method, is novel in the specific context of MHMMs but is very similar to ideas in previous literature that are not cited or discussed.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses an important and practical problem. If the method is sound and robust, it could be a useful tool for researchers and practitioners working with sequence clustering, making spectral methods more accessible for this model class.

**Recommendation:** This paper presents a valuable idea for a practical problem. However, the significant omission of relevant prior work and the issues with clarity and theoretical rigor prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. It is a borderline paper that could become a solid contribution if the authors thoroughly address the weaknesses, particularly by properly situating their work with respect to Chaganty & Liang (2013) and improving the overall presentation.
