

This paper proposes a novel spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) that leverages the method of moments (MoM) and resolves permutation ambiguity through spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The core insight is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing the use of existing spectral HMM learning techniques. The authors then demonstrate how to recover the block structure through eigendecomposition, particularly when the global transition matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1, and provide a practical algorithm for estimating the number of clusters K.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on latent variable models, building upon spectral learning methods for HMMs [1, 2, 3] while addressing a significant gap in MHMM learning. The theoretical framework is sound, and the algorithm is well-motivated. However, several critical concerns undermine the practical applicability of the approach.

Major weaknesses include: (1) The paper lacks empirical validation on real sequences from MHMMs, instead corrupting permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise (Major 4), which fails to reflect real data noise and limited-data effects. (2) The method's practical limitations are not properly addressed—estimating a full HMM first is computationally prohibitive for large, sparse mixtures (Major 2), and there's no guarantee the spectral-learned transition matrix will be nearly sparse (Major 3). (3) Section 4.2 lacks critical parameter details about the mixture HMMs used in experiments (Major 5), making replication difficult. (4) The puzzling observation that EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes underperforms spectral learning alone (Major 6) requires explanation.

While the paper is well-written (Minor 2), it contains a typo in the proof (Minor 1). The theoretical contribution is interesting, but the experimental section significantly weakens the practical significance claim. The authors should clarify when their method is appropriate versus when it is not, and provide experiments that learn from observed sequences rather than corrupted matrices.

Strengths: Solid theoretical foundation, novel application of spectral methods to MHMMs, and potential computational advantages over EM.

Weaknesses: Limited practical validation, unclear practical applicability, and insufficient experimental detail.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach with potential computational benefits, significant doubts remain about its practical performance on real datasets. The paper would be strengthened by more realistic experiments and clearer discussion of method limitations.
