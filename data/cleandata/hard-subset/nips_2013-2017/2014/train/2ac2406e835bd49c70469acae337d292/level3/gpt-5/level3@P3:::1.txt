Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The central observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first estimate a full HMM via existing spectral HMM methods (e.g., Hsu et al.; Anandkumar et al.), which recovers parameters up to a permutation of the MK states. They then resolve the permutation ambiguity by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix Ā: in the noiseless case, Ā has K eigenvalues at 1 and (Ā)e converges to a matrix with K rank-one blocks; in the noisy case, they propose a rank-K reconstruction and a “spectral longevity” heuristic to detect K and re-group states by clustering the columns of the low-rank reconstruction. The paper includes a synthetic perturbation study and a small UCI trajectory dataset experiment, and suggests using the spectral solution to initialize EM.

This work builds on spectral learning for HMMs and other latent variable models (Anandkumar et al.; Hsu-Kakade-Zhang) and relates to prior work on sequence clustering with HMMs. To my knowledge, the specific de-permutation mechanism for recovering MHMM structure from a full spectral HMM estimate is novel.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized exposition; the block-diagonal equivalence and the spectral intuition are presented cleanly.
- Insightful idea: recast MHMM learning as de-permutation of a global HMM, then use spectral gaps and low-rank reconstruction to recover blocks.
- Potential computational benefits in settings where MoM moments can be computed efficiently and K, M are modest.
- The approach also serves as a reasonable initialization for EM in at least some real-data cases.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Major 1—Experimental detail/expansion: The experiments are too limited to support practical claims. Key missing pieces:
- In 4.2, please provide full parameter settings: the exact Ak (transition matrices), emission means/covariances, initial distributions, mixing weights, sequence length distributions, and how many sequences per component. Report the random seeds and how many trials/variance bars.
- Report run-time breakdowns (moment computation vs eigendecomposition vs k-means vs EM), memory footprint, and scaling with K, M, L, and total sequence length.
- Include additional metrics: test log-likelihood/perplexity, block-recovery F1 (precision/recall of block structure), accuracy of K-estimation, and sensitivity to class imbalance.

Major 2—Practicality of estimating a full HMM first: The method requires estimating and storing an MK × MK transition matrix and performing its eigendecomposition. For large, sparse mixtures (large K and/or M), this can be impractical in both time and memory, and undermines the computational advantage over EM. Please clarify:
- Complexity (time/memory) of the moment estimation and eigendecomposition in terms of L, M, K, and total observations.
- The regimes in which your method is appropriate (e.g., MK ≤ a few hundred; L ≥ MK; strong spectral separation), and those where it is not (e.g., large, sparse mixtures with weak separation).
- Whether sparse or structured variants (e.g., enforcing block-sparsity/thresholding, randomized eigensolvers) can be used without harming identifiability.

Major 3—No guarantee of (near) sparsity: Spectral HMM estimation does not promote sparsity or block structure; many dense transition matrices can be close (in Frobenius norm) to a block-diagonal one, especially under noise. The paper should discuss:
- Conditions under which the proposed clustering of Ar’s columns is reliable versus failure modes when dense cross-block leakage appears.
- Whether adding explicit structure (e.g., a post hoc convex projection onto block-sparse sets, regularized de-permutation objectives) can improve robustness.
- Empirical evidence on false-positive cross-block edges after de-permutation as noise increases.

Major 4—Noise model in experiments: In 4.1, matrices are permuted and perturbed by Dirichlet noise, not learned from data. This bypasses real sampling noise, estimation bias, and finite-data effects of spectral HMM learning. Please add experiments that:
- Sample sequences from true MHMMs, learn the global HMM via a standard spectral method, then run the de-permutation procedure.
- Vary sequence lengths, number of sequences per component, and SNR; include imbalanced mixtures and near-degenerate transitions.
- Compare against EM (with/without spectral initialization) in both recovery (up to permutation) and generalization likelihood.

Major 5—Missing parameter specifics in 4.2: Section 4.2 lacks full details of the MHMMs used (both A and O for each component). Please provide all parameters (or generative code) in an appendix/supplement to enable reproducibility.

Major 6—EM initialized with spectral sometimes worse than spectral alone: In Table 1, EM with spectral initialization underperforms spectral in some pairs. Since EM is monotone in likelihood, please:
- Report training/test log-likelihood curves to verify monotonicity and explain why clustering accuracy may drop (e.g., label permutation issues, different objective, overfitting, early stopping).
- Clarify whether K and M are fixed and consistent between spectral and EM; confirm that label alignment is done via an optimal permutation.

Additional technical comments
- The identifiability and sample complexity assurances hinge on Conjecture 1, which is not proved and reuses bounds from a different model. The paper should be more explicit about the status/limitations of this conjecture and delineate the spectral gap conditions required for K recovery.
- The requirement MK < L (as implied by the real-data feature lifting) raises concerns about practicality; discuss when such feature engineering is feasible and its effect on noise and estimation variance.

Minor issues
- Typo: In the proof around the block eigen-decomposition, the subscript of Λ should be K (not 2).
- Figures: Text and numbers are too small to read; please increase font sizes, add clear legends, and provide vector graphics.

Quality: The theoretical intuition is sound and connects well to spectral HMMs, but key claims rely on an unproven conjecture and strong separation assumptions. The empirical evidence is not yet sufficient to validate performance on real-world data and realistic noise.

Clarity: Generally well written and easy to follow. Needs substantially more detail in the experimental sections and a clearer discussion of applicability and limitations.

Originality: The de-permutation via spectral longevity and low-rank reconstruction to recover MHMM structure is novel to my knowledge.

Significance: Potentially useful if it reliably scales to moderate MK and real data. Current evidence leaves significant uncertainty about practical utility in large or weakly separated mixtures.

Questions for the authors
- What concrete ranges of (K, M, L, total sequences, average length) do you expect to handle in practice on a single machine?
- How sensitive is K-estimation to small spectral gaps and to near-deterministic transitions?
- Can you provide ablations comparing different ways of resolving the permutation (e.g., community detection on the state graph) and different ranks?

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and novel perspective on MHMM learning via spectral de-permutation.
- Clear exposition; potentially impactful if made robust.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and partially unrealistic experiments; lack of end-to-end learning from data.
- Practicality concerns for large/sparse mixtures.
- Key theoretical step left as a conjecture.

Overall recommendation
Interesting ideas with solid theory motivation, but significant doubts remain about practical performance and robustness on real datasets. I recommend rejection in the current form, with encouragement to strengthen end-to-end empirical validation, clarify applicability regimes, and tighten theoretical guarantees.
