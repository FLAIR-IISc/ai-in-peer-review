This paper proposes a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using spectral learning based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea is to leverage the block-diagonal structure inherent in MHMMs to circumvent the permutation ambiguity typically encountered in MoM-based learning. The paper presents a theoretically sound framework and demonstrates initial results on synthetic and real data. However, significant concerns remain regarding the practical applicability and performance of the method, particularly as the scale of the problem increases.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a clear mathematical derivation of the proposed method. The connection between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is well-established, and the use of spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity is a clever idea. The theoretical justification, while not fully rigorous (e.g., Conjecture 1), is well-motivated and provides a reasonable basis for the proposed algorithm. However, the experimental evaluation lacks the depth needed to fully validate the claims of efficiency and accuracy. The paper feels incomplete in its assessment of limitations and scaling behavior.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is generally clear, and the main concepts are explained in a straightforward manner. However, Section 3.2.2, dealing with noise and spectral conditions, becomes somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The progression of ideas is logical, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Originality:** The combination of spectral learning, MoM, and MHMMs represents a novel approach. While spectral methods have been applied to HMMs (Hsu et al., 2013), and MoM is gaining traction in latent variable modeling (Anandkumar et al., 2012, 2013), their integration for MHMM learning appears to be a new contribution. The proposed method addresses a key challenge in MHMM learning – the computational cost of EM – by offering a potentially faster alternative.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work lies in its ability to scale MHMM learning to larger datasets than traditional EM-based methods. If the method proves robust and efficient in practice, it could have applications in various domains, including speech recognition, bioinformatics, and time series analysis. However, the current experimental results do not provide strong evidence to support this claim. The method’s reliance on specific spectral conditions and the need for careful parameter tuning raise concerns about its generalizability.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach combining spectral learning and MoM for MHMMs.
*   Clear theoretical foundation and mathematical derivations.
*   Potential for computational efficiency compared to EM.
*   Addresses a key challenge in MHMM learning: permutation ambiguity.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental results (Section 4) lack sufficient detail regarding parameter settings, data preprocessing, and comparative analysis. Specifically, the mixture HMM parameters used in Section 4.2 are not fully specified, hindering reproducibility and thorough evaluation (Major 6, 5).
*   **Scalability Concerns:** Estimating a full HMM *before* resolving the permutation ambiguity appears impractical for large, sparse mixtures (Major 2). The authors need to clearly articulate the scenarios where their method is appropriate and when it is not.
*   **Spectral Robustness:** There's no guarantee that the spectral-learned transition matrix will be (nearly) sparse (Major 3). Noise could easily lead to a full matrix that satisfies the spectral conditions, undermining the block-diagonal assumption.
*   **Synthetic Data Limitations:** The experiments primarily use synthetic data generated using Gaussian emissions (Major 4). Experiments should directly learn from observed sequences sampled from a mixture of HMMs to more accurately reflect real-world noise and limited-data effects. Using Dirichlet noise on permuted matrices doesn't simulate realistic observation scenarios.
*   **Unexpected EM Performance:** The observation that EM initialized with spectral learning *sometimes* performs worse than spectral learning alone (Major 6) is concerning and requires a detailed explanation. EM should almost always improve upon an initial guess.
*   **Minor Errors:** A typo exists in the proof (Minor 1) – the subscript of Λ should be K, not 2. The figures also have text and numbers that are too small to read (Minor 2).

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novelty of the approach.
*   Sound theoretical foundation.
*   Potential for improved scalability.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Lack of robust experimental validation.
*   Scalability concerns with large, sparse mixtures.
*   Sensitivity to noise and spectral conditions.
*   Insufficient detail in experimental setup.
*   Unexplained EM performance degradation.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea with solid theoretical backing. However, the significant doubts regarding practical performance, coupled with the lack of comprehensive experimental validation, make it difficult to recommend acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the concerns outlined above. The authors should focus on expanding the experimental evaluation, clarifying the method's limitations, and providing a more thorough analysis of its scalability and robustness. Specifically, experiments with more realistic data and a detailed comparison with state-of-the-art MHMM learning algorithms are crucial.
