This paper presents a method of moments (MoM) or "spectral" algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The key insight is to view an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, as a single large HMM with MK hidden states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose applying existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012) to learn the parameters of this large, equivalent HMM.

A critical challenge arises from this approach: standard spectral learning for HMMs only recovers parameters up to an arbitrary permutation of the hidden states. In the MHMM case, this permutation mixes the states from different component HMMs, making it impossible to recover the individual model parameters. The main contribution of this paper is a novel "de-permutation" algorithm to solve this component alignment problem. The proposed method exploits the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. In the ideal noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1. By performing a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix using the top K eigenvectors, the authors show that the columns of the resulting matrix form K distinct clusters. Clustering these columns reveals the permutation and allows for the recovery of the original block-diagonal structure and thus the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also proposes a heuristic based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of components K from the data. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic and real-world handwritten character data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound for the most part. The core idea of using spectral properties to undo the permutation is clever and well-motivated. The experimental results on synthetic data convincingly show that the column clustering approach successfully recovers the block structure of the transition matrix, even with moderate noise. The experiments also confirm the significant computational advantage of the spectral method over the iterative EM algorithm.

However, the theoretical analysis has some weaknesses. Conjecture 1, which bounds the estimation error of the low-rank reconstruction, is central to the method's justification but is not proven. The authors state they are reusing a bound from Anandkumar et al. (2012) for a different model, and the justification provided is quite brief and hand-wavy. While a full proof might be out of scope, a more rigorous argument is needed to make the theoretical claims convincing. Similarly, the conditions in Lemma 3, particularly the eigenvalue gap condition, might be strong in practice, and it is unclear how robust the method is when these are not perfectly met.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The progression from the model definition, to the permutation problem, and finally to the spectral solution is logical and easy to follow. Figure 1 is particularly effective at illustrating the core problem and the intuition behind the de-permutation solution.

That said, the manuscript would benefit from a careful proofreading to correct several minor grammatical errors and awkward phrases. For instance, the description of the algorithm could be more precise at times (e.g., in Algorithm 1, "Identify K̂ as the eigenvalue with largest longevity" should be something like "Identify K̂ as the number of components corresponding to the largest longevity gap").

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the de-permutation algorithm based on clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstructed transition matrix—appears to be novel for the MHMM learning problem. This is a significant contribution because this alignment problem is a fundamental barrier to applying spectral methods to many mixture models.

However, the paper fails to place its contribution in the context of highly relevant prior work. The problem of identifying mixture components from a globally estimated model is not new. Specifically, Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013, "Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions") tackle a very similar problem. They also learn a global model and then use a clustering-based approach to recover the individual component parameters. A discussion and comparison of the proposed de-permutation strategy with such existing techniques are essential for properly assessing the paper's originality and contribution.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem. Learning MHMMs is a common task in sequence analysis, and the computational cost of the standard EM algorithm is a major bottleneck for large datasets. A fast, non-iterative, and theoretically grounded spectral algorithm would be a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers. The de-permutation method proposed here is an elegant idea that could potentially be adapted to other hierarchical or mixture models where spectral methods produce similarly permuted outputs.

The experimental results on real data show that the proposed method serves as an excellent initializer for EM, consistently outperforming random initialization. While this is a very useful and practical result, it does slightly temper the claim that the spectral method is a standalone replacement for EM, as EM is still required for the best performance. Nonetheless, a robust and fast initialization scheme is a significant contribution in its own right.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of efficient learning for MHMMs.
*   The proposed de-permutation algorithm is an elegant and novel solution to the key challenge of component alignment in spectral learning for mixture models.
*   The method is computationally much faster than the standard EM algorithm, which is a major practical advantage.
*   The results demonstrate that the method works well as a superior initialization strategy for EM, which is a valuable contribution.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The paper fails to discuss or compare its approach with highly relevant prior work on component recovery in spectral learning, notably Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013).
*   The theoretical analysis is not fully rigorous, relying on an insufficiently justified conjecture (Conjecture 1).
*   The experiments, while illustrative, show that the method does not consistently outperform a well-tuned EM algorithm on its own, positioning it more as an initialization technique than a full replacement.

### Detailed Comments for Authors:
I would encourage the authors to revise the paper to address the following points:
1.  **Related Work:** It is crucial that you discuss the work of Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013), and potentially others who have worked on the component alignment problem in spectral learning for mixture models. How is your spectral clustering approach related to or different from theirs? This will help situate your contribution more clearly.
2.  **Theoretical Justification:** Please try to provide a more detailed and rigorous argument for Conjecture 1. Even if a full proof is too long, a more convincing sketch would greatly strengthen the paper.
3.  **Framing of Contribution:** Your real-data experiments show that MoM+EM is the best performing method. This is a strong and very practical result. I suggest you consider highlighting this "spectral initialization" aspect more strongly in the paper, as it's a clear and demonstrable advance over standard practice (random initialization).
4.  **Minor Edits:** Please perform a thorough proofread of the manuscript to improve clarity and fix grammatical issues.
