Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component can be represented as a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first learn this global HMM with existing spectral HMM techniques (e.g., Hsu et al. 2012; Anandkumar et al.), which returns parameters up to an unknown state permutation. They then propose a “depermutation” step that exploits the spectral structure of the block-diagonal transition matrix: in the noiseless case, the global transition has K unit eigenvalues, and powers of the transition collapse each block to a rank-1 matrix whose columns can be clustered to recover the blocks. In the noisy case, they propose to (i) select K via a “spectral longevity” score over the sorted eigenvalues, (ii) reconstruct a rank-K approximation using the top K “global” eigenvalues, and (iii) cluster columns to infer the permutation and recover per-component parameters. The paper includes lemmas justifying the noiseless case, a conjectured robustness bound for the noisy case, and experiments on synthetic and a handwriting dataset. This sits at the intersection of spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu, Kakade, Zhang; Anandkumar et al.) and classic MHMMs/EM (e.g., Smyth NIPS’97), providing a novel route to scalable MHMM estimation.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea that leverages a well-known equivalence (MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM) and a practical depermutation procedure based on eigen-structure.
- Computationally appealing regime: once moments are computed, parameter estimation does not scale with the number or lengths of sequences.
- Potentially useful heuristic to infer K via eigenvalue “longevity.”
- Real-data experiment suggests spectral estimates can serve as a useful EM initializer.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The robustness analysis is largely heuristic. Conjecture 1 is not proven; the depermutation step relies on strong spectral gap assumptions and a separation between “global” and “local” eigenvalues. A formal condition under which the correct permutation is recovered with high probability is missing.
- No guarantee that the spectral HMM estimate of the global transition will be close to block diagonal under realistic noise and finite-sample errors; many dense matrices may be “close” to multiple block structures, making the clustering step unstable. A principled metric and sensitivity analysis (e.g., recovery vs. Frobenius distance to block-diagonal set, dependence on gaps and sample size) would help.
- Section 4 uses synthetic setups that do not convincingly reflect end-to-end learning from observed sequences. Several experiments corrupt already-permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise rather than sampling sequences from an MHMM and re-estimating moments from data. This bypasses the most brittle step (moment estimation) and weakens the empirical case.
- In Sec. 4.2, the parameterization of the mixture components (Ak, Ok, νk) is underspecified. Reproducibility requires full details (transition sparsity/density, mixing rates, emission parameters, initialization, regularization), ideally in an appendix with seeds and code.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and the main idea is understandable. However, several experimental details are missing (see above), and some figures have text that is too small to read.
- Minor typo: In the eigen-decomposition argument (Sec. 3.2.1, proof structure for Lemma 2), the subscript of Λ in the block corresponding to AK should be K, not 2.

Originality
- Interpreting MHMMs as block-diagonal HMMs is known; using spectral HMM estimation to learn the global model is also known. The novel component is the depermutation via eigen-structure and the “spectral longevity” heuristic for selecting K. This is a reasonable and interesting combination with a concrete algorithmic instantiation.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could make MHMM learning significantly cheaper on large datasets. However:
  - The method requires estimating an MK-state HMM spectrally, which can be memory/time intensive and requires L ≥ MK with O full column rank (as the authors note in the real-data section). This severely restricts applicability for high-state, low-dimensional observations (a common setting).
  - The lack of end-to-end experiments from raw sequences and the absence of robustness guarantees leave its practical performance uncertain.

Specific comments and requests
- Scope/usage regime: Please clarify when the method is appropriate. In particular, discuss the dependence on L ≥ MK and the condition number of Ō, as well as computational and memory complexity vs. EM as functions of N, T, K, M, L. It would help to provide wall-clock and memory comparisons for realistically large K, M.
- Robustness theory: Replace (or complement) Conjecture 1 with a theorem providing conditions under which the depermutation is correct w.h.p., in terms of (i) gaps between global and local eigenvalues, (ii) mixing rates of Ak, (iii) sampling error in moments, and (iv) noise levels. A perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin-style bounds) would strengthen the contribution.
- End-to-end experiments: Add experiments that (i) sample sequences from MHMMs, (ii) estimate moments from those sequences, (iii) run the full pipeline, and (iv) evaluate recovery of permutation, K, parameters, and clustering accuracy. Compare to EM with good restarts and to baselines such as “learn a single HMM and cluster posteriors.”
- Experimental detail: Provide complete generative parameters (Ak, Ok, νk, π), including sparsity patterns, spectral gaps, emission variances, and sequence lengths/distributions. Include code or a detailed appendix.
- Unexpected EM behavior: EM initialized with spectral estimates sometimes underperforms the spectral estimates alone. Please explain: are you reporting training vs. test metrics? Is EM monotone in training likelihood as expected? Could likelihood misspecification or overfitting explain the drop? Consider early stopping or regularization.
- Block structure proximity: Report a quantitative measure of how close the learned Â is to block-diagonal (e.g., ratio of off-block to on-block mass, or distance to nearest block-diagonal matrix under known K), and relate it to permutation recovery accuracy.
- Figures: Increase font sizes and ensure readability.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and potentially impactful idea; elegant use of spectral structure for depermutation.
- Practical algorithm with promising speed advantages over EM.
- Novel heuristic to estimate K from eigenvalue “longevity.”

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees for the noisy case; key robustness result is a conjecture.
- Experimental evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate end-to-end performance under realistic noise/sample sizes; missing details.
- Applicability constraints (L ≥ MK) and potential computational burden for large MK are not thoroughly addressed.
- Some anomalies in EM comparisons are unexplained.

Recommendation
Borderline reject (weak reject). The idea is interesting and seems promising, but the current version lacks rigorous robustness guarantees and sufficiently detailed, end-to-end empirical validation to support publication. Addressing the theoretical gaps and strengthening the experiments as suggested would make this a strong submission.
