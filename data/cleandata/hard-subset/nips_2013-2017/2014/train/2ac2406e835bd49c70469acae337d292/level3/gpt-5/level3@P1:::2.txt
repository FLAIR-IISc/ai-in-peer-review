This paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be recast as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with one block per component. The authors then apply off-the-shelf spectral HMM estimators (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012) to the global HMM to recover parameters up to a permutation of the MK latent states. The main contribution is a stabilization-based depermutation procedure: exploit the spectral structure of the global transition matrix Ā to recover the block structure (and hence the per-HMM parameters) by isolating the K eigenvalues tied to the unit eigenvalue of each block, reconstructing the rank-K subspace, and clustering columns of the stabilized power Āe (or its rank-K reconstruction) to infer the permutation. The paper further proposes a data-driven estimator for K via an eigenvalue “longevity” score (Eq. 7), and presents synthetic and a small real-data experiment.

Relation to prior work: Spectral methods for HMMs and mixtures (Hsu et al., JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012; Anandkumar et al., 2014) are the basis here. Framing an MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is standard, but using eigenvalue stabilization to undo the spectral permutation and to select K is novel to my knowledge. The paper briefly references spectral initialization for EM in other contexts (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2013 for mixtures of regressions) and compares to EM, but could better position itself relative to prior MHMM learning and clustering-sequences literature (e.g., Smyth, NIPS 1997; Qi et al., TSP 2006), and to other ways to resolve permutations in spectral learning.

Quality: The reduction from MHMM to block-diagonal HMM is correct and clearly presented. The depermutation idea is intuitive: in the noiseless case, powers of Ā separate blocks via their stationary distributions; in the noisy case, estimate the leading K “global” eigenvalues/eigenvectors and cluster columns in the corresponding low-rank reconstruction. However, the theoretical support in the noisy case is limited. Conjecture 1 gives an O(1/√TN) error under a spectral separation condition |λGK| > maxk |λL2,k|, but this is neither proved nor related to standard perturbation results (e.g., Davis–Kahan, Weyl), and the condition itself may be fragile when some local second eigenvalues are close to 1 (slow-mixing chains) or when components have similar stationary distributions. The K-estimation criterion (Eq. 7) is plausible but heuristic; a finite-sample analysis or at least an empirical ablation showing robustness to eigenvalue crowding and noise would strengthen the paper. The experimental section demonstrates feasibility but is basic; several design choices (e.g., lifting features to ensure L ≥ MK) are reasonable for spectral methods but deserve discussion of trade-offs and failure modes.

Clarity: The exposition would benefit from substantial polishing. Notation is sometimes confusing or inconsistent:
- Clearly define all dimensions on first use (e.g., 1M vs 1J; MK vs M and K; sizes of O, A, ν, and their “barred” versions).
- In Corollary 1, explicitly write the block structure of lime→∞ Āe, and define v̄k and 1M with dimensions.
- Avoid reusing e both as exponent and as an index/variable; define the noise symbol consistently rather than leaving it implicit.
- Consider aligning notation with common HMM conventions (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009): use T for the transition matrix, n for observation dimension, m for number of hidden states; or, if keeping current symbols, provide a table of notation.
Figures (especially Fig. 4) are hard to read; results would be clearer with tables and confidence intervals.

Originality: The overall pipeline is “spectral HMM + depermutation,” where the spectral step is prior work, and the new pieces are (i) using the stabilized eigenspace of Ā to undo permutations and (ii) the eigenvalue longevity criterion for K. The depermutation idea is simple but neat and practically useful; I am not aware of prior work that leverages the global block structure of Ā in this way for MHMMs. That said, the novelty is incremental and largely algorithmic rather than theoretical.

Significance: If made robust, the approach could materially lower the cost of fitting MHMMs on large datasets and provide strong initializations for EM, which practitioners may adopt. The real-data results suggest spectral initialization can help EM, but the gains are mixed; stronger evidence would increase impact.

Experimental evaluation: The synthetic experiments show the depermutation can work with modest sequence lengths. However:
- Report both average and best-of-m restarts for EM, and use the Hungarian mapping to measure parameter recovery error alongside clustering accuracy.
- Compare against additional baselines (e.g., EM with k-means/Fisher-score initialization; Bayesian sticky-HDP-HMM mixtures; simple clustering on HMM likelihoods).
- Evaluate K estimation accuracy across a grid of mixing rates and noise levels (vary second eigenvalues).
- Test multiple emission families (Poisson, multinomial) as claimed, and discuss the L ≥ MK requirement and the polynomial feature lift used on UCI trajectories.
- Provide runtime tables (moments, SVD/eigendecomposition, EM) with consistent hardware and stopping criteria.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant and intuitive depermutation via spectral stabilization of Ā.
- Computationally attractive pipeline; decouples data size from the core estimation step once moments are computed.
- Practical contribution as an initialization for EM; promising synthetic results.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theory in the noisy case; key claims are conjectural with fragile spectral conditions.
- Experimental evaluation is thin; figures are hard to interpret; comparisons to strong baselines are lacking.
- Clarity/notation issues hinder readability and reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide finite-sample perturbation analysis (e.g., via Davis–Kahan) for the leading eigenspace and derive conditions under which the column clustering is consistent; characterize failure cases (periodic/slow-mixing chains, similar stationary distributions).
- Strengthen K estimation theoretically or compare against standard criteria (BIC, eigen-gap).
- Add ablations and broader baselines; report parameter error and permutation recovery; include confidence intervals.
- Clean up notation, dimensions, and figure/table presentation; release code.

Overall: This is a nice idea with practical appeal, but the current submission would benefit from stronger theoretical backing and a more thorough experimental study. I lean toward borderline reject/weak accept depending on program balance; with the above revisions, it could make a solid contribution.
