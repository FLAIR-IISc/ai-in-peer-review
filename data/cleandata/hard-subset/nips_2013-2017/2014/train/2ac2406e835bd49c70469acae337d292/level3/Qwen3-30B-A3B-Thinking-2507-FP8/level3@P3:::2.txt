

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by recognizing MHMMs as a special case of HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices. The authors leverage existing spectral HMM learning methods (Anandkumar et al. 2012) and introduce a novel stabilization method to resolve the permutation ambiguity that typically prevents recovery of individual HMM parameters from the block-diagonal transition matrix. The key innovation is using the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to estimate a depermutation mapping that recovers the original HMM parameters.

**Strengths:**
The core idea of using spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity is clever and intuitively appealing. The authors successfully demonstrate that under reasonable assumptions (each local transition matrix has a single eigenvalue of 1), the global transition matrix exhibits a structure that can be exploited to recover the block-diagonal form. The theoretical analysis in Lemma 2 and Corollary 1 provides a solid foundation for the approach, and the noise analysis (Equation 7) is a thoughtful addition that acknowledges practical limitations. The experimental results confirm the algorithm's ability to recover the correct number of clusters and block structure even with modest data.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has several significant weaknesses that need addressing. First, the notation is confusing throughout, particularly in Corollary 1 where the meaning of 1J^T is unclear, and the block-diagonal structure of lim_{e->inf} \bar{A}^e isn't properly explained. The paper would benefit greatly from defining all vectors/matrices with their dimensions at first use, as this is critical for understanding the spectral properties.

Second, the paper lacks sufficient engagement with related work. It should cite prior work on using spectral methods to initialize EM (Chaganty and Liang 2013), which is directly relevant to the authors' claim that their approach can serve as a good initialization for EM. The paper also doesn't clearly distinguish its approach from existing methods for learning mixtures of HMMs.

Third, the experimental evaluation is inadequate. Figure 4 is poorly designed and difficult to interpret, and results should be presented in tables rather than in a cluttered figure. The paper reports average EM accuracy rather than best accuracy, which is misleading since EM is highly sensitive to initialization. The authors should also provide more thorough comparisons with other initialization methods.

Fourth, the paper would benefit from adopting established HMM notation (e.g., using T for transition matrices instead of A, and n/m for observation/hidden state counts). The current notation (e.g., using A for transition matrices in the MHMM context) is inconsistent with standard HMM literature (Hsu et al. 2009).

**Quality and Significance:**
While the algorithmic approach is sound, the theoretical analysis is incomplete - the key Conjecture 1 is not proven, and the noise analysis lacks rigorous justification. The practical significance is limited by the basic experiments that don't convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods. The claim that the method "can be used as a very good initialization scheme for EM" is not thoroughly validated, as the real data experiment only shows marginal improvement over random initialization.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to the field. The core algorithm is largely based on existing spectral HMM methods, with the main novelty being the stabilization method for resolving permutation ambiguity. However, the paper's weaknesses in clarity, related work engagement, and experimental evaluation significantly undermine its contribution.

**Recommendation:**
The paper would benefit from major revisions before acceptance. The authors should:
1. Clarify all notation with explicit dimension definitions
2. Add a thorough discussion of related work, including Chaganty and Liang (2013)
3. Replace Figure 4 with properly formatted tables showing both best and average results
4. Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the noise case
5. Adopt standard HMM notation conventions
6. Conduct more thorough comparisons with existing methods

The key insight regarding spectral properties for resolving permutation is interesting and could be valuable if properly developed and evaluated. As written, the paper doesn't fully demonstrate its significance or provide sufficient evidence to support its claims.
