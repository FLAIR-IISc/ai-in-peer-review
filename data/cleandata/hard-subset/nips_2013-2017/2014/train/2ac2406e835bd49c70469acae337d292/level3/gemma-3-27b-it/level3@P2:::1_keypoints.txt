- Proposes learning Mixture of HMMs by treating them as a single HMM with block-diagonal transition, using MoM/spectral learning, then resolving permutation ambiguity spectrally.  
- Builds on existing MoM and spectral HMM work to address applying MoM to MHMMs.  
- Strong motivation: offers a potentially more scalable alternative to EM for large datasets.  
- Theoretical approach is technically sound with logically consistent lemmas and proofs.  
- Critical practical concern: learning a full HMM first may be computationally prohibitive for large, sparse mixtures.  
- Conjectured noise tolerance and estimation error bounds lack rigorous proof and further analysis.  
- Experiments demonstrate feasibility but are too limited to establish practical effectiveness.  
- EM initialized with the spectral method sometimes performs worse than spectral alone, which is puzzling and unexplained.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation overall.  
- Minor typo in Lemma 2 (Λ subscript should be k, not 2).  
- Spectral properties and de-permutation conditions are dense and could be hard for non-experts; more intuition is needed.  
- Figures have small text/numbers, reducing readability.  
- Originality: novel combination of MoM and spectral learning for MHMMs, but relies heavily on established techniques.  
- Potential significance if robust and scalable across domains (speech, bioinformatics, time series), but current results don’t demonstrate this.  
- Missing experimental details (parameter settings) and use of Dirichlet noise instead of real observed-sequence noise limit validity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel technique combination, solid theoretical foundation, feasibility shown on synthetic data.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, scalability concerns, unclear spectral decomposition guarantees under noise, unexplained EM behavior, unrealistic noise modeling.  
- Overall recommendation: Reject, pending stronger experimental evidence, rigorous noise/error analysis, scalability discussion, and clearer presentation of strengths and limitations.
