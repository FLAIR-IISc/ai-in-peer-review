This paper proposes a novel spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), leveraging the Method of Moments (MoM) to address computational challenges associated with traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms. The core idea is to initially learn a global HMM, recognizing the MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, and then resolve the resulting permutation ambiguity using the spectral properties of the transition matrix. This approach aims to achieve parameter estimation without the iterative and potentially expensive forward-backward computations of EM, particularly for large datasets. The work builds upon existing MoM algorithms for latent variable models [1, 2, 3] and related spectral methods for HMM learning [3], addressing a gap in applying MoM to more complex models like MHMMs.

**Strengths:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach. The mathematical derivation connecting MHMMs to HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is well-explained, and the proposed method for resolving the permutation ambiguity through spectral properties is innovative.  The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of EM for MHMMs and offer a potentially scalable alternative. The inclusion of a real-data experiment, albeit limited, demonstrates an attempt to evaluate the method's performance beyond synthetic data. The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the problem and proposed solution. The proof of Lemma 1 effectively demonstrates the equivalence between the MHMM and a specific HMM structure, laying the foundation for the proposed learning approach.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness lies in the limited and somewhat artificial experimental validation. While the synthetic experiments demonstrate the depermutation algorithm's functionality, they lack sufficient complexity and realism. The experiments focus on corrupting *estimated* matrices with Dirichlet noise rather than *learning from observed sequences* directly, failing to represent the noise inherent in real-world data acquisition and the effects of limited sample sizes.  This raises significant concerns about the method’s practical applicability. 

A key concern is the scalability of the initial HMM learning step. The authors acknowledge the computational advantage of MoM, but learning a single HMM, even with a block-diagonal structure, can be impractical for large and sparse mixtures, especially when the number of hidden states (M) is high.  The paper needs to explicitly clarify the conditions under which this method is appropriate—e.g., limitations on M and K—and when EM or other approaches might be more suitable.  

The authors’ conjecture regarding the spectral properties of the learned transition matrix is crucial, yet potentially fragile.  The assumption that the matrix will be "nearly" sparse enough to be reliably clustered is not sufficiently justified. Real-world noise could easily lead to a full transition matrix, making the depermutation step ineffective. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the robustness of the spectral properties to noise and a discussion of potential strategies for handling cases where the spectral decomposition is ambiguous.

The results in Section 4.2 are presented without sufficient detail regarding the MHMM parameters used (transition matrices, observation matrices), making it difficult to assess the experimental setup and reproducibility.  Providing these parameters, ideally in an appendix, is essential.  Furthermore, the observation that EM initialized with the spectral learning approach *sometimes performs worse* than spectral learning alone is puzzling and requires further investigation. The authors suggest this is related to inaccuracies in the initial spectral estimate but need to provide a more convincing explanation; EM should generally refine the initialization.

Minor issues include typographical errors (e.g., the subscript of Λ should be K in the proof) and the small font size in the figures, hindering readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to MHMM learning with theoretical grounding.
*   Potential for computational efficiency compared to EM.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and proposed solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently compelling experimental validation.
*   Scalability concerns regarding the initial HMM learning step.
*   Fragility of the spectral properties to noise.
*   Lack of detailed experimental parameters and explanation for counterintuitive results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of MHMM learning. However, the lack of robust experimental validation and the concerns regarding scalability and noise sensitivity prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The paper's claims regarding practical performance remain unsubstantiated.  I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should significantly expand the experimental section with more realistic datasets and noise models, thoroughly analyze the method’s scalability limitations, and provide a more in-depth discussion of the robustness of the spectral properties.  Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and its potential impact.
