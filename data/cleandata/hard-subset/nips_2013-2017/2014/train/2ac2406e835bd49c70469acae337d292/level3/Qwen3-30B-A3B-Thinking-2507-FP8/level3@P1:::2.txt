

This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by reformulating MHMMs as a special HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage the Method of Moments (MoM) framework from Anandkumar et al. (2012) to learn global parameters up to permutation ambiguity, then introduce a novel spectral stabilization step to resolve the permutation using the eigenstructure of the global transition matrix. The key innovation lies in Lemma 3 and Equation (7), which provide conditions to estimate the number of clusters K and recover individual HMM parameters from the permuted global estimates, even under estimation noise. This addresses a fundamental challenge in MHMM learning where existing EM-based approaches suffer from high computational cost (O(NT) per iteration) and sensitivity to initialization.

**Strengths:**  
The core idea of using spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity is elegant and well-motivated. The noise analysis (Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3) provides a theoretically grounded approach to handle estimation errors, which is a significant contribution over prior work. The computational advantage over EM is empirically validated (Figure 4), with the spectral method being orders of magnitude faster while maintaining comparable accuracy on synthetic data. The real-data experiment (Table 1) demonstrates practical utility as a superior initialization for EM, outperforming random initialization.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Clarity and Notation:** Critical notation is ambiguous (e.g., Corollary 1’s `1J^T`, unclear dimensions of `1J`, and inconsistent matrix notations like `1>M` vs. `1J`). The block-diagonal structure of `lim_{e→∞} Ā^e` (Equation 6) requires explicit dimension specification (e.g., `v̄k ∈ ℝ^{MK}`), which the paper omits. This impedes reproducibility.  
2. **Related Work:** The paper fails to cite pivotal related work. Chaganty & Liang (2013) used spectral methods to initialize EM for linear regressions, and Smyth (1997) pioneered MHMMs with EM. The claim of novelty is weakened by omitting these.  
3. **Experiments:** Results lack rigor. Figure 4 is poorly designed (overlapping error bars, unclear axes), and Table 1 reports only average EM accuracy, not the *best* accuracy over 5 random initializations (as claimed). The "EM initialized with MoM" result (Table 1) is misleading without reporting whether spectral initialization consistently beats random EM. The synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) uses only 2 sequences per cluster, which is insufficient for robustness claims.  
4. **Theoretical Gaps:** Conjecture 1’s error bound (‖Ar − Ar‖_F ≤ O(1/√TN)) relies on a non-justified sample complexity from a mixture model (Anandkumar et al.), not MHMMs. The noise analysis (Definition 1) is mathematically opaque without intuitive interpretation.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel spectral depermutation technique (Lemma 3), valid computational advantage, and practical initialization utility.  
*Con:* Critical notation errors, missing key citations, weak experimental validation (especially EM comparisons), and unverified theoretical claims.  

**Recommendation:**  
Reject. The core algorithmic insight is promising but undermined by insufficient theoretical justification, unclear presentation, and experimental weaknesses that prevent confident assessment of its contribution. The paper would benefit from: (1) rigorous notation with dimension definitions; (2) direct comparison to Chaganty & Liang (2013) and Smyth (1997); (3) corrected experiments reporting best EM accuracy; and (4) tighter theoretical grounding for Conjecture 1. Without these revisions, the work does not meet NIPS’s standards for quality, clarity, and significance. The computational speed gain is meaningful but insufficient to offset the unresolved methodological and presentation issues.
