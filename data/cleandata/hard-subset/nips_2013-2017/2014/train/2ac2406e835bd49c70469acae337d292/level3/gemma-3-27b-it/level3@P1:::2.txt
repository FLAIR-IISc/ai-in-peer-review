This paper presents a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea is to leverage recent advances in MoM for HMMs [1, 2, 3] by recognizing that an MHMM can be reformulated as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. A significant challenge in MHMM learning – the permutation ambiguity – is addressed by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix to estimate a de-permutation mapping, effectively recovering individual HMM parameters. The authors also propose a method for estimating the number of HMMs under certain spectral conditions.

The work builds upon a growing body of research utilizing MoM for latent variable models, offering a computationally efficient alternative to traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) methods [1, 2, 3].  MHMMs are well-established for sequence clustering [4] and have found applications in diverse areas such as music analysis [5], motion analysis [6], and time series clustering [7].  The paper’s contribution lies in extending MoM to this more complex model, tackling the critical permutation problem inherent in MHMM parameter estimation.  The approach is conceptually appealing, framing MHMM learning as a specialized case of HMM learning and employing spectral techniques for disambiguation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed method offers a fresh perspective on MHMM learning, bypassing the computational bottlenecks of EM and addressing the permutation ambiguity effectively.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The approach is grounded in a solid theoretical framework, leveraging the spectral properties of the global transition matrix for parameter recovery.  The discussion surrounding the impact of estimation noise and the conditions for determining the number of HMMs are particularly insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** MoM-based algorithms are inherently more computationally efficient than EM, particularly for large datasets, a benefit explicitly highlighted in the paper.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of MHMM learning and positions the proposed method as a solution to these challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** The notation is often dense and can be difficult to follow. Consistent definitions of matrix and vector dimensions upon their first introduction are crucial. For instance, the meaning of 1J^T and the block-diagonal structure in Lemma 1 needs more elaboration.  Adopting standard HMM notation (e.g., T for transitions, n/m for observation/hidden state counts, as in [8]) would improve readability.
* **Related Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of related work. The authors should engage more fully with existing literature on MHMM learning and, crucially, acknowledge related efforts on using spectral methods to *initialize* EM for HMMs [9].  Citing Chaganty and Liang (2013) is essential, as it explores similar spectral initialization strategies.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is relatively basic and the presentation of results is suboptimal. Figure 4 is particularly difficult to interpret. Results should be presented in clear tables with appropriate statistical analysis.  The surprisingly poor performance of EM reported in the experiments raises questions.  The authors should report the *best* (rather than average) EM accuracy, possibly with multiple random restarts, and provide a more thorough comparative analysis.
* **Proof Sketches:** Some of the proofs and justifications, particularly around Conjecture 1, feel incomplete. While the authors acknowledge the limitations, providing more detail or referencing related theoretical results would strengthen the arguments.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to MHMM learning that addresses a key challenge (permutation ambiguity).
* The theoretical grounding is solid, offering insights into the spectral properties of MHMMs.
* The potential for computational efficiency, particularly for large datasets, is significant.
* The core algorithm builds upon a sound and established method (spectral learning of HMMs) with a clever stabilization step.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from clarity issues due to dense notation and a lack of rigorous definitions.
* The related work section is incomplete and misses key contributions.
* The experimental evaluation is weak and lacks sufficient comparative analysis.
* The proofs, while promising, could benefit from greater detail and justification.


**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising and innovative approach to learning MHMMs.  The core idea of leveraging spectral properties to address the permutation ambiguity is insightful and potentially impactful. However, the paper requires significant improvements in clarity, related work discussion, and experimental evaluation before it can be considered a strong contribution.  I recommend **rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit**, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically, a more thorough literature review, improved clarity in the presentation, and a more robust experimental evaluation are essential.  The authors should also consider expanding upon the theoretical justification for their approach and addressing the questions raised regarding the performance of EM in their experiments.
