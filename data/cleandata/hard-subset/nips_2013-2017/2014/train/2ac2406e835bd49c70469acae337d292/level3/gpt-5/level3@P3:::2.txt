Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a spectral method-of-moments (MoM) algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be cast as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal (one block per component). The authors then apply off-the-shelf spectral HMM learning (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2009) to estimate “global” parameters up to a latent-state permutation. The main novel step is a stabilization-based depermutation procedure that uses the spectral structure of the global transition matrix: under the assumption that each component transition matrix Ak has a single eigenvalue equal to 1 (i.e., an aperiodic, ergodic chain), the global transition Ā has K unit eigenvalues, and powers of Ā converge to a block structure revealing component assignments. With estimation noise, the authors propose a low-rank reconstruction and introduce a “spectral longevity” criterion (Equation (7)) to both estimate K and recover a permutation that re-block-diagonalizes the model, thereby disentangling component parameters. Experiments on synthetic data and a small real dataset illustrate feasibility and speed advantages over EM.

The reduction of MHMM to a structured HMM is standard, but leveraging the (stabilized) limiting behavior of powers of the estimated transition matrix to undo parameter permutation is an interesting and intuitive contribution. The work connects to spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012) and to the use of spectral estimates to initialize likelihood methods (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The MHMM-to-HMM reduction and the noiseless depermutation argument are correct and clearly motivated. The stabilization idea (rank-K reconstruction using the top “global” eigenvalues followed by column clustering) is compelling.
- However, the noise-robustness analysis is weak. Conjecture 1 is central but unproven; the bound reuses results from mixture models without a careful adaptation to HMMs. The eigen-gap conditions and the longevity criterion (Eq. (7)) are plausible but heuristic; they would benefit from standard perturbation tools (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) to bound eigenvector/eigenspace error and from a principled selection rule for K (e.g., eigengap statistics or information criteria).
- Assumptions: Requiring each Ak to have exactly one unit eigenvalue excludes periodic chains; this is reasonable in many applications but should be stated as an aperiodicity assumption and discussed (what happens if a component is nearly periodic?). The requirement MK < L for MoM is nontrivial; in the real-data experiment this necessitated a polynomial feature lift. Please discuss the practicality, sensitivity, and potential alternatives (e.g., tensor methods not constrained by L).
- Empirics: The synthetic experiments show the depermutation can work with moderate data, but the experimental section is basic and does not probe failure modes (small eigengaps, imbalanced components, different emission families, mismatch in K). The EM comparisons sometimes show unusually poor EM performance; best-of-multiple-restarts accuracy should be reported alongside averages and standard deviations.

Clarity
- The paper is readable overall, but notation is confusing in places and sometimes inconsistent.
  - Corollary 1: define all objects and dimensions explicitly. The notation 1J^T vs 1M, and the block-diagonal structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e, should be carefully laid out (e.g., “Ā ∈ R^{MK×MK}, lim_{e→∞} Ā^e consists of K blocks of size M×M, the kth block equals v_k 1_M^T, where v_k is the stationary distribution of A_k”).
  - Define the dimensions of every vector/matrix when first introduced (Ok ∈ R^{L×M}, Ā ∈ R^{MK×MK}, ν̄ ∈ R^{MK}, etc.).
  - Consider conforming to established HMM notation (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009): use T for transitions, m for hidden-state count, n for observation alphabet size/dimension. This would ease comparison with prior work.
- Figures/tables: Figure 4 is hard to read; results should be presented in clear tables with means/variances and best/average EM metrics. Label axes, units, and settings (N, T, K, M, L) consistently.

Originality
- The algorithmic core is a combination of existing spectral HMM estimation with a novel stabilization-based depermutation step. While the reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is known, the specific mechanism to undo permutation via powers of the transition matrix and the spectral longevity heuristic is a neat, intuitive idea. The analysis, however, stops short of a rigorous finite-sample guarantee.

Significance
- If made robust, the approach could be useful for fast MHMM initialization or standalone learning on large datasets. The potential to estimate K from spectra is attractive. Current empirical validation is modest; stronger evidence on real tasks and a tighter theory would increase impact. The feature-lifting requirement (MK < L) limits immediate applicability unless addressed.

Pros
- Clear reduction from MHMM to HMM enabling off-the-shelf spectral estimation.
- Novel, intuitive stabilization idea to resolve parameter permutation; practical depermutation via low-rank reconstruction and clustering.
- Addresses estimation noise and proposes a criterion (Eq. (7)) to select K.
- Demonstrates speed advantages over EM and shows utility as an EM initializer.

Cons
- Central robustness claim is a conjecture; theoretical analysis is incomplete.
- Strong assumptions (single unit eigenvalue per Ak; needs eigengap separation; MK < L).
- Limited experiments; weak baselines/reporting; Figure 4 hard to interpret; no ablations on eigengaps/noise/imbalanced components.
- Related work coverage is thin; missing discussion of spectral-initialized EM (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013) and broader spectral HMM literature.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is depermutation to small eigengaps between global and local eigenvalues? Can you quantify recovery probability vs. gap and sample size?
- What happens if some Ak are nearly periodic (eigenvalues near −1) or reducible? Can the method detect and handle such cases?
- How robust is the k-means step for column clustering? Would an assignment formulation or spectral clustering improve stability?
- Please report best-of-R restarts EM accuracy and variance; why does EM underperform in your plots?
- Can you relax MK < L (e.g., via tensor methods over time-lagged views) to avoid feature lifting?

Suggestions for revision
- Strengthen the theory: replace Conjecture 1 with a finite-sample bound using standard perturbation results; justify Eq. (7) or replace with an eigengap-based selection rule with error control.
- Expand related work: discuss spectral HMMs, MHMM learning, and spectral initializations (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012; Chaganty and Liang, 2013).
- Improve notation and define dimensions early; clarify Corollary 1 and the structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e.
- Redesign experiments: clearer figures/tables; report best/avg EM over multiple restarts; add baselines; include ablations on noise/eigengaps; more real datasets.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents an appealing and practical idea—stabilization for depermutation in MHMM spectral learning—on top of established spectral HMM methods. With stronger theoretical guarantees, clearer notation, and more convincing experiments, the contribution would be solid. As it stands, it is interesting but underdeveloped. My inclination is reject unless the authors can substantially strengthen theory and experiments in revision; however, I would be positive about a workshop/poster version.

Confidence: medium.
