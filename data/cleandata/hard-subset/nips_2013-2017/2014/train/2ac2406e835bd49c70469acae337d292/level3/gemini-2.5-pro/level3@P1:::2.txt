### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a spectral, method-of-moments-based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The core idea is to re-frame the MHMM, which consists of K individual HMMs, as a single, larger HMM with a total of MK hidden states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. This formulation allows the authors to apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2012) to estimate the global parameters.

The main technical challenge, and the paper's key contribution, is resolving the permutation ambiguity that arises from this approach. The spectral HMM learner recovers the global parameter set, but the states are permuted, making it impossible to disentangle the parameters corresponding to the K individual HMMs. The authors propose a novel de-permutation method that leverages the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. Assuming each component HMM has a single eigenvalue of 1, the global transition matrix will have K eigenvalues of 1. By analyzing the powers of the estimated transition matrix, the authors show that its columns will cluster into K groups corresponding to the component HMMs, thereby revealing the permutation. The paper extends this idea to handle estimation noise, where this spectral property is not perfectly preserved, and proposes a heuristic based on "spectral longevity" of eigenvalues to estimate the number of clusters K. The method is validated on synthetic and real-world handwriting data.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—the method for resolving the permutation ambiguity—is novel and elegant. It addresses a fundamental roadblock to applying standard spectral HMM learners to the MHMM setting. By providing a way to "de-permute" the learned parameters, the paper makes spectral learning a viable, computationally efficient alternative to EM for this important model class. The potential for significant speedups on large datasets is a major advantage.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The core idea of exploiting the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix to identify the component HMMs is intuitive and theoretically sound. The paper correctly identifies that analyzing the powers of the transition matrix can reveal its underlying block structure. The attempt to formalize this for the noisy case via eigenvalue analysis (Conjecture 1, Lemma 3) is a valuable step towards a robust algorithm.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper's clarity suffers from inconsistent and sometimes confusing notation, which makes it difficult to follow.
    *   Vectors and matrices are often introduced without their dimensions being explicitly defined (e.g., `O`, `A`, `ν`). Conforming to standard practice by stating dimensions upon first use (e.g., `A \in R^{M \times M}`) would be very helpful.
    *   The notation in Corollary 1 and its proof is opaque. The structure of `lim e→∞ Ā^e` and the meaning of vectors like `v̄k` are not immediately clear from the text. A more detailed explanation of this block structure is needed.
    *   The authors could improve readability by adopting more established notation from the HMM literature (e.g., using `T` for the transition matrix and `n`/`m` for observation/state space sizes, as in Hsu et al., 2009, instead of `A`/`L`/`MK`).

2.  **Engagement with Related Work:** The paper's literature review is insufficient. It fails to cite or discuss highly relevant prior work. A notable omission is the line of work on using spectral estimates to provide high-quality initializations for EM. For example, Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013) explore this for mixtures of linear regressions. Given that the authors' own real-data experiment concludes that their method serves as a "very good initialization scheme for EM," this body of work is directly relevant and must be discussed to properly contextualize the paper's contribution.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments feel preliminary and the presentation could be improved.
    *   The comparison with EM is not entirely convincing. EM appears to perform unusually poorly in Figure 4, especially given its known effectiveness with good initializations. The authors should provide more details about their EM implementation: How many random restarts were used for the "randomly initialized EM"? Is the reported accuracy an average over these restarts, or the best-of-k result? Standard practice is to report the best-of-k, which provides a much stronger baseline.
    *   The presentation of results is poor. Figure 4 is difficult to parse; numerical results of this nature are better presented in a table for clarity and precise comparison.
    *   The real-data experiment in Table 1 shows that the spectral method alone is often outperformed by randomly initialized EM, but excels as an initializer. This result seems to contradict the positioning of the algorithm as a standalone replacement for EM and reinforces the need to engage with the literature on spectral initialization.

### Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting and novel algorithm for learning MHMMs. The core contribution—a spectral method to undo parameter permutation—is a clever solution to an important problem. However, the paper is hampered by significant weaknesses in clarity, its review of related work, and the conclusiveness of its experimental evaluation. The novelty of the de-permutation technique is strong, but the paper would need substantial revision to meet the standards for publication at NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The proposed method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in spectral learning of MHMMs is novel, elegant, and addresses a concrete technical barrier.
*   The approach offers a principled and computationally efficient alternative to EM, which is significant for large-scale sequence clustering tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in its notation.
*   The literature review is inadequate and misses key related work on spectral initialization for EM.
*   The experimental comparison with EM is not rigorous enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method as a standalone algorithm. The results suggest its main value may be as an initializer, a context the paper fails to adequately explore.
