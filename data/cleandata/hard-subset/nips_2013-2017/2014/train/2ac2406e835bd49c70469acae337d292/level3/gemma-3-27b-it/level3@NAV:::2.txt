## Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a spectral method for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by leveraging recent work on spectral learning of HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012). The core idea is to exploit the equivalence between an MHMM and an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, applying the existing spectral method, and then resolving the resulting parameter permutation using spectral properties of the global transition matrix. This addresses a key challenge in MHMM learning – the computational cost of EM, especially for large datasets. 

The paper’s strength lies in its intuitive approach to tackling the permutation ambiguity. The stabilization method, based on the eigenvalues of the transition matrix, appears to be a significant contribution. The authors correctly identify the potential for computational advantages using a Method of Moments approach, making MHMM learning tractable for larger datasets. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The notation is often confusing, particularly in Corollary 1, and lacks consistent definitions. For instance, `1J^T` is introduced without sufficient context, and a clear explanation of the block-diagonal structure detailed in the corollary is missing. Consistently defining vector and matrix dimensions upon introduction would drastically improve clarity. Furthermore, the related work section is surprisingly sparse. The authors fail to cite relevant work, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013) which already explores initializing EM with spectral estimates, a directly related contribution. Adopting established HMM notation (e.g., using 'T' for transitions) as suggested by Hsu et al. (2009) would also aid readability.

The experiments are also a significant concern. They are basic and poorly presented; Figure 4 is difficult to interpret, and results should be summarized in tables. The reported results raise questions, particularly the unusually poor performance of EM. Reporting the *best* EM accuracy, alongside average results, and providing more thorough comparisons to baselines are crucial. 

In summary, the algorithm can be viewed as an existing spectral HMM method augmented with a novel stabilization step to undo parameter permutation. While the core idea is promising, the paper requires substantial revisions to improve clarity, thoroughly address related work, and strengthen the experimental validation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel stabilization method for resolving parameter permutation.
*   Potential for computational efficiency compared to EM.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in MHMM learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Confusing notation and lack of clarity.
*   Insufficient engagement with related work.
*   Weak and poorly presented experimental evaluation.
*   Raises questions about the experimental results (e.g., EM performance).
