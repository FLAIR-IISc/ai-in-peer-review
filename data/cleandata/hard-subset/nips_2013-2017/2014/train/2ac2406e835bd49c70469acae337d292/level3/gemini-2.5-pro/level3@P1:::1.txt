This paper presents a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the Method of Moments (MoM). The key insight is to reframe the MHMM, which consists of K individual HMMs each with M states, as a single, larger HMM with a total of MK states. This equivalent HMM possesses a special block-diagonal transition matrix. The proposed algorithm first applies an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the parameters of this global HMM. This initial step yields parameters that are correct only up to a permutation of the MK hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of the paper is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The authors show that the block-diagonal structure of the true global transition matrix endows it with specific spectral properties—namely, K eigenvalues of value 1. By analyzing the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the estimated (and permuted) transition matrix, they propose a method to identify the number of components K and cluster the hidden states, thereby recovering the block structure and de-permuting the parameters. This approach offers a potential computational advantage over the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be slow on large datasets due to its iterative nature.

### Strengths:

*   **Originality:** The approach of learning an MHMM by first estimating a global HMM and then "de-permuting" its parameters using spectral clustering is novel and clever. It provides an elegant, non-iterative framework for a problem typically solved with EM. The connection drawn between the block-diagonal structure and the eigenspectrum of the transition matrix is the core theoretical contribution and is well-articulated.
*   **Potential Significance:** A fast, consistent, and non-iterative algorithm for learning MHMMs would be a valuable contribution to the field. MHMMs are powerful models for sequence clustering, but the computational burden and local optima issues of EM can be significant drawbacks. This work, if proven robust, could enable the application of MHMMs to much larger datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The theoretical exposition in Section 3, particularly the derivation of the global HMM representation (Lemma 1) and the analysis of its spectral properties (Lemma 2 and Corollary 1), is clear and logically presented.

### Weaknesses:

The paper's primary weaknesses lie in unaddressed practical limitations and an experimental evaluation that does not sufficiently validate the complete proposed pipeline.

1.  **Scalability and Practicality:** The method's first step involves learning a global HMM with MK states. The complexity of spectral learning for HMMs depends polynomially on the number of hidden states (MK) and the observation dimension (L). For mixtures with a large number of components (K) or states per component (M), the size of this global model can become extremely large, potentially rendering the moment estimation and tensor decomposition steps computationally infeasible. The paper does not discuss this significant practical limitation or characterize the regimes (in terms of K, M, and L) where the method is applicable.
2.  **Robustness of the De-permutation Strategy:** The core assumption is that the estimated global transition matrix, Â_perm, will be close enough to a block-diagonal matrix for clustering to succeed. However, in a noisy, finite-data regime, the learned matrix Â_perm is not guaranteed to have this property. It is plausible that a dense matrix could be as close (in Frobenius norm, for instance) to the estimated matrix as the "true" permuted block-diagonal one. The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees or empirical evidence for the robustness of the de-permutation step against estimation noise from the MoM procedure. The argument rests on Conjecture 1, which is not proven and is borrowed from a related but different model.
3.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** This is the most critical weakness. The experiment in Section 4.1, which is meant to demonstrate the de-permutation algorithm's robustness to noise, appears to be flawed in its setup. The text suggests that the experiment involves clustering the columns of (Â_perm)^e, but it is not clear how Â_perm was obtained. A convincing experiment would involve: 1) sampling observation sequences from a known MHMM; 2) running the *entire* spectral learning pipeline to get an estimated Â_perm; and 3) then applying the de-permutation algorithm. The current experiment seems to bypass the challenging MoM estimation step by perhaps directly adding noise to the true permuted matrix. This fails to account for the complex, structured noise introduced by moment estimation from finite data, which is the central challenge in practice.
4.  **Lack of Experimental Detail:** The experiments are not described in enough detail to be reproducible. In Section 4.2, the parameters of the MHMM used to generate the data (e.g., the specific transition matrices A_k and observation matrices O_k) are not provided. This information is essential for interpreting the results and should be included, perhaps in an appendix.
5.  **Inconsistencies in Results:** The results in Table 1 for the real data experiment are puzzling. The "MoM+EM" approach, where the spectral method initializes EM, sometimes performs worse than the spectral method alone ("MoM"). Since EM is guaranteed to not decrease the data likelihood from its starting point, this drop in clustering accuracy is counter-intuitive and requires explanation. It could suggest a bug in the implementation or that the model with higher likelihood achieved lower clustering accuracy, which would be an interesting finding to discuss.

### Minor Issues:

*   There appears to be a typo in the proof of Lemma 2. The final matrix in the decomposition is V_KΛ_KV_K^-1, but the text seems to refer to a subscript of 2 (this may be a rendering issue in my copy).
*   The text and numbers in Figures 3 and 4 are too small to be easily legible, which hinders clarity.

### Overall Assessment:

The paper introduces an interesting and theoretically elegant idea for learning MHMMs. However, its practical viability and robustness are not convincingly demonstrated. The scalability of learning a large global HMM is a major unaddressed concern, and the experimental validation is insufficient as it does not appear to test the full learning pipeline under realistic conditions. While the theoretical contribution is solid, the significant doubts about its practical performance and the flaws in the experimental section lead me to recommend rejection in its current form. The paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the scalability issue and providing a more rigorous and realistic experimental evaluation.
