This paper proposes a novel method for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The traditional approach for learning MHMMs is the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. MoM-based spectral learning methods, as developed for HMMs by Hsu et al. (2012) and Anandkumar et al. (2012), offer a computationally efficient alternative with theoretical guarantees.

The authors' main idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) as a single large HMM with KM hidden states. This large HMM has a special structure: its transition matrix is block-diagonal. Applying existing spectral HMM learning algorithms to this larger model yields estimates of the global parameters. However, these parameters are recovered only up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The key contribution of this work is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The authors show that in the noiseless case, the estimated global transition matrix will have K eigenvalues of 1, corresponding to the K disconnected components. By exponentiating the estimated transition matrix, the columns corresponding to states within the same component HMM will cluster together. This insight allows them to design an algorithm that clusters the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix to find the correct de-permutation mapping and recover the parameters of the individual HMMs.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of scaling up learning for MHMMs. The proposed method is novel and elegant. Framing the MHMM as a large, block-diagonal HMM is a clever insight, and using the spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix to solve the resulting permutation problem is a creative and principled approach. If proven effective, this method could provide a valuable, computationally cheaper alternative to EM.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the theoretical motivation is easy to follow. The progression from the model definition to the proposed algorithm is logical and well-explained. The proofs for the noiseless case are straightforward and provide a solid foundation for the method's intuition.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The core theoretical ideas, particularly Lemma 2 and Corollary 1, are sound and provide a strong justification for the proposed de-permutation algorithm in an ideal, noiseless setting. The conjecture regarding the algorithm's performance under estimation noise is also interesting, establishing a formal condition under which the method is expected to work.

### Weaknesses

The primary weaknesses of this paper lie in the experimental validation, which is not sufficient to support the claims of practical utility, and in some unaddressed concerns about the method's stability and scope.

*   **Experimental Validation:**
    1.  The experiments do not appear to model the estimation process realistically. The key challenge for this method is its robustness to estimation error that arises from learning on a finite, noisy sample of observation sequences. However, the description of the experiments (particularly in Section 4.1) suggests that the validation may rely on directly corrupting the true permuted matrices with synthetic noise rather than running the full learning pipeline on generated data. To be convincing, the experiments must start from sequences sampled from a ground-truth MHMM, apply the full spectral learning algorithm to estimate the global HMM parameters, and then apply the proposed de-permutation procedure. This is the only way to assess performance under the kind of complex, structured noise inherent in the MoM estimation process.
    2.  The experimental results lack sufficient detail for reproducibility and critical evaluation. For instance, in Section 4.2, the parameters of the component HMMs (e.g., transition matrices, specific emission means) are not provided. The difficulty of the clustering task depends heavily on how distinct the component HMMs are. Without these details, it is hard to interpret the reported accuracies. Full experimental parameters should be included, at least in an appendix.
    3.  The result in Table 1 where EM initialized with the spectral method performs worse than the spectral method alone on the 'a' vs 'm' task is highly suspect. Since each EM iteration is guaranteed to not decrease the data likelihood, one would expect it to either improve upon or maintain the quality of the initial solution. This result may indicate a bug in the implementation, a flawed evaluation metric (e.g., accuracy vs. likelihood), or an issue with the EM stopping criterion. This anomaly undermines confidence in the experimental results and requires a thorough explanation.

*   **Technical Soundness and Practicality:**
    1.  A central assumption is that the spectrally learned transition matrix will be close to a permuted block-diagonal matrix. Under estimation noise, the estimated matrix will be dense. The paper does not adequately address the stability of the solution—it is possible that other sparse (or even dense) matrices are just as close to the estimated matrix under the Frobenius norm, which could lead the clustering step to fail. The paper needs to better characterize when the block-diagonal structure is stable enough to be recovered.
    2.  The method's practicality is questionable for MHMMs with a large number of components (K). The approach requires estimating a global HMM with MK states. The sample and computational complexity of spectral HMM learning scales with the total number of states. The paper should include a discussion of the method's limitations and clarify the regimes (in terms of M, K, and data availability) in which it is expected to be a viable alternative to EM.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:**
    1.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small to be legible, which hinders understanding of the experimental results.
    2.  There appears to be a minor typo in the proof of Lemma 2. In the final block matrix decomposition, the last diagonal element of the eigenvalue matrix is written as Λ₂ when it should be Λ_K.

### Summary of Review

This paper presents a very interesting and theoretically motivated approach for spectral learning of MHMMs. The core idea is elegant and the paper is clearly written. However, the work in its current form is undermined by significant weaknesses in the experimental validation. The experiments do not convincingly demonstrate that the method works under realistic conditions, and several results are either difficult to interpret due to missing details or are counter-intuitive and require explanation. Furthermore, important questions about the stability and practical scalability of the method remain unaddressed. While the theoretical contribution is solid, the lack of compelling empirical evidence for its practical performance makes the paper fall short of the standards for publication at NIPS. A major revision with a completely new set of rigorous, realistic, and reproducible experiments would be required to make a convincing case for this otherwise promising method.
