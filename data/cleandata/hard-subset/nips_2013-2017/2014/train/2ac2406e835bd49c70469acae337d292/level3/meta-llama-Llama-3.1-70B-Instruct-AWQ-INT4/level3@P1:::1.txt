The paper "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models" proposes a novel approach for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the Method of Moments (MoM). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on synthetic and real data.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of learning MHMMs and the limitations of existing approaches, such as the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors then present their proposed method, which involves learning an HMM using MoM and then resolving the permutation ambiguity to recover the block-diagonal mixture-of-HMMs structure.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, including the theoretical foundations and the algorithmic details. The experimental results are also presented in a clear and concise manner, although some details are missing, such as the specifics of the mixture HMM parameters used in Section 4.2.

One of the major concerns with the paper is the practicality of the approach for large, sparse mixtures. The authors should clarify when their method is appropriate and when it is not. Additionally, there is no guarantee that the spectral-learned transition matrix will be (nearly) sparse, and many full matrices may be close to the desired sparse block structure under noise.

Another concern is that the experiments corrupt permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise rather than sampling observed sequences from a mixture of HMMs. This may not reflect the real noise and limited-data effects that occur in practice. The authors should provide experiments that learn from observations to demonstrate the robustness of their approach.

The paper also lacks specifics about the mixture HMM parameters used in Section 4.2. Providing full parameter details, such as in an appendix, would be helpful. Furthermore, the authors should explain why EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone, as EM should typically improve the results.
