This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to formulate an MHMM as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. This insight allows the authors to apply the off-the-shelf spectral learning algorithm of Anandkumar et al. (2012). The main contribution is a novel stabilization method to resolve the resulting parameter permutation ambiguity, a key challenge in this approach. This method leverages the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix to identify and separate the parameters of the constituent HMMs, even in the presence of estimation noise.

The key strength of the paper is its proposed method for resolving the permutation ambiguity. The stabilization idea, which uses the limiting behavior of powers of the transition matrix to cluster hidden states, is intuitive and represents an elegant contribution to a difficult problem. This provides a direct, non-iterative alternative to EM for a complex model. The theoretical analysis, which accounts for estimation noise by analyzing the longevity of eigenvalues (notably Equation (7)), provides a principled way to determine the number of mixture components.

Despite the interesting core idea, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the challenging problem of learning MHMMs with a novel spectral approach.
*   The stabilization method for resolving parameter permutation is the main contribution and is an interesting and well-motivated idea.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Clarity:** The notation is often confusing and non-standard. The authors should explicitly define the dimensions of every matrix/vector upon introduction and consider adopting more common notation for HMMs (e.g., from Hsu et al., 2009) to improve readability. Explanations for key results, such as the block-diagonal structure in Corollary 1, need to be more transparent.
*   **Originality and Related Work:** The paper does not sufficiently engage with related literature. While presented as an alternative to EM, a major application of spectral methods is initializing EM. This line of work (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013) should be discussed more thoroughly to properly contextualize the contribution.
*   **Quality of Experiments:** The empirical validation is insufficient. The presentation is poor, with a hard-to-read plot (Figure 4) where a table would be much clearer. More importantly, the comparison with EM is unconvincing. EM's performance seems unusually low, suggesting a potentially un-tuned baseline. Reporting the best accuracy over multiple restarts for EM is standard practice and would provide a more robust comparison. The current experiments are too basic to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.

In summary, the paper introduces a clever technique for a challenging learning problem, but its potential is undermined by significant issues in clarity, positioning with respect to prior work, and weak experimental validation.
