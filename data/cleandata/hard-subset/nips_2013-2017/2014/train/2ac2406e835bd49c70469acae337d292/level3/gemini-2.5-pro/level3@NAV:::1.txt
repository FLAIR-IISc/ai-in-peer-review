This paper proposes a novel method for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using a spectral, moment-based approach. This work builds upon the recent line of research applying spectral methods to learn latent variable models, such as those by Hsu et al. and Anandkumar et al. The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The proposed algorithm first uses an existing spectral method to learn the parameters of this large HMM up to a permutation, and then introduces a novel second step to recover the block structure by analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix, thereby resolving the permutation ambiguity and identifying the component HMMs.

The main strengths of this paper are its originality and theoretical underpinnings. The central idea of recovering the block-diagonal structure from a permuted global transition matrix is elegant, and the theoretical justification is compelling. The paper is also well-written and clearly presents the proposed algorithm.

However, there are several significant weaknesses that temper my enthusiasm. My primary concerns relate to the practical applicability and experimental validation of the method.

First, the approach requires estimating a full, large (MK x MK) HMM as an intermediate step. This seems impractical for many interesting problems where the mixture is large and sparse (i.e., many components K, each with a small number of states M). The authors should clarify the computational and statistical trade-offs and define the regimes where this method is most appropriate.

Second, the de-permutation step critically relies on the estimated transition matrix being nearly sparse or block-diagonal. However, there is no guarantee this will be the case. Under estimation noise from finite data, many dense matrices might be close (in Frobenius norm) to the desired sparse structure, potentially making the clustering of columns unreliable.

Third, the experimental evaluation is not convincing. The synthetic experiments in Section 4.1 corrupt an already-permuted ground-truth matrix with Dirichlet noise rather than learning from observed sequences generated by a true MHMM. This setup does not adequately reflect the real-world challenges of estimation noise and limited-data effects. Furthermore, key details about the HMM parameters used in Section 4.2 are missing, hindering reproducibility. The unexplained result where EM initialized with the spectral method performs worse than the spectral method alone also needs to be addressed, as it contradicts the claim that this serves as a good initialization.

Minor points: The figures contain text that is too small to be legible, and there appears to be a typo in the proof of Lemma 2 (subscript of Λ should be K).

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution, its practical significance is unclear due to strong assumptions and an insufficient experimental evaluation. The work has potential, but requires a more realistic and thorough empirical study to validate its claims.
