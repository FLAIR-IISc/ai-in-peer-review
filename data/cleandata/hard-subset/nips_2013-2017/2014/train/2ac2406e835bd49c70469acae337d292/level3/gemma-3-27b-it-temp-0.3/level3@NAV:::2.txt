## Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a spectral algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by leveraging recent advances in spectral methods for HMM learning (Anandkumar et al., 2012). The core idea is to reformulate an MHMM as a special case of an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling the application of existing spectral techniques. A key contribution lies in addressing the parameter permutation ambiguity inherent in this approach through a stabilization method based on the spectral properties of the global transition matrix, specifically relying on the assumption of a single eigenvalue equal to 1 for each HMM’s transition matrix. The authors also provide an analysis incorporating estimation noise, utilizing eigenvalues in their algorithm (Equation 7).

The approach is intuitively appealing and addresses a significant computational bottleneck in MHMM learning – the iterative nature of the EM algorithm. The stabilization technique for resolving parameter permutation is a particularly clever and valuable contribution. However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The notation is often confusing, particularly in Corollary 1, and lacks sufficient clarity. For instance, the meaning of “1J^T” is not immediately apparent, and a more explicit description of the block-diagonal structure described in the limit (lim e→inf  Ā^e) is needed. Consistently defining the dimensions of all vectors and matrices upon their introduction would significantly improve readability. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough engagement with related work. While citing Anandkumar et al. (2012), it misses relevant contributions such as Chaganty and Liang (2013) which explores initializing EM with spectral estimates. The experimental evaluation is also weak. Figure 4 is difficult to interpret, and results should be presented in tabular form for clarity. The reported results raise questions – the unusually poor performance of EM warrants further investigation, including reporting the best (rather than average) EM accuracy and more comprehensive comparisons against other MHMM learning methods. Adopting established HMM notation (e.g., using ‘T’ for transitions, ‘n/m’ for observation/hidden state counts as suggested by Hsu et al., 2009) would enhance familiarity and understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel stabilization technique to address parameter permutation.
*   Potential for significant computational speedup compared to EM.
*   Interesting application of spectral methods to a challenging problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Confusing notation and lack of clarity.
*   Insufficient engagement with related work.
*   Weak and poorly presented experimental evaluation.
*   Unsubstantiated claims regarding EM performance.



**Overall:** The paper presents a promising algorithm, but its current form requires substantial revision to address the clarity, completeness, and experimental validation issues. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the reviewer comments. The core idea is strong, but the presentation and evaluation need significant improvement to meet the standards of NIPS.
