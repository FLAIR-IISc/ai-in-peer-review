This paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by recasting the MHMM as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, estimating this “global” HMM using spectral methods, and then resolving the resulting permutation ambiguity to recover each component HMM. The key technical idea is to exploit spectral structure: in the noiseless case, the global transition matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1 (one per component), and powers of the matrix reveal K rank-one blocks that allow re-permutation. In the noisy case, the authors propose a low-rank reconstruction and a “spectral longevity” criterion to identify K and to cluster columns for de-permutation. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwriting dataset show speed advantages over EM and suggest the spectral estimates can serve as an EM initializer.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on spectral algorithms for HMMs and latent variable models (Hsu et al.; Anandkumar et al.) and classical MHMM learning via EM (Smyth and follow-ups). The contribution lies in addressing the permutation ambiguity that arises when applying HMM spectral learning to the block-diagonal global HMM, and in proposing a criterion to select K. This situates well alongside spectral learning for mixtures (e.g., spectral experts for mixture regressions) but treats the sequential structure explicitly via block diagonalization.

Quality:
- Theory: The block-diagonal equivalence and noiseless de-permutation are clear and correct. However, the main finite-sample result (Conjecture 1) is left as a conjecture, and the key separability assumption (|λG_K| > max_k |λL_2,k| and each Ak having a unique eigenvalue at 1) is strong and not empirically stress-tested. The conditions under which spectral longevity reliably identifies K are only partially justified (Lemma 3) and depend on spectral gaps that may be small in realistic HMMs.
- Algorithms: The pipeline is well-specified at a high level, but crucial implementation details are missing: how to choose the exponentiation range e, how to set K-means initialization/number of restarts for column clustering, and how sensitive the method is to small eigen-gaps.
- Experiments: The experimental section is too limited to validate the practical reliability of the approach. In particular, one set of synthetic experiments corrupts permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise rather than learning from sequences generated by an MHMM; this does not reflect the real noise (finite-sample estimation from moments) nor label-switching/identifiability issues. Section 4.2 does use generated sequences, but it lacks key parameter details (transition matrices, emission means/variances, mixing proportions), which hinders reproducibility and interpretation. The real-data setting requires an artificial feature expansion solely to satisfy MK < L; the implications for applicability in low-dimensional settings are not discussed. Finally, EM sometimes performs worse when initialized with the spectral solution than the spectral solution alone; this is surprising and needs explanation (e.g., de-permutation mismatch, likelihood vs clustering metric, overfitting, or local maxima).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. However, several figures have text that is too small to read; please enlarge labels and axes.
- Some notation could be clarified (e.g., explicitly state the dimensionality requirement MK < L up front and reiterate its practical impact).
- Minor typo: in the proof around the block-diagonal eigendecomposition, a subscript of Λ appears incorrect (should be K rather than 2).

Originality:
- The observation that MHMMs correspond to block-diagonal HMMs is known, but leveraging the eigen-structure to undo permutation and introducing the spectral longevity heuristic for K selection constitute a novel and interesting combination. The paper is incremental relative to spectral HMM learning, but addresses a genuine gap (recovering individual components and K).

Significance:
- If robust, the approach could offer a scalable alternative to EM for MHMMs, with utility as either a standalone estimator or an initializer. However, practicality is limited by: (i) the need to estimate a full MK×MK transition operator and perform its eigendecomposition (O((MK)^3)), which may be prohibitive for large K or M; (ii) the requirement MK < L for certain moment constructions, which led to artificial feature expansion in the real-data experiment; and (iii) lack of guarantees that the estimated transition will be near block-diagonal under realistic noise. Clarifying the intended regime (e.g., small M, moderate K, sufficiently large L, many short sequences) would help.

Strengths:
- Clear statement of the permutation problem and an intuitive spectral remedy.
- Potentially significant computational savings over EM; useful as an initializer.
- Theoretical insight into spectral structure of the global chain and conditions under which K may be identified.

Weaknesses:
- Key claims rest on unproven conjectures and strong spectral assumptions.
- Experimental validation does not sufficiently reflect end-to-end learning from observed sequences, and important setup details are missing.
- Scalability concerns when MK is large; lack of guidance on when the method is appropriate.
- EM occasionally degrades performance relative to spectral initialization without explanation.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide full details of the synthetic MHMMs (all A_k, O_k, ν_k, π), and report sensitivity to eigen-gaps, mixing proportions, and state persistence.
- Replace or complement the “Dirichlet noise on permuted matrices” test with end-to-end learning from sequences, varying N, T, K, M, and L. Report K-estimation accuracy and de-permutation error rates.
- Quantify runtime as a function of N, T, K, M, L, and compare against EM (with/without accelerations).
- Explain cases where EM initialized by spectral underperforms spectral alone; verify that de-permutation and labeling are consistent between the initializer and EM objective; consider constraining EM to preserve block structure initially.
- Discuss regimes of applicability and failure (e.g., near-degenerate eigenvalues, non-ergodic or slowly mixing chains, unequal component sizes).
- Consider alternative de-permutation strategies (e.g., spectral clustering on a symmetrized transition, stationary-distribution–weighted embeddings) and report robustness.

Pros vs cons for acceptance:
- Pros: Interesting idea with clear motivation; addresses a practical obstacle for spectral MHMM learning; potentially impactful as a fast initializer; generally clear exposition.
- Cons: Insufficient theoretical guarantees; limited and partially non-representative experiments; scalability and applicability not convincingly demonstrated; missing details hinder reproducibility.

Overall: A promising and timely contribution with neat spectral insights, but additional end-to-end experiments, clearer guidance on applicability, and stronger empirical/analytical support are needed to fully establish practical value. I lean toward weak reject unless the authors can substantially strengthen the experimental section and clarify the scope and limitations.
