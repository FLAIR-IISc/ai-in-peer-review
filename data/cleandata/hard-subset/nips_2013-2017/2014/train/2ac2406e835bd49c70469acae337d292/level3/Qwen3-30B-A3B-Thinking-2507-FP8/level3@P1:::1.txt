

The paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by leveraging the method of moments (MoM) and resolving permutation ambiguity through spectral properties of the global transition matrix. This builds on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral HMM work (NIPS 2012) but extends it to handle the block-diagonal structure of MHMMs. The core insight (Lemma 1) treats MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling MoM-based parameter estimation. The key innovation lies in using eigenvalue analysis (Lemma 2, Conjecture 1) to recover the depermutation mapping needed to isolate individual HMM components, with Lemma 3 providing a spectral criterion for cluster count estimation. The approach theoretically avoids EM's computational bottlenecks by decoupling learning complexity from data size.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical contribution is strong and novel. The resolution of permutation ambiguity via spectral properties is elegant and addresses a fundamental limitation in MHMM learning. The connection to existing spectral methods (Anandkumar et al., Hsu et al.) is well-established, and the paper correctly identifies EM's computational burden for large datasets as a key motivation. The algorithm design (Algorithm 1) is logically sound, and the proof sketches (e.g., Lemma 2) are technically rigorous. The theoretical analysis of noise effects (Conjecture 1) provides a reasonable foundation for practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Major experimental flaws undermine significance and quality.* The experiments (Section 4) critically fail to reflect real-world data generation. Section 4.1 corrupts *permuted matrices* with Dirichlet noise instead of sampling sequences from an MHMM, violating the paper's premise. This misrepresents "noise" as a matrix corruption rather than observational noise from a mixture process, making results uninterpretable for real applications. Section 4.2 lacks essential parameter details (e.g., transition matrices, emission means) for reproducibility (Major 5), and the comparison to EM uses insufficiently specified hyperparameters (e.g., restarts, stopping criteria). Crucially, Section 4.3’s real-data experiment (UCI characters) suffers from a technical flaw: the cubic polynomial transformation (to satisfy $MK < L$) is unexplained and potentially invalidates the comparison, as MoM requires $MK < L$ but the original data has $L=3$. The spectral method’s performance is inconsistent with EM (Table 1), and the paper fails to explain why EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes *worsens* accuracy (Major 6), contradicting expectations.

*Practical applicability is questionable.* The method assumes the global transition matrix is nearly block-diagonal (Major 3), but the paper provides no guarantee that spectral learning yields such matrices under noise—many non-block matrices could be near the true structure, leading to incorrect depermutation. The authors claim computational advantages but omit critical context: MoM for full HMMs requires $MK$ states, making the approach impractical for large $K$ or $M$ (e.g., $K=5, M=3$ yields $MK=15$, but Section 4.2 uses $K=5, M=3$, and $L=20$; $MK=15 < L=20$ barely holds). The paper lacks analysis of when this method is *appropriate* versus when it fails (Major 2), such as for sparse mixtures or high $MK$, which is essential for significance.

*Minor issues:* A typo in Lemma 2’s proof (eigenvalue index $K$ vs. $2$) and unreadable figures (Major 2) reduce clarity. The real-data results (Table 1) are underwhelming—spectral learning rarely outperforms random EM initialization, and the "better initialization" claim lacks statistical evidence.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to MHMM learning, but experimental weaknesses severely limit its impact. The core theory is sound, yet the experiments fail to validate the approach under realistic conditions, contradicting the claimed advantages. The lack of parameter details, unexplained EM initialization behavior, and unaddressed practical constraints (e.g., $MK < L$) undermine the significance claim. While the method could be useful for small, dense mixtures where $MK \ll L$, the paper provides no guidance on this regime. Without realistic experiments demonstrating robustness to observational noise and clear boundaries for applicability, the work does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS. The theoretical novelty is offset by practical shortcomings that prevent meaningful adoption.

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Elegant solution to permutation ambiguity; strong theoretical foundation; clear connection to spectral learning literature.  
*Con:* Experiments misrepresent noise and data generation; no analysis of method’s practical limits; inconsistent results with EM; critical parameter details omitted; real-data application lacks justification.
