

# Review for "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

## Summary
This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The authors observe that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. The key contribution is resolving the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM-based HMM learning to recover the individual HMM components in the mixture. This builds on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning for HMMs [1,2] and extends it to the MHMM setting, which has been previously challenging due to computational complexity of EM-based approaches.

## Strengths
The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations with well-constructed lemmas (Lemma 1-3) that justify the approach. The connection between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is elegant and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear algorithmic framework for resolving permutation ambiguity using spectral properties of the global transition matrix, which is a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis of the spectral conditions for estimating the number of clusters (K) is particularly noteworthy. The paper is well-written with clear mathematical notation and logical flow, making the technical content accessible.

## Weaknesses
While the theoretical framework is strong, the experimental section has several significant limitations that undermine the paper's claims about practical utility:

1. The experiments (Section 4) do not properly reflect real-world learning from observations. Instead, the authors corrupt permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise rather than sampling from the actual mixture model, failing to capture the true noise and data limitations inherent in learning from observations.

2. The paper lacks sufficient detail about experimental parameters. For instance, Section 4.2 fails to provide specific details about the transition and observation matrices used in the Gaussian emission MHMM, which are crucial for reproducibility and evaluating the method's scope.

3. There is a lack of clarity on when this method is appropriate versus when it is not. The paper does not address whether the method works well for large, sparse mixtures (as noted in key point 2), which is a critical practical consideration.

4. The real data experiment (Section 4.3) shows that spectral learning sometimes performs worse than EM initialized with random parameters, contradicting the expectation that EM should improve upon the spectral initialization. The authors do not provide an explanation for this counterintuitive result.

5. The paper does not guarantee that the estimated transition matrix will be nearly sparse, which is essential for practical applications (key point 3). This is a significant gap in the practical applicability of the method.

## Quality
The theoretical quality of the paper is high, with well-justified lemmas and a clear algorithm. However, the experimental quality is compromised by the issues noted above. The paper would benefit from more realistic experiments that learn from observed sequences rather than artificially corrupted matrices.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation. However, the figures have text and numbers that are too small to read (minor issue), and the explanation of the depermutation algorithm could be enhanced with more intuitive examples.

## Originality
The paper makes an important contribution by extending spectral learning to MHMMs and resolving the permutation ambiguity. This is a novel combination of familiar techniques that addresses a significant gap in the literature. The approach differs from previous work on MHMMs [4,5,6,7] by avoiding the computational burden of EM.

## Significance
The potential significance of this work is high, as it could provide a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMM learning. However, the significance is currently limited by the lack of convincing experimental results on real data and the absence of clear boundaries for when the method is applicable. The paper's claim that it "can be used as a good initialization scheme for EM" is not sufficiently supported by the experiments.

## Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with novel contribution to resolving permutation ambiguity in MHMM learning
- Builds on and extends existing spectral learning literature in a meaningful way
- Well-written with clear mathematical presentation
- Demonstrates computational advantages over EM in synthetic experiments

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Experimental section lacks realistic evaluation from observed sequences
- Insufficient detail about experimental parameters and settings
- No clear characterization of when the method is applicable versus when it fails
- Real data experiment shows inconsistent performance compared to EM
- Does not address the sparsity requirement of the transition matrix

## Recommendation
The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to MHMM learning that could be valuable for specific applications. However, the current experimental evaluation does not adequately demonstrate the practical utility of the method, and the lack of clarity about the method's applicability boundaries significantly weakens the contribution. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with substantial revisions addressing the experimental limitations and providing more detailed parameter specifications, this could become a strong NIPS contribution. The authors should focus on creating realistic experiments that learn from actual observations rather than artificially corrupted matrices, and provide clear guidance on when this approach should be used versus other methods.
