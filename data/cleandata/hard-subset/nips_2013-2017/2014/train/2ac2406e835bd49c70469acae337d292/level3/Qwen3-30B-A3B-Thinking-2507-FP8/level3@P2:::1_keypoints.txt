- Summary: Proposes a spectral Method-of-Moments approach to learn Mixtures of HMMs by viewing an MHMM as a single HMM with block-diagonal transitions and resolving permutation ambiguity to recover components.

- Strength — theory: Strong theoretical foundation with well-constructed lemmas justifying the approach.

- Strength — modeling insight: Elegant, well-motivated connection between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices.

- Strength — algorithmic contribution: Clear algorithm to resolve permutation ambiguity via spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

- Strength — cluster estimation: Theoretical analysis of spectral conditions for estimating the number of clusters (K) is notable.

- Strength — presentation: Well-written with clear notation and logical flow, making technical content accessible.

- Weakness — experiment realism: Experiments corrupt permuted matrices with Dirichlet noise instead of sampling from the mixture model, failing to reflect real observation-based learning.

- Weakness — missing experimental detail: Insufficient details about experimental parameters (e.g., transition and observation matrices in Gaussian MHMM) hinder reproducibility.

- Weakness — applicability unclear: No clear guidance on when the method is appropriate, particularly for large, sparse mixtures.

- Weakness — real data results: Real-data experiment sometimes shows spectral learning worse than random-initialized EM, with no explanation for this counterintuitive result.

- Weakness — sparsity guarantee: No guarantee that the estimated transition matrix will be nearly sparse, which undermines practical utility.

- Quality: Theoretical quality is high but experimental quality is compromised by the issues above; more realistic experiments are needed.

- Clarity: Generally clear and organized, but figures are hard to read and the depermutation algorithm needs more intuitive examples.

- Originality: Novel extension of spectral learning to MHMMs that resolves permutation ambiguity and avoids EM’s computational burden.

- Significance: Potentially high computational alternative to EM, but current impact limited by unconvincing experiments and unclear applicability boundaries.

- Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, meaningful extension of spectral learning, clear presentation, and computational advantages in synthetic settings.

- Arguments against acceptance: Unrealistic experimental evaluation, missing parameter details, unclear failure modes, inconsistent real-data performance, and unaddressed sparsity requirement.

- Recommendation: Reject in current form but encourage resubmission after substantial revisions—run realistic observation-based experiments, provide detailed parameters, and clarify applicability and sparsity behavior.
