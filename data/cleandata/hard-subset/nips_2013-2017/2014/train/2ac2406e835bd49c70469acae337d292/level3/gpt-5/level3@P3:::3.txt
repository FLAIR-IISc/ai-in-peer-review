Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies learning mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs) via the method of moments (MoM). The core observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block diagonal, with one block per component. The authors apply a spectral/MoM HMM learner (essentially Hsu, Kakade & Zhang, JCSS 2012) to this “global” HMM. As with all spectral methods, the recovered states are arbitrarily permuted; here, that permutation mixes states across components, so the blocks are not aligned and the component-specific parameters cannot be read off directly. The main technical contribution is a de-permutation procedure that uses spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix: in the noiseless case there are K eigenvalues equal to one (one per component), and powers of the transition matrix reveal K rank-one stationary blocks; with noise, the authors propose a rank-K reconstruction via the leading eigenvalues, a “spectral longevity” score to estimate K, and then cluster the columns of the rank-K reconstruction to infer the permutation. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI trajectory dataset show the approach can de-permute reasonably well and offers speed advantages over EM, while also serving as a good EM initializer.

The work builds on a substantial literature on spectral learning for latent variable models (e.g., Hsu–Kakade–Zhang for HMMs; Anandkumar et al. on tensor methods). The paper would benefit from a tighter comparison to related efforts that face and address permutation/alignment issues in mixtures and multi-view models, such as Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013) for mixtures of linear regressions and other spectral initialization/alignment strategies.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction from MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is standard and correct, and the permutation ambiguity is a real impediment in practice. The noiseless de-permutation argument is clear. However, the noisy case relies on a conjecture and heuristic conditions (e.g., global vs local eigenvalue separation and the “spectral longevity” criterion). There is no perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) to justify that the top-K invariant subspace isolates component structure or that k-means on the reconstructed columns recovers the true grouping with high probability. Lemma 3’s “longevity” argument is intuitive but informal.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments show the de-permutation working across a range of sequence lengths; speed/accuracy comparisons to EM are helpful. The real-data experiment suggests the method is a useful initializer. Nonetheless, the evaluation is limited in scope (small K, M; only Gaussian emissions), lacks strong baseline variants (e.g., EM with k-means or spectral initializations that do not rely on the proposed de-permutation), and does not quantify de-permutation accuracy directly (e.g., block recovery rates, permutation error).
- Assumptions and practicality: The requirement MK < L for the spectral HMM estimator is acknowledged but sidestepped on the real dataset via a polynomial feature expansion; this choice should be discussed more critically, since it changes the observation model and may impact HMM assumptions.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and the high-level algorithm is easy to follow. However, there are occasional typos and proofreading issues (e.g., “For each e, identify of eigenvalues...”), inconsistent notation (switching ĀP to A for brevity mid-proof), and some overloaded terms (“global” vs “local” eigenvalues defined via ad hoc α, β noise factors). Algorithm 1 could be more precise about the range for e, how longevity is computed in practice, and the exact clustering features used. A brief complexity discussion separating moment computation vs eigendecomposition/clustering would help.

Originality
- The learning algorithm up to permutation is largely a straightforward application of Hsu–Kakade–Zhang to the block-diagonal HMM view; that part is not new. The novelty lies in the de-permutation/alignment procedure and the heuristic K-estimation via spectral longevity. The idea of using powers of a Markov operator and the multiplicity-1 unit eigenvalues is classical; adapting it to align MHMM blocks under noise is a modest but useful contribution. The related-work discussion should be expanded to clearly situate what is new relative to prior spectral methods that confront permutation/alignment (e.g., in mixtures of regressions, topic models, multi-view mixtures), and to MHMM learning baselines.

Significance
- If robust, a practical de-permutation step makes spectral MHMM learning far more useful, offering scalability and initialization for EM. The current theoretical underpinnings are not strong enough to claim broad reliability, and the empirical study is narrow, so the impact is likely moderate unless strengthened. Still, addressing the alignment problem is an important and under-discussed aspect of making spectral outputs actionable in mixtures of sequential models.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Theory: Replace Conjecture 1 with a perturbation bound showing when the top-K invariant subspace of the estimated transition matrix preserves block structure; formalize guarantees for the column-clustering step. Analyze failure modes when components have multiple slow eigenmodes close to 1 or when spectral gaps shrink.
- K estimation: Provide a more principled criterion (e.g., eigengap statistics with confidence bounds) and an ablation comparing longevity vs alternatives.
- Empirics: Report de-permutation accuracy (e.g., adjusted Rand index on state-to-block assignments), sensitivity to K, K̂ estimation accuracy, robustness to mixing rates and emission overlap, and results for discrete/multinomial emissions. Compare to EM with several initializations (random, k-means on sequence summaries, spectral without de-permutation), and to alternate alignment heuristics (e.g., Hungarian matching using emission similarities).
- Practicalities: Discuss the MK < L requirement and the implications of feature expansions on HMM likelihood modeling.
- Related work: Expand discussion around alignment in spectral methods (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2013) and any prior MHMM-specific approaches.
- Proofreading: Fix typos and polish notation consistency.

Pros
- Addresses a key practical issue (de-permutation/alignment) that often blocks use of spectral methods for mixtures of HMMs.
- Simple, computationally attractive pipeline; can serve as an effective initializer for EM.
- Clear intuition in the noiseless case; empirical indications that the heuristic works reasonably.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond HKZ; main new idea is a relatively straightforward de-permutation heuristic.
- Theoretical guarantees are weak (central claims left as conjecture/heuristic).
- Evaluation is narrow, lacks strong baselines and detailed diagnostics, and relies on feature expansion to meet identifiability conditions.
- Related work coverage and writing quality need improvement.

Recommendation
Borderline. The paper tackles an important practical gap and provides a simple approach that seems to work, but the novelty is modest and the theory/empirics need strengthening. With tighter analysis, broader experiments, and improved exposition, this could be a solid contribution.
