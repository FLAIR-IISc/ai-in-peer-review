

This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing the computational limitations of EM-based approaches. The core insight is recognizing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices, enabling application of spectral learning methods. The authors then tackle the critical permutation ambiguity in the spectral estimates via a de-permutation algorithm based on spectral properties of the global transition matrix, validated through synthetic and real-data experiments.

**Strengths**  
The paper makes a clear technical contribution by solving a well-known practical problem: the permutation ambiguity that prevents direct application of spectral methods to MHMMs. The de-permutation algorithm—using eigenvalue clustering and spectral longevity (Lemma 3)—is novel for MHMMs and effectively recovers component identities even with noisy estimates. The computational advantage over EM is significant (Fig. 4), with the spectral method achieving comparable accuracy while scaling linearly with sequence count (vs. EM’s quadratic cost). Real-data validation on handwritten character trajectories (Table 1) demonstrates practical utility, particularly as an initialization scheme for EM. The experimental design is thorough, covering noise sensitivity (Fig. 3), data quantity vs. accuracy (Fig. 4), and real-world applicability.

**Weaknesses**  
The paper’s originality is overstated relative to prior work. The spectral learning framework is directly adapted from Hsu et al. (2012) and Anandkumar et al. (2012), with the key innovation being the de-permutation step. However, the paper fails to adequately situate this contribution: Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013) solved analogous de-permutation challenges for mixtures of linear regressions, and the MHMM structure bears similarity to their framework. A clear comparison to this work is missing, weakening the novelty claim. The theoretical justification for the de-permutation algorithm (Conjecture 1) is incomplete; the error bound derivation assumes sample complexity from a different model (mixture of Gaussians), and the justification lacks rigor. Minor proofreading issues (e.g., "For each e, identify of eigenvalues" in Algorithm 1) distract from the technical content.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance**  
- *Quality*: The experimental results are strong and well-justified, but the theoretical claims require strengthening. The method is sound, though the de-permutation relies on spectral conditions (e.g., |λ_GK| > max_k |λ_L2,k|) that may not hold in practice.  
- *Clarity*: The paper is generally well-structured, but the related work section is inadequate. The connection to Chaganty & Liang (2013) should be explicit. Typos and minor notation inconsistencies (e.g., "π1ν1" vs. "π_k ν_k" in Section 3.2) reduce readability.  
- *Originality*: The de-permutation mechanism is the paper’s strongest contribution, but it is framed as a "new method" rather than a specialized solution to an existing gap. The core spectral learning approach is not novel.  
- *Significance*: The work addresses a practical bottleneck (EM’s scalability for large sequence datasets), making it valuable for applications like time-series clustering. The initialization utility for EM (Table 1) is a tangible contribution.

**Recommendation**  
This paper presents a solid, practical solution to a real problem in sequence modeling. While the core spectral framework is inherited, the de-permutation algorithm is a meaningful contribution that enables MHMM learning at scale. However, the lack of engagement with Chaganty & Liang (2013) and the theoretical gaps prevent it from being a strong *original* contribution. With revisions to clarify the novelty relative to prior work (especially ICML 2013) and strengthen the theoretical justification, this would be a compelling NIPS paper. As-is, it is a **borderline accept**—the technical execution is strong, but the novelty argument needs refinement.  

**Pro vs. Con**  
*Pro*: Effective solution to permutation ambiguity; strong empirical validation; clear computational advantage over EM.  
*Con*: Overstated originality; missing comparison to Chaganty & Liang (2013); theoretical gaps; minor proofreading errors.
