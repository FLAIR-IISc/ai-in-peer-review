

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by recognizing MHMMs as a special case of HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices. The authors build upon Anandkumar et al. (2012) for spectral HMM learning and introduce a novel stabilization method to resolve parameter permutation ambiguity, leveraging spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The key theoretical contribution is the analysis of how to recover individual HMM parameters from the permuted global estimates, particularly through the spectral longevity measure in Equation (7).

The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its theoretical development, with interesting insights about spectral properties (Lemma 2, Corollary 1). The stabilization idea to address parameter permutation is indeed intuitive and represents a meaningful contribution. However, the experimental evaluation is limited: Figure 4 is poorly presented, results should be tabulated, and the comparison with EM is incomplete (only average EM performance is reported, not best-case). The authors should cite relevant prior work on spectral initialization for EM (Chaganty and Liang 2013).

Several clarity issues need addressing: the notation is inconsistent (e.g., 1J^T in Corollary 1), matrix dimensions are rarely defined upon introduction, and the block-diagonal structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e requires clearer exposition. The paper would benefit from conforming to standard HMM notation (e.g., using T for transitions rather than A).

While the core algorithm combines existing spectral HMM learning with a novel stabilization step, the significance is somewhat limited by modest experimental results and insufficient engagement with related work. The computational advantage over EM is promising, but the paper doesn't demonstrate sufficient superiority to justify adoption in practice. The real data experiment shows spectral learning can serve as a good EM initialization, but this potential isn't fully explored.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound approach with an interesting theoretical contribution to resolving permutation ambiguity, but suffers from unclear presentation, limited experimental validation, and insufficient context within the literature. The core contribution is incremental rather than transformative, though the stabilization method is noteworthy.
