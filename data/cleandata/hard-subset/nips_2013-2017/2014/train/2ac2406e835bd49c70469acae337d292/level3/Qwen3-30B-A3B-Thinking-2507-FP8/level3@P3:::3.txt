

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using spectral techniques. The authors recognize that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. The main contribution is addressing the permutation ambiguity that arises when recovering the block structure from spectral learning, which is a key challenge for MHMMs. The authors propose a de-permutation algorithm based on spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

The paper builds on the foundational work of Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012) for spectral learning of HMMs, but extends it to MHMMs. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently acknowledge that their approach is largely a trivial extension of existing HMM spectral learning methods. The core MHMM learning procedure essentially reduces to applying Hsu et al.'s algorithm to the block-diagonal transition matrix, with the novel contribution being the de-permutation step. This is a significant oversight, particularly since Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013) already addressed similar permutation issues for mixture models, which should have been properly situated in the related work.

The paper's main strength is the de-permutation algorithm, which appears to work reasonably well in practice. The authors provide a theoretical justification (Conjecture 1) for why the spectral properties of the global transition matrix can be used to recover the block structure, though the proof is incomplete and relies on results from other papers without sufficient justification. The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate that the method can recover the correct number of clusters and block-diagonal structure, even with limited data. The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories shows the method can serve as a good initialization for EM, which is a practical contribution.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The theoretical analysis is incomplete, with key claims presented as conjectures rather than proven results. The paper contains several proofreading issues (e.g., "For each e, identify of eigenvalues..."), which suggest insufficient care in preparing the manuscript. The related work section is inadequate, failing to properly situate the paper in the context of existing mixture model learning approaches. The significance of the work is somewhat limited because the computational advantage over EM is only meaningful for very large datasets, and the accuracy gains are modest compared to EM with good initialization.

Regarding the four review criteria:

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach to a non-trivial problem, but the theoretical foundation is incomplete. The experimental results support the claims, though the comparison to EM is limited to a single real dataset. The paper would be stronger with more comprehensive theoretical analysis.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written, but contains several typos and unclear notations (e.g., the notation for the global transition matrix in Section 3.2). The de-permutation algorithm is clearly described, but the theoretical justification could be more rigorous.

**Originality**: The core MHMM learning procedure is not novel—it's a straightforward application of existing HMM spectral learning. The de-permutation algorithm is the main novel contribution, but its novelty isn't sufficiently established in the context of existing mixture model learning literature.

**Significance**: The method offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM for large-scale MHMM learning, which could be valuable for applications with massive sequence datasets. The practical demonstration of the method as an initialization for EM is a meaningful contribution, though the paper doesn't fully explore the computational savings.

Arguments for acceptance: The de-permutation algorithm addresses a genuine problem in spectral learning for MHMMs, and the method is computationally efficient with reasonable accuracy. The real data experiment demonstrates practical utility.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper fails to properly situate itself in related work, presents incomplete theoretical analysis, and the core contribution is largely a trivial extension of existing methods. The proofreading issues and theoretical gaps undermine the paper's scientific contribution.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses a genuine problem and presents a practically useful algorithm, it fails to sufficiently establish its novelty or provide a rigorous theoretical foundation. The authors should substantially revise the paper to better position their work in relation to Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013), provide more rigorous theoretical analysis, and carefully proofread the manuscript. The current version does not make a sufficiently strong scientific contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
