This paper presents a method-of-moments approach for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The main idea is to re-frame the MHMM as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. This insight allows the authors to apply existing spectral HMM learning algorithms, such as the one by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012), to learn the global model parameters. The central technical challenge addressed is the permutation ambiguity inherent in the spectral output, which arbitrarily shuffles the latent states and mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The key contribution is a novel de-permutation algorithm that leverages the spectral properties of the learned transition matrix to cluster the permuted states, thereby recovering the individual component HMMs.

**Strengths:**
- **Significance:** The paper tackles a crucial and practical bottleneck in applying spectral methods to mixture models. The problem of how to de-permute or align the estimated spectral factors to the true underlying mixture components is a significant hurdle, and this work proposes a direct and compelling solution for the MHMM case.
- **Originality:** While the initial learning step is a direct application of prior work, the proposed de-permutation algorithm is novel and elegant. The idea of using the limiting behavior of the powered-up transition matrix to reveal its block structure and cluster the states is clever and well-motivated.
- **Quality:** The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method is not only much faster than traditional EM but also that the de-permutation algorithm works effectively in practice, even with moderate noise.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity and Originality:** The paper's framing could be improved. The application of the spectral HMM algorithm is presented as a main component but is a relatively trivial variant of existing work. The novelty lies almost exclusively in the de-permutation procedure, and the manuscript would be stronger if it focused more explicitly on this as its primary contribution.
- **Related Work:** The paper needs to better situate itself within the broader literature on aligning spectral outputs. A critical omission is the work by Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013), which addresses a very similar alignment problem in the context of mixtures of linear regressions. A discussion of this and related "spectral experts" literature is necessary to properly contextualize the paper's novelty.
- **Clarity and Quality:** The manuscript suffers from occasional typos and awkward phrasing that detract from its readability and requires a careful proofreading pass. Some technical arguments, such as the justification for Conjecture 1, are presented as high-level sketches and would benefit from more rigorous detail.
