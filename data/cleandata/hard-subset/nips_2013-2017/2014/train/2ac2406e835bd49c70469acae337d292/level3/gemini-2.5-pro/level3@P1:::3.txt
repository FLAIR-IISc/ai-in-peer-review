This paper presents a method of moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a total of MK hidden states. This larger HMM possesses a special block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block corresponds to one of the component HMMs. The authors then apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs, such as the one by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012), to learn the parameters of this global HMM.

A key challenge with this approach, which the authors correctly identify, is that the output of the spectral learning algorithm is only identifiable up to a permutation of the hidden states. This "permutation ambiguity" mixes the states from the K different components, making it impossible to recover the parameters of the individual HMMs directly. The main contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm to resolve this ambiguity. The proposed "de-permutation" method leverages the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. In the noiseless case, the K largest eigenvalues of this matrix are all 1, corresponding to the stationary distributions of the K component HMMs. By performing a low-rank reconstruction using only the eigenvectors associated with these top K eigenvalues, the authors show that the resulting matrix has a block structure. They propose clustering the columns of this matrix to identify the state groupings that correspond to the original HMMs, thereby recovering the permutation. The paper also provides a heuristic for estimating the number of components K from the data based on the spectral gaps of the estimated transition matrix. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic and real-world handwritten character data, showing significant speed advantages over traditional EM.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial and non-trivial problem. The permutation or component alignment issue is a fundamental bottleneck in applying spectral methods to many mixture models. Providing a principled way to resolve this for MHMMs is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Elegant and Intuitive Solution:** The proposed de-permutation algorithm is clever and intuitive. Using the spectral properties of the transition matrix and clustering the columns of its low-rank reconstruction is an elegant way to recover the block structure and thus the component HMMs.
3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for using a spectral method is computational efficiency, and the paper successfully demonstrates this. By avoiding the iterative nature of EM, which requires passes over the entire dataset at each step, the proposed method offers a much faster alternative, particularly for large datasets. This makes learning MHMMs more practical.
4.  **Practical Utility:** The experiments show that the method is not only fast but also provides good results. Furthermore, the real-data experiment highlights its utility as a high-quality initialization for EM, which can help EM find better local optima and converge faster. This dual use as both a standalone algorithm and an initializer increases its potential impact.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Positioning and Related Work:** The paper positions its main novelty in the de-permutation algorithm, which is appropriate. However, it understates that the first part of the method—applying spectral HMM learning to a global model—is a relatively straightforward application of existing work. More importantly, the paper fails to situate its core contribution within the broader context of recent work on component alignment for spectral learning. For instance, Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013, "Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions") address a very similar problem of identifying components after a spectral estimation step. A discussion comparing and contrasting the proposed technique to such related alignment methods is missing and would significantly strengthen the paper.
2.  **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical support for the de-permutation algorithm in the noisy case is weak. Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank reconstruction, is presented without proof and relies on reusing a bound from a related but different model. The justification provided is hand-wavy. Similarly, the method for estimating the number of clusters K (Lemma 3) is based on a heuristic about spectral gaps and "spectral longevity." While intuitively appealing, the paper lacks a formal analysis of the conditions under which these methods are guaranteed to succeed.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper could benefit from a careful proofreading pass. There are occasional typos and grammatical errors that detract from the overall clarity. The exposition is generally clear, but tightening the language and fixing these minor issues would improve the manuscript's quality.

### Summary of Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound for the most part, and the experimental results appear to support the claims of speed and comparable accuracy. However, the theoretical contributions (Conjecture 1, Lemma 3) are not rigorously proven, which makes this feel more like a strong workshop paper than a complete piece of work. The core claims about the algorithm's performance are backed by experiments, but the underlying theory needs to be solidified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core idea of framing the MHMM as a large HMM and then solving the resulting permutation problem is communicated effectively. As noted, the manuscript would benefit from a final round of proofreading to eliminate minor errors.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution—the spectral de-permutation algorithm for MHMMs—is novel and interesting. However, the originality is somewhat diminished by the lack of engagement with related work on component alignment in other spectral mixture model settings. The initial application of spectral HMM learning is not novel.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant. The problem of efficiently learning MHMMs is important in many application areas, and the proposed method provides a practical, fast, and effective solution. Its potential use as an initializer for EM further increases its significance. Researchers and practitioners working with sequence clustering are likely to find these ideas useful and may build upon them.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable and practical contribution by developing a fast spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs, with a clever solution to the critical component alignment problem. While the theoretical analysis could be stronger and the relation to prior work on alignment should be clarified, the proposed method is well-motivated and empirically effective.
