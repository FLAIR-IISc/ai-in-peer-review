

The paper introduces a spectral method for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (HMMs), building upon the Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang framework. While the core algorithm constitutes a straightforward adaptation of the standard approach, the authors acknowledge a key limitation: the recovered transition matrix is an arbitrary permutation of the true one, thereby conflating component identities. The proposed de-permutation procedure addresses this issue and appears empirically effective, though its theoretical guarantees and robustness under noisy conditions warrant further discussion.

The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and minor typographical issues that detract from clarity. For instance, the phrase "For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy" should read "For each \( e \), identify the eigenvalues containing all the energy." Such errors suggest insufficient proofreading; careful revision is strongly recommended before publication.

The paper would benefit significantly from stronger contextualization within the broader literature on spectral methods for mixture models. Specifically, it should explicitly connect to Chaganty and Liang's work on spectral experts for mixtures of linear regressions (ICML 2013), which shares conceptual similarities in leveraging spectral decomposition for component identification. This comparison would clarify the paper's novelty and position it relative to established techniques for mixture learning. The authors should also discuss how their de-permutation step relates to similar challenges in other mixture models (e.g., Gaussian mixtures or topic models), explaining why the HMM setting necessitates a distinct approach.

The primary technical contribution—handling the permutation ambiguity—requires more rigorous justification. The paper states the de-permutation algorithm "works reasonably well," but provides no theoretical analysis of its success conditions or failure modes. How does the algorithm scale with the number of components \( K \), the number of states \( d \), or the signal-to-noise ratio in the data? Empirical results should include ablation studies demonstrating the necessity of the de-permutation step (e.g., by comparing performance with and without it) and robustness analyses across varying data conditions. The current experiments lack sufficient detail on these aspects.

Additionally, the paper does not sufficiently clarify the computational complexity of the de-permutation step relative to the core spectral algorithm. Given that the main algorithm is already computationally intensive, the added cost of de-permutation must be justified, especially for large-scale applications. A brief complexity analysis would strengthen the practical contribution.

The writing occasionally lacks precision. For example, the phrase "the transition matrix recovered is an arbitrary permutation of the true one" is ambiguous: does this mean the matrix is a permutation of the true transition matrix (i.e., the rows/columns are reordered), or that the algorithm recovers a transition matrix whose structure is a permutation of the true one? This distinction is critical for understanding the problem. The authors should explicitly define the permutation model (e.g., "the algorithm recovers a transition matrix \( \Pi T \Pi^\top \) for some unknown permutation \( \Pi \)").

The paper also omits a clear statement of the problem's motivation. Why is learning mixtures of HMMs particularly challenging or important compared to other mixture models? A concise paragraph on applications (e.g., multi-sensor data analysis, speech processing with multiple speakers) would better justify the work's significance.

In summary, while the core idea is plausible and the de-permutation step addresses a genuine technical hurdle, the paper requires substantial revision to meet NIPS standards. Key improvements needed include: (1) rigorous proofreading to eliminate grammatical errors and typos; (2) deeper contextualization with related work (especially Chaganty & Liang); (3) theoretical and empirical analysis of the de-permutation step; (4) clearer problem formulation and motivation; and (5) more precise technical descriptions. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the literature on spectral mixture learning. As currently written, it lacks sufficient novelty and technical depth for acceptance.
