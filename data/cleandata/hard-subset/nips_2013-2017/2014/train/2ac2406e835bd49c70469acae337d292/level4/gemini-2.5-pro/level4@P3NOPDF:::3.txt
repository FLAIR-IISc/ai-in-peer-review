This paper presents a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The proposed algorithm builds upon the well-established spectral method of Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang for learning a single HMM. The central technical challenge arises from the fact that a direct application of this method to a mixture model yields a permuted transition matrix, where state transitions from different component HMMs are conflated. The main contribution of this work is a novel "de-permutation" algorithm designed to disentangle these components and recover the individual transition matrices. The authors provide empirical evidence suggesting their method performs effectively.

**Strengths:**

The problem of learning mixtures of HMMs is significant and has numerous applications. Tackling this with spectral methods is a promising direction, offering potential advantages in terms of computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees over traditional EM-based approaches. The identification of the permutation ambiguity as the key bottleneck for applying spectral methods in this context is insightful, and the proposed de-permutation algorithm appears to be a reasonable and novel contribution to address this specific challenge.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Contextualization and Related Work:** The paper would be substantially strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its relationship to prior work. The current draft does not sufficiently place the contribution in the context of the broader literature on spectral learning for mixture models. While the algorithm is presented as a variant of the Hsu, Kakade & Zhang (HKZ) method, the core innovation lies in the post-processing (de-permutation) step. It is crucial to discuss how this disentanglement problem has been addressed in other mixture model settings.

    For instance, the work of Chaganty and Liang ("Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions," ICML 2013) addresses a similar conceptual challenge of disentangling components within a spectral learning framework. A comparison, even if just a discussion, of the techniques used there and the ones proposed in this paper would provide valuable context and highlight the unique aspects of the HMM mixture problem. This would help the reader understand what aspects of the proposed solution are adapted from existing ideas versus what is fundamentally new.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The overall presentation is generally clear, but the manuscript would benefit from a careful round of proofreading to correct several grammatical errors and typos. These minor issues currently detract from the paper's professionalism and can occasionally obscure the authors' intended meaning. For example, a sentence in the draft reads: "For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy." A thorough review of the manuscript is needed to eliminate such errors and improve readability.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper presents an interesting spectral method for learning mixtures of HMMs, with a novel algorithm for resolving the component permutation ambiguity. The core idea is sound and the problem is well-motivated. The primary weaknesses are the lack of contextualization with related literature and the need for careful proofreading. Addressing these issues, particularly by discussing connections to other spectral mixture model estimation techniques, would significantly improve the quality and impact of this work.
