Summary: The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models. They propose using a spectral learning algorithm to estimate the parameters of a hidden Markov model, followed by a method to resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix and recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. Overall, this is a well-written paper. The experimental results section, however, could benefit from further elaboration. Specifically, the…
2. The authors leverage the fact that a mixture of Hidden Markov Models can be represented as a single HMM. A key motivation for learning a mixture of HMMs, as opposed to a single HMM directly, is the inherent sparsity of the mixture’s block-diagonal structure. This sparsity is particularly valuable when dealing with problems that are too large to model effectively with a full model, especially given limited training data. However, the authors’ algorithm relies on first accurately estimating the *full* HMM parameters and then reversing the permutation to reveal the block-diagonal structure. This approach seems impractical for most real-world problems. Do the authors offer any guidance on when their algorithm might be applicable, and when it might not?
3. Similarly, in practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is there any way to guarantee that the transition matrix learned by the proposed spectral algorithms will be (approximately) sparse? I suspect that many full transition matrices can closely approximate the sparse, block-diagonal model the authors are targeting. It’s not clear that the matrices learned via [1,2] will be sparse, particularly with even small amounts of noise.
4. I am concerned that the experimental results do not adequately reflect the impact of noise on the transition matrix. For example, in Section 4.1, the algorithm is applied to a transition matrix permuted with noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this is a paper about learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not simply depermuting matrices. Wouldn’t it be more appropriate to sample observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then attempt to learn all parameters from that observed data? This would provide a more realistic assessment of how noise in observations and limited data affect the learned transition matrix, and the difficulty of depermuting it in the presence of such noise.
5. The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address the concerns I raised previously, but insufficient information is provided to evaluate its quality. Specifically, I lack clarity regarding the underlying structure of the mixture of HMMs. The specific parameters of the transition and observation matrices are crucial for evaluating the difficulty of learning the model from data. I would appreciate more detail on these parameters – perhaps in an appendix if space is limited.
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialization with spectral learning sometimes perform *worse* than spectral learning alone? Shouldn’t EM always improve the results?

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, I believe the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix should be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small to read comfortably.

The paper presents some interesting ideas and sound theoretical results. However, I am skeptical that the method will perform reliably on real-world datasets.
