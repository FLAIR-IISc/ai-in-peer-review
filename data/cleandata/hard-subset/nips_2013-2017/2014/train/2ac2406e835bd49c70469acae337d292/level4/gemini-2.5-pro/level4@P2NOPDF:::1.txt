**Summary**

The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They propose a two-stage approach: first, a spectral algorithm is used to learn the parameters of a single, larger HMM that represents the mixture. Second, they introduce a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the learned transition matrix, thereby recovering the underlying block-diagonal structure corresponding to the individual HMM components.

**Major Comments**

1.  The paper is generally well-written and the theoretical contributions are interesting. However, the experimental evaluation would benefit significantly from greater detail and more realistic test cases, as outlined in the points below.

2.  The core premise of the algorithm is that a mixture of HMMs can be learned by first fitting a single, larger HMM. A primary motivation for using HMM mixtures in the first place is to leverage the sparse, block-diagonal structure of the transition matrix. This sparsity is particularly valuable for large-scale problems where training data is limited and learning a full, dense model is computationally or statistically prohibitive. The proposed algorithm, however, requires an accurate estimation of the full HMM parameters *before* recovering the sparse structure. This seems practically challenging for the very scenarios where HMM mixtures are most needed. The authors should discuss the practical limitations of this approach and clarify the types of problems for which it is best suited.

3.  A key challenge in practice is that the transition matrix recovered by the spectral learning algorithm will invariably contain estimation noise and is unlikely to be perfectly sparse (or a clean permutation of a sparse matrix). It is unclear whether the spectral methods cited [1, 2] can guarantee a learned transition matrix that is sufficiently close to the desired block-diagonal form, especially with limited data. It is plausible that many dense transition matrices could be statistically close to the true sparse model, making it difficult to distinguish signal from noise. It is not obvious that the de-permutation algorithm will succeed when the input matrix is not just a permuted block-diagonal matrix, but one corrupted with small, non-zero noise terms throughout.

4.  My most significant concern is that the experimental setup does not reflect a realistic learning scenario. In Section 4.1, the algorithm is tested by applying a permutation to a ground-truth transition matrix and then adding noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. This experiment only evaluates the de-permutation step in isolation. A more convincing and relevant evaluation would involve the full end-to-end process:
    *   Generate synthetic observation sequences from a known mixture of HMMs.
    *   Use the spectral learning method to estimate the full HMM parameters from these sequences.
    *   Apply the proposed de-permutation algorithm to the learned (and inherently noisy) transition matrix.
    *   Evaluate the accuracy of the recovered block-diagonal structure against the ground truth.
    This would provide a much clearer understanding of how observation noise and limited data affect the entire pipeline.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address the end-to-end learning problem, but it lacks sufficient detail for a proper evaluation. The performance of any HMM learning algorithm is highly dependent on the specific parameters of the underlying model. To assess the difficulty of the task and the quality of the results, crucial information is missing, such as the structure and values of the ground-truth transition and observation matrices. The authors should provide these specifics, perhaps in an appendix, to allow for a thorough assessment and ensure reproducibility.

6.  In Table 1, the results show that initializing EM with the spectral learning solution sometimes performs worse than using the spectral learning solution alone. This is counterintuitive, as one would expect the EM algorithm to, at worst, remain at the same likelihood as its initialization. Could the authors explain this phenomenon? Is a different performance metric being used, or is the EM algorithm converging to a poorer local optimum?

**Minor Comments**

1.  In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right block of the first matrix appears to be 2. I believe it should be K.
2.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small, which hinders readability.

In summary, the paper presents interesting ideas and solid theoretical results. However, I have significant concerns about the practical viability of the proposed method, as the reliance on first learning a full, dense HMM seems to contradict the primary motivation for using sparse mixtures. The experimental evaluation needs to be strengthened with more realistic, end-to-end tests and greater detail to convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness on noisy, real-world data.
