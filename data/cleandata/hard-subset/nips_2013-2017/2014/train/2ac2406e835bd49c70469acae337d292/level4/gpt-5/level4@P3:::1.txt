Summary
This paper addresses learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using spectral (method-of-moments) techniques. The key observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with each block corresponding to a component HMM. The authors first apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al.; Anandkumar et al.) to estimate a “global” HMM, which is identifiable only up to a permutation of hidden states. They then propose a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, the K blocks yield K eigenvalues equal to 1, and powers of the transition matrix isolate block structure. In the noisy case, they introduce a rank-K reconstruction and a “spectral longevity” criterion to select K and cluster columns to recover the block structure. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwriting dataset compare the proposed method to EM, and the spectral estimator is also used as an EM initializer.

Relation to prior work
Prior work on spectral learning provides polynomial-time, consistent estimators for HMMs and other latent variable models via low-order moments (Hsu et al., JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al.). Mixtures of HMMs are commonly fit with EM (e.g., Smyth, NIPS). The observation that an MHMM induces a block-diagonal transition matrix for a larger HMM is standard; the contribution here is to show how to use spectral HMM estimation plus a de-permutation step to recover component parameters and to propose a data-driven way to estimate K via eigenvalue behavior.

Strengths
- Clear core idea and a simple, modular algorithm: estimate a global HMM spectrally, then de-permute using eigen-structure.
- The spectral approach can be computationally attractive when many long sequences make EM expensive.
- The noiseless analysis is intuitive, and the spectral longevity heuristic for estimating K is interesting and practical.
- The method appears useful as an initializer for EM on real data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality of the pipeline: The approach requires first estimating the full MK×MK global HMM (with dense parameters) before discovering sparsity/block structure. In settings where MHMMs are preferred precisely because the full model is too large or poorly conditioned, it is unclear when accurate global estimation is feasible. Please delineate the intended operating regime (e.g., ranges of M, K, L, sample sizes, and mixing/separation conditions) where the method is expected to work reliably.
- Assumptions and identifiability: Lemma 2 assumes each Ak has a single eigenvalue of magnitude 1 (ergodic, aperiodic, unique stationary distribution). How sensitive is de-permutation to near-violations (e.g., slow mixing or nearly periodic chains)? Conjecture 1 is central yet unproven; some empirical or theoretical justification (e.g., perturbation bounds that separate “global” from “local” eigenvalues) would strengthen the claims.
- Sparsity concerns: In practice the spectrally learned global transition matrix will not be exactly block-diagonal and may be quite dense. Are there procedures (e.g., thresholding guided by eigenvectors, structured regularization, or post hoc projection onto the nearest block-diagonal stochastic matrix) that improve recovery and provide a measure of confidence in inferred blocks?
- End-to-end evaluation: Section 4.1 studies de-permutation under synthetic perturbations of a transition matrix. Since the method is meant to learn from observations, please report end-to-end experiments where sequences are generated from an MHMM, moments are estimated from data, the global HMM is learned spectrally, and the full de-permutation is performed. This would more faithfully capture how observation noise and limited data affect eigenstructure and downstream block recovery.
- Experimental detail and reproducibility: Section 4.2 lacks crucial specifics (transition matrices, emission parameters, concentration parameters for Dirichlet draws, mixing rates). Recovery difficulty depends strongly on these choices. Please include the exact generative settings (possibly in an appendix) and report de-permutation accuracy, K-estimation accuracy, and sensitivity to spectral gaps and sample sizes.
- Real-data setup: The requirement MK < L led you to expand L via a polynomial feature map. This could distort the observation model and complicate interpretation. Please discuss the implications and whether alternative moment constructions (or dimensionality reduction) could avoid this step. Also, clarify why EM initialized with spectral parameters sometimes underperforms the spectral solution alone (Table 1); is this due to local optima, likelihood mismatch, or model misspecification?
- Complexity and sample complexity: Provide concrete time/memory costs for moment computation and eigendecomposition as functions of N, T, M, K, and L, and compare to EM per-iteration costs. The sample complexity bound is borrowed from related work; clarifying its applicability to the MHMM-as-HMM setting would be helpful.

Quality
- Soundness: The noiseless spectral arguments are sound. The key robustness claim is stated as a conjecture; more evidence (proof sketch or systematic empirical validation) is needed.
- Empirics: Promising but not yet sufficient to establish robustness across realistic noise levels and model sizes.

Clarity
- Generally well written. Figures need larger fonts and clearer legends. Tighten notation (e.g., consistently use subscripts/superscripts; fix small typos noted below). Make the assumptions for Lemma 2 explicit early.

Originality
- Moderate. The reduction of MHMM to HMM is known; the main novelty is the spectral de-permutation and K-selection heuristic. The idea is neat and could be of practical value if robustified.

Significance
- Potentially useful for initializing EM or for fast, approximate MHMM learning at scale. Current empirical support is limited; stronger end-to-end experiments and robustness analysis would raise impact.

Specific suggestions
- Add end-to-end synthetic studies varying: number of sequences, sequence length, spectral gaps, degree of block separation, emission noise; report recovery errors for O, A, ν, cluster assignment, K.
- Evaluate K estimation accuracy vs. eigenvalue separations and sample sizes; compare longevity to simpler criteria (e.g., eigengap or information criteria).
- Explore post-processing to enforce near block-diagonality/sparsity.
- Provide failure modes and guidance on when the approach is appropriate.

Minor comments
- Section 3.2.1: In the displayed block-diagonal Λ matrix, the bottom-right subscript should be K (not 2).
- Fix possessives (its vs. it’s; authors’ vs. author’s).
- Increase font sizes in all figures; ensure axis labels and legends are readable.
- Clarify the MK < L requirement and its practical implications.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant use of spectral structure to resolve permutation and estimate K.
- Computationally attractive alternative or initializer to EM.
- Clear exposition of the core idea with a simple algorithm.

Cons for acceptance
- Central robustness claim is a conjecture; limited theoretical guarantees under noise.
- Experiments do not yet provide end-to-end validation or sufficient detail.
- Practical applicability unclear when global HMM estimation is itself difficult.

Overall
Interesting and promising idea with a clear algorithmic contribution, but the current empirical validation and robustness analysis are not yet sufficient to justify acceptance. Strengthening end-to-end experiments, clarifying assumptions and operating regimes, and adding either proofs or thorough empirical support for the conjectured spectral separation would substantially improve the paper.
