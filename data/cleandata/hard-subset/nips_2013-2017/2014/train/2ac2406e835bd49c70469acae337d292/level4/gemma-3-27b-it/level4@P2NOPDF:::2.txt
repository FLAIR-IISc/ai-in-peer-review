This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The authors demonstrate that a previously published spectral algorithm for learning hidden Markov models (Anandkumar et al., 2012) can be directly applied to MHMMs by reformulating the latter as a specific instance of the former. A key contribution is a stabilization technique addressing the parameter permutation problem, achieved by assuming each HMM’s transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue equal to one.

While the core idea of leveraging existing spectral methods is sound, the presentation suffers from some shortcomings. The notation requires clarification; consistently defining vectors and matrices – including their dimensions – upon first introduction would significantly improve readability. For example, terms in Corollary 1, such as 1J^T (a row vector of length J filled with ones), would benefit from explicit definition. Further elaboration on the limiting behavior of bar{A}^e as e approaches infinity – specifically, its structure as a JK x JK block diagonal matrix with identical columns within each J x J block – would enhance understanding.

The related work section is notably thin. The authors should acknowledge and discuss relevant prior work, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013), which explores initializing Expectation-Maximization (EM) with spectral estimates. 

The experimental evaluation, as currently presented, is insufficient. Figure 4 is difficult to interpret and should be replaced with a tabular presentation of results. Furthermore, the performance of EM in Table 1 is surprisingly poor; typically, even with random initialization, EM converges to solutions exceeding the accuracy of spectral methods given sufficient iterations, despite being susceptible to local optima. Reporting the best observed accuracy for EM, rather than the average, is standard practice and should be included for a fair comparison. Minor notational consistency with Hsu et al. (2009) – specifically using *T* for transition matrices (instead of *A*) and *n*, *m* for the numbers of observations and hidden states (instead of *L*, *J*) – would also be beneficial.

Despite these weaknesses, the proposed approach to mitigating parameter permutation is both interesting and intuitive. The authors also provide a non-trivial analysis of estimation noise, leading to an insightful application of eigenvalues (Equation (7)) within the algorithm.

**Specific Comments & Requests:**

*   **Corollary 1:** Define 1J^T and provide a detailed explanation of the structure of lim{e->inf} bar{A}^e.
*   **Notation:** Define all vectors and matrices, along with their dimensions, upon first use.
*   **Figure 4:** Replace with a table for improved clarity.
*   **Table 1:** Report the best (maximum) accuracy achieved by EM, not the average. Investigate and discuss the unexpectedly poor EM performance.
*   **Notation Consistency:** Consider adopting the notation of Hsu et al. (2009) (T for transition matrices, n and m for observation/hidden states).



In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution in adapting spectral methods for MHMM learning, particularly in addressing the parameter permutation issue. However, improvements in notation, related work discussion, and experimental presentation are crucial to strengthen the paper and facilitate its understanding. The core algorithmic idea shows promise, but a more polished and comprehensive presentation is necessary for a strong publication.
