**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The proposed approach involves two stages: first, using a spectral algorithm to learn the parameters of a single, larger HMM that represents the mixture; and second, resolving the permutation ambiguity in the learned transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure, thereby identifying the individual component HMMs.

**Major Comments**

1.  The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, the experimental section would benefit from additional detail to substantiate the claims made.

2.  The core of the proposed method relies on the observation that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single, larger HMM. However, a primary motivation for using HMM mixtures is to leverage the sparse, block-diagonal structure to model complex systems with fewer parameters than a single large, dense HMM. This is particularly crucial in settings with limited training data where estimating a dense model is infeasible. The proposed algorithm appears to work in the opposite direction: it first requires an accurate estimation of the full, dense HMM parameters before recovering the sparse structure. This raises a significant practical concern: if one has enough data to accurately learn the dense HMM, the original motivation for using a sparse mixture model is diminished. Could the authors comment on the specific application scenarios or data regimes where this approach would be more advantageous than directly learning a large HMM or using other mixture estimation techniques?

3.  Following the previous point, a key assumption is that the transition matrix learned by the spectral algorithm will be a noisy permutation of a truly block-diagonal matrix. In practice, however, estimation errors from finite data will likely result in a dense matrix. Is there any theoretical guarantee or empirical evidence to suggest that the output of the spectral learning algorithms ([1, 2] in the paper) will be sufficiently close to a permuted sparse matrix, rather than another dense matrix that is also statistically close to the true model? It seems plausible that many dense transition matrices could approximate the target data distribution, making the de-permutation step fragile to estimation noise.

4.  I have significant concerns about the experimental validation, particularly in Section 4.1. The experiment tests the de-permutation algorithm on a ground-truth transition matrix that is artificially permuted. This setup only evaluates one component of the proposed method in isolation. A more convincing and realistic experiment would involve evaluating the entire pipeline: 1) generate observation sequences from a known HMM mixture, 2) use the spectral method to learn the single HMM parameters from this data, and 3) apply the de-permutation algorithm to the learned (and now noisy) transition matrix. This end-to-end evaluation would provide a much clearer picture of the algorithm's robustness to the statistical noise inherent in parameter estimation from finite data.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but it lacks the necessary details to fully assess the results. The difficulty of the learning task is highly dependent on the specific parameters of the component HMMs (e.g., the separability of observation distributions, the mixing rates in the transition matrices). To allow for proper evaluation and reproducibility, the authors should provide the full parameters of the HMM mixtures used in the experiments, perhaps in an appendix if space is a concern.

6.  In Table 1, the results for 'EM (init. spectral)' are sometimes worse than for 'Spectral' alone. Since Expectation-Maximization is an iterative procedure that should, in principle, improve upon or maintain the likelihood of its initialization, this result is counter-intuitive. Could the authors provide an explanation for this phenomenon? Does it indicate that EM is converging to a poor local optimum?

**Minor Comments**

1.  In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be 2. It should likely be K.
2.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small to be easily legible.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the problem of learning HMM mixtures. The two-stage approach of learning a dense model and then recovering the sparse structure is novel. However, I have significant reservations about the practical viability of this method, primarily because its core assumption seems to run counter to the main motivation for using mixture models in data-limited regimes. The experimental validation is currently insufficient to alleviate these concerns. While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper would be much stronger if the authors could address the practical issues raised and provide a more comprehensive, end-to-end empirical evaluation.
