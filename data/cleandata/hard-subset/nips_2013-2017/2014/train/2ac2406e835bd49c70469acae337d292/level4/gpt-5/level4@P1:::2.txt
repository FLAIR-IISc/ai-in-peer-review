This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning mixtures of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The core idea is to view an MHMM as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with one block per component HMM. This allows the authors to apply existing spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012) “off the shelf” to estimate the global parameters, which are identifiable up to a permutation of latent states. The paper then proposes a stabilization-based depermutation step that exploits the spectral structure of the global transition matrix: under the assumption that each component transition matrix has a single eigenvalue equal to 1, the authors show (in the noiseless case) that repeated powers of the global transition matrix reveal K rank-1 blocks, enabling recovery of a consistent state ordering. They further propose a noise-aware heuristic that uses eigenvalue “longevity” (Equation (7)) to infer the number of components K and to perform depermutation when only an approximate global model is available. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and a UCI handwritten character trajectory dataset. The authors also explore using the spectral estimates to initialize EM.

Strengths
- Reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is clean and allows direct use of established spectral HMM methods, yielding computational benefits for large datasets.
- The depermutation strategy is intuitive and well-motivated: it leverages the eigenstructure induced by disconnected latent-state blocks in the global transition matrix.
- The noise-aware analysis, including the eigenvalue longevity criterion in Equation (7), is interesting and leads to a practical procedure for estimating K and undoing permutations.
- The real-data experiment suggests that spectral estimates can serve as effective initializations for EM, which is useful in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption: The depermutation relies on each component transition matrix having exactly one eigenvalue equal to 1. This is a substantive restriction, and its practical scope should be discussed more thoroughly (e.g., how often it holds, sensitivity to violations).
- Notation and clarity: There are several places where notation is unclear or undefined on first use, which makes the paper harder to follow.
- Related work: The paper needs to better position itself relative to prior work. For example, using spectral methods to initialize EM has already been explored (Chaganty and Liang, 2013).
- Experiments: The synthetic experiments are basic, and some presentation choices make it difficult to interpret results. The EM baselines also appear weaker than expected, raising questions about setup and reporting.
- Theory under noise: Some key statements are conjectural (e.g., Conjecture 1). While acceptable for an initial exploration, this weakens the theoretical support for the method’s robustness.

Quality
- Technically, the noiseless case is clear and the reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is correct. The depermutation idea follows from standard spectral properties.
- In the noisy case, the justification is partial and framed as a conjecture; stronger guarantees or more extensive empirical validation would improve confidence.
- Experimental evaluation demonstrates speed advantages but is limited in scope and does not convincingly establish accuracy improvements over EM.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from careful notation cleanup and dimension annotations. In particular:
  - In Corollary 1, define 1J^T explicitly as the length-J row vector of ones, and clarify the structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e (it is JK × JK with K blocks of size J × J, and columns within each block are identical).
  - Define every vector/matrix and its dimensions at first appearance.
  - Consider harmonizing notation with Hsu et al. (2009): T for transition matrices (instead of A), and n, m for numbers of observation and hidden states (instead of L, J).
- Figures: Figure 4 is hard to read; a table would be clearer for reporting accuracies and timings.

Originality
- The reduction to an HMM is known, but applying spectral HMM learning directly followed by a stabilization/depermutation step for MHMMs is a neat idea.
- The eigenvalue longevity heuristic (Equation (7)) is a novel and intriguing way to estimate K and stabilize depermutation under noise.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could be valuable for large-scale sequence clustering, given the computational advantages of MoM over EM.
- However, the experimental evidence is not yet strong enough to demonstrate clear accuracy benefits. The most convincing use case here is as an initialization for EM, consistent with prior practice.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Please clarify the notation in Corollary 1 and explicitly describe the block structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e.
- Define the dimensions of all vectors/matrices on first use.
- Expand the related work section. Cite and discuss Chaganty and Liang (2013) on spectral initializations for EM, and ensure comprehensive coverage of spectral HMM learning (Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012).
- Replace Figure 4 with tables for readability. Report both average and best EM performance across multiple random restarts, as it is standard that EM, with sufficient iterations and restarts, often outperforms pure spectral methods in accuracy despite local optima.
- Consider conforming to the notation of Hsu et al. (2009): T for transitions; n, m for numbers of observations and hidden states.
- Discuss the practicality of the “single unit eigenvalue per component” assumption and provide sensitivity analyses when it does not strictly hold.

Overall assessment
The paper proposes an algorithm that combines an existing spectral HMM learner with a novel stabilization step to resolve the permutation ambiguity in MHMMs. The idea is appealing and computationally attractive, but the current submission would benefit from clearer notation, stronger engagement with related work, more robust empirical baselines (especially EM), and either stronger theoretical guarantees under noise or more extensive empirical validation of the proposed heuristics.
