

The paper proposes a spectral method for learning mixtures of hidden Markov models (HMMs), building on the framework of Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012). While the core algorithm is a straightforward adaptation of the standard approach, the work hinges on addressing a critical challenge: the recovered transition matrix is an arbitrary permutation of the true one, effectively mixing component labels. The authors introduce a depermutation procedure to resolve this issue, which appears effective in practice. However, the paper’s presentation of this key contribution lacks sufficient technical depth. The depermutation algorithm is described briefly without rigorous justification or performance guarantees, leaving questions about its robustness under noise or in high-dimensional settings. The authors should clarify *how* the depermutation step uniquely identifies the correct permutation, especially given that permutation ambiguities are inherent to spectral methods for mixture models.

The manuscript contains several minor but distracting errors. For instance, the phrase "identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy" (Section 3.1) is grammatically incorrect and should read "identify eigenvalues that capture all the energy." Additional typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., "de-permute" vs. "depermutation") suggest insufficient proofreading. These issues undermine confidence in the technical presentation and should be corrected.

The paper also fails to adequately contextualize its contribution within the broader literature on spectral mixture learning. It would significantly strengthen the work to explicitly connect to foundational approaches like Chaganty and Liang (2013), who developed spectral methods for mixtures of linear regressions. The relationship between their work (which handles regression mixtures) and this paper’s HMM mixture setting merits discussion: for instance, how the depermutation challenge here differs from or parallels issues in other mixture models. Without this, the novelty and significance of the proposed method remain unclear. The authors should explicitly compare their depermutation strategy to techniques used in related work, such as those for Gaussian mixtures or linear models.

Furthermore, the experimental section lacks quantitative evaluation of the depermutation step’s efficacy. While the method appears to work "reasonably well" in practice, the paper does not provide metrics (e.g., permutation error rates, label recovery accuracy) or ablation studies demonstrating its necessity. This omission is critical, as the core innovation hinges on this step. The authors should include results showing how depermutation improves downstream performance compared to using the permuted transition matrix directly.

Overall, the technical idea is plausible but underdeveloped. The spectral framework is sound, yet the paper’s contribution is primarily algorithmic—specifically, the depermutation module—without sufficient theoretical grounding or comparative analysis. The contextual gap with existing spectral mixture literature is a notable weakness, and the presentation requires careful revision to fix grammatical errors and clarify the method’s key advances. With substantial revisions to address these points—including a rigorous depermutation analysis, contextualization with Chaganty & Liang (2013), and improved experimental validation—the paper could make a meaningful contribution. As written, it falls short of NIPS standards due to insufficient novelty and incomplete justification of its core mechanism. I recommend **major revision**.
