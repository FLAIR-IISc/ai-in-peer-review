**Summary:**
This manuscript addresses the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a two-step approach, first employing a spectral learning algorithm to estimate the parameters of a hidden Markov model and then resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

**Major Comments:**

1. The paper is well-written, but the experimental results section could benefit from additional details. Specifically, the presentation of the results could be expanded to provide a clearer understanding of the findings.

2. The authors' reliance on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be expressed as a single HMM raises concerns. The primary motivation for learning a mixture of HMMs is to leverage the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure, which is often imposed on large problems with limited training data. However, the proposed algorithm requires accurate estimation of the full HMM parameters, followed by permutation reversal to recover the block-diagonal structure. This approach may not be feasible for most real-world problems. The authors should provide guidance on the applicability and limitations of their algorithm.

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. It is unclear whether the spectral algorithms proposed in this paper can guarantee a sparse transition matrix. There may be multiple HMM models with full transition matrices that are close to the desired sparse model. The authors should address this concern and discuss the potential impact of noise on the learned matrix.

4. The experimental results in Section 4.1 do not accurately reflect the reality of noise in the transition matrix. Instead of permuting a transition matrix with noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution, the authors should have sampled observation data from a mixture of HMMs and learned the parameters from the observed data. This would provide a more accurate assessment of the algorithm's performance in the presence of noise.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but lacks sufficient information to evaluate the quality of the experiment. The authors should provide more details about the mixture of HMMs used in the experiment, including the transition and observation matrix parameters. This information is crucial for understanding the difficulty of learning the model from data.

6. The results in Table 1 show that EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone. This is counterintuitive, as EM should only improve the results. The authors should provide an explanation for this observation.

**Minor Comments:**

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrect. It should be K, not 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small and should be enlarged for better readability.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents some interesting ideas and good theoretical results, there are concerns about the practical applicability of the method. The authors should address these concerns and provide more comprehensive experimental results to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
