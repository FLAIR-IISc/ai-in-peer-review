This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The core insight is that an MHMM with K components can be viewed as a single, larger HMM whose transition matrix has a specific block-diagonal structure. This formulation allows the authors to apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs directly. The main technical contribution of the paper is a novel method for resolving the parameter permutation ambiguity that arises from this approach. The authors propose a procedure based on the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix to correctly group the hidden states belonging to each component HMM, thereby recovering the parameters of the individual models. The analysis is extended to the noisy case, providing a method to estimate the number of components from the data.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed method for resolving the parameter permutation ambiguity is the paper's main strength. The idea of using the eigenvectors corresponding to the K largest eigenvalues of the global transition matrix to cluster the hidden states is elegant and intuitive.
*   The analysis for the case with estimation noise, which leads to the "spectral longevity" criterion (Equation 7) for model selection (i.e., finding K), is an interesting and valuable contribution.
*   The paper addresses a significant practical problem. Learning MHMMs with the standard EM algorithm can be computationally prohibitive for large datasets, and a fast, moment-based alternative is a welcome development. The proposed algorithm's computational cost is independent of the number of sequences after the initial moment computation, which is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The experimental evaluation feels preliminary and could be strengthened. The comparison with the EM algorithm, which is the primary baseline, needs to be more thorough. For example, the EM results reported in Table 1 appear unusually weak, which raises questions about the experimental setup.
*   The paper could do a better job of positioning its contribution within the existing literature. Specifically, the idea of using spectral estimates as a warm-start for the EM algorithm is a key application, but the authors do not cite relevant prior work in this area, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013), who explored this for mixture of regressions.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved in several places. Key notation is sometimes used without a clear definition of its dimensions, and some figures are difficult to interpret.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Notation:** To improve readability, please define each vector and matrix, along with its dimensions, upon its first appearance. For example, in Corollary 1, the notation `1_M` should be explicitly defined as a column vector of ones of length M. A clear and consistent notational scheme would greatly benefit the reader.

2.  **Explanation of Corollary 1:** The structure of `lim e→∞ Ā^e` could be explained more explicitly. Clarifying that this results in a MK × MK matrix composed of K blocks, where all columns within the k-th M x M block are identical and equal to the stationary distribution `v_k`, would make the subsequent clustering argument easier to follow.

3.  **Experimental Comparison with EM:** The performance of EM in Table 1 is surprisingly low. Typically, while spectral methods provide a good initialization, a well-tuned EM algorithm (run until convergence from multiple random starts) often achieves higher accuracy, despite the risk of local optima. It is standard practice to report the best result for EM over several random initializations. Please clarify the setup for the EM baseline and consider including results from this more robust evaluation protocol.

4.  **Presentation of Results:** Figure 4 is very difficult to read due to the overlapping lines and error bars. These results would be much clearer and more impactful if presented in a table. This would allow for a direct numerical comparison of accuracy and runtime between the methods under different data regimes.

5.  **Related Work:** The use of spectral methods for initializing EM is a powerful idea and a major motivation for this work. The authors should acknowledge and cite previous work that has explored this direction, such as Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013), to better contextualize their contribution.

6.  **Consistency with Prior Notation (Minor):** As a minor suggestion, consider aligning the notation with that of seminal papers in this area, like Hsu et al. (2009). For instance, using `T` for transition matrices and `n, m` for the number of observation and hidden states, respectively, could improve accessibility for readers familiar with the literature.
