Summary: The paper studies the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose first using a spectral learning algorithm to estimate parameters of an equivalent single HMM, and then resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure corresponding to the mixture components.

Major Comments:
1. The paper is generally well written. However, the experimental results section needs to be expanded. In particular, several key experimental details and analyses are missing, which makes it hard to assess robustness and practical utility.

2. The approach relies on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single HMM. One of the primary motivations for modeling a mixture (rather than learning a single HMM directly) is that the block-diagonal structure of the mixture transition matrix is sparse, which is useful when the state space is large and training data are limited. In contrast, the proposed algorithm first requires accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and only then recovering the block structure via de-permutation. For many real-world settings, this seems untenable. Under what conditions (e.g., sample size, noise levels, separation assumptions) do the authors expect their algorithm to be appropriate, and when would it not be?

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is there any way to guarantee or encourage that the transition matrix learned by the suggested spectral methods will be (approximately) sparse? It seems plausible that many full-transition HMMs are close—in likelihood or total variation—to a block-sparse model, especially with small amounts of noise. It is not at all obvious that the matrices learned via [1,2] will be sparse (or close to sparse) in such regimes.

4. I am concerned that the experimental design does not adequately reflect realistic noise and estimation error in the transition matrix. In Section 4.1, the algorithm is applied to a transition matrix perturbed by Dirichlet noise; however, this paper is about learning mixtures of HMMs, not only de-permuting matrices. A more faithful evaluation would sample observation sequences from a mixture of HMMs and then learn all parameters from the observations, so we can see how observation noise and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and how difficult de-permutation becomes under realistic estimation error.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 moves in the right direction, but there is insufficient information to evaluate it. The actual mixture parameters (transition and observation matrices) matter greatly for assessing learnability. Please provide the specifics of these parameters (e.g., number of components, state counts per component, transition sparsity levels, emission distributions, separation between components), perhaps in an appendix if space is limited.

6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes perform worse than spectral learning alone? Typically, EM initialized from a good estimate should not degrade performance. Please clarify the initialization, stopping criteria, and whether EM is optimizing the same objective evaluated in the table, as well as any regularization that might explain this outcome.

Minor Comments:
1. In Section 3.2.1, in the proof, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix should be K, not 2.
2. Text and numbers in the figures are too small to read comfortably; please increase font sizes and line widths.

Overall, the paper presents interesting ideas and solid theoretical results, but I am concerned about the practicality of the method on real datasets and would like to see more realistic and thoroughly documented experiments.
