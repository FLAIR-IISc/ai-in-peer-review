### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea is to re-frame the MHMM learning problem as learning a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs to estimate the global parameters. The main technical contribution is a method to resolve the resulting permutation ambiguity, which scrambles the parameters of the individual component HMMs. This is achieved by analyzing the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix to identify its underlying block structure and thereby recover the parameters of the constituent HMMs. The paper demonstrates the approach on both synthetic and real-world data, highlighting its computational advantages over the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposed approach is novel and addresses the challenging problem of learning MHMMs from a fresh perspective. Bypassing the iterative nature of EM with a non-iterative spectral method offers a significant computational advantage, especially for large datasets. This could make MHMMs more practical for large-scale sequence clustering tasks.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical derivation is clever and well-founded. The insight that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a specific block-diagonal structure is powerful. The subsequent method for depermuting the learned transition matrix by exploiting its spectral properties (specifically, the `K` eigenvalues that are close to 1) is elegant and well-justified in the noiseless case.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, very well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly explain the model, the equivalence to a structured HMM, the permutation problem, and their proposed spectral solution.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments Against Acceptance)**

*   **Practicality and Scalability:** The primary motivation for using an MHMM over a single, large HMM is often the sparsity inherent in its block-diagonal structure. This is particularly beneficial when the state space is large but the data is limited. The proposed algorithm, however, requires first learning the parameters of a full `MK x MK` HMM. This seems to contradict one of the main motivations for the MHMM model. For problems with large `K` or `M`, estimating this dense global transition matrix accurately could be statistically and computationally challenging, potentially requiring more data than a standard EM approach. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the regimes (e.g., in terms of `M`, `K`, `N`, and `T`) where this approach is most viable.
*   **Assumptions about the Learned Matrix:** The depermutation method relies on the learned global transition matrix being a slightly perturbed version of a true block-diagonal matrix. However, it is not obvious that the matrix learned via spectral methods from finite data will necessarily have this near-block-diagonal structure. There could exist other, denser transition matrices that are also consistent with the observed moments and yield a similar likelihood, which would cause the depermutation algorithm to fail. The paper lacks a discussion or guarantee that the output of the spectral HMM learning step will indeed be close to the desired permuted block-diagonal form.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while promising, have weaknesses that limit their impact:
    1.  The experiment in Section 4.1 tests the depermutation algorithm in isolation by adding noise directly to a permuted matrix. A more convincing validation would involve an end-to-end evaluation: generate sequence data from a known MHMM, apply the full spectral learning pipeline to estimate the noisy, permuted parameters, and *then* demonstrate that the depermutation step successfully recovers the ground-truth components. This would provide a more realistic assessment of how observation noise and finite sample effects propagate through the entire estimation process.
    2.  The experiments in Section 4.2 lack crucial details. The difficulty of learning an MHMM depends heavily on the specific parameters—for instance, the separability of the emission distributions and the mixing rates of the transition matrices. Without these details, it is difficult to assess the difficulty of the task and the true performance of the algorithm. These parameters should be provided, perhaps in an appendix.
    3.  In Table 1, the "EM initialized with Spectral" method sometimes performs worse in terms of accuracy than the "Spectral" method alone. This is counter-intuitive, as EM, given an initialization, is expected to find a solution with equal or greater likelihood. While likelihood and clustering accuracy are not the same, this result is surprising and warrants an explanation.

### **Minor Comments and Suggestions**

*   In the proof of Lemma 2 (Section 3.2.1), the subscript of `V` and `Λ` in the bottom-right block of the first block matrix appears to have a typo. It should likely be `K`, not `2`.
*   The text and numbers in the figures (particularly Figures 3 and 4) are too small, making them difficult to read. Increasing the font size would improve clarity.

### **Overall Assessment**

This paper introduces an interesting and theoretically elegant spectral algorithm for learning Mixtures of HMMs. The core contribution—the depermutation technique—is novel. However, there are significant concerns about the practical applicability of a method that requires learning a full, dense HMM as an intermediate step. Furthermore, the experimental validation is not yet convincing enough to fully support the paper's claims about robustness to noise in a realistic, end-to-end setting.

While the ideas are promising, the current manuscript feels incomplete due to the gap between the theoretical framework and its practical validation. I am currently leaning towards rejection, but I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can convincingly address the major concerns regarding practicality and provide more thorough, end-to-end experimental results.
