

Summary: This paper addresses learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) by first applying spectral learning to estimate full HMM parameters, then resolving permutation ambiguity to recover the block-diagonal transition structure. The theoretical contributions are sound, and the writing is generally clear. However, significant concerns regarding the method's practical applicability and experimental validation require addressing before acceptance.

Major Comments:  
1.  **Core Methodological Flaw:** The authors' approach—estimating a full HMM before depermuting to recover block structure—contradicts the primary motivation for using mixture HMMs. The key advantage of mixture models is their *inherent sparsity* (via block-diagonal structure), which enables learning on large-scale or data-scarce problems where a full HMM would be intractable. Here, the method *first requires accurate estimation of a full HMM*, which defeats the purpose for the very problems it aims to solve. The authors should explicitly clarify *when their algorithm is appropriate* (e.g., only when the full HMM is feasible to estimate) and *when it is not* (e.g., for large-scale or low-data settings where sparsity is essential).

2.  **Sparsity Guarantee:** The paper assumes the learned transition matrix will be sparse, but this is unsupported. Spectral learning typically produces dense matrices, and many full-HMM models are close to sparse block-diagonal structures under small noise. Without a mechanism to enforce or guarantee sparsity (e.g., regularization), the method risks recovering dense matrices that do not reflect the intended mixture structure. The authors must address how their algorithm ensures sparsity, especially with noisy data.

3.  **Experimental Design Mismatch:** Section 4.1’s noise model (Dirichlet-sampled permutations applied to a *matrix*) does not align with the paper’s contribution. The core problem is *learning from mixture HMM observation data*, not depermuting pre-specified matrices. To validate the method, experiments must:  
    (a) Generate synthetic observation sequences from a *mixture of HMMs* (not just permute a transition matrix),  
    (b) Learn all parameters (including the block structure) from these sequences, and  
    (c) Quantify how noise in observations/data affects the ability to recover the true block structure.  
    Current experiments fail to model the full generative process, making results misleading for real-world applicability.

4.  **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** Section 4.2 begins to address the above but lacks critical information. The specific parameters of the mixture HMM (transition matrices, observation matrices, mixing weights) are essential for evaluating difficulty and reproducibility. For example, the block structure’s size, inter-block connectivity, and observation likelihoods drastically impact learnability. The authors should provide these parameters in an appendix to allow meaningful assessment of the results.

5.  **EM Performance Anomaly:** Table 1 shows EM initialized with spectral learning *worsening* over spectral learning alone. This contradicts expectations, as EM should refine estimates. The authors must explain this counterintuitive result—e.g., due to poor initialization, overfitting, or a fundamental limitation of their spectral step.

Minor Comments:  
1.  In Section 3.2.1, the subscript for $\Lambda$ in the bottom-right matrix should be $K$ (not 2), as it denotes the number of components.  
2.  Figure text and labels are too small to be legible; please increase font size.

Overall Assessment:  
The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to HMM mixtures, but its practical relevance is severely undermined by the method’s reliance on full-HMM estimation and the lack of realistic experimental validation. The core idea—using spectral learning to recover block structure—has merit, but the current implementation and evaluation fail to demonstrate viability for the sparse, large-scale problems that motivate mixture HMMs. The experimental section requires substantial revision to simulate data from the full generative model and provide parameter details. Without these fixes, the method appears largely unsuited for real-world applications where sparsity is the key advantage. While the theory is solid, the practical gap is too large for acceptance in its current form. The authors should either: (a) revise the method to avoid full-HMM estimation, or (b) provide compelling evidence from realistic data simulations showing robustness to noise and data scarcity. As written, the paper’s contribution is limited to a niche scenario where full-HMM learning is feasible—a case that largely negates the need for the mixture model itself.
