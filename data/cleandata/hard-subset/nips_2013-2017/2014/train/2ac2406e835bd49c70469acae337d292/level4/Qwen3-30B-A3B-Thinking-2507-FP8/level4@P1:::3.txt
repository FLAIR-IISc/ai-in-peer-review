

The paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a key challenge in this model class: the permutation ambiguity in parameter estimation. The authors demonstrate that an MHMM can be reformulated as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling application of existing spectral HMM learning algorithms. Crucially, they develop a depermutation procedure leveraging the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover individual HMM parameters from the permuted output. The method is validated on synthetic data and a real-world handwritten character dataset, showing competitive accuracy with EM while significantly reducing computational cost.  

**Strengths**  
*Originality & Significance*: This work makes a clear contribution by solving the permutation ambiguity problem specific to MHMMs, a gap in prior spectral learning literature. While spectral methods for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012) and mixtures of linear regressions (Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2013) exist, the authors extend these to MHMMs—a non-trivial step requiring novel spectral analysis. The depermutation algorithm (Section 3.2) is particularly significant: it provides a theoretically grounded solution under spectral conditions (Lemmas 2–3), moving beyond ad-hoc heuristics. The real-data experiment (Section 4.3) further demonstrates practical utility, showing the method serves as an effective EM initialization. This advances the state of the art in scalable sequence clustering.  
*Quality*: The technical arguments are sound. The equivalence between MHMMs and structured HMMs (Lemma 1) is rigorously proven, and the spectral conditions for depermutation (Definitions 1–3, Lemma 3) are well-motivated. The experiments (Section 4) effectively compare accuracy/speed against EM across data regimes, with appropriate baselines (e.g., random vs. MoM-initialized EM). The theoretical bound in Conjecture 1 (while not fully proven) is plausible given the sample complexity literature cited.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Clarity*: The paper’s presentation could be significantly improved. Key concepts—like the "global transition matrix" (Section 3.1) and the depermutation logic (Section 3.2)—lack intuitive explanations. For instance, the phrase "it is not possible to directly learn an MHMM with existing approaches" (Abstract) is vague; the paper should explicitly state that permutation ambiguity prevents direct parameter recovery. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is concise but omits critical details: how exactly the spectral longevity (Definition 3) is computed in practice, and why the clustering step (Section 3.2.1) avoids the infeasible brute-force search. A brief example or pseudocode for the depermutation step would aid reproducibility.  
*Context & Related Work*: The paper understates its novelty relative to prior spectral work. While Chaganty & Liang (ICML 2013) address mixtures of linear regressions, the MHMM case is distinct due to the sequential structure and permutation ambiguity in transition matrices. The authors should explicitly contrast their spectral depermutation approach with Chaganty & Liang’s method (which handles mixtures of regressions without sequential dependencies) to clarify the conceptual advance. The reference to Smyth (NIPS 1996) for MHMMs is appropriate, but the paper misses a chance to discuss how their spectral method differs from EM-based MHMM approaches (e.g., Qi et al., IEEE Trans. SP 2006).  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable technical contribution to scalable learning of structured sequence models. The core innovation—solving the permutation ambiguity via spectral properties—is novel, well-justified, and empirically validated. However, the presentation impedes clarity, particularly regarding the depermutation mechanism, which is the paper’s key technical insight. Minor typographical errors (e.g., "identify of eigenvalues" in Section 4.1) and insufficient contextualization reduce accessibility.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary**  
*Pro*: Solves a non-trivial problem (permutation ambiguity in MHMMs) with a theoretically grounded spectral solution; demonstrates clear computational advantages over EM; provides a practical initialization method for EM.  
*Con*: Clarity issues obscure the core algorithm; insufficient discussion of how this differs from related spectral mixture work (e.g., Chaganty & Liang); minor presentation errors.  

**Overall**: The paper’s technical merit and significance justify acceptance, but the authors should address clarity concerns in revision (e.g., by adding a concise example of the depermutation step and sharpening the related-work discussion). The method is a meaningful advance for sequence clustering, and the results are convincing. With minor revisions to exposition, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
