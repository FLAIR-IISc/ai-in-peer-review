This paper proposes a spectral, method-of-moments-based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key insight is to view an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, as a single large HMM with KM hidden states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. This observation allows the authors to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the global parameters. However, this direct application leads to a fundamental parameter permutation ambiguity, where the estimated states are shuffled, making it impossible to recover the parameters of the individual component HMMs.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel procedure to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The proposed method leverages the spectral properties of the ideal block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors show that in the noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1. By raising the estimated transition matrix to a high power (or, more robustly, by computing a low-rank reconstruction using the top K eigenvectors), the block structure is revealed. The columns of this reconstructed matrix naturally form K clusters, and the cluster assignments directly provide the mapping needed to de-permute the parameters and recover the individual HMMs. The paper also provides an analysis for the more realistic case where estimation noise is present and proposes a heuristic method for estimating the number of HMM components, K, from the data. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed solution to the parameter permutation problem is elegant, intuitive, and novel. While the initial formulation of an MHMM as a large HMM is straightforward, identifying and resolving the resulting permutation issue using spectral clustering on the transition matrix's eigenspace is a clever and significant contribution. This work makes spectral learning, a computationally attractive alternative to EM, applicable to the important class of MHMMs.

2.  **Theoretical Insight:** The analysis connecting the block-diagonal structure of the transition matrix to its spectral properties is sound. The development from the noiseless case (Corollary 1) to the noisy case (Conjecture 1, Lemma 3) provides a clear and principled motivation for the final algorithm. The proposed "spectral longevity" metric for model selection (estimating K) is an interesting idea.

3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The method offers a significant computational advantage over the standard EM algorithm, which can be prohibitively slow on large datasets due to the need for repeated forward-backward passes for every sequence. The proposed method's core computation, after calculating moments, involves matrix factorizations whose size is independent of the number of sequences, making it highly scalable.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper's presentation could be significantly improved.
    *   Several terms are used without clear definition. For example, in Corollary 1, the notation `1>M` should be explicitly defined as a row vector of ones of length M. A brief explanation of the resulting structure of `lim e→∞ Ā^e`—that it is a KM x KM matrix where columns within each of the K blocks of size M are identical and represent the stationary distributions—would greatly aid reader understanding.
    *   It is good practice to define all vectors and matrices with their dimensions upon their first appearance. This would make the mathematical arguments much easier to follow.
    *   To improve readability for experts in this area, the authors might consider adopting the notation used in seminal spectral HMM papers (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012), such as using T for transition matrices and n, m for observation and state dimensions.

2.  **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper needs to better position itself with respect to existing literature. The idea of using spectral methods as an initialization for EM is not new and has been explored for other mixture models (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, ICML 2013). The real-data experiment in Section 4.3 demonstrates that the proposed method serves as an excellent initialization for EM. This connection should be discussed explicitly, and the paper would be stronger if it acknowledged and differentiated its contribution from prior work on spectral initializations.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while demonstrating feasibility, could be more thorough.
    *   Figure 4 is difficult to read and interpret. These results would be much clearer if presented in a table, allowing for a direct comparison of accuracy and runtime numbers.
    *   The performance of the randomly initialized EM baseline in Table 1 appears unusually low. For a fair comparison, it is standard practice to run EM from multiple random initializations and report the result corresponding to the highest log-likelihood, not the average. Please clarify if this was done, and if not, please include results for the *best* EM run. While spectral methods provide a good initialization, a well-tuned EM algorithm often achieves higher accuracy, even if it requires more restarts and computation time.

4.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical justification for the noisy case rests on Conjecture 1, which adapts a sample complexity bound from a related but different model without a formal proof. While a full proof might be beyond the scope of this paper, acknowledging this limitation and framing the result more carefully would strengthen the paper.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    1.  Presents a novel, elegant, and effective solution to the critical problem of parameter permutation in spectral learning of MHMMs.
    2.  Addresses the important goal of developing scalable learning algorithms for a widely used model class.
    3.  The core idea is well-motivated, and the proposed algorithm is intuitive and practical.

*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:**
    1.  The paper's clarity is hampered by imprecise notation and a lack of clear definitions.
    2.  The discussion of related work is insufficient, particularly regarding the use of spectral methods for initializing EM.
    3.  The experimental comparison with EM could be more rigorous, and the presentation of results needs improvement.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this paper presents a valuable and interesting contribution to the literature on spectral learning methods. The core idea for resolving the permutation ambiguity is strong. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation, the positioning relative to prior work, and the thoroughness of the experiments. These issues seem addressable in a revision. I am leaning towards acceptance but strongly recommend that the authors address the points raised above to improve the quality and impact of their work.
