Summary:
The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They propose a spectral learning algorithm to estimate the parameters of an HMM and introduce a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix, enabling the recovery of its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-written, but the experimental results section requires more detail. Specifically, the description of the experiments could be expanded to provide a clearer understanding of the results.

2. The authors leverage the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single HMM. However, one of the primary motivations for learning a mixture of HMMs instead of a single HMM is the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure in the mixture. This sparsity is particularly important when dealing with large problems and limited training data. Nevertheless, the authors' algorithm relies on accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and then reversing the permutation to find the block-diagonal structure. It is unclear whether this approach is feasible for most real-world problems. The authors should provide guidance on when their algorithm is suitable and when it is not.

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. It is uncertain whether the spectral algorithms proposed in this paper can guarantee that the learned transition matrix will be sparse or close to sparse. There may be multiple HMM models with full transition matrices that are similar to the sparse model with a block-diagonal structure. It is not evident that the matrix learned via the proposed algorithms will be sparse, especially with small amounts of noise.

4. The experimental results may not accurately reflect the impact of noise in the transition matrix. For instance, in Section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a transition matrix permuted by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this approach does not accurately represent the problem of learning mixtures of HMM parameters. A more realistic approach would be to sample observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then learn all the parameters from the observed data. This would provide a more accurate understanding of how noise in observations and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and how challenging it is to depermute the matrix in the presence of noise.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but it lacks sufficient information to evaluate its quality. Specifically, the details of the mixture of HMMs used in the experiment are unclear. The actual parameters of the transition and observation matrices are crucial in assessing the difficulty of learning the model from data. Providing more information about these parameters, possibly in an appendix, would be beneficial.

6. In Table 1, it is unclear why EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone. Since EM is expected to improve the results, this discrepancy requires further explanation.

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrect, and it should be K instead of 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small and should be increased for better readability.

The paper presents some interesting ideas and good theoretical results. However, the practical applicability of the method to real datasets is a concern.
