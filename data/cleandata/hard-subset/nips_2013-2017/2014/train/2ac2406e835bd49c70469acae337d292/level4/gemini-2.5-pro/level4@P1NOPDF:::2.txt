This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The key insight is to formulate the MHMM as a single, larger HMM, which allows a standard spectral learning algorithm (Anandkumar et al., 2012) to be applied directly. To address the resulting parameter permutation problem, the authors introduce a novel stabilization-based solution that relies on the assumption that each component HMM has a unique stationary distribution (i.e., its transition matrix has a single eigenvalue of 1).

The proposed scheme for resolving parameter permutation is intuitive and interesting, and the analysis of estimation noise, which leads to an intriguing use of eigenvalues in Equation (7), is a valuable contribution. However, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form. The presentation suffers from unclear notation and an insufficient discussion of related work; for instance, the idea of using spectral estimates to initialize EM has been explored previously (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013). Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is quite basic, and the presentation of results needs significant improvement.

Specific comments:

*   The terms in Corollary 1 are not clearly defined, which may confuse readers. For clarity, the authors should explicitly define `1_J^T` (presumably a row vector of ones of length J) and elaborate on the structure of `lim_{e→∞} Ā^e`. Specifically, it would be helpful to state that it is a JK x JK block-diagonal matrix where the columns within each J x J block are identical.

*   To improve the paper's clarity, it is recommended that all vectors and matrices are defined along with their dimensions when first introduced.

*   The experimental results are not presented clearly. Figure 4 is difficult to interpret and should be replaced with a table for a more direct comparison. More importantly, the performance of the EM baseline reported in Table 1 seems unusually poor. Typically, EM with multiple random initializations, given enough iterations, is a strong baseline that often outperforms spectral methods, despite its susceptibility to local optima. It is standard practice to run EM multiple times and report the result from the best run (in terms of likelihood or accuracy), not the average. The authors should include this result to provide a more conventional and challenging baseline.

*   A minor suggestion for improving readability is to adopt the established notation from prior work, such as Hsu et al. (2009). For example, using `T` for transition matrices and `n, m` for the number of observation and hidden states, respectively, would align the paper with community standards and make it easier for readers to follow.
