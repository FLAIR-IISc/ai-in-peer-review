Summary: The authors consider the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models. They propose a learning approach based on the Method of Moments (MoM), leveraging the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be expressed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. They address the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation using spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the individual HMM parameters.

Major Comments:

1. I found this paper to be very well written for the most part. The experimental results section could be fleshed out to provide more detailed analysis and discussion.

2. The authors exploit the equivalence between a mixture of HMMs and a single HMM. However, a key motivation for employing a mixture of HMMs, rather than a single HMM, often stems from the inherent sparsity of the block-diagonal structure. This sparsity is particularly crucial when dealing with large-scale problems with limited training data. The proposed algorithm, however, relies on first accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and *then* recovering the block-diagonal structure via permutation reversal. This approach appears impractical for many real-world scenarios. Do the authors have insights into the conditions under which their algorithm would be most appropriate, and when it might be less suitable? 

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be strictly sparse. Is there a way to guarantee that the transition matrix learned using the proposed spectral algorithm will be (or is close to) sparse? It’s conceivable that numerous full transition matrix HMMs could approximate the desired sparse, block-diagonal model. It’s unclear whether the matrix learned via the referenced methods [1, 2] will exhibit sufficient sparsity, especially with even modest levels of noise.

4. I am concerned that the experimental results may not accurately reflect the impact of noise inherent in real-world transition matrices. Specifically, in Section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a transition matrix perturbed by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this is a paper focused on learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not merely depermuting matrices. A more relevant evaluation would involve sampling observation data directly from a mixture of HMMs and then attempting to learn all parameters from this observed data. This would provide a more realistic assessment of how observational noise and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of depermutation.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address this concern, but insufficient detail is provided for a thorough evaluation. Specifically, more information is needed regarding the characteristics of the mixture of HMMs used. The actual parameters of the transition and observation matrices significantly influence the ease (or difficulty) of learning the model from data. Providing these details – perhaps in an appendix – would strengthen the experimental analysis.

6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with the spectral learning method sometimes perform *worse* than spectral learning alone? Shouldn't EM always refine the initial estimate and improve the results?

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrect; it should be K, not 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small, hindering readability.

Overall, the paper presents some interesting ideas and solid theoretical results. However, I am cautious about the practical applicability of the method to real-world datasets given the potential challenges related to sparsity and noise.

Arguments for acceptance:

*   The paper provides a novel approach to learning MHMMs based on MoM, offering a potentially computationally efficient alternative to EM.
*   The theoretical analysis is strong and provides justification for the proposed method.
*   The authors identify and address the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   The assumptions about sparsity may not hold in many real-world applications.
*   The experiments, while well-conducted, do not fully demonstrate the robustness of the method to realistic noise levels and data limitations.
*   The practical benefits of the method compared to existing approaches are not convincingly established.
