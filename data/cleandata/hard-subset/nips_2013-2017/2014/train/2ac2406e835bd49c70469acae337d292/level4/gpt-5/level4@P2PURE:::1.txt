Summary:
The authors study the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They first propose using a spectral learning algorithm to estimate parameters of a single HMM, and then introduce a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:
1. The paper is generally very well written. The experimental results section, however, should be expanded; in particular, additional detail and analysis would be helpful.
2. The method relies on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be expressed as a single HMM. A key motivation for learning a mixture rather than fitting a single HMM directly is that the mixture’s block-diagonal transition structure is sparse. This sparsity can be imposed in problems that are too large for a full model, especially when the training set is small. In contrast, the proposed algorithm first requires accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and then reversing the permutation to recover the block-diagonal structure. For most real-world problems this seems infeasible. Do the authors have suggestions for when their algorithm is appropriate and when it is not?
3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is there any way to guarantee that the transition matrix learned by the suggested spectral algorithms will be (approximately) sparse? I suspect there are many HMMs with dense transition matrices that are close to the sparse, block-diagonal model the authors seek. It is not obvious that the matrix learned via [1,2] will remain sparse under small amounts of noise.
4. I am concerned that the experimental results do not reflect realistic noise in the transition matrix. For example, in Section 4.1 the algorithm is applied to a transition matrix that has been permuted and then perturbed with noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution—but the paper is about learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not just depermuting matrices. Shouldn’t the authors sample observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then recover all parameters from the observed data? This would more accurately show how observational noise and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and how difficult depermutation becomes in the presence of such noise.
5. The experiment in Section 4.2 moves in the right direction, but there is far too little information to assess it. I have no idea what the underlying mixture of HMMs looked like. The actual transition and observation parameters matter greatly for evaluating how easy or hard it is to learn the model from data. Please provide these specifics (an appendix would suffice if space is limited).
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with spectral learning ever perform worse than spectral learning alone? Shouldn’t EM only improve the results?

Minor Comments:
1. I believe the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix in the proof in Section 3.2.1 should be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers in the figures are much too small.

The paper contains interesting ideas and strong theoretical results. I am concerned, however, about the method’s likelihood of succeeding on real datasets in practice.
