### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The core idea is to reframe an MHMM with K components as a single, larger HMM whose transition matrix has a block-diagonal structure. This insight allows the authors to apply existing Method of Moments (MoM) algorithms for HMMs to learn the global model parameters. However, this process introduces a permutation ambiguity, mixing the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of the paper is an elegant method to resolve this ambiguity. By analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix, specifically by raising it to a high power, the authors show that its columns naturally cluster according to the underlying HMM components. This allows for the recovery of a de-permutation mapping, and consequently, the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also provides an analysis for handling estimation noise and a method for determining the number of HMM components from the data. The approach is validated on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its computational advantages over the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper addresses the important and practical problem of efficiently learning MHMMs, which are often limited by the computational cost of EM on large datasets. The proposed spectral approach presents a compelling alternative.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the De-permutation Method:** The primary contribution—resolving the permutation ambiguity by clustering the columns of the exponentiated global transition matrix—is both novel and intuitive. It provides a clear and well-motivated solution to a fundamental challenge in applying MoM to mixture models.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical development is solid. The paper clearly establishes the equivalence between an MHMM and a structured HMM (Lemma 1) and leverages this to derive the de-permutation strategy in the noiseless case (Corollary 1). The analysis is thoughtfully extended to the more realistic noisy setting, leading to an interesting method for model selection (estimating K) based on the concept of "spectral longevity" (Lemma 3).

3.  **Potential for Significant Impact:** The proposed algorithm is computationally much faster than EM, as its core factorization step is independent of the dataset size once moments are computed. This makes it highly suitable for large-scale sequence clustering tasks. Furthermore, the experiments demonstrate its utility as a high-quality initialization strategy for EM, potentially helping to overcome EM's well-known sensitivity to local optima.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper's presentation could be improved. Several matrices and vectors are used without their dimensions being explicitly defined at their first appearance. This makes parts of the theoretical exposition difficult to follow. Adhering to a more rigorous notational standard would greatly enhance readability.

2.  **Contextualization with Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a broader discussion of related work. In particular, the idea of using spectral estimates to initialize EM has been explored in other contexts (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, ICML 2013, for mixtures of linear regressions). Acknowledging and citing such work is important for properly contextualizing the paper's contribution, especially concerning the experiments where this initialization strategy is used.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the method's promise, they could be more thorough.
    *   The performance of randomly initialized EM reported in Table 1 seems unusually low. For a fair comparison, it is standard practice to run EM from multiple random starts and report the result corresponding to the highest log-likelihood. It is unclear if this was done, and clarifying this point would strengthen the empirical claims.
    *   The results in Figure 4 are hard to read and compare. Presenting this information in a table would allow for a much clearer assessment of the trade-offs between accuracy and computation time.
    *   The real-data experiment required a feature transformation to satisfy the dimensionality constraint `MK < L`. A brief discussion on the practical implications of this constraint and the sensitivity of the method to it would be valuable.

**Detailed Comments for Authors:**

*   **Clarity:**
    *   Please define the dimensions of all vectors and matrices when they are introduced (e.g., `1_M` in Eq. 1).
    *   In Corollary 1, it would be helpful to elaborate on the block structure of the resulting matrix `lim_{e→∞} Ā^e`. Explaining that it is a `MK x MK` matrix where the columns within each of the K `MK x M` blocks are identical and correspond to the stationary distribution of a component HMM would aid reader understanding.
    *   The suggestion to replace Figure 4 with tables is strongly encouraged for clarity.

*   **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   Please clarify the protocol for the baseline EM algorithm in Table 1. Was the best result from multiple random initializations reported? Comparing against a strong EM baseline is crucial.

*   **Minor Suggestions:**
    *   To improve accessibility for readers familiar with the foundational work in this area, consider aligning your notation with that used in seminal papers like Hsu et al. (2012), for instance, using `T` for transition matrices and `n, m` for observation and state dimensions.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clever and well-founded core idea. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation and the comprehensiveness of the experimental comparison, which can be addressed in a final version. The proposed method is a valuable contribution to the literature on spectral learning for latent variable models.
