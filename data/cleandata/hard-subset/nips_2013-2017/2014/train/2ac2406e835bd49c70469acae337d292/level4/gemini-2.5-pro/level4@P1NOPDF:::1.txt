**Summary**

The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They propose a two-step approach: first, a spectral algorithm is used to learn the parameters of a single, large HMM that represents the mixture. Second, they introduce a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the learned transition matrix, thereby recovering the underlying block-diagonal structure of the individual component HMMs.

**Major Comments**

1.  The paper is generally well-written, but the experimental section could be strengthened with more detail.

2.  The method relies on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single, larger HMM. However, a primary motivation for modeling a problem as an HMM mixture, rather than a single large HMM, is to leverage the sparse, block-diagonal structure of the combined transition matrix. This structure is particularly advantageous for large-scale problems where training data may be limited. The proposed algorithm, however, first requires an accurate estimation of the full, dense HMM parameters before recovering the block-diagonal structure. This initial step seems untenable for many real-world problems where such a dense model would be too large to estimate reliably. The paper would benefit from a discussion on the practical settings where this approach is expected to be effective and those for which it might be less suitable.

3.  Related to the previous point, the transition matrix estimated from finite data is unlikely to be perfectly block-diagonal (up to a permutation). I am concerned that in the space of all HMMs, many models with dense transition matrices could be statistically close to the target block-diagonal model. It is not obvious that the estimator from [1,2] will yield a matrix that is merely a noisy version of the permuted block-diagonal target, as opposed to a fundamentally different, dense matrix that happens to be nearby. Are there theoretical guarantees that the transition matrix learned by the chosen spectral methods will be close to a permuted block-diagonal matrix?

4.  A significant concern is that the experimental setup may not reflect the challenges of real-world applications. For instance, the experiment in Section 4.1 applies the algorithm to a transition matrix that is directly permuted and then perturbed with Dirichlet noise. However, the core problem is learning the HMM parameters from observational data, not just de-permuting a known (but noisy) matrix. A more realistic and convincing experiment would involve generating observation sequences from a known mixture of HMMs, applying a spectral method to learn the full HMM parameters from this data, and then using the proposed algorithm to recover the block structure. This end-to-end evaluation would provide a clearer picture of how observational noise and finite data samples affect the estimated transition matrix and, consequently, the difficulty of the de-permutation task.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address the end-to-end evaluation I hoped to see, but it lacks sufficient detail to properly assess the results. Specifically, the paper does not describe the ground-truth parameters of the HMM mixture used. The specific parameters of the transition and observation matrices are crucial, as they heavily influence the difficulty of the learning problem. The authors should provide these details, perhaps in an appendix if space is a concern, to allow for a thorough evaluation of the experimental findings.

6.  In Table 1, the results show that EM initialized with the spectral solution sometimes performs worse than the spectral solution alone. This is counter-intuitive, as one would expect EM to either improve upon or, at worst, maintain the quality of its initialization, given that it monotonically increases the data likelihood. Could the authors provide an explanation for this phenomenon?

**Minor Comments**

1.  In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix appears to be '2' but should likely be 'K'.

2.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small to be easily legible.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper presents interesting ideas and valuable theoretical results. However, I have significant concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed method to real-world datasets, primarily because it relies on first learning a large, dense model which may not be feasible in the data-limited regimes where mixture models are most useful. The experimental validation should also be made more rigorous to better reflect the end-to-end learning problem.
