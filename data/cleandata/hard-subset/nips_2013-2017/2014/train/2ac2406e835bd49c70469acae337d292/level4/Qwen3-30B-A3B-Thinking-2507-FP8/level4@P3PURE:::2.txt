

This paper proposes a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). It demonstrates that an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012) can be directly applied to MHMMs by treating the MHMM as a special case of an HMM. The key contribution is a stabilization-based approach to resolve parameter permutation, achieved under the assumption that each HMM's transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue of 1.

The notation requires clarification. The authors should explicitly define all vectors and matrices with their dimensions upon first introduction. For instance, the term $1_J^T$ (a row vector of length $J$ with all entries 1) in Corollary 1 should be defined, and the structure of $\lim_{e \to \infty} \bar{A}^e$ (which is a $JK \times JK$ block diagonal matrix with identical columns within each $J \times J$ block) merits elaboration. Additionally, the paper should acknowledge relevant prior work; specifically, initializing EM with spectral estimates has been explored in Chaganty and Liang (2013), and this connection should be referenced.

The experimental evaluation is relatively simple. Results should be presented in tabular format rather than the graphical format of Figure 4, which is difficult to interpret. Furthermore, the notably poor performance of EM in Table 1 warrants explanation. Typically, even with random initialization, EM converges to solutions that outperform spectral methods after sufficient iterations (despite local optima concerns). The authors should report the best (not average) accuracy achieved by EM across multiple runs to provide a fair comparison.

Minor notational inconsistencies should be addressed to align with established conventions (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009): use $T$ for transition matrices instead of $A$, and employ $n$ and $m$ for the numbers of observation and hidden states, respectively, rather than $L$ and $J$.

Despite these issues, the proposed stabilization step for parameter permutation is conceptually sound and well-motivated. The analysis of estimation noise (Equation 7) and its utilization of eigenvalues represents an interesting technical contribution. The core algorithm—applying a spectral HMM learning method followed by the novel stabilization step—is clearly articulated.
