

This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), leveraging the observation that an MHMM can be viewed as a special case of a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors adapt existing spectral HMM learning methods (Anandkumar et al., 2012) to MHMMs but address a critical challenge: the permutation ambiguity in the estimated global parameters. They resolve this by exploiting the spectral properties of the global transition matrix, particularly the eigenvalue structure under noise, to recover the individual HMM parameters. The approach is theoretically grounded with lemmas analyzing the eigenvalue behavior and a practical algorithm for depermutation. The paper demonstrates the method on synthetic data and a real-world handwritten character dataset, showing competitive accuracy with EM while offering significant computational advantages, and highlights its utility as an initialization strategy for EM.

**Strengths:**  
The core technical contribution—resolving permutation ambiguity via spectral analysis—is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis (Lemmas 2–3, Conjecture 1) provides a clear framework for handling noise in the transition matrix, particularly the insightful use of "spectral longevity" (Equation 7) to estimate the number of clusters. The algorithm is cleanly designed and the experiments are well-structured, with the real data results (Table 1) effectively demonstrating the method’s practical value as an EM initialization. The computational efficiency claim is compelling, as spectral learning scales independently of data size—unlike EM’s O(NT) per iteration complexity. The paper also appropriately contextualizes its work within recent spectral learning literature (Anandkumar et al., Hsu et al.).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
1. **Clarity and Notation:** The paper’s notation requires significant refinement. Key terms like 1<sub>J</sub><sup>T</sup> (a row vector of ones) and the structure of lim<sub>e→∞</sub> Ā<sup>e</sup> (which is block-diagonal with identical columns per block) are introduced without sufficient explanation (Corollary 1). The authors should define all vectors/matrices with dimensions at first use (e.g., "1<sub>M</sub> ∈ ℝ<sup>M</sup> denotes a row vector of ones"). Consistency with established HMM notation (e.g., using T for transition matrices instead of A, as in Hsu et al., 2009) would improve readability.  

2. **Related Work:** The discussion lacks awareness of prior work on spectral initialization for EM. Specifically, Chaganty & Liang (2013) already explore spectral methods for initializing mixture models (including HMMs), which directly relates to the paper’s real-data results (Table 1). This omission weakens the novelty claim. The authors should explicitly contrast their permutation-resolution step with existing initialization techniques.  

3. **Experiments:**  
   - Figure 4’s scatter plot format obscures numerical trends; results should be presented in a table (e.g., "Clustering accuracy (%) for EM vs. spectral method across sequence lengths (20–200) and sequence counts (1–100)").  
   - Table 1 reports average EM accuracy over random initializations, but the standard practice is to report the *best* accuracy (over 5 restarts), as stated in the draft. The current results (e.g., spectral: 78.5% vs. random EM: 76.2%) may understate EM’s potential.  
   - The synthetic experiments (Section 4.1) lack error bars or statistical significance testing, making it hard to assess robustness to noise.  

4. **Theoretical Rigor:** Conjecture 1’s justification cites a sample complexity bound from Anandkumar et al. (2012) for mixture models, but the MHMM case requires careful adaptation. The authors should clarify why the bound applies directly or state it as a plausible extension (e.g., "as the estimation procedure mirrors that of mixture models, we expect similar sample complexity").  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a solid contribution to spectral learning for structured latent variable models. The permutation resolution method is technically sound and well-motivated, with compelling empirical results showing computational advantages and utility as an EM initializer. While the notation and related work require strengthening, these are fixable in revision. The significance is clear: it enables scalable MHMM learning for large datasets where EM is infeasible. The theoretical analysis (despite Conjecture 1’s informal treatment) provides a foundation for future work on noisy spectral methods.  

*Pro (acceptance):* Novel permutation solution via spectral analysis; strong computational efficiency claims; effective real-data validation as EM initialization.  
*Con (revisions needed):* Notation inconsistencies; missing contrast with Chaganty & Liang (2013); experimental presentation (Figure 4, Table 1) should follow standard practices.
