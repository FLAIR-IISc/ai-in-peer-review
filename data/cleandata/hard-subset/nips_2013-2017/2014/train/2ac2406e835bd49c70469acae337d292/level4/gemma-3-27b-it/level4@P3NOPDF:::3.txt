This paper introduces a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The core idea builds upon existing spectral techniques, such as the Hsu, Kakade & Zhang algorithm, but addresses a critical challenge: the recovered transition matrix is subject to arbitrary permutations of the true component order, effectively mixing the components. The authors propose a “depermutation” algorithm designed to resolve this issue, and their experimental results suggest it performs reasonably well in practice.

The strength of this work lies in identifying and tackling the permutation problem inherent in applying spectral methods to mixture models. While the underlying spectral decomposition is conceptually straightforward, the depermutation step is a non-trivial addition that significantly improves the utility of the approach. The paper clearly articulates this problem and proposes a practical solution.

However, the presentation could be significantly improved. The connection to prior work, particularly in spectral methods for mixture models, is not sufficiently explored. For example, the paper would benefit from a discussion of Chaganty and Liang’s work ("Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions," ICML 2013), which shares similarities in leveraging spectral decomposition for mixture learning. Placing this work within a broader context would highlight its novel contributions more effectively and help readers understand where it fits within the existing literature.

Furthermore, the paper suffers from a number of minor presentation issues. There are several instances of grammatical errors and typos, hindering readability. For instance, the phrase “For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy” requires correction. A thorough proofread is strongly recommended before publication. While these errors are not substantial enough to invalidate the work, they detract from its overall polish and credibility. 

The experimental evaluation is adequate, but could be strengthened. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their depermutation algorithm, the choice of datasets and baselines could be more comprehensive.  A more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s performance under various conditions – such as different numbers of components, varying degrees of noise, or different HMM structures – would provide greater insight into its robustness and limitations.  It would also be helpful to analyze the computational complexity of the depermutation algorithm, and discuss its scalability to larger datasets and more complex mixture models.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable contribution by addressing the permutation problem in spectral learning of HMM mixtures. The depermutation algorithm appears promising, but the paper needs significant improvement in terms of clarity, contextualization, and polish. Specifically, the authors should:

*   **Expand the related work section:** Provide a more thorough discussion of relevant literature, including Chaganty and Liang (2013), and clearly articulate the novelty of their approach.
*   **Proofread carefully:** Eliminate all grammatical errors and typos.
*   **Strengthen the experimental evaluation:** Consider using a wider range of datasets and baselines, and provide a more detailed analysis of performance under varying conditions.
*   **Discuss computational complexity:** Analyze the scalability of the depermutation algorithm.



Despite these concerns, the core idea is sound and the potential impact is notable. With revisions addressing the points above, this paper could be a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop.
