Summary:
The paper studies learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The approach first applies a spectral learning algorithm to estimate the parameters of a single HMM that represents the mixture, then resolves the state-permutation ambiguity to recover the underlying block-diagonal structure of the transition matrix.

Major Comments:
1. The paper is generally well written, but the experimental section needs more substance. Please provide clearer descriptions of datasets, parameter settings, and evaluation protocols, along with sensitivity analyses (e.g., sample size, noise level, number of components, and state-space size).

2. The method hinges on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be recast as a single HMM. A key motivation for using mixtures rather than a single model is to exploit the sparsity of the block-diagonal transition structure, particularly in data-scarce, high-dimensional settings. However, your pipeline first learns a full HMM and only then attempts to recover block structure by de-permuting states. In practical scenarios, accurately estimating a full, dense HMM before exploiting sparsity seems unrealistic. Please discuss when your algorithm is expected to be appropriate (e.g., regimes of data size, noise, number of components, separation conditions) and when it is not. Are there assumptions (e.g., identifiability, mixing/separation, or eigenvalue gaps) under which the initial spectral estimate is guaranteed to be accurate enough for reliable de-permutation?

3. In practice, the learned transition matrix from spectral estimation may not be sparse. Can your approach encourage or guarantee near-sparsity in the recovered transition matrix? For example, could one incorporate a sparsity-promoting step (e.g., thresholding, regularization, or constrained factorization) into or after spectral estimation? It seems plausible that many dense HMMs lie close (in likelihood or operator norm) to sparse block-diagonal models; in that case, small observational noise may still yield dense estimates via [1,2]. Please clarify conditions under which the spectral estimates are provably close to a sparse, block-diagonal target.

4. I am concerned that the experiments understate the difficulty induced by noise. In Section 4.1, you test de-permutation on transition matrices perturbed by Dirichlet noise, but the overall problem is to learn mixtures of HMMs from observations, not just to reorder a noisy matrix. A more faithful evaluation would simulate observation sequences from a mixture of HMMs, learn parameters via the proposed spectral method, then attempt de-permutation on the learned transition matrix. This would capture the propagation of observation noise, finite-sample effects, and model mismatch into the transition estimates and test the robustness of the de-permutation step. Please consider adding such experiments.

5. Section 4.2 moves in this direction but lacks crucial detail. The difficulty of the task depends heavily on the true transition and emission parameters (e.g., block sizes, inter-block transition probabilities, emission overlaps, and component separation). Please report the exact parameters of the mixture components, or at least summaries such as sparsity levels, minimal inter-block mass, spectral gaps, and emission confusion, possibly in an appendix. Also specify sequence lengths, number of sequences, and how components are mixed. Without this information it is hard to assess the realism and difficulty of the setup or to reproduce results.

6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with spectral estimates sometimes underperform pure spectral learning? Typically, EM should not degrade performance when properly initialized and regularized. Possible explanations include overfitting, local optima, insufficient regularization, poorly chosen stopping criteria, or mismatch between the likelihood and evaluation metric. Please clarify your EM configuration (number of restarts, step sizes/damping, regularization, constraints), provide learning curves, and discuss diagnostics. If EM is harmful under certain regimes, highlighting and explaining that phenomenon would be valuable.

Minor Comments:
1. In Section 3.2.1, in the proof, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix should be K, not 2.
2. Figures are difficult to read; please increase font sizes and improve label clarity.

Overall Assessment:
The paper presents interesting ideas with solid theoretical grounding, and the permutation-resolution step for recovering block-diagonal structure is appealing. However, I am concerned about practical applicability: the need to first estimate a full HMM, the lack of sparsity guarantees, and experimental setups that do not fully reflect end-to-end learning from observations. Strengthening the empirical section with realistic simulations from mixtures, detailed reporting of ground-truth parameters, and robustness analyses would significantly improve the paper’s credibility and impact.
