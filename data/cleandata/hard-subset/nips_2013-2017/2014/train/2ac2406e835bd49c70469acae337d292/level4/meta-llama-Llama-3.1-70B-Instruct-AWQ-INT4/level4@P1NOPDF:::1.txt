Summary: The authors consider the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models. They first suggest using a spectral learning algorithm to learn a set of parameters for a hidden Markov model, and then provide a method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-written for the most part, but the experimental results section could be fleshed out. In particular, the authors could provide more details about the experiments to facilitate better understanding and evaluation of their method.

2. The authors rely on the fact that a mixture of Hidden Markov Models can be expressed as a single HMM. However, one of the main reasons for learning a mixture of HMMs as opposed to a single HMM directly is that the block diagonal structure of the mixture of HMMs is sparse. This sparseness may be imposed on problems that are deemed too large to learn with a full model, especially if the problem is large and the amount of training data is small. Nevertheless, the authors' algorithm relies on first accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and then reversing the permutation to find the block diagonal structure. For most real-world problems, this seems completely impractical. Do the authors have suggestions for when their algorithm might be appropriate and when it might not be?

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is there any way to guarantee that the transition matrix learned by the spectral algorithms suggested in this paper will be (close to) sparse? It is possible that there may be many HMM models with full transition matrices that are close to the sparse model with block diagonal structure that the authors are looking for. It is not at all obvious that the matrix learned via [1,2] will be sparse even with small amounts of noise.

4. I am concerned that the experimental results do not accurately reflect the reality of noise in the transition matrix. For example, in Section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a transition matrix that has been permuted by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this is a paper about learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not just depermuting matrices. A more realistic experiment would involve sampling observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then learning all of the parameters back from the observed data. This would provide a more accurate idea of how noise in observations and small quantities of data are actually reflected in the learned transition matrix, and how hard it is to depermute the matrix in the presence of this noise.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but there is not enough information to evaluate the quality of the experiment. In particular, the specifics of the mixture of HMMs used in the experiment are not provided. The actual parameters of the transition and observation matrix matter significantly for evaluating how easy or hard it is to learn the model from data. The authors could provide more details about these parameters, possibly in an appendix if space is a concern.

6. In Table 1, EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone. However, one would expect EM to only improve the results. This discrepancy requires further explanation.

Minor Comments:

1. I believe that the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix in the proof in Section 3.2.1 should be K, not 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small.

The paper presents some interesting ideas and good theoretical results. However, I am concerned that the method may not work well on real datasets in practice.
