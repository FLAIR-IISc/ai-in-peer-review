

The paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a key challenge in the literature: resolving permutation ambiguity in the method-of-moments (MoM) estimation of MHMM parameters. The authors recognize that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling application of existing spectral HMM learning algorithms (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012). However, this approach yields parameters permuted across components, preventing direct recovery of individual HMMs. The core contribution is a novel depermutation algorithm leveraging spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the block structure. Specifically, they establish conditions under which the number of clusters \(K\) can be identified via spectral longevity (Definition 3) and demonstrate that a depermutation mapping \(\tilde{P}\) can be estimated from the eigendecomposition of the noisy transition matrix. Theoretical results (Lemmas 2–3, Conjecture 1) provide justification for this approach under sufficient data and low noise.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality & Technical Contribution:** The paper identifies a non-trivial gap in applying MoM to MHMMs (noted by Hsu et al. as an open problem) and provides a theoretically grounded solution. The spectral longevity criterion (Definition 3) is a novel and well-motivated extension to existing spectral methods for mixture models (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, ICML 2013, which addresses mixtures of linear regressions). This distinguishes the work from prior MoM approaches for simpler models.  
2. **Significance:** MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering, but EM-based learning is computationally prohibitive for large datasets. The proposed method offers a theoretically sound, scalable alternative with computational complexity independent of data size. Experiments (Section 4) confirm it achieves comparable accuracy to EM while being significantly faster (Fig. 4), and it serves as an effective initialization for EM (Table 1).  
3. **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear model definitions (Section 2) and a logical progression from the MHMM-as-HMM equivalence (Lemma 1) to the depermutation solution (Section 3.2). The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is concise and executable.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Theoretical Rigor:** Conjecture 1 lacks a formal proof, relying on a reference to a mixture model result (Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012) that is not directly applicable to MHMMs. The justification for the error bound \(\| \mathbf{A}_r - \mathbf{A}_r^\dagger \|_F = O(1/\sqrt{T_N})\) is insufficiently justified for the MHMM context. The paper should either provide a full proof or clarify the limitations of the cited bound.  
2. **Experimental Evaluation:** The real-data experiment (Section 4.3) uses a cubic polynomial feature transformation to satisfy \(MK < L\) (a MoM requirement), but this transformation is not adequately justified or analyzed. The dataset has \(L=3\) originally, and transforming to \(L=10\) may alter the problem structure, potentially masking limitations of the method. The comparison to EM with MoM initialization (Table 1) is limited to \(K=2\), leaving open questions about performance at higher \(K\).  
3. **Presentation:** Minor grammatical errors and unclear phrasing persist (e.g., "For each \(e\), identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy" in Section 3.2.2). The spectral longevity definition (Definition 3) is mathematically precise but could be clarified with an intuitive explanation. The paper should explicitly contrast their depermutation approach with alternatives (e.g., brute-force search or clustering-based methods) to emphasize novelty.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a technically sound contribution that advances spectral learning for latent variable models. The core insight—using spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity in MHMMs—is novel and well-motivated, addressing a clear gap in the literature. The computational advantages over EM are compelling, and the real-data validation (though limited in scope) supports practical utility. However, the theoretical justification for the depermutation algorithm requires strengthening, and the experimental design could better address the impact of data transformations and cluster count. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality (sound technical approach, clear presentation) and significance (scalable solution for a common problem), though minor revisions would elevate it.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
- *Pro:* Novel solution to permutation ambiguity; strong computational efficiency; clear theoretical foundation for MHMM-as-HMM equivalence; significant speed-accuracy trade-off.  
- *Con:* Conjecture 1 lacks rigorous proof; real-data experiment lacks justification for feature transformation; limited cluster count evaluation; minor presentation errors.  

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to spectral learning and latent variable modeling, but the theoretical gaps and experimental limitations prevent a strong accept. Addressing the proof sketch in Conjecture 1 and clarifying the data transformation in Section 4.3 would substantially improve the work. The authors have already engaged with relevant prior work (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, 2013), but could further distinguish their spectral longevity criterion from existing mixture model techniques.
