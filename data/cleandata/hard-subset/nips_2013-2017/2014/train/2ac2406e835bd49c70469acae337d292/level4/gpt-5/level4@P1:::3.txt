Summary
This paper proposes a spectral, method-of-moments approach to learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors apply the standard spectral algorithm for HMMs (Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang) to recover global parameters, which are identifiable only up to a permutation. They then introduce a depermutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix to recover the parameters of the individual HMM components and, under certain spectral conditions, to estimate the number of mixture components K. Experiments on synthetic data and a real trajectory dataset suggest the approach is computationally efficient and yields reasonable clustering accuracy; it can also serve as an effective initializer for EM.

Strengths
- Clear reduction of MHMM learning to an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix.
- Practical depermutation method that appears to work well empirically.
- Computational advantages over EM are apparent, and the method seems to scale with data size.
- Real-data results indicate utility both standalone and as an initialization for EM.

Weaknesses
- Beyond the depermutation step, the method is largely a straightforward variant of the Hsu–Kakade–Zhang spectral algorithm, limiting novelty.
- The presentation contains a number of typos and small grammatical errors that detract from clarity; the paper would benefit from careful proofreading.
- The work could be better contextualized relative to recent spectral methods for mixtures, e.g., Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013).

Quality
- The core technical idea—using spectral properties to undo the permutation induced by spectral estimation—appears sound and is supported empirically. The depermutation “seems to work reasonably well” in the reported experiments.
- However, the theoretical justification for the noisy setting relies on conditions and conjectures that are not fully established. While this may be acceptable for a primarily empirical contribution, clarifying assumptions and tightening the analysis would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but several typos and phrasing issues impede flow. For example: “For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy.” Please proofread thoroughly.
- It would help to more explicitly distinguish what is inherited from prior spectral HMM work versus what is new (the depermutation scheme and K estimation), and to streamline notation where possible.

Originality
- The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is standard, and the use of spectral HMM learning is well established. The main novelty lies in the depermutation strategy and the heuristic for estimating K from spectral longevity. This is a useful but incremental contribution.

Significance
- If robust in practice, the proposed depermutation step addresses an important practical obstacle in applying spectral methods to MHMMs, making the overall approach attractive for large datasets where EM is expensive.
- The impact would be higher with stronger theoretical guarantees or broader comparisons, but the practical value is evident.

Context and related work
- The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of related spectral methods for mixtures beyond HMMs, for example: Chaganty and Liang, “Spectral Experts for Estimating Mixtures of Linear Regressions,” ICML 2013, and other recent developments in tensor/moment methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMMs.
- Simple and effective depermutation approach with promising empirical results.
- Potentially useful as an initializer for EM in real applications.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond adapting standard spectral HMM techniques and adding a depermutation step.
- Presentation issues (typos, minor clarity problems).
- Theoretical treatment in the noisy setting is tentative and would benefit from stronger analysis.

Recommendation
Borderline. The paper offers a practical and computationally appealing method with a sensible depermutation procedure, but the contribution is incremental and the presentation needs polishing and better contextualization. Proofreading and a clearer positioning relative to recent spectral literature (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, ICML 2013) are recommended.
