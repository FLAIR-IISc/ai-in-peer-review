Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) approach to learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M latent states, can be represented as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. Using this equivalence, the authors apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012/2013) to estimate a “global” HMM. The central challenge is the permutation ambiguity intrinsic to spectral methods: the learned global parameters are correct only up to an unknown permutation of the MK latent states, which mixes states across MHMM components. The paper proposes to resolve this ambiguity via spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the block-diagonal transition matrix reveal K clusters corresponding to components; in the noisy case, the authors propose a rank-K reconstruction and an eigenvalue “longevity” criterion (Eq. 7) to identify K and recover a de-permutation mapping.

Relative to prior work, the reduction of MHMM to an HMM is standard, and spectral learning for HMMs is well-established. The novelty here lies in the de-permutation strategy and the use of eigenvalue longevity to select K. The paper also explores using spectral estimates as an initialization for EM; this idea has precedents in the spectral learning literature (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013, for mixtures of linear regressions), and it would help to more clearly position the contribution against such uses.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: recast MHMM as block-diagonal HMM, then exploit spectral structure to undo permutations.
- Potential computational benefits: once moments are computed, parameter recovery is data-size independent, making the approach attractive for large datasets.
- Practical contribution: proposes a simple post-processing (clustering of columns after rank-K reconstruction) to unmix components, and shows it can work even with moderate noise.
- The eigenvalue longevity heuristic is intriguing and could be broadly useful for model selection in related spectral methods.

Weaknesses
- Theory is incomplete: key steps (Conjecture 1, the use of sample complexity bounds borrowed from other models, and claims around noise tolerance) lack formal proofs. Assumptions needed for identifiability and stability (e.g., ergodicity, aperiodicity, spectral gaps) are not fully spelled out.
- Notation and clarity issues hinder readability. Several vectors/matrices are introduced without dimensions; some symbols (e.g., 1J vs 1M) appear inconsistently.
- Experimental evaluation is basic. Synthetic settings are small; the real-data experiment is limited in scope; and EM baselines are not as strong or thoroughly reported as they should be (e.g., best-of-many restarts, convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to initialization).
- The requirement MK < L for the MoM estimator forces a feature expansion in the real-data experiment; this deserves discussion about the validity and potential bias introduced by the transformation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction and the noiseless de-permutation argument are sound. However, results under noise rely on unproven conjectures and heuristic criteria. The sample complexity claim cites bounds from a different (but related) spectral setting; a tailored bound, even if weaker, would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical support: The synthetic experiments show the de-permutation procedure can work at moderate sequence lengths. The real-data results suggest the spectral method is a good initializer for EM. Still, stronger baselines, more repetitions, and clearer reporting (tables with mean ± std and best over restarts) are needed.

Clarity
- Please define every vector/matrix on first use with dimensions. For example, 1J should be a length-J column vector of ones; 1J^T is its transpose.
- Corollary 1: explicitly state the block structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e as K blocks of size M×M where each block has identical columns proportional to the stationary distribution of the corresponding Ak. Make explicit that v̄k stacks vk in the kth block and zeros elsewhere.
- Consider aligning notation with Hsu et al. (2009): T for transitions, m/n for numbers of hidden/observed states; this would ease comparison and reuse by readers.
- Figures: Figure 4 is hard to read. Tabular summaries (e.g., accuracies and times across varying N and T) would be clearer.

Originality
- Recasting MHMM as an HMM is known. The novelty is the specific de-permutation via spectral clustering of columns in the rank-K reconstruction and the eigenvalue longevity statistic for selecting K. This is a modest but interesting contribution.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could make MHMM learning more scalable and provide a principled initialization for EM. However, absent stronger guarantees and broader experiments (including larger K, M, varying L; non-Gaussian emissions; unbalanced mixtures; different M across components), the impact is currently limited.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Assumptions: Clarify that each Ak is irreducible and aperiodic so that 1 is a simple eigenvalue and vk is unique. Discuss how close eigenvalues across clusters affect recoverability and the longevity heuristic.
- K estimation: Provide guidance on choosing γ and the exponent range for Eq. (7). Compare longevity against standard spectral gap heuristics or information criteria (BIC/AIC) where feasible.
- Complexity: Report time for (i) moment computation, (ii) spectral decomposition, (iii) de-permutation clustering, and (iv) EM iterations. Include memory costs.
- EM baseline: Report best-of-R restarts (e.g., R≥10), not just averages, and ensure sufficient iterations for convergence. Include initialization strategies (random, k-means on emissions, spectral) and show their effects.
- Real data: Justify the polynomial feature map and discuss whether the MK < L requirement can be relaxed (e.g., via multi-view features or tensor methods). Consider additional datasets (speech, motion capture) and discrete emissions.
- Unequal M: Comment on whether the approach extends to components with different numbers of states, and how to detect this case.
- Related work: Expand the discussion to include prior MHMM learning and clustering approaches (e.g., Smyth, 1997; variants using DTW baselines) and spectral initializations for latent-variable models (e.g., Chaganty & Liang, 2013) to better situate the contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple and potentially effective de-permutation strategy with an intuitive spectral rationale.
- Computationally appealing alternative to EM for large sequence datasets.
- Offers a practical initialization that can improve EM.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical foundations under noise are incomplete; key claims are conjectural.
- Limited empirical evaluation and somewhat weak baselines/reporting.
- Clarity/notation issues that impede understanding.

Overall assessment
This paper presents a neat and potentially useful idea for resolving permutation ambiguity when applying spectral learning to MHMMs. With stronger theoretical grounding, clearer exposition, and more thorough experiments and baselines, it could be a solid contribution. In its current form, I view it as promising but not yet fully convincing. I encourage the authors to revise along the lines above.
