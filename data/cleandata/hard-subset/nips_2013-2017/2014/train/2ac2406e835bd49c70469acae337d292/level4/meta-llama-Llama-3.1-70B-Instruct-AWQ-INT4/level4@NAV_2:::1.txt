The authors consider the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They first suggest using a spectral learning algorithm to learn a set of parameters for a hidden Markov model and then provide a method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

The main idea of the paper is to leverage the Method of Moments (MoM) to learn the parameters of a Mixture of HMMs (MHMM). The authors show that an MHMM can be expressed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. They then use an existing MoM algorithm to learn the parameters up to a permutation ambiguity. To resolve this ambiguity, they exploit the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to estimate a de-permutation mapping that enables them to recover the parameters of the individual HMMs.

The paper is well-written, and the experimental results section is informative. However, there are some concerns regarding the practicality of the proposed algorithm. One of the main reasons for learning a mixture of HMMs as opposed to a single HMM directly is that the block diagonal structure of the mixture of HMMs is sparse. However, the authors' algorithm relies on first accurately estimating the full HMM parameters, then reversing the permutation in order to find the block diagonal structure. This approach may not be feasible for large problems with limited training data.

The authors' experimental results do not fully address the issue of noise in the transition matrix. In Section 4.1, they apply their algorithm to a transition matrix that has been permuted by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this does not accurately reflect the reality of noise in the transition matrix when learning a mixture of HMMs from observed data.

In Section 4.2, the authors compare the clustering accuracies of their spectral learning approach with the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. However, the experimental setup is not fully described, making it difficult to evaluate the quality of the experiment. The actual parameters of the transition and observation matrix matter a lot for evaluating how easy or hard it is to learn the model from data.

The paper has some interesting ideas and good theoretical results. However, the practicality of the proposed algorithm is a concern. The authors should provide more insights into when their algorithm might be appropriate and when it might not be. Additionally, they should address the issue of noise in the transition matrix and provide more detailed experimental results.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to learning a mixture of HMMs using the Method of Moments.
* The authors provide a theoretical framework for resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix.
* The experimental results are informative and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

Weaknesses:

* The practicality of the proposed algorithm is a concern, particularly for large problems with limited training data.
* The experimental results do not fully address the issue of noise in the transition matrix.
* The experimental setup in Section 4.2 is not fully described, making it difficult to evaluate the quality of the experiment.
