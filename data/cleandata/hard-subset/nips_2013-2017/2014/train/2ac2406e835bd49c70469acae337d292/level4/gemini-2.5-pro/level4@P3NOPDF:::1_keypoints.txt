- Proposes a two-stage method: learn a single large HMM via spectral methods, then de-permute its transition matrix to recover block-diagonal HMM mixture components.  
- Paper is well-written and clear, but the experimental section lacks sufficient detail.  
- Major practical concern: method requires accurately estimating a dense HMM first, which undermines the motivation for using sparse mixtures in low-data regimes; need clarification on useful application/data regimes.  
- Key assumption questionable: no theoretical or empirical guarantee that spectral learning yields a noisy permutation of a block-diagonal matrix rather than another dense matrix, making de-permutation potentially fragile.  
- Section 4.1 experiment is inadequate: it tests de-permutation on an artificially permuted ground-truth matrix instead of on a spectral-estimated noisy matrix; an end-to-end evaluation is needed.  
- Section 4.2 moves toward end-to-end testing but omits crucial details (component HMM parameters, separability, mixing rates) needed for assessment and reproducibility.  
- Table 1 oddity: EM initialized with spectral sometimes performs worse than spectral alone; authors should explain whether EM is converging to poor local optima.  
- Minor issues: typo in proof (Λ subscript likely should be K) and figures’ text/numbers are too small to read.  
- Conclusion: theoretically interesting and novel approach, but significant doubts remain about practical viability without stronger end-to-end empirical validation.
