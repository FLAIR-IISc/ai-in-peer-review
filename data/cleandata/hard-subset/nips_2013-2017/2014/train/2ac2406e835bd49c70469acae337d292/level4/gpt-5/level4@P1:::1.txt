Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles the problem of learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via spectral methods based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The key idea is to reinterpret an MHMM as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first use an existing spectral/MoM algorithm for HMMs to estimate global parameters, which are recovered up to a permutation of the latent states. They then propose a depermutation method that leverages spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the block structure and identify the parameters of each component HMM. A spectral “longevity” heuristic is introduced to estimate K, the number of HMMs. Experiments on synthetic and real data support the approach.

The work builds on spectral learning for HMMs and mixtures [e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang; Anandkumar et al.] and on earlier work using HMMs for sequence clustering (e.g., Smyth). The contribution lies in adapting spectral learning to MHMMs via a depermutation scheme and proposing a practical criterion to estimate K from eigenvalue behavior.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual reduction of MHMM learning to HMM learning with a block-diagonal transition matrix, with supporting lemmas.
- Computationally attractive alternative to EM: moment calculation costs scale with data, but the core estimation is data-size independent.
- A concrete algorithm to resolve the permutation ambiguity using eigen-structure, plus a heuristic to estimate K via “spectral longevity.”
- Well-written in most parts, with useful intuition about why naive eigenvalue clustering fails in the noisy case and how to mitigate it.

Weaknesses and questions:
1) Practicality of learning a dense global HMM first:
- The main advantage of MHMMs over a large single HMM is sparsity: block-diagonal transitions reduce parameters and help when data are limited. The proposed pipeline first estimates a full MK×MK transition matrix and only then attempts to recover block structure. For many real problems, accurate estimation of a dense global HMM may be infeasible with limited data, undermining the benefits of the mixture formulation. Please clarify the regimes where your approach is appropriate (e.g., large N and T, strong spectral gaps, well-separated emissions) and when it is not.

2) Sparsity of the recovered transition matrix:
- In practice, spectral estimators [1,2] need not yield sparse transitions, and many dense HMMs may fit the data nearly as well as a block-diagonal model. Do you have conditions under which the spectral estimator is provably close to block-diagonal (e.g., perturbation bounds showing small off-block mass under separation assumptions)? If not, could you incorporate a sparsity-promoting post-processing step (e.g., structured projection, thresholding with guarantees), and evaluate its effect?

3) Noise model and end-to-end evaluation:
- Section 4.1 perturbs a permuted transition matrix with Dirichlet noise and then depermutes. However, the practical question is how well the procedure works when the matrix arises from actual MoM estimation on finite data drawn from an MHMM. Please provide end-to-end experiments: sample sequences from an MHMM, estimate moments, learn the global HMM, and then depermute—reporting success rates as a function of data and noise.

4) Experimental detail and replicability (Section 4.2):
- The clustering accuracy comparison to EM is promising, but the experimental setup is underspecified. The particular transition and emission parameters (and their separability, spectral gaps, stationary distributions) critically affect difficulty. Please provide full generative parameters (or well-defined distributions and seeds), moment estimation details, and ablation on key factors (e.g., spectral gaps, emission overlap). An appendix would suffice if space is tight.

5) EM initialized with spectral learning (Table 1):
- Why does EM initialized with spectral parameters sometimes underperform spectral learning alone? In principle EM should not decrease likelihood; if accuracy drops, is it due to overfitting, local minima, or model mismatch (e.g., MK < L constraint via feature mapping)? Please report likelihoods and clarify stopping criteria, regularization, and whether EM was constrained to maintain block structure.

6) Theoretical guarantees:
- Conjecture 1 is central for justifying the rank-K reconstruction and the depermutation under noise. While a full proof may be beyond scope, additional empirical evidence and sensitivity analysis (e.g., how performance degrades as |λG_K| approaches max_k |λL_2,k|) would strengthen the claim. Clarify whether your longevity criterion reliably identifies K when eigenvalues are closely spaced.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The technical framing is sound, with clear lemmas and a plausible depermutation strategy. However, key robustness aspects are conjectural, and the empirical evaluation does not yet convincingly reflect real noise conditions or provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and readable; the experiments section needs more detail, and figures need larger fonts.
- Originality: The reduction plus spectral depermutation and the longevity heuristic appear novel combinations of known techniques.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for scalable sequence clustering, provided robustness is demonstrated. Current experiments leave uncertainty about applicability to challenging real-world settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and elegant reduction from MHMM to HMM, with a concrete depermutation algorithm.
- Computational benefits over EM; promising direction for large-scale sequence clustering.
- Novel heuristic for estimating K from eigenvalue behavior.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical viability hinges on accurate estimation of a dense global HMM; conditions for success are not clearly delineated.
- Lack of end-to-end experiments that reflect real estimation noise; limited experimental detail.
- Central robustness result stated as a conjecture; limited empirical stress-testing near failure regimes.
- Occasional inconsistencies (e.g., EM initialized with spectral sometimes worse than spectral alone) not fully explained.

Minor comments:
- In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right of the block-diagonal matrix should be K, not 2.
- Figures: text and numbers are too small to read; please enlarge fonts and improve contrast.
- Typographical fixes: replace “it’s” with “its” where appropriate; avoid possessive apostrophes in “authors’ algorithm,” etc.

Overall:
The paper contains interesting ideas with potential computational advantages, but the experimental validation is not yet sufficient to establish robustness on realistic data. Strengthening the end-to-end evaluation, clarifying regimes of applicability, and providing more detail and stress tests would substantially improve the paper.
