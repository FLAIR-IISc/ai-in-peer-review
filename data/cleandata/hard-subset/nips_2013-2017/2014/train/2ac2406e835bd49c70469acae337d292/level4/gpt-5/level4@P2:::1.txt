Summary
The paper addresses learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via a method-of-moments (MoM) approach. The key observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. The authors first apply a spectral/MoM algorithm for HMMs (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang; Anandkumar et al.) to estimate global parameters up to a permutation of the latent states, then propose a procedure to undo the permutation by exploiting the spectral structure of the global transition matrix. They further propose a method to infer the number of mixtures K through a “spectral longevity” criterion based on eigenvalue gaps. Experiments on synthetic and real data illustrate the approach and its computational advantages over EM.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on spectral learning for HMMs and MoM/tensor methods for latent-variable models. Casting MHMMs as block-diagonal HMMs is standard, but the depermutation procedure that uses the multiplicity and eigenstructure near eigenvalue 1 appears to be the main technical contribution, along with a practical heuristic to infer K.

Strengths
- Clear and mostly well-organized exposition; the equivalence between MHMM and block-diagonal HMM is crisply stated.
- Addresses a core practical obstacle for MHMM spectral learning: permutation ambiguity that mixes states across components.
- Proposes an efficient, scalable pipeline whose runtime after moment computation does not depend on dataset size.
- Empirical results suggest favorable speed-accuracy trade-offs compared to EM, and spectral estimates can serve as strong initializations.

Major concerns and suggestions
1) Practicality of relying on full global HMM estimation
- The approach first estimates a full MK-state HMM, then recovers block structure. In many real problems, one prefers MHMMs specifically to exploit sparsity/structure because fully connected MK-state HMMs are hard to estimate with limited data.
- Please clarify regimes where your approach is appropriate (e.g., when L ≥ MK, strong spectral gaps, sufficient sequence counts and lengths). Provide guidance on failure modes (weak gaps, small N or T, overlapping emissions).

2) Sparsity and robustness
- In practice, the estimated transition matrix will not be exactly sparse, especially under model mismatch or noise. Do you have guarantees or regularization strategies that promote near-block-diagonality (e.g., structured penalties or constrained reconstructions)? As stated, many dense HMMs might fit nearly as well as a sparse block-diagonal model.

3) Noise modeling and depermutation evaluation
- Section 4.1 perturbs a permuted transition matrix with Dirichlet noise, but the paper is about learning from observations. A more faithful evaluation would simulate sequences from MHMMs, learn all parameters via MoM, then assess depermutation performance under realistic estimation noise. This would better capture how observation noise and sample size affect ĀP and the success of depermutation.

4) Experimental details in Section 4.2
- The clustering accuracy comparisons are promising, but key generative details are missing. Please report the actual Ak and Ok construction (e.g., expected mixing rates, spectral gaps, overlap of emissions, initial distributions, variance levels). These strongly determine difficulty. If space is tight, include an appendix.

5) EM initialized with spectral learning
- In Table 1, why can EM initialized with spectral estimates underperform spectral alone? Did EM overfit or get stuck due to constraints or numerical issues? Clarify stopping criteria, regularization, and whether emissions and transitions were constrained consistently across methods.

6) Theoretical guarantees and assumptions
- Several results are framed as conjectures. The proposed longevity-based K selection depends on spectral gap conditions and a clean eigenvalue separation between global and local modes. Can you provide empirical diagnostics (e.g., estimated gaps, sensitivity to e-range, robustness across runs) and a discussion of when the conditions plausibly hold?

Quality
- The methodological idea is sound and well-motivated, but theoretical support is partly conjectural and the noise model in experiments is not always aligned with end-to-end learning. Additional empirical validation under realistic data-generating processes would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- Generally clear. The experimental section would benefit from more detail (parameters, data sizes, variability across trials). Figures are hard to read due to small fonts.

Originality
- While the block-diagonal reduction is known, the specific depermutation via eigenstructure and the spectral longevity criterion for K appear novel and potentially useful.

Significance
- If robust in practice, the approach could offer a scalable alternative or strong initializer for MHMMs. Current evidence is promising but limited by evaluation design and lack of sparsity-promoting mechanisms.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem formulation and practical algorithm.
- Addresses a nontrivial issue (permutation) largely overlooked in prior spectral MHMM work.
- Fast and potentially scalable; good initializer for EM.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited end-to-end evaluations from raw sequences; noise model mismatch in Section 4.1.
- Unclear practical regimes of success; dependence on strong spectral gaps.
- No mechanism to enforce or encourage block sparsity in learned transitions.
- Some theoretical claims are conjectural.

Minor comments
- In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of the bottom-right Λ in the first matrix should be K, not 2.
- Increase font sizes and line weights in figures; current text and numbers are difficult to read.
- Please standardize notation and ensure all referenced quantities (e.g., λ̃, αk, βj,k) are defined where first used.
- Ensure complete and consistent citations for foundational spectral methods.

Overall
An interesting and well-written paper with a neat depermutation idea and practical promise. Strengthen the experiments with end-to-end learning from data, provide more generative detail, discuss regimes of applicability, and clarify why EM+MoM can sometimes underperform MoM. Adding a sparsity-aware variant would further increase practical impact.
