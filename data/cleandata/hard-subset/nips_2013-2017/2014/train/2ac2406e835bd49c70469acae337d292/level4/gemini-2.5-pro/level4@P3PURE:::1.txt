**Summary:**
The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). Their proposed method first uses a spectral learning algorithm to learn the parameters of a single HMM that is equivalent to the mixture. Subsequently, they introduce a technique to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the learned transition matrix, thereby recovering the underlying block-diagonal structure of the original mixture model.

**Major Comments:**

1.  The paper is generally well-written. However, the experimental results section would benefit from additional detail and expansion.

2.  The proposed approach is predicated on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single, larger HMM. A primary motivation for using mixture models, however, is to leverage the sparse, block-diagonal structure of the transition matrix. This sparsity is particularly advantageous for large-scale problems, especially when training data is limited, as it makes learning more tractable than for a full, dense model. The authors' algorithm, in contrast, requires first learning the parameters of the full, dense HMM and only then recovering the block-diagonal structure. This initial step of accurately estimating a large, dense transition matrix seems to counteract the very benefits of using a mixture model, potentially making the approach impractical for the large-scale problems where it would be most needed. Could the authors comment on the practical scenarios or problem scales where their algorithm is expected to be effective versus those where it may not be appropriate?

3.  A related concern is that, in practice, the transition matrix estimated by the spectral algorithm will be subject to estimation error and is unlikely to be an exact permutation of a sparse, block-diagonal matrix. Is there any theoretical guarantee that the transition matrix learned via the methods in [1, 2] will be close to a permuted block-diagonal matrix? I am concerned that there could be many dense transition matrices that are equally, or even more, consistent with the observed data than the target sparse model. It is not clear that the spectral estimate will retain the necessary permuted-sparse structure, even in the presence of small amounts of noise.

4.  The experimental setup in Section 4.1 does not appear to realistically evaluate the proposed method's full pipeline. The experiment focuses on de-permuting a transition matrix directly perturbed with Dirichlet noise. However, the paper's contribution is about learning the *entire* mixture of HMMs from data. A more convincing evaluation would involve generating observation sequences from a ground-truth mixture of HMMs, applying the spectral algorithm to learn the parameters from this data, and *then* applying the proposed de-permutation algorithm to the estimated transition matrix. This end-to-end evaluation is crucial for understanding how estimation errors, stemming from finite data and observation noise, propagate through the learning process and affect the final recovery of the block-diagonal structure.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 moves closer to an end-to-end evaluation, but it lacks sufficient detail to assess its validity and difficulty. To properly interpret the results, crucial information about the ground-truth mixture of HMMs is needed. Specifically, the parameters of the transition and observation matrices should be provided, as these details significantly influence the difficulty of the learning problem. The authors should include these specifics, perhaps in an appendix, to allow for a thorough evaluation of their results.

6.  In Table 1, the results for "EM initialized with spectral learning" are sometimes worse than for "spectral learning" alone. Since the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, when initialized this way, should monotonically improve or maintain the likelihood of the solution found by spectral learning, this outcome is unexpected. Could the authors clarify why EM appears to degrade performance in these cases?

**Minor Comments:**

1.  In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix appears to be a '2' but should likely be 'K'.

2.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small and difficult to read.

In summary, the paper presents interesting ideas and solid theoretical results. However, there are significant concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed method to real-world datasets, primarily due to its reliance on first learning a large, dense HMM.
