Summary: The paper addresses learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The authors first apply a spectral method-of-moments approach to estimate the parameters of a single HMM, and then introduce a procedure to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the learned transition matrix so as to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure corresponding to the component HMMs.

Major Comments:
1. Overall, the paper is clearly written. However, the experimental section feels underdeveloped and would benefit from additional detail and analysis to substantiate the claims.
2. The approach hinges on the equivalence between an MHMM and a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. A key motivation for learning an MHMM instead of a single full HMM is the induced sparsity (block structure), which is practically important when data are limited or models are large. In contrast, the proposed pipeline requires first estimating the full HMM accurately and then undoing the permutation to reveal the block structure. For many real applications, this may be impractical. Guidance on the regimes where the proposed method is expected to work well (and where it may not) would be very helpful.
3. In realistic settings, the recovered transition matrix will not be exactly sparse. Is there any mechanism to encourage or guarantee that the learned transition matrix is (approximately) sparse or close to block-diagonal? It seems plausible that dense HMMs could approximate the desired block structure under small noise, and it is not clear that the spectral estimators in [1, 2] will produce a near-sparse matrix in such cases.
4. I am concerned that the experiments may not reflect the true noise characteristics of end-to-end learning. In Section 4.1, the method is tested on a transition matrix perturbed via a Dirichlet distribution; but the paper is about learning MHMM parameters from observations, not merely depermuting matrices. It would be more informative to sample observation sequences from an MHMM, apply the full learning pipeline, and then assess how observational noise and limited data affect the estimated transition matrix and the difficulty of depermutation.
5. Section 4.2 moves toward such an evaluation, but the description lacks sufficient detail to assess the setup. The specific transition and emission parameters matter greatly for difficulty and identifiability. Please provide the exact parameter settings (e.g., transition matrices, means/covariances or emission distributions, mixing proportions), perhaps in an appendix if space is limited.
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with the spectral method sometimes underperform the spectral method alone? Typically, EM should refine or at least not degrade a reasonable initialization. An explanation (e.g., due to local minima, overfitting, model mismatch, or regularization differences) would be useful.

Minor Comments:
1. In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right entry of the first displayed matrix in the proof appears to be K rather than 2.
2. The figures are difficult to read; please enlarge text and numerical labels.

The paper presents interesting ideas and appealing theoretical insights, but I remain concerned about practical robustness on real data.
