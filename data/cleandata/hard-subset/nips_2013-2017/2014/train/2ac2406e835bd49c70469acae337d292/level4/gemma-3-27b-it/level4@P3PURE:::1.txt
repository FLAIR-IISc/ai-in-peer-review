Summary: The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models. They propose a spectral learning algorithm to estimate the parameters of a hidden Markov model, followed by a method to resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix and recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. This paper is, for the most part, very well written. The experimental results section could benefit from further elaboration. Specifically, the…
2. The authors leverage the fact that a mixture of Hidden Markov Models can be represented as a single HMM. A key motivation for employing a mixture of HMMs, rather than directly learning a single HMM, is the inherent sparsity arising from the block-diagonal structure of the mixture. This sparsity is particularly valuable when dealing with problems that are too large for a full model, especially given limited training data. However, the presented algorithm first necessitates accurate estimation of the full HMM parameters, subsequently reversing the permutation to reveal the block-diagonal structure. For many real-world applications, this approach appears impractical. Do the authors offer guidance on the conditions under which their algorithm might be applicable, and conversely, when it would be unsuitable?
3. Similarly, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse in practice. Is there a mechanism to guarantee that the transition matrix learned using the spectral algorithms described in this paper will be (approximately) sparse? I suspect numerous HMMs with full transition matrices could approximate the sparse, block-diagonal model the authors target. It is not obvious that the matrix learned via [1,2] will exhibit sparsity, even with minimal noise.
4. I am concerned that the experimental results do not adequately reflect the impact of noise in a realistic setting. For instance, in Section 4.1, the algorithm is applied to a transition matrix perturbed by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this paper focuses on learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not simply depermuting matrices. A more relevant evaluation would involve sampling observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then attempting to recover all parameters from the observed data. This would provide a more accurate assessment of how noise in observations and limited data quantity influence the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of depermutation.
5. The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address my concerns from the previous point, but insufficient information is provided to adequately evaluate its quality. Specifically, I lack clarity regarding the characteristics of the underlying mixture of HMMs. The specific parameters of the transition and observation matrices are crucial for assessing the ease or difficulty of learning the model from data. The authors could include these details in an appendix if space is a constraint.
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialization with spectral learning sometimes yield worse results than spectral learning alone? Shouldn’t EM always improve the initial estimate?

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix should likely be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers within the figures are too small to read comfortably.

Overall, the paper presents some interesting ideas and strong theoretical results. However, I am concerned that the method may be less effective on real-world datasets in practice.
