

This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The authors demonstrate that an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012) can be applied directly to MHMMs by recognizing the latter as a special case of the former. They propose a novel stabilization approach to address the parameter permutation issue, leveraging the assumption that each HMM's transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue of value 1.

The paper exhibits several notational inconsistencies that require attention. The authors should incorporate more explicit references to related work; for instance, the initialization of EM with spectral estimates was previously explored in Chaganty and Liang (2013). Furthermore, the experimental results are presented in an overly simplistic manner. Figure 4's graphical format should be replaced with a table for clearer presentation of results. 

Notwithstanding these concerns, the proposed stabilization method for resolving parameter permutation is both interesting and intuitively sound. The accompanying analysis for the case with estimation noise is particularly noteworthy, leading to a clever application of eigenvalues (Equation (7)) within the algorithm.

Specific comments:
- Corollary 1 requires clearer exposition. Define the term $1_J^T$ (a row vector of length $J$ filled with ones) explicitly and elaborate on the structure of $\lim_{\epsilon \to \infty} \bar{A}^\epsilon$ (which is a $JK \times JK$ block diagonal matrix where all columns within each $J \times J$ block are identical).
- Define all vectors and matrices with their dimensions upon first introduction; this would significantly improve clarity.
- Figure 4 is difficult to interpret. Replace it with a table format. Additionally, the poor performance of EM in Table 1 is puzzling. Typically, even with random initialization, EM achieves better results than spectral methods after sufficient iterations (despite local optima), as it converges to a local maximum. Please report the best (not average) accuracy for EM to enable fair comparison.
- Minor point: Adopting the standard notation from Hsu et al. (2009) would enhance consistency. Specifically, use $T$ for transition matrices (not $A$) and $n$, $m$ for the numbers of observation and hidden states (not $L$, $J$).

The core contribution—applying a spectral HMM learning algorithm to MHMMs followed by a stabilization step to resolve permutation—is well-motivated. However, the current presentation requires substantial refinement in notation, experimental reporting, and contextualization of prior work to meet the standards expected at NIPS. The proposed stabilization technique merits attention, but the paper's current form risks obscuring its value due to presentation issues.
