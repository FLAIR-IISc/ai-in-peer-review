This paper presents a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The core challenge in applying standard spectral learning algorithms, such as the one by Hsu, Kakade & Zhang, to this problem is that the recovered transition and observation operators are arbitrarily permuted, effectively mixing the parameters of the different HMM components. The central contribution of this work is a novel de-permutation algorithm designed to disentangle these components and recover the individual models. The proposed method appears to be effective based on the empirical results.

While the core idea is sound and the problem is significant, the paper could be substantially improved in its presentation and contextualization.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem: the unsupervised learning of a mixture of HMMs.
*   The key technical contribution, the de-permutation algorithm, is a novel approach to a critical bottleneck that prevents the direct application of existing spectral methods to this mixture setting.
*   The empirical evaluation suggests that the proposed algorithm is practically effective.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Framing:** The initial application of the spectral method is a direct extension of prior work. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly framed the de-permutation step as its primary contribution. The current presentation could be interpreted as a simple variant of the Hsu et al. algorithm, whereas the main novelty lies in solving the subsequent component-mixing problem, which is non-trivial.

2.  **Contextualization and Related Work:** The paper needs to better situate its contributions within the broader literature on spectral methods for mixture models. For instance, the problem of recovering mixed components has been addressed in other contexts. A relevant comparison and discussion would be with work such as:
    *   Chaganty, A. and Liang, P. "Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions." In *International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML)*, 2013.
    This and similar papers tackle related "de-mixing" problems in spectral learning, and discussing the connections and differences would strengthen the paper's positioning.

3.  **Presentation and Typos:** The manuscript requires a thorough round of proofreading to correct several grammatical errors and typos. For example, a sentence in the draft reads: "For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy." Such errors detract from the paper's clarity and professionalism. A careful review of the entire text is strongly recommended to improve readability.

In summary, this paper introduces a valuable technique for a challenging learning problem. The main contribution is the algorithm for de-permuting the output of a spectral method applied to a mixture of HMMs. To improve the paper for publication, the authors should refine the manuscript's clarity, enhance the discussion of related work to better contextualize their contribution, and meticulously proofread the text to eliminate grammatical errors.
