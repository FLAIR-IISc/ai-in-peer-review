**Summary:** The authors consider the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models. The authors first suggest using a spectral learning algorithm to learn some parameters for a Hidden Markov Model, and then provide a method to solve the permutation ambiguity of the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

**Key Comments:**

1.  I found this paper to be very well-written for the most part. The experimental results section could be a bit more detailed, especially.
2.  The authors rely on the information that a mixture of Hidden Markov Models can be expressed as a single HMM. One of the main reasons for learning a mixture of HMMs, as opposed to learning a single HMM, if not the main reason, is that the block-diagonal structure of the mixture of HMMs is sparse. This sparsity can be imposed on problems that are considered too large to learn with a complete model, especially if the problem is large and the amount of training data is small. However, in this paper, the author's algorithm relies on first accurately estimating the complete HMM parameters, then performing a permutation inversion to find the block-diagonal structure. This seems completely unacceptable for most real-world problems. Does the author have any suggestions for when their algorithm might be suitable and when it might not?
3.  Again, in reality, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is there any way to ensure that the transition matrix learned by the spectral algorithm proposed in this paper (approximately) will be sparse? I doubt that there are many HMM models that have a complete transition matrix that has a block-diagonal structure close to the sparse model that the authors are looking for. It is not at all clear that the matrix learned through [1,2] will be sparse with very little noise.
4.  I am really concerned that the experimental results do not accurately reflect the reality of the transition matrix's noise. For example, in section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a transition matrix that is perturbed by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution... but this is a paper about learning the mixture of HMM parameters, not just de-permuting the matrix. Should the authors have sampled the observation data from the mixture of HMMs and then learned all the parameters back from the observed data? This would give a more accurate idea of how the noise of the observation and small amount of data is reflected in the learned transition matrix and how difficult it is to de-permute the matrix in the presence of this noise.
