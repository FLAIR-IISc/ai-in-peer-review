Summary
The paper proposes a spectral approach to learning mixtures of HMMs. The core estimation procedure closely follows the Hsu–Kakade–Zhang algorithm for single HMMs, but when applied to mixtures, the recovered parameters are subject to arbitrary permutations that mix components. The main contribution is a de-permutation step that aligns the estimated parameters to their respective HMM components. Empirically, the proposed de-permutation method appears to work well.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: addressing permutation ambiguities that arise when extending spectral HMM learning to mixtures.
- Conceptually simple and computationally appealing approach: leverages established spectral machinery and adds a lightweight alignment procedure.
- Promising empirical behavior: the de-permutation appears effective in practice and could make spectral learning more usable for mixtures where EM can be slow or unstable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty in the core estimation step: the spectral recovery largely mirrors Hsu–Kakade–Zhang; the main novelty is the de-permutation heuristic.
- Theoretical underpinnings of de-permutation are unclear: the paper would benefit from formal conditions under which the alignment is correct (e.g., separability/identifiability assumptions on emissions or transitions), and from finite-sample guarantees or at least perturbation analyses.
- Empirical evaluation needs stronger baselines and diagnostics: comparisons to EM-based mixture-of-HMMs, variational methods, and other spectral mixture methods are important. Ablations that isolate the benefit of the de-permutation step are also needed.
- Presentation/clarity issues: multiple typos and occasional unclear phrasing detract from readability.

Clarity and presentation
- Please proofread carefully. For example, the sentence “For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy.” is ungrammatical; consider “For each e, identify the eigenvalues that capture most of the energy.”
- Clearly state assumptions required for identifiability: e.g., full-rank emission matrices, minimality, nondegenerate transition matrices, mixture weight bounds, and any separation conditions used by de-permutation.
- Precisely define the permutation ambiguity for mixtures: is the ambiguity per state, per component, or global across time? Explain how the proposed procedure yields a consistent global alignment.
- Provide pseudocode for the de-permutation algorithm, with a clear statement of its input/output, objective (e.g., minimizing a matching loss), and computational complexity. If you use a matching solver (e.g., Hungarian algorithm) or a spectral similarity metric, state that explicitly.
- Clarify moment construction for mixtures: specify which cross-moments are estimated, how components contribute, and how whitening/truncation is performed. Detail how ranks are selected (e.g., via singular value thresholding) and how regularization handles small eigen-gaps.
- Include guidance on practical details: initialization, choice of the number of components/states, sensitivity to truncation level, and how you prevent degenerate solutions.

Empirical evaluation
- Add or expand comparisons to:
  - EM for mixture-of-HMMs and strong HMM baselines (single-HMM EM on pooled data).
  - Spectral methods for mixtures/latent-variable models, including tensor-decomposition approaches.
- Report metrics beyond qualitative fits: parameter recovery error (modulo permutation), predictive log-likelihood on held-out sequences, and sequence prediction accuracy when applicable.
- Provide robustness analyses: performance vs. sample size, number of components/states, sequence length, state overlap, and model misspecification (e.g., non-Gaussian emissions if you assume Gaussian).
- Include ablations: performance without de-permutation, with random or naive alignment strategies, and with alternative similarity measures for matching components.
- Report runtime and memory usage to substantiate the claimed efficiency advantages.

Related work and positioning
- Situate the contribution relative to:
  - Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang’s spectral algorithm for HMMs (core technique you build on).
  - Chaganty and Liang (2013), “Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions,” which addresses spectral learning for mixtures with component alignment issues analogous to your de-permutation step.
  - Tensor-based spectral methods for latent-variable models (e.g., Anandkumar et al.), and other multi-view or mixture identification techniques.
- Clarify what is genuinely new (the specific de-permutation strategy tailored to mixtures of HMMs) and where you borrow standard tools (moment construction, whitening, eigen-decomposition).

Questions for the authors
- What conditions guarantee correct de-permutation (e.g., distinct emission distributions across components, bounds on overlap, or spectral separation)? Can you provide perturbation bounds showing stability under sampling noise?
- Is the alignment step deterministic and globally consistent across time? Does it require knowing the number of components, and how sensitive is it to misspecification of this number?
- How does the method scale with the number of components and states? Please provide complexity in terms of observations, state size, and component count.
- How are eigenvalue thresholds or truncation ranks chosen in practice? Do you use automated criteria?

Minor comments
- Unify notation and ensure symbols are introduced before use.
- Replace vague terms like “energy” with precise definitions (e.g., proportion of spectral mass explained).
- Include a brief discussion of limitations and potential failure modes (e.g., highly overlapping components).

Overall assessment
This work addresses a practical and important gap in extending spectral HMM learning to mixtures by proposing a de-permutation step that appears effective in experiments. The contribution is incremental relative to prior spectral methods, and the current write-up lacks theoretical guarantees and comprehensive empirical comparisons. With stronger positioning, clearer exposition, and more rigorous evaluation (and/or analysis of when de-permutation succeeds), the paper would be a solid contribution. In its current form, I view it as borderline, but potentially acceptable if the empirical and theoretical support is substantially strengthened.
