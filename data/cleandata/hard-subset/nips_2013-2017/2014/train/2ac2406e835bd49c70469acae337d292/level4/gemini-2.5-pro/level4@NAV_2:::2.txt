This paper introduces a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. This insight allows the authors to apply established spectral algorithms for HMMs, such as the one by Hsu et al. (2012), to estimate the global parameters. The paper's main technical contribution is a novel method for resolving the permutation ambiguity that arises from this estimation process. The proposed solution exploits the spectral properties of the global transition matrix; by clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of this matrix (formed from its top K eigenvalues), the algorithm recovers the block structure and thereby identifies the parameters of the individual HMM components. The authors also analyze the behavior of this approach in the presence of estimation noise and propose a method to determine the number of mixture components from the spectrum.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this work is its elegant and intuitive solution to the permutation problem, which is a key obstacle in applying spectral methods to MHMMs. The proposed technique of using the spectral signature of the global transition matrix to de-permute the estimated parameters is novel and well-motivated. The analysis is extended to the practical scenario involving estimation noise, leading to a complete algorithm that can also estimate the number of mixture components, which is a significant contribution.

However, the paper could be improved in several areas. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating feasibility, is not comprehensive enough to fully substantiate the claimed advantages in speed and accuracy compared to standard methods like Expectation-Maximization (EM). The clarity of the manuscript is also a point of concern; inconsistent notation and insufficient definitions for mathematical objects make certain sections challenging to follow and could hinder reproducibility. Finally, the discussion of related work could be more thorough. For example, using spectral estimates to initialize EM is a known technique (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013), and positioning the current work in relation to such prior art would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of efficiently learning MHMMs, especially for large datasets.
*   The proposed de-permutation method based on the spectral properties of the transition matrix is a novel and clever technical contribution.
*   The paper provides an analysis for the noisy case and a method for model selection (i.e., identifying the number of components K), making the algorithm more practical.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is preliminary and lacks the depth required to make a strong case for the method's superiority.
*   The manuscript's clarity needs significant improvement, particularly concerning mathematical notation and the presentation of results.
*   The paper does not fully contextualize its contributions within the existing literature, notably regarding the use of spectral methods for EM initialization.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Clarity and Notation:** The paper would greatly benefit from more rigorous and consistent notation. Please define each vector and matrix with its dimensions when it is first introduced. For instance, in Corollary 1, explicitly defining `1_M` as a column vector of ones and describing the resulting block structure of `lim e→∞ Ā^e` would improve comprehension. Conforming to established notation from key references like Hsu et al. (2012) could also enhance readability.
*   **Presentation of Results:** The graphical results in Figure 4 are difficult to interpret and compare. Presenting these accuracy and runtime comparisons in a tabular format is strongly recommended for clarity.
*   **Experimental Baselines:** The performance of the randomly initialized EM algorithm in Table 1 appears unusually low. It is standard practice to report the performance of the best run over multiple random initializations (i.e., the one with the highest data likelihood), as this provides a stronger baseline. Please clarify the reporting methodology. Without this, the comparison to the proposed method may not be entirely fair, as even with its non-convexity, a well-run EM often achieves high accuracy.
