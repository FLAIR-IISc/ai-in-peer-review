This paper addresses the problem of learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs) using a spectral approach. The core idea is to reframe an MHMM as a larger, single HMM, which allows the authors to apply an existing spectral learning algorithm (Anandkumar et al., 2012) directly. The main technical contribution is a novel "stabilization" method to resolve the inherent parameter permutation problem that arises in this formulation. This method relies on the assumption that the transition matrix of each component HMM has a single eigenvalue of one.

The paper's primary strength lies in its proposed scheme for resolving the parameter permutation issue. The stabilization-based approach is both intuitive and elegant. Furthermore, the theoretical analysis that accounts for estimation noise is a valuable contribution, leading to an intriguing and principled use of eigenvalues (as seen in Equation 7) within the final algorithm.

Despite these interesting ideas, the paper requires significant revision in several key areas before it can be considered for publication.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper's presentation suffers from a lack of precision, which hinders readability. For the work to be accessible and verifiable, the authors must define all vectors and matrices, including their dimensions, upon their first introduction. For instance, Corollary 1 was initially confusing; terms like `1_J^T` should be explicitly defined (e.g., as a row vector of ones of length J), and the resulting block-diagonal structure of `lim_{e->inf} bar{A}^e` requires a more detailed explanation. Adopting established notation from the spectral learning literature, such as that used in Hsu et al. (2009) (e.g., using T for transition matrices, and n, m for observation and hidden state counts instead of L, J), would also greatly improve readability for experts in the field.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation is currently insufficient and requires substantial improvement.
    *   **Presentation:** The results in Figure 4 are difficult to interpret in their current graphical format. Presenting these results in a clear, tabular format would allow for a much more direct and effective comparison between methods.
    *   **Baselines:** The performance of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm reported in Table 1 appears unusually poor. While EM is susceptible to local optima, it is a strong baseline. It is standard practice to run EM from multiple random initializations and report the result corresponding to the best final likelihood, as this reflects its practical potential. Reporting an average performance, which may include runs trapped in poor local optima, does not provide a fair comparison. The authors should clarify their experimental setup and include results from this "best-of-N" EM baseline.
    *   **Scope:** The experiments feel preliminary. Expanding the evaluation to include more complex datasets or a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to its assumptions would bolster the paper's claims.

3.  **Positioning within Related Work:** The manuscript would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of related work. The connection to prior art is currently underdeveloped. For example, the idea of using spectral estimates to initialize the EM algorithm is a key motivation, yet the paper fails to cite relevant, foundational work in this area, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013). A comprehensive literature review is necessary to properly contextualize the paper's contributions and demonstrate a clear understanding of the field.

In summary, the paper presents a clever and promising method for a challenging learning problem. However, its current form is undermined by significant issues in presentation, experimental rigor, and contextualization. I would encourage the authors to address the detailed comments above and resubmit a revised version.
