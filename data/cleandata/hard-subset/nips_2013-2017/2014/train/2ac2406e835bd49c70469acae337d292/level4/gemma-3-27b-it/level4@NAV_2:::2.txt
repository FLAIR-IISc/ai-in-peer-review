This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMM). It demonstrates that an existing spectral algorithm for learning hidden Markov models (Anandkumar et al., 2012) can be leveraged by formulating an MHMM as a specific instance of an HMM. The authors propose a method to address the parameter permutation problem, relying on the assumption that each HMM’s transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue equal to one.

The paper begins by appropriately framing the problem of MHMM learning, highlighting the computational challenges of traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) approaches, particularly with large datasets. It correctly positions Method of Moments (MoM) as a potential alternative, emphasizing its computational advantages and theoretical guarantees. The core idea of viewing an MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix is insightful and lays the foundation for applying existing spectral learning techniques. The subsequent development of a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity using spectral properties is a significant contribution.

The strengths of this work lie in its novel approach to addressing a known challenge in MHMM learning – parameter identifiability. The analysis of the global transition matrix’s spectral properties, particularly in the presence of estimation noise, is well-motivated and leads to a practical algorithm. The conjecture regarding spectral longevity (Definition 3 and Lemma 3) offers a promising direction for determining the number of HMMs.

However, there are several weaknesses. The notation is occasionally unclear and could benefit from more consistent and detailed definitions of variables and matrices upon their introduction, including dimensions. The presentation of related work is somewhat lacking. While the authors cite Anandkumar et al. (2012), they do not adequately acknowledge or discuss related approaches for initializing EM with spectral estimates, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013). The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the potential of the proposed algorithm, is relatively limited. Figure 4 is difficult to interpret and should be replaced with a tabular representation of the results. The performance of EM in Table 1 appears unexpectedly poor; reporting the best (rather than average) EM accuracy, obtained after multiple random initializations, would provide a more fair comparison and valuable insight. Finally, aligning the notation with established conventions in the field, such as those used in Hsu et al. (2009) – employing 'T' for transition matrices and 'n', 'm' for observation and hidden state numbers, respectively – would improve readability and facilitate comparison with existing literature.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper addresses a challenging problem in MHMM learning – parameter identifiability – with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
* The proposed algorithm offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM, particularly for large datasets.
* The analysis of spectral properties and the development of a depermutation mapping are significant contributions.
* The conjecture regarding spectral longevity provides a promising avenue for future research.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The notation is sometimes unclear and inconsistent, hindering readability.
* The presentation of related work is incomplete.
* The experimental evaluation is limited, and the results are difficult to interpret in some cases.
* The performance of EM in the experiments is surprisingly poor and requires further investigation.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of MHMM learning. While some improvements in clarity, related work discussion, and experimental evaluation are needed, the novel approach and theoretical insights warrant consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
