## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The key idea is to leverage an existing spectral method for learning hidden Markov models (HMMs) – specifically, Anandkumar et al. (2012) – by demonstrating that an MHMM can be cast as a special case of an HMM. To address the inherent problem of parameter permutation in spectral methods, the authors propose a stabilization technique based on the assumption that each HMM’s transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue equal to one.

The paper’s strength lies in its intuitive approach to tackling parameter permutation, a common issue in spectral HMM learning. The analysis presented for the case with estimation noise, leading to the intriguing use of eigenvalues in Equation (7), is also a notable contribution. However, several aspects of the presentation and experimental evaluation require improvement.

My primary concern relates to clarity and readability. The notation throughout the paper is inconsistent and often lacks sufficient definition. Specifically, the authors should define each vector and matrix, including its dimensions, upon its first introduction. For example, in Corollary 1, terms like 1J^T are initially unclear; explicitly defining 1J^T as a row vector of length J filled with ones would improve understanding.  Furthermore, a clearer explanation of the structure of lim{e->inf} Ā^e – namely, its JK x JK block diagonal form with identical J x J columns within each block – would be beneficial. Adopting established notation from Hsu et al. (2009), such as using ‘T’ for transition matrices (instead of ‘A’) and ‘n’, ‘m’ for the numbers of observations and hidden states (instead of ‘L’, ‘J’), would also enhance readability and facilitate comparison with existing literature.

The related work section is currently insufficient. The authors need to demonstrate greater awareness of prior research. For instance, the technique of initializing Expectation-Maximization (EM) with spectral estimates has already been explored, as evidenced by Chaganty and Liang (2013), and should be discussed. 

The experimental evaluation is also a significant weakness. The current presentation of results in Figure 4 is difficult to interpret and obscures meaningful comparisons. I strongly recommend reporting results in a tabular format. Additionally, the performance of EM in Table 1 is surprisingly poor. Typically, even with random initialization, EM converges to a better solution than spectral methods given sufficient iterations, despite the potential for getting stuck in local optima. The authors should report the *best* accuracy achieved by EM (rather than the average) to provide a fair comparison.  

**Specific Comments:**

* **Corollary 1:** Define 1J^T and elaborate on the structure of lim{e->inf} Ā^e.
* **Notation:** Consistently define dimensions of all vectors and matrices upon introduction. Consider adopting the notation from Hsu et al. (2009).
* **Figure 4:** Replace the graphical representation with a table for clearer presentation.
* **Table 1:** Report the best accuracy of EM, not the average. Investigate the reason for the poor EM performance.
* **Related Work:** Expand the related work section to acknowledge and discuss relevant prior research, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013).



In summary, while the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution in addressing parameter permutation within spectral MHMM learning, significant improvements are needed in terms of clarity, notation, experimental evaluation, and a more thorough review of related work. Addressing these points will substantially strengthen the paper and its impact.
