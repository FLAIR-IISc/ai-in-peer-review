## Review: Learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models

**Summary:** This paper addresses the problem of learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a spectral learning approach to estimate HMM parameters, followed by a permutation recovery method designed to reveal the underlying block-diagonal structure inherent in mixture models.

**Major Comments:**

This is a generally well-written paper with a solid theoretical foundation. However, the experimental evaluation could be substantially strengthened.

A key motivation for using a mixture of HMMs rather than a single HMM is to exploit sparsity, particularly in high-dimensional problems or scenarios with limited training data where a full model might be intractable. The authors’ approach, however, relies on first accurately estimating the parameters of a full HMM and *then* attempting to recover the block-diagonal structure through permutation reversal. This seems problematic in practice. Accurately estimating a full HMM can be computationally expensive and inaccurate, especially with limited data. The authors should discuss the conditions under which their algorithm is likely to be effective and those where it may struggle. A clearer understanding of the algorithm’s applicability is crucial.

Relatedly, a significant concern is the likelihood of obtaining a sparse transition matrix. The spectral learning method doesn’t inherently enforce sparsity, and it’s plausible that many dense HMMs could approximate the desired sparse, block-diagonal structure. With even minor noise, the learned transition matrix might not exhibit the necessary sparsity. The authors should address whether they have considered methods to encourage sparsity during the spectral learning phase or analyze the sensitivity of their approach to noise.

My primary concern lies with the experimental setup. The experiments, as presented, do not fully capture the challenges of learning from realistic data. For instance, Section 4.1 permutes a *transition matrix* with Dirichlet-distributed noise, but the core problem is learning HMM parameters from *observations* generated by a mixture of HMMs. A more representative evaluation would involve sampling observation data from the mixture model and then attempting to recover all parameters, including the transition matrix, from this data. This would better reflect the impact of observational noise and limited data on the permutation recovery step.

Section 4.2 begins to address this concern, but lacks sufficient detail for a thorough assessment. Providing more information about the underlying mixture of HMMs—specifically, the parameters of both the transition and observation matrices—is essential. Even an appendix containing these details would significantly improve the evaluation. Knowing the characteristics of the HMMs used to generate the data will allow for a better understanding of the experiment’s results and the difficulty of the learning task.

Finally, Table 1 presents a puzzling result: EM initialization using spectral learning sometimes performs *worse* than spectral learning alone. Since EM is designed to refine an initial parameter estimate, it should, at a minimum, not degrade performance. The authors should investigate and explain this observation.

**Minor Comments:**

1. In the proof of Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrectly labeled as ‘2’ and should be ‘K’.
2. The font size in the figures is too small, making the figures difficult to read.

**Overall:**

The paper presents some interesting ideas and valuable theoretical contributions. However, I have reservations about its practical applicability due to concerns about sparsity, the experimental setup, and the observed performance of EM initialization. Further investigation and a more robust experimental validation are needed to demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness on real-world datasets.
