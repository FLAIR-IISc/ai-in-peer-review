

This paper proposes a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs) by reducing the problem to a special case of standard HMM learning. The authors adapt Anandkumar et al.'s (2012) spectral HMM algorithm and introduce a novel stabilization step to resolve parameter permutation issues under the assumption that each component HMM has a single eigenvalue of 1 in its transition matrix. While the core idea of leveraging existing spectral methods is valid, the presentation and experimental evaluation require significant improvement.

**Major Concerns**  
1.  **Notational Inconsistency and Clarity:** The paper suffers from inconsistent and poorly defined notation. Key terms like `1_J^T` (a row vector of ones) and the structure of `lim_{蔚鈫掆垶} \bar{A}^蔚` (a block-diagonal matrix with identical columns within each `J脳J` block) are introduced without clear definitions or context in Corollary 1. The authors should rigorously define all vectors/matrices with their dimensions upon first use. Adherence to established HMM notation (e.g., using `T` for transition matrices instead of `A`, and `n`, `m` for observation/hidden state counts instead of `L`, `J` as in Hsu et al., 2009) would greatly enhance readability and align with the community standard.

2.  **Insufficient Related Work:** The discussion fails to acknowledge prior work on spectral initialization for HMMs. For instance, Chaganty and Liang (2013) explicitly explore initializing EM with spectral estimates鈥攁 direct precursor to the authors' approach. This omission weakens the contribution's novelty claim.

3.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are underdeveloped. Figure 4's graphical presentation obscures critical numerical results; a table format is essential for precise comparison. Furthermore, Table 1 reports average EM accuracy, which is misleading. Since EM typically converges to better local optima with sufficient iterations (despite local minima concerns), the authors *must* report the *best* (not average) EM performance achieved across multiple runs. The poor average EM results in Table 1 warrant explanation鈥攄id they use inadequate initialization or iteration counts? Without best-case EM results, the comparison to spectral methods is invalid.

**Minor but Important Issues**  
- The stabilization technique (Section 3.2) is conceptually interesting but its necessity should be more clearly justified relative to existing permutation-resolution methods.  
- Equation (7)鈥檚 analysis of estimation noise is a strong point, but its connection to the algorithm鈥檚 design could be elaborated.  
- Figure 4 should be replaced with a table showing all relevant metrics (e.g., accuracy, AIC, runtimes) for spectral, EM, and MHMM variants across datasets.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in MHMM learning. The adaptation of spectral methods is technically sound, and the stabilization approach is intuitively appealing. However, the lack of notational rigor, omission of key related work, and substandard experimental reporting significantly undermine the contribution. The core algorithmic idea has merit, but the current presentation fails to convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing methods or establish clear empirical superiority.

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. The paper requires substantial revision to address the critical issues outlined above. Without improved notation, proper contextualization within the literature, and a rigorous experimental evaluation (including best-case EM results and tabular presentation), the work does not meet NIPS standards for clarity, novelty, or empirical validation. The authors should prioritize these fixes before reconsideration.
