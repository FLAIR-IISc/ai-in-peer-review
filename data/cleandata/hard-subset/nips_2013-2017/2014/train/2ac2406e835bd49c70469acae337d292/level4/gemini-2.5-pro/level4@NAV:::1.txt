**Summary:**
This paper addresses the challenge of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a two-stage approach. Initially, a spectral learning algorithm is employed to estimate the parameters of a single, larger HMM that encapsulates the mixture. Subsequently, they introduce a technique to resolve the inherent permutation ambiguity within the estimated transition matrix, aiming to recover the block-diagonal structure that corresponds to the individual HMM components.

**Major Comments:**
1.  Overall, the paper is well-written and clear. However, the experimental evaluation would benefit from greater detail.

2.  The core premise of the paper is that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single, larger HMM. A primary motivation for using mixture models over a monolithic HMM is the sparsity afforded by the block-diagonal transition matrix, which is particularly advantageous for large-scale problems with limited training data. The proposed algorithm, however, first requires an accurate estimation of the full, dense HMM parameters before recovering the sparse structure. This approach seems impractical for the very real-world scenarios where mixture models are most needed. Could the authors comment on the specific conditions or problem domains where their method is expected to be most effective, and where its applicability might be limited?

3.  In a practical setting, the transition matrix estimated from data will invariably contain noise and is unlikely to be perfectly sparse. Are there theoretical guarantees that the spectral methods cited in this paper will yield a transition matrix that is provably close to a sparse, block-diagonal form? It seems plausible that a neighborhood of dense HMMs could exist that are statistically close to the true sparse model. It is not immediately clear why the learning algorithm would necessarily converge to a solution that is merely a noisy version of the block-diagonal matrix, as opposed to one of these nearby dense models.

4.  A significant concern is that the experimental setup may not realistically model the challenges of the learning problem. In Section 4.1, the evaluation focuses on de-permuting a transition matrix perturbed by noise from a Dirichlet distribution. This setup isolates the de-permutation step but overlooks the primary challenge: learning the HMM parameters from observational data. A more convincing evaluation would involve sampling observation sequences from a ground-truth mixture of HMMs, applying the spectral method to learn the full parameter set from these sequences, and then demonstrating the de-permutation algorithm's effectiveness on the resulting estimated transition matrix. This would provide a far more accurate assessment of the method's robustness to noise originating from finite data samples.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 moves in this direction, but it lacks the necessary details for a thorough evaluation. Specifically, the characteristics of the ground-truth mixture of HMMs are not described. The difficulty of this learning problem is highly sensitive to the specific parameters of the transition and observation matrices. The authors are encouraged to provide these details, possibly in an appendix, to allow for a complete assessment of the experimental results.

6.  In Table 1, the results for "EM initialized with spectral learning" are occasionally worse than those of "spectral learning" alone. This is counter-intuitive, as EM is expected to act as a refinement step that should not degrade performance. Could the authors provide an explanation for this phenomenon?

**Minor Comments:**
1.  In the proof in Section 3.2.1, it appears there is a typo in the first matrix expression. The subscript of Λ in the bottom-right block should be K, not 2.
2.  The font size for text and numbers within the figures is too small, making them difficult to read.

The paper presents compelling ideas and solid theoretical contributions. However, there are substantial concerns regarding the practical viability of the proposed method on real-world datasets.
