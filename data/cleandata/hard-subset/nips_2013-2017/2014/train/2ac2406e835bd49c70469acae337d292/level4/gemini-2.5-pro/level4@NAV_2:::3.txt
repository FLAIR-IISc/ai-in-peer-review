This paper presents a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to treat the MHMM as a single, larger HMM which has a block-diagonal transition matrix. This observation allows the authors to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, such as the one by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012), to estimate the model parameters. The primary technical challenge, however, is that this estimation is only unique up to a permutation of the hidden states, which conflates the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this work is a novel de-permutation algorithm that uses the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix to identify the state groupings corresponding to each component HMM, thereby recovering the individual model parameters.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and practical problem: learning MHMMs on large datasets where traditional EM-based approaches can be computationally prohibitive. The proposed MoM-based algorithm offers a compelling, computationally efficient alternative whose learning time is independent of the dataset size after the initial moment computation.
*   The central contribution—a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity for MHMMs—is novel and well-reasoned. The approach of identifying the block structure by clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix is an elegant solution to a fundamental challenge in applying spectral methods to this model class.
*   The paper provides a method for estimating the number of mixture components (K) from the data, based on the eigenspectrum of the transition matrix, which adds to the practical utility of the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results, though preliminary, effectively demonstrate the significant speed advantage of the spectral method over EM and show its promise as a powerful initialization technique for EM.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The theoretical justification for the algorithm's robustness to noise hinges on Conjecture 1, which states that the low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix remains close to the true one. The justification provided is quite informal and relies on adapting a bound from a related but distinct model. Strengthening this part of the paper with a more rigorous analysis or proof would significantly enhance its technical quality.
*   The clarity of the manuscript could be improved with a careful proofreading pass to address minor typographical errors and awkward phrasing. For example, the intuitive motivation behind the "spectral longevity" criterion in Definition 3 could be explained more clearly to aid reader comprehension beyond the mathematical formula.
*   The paper could better contextualize its contribution by discussing related work on resolving label-switching or permutation ambiguities in other spectrally learned mixture models. For instance, drawing a connection to the work of Chaganty and Liang (2013) on spectral experts for mixtures of linear regressions would highlight common challenges and situate this work within a broader line of research.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The condition |λGK| > max_k |λL2,k| is critical for the de-permutation to succeed. An experiment analyzing the algorithm's sensitivity to this spectral gap (e.g., as the component HMMs become more similar) would provide valuable insight into the method's limitations.
