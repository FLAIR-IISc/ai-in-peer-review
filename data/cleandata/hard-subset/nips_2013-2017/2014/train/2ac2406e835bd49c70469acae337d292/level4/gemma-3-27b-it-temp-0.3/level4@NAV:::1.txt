## Review:

**Summary:** This paper addresses the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a spectral learning approach to estimate HMM parameters, followed by a permutation recovery method designed to reveal the underlying block-diagonal structure of the mixture.

**Major Comments:**

1. The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, the experimental evaluation could benefit from further detail and expansion.

2. The authors leverage the equivalence between a mixture of HMMs and a single HMM. A key motivation for employing a mixture model – rather than a single, monolithic HMM – often stems from the sparsity inherent in the mixture’s block-diagonal structure, particularly when dealing with large, data-limited problems where a full model would be intractable. However, the presented algorithm first requires accurate estimation of the *full* HMM parameters before attempting to recover the block-diagonal form. This approach appears impractical for many real-world scenarios. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the conditions under which their algorithm might be applicable and when it might be less suitable.

3. In practical applications, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to exhibit sparsity. Is it possible to incorporate mechanisms to encourage or guarantee that the transition matrix learned via the proposed spectral algorithms is sparse, or at least approximately so? I am concerned that numerous full transition matrices could closely approximate the desired sparse, block-diagonal structure, and that the learned matrix from the cited work [1,2] may not consistently be sparse, especially with limited or noisy data.

4. I am concerned that the experimental results may not adequately reflect the impact of realistic noise on the transition matrix. For instance, Section 4.1 introduces noise via a Dirichlet distribution applied directly to the transition matrix. However, this work focuses on learning HMM mixtures, not simply depermuting matrices. A more relevant evaluation would involve sampling observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then attempting to recover all parameters from that observed data. This would provide a more realistic assessment of how observational noise and data scarcity affect the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of permutation recovery.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address the concerns raised previously, but lacks sufficient detail for a thorough evaluation. Specifically, the characteristics of the underlying mixture of HMMs are not clearly defined. The parameters of the transition and observation matrices significantly influence the ease of learning the model from data. Providing more specifics regarding these parameters (potentially in an appendix) would be beneficial.

6. In Table 1, the performance of Expectation-Maximization (EM) initialized with spectral learning is occasionally worse than spectral learning alone. This is counterintuitive, as EM should, at minimum, not degrade the initial solution. An explanation for this observation is warranted.

**Minor Comments:**

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrectly labeled as ‘2’; it should be ‘K’.

2. The text and numerical labels within the figures are too small for comfortable readability. 

Overall, the paper presents interesting ideas and solid theoretical contributions. However, I have reservations about the practical applicability of the method to real-world datasets.
