Summary: The authors consider the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They propose using a spectral learning algorithm to learn a set of parameters for an HMM, followed by a method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-written, but the experimental results section could be expanded. Specifically, it would be beneficial to provide more details about the experiments to facilitate a deeper understanding of the results.

2. The authors rely on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be expressed as a single HMM. However, one of the primary reasons for learning a mixture of HMMs instead of a single HMM is that the block-diagonal structure of the mixture is sparse. This sparsity is often necessary for problems that are too large to be modeled with a full HMM, especially when the problem is large and the amount of training data is limited. Nevertheless, the authors' algorithm requires accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and then reversing the permutation to find the block-diagonal structure. This approach seems impractical for most real-world problems. The authors should provide guidance on when their algorithm is suitable and when it is not.

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is it possible to guarantee that the transition matrix learned by the spectral algorithms will be close to sparse? It is likely that there are many HMM models with full transition matrices that are similar to the sparse model with a block-diagonal structure that the authors are seeking. It is not clear that the matrix learned via the proposed algorithms will be sparse, even with small amounts of noise.

4. The experimental results do not accurately reflect the reality of noise in the transition matrix. For example, in Section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a transition matrix that has been permuted by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this is a paper about learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not just de-permuting matrices. A more realistic approach would be to sample observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then learn all the parameters from the observed data. This would provide a more accurate representation of how noise in observations and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and how difficult it is to de-permute the matrix in the presence of noise.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but it lacks sufficient information to evaluate the quality of the experiment. In particular, the specifics of the mixture of HMMs, including the parameters of the transition and observation matrices, are crucial for assessing the difficulty of learning the model from data. Providing more details about these parameters, possibly in an appendix, would be beneficial.

6. In Table 1, it is unclear why EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone. It would be expected that EM should only improve the results.

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrect; it should be K instead of 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small and difficult to read.

The paper presents some interesting ideas and good theoretical results. However, concerns remain about the practical applicability of the method to real-world datasets.
