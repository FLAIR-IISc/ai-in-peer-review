This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The core idea is to formulate an MHMM as a large HMM, allowing the authors to directly apply an existing spectral learning algorithm (Anandkumar et al., 2012). The main contribution is a novel stabilization-based step designed to resolve the parameter permutation ambiguity inherent in this formulation. The proposed method for addressing this permutation problem is intuitive and interesting, and it relies on the assumption that each component HMM's transition matrix has a single eigenvalue of 1. The analysis of the algorithm under estimation noise is also a valuable contribution, leading to an intriguing use of eigenvalues as described in Equation (7).

However, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed. The discussion of related work is insufficient; for instance, the idea of initializing EM with spectral estimates has been previously explored (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013) and should be cited. The experimental evaluation is also limited, and the overall presentation suffers from a lack of notational clarity.

Specific comments:

*   The terms in Corollary 1 are not clearly defined. To improve clarity, please explicitly define `1_J^T` (presumably a row vector of ones of length J) and elaborate on the structure of `lim_{e->inf} \bar{A}^e`, noting that it is a JK x JK block-diagonal matrix where the columns within each J x J block are identical.
*   Throughout the paper, vectors and matrices should be defined with their dimensions upon their first introduction. This would significantly improve readability.
*   Figure 4 is difficult to interpret and should be replaced with a table for clarity.
*   The performance of the EM baseline in Table 1 is surprisingly poor. Typically, EM with sufficient iterations, even from random initializations, is expected to outperform spectral methods despite issues with local optima. It is standard practice to report the best result achieved by EM over multiple runs, not the average. The authors should include this result to provide a more conventional and fair comparison.
*   A minor suggestion on notation: To improve accessibility for readers familiar with the literature, consider adopting the established notation from Hsu et al. (2009), such as using T for transition matrices (instead of A) and n, m for the number of observation and hidden states (instead of L, J).
