This paper presents a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), which is an extension of the standard Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang algorithm. The main contribution of the paper is a depermutation algorithm that addresses the issue of arbitrary permutations in the transition matrix recovered by the standard algorithm. This permutation mixes the various components of the mixture model, making it difficult to recover the individual HMMs.

The authors' approach is based on the observation that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. They use this representation to develop a spectral learning algorithm that can recover the parameters of the individual HMMs. The depermutation algorithm is a crucial component of this approach, as it allows the authors to recover the correct permutation of the transition matrix.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between theoretical and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the spectral properties of the transition matrix and show how these properties can be used to develop an efficient depermutation algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is its experimental evaluation, which includes a comparison with the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The results show that the spectral algorithm is computationally much cheaper than EM, while being comparable in accuracy. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a real-world dataset, where it is used as a good initialization scheme for EM.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. First, the authors could provide more context and relate their work to other papers in the field, such as Chaganty and Liang's work on spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions. This would help to situate their contribution within the broader literature.

Second, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the depermutation algorithm. While the authors provide a good intuitive explanation of how the algorithm works, they could provide more mathematical details and analysis of its performance.

Finally, the paper could be improved by a more careful proofreading. There are a few typos and minor errors throughout the text that detract from the overall quality of the paper.

The authors present a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to learning mixture models, and their experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.
