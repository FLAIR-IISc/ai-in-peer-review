Summary
The paper proposes a spectral approach to learning mixtures of hidden Markov models (MHMMs) by reducing the problem to learning a single HMM with a larger state space and then applying the spectral algorithm of Anandkumar et al. (2012) off the shelf. To address the parameter permutation ambiguity that arises in this reduction, the authors introduce a stabilization step that leverages a structural assumption on each component HMM—namely, that its transition matrix has a unique eigenvalue equal to 1. The paper also analyzes the effect of estimation noise, leading to an eigenvalue-based adjustment (Eq. 7) in the algorithm.

Strengths
- Simple, elegant reduction: Recasting MHMM learning as a special case of HMM learning enables the reuse of a well-studied spectral algorithm with minimal machinery.
- Intuitive stabilization: The permutation-resolving step is conceptually clear and practically motivated.
- Noise-aware analysis: The discussion around estimation noise and how it motivates the eigenvalue usage in Eq. (7) is interesting and adds credibility to the method.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and notation: Several definitions and notational choices are unclear or nonstandard, impeding readability.
- Related work: Important connections are under-cited; for example, initializing EM with spectral estimates has been explored (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013).
- Experimental evaluation: Experiments are limited, figures are hard to read, and the EM baseline appears unusually weak. Reporting practices could be improved to more fairly assess EM and the proposed method.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify the stabilization assumption:
  - Please state precisely that each component HMM’s transition matrix has a simple eigenvalue at 1 (i.e., algebraic and geometric multiplicity 1), with all other eigenvalues having magnitude strictly less than 1. This is the standard ergodicity condition; using “single eigenvalue of value 1” could be misread.
  - Discuss the practical scope of this assumption: does it hold for all datasets considered? What breaks if a chain is reducible or periodic, or if the second eigenvalue is very close to 1 (mixing is slow)? Some sensitivity analysis would be welcome.
- Formalize the MHMM-to-HMM reduction:
  - Clearly specify the enlarged state space, the resulting transition and emission structures, and how mixture weights are encoded. Provide the exact dimensions of all constructed matrices/tensors, and state any identifiability conditions required for the spectral step to succeed in the mixture setting.
  - Include a concise pseudocode or numbered algorithm describing the full pipeline: reduction, spectral estimation, stabilization, and post-processing.
- Notation and definitions:
  - In Corollary 1, define 1_J^T explicitly as the length-J row vector of ones.
  - Elaborate on the structure of lim_{e→∞} \bar{A}^e: it is a JK × JK block-diagonal matrix where each J × J block has identical columns (i.e., projects onto the stationary distribution of the corresponding component).
  - In general, define every vector/matrix upon first use and give dimensions. This will significantly improve readability.
  - Consider aligning with standard HMM notation (e.g., Hsu et al., 2009): use T for transitions, n and m for the numbers of observation and hidden states, etc., instead of A, L, J.
- Related work:
  - Expand the discussion of prior work on spectral learning for HMMs and mixtures, and on hybrid spectral–EM approaches. In particular, acknowledge that initializing EM with spectral estimates has precedent (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013) and position your contribution relative to it.
  - Clarify whether your stabilization step can be combined with EM refinement, and if so, whether this improves performance.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - Replace hard-to-read plots (e.g., Figure 4) with tables summarizing key metrics. Include mean, standard deviation, and number of trials. If curves are essential, supplement them with tables.
  - EM baseline:
    - Report the best performance across multiple random restarts (not only averages), as is standard practice, and specify the number of restarts.
    - Ensure sufficient iterations and a clear convergence criterion; EM is known to improve with more iterations even from poor initializations.
    - Provide details on initialization, hyperparameters, stopping criteria, and any regularization used for both methods.
    - Explain why EM underperforms in Table 1; this is atypical without careful tuning or sufficient restarts. Consider including a warm-started EM initialized with your spectral estimates.
  - Datasets and metrics:
    - Describe data generation (if synthetic) and the emission models used (discrete vs. Gaussian, parameter ranges). For real data, provide dataset statistics and preprocessing.
    - Report both parameter recovery (e.g., permutation-invariant distance) and predictive performance (likelihood or accuracy), with runtime comparisons.
  - Ablations:
    - Evaluate the impact of the stabilization step by comparing against the raw spectral estimates.
    - Test robustness to estimation noise, particularly in regimes where non-dominant eigenvalues approach 1.
- Theoretical aspects:
  - Provide conditions under which the permutation is guaranteed to be resolved by the stabilization step, and discuss failure modes (e.g., approximate multiplicities, near-degenerate spectra).
  - If possible, give bounds on the perturbation of the stabilized solution in terms of spectral gaps and sample size.
- Minor presentation issues:
  - Ensure all symbols in Eq. (7) are defined locally and that units/dimensions match.
  - When referring to figures and tables, make them self-contained with clear captions, axis labels, and legends.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a neat reduction from MHMM learning to standard HMM spectral learning and introduces a practical stabilization step to address parameter permutation, supported by a thoughtful noise-aware perspective. However, the submission currently suffers from clarity issues, incomplete positioning with respect to prior work, and limited/unclear experimental validation. With stronger exposition (precise definitions, standard notation), expanded citations, and a more thorough and fair empirical evaluation—especially of EM—the contribution could be significantly strengthened.
