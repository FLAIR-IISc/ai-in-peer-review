

This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a special case of a standard HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012) and address the critical challenge of parameter permutation that arises during the learning process. The key innovation is their method for resolving this permutation ambiguity using spectral properties of the global transition matrix, even in the presence of estimation noise. The paper demonstrates that this approach can recover the parameters of individual HMMs within the mixture and provides theoretical analysis for the noisy case, including a novel spectral longevity measure (Equation 7) for cluster identification.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on latent variable models by extending spectral learning techniques to MHMMs, a model that has been challenging to learn efficiently due to computational limitations of traditional methods like EM. This work builds on Anandkumar et al.'s (2012) spectral learning framework for HMMs and connects to related work on clustering sequences (Smyth, 1996) and mixture models. However, the authors could better acknowledge prior work on initializing EM with spectral estimates (Chaganty & Liang, 2013), which is directly relevant to their real-data experiments.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear proofs for the MHMM-HMM equivalence (Lemma 1) and the spectral approach for permutation resolution (Lemma 2, Corollary 1, Lemma 3). The noise analysis provides meaningful theoretical guarantees about the algorithm's behavior, and the experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's computational advantages over EM, particularly on larger datasets. The paper acknowledges limitations, including the requirement that MK < L for the method to work, and addresses the computational complexity trade-offs.

*Clarity:* The core idea is well-motivated and the technical development is logically presented. The authors effectively use matrix notation to simplify complex expressions. However, the paper would benefit from more precise notation definitions (e.g., explicitly defining 1^T_J as a row vector of ones) and consistent notation (using T for transition matrices as in Hsu et al., 2009 rather than A).

*Originality:* While the spectral learning approach builds on existing work, the specific application to MHMMs and the novel solution to the permutation problem constitute a meaningful contribution. The spectral longevity measure (Equation 7) is particularly clever and addresses a non-trivial challenge in mixture learning.

*Significance:* The computational advantages over EM are compelling, especially for large datasets where EM becomes prohibitively expensive. The real-data experiment shows promise as an initialization method for EM, which is valuable for practitioners. The approach could be extended to other hierarchical latent variable models, suggesting broader applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. The synthetic experiments (Section 4.1) show the algorithm works with sufficient data, but the real-data experiment (Section 4.3) doesn't provide enough detail about the dataset or the transformation applied to meet the MK < L constraint. The comparison with EM (Section 4.2) uses average accuracy rather than best-case accuracy, which is misleading since EM with multiple restarts typically outperforms random initialization.

*Clarity:* The paper would be significantly improved by:
1) Defining all vectors/matrices with dimensions upon first introduction
2) Clarifying the structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e (as a block-diagonal matrix with identical columns within each block)
3) Replacing Figure 4 with a table format for clearer presentation of results
4) Explaining why EM performs poorly in Table 1 (the authors report average accuracy rather than best-case accuracy, which is standard practice)

*Originality:* The paper could better differentiate itself from related work. The connection to Chaganty and Liang (2013) regarding EM initialization is particularly relevant and should be more thoroughly discussed.

*Significance:* While the computational benefits are clear, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this approach advances the state-of-the-art beyond the EM baseline. The accuracy comparisons with EM are not as strong as they could be due to the experimental design choices mentioned above.

**Recommendation:**

Pro:
- Novel solution to permutation ambiguity in MHMM learning
- Theoretical analysis of noise effects
- Significant computational advantages over EM
- Clear demonstration of the block-diagonal structure recovery
- Potential as EM initialization method

Con:
- Limited experimental evaluation (lack of best-case EM results)
- Inconsistent notation
- Insufficient discussion of related work (Chaganty & Liang, 2013)
- Basic experimental setup (Figure 4 should be a table)
- Unclear explanation of EM performance in Table 1

The paper presents a technically sound contribution that extends spectral learning methods to MHMMs with a novel solution to the permutation problem. While the core idea is strong, the presentation would benefit from clearer notation, better experimental reporting, and more thorough engagement with related work. The computational advantages are compelling, but the significance would be stronger with more rigorous comparisons to existing methods. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and experimental concerns.
