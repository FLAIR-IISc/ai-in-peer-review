

Summary: The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They propose using a spectral learning algorithm to estimate parameters for an HMM and then resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.  

Major Comments:  
1. The paper is generally well-written. However, the experimental results section requires significant expansion. Specifically, the authors should provide more detailed analysis of the empirical performance.  

2. The approach relies on expressing a mixture of HMMs as a single HMM, which contradicts the primary motivation for using mixtures: the block-diagonal structure imposes sparsity, making the model feasible for large-scale problems with limited data. The proposed algorithm first estimates the full HMM parameters and then reverses the permutation to recover block structure. This approach appears impractical for most real-world scenarios where the full HMM is intractable. The authors should explicitly discuss when their method is appropriate and when it is not.  

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. The authors do not address whether the spectral learning method guarantees (near) sparsity. It is plausible that many HMMs with full transition matrices are close to the sparse block-diagonal target, making sparsity non-robust under minimal noise. The paper should clarify whether sparsity can be guaranteed.  

4. The experimental design in Section 4.1 is problematic. The authors test on a permuted transition matrix with noise from a Dirichlet distribution, but this addresses matrix depermutation, not learning mixtures of HMMs. To evaluate the core contribution, the authors should generate observation data from a mixture of HMMs, learn all parameters from this data, and assess how noise in observations and limited data affect the depermutation process and final model quality.  

5. Section 4.2 partially addresses this, but the experiment lacks critical details. The specific parameters of the mixture HMM (transition and observation matrices) are omitted, yet these directly determine the difficulty of learning. The authors should provide these parameters (e.g., in an appendix) to enable meaningful evaluation of the method’s robustness.  

6. In Table 1, EM initialized with spectral learning performs worse than spectral learning alone. This contradicts expectations, as EM should refine the spectral solution. The authors must explain this counterintuitive result.  

Minor Comments:  
1. In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of $\Lambda$ in the bottom-right matrix should be $K$, not $2$.  
2. Text and numbers in figures are too small for readability.  

While the paper presents interesting theoretical insights, the practical feasibility of the method is questionable. The experimental validation does not adequately reflect real-world challenges, particularly regarding noise in observations and data scarcity. The authors should address these concerns to strengthen the contribution.
