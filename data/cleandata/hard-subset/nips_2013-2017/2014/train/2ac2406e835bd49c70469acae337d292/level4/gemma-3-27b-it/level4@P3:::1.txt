## Review of “Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models”

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to leverage the block-diagonal structure inherent in MHMMs, express it as a single HMM, apply a spectral learning algorithm, and then resolve the resulting permutation ambiguity using spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The paper is generally well-written and presents a novel approach to MHMM learning.

The authors correctly position their work within the context of recent MoM-based algorithms for latent variable models [1, 2, 3] and highlight the computational advantages they offer, particularly for large datasets. The problem of permutation ambiguity is well-defined, and the proposed method for addressing it is clearly explained. The theoretical justification for using spectral properties is a key contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of MoM and spectral learning for MHMMs is a novel approach. It offers a potentially more scalable alternative to traditional EM-based methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, particularly the lemmas relating MHMMs to single HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices, appears sound and provides a strong foundation for the proposed algorithm.
* **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is generally accessible, and the explanations are easy to follow.
* **Potential for Scalability:** The MoM approach intrinsically offers scalability advantages over EM due to its independence from the number of data points.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Applicability Concerns:** A major concern is the practical applicability of the approach, particularly regarding the assumption of accurate full HMM parameter estimation *before* recovering the block-diagonal structure. The authors acknowledge this to some extent, but a more detailed discussion of scenarios where this approach is suitable (and unsuitable) would be beneficial. The current presentation seems to assume a relatively noise-free estimation of the initial HMM parameters, which is unlikely in real-world applications.
* **Sparsity Guarantee:** The paper doesn't address the issue of sparsity in the learned transition matrix. While the underlying MHMM structure is sparse, the spectral learning process may not inherently enforce or encourage sparsity. The authors should discuss whether the learned transition matrix is likely to be sparse and, if not, the implications for computational efficiency and interpretability.  The possibility of multiple dense HMMs approximating the sparse block-diagonal structure is a valid concern and needs to be addressed.
* **Experimental Design:** The experimental setup, while providing some initial results, needs significant improvement. Section 4.1’s use of a permuted matrix as the primary test case feels somewhat artificial and doesn’t fully capture the challenges of learning from noisy observations. A more realistic evaluation should involve generating data from true MHMMs and then attempting to recover the parameters, including the cluster assignments.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experiments in Section 4.2 lack sufficient detail regarding the specific parameters of the mixture of HMMs (transition probabilities, emission distributions). This makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results. Providing these details, potentially in an appendix, would greatly enhance the evaluation.
* **EM Initialization Anomaly:** The observation in Table 1 that EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs *worse* than spectral learning alone is puzzling and warrants further investigation. The expectation is that EM should, at least, refine the initial parameters.
* **Figure Readability:** The text and numbers within the figures are too small, hindering their interpretability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MHMM learning.
* The use of spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity is a significant contribution.
* The MoM-based approach offers the potential for scalability advantages.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical applicability of the algorithm is questionable due to its reliance on accurate full HMM parameter estimation.
* The lack of a guarantee of sparsity in the learned transition matrix raises concerns about computational efficiency.
* The experimental evaluation is insufficient and doesn't adequately demonstrate the algorithm’s performance in realistic scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of MHMM learning. However, the concerns regarding practical applicability and the need for a more robust experimental evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I believe the paper has merit and could be significantly improved with further revisions.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I recommend rejection with a request for significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Provide a detailed discussion of the conditions under which their algorithm is most likely to succeed and fail.
2. Investigate methods for promoting sparsity in the learned transition matrix.
3. Conduct more realistic experiments, generating data from true MHMMs and evaluating the algorithm’s ability to recover the parameters accurately.
4. Provide more detailed experimental parameters and analyze the results more thoroughly.
5. Investigate and explain the anomalous results observed in Table 1.
6. Improve the readability of the figures.



**Minor Comments:**

1. In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix in the proof should be K, not 2.
2. Increase the font size in the figures for better readability.
