The authors present a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The algorithm is a straightforward extension of the Hsu, Kakade & Zhang algorithm, with the key difference being that the recovered transition matrix is an arbitrary permutation of the true matrix, effectively mixing the mixture components. To address this, the authors propose a depermutation algorithm that appears to perform reasonably well.

There are a few minor typos throughout the paper. For example, the phrase “For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy” would benefit from careful proofreading. 

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, specifically connections to papers such as Chaganty and Liang’s “Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions” (ICML, 2013). 

The authors effectively address the central challenge of “de-permuting” the results when using a spectral method to learn a mixture of HMMs. The core idea – leveraging spectral methods but accounting for the potential reordering of mixture components – is a valuable contribution. However, the presentation feels somewhat incremental. While the depermutation algorithm is a necessary addition given the spectral approach, its performance gain over simply running the standard algorithm multiple times with different random initializations isn’t fully justified. More extensive empirical evidence demonstrating a clear advantage would strengthen the paper considerably.

Specifically, the experiments primarily focus on synthetic data. While this allows for ground truth evaluation and control, it’s unclear how well the method generalizes to real-world HMM mixtures. Experiments on more realistic datasets would be highly valuable. Furthermore, the ablation studies could be expanded to analyze the sensitivity of the depermutation algorithm to its parameters and to compare its performance against simpler permutation strategies.

The theoretical analysis provides some justification for the approach, but it could be more detailed. For instance, exploring the conditions under which the depermutation algorithm is guaranteed to succeed, or providing bounds on its error rate, would be beneficial. 

Overall, the paper presents a sound technical contribution that addresses a practical issue in applying spectral methods to mixture models. However, the incremental nature of the work, the reliance on synthetic data, and the limited theoretical analysis prevent it from being a particularly strong submission. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly with regard to demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods and expanding the experimental evaluation.
