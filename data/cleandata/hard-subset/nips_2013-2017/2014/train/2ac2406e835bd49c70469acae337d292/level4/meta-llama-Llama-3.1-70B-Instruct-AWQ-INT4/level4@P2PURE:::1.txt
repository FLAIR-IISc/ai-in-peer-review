Summary: The authors consider the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models. They propose using a spectral learning algorithm to learn a set of parameters for a hidden Markov model, and then provide a method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-written overall, but the experimental results section could be expanded. Specifically, more details would be helpful in this section.

2. The authors' approach relies on the fact that a mixture of Hidden Markov Models can be expressed as a single HMM. However, one of the primary reasons for learning a mixture of HMMs instead of a single HMM is that the block diagonal structure of the mixture of HMMs is sparse. This sparsity is often necessary for problems that are too large to be learned with a full model, especially when the problem is large and the amount of training data is limited. Nevertheless, the authors' algorithm requires accurately estimating the full HMM parameters and then reversing the permutation to find the block diagonal structure. This approach seems impractical for most real-world problems. The authors should provide guidance on when their algorithm is suitable and when it is not.

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. It is unclear whether the transition matrix learned by the spectral algorithms proposed in this paper will be close to sparse. There may be many HMM models with full transition matrices that are similar to the sparse model with a block diagonal structure that the authors are seeking. It is not obvious that the matrix learned via the proposed algorithms will be sparse, even with small amounts of noise.

4. The experimental results may not accurately reflect the reality of noise in the transition matrix. For instance, in Section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a transition matrix that has been permuted by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this is a paper about learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not just depermuting matrices. A more accurate evaluation would involve sampling observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then learning all of the parameters back from the observed data. This would provide a better understanding of how noise in observations and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and how difficult it is to depermute the matrix in the presence of this noise.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but there is insufficient information to evaluate the quality of the experiment. Specifically, the details of the mixture of HMMs used in the experiment are unclear. The actual parameters of the transition and observation matrix are crucial for evaluating how easy or hard it is to learn the model from data. The authors should provide more information about these parameters, possibly in an appendix.

6. In Table 1, it is unclear why EM initialized with spectral learning ever performs worse than spectral learning alone. One would expect EM to only improve the results.

Minor Comments:

1. The subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix in the proof in Section 3.2.1 appears to be incorrect; it should be K, not 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small.

The paper presents some interesting ideas and good theoretical results. However, there are concerns that the method may not work well on real datasets in practice.
