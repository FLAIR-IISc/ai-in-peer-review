

This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) that leverages the method of moments (MoM) approach. The authors make the key observation that an MHMM can be viewed as a special case of a standard HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Building on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning framework for HMMs (COLT 2012), they address the critical challenge of permutation ambiguity that arises when applying standard MoM to MHMMs. The authors propose a novel stabilization procedure that exploits the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the individual HMM parameters. This is particularly significant as permutation ambiguity has been a fundamental barrier to applying MoM to MHMMs. The work relates well to prior NIPS contributions on spectral learning for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2009) and extends them to the more complex MHMM setting.

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The paper presents a technically sound approach that addresses a genuine challenge in MHMM learning. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with clear lemmas establishing the connection between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the permutation ambiguity issue and propose a novel solution based on spectral properties. Their experimental validation on both synthetic and real data demonstrates the algorithm's effectiveness and computational advantages over EM. The noise analysis in Section 3.2.2, while presented as conjectures, provides valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior under practical conditions.

*Originality*: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending spectral learning to MHMMs, a problem that has been largely overlooked due to the permutation ambiguity. The key insight of viewing MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal structure is both elegant and novel. The proposed depermutation technique using spectral properties of the transition matrix represents a creative solution to a non-trivial problem that has prevented previous applications of MoM to MHMMs.

*Significance*: MHMMs are important for sequence clustering applications, but their computational complexity has limited their use on large datasets. The proposed spectral approach offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM that scales better with dataset size. The paper demonstrates this through experiments showing comparable accuracy to EM with significantly reduced computation time. The real-data experiment on character trajectories further validates the practical utility of the approach, particularly as a good initialization for EM.

*Clarity*: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. However, the presentation would benefit from more precise notation and definitions. The paper would be significantly improved by defining all vectors and matrices with their dimensions upon first introduction, as this would enhance readability and reduce confusion.

**Weaknesses:**

*Clarity*: The paper uses inconsistent notation (e.g., L for observation dimension, J for hidden state count) that conflicts with standard conventions in the HMM literature (e.g., T for transition matrix, n/m for state dimensions). This inconsistency causes confusion, particularly in Section 3.2.1. The authors should adopt standard notation to align with prior work and improve accessibility.

*Clarity*: The explanation of Corollary 1 and the structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e would be clearer if explicitly defined. The paper should specify that 1_J^T represents a row vector of length J filled with ones, and clarify that lim_{e→∞} Ā^e is a block-diagonal matrix where each J×J block consists of identical columns (the stationary distributions of each HMM component).

*Clarity*: Figure 4 is difficult to interpret due to its graphical format. The authors should present accuracy and speed results in a table format for better readability and precision. Similarly, Table 1 in the real-data experiment would benefit from including the best EM accuracy (over 5 random initializations) rather than just the average.

*Originality*: The paper could better acknowledge related work, particularly Chaganty and Liang's (ICML 2013) exploration of using spectral methods for initializing EM in mixture models. This would strengthen the paper's positioning within the existing literature.

*Significance*: While the computational advantage is well-demonstrated, the paper could provide more specific context about the scale of datasets where the spectral approach becomes practically advantageous over EM. A more detailed comparison of computational complexity would strengthen the significance argument.

**Recommendation:**

This is a solid contribution that addresses a meaningful challenge in MHMM learning. The core insight is novel, the theoretical development is sound, and the experimental validation is convincing. The paper would be significantly improved with clearer notation, better presentation of results (tables instead of graphs), and more thorough engagement with related work. With these revisions, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**

*Pro:*
- Novel solution to permutation ambiguity in MHMM learning
- Theoretically grounded with clear lemmas
- Demonstrated computational advantage over EM
- Validated on both synthetic and real data
- Provides a good initialization method for EM

*Con:*
- Inconsistent notation with standard HMM literature
- Experimental results presented in confusing graphical format
- Insufficient engagement with related work on spectral initialization
- Limited discussion of computational complexity scaling

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to spectral learning methods for latent variable models. With minor revisions to notation, presentation, and literature engagement, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
