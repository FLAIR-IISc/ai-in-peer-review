## Review:

**Summary:** This paper addresses the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a spectral learning approach to estimate HMM parameters, followed by a permutation resolution technique to recover the underlying block-diagonal structure of the transition matrix.

**Major Comments:**

1. The paper is generally well-written and clear. However, the experimental evaluation could benefit from further detail and expansion.

2. The authors leverage the equivalence between a mixture of HMMs and a single HMM. A key motivation for employing a mixture of HMMs, rather than a single HMM, often stems from the sparsity inherent in the block-diagonal structure, enabling modeling of larger, more complex problems, particularly those with limited training data. However, the presented algorithm first estimates the full HMM parameters before attempting to recover the block-diagonal structure via permutation. This approach appears impractical for many real-world applications. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the scenarios where their algorithm is applicable and those where it is likely to struggle.

3. The practical recovery of a sparse transition matrix is a concern. It is unclear whether the spectral algorithms employed can guarantee (or even consistently produce) transition matrices that are close to sparse.  Many full transition matrices may approximate the desired sparse, block-diagonal structure, and it’s not obvious that the learned matrix from the proposed method will exhibit the necessary sparsity, particularly with noisy data.

4. The experimental results raise concerns regarding their relevance to realistic noise conditions. For instance, Section 4.1 evaluates performance on a permuted transition matrix with noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, the core problem is learning HMM *parameters* from data, not merely matrix depermutation. A more representative evaluation would involve generating observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then attempting to recover all parameters from these observations. This would better reflect the impact of observational noise and limited data on transition matrix depermutation.

5. Section 4.2 begins to address the concerns raised previously, but lacks sufficient detail for a thorough evaluation. Crucially, the characteristics of the underlying mixture of HMMs are not clearly defined. The specific parameters of the transition and observation matrices significantly influence the difficulty of model learning. Providing these details, potentially in an appendix, would greatly enhance the assessment of the experiment.

6. The results in Table 1 are puzzling. Why does EM initialization with spectral learning sometimes yield worse results than spectral learning alone? EM should, in principle, refine the initial estimates.



**Minor Comments:**

1. In the proof within Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix should be *K*, not 2.

2. The text and numbers within the figures are too small and difficult to read.

Overall, the paper presents interesting ideas and sound theoretical contributions. However, I have reservations about the practical applicability of the method to real-world datasets.
