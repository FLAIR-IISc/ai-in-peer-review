The authors present a spectral method for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The algorithm builds upon existing spectral methods for HMM learning, such as Hsu, Kakade, & Zhang (2013), but addresses the critical issue of permutation ambiguity when applied to mixture models – the recovered transition matrix represents an arbitrary permutation of the true components, effectively mixing them. The authors propose a depermutation algorithm that appears to work reasonably well, leveraging spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

The paper begins by correctly identifying the computational burden of traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) approaches for MHMMs, particularly with large datasets. They then present a compelling argument for using a Method of Moments (MoM) approach, benefiting from its computational advantages and uniqueness guarantees. The key insight – representing the MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix – is well-motivated and forms the foundation of their method. The subsequent development of the depermutation algorithm is the core contribution, and the authors detail a procedure based on analyzing the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the estimated transition matrix. They also provide a conjecture regarding the conditions necessary for successful depermutation and cluster number recovery.

The experimental results are a strong point of the paper. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both synthetic and real-world data. The comparisons with EM, both with random and MoM initialization, are insightful. The results showing comparable accuracy with significantly reduced computational cost are particularly noteworthy. The inclusion of a real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories further strengthens the paper's practical relevance.

However, the paper could benefit from more careful proofreading. There are occasional minor typos (e.g., phrasing in section 3.2.2 could be clearer). Additionally, while the authors reference related work on MoM and HMM learning, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of spectral methods for mixture models. Specifically, referencing Chaganty and Liang (2013) – "Spectral experts for estimating mixtures of linear regressions" – would be valuable, as it provides a relevant context for the authors’ depermutation strategy. The connection to that work, and how this paper extends or differs from it, should be explicitly addressed. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach addresses a significant challenge in spectral learning of MHMMs – the permutation ambiguity – with a novel depermutation algorithm.
*   **Significance:** The method offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM for learning MHMMs, potentially enabling applications with large datasets.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the depermutation algorithm, while presented as a conjecture, is well-motivated and appears plausible.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly explains the technical details of the algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on both synthetic and real data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Minor Typos/Clarity:**  A more careful proofread and some minor clarifications would improve readability.
*   **Limited Context:** The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work on spectral methods for mixture models.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a solid contribution with a clear technical novelty and promising experimental results. While some minor improvements in clarity and contextualization would be beneficial, the paper represents a significant advancement in spectral learning for MHMMs and is worthy of acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper.
