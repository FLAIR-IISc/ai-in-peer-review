

Summary: The authors address the challenge of learning a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) by leveraging spectral learning techniques. They establish that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, then propose a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral parameter estimation to recover the underlying block structure. This approach offers computational advantages over traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms.

Strengths:
- The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to resolving permutation ambiguity in MHMM learning using spectral properties of the global transition matrix.
- The connection between MHMM and HMM with block-diagonal transition matrices is clearly established through mathematical derivation.
- The experimental results demonstrate the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to EM, particularly for larger datasets.
- The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the spectral conditions required for successful depermutation.

Weaknesses:
1. While the paper is well-written overall, the experimental section could benefit from greater detail. Specifically, the authors should provide more comprehensive information about the parameters used in their experiments, particularly regarding the mixture of HMMs' actual structure.

2. The authors' approach relies on first estimating a full HMM and then reversing the permutation to recover the block-diagonal structure. However, one of the primary motivations for using MHMMs over a single HMM is the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure, which is particularly important when dealing with limited training data. The proposed method's dependence on accurately estimating the full HMM parameters before depermutation seems impractical for many real-world applications where data is scarce.

3. The transition matrix learned through the spectral algorithm is unlikely to be sparse in practice. The paper does not address how to guarantee that the learned transition matrix will be (close to) sparse, which is essential for the MHMM's intended application. It's unclear whether many HMMs with full transition matrices would be close to the sparse block-diagonal structure the authors seek.

4. The experimental results in Section 4.1 do not adequately reflect real-world noise conditions. Instead of sampling observation data from a mixture of HMMs and learning all parameters from the observed data, the authors test their depermutation algorithm on a transition matrix already perturbed by noise. This does not properly evaluate how observation noise and limited data affect the depermutation process.

5. Section 4.2 begins to address the practical impact of the method, but lacks sufficient detail to properly assess the quality of the experiment. The authors should provide more information about the specific parameters of the transition and observation matrices used in the experiments, as these details significantly impact the difficulty of learning the model from data.

6. In Table 1, the results show that EM initialized with the spectral method sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone. The authors should explain this counterintuitive result, as one would expect EM to improve results when properly initialized.

Minor Comments:
1. In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom right corner of the first matrix should be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small to be readable.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical contributions to MHMM learning with sound mathematical analysis. However, the practical applicability of the method appears questionable, particularly for real-world scenarios with limited data where the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure is most valuable. The authors should provide clearer guidance on when their approach is appropriate and when it might fail, as well as more comprehensive experimental validation that reflects real-world noise conditions.
