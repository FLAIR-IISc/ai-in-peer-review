This paper presents a method-of-moments based algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to view an MHMM with K components as a single, larger HMM whose transition matrix has a block-diagonal structure. This formulation allows the authors to apply existing spectral algorithms for HMMs to learn the global model parameters. However, this direct application recovers the parameters only up to an unknown permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components and prevents their recovery. The main contribution of this paper is a novel "depermutation" algorithm that exploits the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By identifying and clustering eigenvectors corresponding to the K largest eigenvalues, the method aims to group the permuted states back into their original HMM components, thereby recovering the parameters of the individual models. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Extending computationally efficient spectral methods to more complex hierarchical models like MHMMs is a valuable research direction. The central challenge in this endeavor is the component-mixing permutation ambiguity, and the proposed depermutation technique, which leverages the eigenspectrum of the transition matrix, is an elegant and novel solution for this model class.
*   **Quality and Practicality:** The proposed algorithm offers a significant computational advantage over the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be prohibitively slow on large datasets due to its iterative nature. By relying on moment computations and matrix factorization, the algorithm's runtime is independent of the dataset size after the initial moment calculation. The experiments demonstrate that the method is substantially faster than EM and can serve as an effective initialization strategy to improve EM's performance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper does a good job of explaining the central insight that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure. This key concept is clearly articulated and formally established in Section 3.1.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Contextualization and Related Work:** The paper could better position its contribution within the broader literature on spectral learning for mixture models. The problem of identifying and separating components from a spectrally learned mixture is not unique to MHMMs. For instance, the work of Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013) on mixtures of linear regressions addresses a very similar depermutation or "demixing" problem in a different context. Discussing such related work would help clarify the specific novelty of the proposed technique and place it in a richer scientific context.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical underpinnings of the depermutation algorithm, especially in the presence of estimation noise, could be strengthened. Key results supporting the method's robustness are presented as conjectures (Conjecture 1) or rely on strong assumptions (Lemma 3). For instance, the sample complexity argument in Conjecture 1 is borrowed from prior work on a different model and is not formally proven for the MHMM case. A more rigorous analysis of the conditions under which the K clusters are spectrally separable and the depermutation is guaranteed to succeed would significantly enhance the paper's contribution.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to correct several typographical errors and instances of awkward phrasing. Polishing the language would improve the overall clarity and readability of the paper.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the algorithm's potential, the evaluation could be more comprehensive. The real-data experiment shows that the spectral method is a strong initializer for EM but does not always surpass randomly initialized EM on its own. It would be valuable to include experiments on a wider variety of datasets and to analyze the algorithm's sensitivity to its assumptions—for example, how performance degrades as the spectral gap between the "global" and "local" eigenvalues closes. This would provide a clearer picture of the method's practical strengths and limitations.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and computationally efficient spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs, centered on a clever solution to the state permutation problem. While the core idea is strong, the paper could be improved by strengthening its theoretical analysis, providing a broader discussion of related work, and expanding the experimental evaluation.
