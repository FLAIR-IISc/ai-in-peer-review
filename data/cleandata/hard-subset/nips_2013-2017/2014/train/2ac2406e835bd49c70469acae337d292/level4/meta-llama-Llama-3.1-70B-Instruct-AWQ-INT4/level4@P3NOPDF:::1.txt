Summary:

This paper addresses the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a spectral learning algorithm to estimate the parameters of a single HMM, followed by a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix and recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-written, but the experimental results section could be expanded. In particular, more details about the experimental setup and results would strengthen the paper.

2. The authors' approach relies on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single HMM. However, one of the primary reasons for learning a mixture of HMMs instead of a single HMM is that the block-diagonal structure of the mixture is often sparse. This sparsity is particularly important when dealing with large problems and limited training data. The proposed algorithm, however, requires accurate estimation of the full HMM parameters before reversing the permutation to find the block-diagonal structure. This approach may not be feasible for most real-world problems. The authors should provide guidance on when their algorithm is suitable and when it is not.

3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix may not be sparse. Is it possible to guarantee that the transition matrix learned by the proposed spectral algorithm will be close to sparse? There may be many HMM models with full transition matrices that are similar to the sparse model with a block-diagonal structure that the authors are seeking. It is unclear whether the learned matrix will be sparse, especially in the presence of noise.

4. The experimental results may not accurately reflect the impact of noise on the transition matrix. For instance, in Section 4.1, the authors apply their algorithm to a permuted transition matrix with noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution. However, this setup does not accurately represent the problem of learning a mixture of HMM parameters. A more realistic approach would be to sample observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then learn the parameters from the observed data. This would provide a better understanding of how noise in observations and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of resolving the permutation.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, but it lacks essential details to evaluate its quality. Specifically, the parameters of the transition and observation matrices are crucial in assessing the difficulty of learning the model from data. The authors should provide more information about these parameters, possibly in an appendix.

6. In Table 1, the EM algorithm initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs worse than spectral learning alone. This is counterintuitive, as EM should typically improve the results. The authors should clarify this discrepancy.

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrect. It should be K, not 2.

2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small and difficult to read.

Overall, the paper presents interesting ideas and good theoretical results. However, the proposed method may be less effective in practice, particularly with real-world datasets.
