This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The core idea is to frame an MHMM as a special instance of a larger HMM, which allows for the direct application of an existing spectral learning algorithm (Anandkumar et al., 2012). To address the resulting parameter permutation problem, the authors introduce a novel stabilization-based method that relies on the assumption that each component HMM's transition matrix has a single eigenvalue of 1.

The proposed scheme for resolving parameter permutation is both intuitive and interesting. Furthermore, the analysis of the algorithm under estimation noise, which motivates a novel use of eigenvalues (Equation (7)), is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The notation is occasionally unclear, and the literature review appears incomplete; for instance, the idea of initializing EM with spectral estimates has been previously explored (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013). The experimental evaluation is also preliminary. The graphical format of Figure 4 makes results difficult to interpret, and a tabular format is strongly recommended.

Comments:
- The terms in Corollary 1 were initially confusing. The presentation would be improved by explicitly defining 1_J^T (e.g., as a row vector of J ones) and elaborating on the block-diagonal structure of lim_{e->inf} \bar{A}^e, noting that the columns within each J x J block are identical.
- To improve clarity, all vectors and matrices should be defined with their dimensions upon their first introduction.
- Figure 4 is difficult to read and should be replaced with a table. Additionally, the performance of the EM algorithm reported in Table 1 is surprisingly low. Typically, EM, even with random initializations, can outperform spectral methods given a sufficient number of iterations, despite issues with local optima. It is standard practice to report the best result achieved by EM across multiple runs, rather than the average; this result should be included for a more complete comparison.
- A minor suggestion: for consistency with prior work, consider adopting the established notation from Hsu et al. (2009), such as using T for transition matrices (instead of A) and n, m for the number of observation and hidden states (instead of L, J).
