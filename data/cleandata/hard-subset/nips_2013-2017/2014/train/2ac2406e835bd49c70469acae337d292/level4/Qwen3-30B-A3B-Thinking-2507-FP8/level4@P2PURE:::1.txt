

Summary: The authors address the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They propose using a spectral learning algorithm to estimate parameters for a single HMM and then resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.  

Major Comments:  
1. The paper is well-written overall, though the experimental results section could be expanded. Specifically, the description of experimental setups lacks sufficient detail.  
2. The authors rely on expressing a mixture of HMMs as a single HMM. However, a primary motivation for using a mixture of HMMs (rather than a single HMM) is to exploit the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure, which is essential for scalability when dealing with large problems and limited training data. In contrast, the proposed algorithm first estimates full HMM parameters and then reverses the permutation to recover sparsity. This approach appears impractical for most real-world scenarios where full parameter estimation is infeasible. The authors should clarify under what conditions their method is appropriate and when it is not.  
3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Can the authors guarantee that the spectral learning method will produce a (near) sparse transition matrix? I suspect many HMMs with full transition matrices may closely approximate the sparse block-diagonal structure the authors target. It is unclear whether the learned matrix will remain sparse under minimal noise.  
4. The experimental results do not adequately reflect real-world noise in transition matrices. For instance, Section 4.1 applies the algorithm to a permuted transition matrix with noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution—but this tests matrix depermutation, not mixture HMM learning. The authors should generate observation data from a mixture of HMMs, then learn all parameters from this data. This would better quantify how observation noise and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of depermutation.  
5. Section 4.2 begins to address this, but the experiment lacks sufficient detail for evaluation. The specific parameters of the mixture HMM (e.g., transition and observation matrices) are not provided, yet these critically determine learning difficulty. The authors should include these details in an appendix to enable meaningful assessment.  
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with spectral learning ever perform worse than spectral learning alone? EM should only improve results; this discrepancy requires explanation.  

Minor Comments:  
1. In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of $\Lambda$ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix should be $K$, not $2$.  
2. Text and numbers in figures are too small to read.  

The paper presents interesting ideas and strong theoretical results, but I am concerned that the method may not generalize well to real-world datasets due to the practical limitations outlined above.
