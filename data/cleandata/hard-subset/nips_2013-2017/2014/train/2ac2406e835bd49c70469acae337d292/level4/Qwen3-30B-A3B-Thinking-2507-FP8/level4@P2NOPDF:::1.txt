

Summary: This paper addresses learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) by first applying spectral learning to estimate a full HMM, then resolving permutation ambiguity to recover the underlying block-diagonal structure. The theoretical contributions are sound, but practical applicability raises significant concerns.

Major Comments:  
1.  The paper is well-written overall, though the experimental section requires substantial expansion. Key details about the data generation process and model specifications are missing, limiting the interpretation of results.  

2.  The core approach—learning a full HMM before extracting block structure—contradicts the primary motivation for using mixture HMMs. Mixture models are typically adopted precisely to exploit sparsity (block-diagonal structure) for scalability with limited data, avoiding the infeasibility of learning a full HMM. The authors’ method, however, requires accurate estimation of the full model *first*, which undermines the practical utility of their approach. The authors must clarify: *under what conditions is this method viable?* For instance, does it require unrealistically large datasets or negligible noise? Without such justification, the method appears inapplicable to real-world scenarios where sparsity is the key advantage.  

3.  Crucially, the learned transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse in practice. The authors provide no guarantee that spectral learning (as referenced in [1,2]) will yield a sparse matrix, even with minimal noise. Many full HMMs could closely approximate the target block-diagonal structure, making recovery ambiguous. This gap invalidates the premise that the method reliably recovers sparsity—a central claim of the work.  

4.  Section 4.1’s noise model is fundamentally misaligned with the problem. The authors test matrix permutation under Dirichlet noise but ignore the *observation process*—the core of HMM learning. To evaluate the method’s robustness, they should:  
    (a) Sample *observation sequences* from a true mixture HMM,  
    (b) Apply their full algorithm to recover parameters from these sequences,  
    (c) Assess how noise in *observations* and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and depermutation accuracy.  
    Without this, the noise experiments are irrelevant to the paper’s stated goal.  

5.  Section 4.2 partially addresses this but lacks critical details. The paper omits the *actual parameters* of the mixture HMM used (e.g., transition/observation matrices), which heavily influence learnability. For instance, matrices with near-identical blocks or weak emission probabilities would be exceptionally hard to recover. These details are essential for assessing the method’s feasibility; they should be included in an appendix to conserve space.  

6.  Table 1 presents a puzzling result: spectral learning alone outperforms EM initialized with spectral learning. Since EM should refine spectral estimates, this outcome suggests either a flaw in the implementation or an overfitting issue. The authors must explain this discrepancy—otherwise, it undermines confidence in their algorithm.  

Minor Comments:  
1.  In Section 3.2.1’s proof, the bottom-right subscript of $\Lambda$ should be $K$, not $2$.  
2.  Figure text and numerical labels are too small to read; please increase font size.  

Overall, the paper presents a technically valid theoretical framework but overlooks the practical context that motivates mixture HMMs. The method’s reliance on full-model estimation before enforcing sparsity renders it impractical for the very problems it aims to solve. While the spectral learning component is sound, the experimental validation fails to demonstrate utility under realistic noise and data constraints. The authors should either:  
- Provide a clear use-case where full-model estimation is feasible (e.g., tiny state spaces), or  
- Revise the approach to directly leverage sparsity during learning (e.g., via constrained spectral methods).  

Without this, the work’s contribution remains limited to a niche theoretical setting rather than advancing practical mixture HMM learning. The paper’s potential is promising but currently unfulfilled.
