

The paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) by recognizing that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors address the permutation ambiguity in spectral learning by leveraging the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the underlying block structure. This extends existing spectral learning methods for HMMs to the more complex MHMM setting.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is sound and well-justified. The authors establish the MHMM-HMM equivalence (Lemma 1) and provide a principled approach to resolving permutation ambiguity through spectral properties (Lemmas 2-3 and Conjecture 1). The mathematical formulations are clear and logically presented.

2. The paper is well-written with excellent organization and clear exposition. The theoretical development flows naturally from model definitions to the proposed algorithm, making it accessible to readers familiar with HMMs and spectral learning.

3. The experimental evaluation includes synthetic data demonstrating the algorithm's ability to recover the correct number of clusters and block-diagonal structure, as well as a real-world application on handwritten character trajectories. The comparison with EM shows computational advantages that are promising for large-scale applications.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper fails to adequately address when this approach would be practically useful compared to standard EM. The primary motivation for using MHMMs is the sparse block-diagonal structure, yet the proposed method first estimates a full HMM before recovering the block structure. This seems counterproductive for problems where sparsity is the key advantage of MHMMs. The authors should clarify the specific scenarios where their method would be preferable to EM.

2. The paper does not address whether the learned transition matrix will be (close to) sparse, which is critical for MHMMs. The spectral learning approach may produce dense transition matrices, making block recovery difficult. The authors should discuss conditions under which the learned matrix would maintain sparsity.

3. The experimental evaluation in Section 4.1 tests the depermutation algorithm on a permuted transition matrix rather than on data generated from an MHMM. This does not reflect the actual learning problem, as noise should arise from observing data, not from artificially permuting a known matrix. The authors should evaluate their method on data generated from MHMMs and learn all parameters from observed data.

4. Section 4.2 lacks sufficient detail about the underlying MHMM model. Without specific parameters for the transition and observation matrices, it's impossible to assess the difficulty of the learning problem. The authors should provide these details (e.g., in an appendix) to allow proper evaluation.

5. Table 1 shows EM initialized with spectral learning performing worse than spectral learning alone, which contradicts expectations. The authors should explain this surprising result, as it undermines the proposed initialization approach.

6. The paper does not sufficiently address the practical challenges of applying this method to real-world datasets. The real data experiment is limited to a single dataset with specific parameter choices, and the authors do not analyze performance on more challenging datasets.

Quality: The theoretical results are well-supported and technically sound. However, the experimental evaluation does not fully align with the problem statement, as it tests the depermutation algorithm rather than the complete learning pipeline from raw observations.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with excellent mathematical exposition. The main weakness is the experimental section, which would benefit from more detailed descriptions of the model parameters and experimental setup.

Originality: The extension of spectral learning to MHMMs is novel, particularly the approach to resolving permutation ambiguity using spectral properties. The connection to existing spectral learning methods for HMMs is well-established, and the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to this area.

Significance: The method could be valuable for large-scale MHMM learning where EM is computationally prohibitive. However, the practical utility is limited by the requirement to learn the full HMM first, which may not be advantageous when the block-diagonal structure is the primary reason for using MHMMs. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the problem settings where this approach provides a genuine advantage.

Pro:
- Strong theoretical contribution with well-justified results
- Excellent writing and organization
- Demonstrated computational advantages over EM
- Clear connection to existing spectral learning methods

Con:
- Insufficient discussion of practical applicability
- Experimental evaluation does not reflect the actual learning problem
- Lack of details about model parameters in experiments
- No analysis of sparsity in learned transition matrices
- Unexplained performance discrepancy in Table 1

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to spectral learning methods. The main weakness is the lack of clarity about when this approach would be practically useful compared to existing methods. The experimental evaluation needs to better reflect the actual learning problem by testing on data generated from MHMMs rather than on artificially permuted transition matrices. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be accepted. I recommend a borderline decision with a strong recommendation for revision.
