### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using the Method of Moments. The proposed method first employs a spectral learning algorithm to learn the parameters of a single, larger HMM that is equivalent to the MHMM. It then introduces a technique for resolving the resulting state permutation ambiguity by analyzing the spectral properties of the learned transition matrix. This allows the recovery of its underlying block-diagonal structure, thereby identifying the parameters of the individual HMMs in the mixture.

**Major Comments**

1.  The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the experimental results section could be significantly strengthened with more detail and more realistic evaluations.

2.  The core idea of the paper is that an MHMM can be expressed as a single, larger HMM. However, a primary motivation for using an MHMM over a single large HMM is precisely to exploit the sparse, block-diagonal structure of its transition matrix. This structure makes the model more constrained and easier to learn, particularly when the state space is large and training data is limited. The proposed algorithm, in contrast, requires first learning the parameters of the full, non-sparse HMM and then recovering the block-diagonal structure. This seems to defeat the purpose of using an MHMM, and it is unclear how well the initial spectral learning step would perform in a realistic, data-scarce scenario. For this approach to be practical, the authors should discuss the conditions under which it is applicable. When might this two-step approach be preferable to alternatives?

3.  A related concern is that the transition matrix estimated from data using spectral methods is unlikely to be truly block-diagonal; it will be a full matrix with noise. The authors' depermutation method relies on clustering the columns of this matrix after exponentiation. However, it is plausible that many different full transition matrices are statistically close to the true block-diagonal model. There is no guarantee that the spectral learning algorithm will recover a matrix that is merely a permutation of a block-diagonal one plus a small amount of noise. It might instead find another full matrix that explains the data equally well. The paper needs to provide more convincing arguments or evidence that the learned matrix will reliably possess a structure that can be successfully depermuted.

4.  The experiment in Section 4.1 aims to test the depermutation algorithm's robustness to noise. However, the experimental setup does not appear to model the end-to-end learning process realistically. A much more convincing evaluation would be to first generate observation sequences from a ground-truth MHMM, then apply the full spectral learning pipeline to estimate the global HMM parameters from these observations, and finally apply the depermutation algorithm to the learned transition matrix. This would provide a clearer picture of how noise from limited and variable observations propagates through the moment estimation and parameter learning stages, and how this ultimately affects the ability to recover the block-diagonal structure. The current experiment does not adequately demonstrate the algorithm's performance under these more realistic conditions.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 is a step in the right direction, comparing the proposed method with EM. However, it lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the results. The difficulty of the learning task depends heavily on the specific parameters of the ground-truth MHMM used for data generation. For instance, the separability of the emission distributions and the mixing rates of the transition matrices are crucial factors. The authors should provide the specific parameters used for the transition and observation matrices, perhaps in an appendix, to allow for a proper evaluation of the experimental setup.

6.  In Table 1, the results for "EM initialized with MoM" are sometimes worse than for the spectral learning (MoM) method alone. This is counterintuitive, as one would expect the EM algorithm, which monotonically increases data likelihood, to improve upon or at least maintain the quality of its initialization. Could the authors explain this outcome? Is it possible that EM, while finding a solution with higher likelihood, converges to a local optimum that corresponds to a worse clustering of the sequences?

**Minor Comments**

1.  In the proof for Lemma 2 (Section 3.2.1), I believe the subscript of the bottom-right block in the eigendecomposition of Ā should be K, i.e., VₖΛₖVₖ⁻¹, not V₂Λ₂V₂⁻¹.

2.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small, making them difficult to read.

**Overall Assessment**

In summary, the paper presents an interesting approach with some appealing theoretical properties. However, I have significant concerns about the practical viability and evaluation of the proposed method. The core assumption that one can reliably learn a large, dense transition matrix and then depermute it seems questionable for many real-world scenarios where MHMMs are typically applied. The experiments do not yet provide sufficient evidence to alleviate these concerns.
