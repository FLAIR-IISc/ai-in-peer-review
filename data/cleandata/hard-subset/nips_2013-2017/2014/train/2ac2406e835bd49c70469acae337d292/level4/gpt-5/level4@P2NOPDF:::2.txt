This paper introduces a spectral method for learning mixtures of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The key idea is to reformulate an MHMM as a single HMM with an expanded hidden state space, enabling the direct use of an existing spectral HMM learner (Anandkumar et al., 2012) without modification. To resolve the parameter permutation (label-switching) issue across mixture components, the paper proposes a stabilization step based on the assumption that each component’s transition matrix has a unique eigenvalue at 1.

While the approach is conceptually appealing, there are several issues that weaken the current presentation. The notation is often unclear, and many vectors/matrices are introduced without dimensions. The related-work discussion is thin: for example, initializing EM with spectral estimates has been studied by Chaganty and Liang (2013), and such connections should be acknowledged. The experimental evaluation is basic and difficult to read, in part due to the graphical presentation; reporting results in tables would substantially improve clarity. Moreover, EM’s poor performance is surprising; with sufficient iterations and restarts, EM typically matches or outperforms spectral methods despite local optima.

On the positive side, the stabilization scheme for addressing parameter permutation is interesting and intuitive. The analysis that accounts for estimation noise and motivates the use of eigenvalues in the algorithm (e.g., Equation (7)) is a notable contribution.

Comments and suggestions:
- Corollary 1 is hard to parse on first read. Please define 1_J^T explicitly as the length-J row vector of ones, and describe the structure of lim_{e->∞} \bar{A}^e: it is a JK-by-JK block-diagonal matrix whose J-by-J blocks have identical columns.
- Define every vector/matrix and its dimensions when first introduced. This will greatly improve readability.
- Replace Figure 4 with tables that report means, standard deviations, and sample sizes. Also clarify the EM setup (number of restarts, iterations, convergence criteria, and initialization). In addition to averages, report the best EM accuracy across restarts, which is standard practice and a fairer comparison.
- Related work: cite prior uses of spectral estimates to initialize EM (e.g., Chaganty and Liang, 2013) and discuss how your stabilization differs from or complements existing identifiability strategies in spectral HMM literature.
- Notation: consider aligning with established conventions (Hsu et al., 2009): use T for transition matrices (instead of A) and n, m for the numbers of observation and hidden states (instead of L, J). Consistency with common notation will help readers.
- Clarify the eigenvalue assumption. For stochastic transition matrices, 1 is always an eigenvalue; specify that it is simple (algebraic and geometric multiplicity 1) and discuss how violations (e.g., nearly reducible chains or multiple recurrent classes) affect the stabilization and the robustness analysis.
- Provide more implementation details for reproducibility (regularization, numerical stabilization, handling of small singular values, and how Equation (7) is computed in finite samples).

In summary, the paper proposes an MHMM learning procedure that applies a standard spectral HMM estimator followed by a novel stabilization step to undo parameter permutation. The idea is promising, but the paper would benefit from clearer notation, stronger experimental methodology and reporting, and a more thorough positioning within related work.
