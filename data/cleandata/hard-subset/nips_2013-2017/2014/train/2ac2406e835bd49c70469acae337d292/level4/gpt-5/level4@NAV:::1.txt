Review- Summary: The authors study the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). They first propose using a spectral learning algorithm to estimate parameters for a single HMM, and then introduce a method to resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:
1. Overall, the paper is clearly written. However, the experimental results section needs more depth and detail, particularly to help interpret the findings.
2. The approach hinges on the fact that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single HMM. A primary motivation for learning a mixture rather than a single HMM is the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure, which is useful when the model is large and data are limited. In contrast, the proposed algorithm first requires accurately estimating the full HMM and only then reversing the permutation to reveal the block-diagonal structure. For many real-world problems this seems impractical. Could the authors clarify when their method is appropriate and when it is not?
3. In practice, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse. Is there any way to ensure that the transition matrix produced by the suggested spectral methods is (approximately) sparse? It seems plausible that many full (dense) HMMs are close to the desired sparse, block-diagonal model. It is not obvious that the matrix learned via [1,2] will be sparse under small amounts of noise.
4. I am concerned that the experiments do not reflect realistic noise in the learned transition matrix. In Section 4.1, the algorithm is applied to a transition matrix perturbed by Dirichlet noise, but the paper is about learning mixtures of HMMs, not merely depermuting matrices. Wouldn’t it be more appropriate to sample observation sequences from a mixture of HMMs and then learn all parameters from the observed data? This would more accurately show how observation noise and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of depermutation.
5. Section 4.2 moves in this direction, but there is insufficient information to assess the experiment’s quality. The actual mixture of HMMs is unspecified. The precise transition and observation parameters critically affect how easy or hard it is to learn from data. Please provide these specifics (an appendix would suffice if space is tight).
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialized with spectral estimates sometimes perform worse than spectral learning alone? In principle, EM should not degrade performance—please explain.

Minor Comments:
1. In the proof of Section 3.2.1, the subscript of Lambda in the bottom-right entry of the first matrix should be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers in the figures are much too small.

The paper presents interesting ideas and strong theoretical results, but I am concerned about its practicality on real datasets.
