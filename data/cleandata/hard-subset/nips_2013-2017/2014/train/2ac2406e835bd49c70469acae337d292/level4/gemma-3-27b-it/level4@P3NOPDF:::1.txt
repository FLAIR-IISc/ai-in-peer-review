## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

**Summary:** This paper addresses the problem of learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose a spectral learning algorithm to estimate HMM parameters, followed by a method to resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix and recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

**Major Comments:**

This paper presents a well-written and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem. However, I have concerns regarding its practical applicability and the validity of the experimental evaluation. 

My primary concern revolves around the reliance on accurately estimating the *full* HMM parameters as a precursor to recovering the block-diagonal structure. A key motivation for employing a mixture of HMMs, especially in large-scale problems with limited data, is the sparsity afforded by the block-diagonal form. Learning a full HMM is often intractable in such scenarios. The proposed algorithm, however, appears to circumvent this advantage by first learning a dense model and *then* attempting to identify the block structure. This seems counterintuitive and potentially computationally prohibitive for the very problems where mixture models are most beneficial.  The authors should address under what conditions their approach might be suitable – for example, when dealing with relatively small state spaces or abundant data – and explicitly discuss its limitations concerning scalability and data requirements. 

Relatedly, there is little discussion of whether the spectral learning algorithm itself can generate sparse or near-sparse transition matrices.  It is plausible that multiple HMMs with full transition matrices could approximate a sparse, block-diagonal model, especially with even a small amount of noise. The algorithm might therefore converge to a solution that, while theoretically correct, lacks the desired sparsity characteristics. Exploring techniques to encourage sparsity within the spectral learning step, or analyzing the sensitivity of the learned matrices to noise, would strengthen the paper.

The experimental evaluation, as presented, does not convincingly demonstrate the method's robustness to realistic noise conditions. Section 4.1’s use of a permuted transition matrix as input, while a useful initial test, is insufficient. The core problem is learning HMM parameters from *observations*, not merely depermuting matrices. A more informative evaluation would involve generating observation data from mixtures of HMMs and then attempting to recover the underlying parameters – including the transition matrix – from this data. This would better reflect the impact of observation noise and limited data on the depermutation process.

Section 4.2 begins to address this concern, but lacks crucial detail. Knowing the general setup is insufficient; understanding the specific parameters of the mixture HMMs (transition and observation matrices) is essential for evaluating the difficulty of the learning task and interpreting the results.  The authors could include this information in an appendix if space is a constraint.

Finally, the results in Table 1 are puzzling. Why does initializing EM with spectral learning *sometimes* yield worse performance than spectral learning alone? EM is designed to improve upon an initial parameter estimate, so this outcome warrants further investigation and explanation.

**Minor Comments:**

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix should be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers in the figures are impractically small, hindering readability.

**Overall:**

The paper presents interesting theoretical contributions and a novel approach to learning mixtures of HMMs. However, my concerns about the practical limitations of the algorithm and the insufficient validation with realistic noise make me question its likely impact on real-world applications. While the theoretical results are solid, I believe significant revisions addressing the issues raised above are necessary before this work can be considered a strong contribution.
