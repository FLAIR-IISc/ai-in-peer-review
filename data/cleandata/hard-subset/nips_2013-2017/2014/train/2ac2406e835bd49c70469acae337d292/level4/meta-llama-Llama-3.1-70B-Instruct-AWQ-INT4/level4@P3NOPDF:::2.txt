This paper proposes a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMM). The authors demonstrate that an existing spectral algorithm for learning hidden Markov models (HMMs) (Anandkumar et al., 2012) can be applied directly to MHMMs by formulating the latter as a special case of the former. A key contribution of the paper is a stabilization-based solution for addressing the issue of parameter permutation, which is achieved by introducing an assumption that each HMM's transition matrix has a single eigenvalue of value 1.

While the proposed approach has merit, there are several areas that require improvement. Notably, the notation used throughout the paper is not consistently clear, and the authors would benefit from providing more context and definitions for the variables and matrices introduced. For instance, the terms in Corollary 1 are initially confusing, and it would be helpful to explicitly define 1J^T (a row vector of length J filled with ones) and elaborate on the structure of lim{e->inf} bar{A}^e (a JK x JK block diagonal matrix where the columns within each J x J block are the same). Additionally, defining each vector/matrix along with its dimensions when it is first introduced would greatly enhance the presentation.

The authors should also be more aware of related work in the field. For example, the idea of initializing EM with spectral estimates has been explored previously in Chaganty and Liang (2013), and a reference to this work would be a welcome addition.

The experimental evaluation is somewhat basic and could be improved by reporting results in a table format rather than the graphical format presented in Figure 4. This would facilitate easier comparison and interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the performance of EM in Table 1 is surprisingly poor, and it is unclear why this is the case. Typically, even with random initialization, EM is able to achieve better performance than spectral methods with sufficient iterations, despite the issue of local optima. A standard practice is to report the best (rather than average) accuracy of EM, and including this result would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's performance.

On a minor note, conforming to the established notation of Hsu et al. (2009) would improve the readability of the paper. Specifically, using T for transition matrices (instead of A) and n, m for numbers of observation and hidden states (instead of L, J) would be beneficial.

Despite these areas for improvement, the proposed scheme for addressing the problem of parameter permutation is interesting and intuitive. The authors also provide an analysis for the case with estimation noise, which leads to an intriguing usage of eigenvalues (Equation (7)) in the algorithm. Overall, with some revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of MHMM learning.
