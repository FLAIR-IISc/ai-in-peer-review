

Summary: This paper addresses learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) by first applying spectral learning to estimate a single HMM's parameters and then resolving permutation ambiguity to recover the underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:
1.  The paper is generally well-written. However, the experimental section requires more detail. Specifically, the evaluation lacks sufficient depth to support the claims.

2.  The authors' approach relies on representing a mixture of HMMs as a single HMM, which contradicts the primary motivation for using mixtures: the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure. This sparsity is crucial for handling large-scale problems with limited data, where a full HMM would be infeasible. The proposed method, however, first estimates the full HMM parameters accurately before reversing the permutation. For most real-world applications with limited data and noisy observations, this two-step process is highly impractical. The authors should clarify under what specific conditions their algorithm becomes viable and where it would fail.

3.  Critically, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse in practice. The paper does not establish how spectral learning (citing [1,2]) guarantees sparsity (or near-sparsity) of the learned transition matrix. It is unclear whether models with full transition matrices could closely approximate the target block-diagonal structure, especially under minimal noise. Without this guarantee, the method's applicability to sparse mixture models remains questionable.

4.  The experimental results in Section 4.1 do not reflect realistic noise in the context of learning from observations. The authors test their depermutation algorithm on a transition matrix perturbed by Dirichlet noise, but this is not a test of learning mixture HMM parameters from data. To properly evaluate the method, they should generate observation sequences from a *mixture of HMMs*, then learn all parameters (including the block structure) from these sequences. This would reveal how noise in observations and limited data impact the ability to recover the block-diagonal structure.

5.  Section 4.2 moves closer to a meaningful evaluation but lacks critical details. The quality of the results cannot be assessed without knowing the specific mixture HMM parameters used (e.g., transition and emission matrices). These parameters significantly influence the difficulty of learning. The authors should provide these details, potentially in an appendix, to enable proper evaluation of the experiment's difficulty and results.

6.  Table 1 shows spectral learning alone outperforming EM initialized with spectral learning. This is counterintuitive, as EM should refine the spectral estimate. The authors must explain this discrepancy.

Minor Comments:
1.  In Section 3.2.1, the subscript of $\Lambda$ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix should be $K$, not $2$.
2.  Figure text and numbers are too small for readability.

The paper presents interesting theoretical insights and a novel approach to resolving permutation ambiguity. However, the core method's reliance on accurately estimating a full HMM before recovering sparsity—without guarantees of sparsity or realistic experimental validation—raises serious doubts about its practical utility for learning mixture HMMs from real data. The experimental design fails to address the fundamental challenge of learning from observed sequences, making the results misleading. The authors must address these critical gaps to justify the method's applicability.
