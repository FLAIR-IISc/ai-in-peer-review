Summary
The paper studies learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The authors propose first estimating a single HMM using spectral methods, then resolving the permutation ambiguity in the estimated transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure corresponding to the mixture components.

Strengths
- Clear exposition of the core idea and a clean algebraic perspective on permutation resolution for block-diagonal transitions.
- Theoretical contributions appear sound and potentially useful when the block structure is strong and the spectral estimates are sufficiently accurate.

Major Comments
1) Scope and experimental depth
- The paper is generally well written, but the experimental section is too brief to assess practical viability. Key experimental details and stress tests are missing (see comments below). Please expand the empirical evaluation and include essential setup details, or add an appendix if space is limited.

2) Practicality of the two-step approach
- The proposed method hinges on first accurately estimating a full HMM, then depermuting the transition matrix to recover block structure. In many real-world problems with limited data, estimating the full HMM robustly is challenging, and the spectral estimate may not reflect the desired sparsity. Please clarify:
  - Under what regimes (e.g., sample size, noise levels, mixture separation, number of states per component, block sizes, identifiability conditions) is your approach expected to work well?
  - When would you not recommend using this method (e.g., weak separation between components, heavy model mismatch, high observation noise)?
  - How sensitive is the permutation resolution to errors in the spectral estimates?

3) Sparsity and robustness of the recovered transition matrix
- In practice, spectral estimators need not produce sparse transitions, even when the truth is block-diagonal. Small perturbations can yield dense matrices close to a sparse model. Please discuss whether your method can:
  - Theoretically guarantee near-sparsity under perturbation (e.g., via stability bounds that translate to recoverable block structure), or
  - Practically enforce sparsity (e.g., via post-processing, L1-regularized projections, thresholding with error control).
- A comparison between your depermutation method on the raw spectral matrix vs. a sparsity-promoting refinement would be informative.

4) Synthetic setup in Section 4.1
- Section 4.1 perturbs a known block-diagonal matrix via a Dirichlet noise and evaluates depermutation. This does not reflect the end-to-end learning problem studied in the paper (learning from observed sequences). To better reflect realistic noise, please:
  - Generate observation sequences from a known mixture of HMMs,
  - Estimate the HMM parameters using your spectral method,
  - Then run the depermutation step and evaluate recovery.
- This would quantify how observation noise, finite-sample effects, and estimation error propagate to the transition matrix and affect depermutation accuracy.

5) Missing details in Section 4.2
- The simulation in Section 4.2 is promising but underspecified. Please report:
  - The mixture size, hidden-state counts per component, and block sizes.
  - Transition and emission parameters (including sparsity levels), mixture weights, observation alphabet or emission model, sequence lengths, number of sequences, train/test splits, and initialization.
  - Metrics (e.g., block-recovery accuracy, clustering purity, per-state permutation error, train/test log-likelihood), number of runs, and variance.
  - The degree of separation between components and any identifiability assumptions.
- Without these details, it is difficult to evaluate the difficulty of the task and the generality of the results.

6) EM initialized by spectral vs. spectral alone (Table 1)
- Table 1 shows cases where EM initialized by spectral performs worse than spectral alone. Please clarify:
  - Are you reporting train or test performance? EM should not decrease the training likelihood, but test metrics can worsen due to overfitting or local minima.
  - What are the convergence criteria, number of iterations, and any regularization or constraints?
  - How is performance measured—likelihood, parameter error, recovery of block structure, or prediction accuracy?
- Consider including both train/test likelihoods and structure-recovery metrics, and report variability across random seeds.

Minor Comments
1) Notation: In Section 3.2.1, in the proof, the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix appears to be K rather than 2.
2) Figures: Text and numbers in the figures are too small to read. Please enlarge fonts and line widths and ensure axis labels and legends are legible.

Overall Assessment
The paper presents an appealing theoretical framework for recovering block-diagonal structure in mixtures of HMMs using spectral estimation followed by permutation resolution. However, I am concerned about the practical applicability of the two-step approach in realistic settings, where spectral estimates may be noisy and not sparse, and where end-to-end recovery from observations can be substantially harder than depermuting a perturbed ground-truth matrix. Strengthening the empirical section with fully specified simulations that start from observed sequences, reporting comprehensive metrics, and clarifying the regimes where the method is most effective would significantly improve the paper.
