This paper proposes a method of moments based algorithm for learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The authors first suggest using a spectral learning algorithm to learn a set of parameters for a hidden Markov model, and then provide a method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The experimental results section could be fleshed out a bit, but overall, the paper is well-organized and easy to follow.

The authors' approach relies on the fact that a mixture of Hidden Markov Models can be expressed as a single HMM. This is a useful insight, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how to leverage this fact to learn the parameters of the MHMM.

One potential concern is that the authors' algorithm relies on first accurately estimating the full HMM parameters, then reversing the permutation in order to find the block diagonal structure. For most real-world problems, this may be computationally expensive, especially if the problem is large and the amount of training data is small.

The authors' experimental results show that their approach can be effective in certain cases, but more work is needed to demonstrate its robustness to noise and its ability to generalize to more complex problems.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on clarity and originality. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, and their use of spectral learning to resolve the permutation ambiguity is a novel contribution.

However, the paper could be improved in terms of significance and quality. While the authors provide some experimental results, more work is needed to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of their approach. Additionally, the authors could provide more discussion of the limitations of their approach and potential avenues for future work.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with the suggestion that the authors provide more experimental results and discussion of the limitations of their approach.
