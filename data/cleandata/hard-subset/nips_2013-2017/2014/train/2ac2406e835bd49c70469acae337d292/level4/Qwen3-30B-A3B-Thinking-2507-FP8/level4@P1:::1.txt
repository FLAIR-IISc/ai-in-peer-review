

Summary: This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The authors demonstrate that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, allowing them to leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. They then develop a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the estimated transition matrix, which is necessary to recover the individual HMM components. The paper provides theoretical analysis of the spectral properties required to identify the number of clusters and recover the block-diagonal structure, along with experimental validation on synthetic and real data.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the theoretical contributions. However, the experimental section would benefit from more detailed descriptions. For instance, the parameters of the synthetic models used in experiments should be specified more thoroughly to allow for proper evaluation of the method's performance under various conditions.

2. The authors' approach relies on first estimating a full HMM with a block-diagonal structure and then resolving the permutation ambiguity to recover the underlying MHMM components. This is problematic because the primary motivation for using MHMMs over single HMMs is precisely to exploit the sparsity of the block-diagonal structure, which enables learning on larger datasets with limited training data. The proposed method, however, requires accurate estimation of the full transition matrix before recovering the block structure, which seems counterproductive for the very cases where MHMMs are most useful. The authors should clarify the specific scenarios where their approach would be advantageous compared to directly learning the MHMM structure.

3. A critical issue is the assumption that the estimated transition matrix will be close to block-diagonal. In practice, spectral learning algorithms for HMMs produce full transition matrices, and it's not clear how the authors can guarantee that these matrices will have the desired block-diagonal structure when dealing with real-world data and limited observations. The authors should address whether their method can handle cases where the transition matrix is not exactly block-diagonal but only approximately so, and how this affects the recovery of the individual HMMs.

4. The experiments in Section 4.1 do not fully reflect the practical challenges of learning MHMMs from data. Instead of applying their depermutation algorithm to a transition matrix that has been permuted by noise, the authors should have generated observation sequences from an MHMM and then learned all parameters from these sequences. This would provide a more realistic assessment of how noise in observations and limited data affect the depermutation process and the overall model learning.

5. The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address this concern but lacks sufficient detail to evaluate the method's effectiveness. The authors should provide more information about the specific MHMM parameters used (e.g., the exact transition matrices, emission matrices, and initial distributions), as these details significantly impact the difficulty of learning the model. The authors could include these parameters in an appendix to maintain conciseness while providing necessary information.

6. In Table 1, the spectral learning approach sometimes outperforms EM initialized with spectral learning. This is counterintuitive since EM should improve upon the spectral initialization. The authors should explain this observation, as it suggests either a potential issue with their experimental setup or a limitation in the EM implementation.

Minor Comments:

1. In Section 3.2.1, the subscript in the bottom right corner of the first matrix in the proof should be K rather than 2.

2. The figures contain text and numbers that are too small to be legible.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the spectral learning of MHMMs. The method is theoretically sound, and the experiments demonstrate reasonable performance on synthetic data. However, the practical applicability of the method is questionable given that it requires learning a full HMM before recovering the block structure, which undermines one of the main motivations for using MHMMs. The authors should provide a clearer justification for when their approach would be preferable to existing MHMM learning methods, particularly in the context of large-scale or data-limited scenarios. While the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution, the practical relevance of the approach needs further clarification to justify its inclusion in NIPS.
