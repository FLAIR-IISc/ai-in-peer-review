Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a spectral Method-of-Moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M latent states, can be represented as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. Off-the-shelf spectral learning for HMMs (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang, JCSS 2012) can then estimate the “global” HMM parameters up to a permutation of states. The central contribution is a depermutation procedure that exploits the spectral structure of the global transition matrix to recover the parameter blocks corresponding to the individual HMMs and to estimate K. Specifically, the method uses a low-rank reconstruction based on the top K eigenvalues and clusters columns to recover blocks, alongside a “spectral longevity” heuristic to select K. The authors provide synthetic and real-data experiments, comparing against EM and showing that the spectral method is fast and can also serve as an effective EM initializer.

The work builds on and is consistent with recent MoM/tensor approaches for latent variable models and HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al. 2012; Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012; Anandkumar et al., JMLR/Tensor methods). The idea of tackling mixtures through MoM has parallels with “Spectral experts” (Chaganty and Liang, ICML 2013) for mixtures of regressions, and with earlier work on MHMMs and sequence clustering (e.g., Smyth, NIPS 1997). The reduction of MHMM to block-diagonal HMM is standard but useful; the novelty lies in resolving the permutation ambiguity and estimating K from spectral information under noise.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is correct and clearly presented. The depermutation procedure is plausible and empirically effective. However, a central piece (Conjecture 1) remains a conjecture; theoretical guarantees (conditions on noise, spectral gaps, and sample complexity) are not proven. The proposed criterion for selecting K via “spectral longevity” is intuitive, but again lacks formal justification beyond Lemma 3, which itself relies on unproven separation assumptions.
- Empirical evaluation: Synthetic experiments illustrate the depermutation’s robustness as sequence length grows. The real-data experiment (UCI handwritten trajectories) shows that the spectral method is competitive and a strong initializer for EM. However, the evaluation would benefit from more systematic reporting (variance/error bars, sensitivity to K/M mis-specification, and robustness to small spectral gaps).
- Reproducibility: Some details are missing or need clarification: how MK is chosen when K is unknown, the range and step of the exponent e for longevity, the clustering algorithm/hyperparameters, runtime environment, and whether EM baselines use the same feature expansion as the spectral method.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with a clear statement of the main idea and algorithm. There are several typos and grammatical issues that should be corrected; for example: “For each e, identify of eigenvalues that contain all of the energy.” Please proofread carefully. Notation is mostly consistent, but a summary table of symbols would help. The conditions under which the eigenvalue-1 multiplicity holds (ergodicity/aperiodicity; exactly one unit-magnitude eigenvalue per Ak) should be stated upfront and discussed practically.

Originality
The block-diagonal reduction is known; the main novelty is the depermutation algorithm based on spectral properties of the global transition matrix and the heuristic to estimate K from eigenvalue “longevity.” This is a natural and useful extension of spectral HMM learning to mixtures. The contribution is incremental but addresses a real stumbling block in applying spectral methods to MHMMs.

Significance
The contribution is practically relevant: depermutation is a central barrier to using spectral methods for mixtures, and the proposed approach is simple, computationally attractive, and appears effective. If theoretical guarantees can be established or strengthened, this could become a standard tool for MHMM learning at scale. As is, the significance is moderate: helpful empirically, but with limited theoretical rigor.

Strengths
- Clear reduction of MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM enabling off-the-shelf spectral estimation.
- Practical depermutation procedure and a heuristic for estimating K that work well empirically.
- Computational efficiency compared to EM, with additional value as an EM initializer.
- Results on both synthetic and real data; fair reporting that EM can outperform when data are scarce.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory: The central separation and convergence claims are conjectural. Provide formal conditions (e.g., perturbation bounds on eigenvalues/eigenvectors under sampling noise; minimal spectral gap between global and local eigenvalues) and at least partial proofs, or clearly delineate the regimes where the method is expected to succeed/fail.
- Assumptions: The requirement that each Ak has exactly one eigenvalue at 1 implies aperiodicity/ergodicity. Discuss how violations (e.g., near-periodic chains) affect the method. Also discuss failure modes when stationary distributions vk across components are similar, as this may undermine column clustering.
- Choice of MK and K: The algorithm requires MK to run the HMM MoM, yet K is estimated post hoc. Discuss how to choose MK in practice, and the consequences of over/under-specifying MK. Evaluate robustness when MK is misspecified.
- Real-data comparability: The feature expansion (cubic polynomial) to enforce MK < L is sensible for MoM rank conditions, but may give the spectral method an advantage. Clarify whether EM baselines use the same transformed features; if not, include both comparisons. Discuss the effect of feature engineering on performance and whether kernelized spectral methods could avoid explicit expansion.
- Experiments: Add error bars over multiple runs, report standard deviations, and include runtime numbers with hardware details. Include ablations (e.g., clustering method, exponent range for longevity, sensitivity to spectral gaps), and report metrics beyond clustering accuracy (e.g., parameter recovery error where applicable).
- Related work and references: Improve the positioning relative to spectral/tensor methods for mixtures and HMMs (Anandkumar et al., Hsu et al.), MHMM literature (Smyth), and mixtures of regressions (Chaganty & Liang). Complete citations with venues and years.

Minor comments
- Fix typos/grammar (e.g., the example above; spacing around punctuation; consistent notation for vectors/matrices).
- Define all symbols when first introduced and standardize MATLAB-style colon notation explanations.
- Provide pseudocode with clearly stated inputs/outputs and complexity.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a practical and nontrivial obstacle (permutation/identifiability) in applying spectral learning to MHMMs.
- Simple, computationally efficient method with promising empirical results.
- Useful as a fast initializer for EM.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical foundations are incomplete; key results are conjectural.
- Evaluation, while suggestive, lacks comprehensive robustness and reproducibility details.
- Some assumptions (unique unit eigenvalue; rank conditions requiring feature expansion) limit scope and are not thoroughly analyzed.

Overall
A solid and useful empirical contribution with an elegant idea, but currently short on theory and comprehensive evaluation. I recommend borderline acceptance, contingent on improved proofs (or clearer conditions), stronger experimental analysis, and polished presentation and references.
