### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The key insight is that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, can be viewed as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix of size MK x MK. The proposed algorithm first employs an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this larger model. This initial estimation, however, is subject to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main contribution of the paper is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The authors show that the underlying block-diagonal structure can be recovered by exploiting the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix, specifically by clustering its columns after raising it to a high power. The paper also proposes a heuristic for determining the number of HMM components (K) from the eigenvalues of the transition matrix. The effectiveness of the approach is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The approach of framing the MHMM learning problem as learning a single structured HMM and then recovering the structure is novel. The de-permutation technique based on the spectral properties of the transition matrix is a clever and interesting contribution. This provides a new perspective on learning mixture models that circumvents the local optima issues of traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed MoM-based method has a significant computational advantage over EM, especially for large datasets. Once the moments are computed from the data, the core parameter estimation steps are independent of the number of sequences, making the approach potentially much faster than EM, which requires iterative passes over the entire dataset.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core ideas are presented clearly. The derivation of the equivalence between an MHMM and a structured HMM in Section 3.1 is elegant and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Despite its interesting ideas, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily concerning the practical viability of the approach and the experimental validation.

1.  **Questionable Premise for Practical Applications:** The algorithm's foundation rests on accurately estimating the parameters of a large, global HMM with MK hidden states. However, a primary motivation for using an MHMM is often to model complex systems with a sparse, structured model, which is more statistically and computationally tractable than a single, dense HMM, especially when data is limited. The proposed method requires learning a full MK x MK transition matrix first. This seems to defeat the purpose of using the sparse MHMM structure, as accurately estimating this large, dense matrix likely requires a prohibitive amount of data, far more than what would be needed to estimate K smaller M x M matrices.
    *   Could the authors comment on the data complexity of their approach? In what practical scenarios would one have enough data to reliably estimate the full MK x MK transition matrix via spectral methods, but still prefer an MHMM over a single large HMM?
    *   The spectral learning algorithm of Hsu et al. [3] is not guaranteed to return a nearly block-diagonal matrix, even if the data comes from an MHMM. With finite data, it might learn a dense matrix that is close in the moment space but structurally different. How robust is the de-permutation procedure to this form of model misspecification?

2.  **Unrealistic Experimental Evaluation of Noise Robustness:** The experiment in Section 4.1 is intended to show the de-permutation algorithm's robustness to noise. However, it does so by applying the algorithm to a ground-truth transition matrix artificially perturbed with noise. This setup does not reflect the true nature of the problem. A more convincing experiment would be to:
    1.  Generate observation sequences from a known MHMM.
    2.  Apply the full pipeline: learn the global HMM parameters (ŌP, ĀP) from these sequences using the spectral method.
    3.  Apply the proposed de-permutation algorithm to the *estimated* matrix ĀP.
    This would provide a much more realistic assessment of how estimation errors arising from finite data impact the algorithm's ability to recover the block-diagonal structure.

3.  **Lack of Experimental Detail and Reproducibility:** The experiments in Section 4.2 and 4.3 lack sufficient detail to fully evaluate the results.
    *   In Section 4.2, the parameters of the ground-truth MHMM are not specified. The difficulty of the learning and clustering task is highly dependent on the separability of the clusters and the mixing properties of the individual transition matrices (Ak). Please provide these details, perhaps in an appendix.
    *   In Table 1, the result for EM initialized with the spectral method sometimes shows lower accuracy than the spectral method alone (e.g., for characters 'o' vs 'q'). This is counter-intuitive, as EM, when initialized with a solution, is expected to find a new solution with equal or higher likelihood. While accuracy and likelihood are not the same, this discrepancy warrants an explanation. Did EM converge to a worse local optimum in terms of clustering accuracy?

**Minor Comments**

*   In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of the bottom-right Λ in the first block matrix appears to be a '2' but should likely be 'K'.
*   The text and numbers in Figures 3 and 4 are too small to be read comfortably. Please increase the font size.

**Conclusion**

The paper introduces an interesting and theoretically elegant algorithm for learning MHMMs. However, I have significant concerns about its practical applicability, as the core requirement of learning a large, dense HMM seems to undermine the benefits of the sparse MHMM structure. The experimental validation is also not yet convincing enough to demonstrate the method's robustness in realistic scenarios. While the ideas are novel, the paper would be much stronger if these practical concerns were addressed and the experiments were made more thorough and realistic.
