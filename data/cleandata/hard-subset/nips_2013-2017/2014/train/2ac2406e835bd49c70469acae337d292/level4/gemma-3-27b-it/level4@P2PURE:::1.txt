Summary: The authors address the problem of learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models. They propose a spectral learning algorithm to estimate HMM parameters, followed by a method to resolve permutation ambiguity in the transition matrix and recover its underlying block-diagonal structure.

Major Comments:

1. This paper is generally very well written. The experimental results section, however, could benefit from further elaboration. Specifically, the…
2. The authors leverage the equivalence between a mixture of HMMs and a single HMM. A key motivation for using a mixture of HMMs, rather than a single HMM, is the inherent sparsity of the mixture’s block-diagonal structure. This sparsity is particularly valuable when dealing with problems that are too large to model with a full HMM, especially with limited training data. However, the presented algorithm first requires accurate estimation of the full HMM parameters and *then* attempts to recover the block-diagonal structure through permutation reversal. This approach seems impractical for most real-world applications. Do the authors offer guidance on the scenarios where their algorithm might be applicable, and those where it might not?
3. Consequently, the recovered transition matrix is unlikely to be sparse in practice. Is there any mechanism to guarantee that the transition matrix learned by the proposed spectral algorithms will be (approximately) sparse? I suspect that numerous HMMs with full transition matrices could closely approximate the desired sparse model with block-diagonal structure. It’s unclear whether the matrix learned via [1,2] will exhibit sparsity even with minimal noise.
4. I am concerned that the experimental results do not adequately reflect the impact of noise commonly found in transition matrices. For example, in Section 4.1, the algorithm is applied to a transition matrix perturbed by noise sampled from a Dirichlet distribution, but this work focuses on learning mixtures of HMM parameters, not solely on depermuting matrices. A more relevant evaluation would involve sampling observation data from a mixture of HMMs and then attempting to recover *all* model parameters from this observed data. This would provide a more realistic assessment of how noise in observations and limited data affect the learned transition matrix and the difficulty of depermutation.
5. The experiment in Section 4.2 begins to address the concerns raised previously, but lacks sufficient detail for proper evaluation. Specifically, I need to understand the underlying structure of the mixture of HMMs used in the experiment. The specific parameters of both the transition and observation matrices are crucial for assessing the ease or difficulty of learning the model from data. This information could be included in an appendix if space is limited.
6. In Table 1, why does EM initialization with spectral learning sometimes yield worse results than spectral learning alone? Shouldn’t EM consistently improve upon the initial spectral learning solution?

Minor Comments:

1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix should likely be K, not 2.
2. The text and numbers in the figures are too small for comfortable readability. 

Despite some interesting ideas and sound theoretical results, I am skeptical about the method’s practical applicability to real-world datasets.
