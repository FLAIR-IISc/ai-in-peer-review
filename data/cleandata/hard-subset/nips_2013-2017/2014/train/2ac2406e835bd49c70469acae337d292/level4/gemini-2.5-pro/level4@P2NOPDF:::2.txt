This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs). The key idea is to formulate an MHMM as a single, larger HMM, which allows the authors to apply an existing spectral learning algorithm (Anandkumar et al., 2012) directly. The main contribution is a novel stabilization-based method to resolve the inherent parameter permutation problem that arises in this formulation. This is achieved by assuming that the transition matrix of each component HMM has a unique eigenvalue of 1, which corresponds to its stationary distribution.

The proposed stabilization-based method for resolving the parameter permutation ambiguity is a key strength. It is elegant, intuitive, and leverages a fundamental property of transition matrices. Furthermore, the theoretical analysis that accounts for estimation noise is a valuable addition. The resulting use of eigenvalues in the algorithm (as seen in Equation (7)) to handle this noise is particularly intriguing.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed. The clarity of the paper is hindered by inconsistent and often undefined notation. The discussion of related work is insufficient; for instance, the idea of using spectral estimates to initialize the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm has been previously explored (e.g., in Chaganty and Liang, 2013), but this is not acknowledged. Finally, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the presentation of results could be significantly improved.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Clarity of Theoretical Results:** The presentation of Corollary 1 was difficult to follow. Please explicitly define terms like `1_J^T` (e.g., as a row vector of ones of length J) upon first use. It would also be beneficial to elaborate on the block-diagonal structure of the limit matrix `lim_{e->inf} \bar{A}^e`, explaining that the columns within each `J x J` block are identical and correspond to the stationary distribution.

*   **Notation and Definitions:** Throughout the manuscript, many vectors and matrices are introduced without their dimensions being specified. Consistently defining all mathematical objects and their dimensions upon their first appearance would greatly enhance the paper's readability. For consistency with foundational work in this area, consider adopting the notation established in Hsu et al. (2009). For example, using `T` for transition matrices (instead of `A`), and `n` and `m` for the number of observation and hidden states (instead of `L` and `J`) would make the paper more accessible to readers familiar with the spectral learning literature.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The graphical format of Figure 4 makes it very difficult to interpret and compare results quantitatively. I strongly recommend replacing this figure with a table.
    *   The performance of the EM algorithm reported in Table 1 is surprisingly poor. With a sufficient number of iterations, EM, even with random initializations, often surpasses the performance of spectral methods despite the problem of local optima. The authors should clarify the experimental setup for EM. Furthermore, it is standard practice to report the result from the *best* run of EM (over multiple random initializations) rather than the average, as this better reflects its potential performance. Please include these results for a more complete and fair comparison.
