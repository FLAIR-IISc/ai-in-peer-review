This paper presents a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMM). The core idea is to leverage an existing spectral algorithm for learning hidden Markov models [Hsu et al., 2009; Anandkumar et al., 2012] by reformulating the MHMM as a specific type of HMM – one with a block-diagonal transition matrix. A key contribution lies in addressing the parameter permutation ambiguity inherent in this approach through a spectral analysis of the global transition matrix, relying on the assumption that each component HMM’s transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue equal to one.

The paper appropriately contextualizes its work within the field of latent variable model learning, referencing the growing popularity of method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithms [Anandkumar et al., 2012; Chaganty & Liang, 2013; Ge et al., 2011]. It correctly identifies the computational challenges of traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) for MHMMs [Smyth, 1997] and positions the proposed spectral method as a more efficient alternative.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of adapting spectral HMM learning to the MHMM setting, coupled with the permutation-handling strategy, offers a potentially valuable contribution. The analysis relating spectral properties to permutation recovery is particularly interesting.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The method's reliance on MoM promises scalability to large datasets, a significant advantage over iterative EM-based approaches.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides theoretical justification for the permutation recovery step, even in the presence of noise, through the proposed use of eigenvalues (Equation 7).
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the problem and outlines the limitations of existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Notation:** The presentation suffers from notational inconsistencies and a lack of clear definitions. Terms are introduced without specifying dimensions, hindering understanding (e.g., `1J^T`). Elaborating on key mathematical derivations, such as the structure of `lim{e->inf} bar{A}^e`, would improve readability.
*   **Related Work:** The paper lacks a thorough engagement with related work. The authors should acknowledge and discuss prior approaches to spectral initialization for EM [Chaganty & Liang, 2013], and other methods for addressing permutation ambiguity in mixture models.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are presented in a suboptimal format. Figure 4 is difficult to interpret and should be replaced with a table. The reported results in Table 1 are unexpected (poor EM performance) and warrant further investigation. Reporting the *best* EM accuracy (rather than an average) is standard practice.
*   **Standardization of Notation:** Adopting standard notation used in the HMM literature [Hsu et al., 2009] – using `T` for transition matrices and `n`, `m` for observation and hidden state counts – would enhance accessibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a computationally challenging problem in a novel and potentially effective manner.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results, despite their presentation issues, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity in presentation and notation detracts from the paper's overall quality.
*   The insufficient discussion of related work weakens the paper’s contribution.
*   The experimental evaluation needs improvement in terms of presentation and analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions to address the issues outlined above. The core idea is sound, and the theoretical analysis is promising, but the presentation and experimental evaluation need substantial improvement.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. I would be willing to reconsider with major revisions that address the concerns regarding clarity, related work, and experimental presentation.
