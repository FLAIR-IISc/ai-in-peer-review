

This paper introduces a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), building upon recent advances in method-of-moments (MoM) approaches for latent variable models, particularly Anandkumar et al. (2012) and Hsu et al. (2009) for standard HMMs. The authors demonstrate that an MHMM can be equivalently formulated as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling the application of existing MoM techniques. The key contribution lies in resolving the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM-based HMM learning through spectral analysis of the global transition matrix, even in the presence of estimation noise. The authors provide theoretical analysis of the spectral properties and propose a practical algorithm for estimating the number of clusters and de-permuting the transition matrix.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a significant computational bottleneck in MHMM learning. The proposed approach offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM, with experiments showing comparable accuracy to EM but significantly reduced computational cost, especially for large datasets. The theoretical analysis of the spectral properties (Lemmas 2-3 and Conjecture 1) provides a solid foundation for the de-permutation procedure, and the real data experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the method as an initialization scheme for EM.

Strengths:
- Quality: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for the MHMM spectral learning approach, with well-motivated analysis of the spectral properties necessary for de-permutation. The experimental results are well-designed and support the main claims.
- Clarity: The core idea is well-motivated and the algorithmic description is generally clear, though some notation could be improved.
- Originality: The adaptation of spectral learning to MHMMs with the permutation resolution technique represents a meaningful extension of existing MoM approaches for HMMs, addressing a gap in the literature.
- Significance: The method provides a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMM learning, which is particularly valuable for large-scale sequence clustering applications.

Weaknesses:
- Quality: The theoretical analysis of the noise case (Conjecture 1) lacks formal proof and relies on a reference to a different model's sample complexity. The experimental setup for the real data experiment (polynomial feature transformation) is not thoroughly justified.
- Clarity: The paper suffers from inconsistent notation (e.g., use of L for observation dimension instead of standard M, J for state dimension instead of K). Key terms like 1J^T are not properly defined. The description of the transition matrix structure in Corollary 1 is confusing.
- Originality: The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from Chaganty and Liang (2013), who explored using spectral methods for initialization in mixture models, though the authors acknowledge this in the revision.
- Significance: The experiments are somewhat limited in scope, particularly the comparison with EM. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art MHMM learning methods.

Specific comments:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer definition of all vectors and matrices with their dimensions at first use (e.g., 1J^T, ν̄, etc.).
- The claim in Corollary 1 about the structure of lim_{e→∞} Ā^e should be elaborated to clarify that it forms a block matrix with identical columns within each block.
- Figure 4 is difficult to interpret; results would be better presented in a table format to facilitate comparison of accuracy and speed across different data sizes.
- The comparison with EM in Table 1 is misleading; the paper should report the best EM accuracy (over multiple initializations) rather than the average, as is standard practice.
- The paper should conform to established HMM notation (e.g., use T for transition matrix, n for number of observation states, m for number of hidden states) to improve readability for the HMM community.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to an important problem in sequence modeling, with a clear theoretical contribution that resolves a key challenge in applying spectral methods to MHMMs. The experimental results support the main claims and demonstrate practical utility.

Con arguments against acceptance: The theoretical analysis in the noisy case requires more rigorous justification, the experimental comparison with EM is not sufficiently thorough, and the paper would benefit from more careful attention to notation and presentation.
