This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the Method of Moments (MoM), also known as spectral learning. The key insight is to re-frame the MHMM, which consists of K individual HMMs, as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix and concatenated emission/initial state parameters. This reformulation allows the authors to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, such as the one developed by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012), to estimate the global parameters.

The main technical challenge, and the paper's core contribution, is addressing the permutation ambiguity inherent in this approach. The spectral learning algorithm recovers the global parameters only up to an unknown permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the K individual HMM components, making them unidentifiable. The authors propose an elegant solution to this "de-permutation" problem. They leverage the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. In the noiseless case, this matrix would have K eigenvalues equal to 1, corresponding to the K disconnected components. By taking this matrix to a high power, the columns group into K distinct clusters, revealing the original block structure. In the more realistic noisy case, the authors propose a method to identify the number of components (K) and perform a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix using its top K eigenvalues. Clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix provides the permutation needed to recover the parameters of the individual HMMs. The approach is validated on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem: efficiently learning MHMMs. While spectral learning for H-MMs is established, its application to MHMMs is not straightforward due to the permutation ambiguity. The proposed method for resolving this ambiguity by analyzing the spectral structure of the global transition matrix is novel, clever, and well-motivated. This work represents a significant extension of the spectral learning paradigm to a more complex hierarchical model.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed method offers substantial computational advantages over the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for MHMMs. By relying on moment computations and matrix factorization, its runtime is independent of the number of sequences after the initial moment calculation step. This makes it highly scalable for large datasets, a crucial advantage as demonstrated in the speed comparisons in Figure 4.

3.  **Theoretical Foundation:** The approach is grounded in the spectral properties of the model. The paper provides clear derivations showing the equivalence of an MHMM to a structured HMM (Lemma 1) and analyzes the spectral properties of the resulting transition matrix (Lemma 2). The method for estimating the number of components K (Lemma 3) is also a valuable contribution.

4.  **Practical Utility:** The experiments show that the proposed method is not only fast but also achieves accuracy comparable to EM. Furthermore, the real-data experiment highlights its utility as a high-quality initialization strategy for EM, potentially combining the speed and global properties of spectral methods with the local refinement of EM.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Suggestions for improvement):**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis could be strengthened. Conjecture 1, which provides a finite-sample error bound for the low-rank matrix reconstruction, is central to justifying the de-permutation step in the noisy case. However, it is presented as a conjecture without a formal proof, instead adapting a result from Anandkumar et al. (2012) for a different model. A more rigorous, self-contained analysis would make the paper's theoretical contributions more solid.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, but could benefit from a thorough proofreading to correct several minor typos and grammatical errors. Additionally, the paper could do a better job of situating its contribution within the broader context of spectral methods for mixture models. For instance, while Chaganty and Liang (2013) is cited, a more explicit discussion in the introduction or related work section about the parallels in solving component ambiguity in different mixture models (e.g., mixture of regressions vs. mixture of HMMs) would help readers appreciate the novelty and connections of this work.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are promising but could be more comprehensive. The performance of the de-permutation algorithm likely depends on the spectral gap condition outlined in Definition 1 and Lemma 3. It would be valuable to include an analysis of the algorithm's robustness when this condition is only weakly met or violated (e.g., when the local eigenvalues of one component are close in magnitude to the global eigenvalues of another). This would provide a clearer picture of the practical limits of the approach.

In summary, this paper makes a strong and valuable contribution by developing a practical and computationally efficient spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of HMMs. The core idea of using the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to solve the permutation ambiguity is both novel and effective. While the theoretical justification could be more rigorous and the presentation could be polished, the strengths in originality, significance, and practical utility are compelling. The method provides a powerful alternative to EM and is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working with sequence clustering and hierarchical models.
