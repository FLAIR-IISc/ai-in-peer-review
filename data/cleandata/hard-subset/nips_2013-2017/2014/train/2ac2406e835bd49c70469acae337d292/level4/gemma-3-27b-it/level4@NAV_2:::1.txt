Summary: The authors address the challenging problem of learning a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). They propose a novel approach based on the Method of Moments (MoM), leveraging the equivalence between an MHMM and a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. A key contribution is resolving the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation using spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

Major Comments:
1. This paper presents a well-written and theoretically grounded approach to MHMM learning. The experimental evaluation, however, could be significantly strengthened to better demonstrate the method's practical utility.
2. The authors correctly identify the potential for sparsity in MHMMs as a motivation for their use. However, the proposed algorithm’s reliance on initially accurately estimating a full transition matrix before de-permuting it raises concerns about its applicability to large-scale, data-limited problems where the inherent sparsity is precisely what makes learning feasible. It would be valuable for the authors to explicitly discuss scenarios where their approach is most appropriate and to acknowledge limitations related to the computational cost of estimating a dense transition matrix.
3. A crucial question concerns the guarantee of sparsity in the learned transition matrix. While the MHMM is inherently sparse, the spectral learning algorithm may recover a dense matrix approximating the true block-diagonal structure. Exploring methods to enforce or encourage sparsity during the learning process – perhaps through regularization or post-processing – would strengthen the approach. It’s important to consider whether multiple full HMMs can adequately approximate the target sparse model, and how the algorithm handles this ambiguity.
4. The experimental setup in Section 4.1, focused on de-permuting matrices perturbed with Dirichlet noise, feels somewhat detached from the core task of learning MHMM parameters from observed data. A more realistic evaluation would involve sampling data from MHMMs and then attempting to recover the model parameters, including the transition matrix, in the presence of typical observational noise. This would provide a more accurate assessment of the algorithm's robustness.
5. Section 4.2 begins to address this concern, but the lack of detail regarding the underlying MHMM parameters hinders a thorough evaluation. Providing specific parameter values (e.g., transition probabilities, emission distributions) – potentially in an appendix – is crucial for understanding the experimental conditions and the algorithm’s performance.
6. The observation in Table 1 that EM initialized with spectral learning sometimes performs *worse* than spectral learning alone is puzzling and warrants further investigation. A detailed explanation of this phenomenon, and potential reasons for the performance degradation, is needed. Is it due to local optima, sensitivity to initialization, or other factors?

Minor Comments:
1. In the proof in Section 3.2.1, the subscript of \Lambda in the bottom right corner of the first matrix appears to be incorrect; it should likely be K, not 2.
2. The font size in the figures is excessively small, making them difficult to read. Increasing the font size and improving the figure resolution is recommended.

Overall, this paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of MHMM learning. However, concerns about the practical applicability and robustness of the algorithm, along with the need for more comprehensive experimental validation, temper my enthusiasm. Despite these concerns, the paper demonstrates significant technical merit and introduces an intriguing approach to a challenging problem.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novel approach to MHMM learning leveraging spectral properties.
* Strong theoretical justification for the proposed method.
* Demonstrates potential for faster learning compared to traditional EM.
* Provides a novel initialization scheme for EM.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Concerns about the algorithm's scalability and practicality in large-scale, data-limited settings.
* Lack of guarantee of sparsity in the learned transition matrix.
* Limited experimental validation with insufficient detail regarding parameters and noise models.
* Puzzling results in Table 1 regarding EM initialization.
