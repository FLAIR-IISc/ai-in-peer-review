

This paper proposes a spectral algorithm for learning a mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs) by adapting an existing HMM learning method (Anandkumar et al., 2012) as a special case. The core contribution lies in a stabilization step that resolves parameter permutation, achieved through the assumption that each HMM component's transition matrix possesses a single eigenvalue of 1. While the adaptation of the spectral algorithm is straightforward, the permutation solution offers a novel and intuitive approach to a key challenge in MHMM learning.

The paper exhibits several presentation and technical shortcomings that require attention. First, notation is inconsistent and inadequately defined. Critical symbols like $1_J^T$ (a row vector of $J$ ones) and the structure of $\lim_{\epsilon \to \infty} \bar{A}^\epsilon$ (a $JK \times JK$ block-diagonal matrix with identical columns within each $J \times J$ block) are introduced without clear explanation, causing confusion (e.g., Corollary 1). The authors must define all vectors and matrices with their dimensions upon first use to ensure clarity. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from adopting the standard notation of Hsu et al. (2009), using $T$ for transition matrices and $n, m$ for the numbers of observation and hidden states (replacing $A$, $L$, and $J$), which would improve consistency with the broader literature.

The related work section is insufficiently contextualized. The authors fail to acknowledge prior work on spectral initialization for EM, such as Chaganty and Liang (2013), which directly addresses initializing EM with spectral estimates—making the paper’s approach less novel than implied. This omission weakens the contribution narrative.

Experiments are underdeveloped. Figure 4’s graphical presentation of results is difficult to interpret; tabular formatting would significantly enhance readability and comparability. More critically, Table 1 reports only average EM accuracy, which is misleading. Standard practice requires reporting the *best* (not average) accuracy achieved by EM across multiple runs, as EM typically converges to superior solutions with sufficient iterations despite local optima. The unexpectedly poor EM performance in Table 1 warrants explanation—why does it underperform spectral methods even with optimal initialization? Including best-case EM results is essential for a fair comparison.

The analysis of estimation noise (Equation 7) is a strong point, demonstrating how eigenvalue properties can be leveraged for robustness. However, the experimental validation of this theoretical insight is weak. The basic experiments do not sufficiently test the algorithm’s robustness or scalability, limiting the paper’s impact.

Specific recommendations for revision:
1.  **Clarify notation:** Define all symbols (e.g., $1_J^T$, $\bar{A}^\epsilon$) with dimensions and structure immediately upon introduction. Explicitly state the block-diagonal nature of $\lim_{\epsilon \to \infty} \bar{A}^\epsilon$.
2.  **Expand related work:** Cite Chaganty and Liang (2013) and discuss how their spectral initialization approach relates to the paper’s stabilization step.
3.  **Revise experiments:**
    *   Replace Figure 4 with a clear table.
    *   Report EM’s *best* accuracy (not average) in Table 1 and explain the discrepancy with spectral methods.
    *   Include additional experiments demonstrating robustness to noise or scalability.
4.  **Adopt standard notation:** Use $T$ for transition matrices and $n, m$ for state counts (following Hsu et al., 2009).
5.  **Strengthen contribution statement:** Explicitly contrast the paper’s stabilization step with prior work on permutation (e.g., Anandkumar et al.’s approach for HMMs).

The core idea—using the eigenvalue assumption to stabilize the permutation step—is promising and addresses a genuine challenge in MHMM learning. The spectral adaptation itself is valid, but the paper’s execution falls short in presentation, context, and experimental rigor. With the suggested revisions, particularly improved notation, a more comprehensive related work discussion, and properly presented experiments, the contribution would be clearer and more impactful. As written, the current presentation obscures the novelty and weakens the technical contribution. The paper would benefit significantly from the proposed clarifications and experimental updates to meet NIPS standards.
