**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning a mixture of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). The proposed approach first employs a spectral algorithm to learn the parameters of a single, larger HMM that represents the mixture. Subsequently, it introduces a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity in the learned transition matrix, thereby recovering the underlying block-diagonal structure inherent to the HMM mixture.

**Major Comments**

1.  The paper is generally well-written. However, the experimental results section could be significantly strengthened with more detail.

2.  The core premise of the paper is that a mixture of HMMs can be represented as a single, larger HMM. However, a primary motivation for using HMM mixtures is to leverage the sparse, block-diagonal structure of the combined transition matrix. This sparsity is particularly advantageous for large-scale problems or in settings with limited training data, where learning a full, dense model is infeasible. The proposed algorithm appears to contradict this motivation by first requiring an accurate estimation of the full HMM parameters before recovering the block-diagonal structure. This initial step seems computationally prohibitive for the very problems where HMM mixtures are most useful. Could the authors comment on the practical settings where this approach would be appropriate and discuss its limitations?

3.  Following the previous point, the transition matrix estimated via spectral methods is unlikely to be perfectly block-diagonal (up to permutation) in practice. Estimation noise will likely result in a dense matrix. Is there any theoretical guarantee that the matrix learned by the referenced spectral algorithms [1, 2] will be close to a permuted block-diagonal matrix? I am concerned that in the presence of noise, the true sparse model may be just one of many dense models in its vicinity, and it is not clear that the spectral method will recover a matrix that is amenable to the proposed de-permutation technique.

4.  A significant concern is that the experimental evaluation does not realistically model the challenges of the proposed method. In Section 4.1, the algorithm is tested on a transition matrix permuted and then perturbed with noise from a Dirichlet distribution. This setup isolates the de-permutation step but does not reflect the full problem of learning from data. A more convincing experiment would involve generating observation sequences from a ground-truth HMM mixture, applying the spectral learning algorithm to estimate the full model parameters from this data, and then attempting to recover the block-diagonal structure. Such an end-to-end evaluation would provide a much clearer understanding of how estimation errors, stemming from noisy observations and finite data, propagate to the learned transition matrix and affect the performance of the de-permutation algorithm.

5.  The experiment in Section 4.2 moves closer to the end-to-end evaluation suggested above, but it lacks the necessary details to fully assess the results. The paper does not specify the parameters of the ground-truth HMM mixture used for data generation. The properties of the transition and observation matrices are critical for determining the difficulty of the learning task. To properly evaluate the experiment, details about these parameters are essential and should be provided, perhaps in an appendix.

6.  In Table 1, the results show that initializing EM with the spectral learning solution sometimes performs worse than using the spectral learning solution alone. This is counter-intuitive, as one would expect EM to either improve upon or maintain the quality of its initialization. Could the authors provide an explanation for this observation?

**Minor Comments**

1.  In the proof in Section 3.2.1, I believe the subscript of Λ in the bottom-right corner of the first matrix equation should be K, not 2.

2.  The text and numbers in the figures are too small and difficult to read.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the paper presents interesting ideas and valuable theoretical contributions. However, I have significant reservations about the practical applicability of the proposed method on real-world datasets, stemming from the concerns raised above.
