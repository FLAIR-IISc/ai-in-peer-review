This paper presents a Method of Moments (MoM) approach for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The authors' key insight is that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, can be viewed as a single, larger HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. This observation allows them to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, such as the one by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012), to learn the parameters of this larger model.

The main technical challenge, and the paper's primary contribution, is addressing the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral learning. The standard algorithm recovers the parameters of the large HMM only up to an unknown permutation of its MK states. This permutation mixes states from different HMM components, making it impossible to directly recover the parameters of the individual models. The authors propose a novel de-permutation algorithm to resolve this ambiguity. In the noiseless case, the block-diagonal structure of the true transition matrix implies that it has K eigenvalues equal to 1. By analyzing the column space of high powers of the estimated transition matrix, the component clusters can be identified. In the more realistic noisy case, the authors propose a method based on a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix, using the K eigenvalues of largest magnitude. The columns of this reconstructed matrix are then clustered to find the permutation that restores the block-diagonal structure, thereby demixing the parameters of the constituent HMMs. The paper also proposes a heuristic for estimating the number of components K based on the "spectral longevity" of the eigenvalues. The approach is validated on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating its computational advantages over the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem: efficiently learning MHMMs, which are widely used for sequence clustering. While applying spectral methods to an MHMM (viewed as a large HMM) is a natural idea, the permutation ambiguity is a critical and non-trivial obstacle. The proposed de-permutation algorithm is a novel and clever solution to this core problem, making spectral learning a viable approach for this model class. This is a significant contribution.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The central idea of using the spectral properties of the permuted transition matrix to recover the underlying block structure is elegant and well-motivated. The progression from the ideal noiseless case to the practical noisy case is logical and provides clear intuition for the proposed algorithm.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The formulation of an MHMM as a special HMM in Section 3.1 is clear and effectively sets up the problem. The figures, particularly Figure 1, provide a helpful visualization of the de-permutation problem and the effect of noise.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithm offers a significant speed advantage over EM, as demonstrated in Figure 4. Furthermore, the experiments show that even if the spectral method's accuracy is slightly lower than a well-converged EM, it serves as an excellent initialization strategy (Table 1). This is a valuable practical contribution, as EM for MHMMs is known to be sensitive to initialization and computationally demanding.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical justification for the de-permutation algorithm in the presence of noise relies on several conjectures and assumptions. For instance, Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank reconstruction, is justified by reusing a result from a related but different model, with the authors noting that a full analysis is beyond the paper's scope. Lemma 3, which supports the method for estimating K, relies on assumptions about the spectral gaps of the estimated matrix. While the intuition is strong, the lack of formal proofs or more rigorous analysis weakens the paper's theoretical claims. A more thorough discussion of the conditions under which these conjectures are expected to hold would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Contextualization and Related Work:** The paper could do a better job of placing its contribution within the broader context of spectral learning for mixture models. The problem of "demixing" components from a permuted output is not unique to MHMMs. For example, the work of Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013) on spectral experts for mixtures of linear regressions tackles a very similar challenge. A discussion of how the proposed de-permutation technique relates to or differs from similar ideas in related literature would provide valuable context and strengthen the originality claim.

3.  **Clarity and Polish:** The manuscript would benefit from another round of careful proofreading to fix minor typos and grammatical errors that occasionally hinder readability. For example, in the description of Algorithm 1, the step "Identify K̂ as the eigenvalue with largest longevity" is imprecise; K̂ is the *number* of components inferred from the longevity of eigenvalues, not an eigenvalue itself. Sharpening such descriptions would improve the paper's overall quality.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The real-data experiment on the character trajectory dataset is informative but somewhat limited. The results in Table 1 show that the proposed method, when used as an initializer for EM, is very effective. However, as a standalone algorithm, its performance is comparable to or sometimes worse than randomly initialized EM. To compellingly demonstrate the method's value beyond initialization, experiments on larger or more complex real-world datasets where the computational benefits of the spectral approach become decisive would be beneficial.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to learning mixture models. The proposed method for resolving the permutation ambiguity in spectral learning of MHMMs is the main strength and is both elegant and practical. While the theoretical analysis could be more rigorous and the experimental validation more extensive, the work addresses a significant problem with a creative and effective solution. The ideas presented are likely to be of interest to the NIPS community and could inspire further work on spectral methods for other hierarchical models.
