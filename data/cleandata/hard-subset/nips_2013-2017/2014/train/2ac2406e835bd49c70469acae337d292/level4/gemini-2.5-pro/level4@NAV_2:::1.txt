This paper addresses the problem of learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using a Method of Moments (MoM) approach. The central idea is to view an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a `MK x MK` block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors propose using existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this large HMM. The main technical contribution is a method to resolve the resulting state permutation ambiguity by exploiting the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix to recover its underlying block-diagonal structure, and consequently, the parameters of the individual HMM components.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The proposed method for depermuting the global transition matrix using its spectral properties is novel and theoretically motivated. This provides a new avenue for applying MoM-based techniques to more complex hierarchical models like MHMMs.
*   **Significance:** The approach offers a significant computational advantage over the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, whose per-iteration cost scales with the size of the dataset. A faster, non-iterative learning algorithm for MHMMs could be highly valuable for applications involving large sequence datasets.
*   **Clarity:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and the theoretical development is easy to follow. The connection between an MHMM and a single HMM with a special structure is clearly established.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Practicality of the Core Assumption:** The method relies on first learning a full `MK x MK` HMM. However, a primary motivation for using MHMMs over a single large HMM is precisely to leverage the sparse, block-diagonal structure to make learning tractable, especially when the total number of states `MK` is large and data is limited. The proposed approach appears to require accurately estimating a potentially very large, dense transition matrix before recovering the sparse structure. It is unclear if this is feasible for the very problems where MHMMs are most needed. The authors should discuss the scalability of their approach and the specific regimes (in terms of data size vs. `MK`) where it is most applicable.

2.  **Robustness to Estimation Noise:** A key concern is that the transition matrix recovered by spectral methods from finite data will not be perfectly block-diagonal up to a permutation, but rather a noisy, dense matrix. It is not obvious that this estimated matrix will be close enough to the true permuted block-diagonal form for the depermutation algorithm to succeed. The authors should provide more justification or analysis on whether the standard spectral HMM learning algorithms are guaranteed to yield a matrix that is amenable to their de-clustering technique, especially in low-data regimes.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while promising, could be more convincing.
    *   The experiment in Section 4.1 seems to test the depermutation algorithm on a matrix with artificially added noise, rather than on a transition matrix estimated from observed data. A more realistic evaluation would involve generating sequences from a ground-truth MHMM, applying the spectral HMM learning algorithm to this data, and then demonstrating that the resulting noisy transition matrix can be successfully depermuted. This would better reflect the true challenges of the end-to-end process.
    *   The experiment in Section 4.2 is more aligned with this, but lacks crucial details. To properly evaluate the results, the reader needs to know the specific parameters of the ground-truth MHMMs (e.g., the transition and emission matrices). This information is critical for understanding the difficulty of the learning problem (e.g., how separable the clusters were). We recommend including these details in an appendix.

4.  **Inconsistent Results:** In Table 1, the performance of "EM initialized with Spectral" is sometimes worse than "Spectral" alone. Since EM iterations are guaranteed to not decrease the likelihood, this outcome is unexpected and requires an explanation. Is it possible the clustering accuracy metric does not perfectly correlate with the likelihood, or was there an issue with the EM implementation?

**Minor Comments:**
*   The figures are difficult to read due to the very small font size for text and numbers. Please increase the font size for clarity.
*   In the proof within Section 3.2.1, the subscript on the bottom-right `V` matrix in the first line appears to be `K`, not `2`. Please verify.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and elegant theoretical idea for learning MHMMs. However, there are significant concerns about the practical viability and the experimental validation of the proposed method. Addressing the points above would substantially strengthen the paper.
