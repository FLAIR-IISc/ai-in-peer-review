This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing the computational challenges of traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) methods. The core idea is to leverage the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM transition matrix and employ spectral techniques to resolve permutation ambiguities arising from the MoM estimation.

The paper is well-motivated. MHMMs are valuable for sequence clustering, but EM’s computational cost limits scalability. MoM offers computational advantages, but applying it directly to MHMMs is complicated by permutation issues. The authors correctly identify this and propose a clever solution based on the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The connection between the MHMM and an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix is clearly stated and proven (Lemma 1), providing a solid theoretical foundation.  The paper builds upon recent work in spectral learning of HMMs [Hsu et al., 2013] and extends it to the MHMM setting.

The strengths of this work lie in its originality and the technical depth of the proposed solution. The use of spectral properties to resolve the permutation ambiguity is a novel and insightful approach. The theoretical analysis, while relying on conjectures (which are clearly stated as such), provides a reasonable justification for the method. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real data provides evidence of the approach’s effectiveness and efficiency, particularly highlighting the computational benefits compared to EM. The real data experiment, although using a pre-processing step (cubic polynomial feature transformation), demonstrates the practical applicability of the method. The inclusion of comparisons with different EM initializations (random and MoM) is a plus.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The leap from the idealized spectral analysis to the noisy, real-world scenario isn't fully bridged. The conjectures about the conditions required for successful depermutation (Definitions 1 and 3) feel somewhat hand-wavy and lack rigorous justification. While the authors acknowledge the limitations in the experimental setup (e.g., data transformation for the UCI dataset), they don’t fully address how sensitive the method is to these preprocessing choices. Furthermore, the presentation could be improved. For instance, Figure 1 is referenced but not explained sufficiently within the text.  The notation, while generally clear, can be dense, making some sections difficult to follow without careful reading. The experiments, while demonstrating the speed advantage, don't always show a clear accuracy advantage over well-initialized EM, raising questions about the practical benefits when accuracy is paramount.  The references are a bit sparse; for example, work on spectral clustering in general might be relevant when discussing the clustering of columns in the transition matrix. While [Anandkumar et al., 2013] and [Anandkumar et al., 2014] are properly cited for MoM, it’s unclear if the authors are aware of more recent advances in spectral methods for latent variable models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful approach to MHMM learning.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with a clear connection to existing work on spectral learning.
*   Demonstrated computational advantages over EM.
*   Successful application to both synthetic and real data.
*   Clear identification of limitations and directions for future work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical analysis relies on conjectures that require further justification.
*   Transition from idealized spectral analysis to noisy real-world scenarios isn't fully elaborated.
*   Experimental results don't consistently demonstrate a clear accuracy advantage over optimized EM.
*   Some sections are dense and could benefit from improved clarity.
*   Limited discussion of sensitivity to preprocessing choices in the real data experiment.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of MHMM learning. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and offers a compelling solution to a challenging problem. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened and the experimental evaluation expanded, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the concerns raised above. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the robustness of their method to noise and provide a more detailed discussion of the implications of the data transformation used in the real data experiment.  A more thorough exploration of relevant literature on spectral clustering techniques could also be beneficial.
