Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning mixture of hidden Markov models (MHMMs) using method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral techniques. The core observation is that an MHMM with K components and M hidden states per component is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal and whose emission matrix concatenates the component emissions. This enables application of existing spectral learning methods for HMMs (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al., COLT/ICML) to estimate “global” parameters. The key challenge is the permutation ambiguity inherent to spectral estimation, which mixes states across components and prevents recovery of individual HMM parameters. The paper proposes to exploit spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, the global transition matrix has exactly K eigenvalues equal to 1 (one per block), and high powers of the matrix reveal K clusters of columns. In the noisy case, the paper proposes a low-rank reconstruction using the top K eigenvalues and a heuristic, “spectral longevity,” to estimate K. A de-permutation mapping is obtained by clustering the columns of the low-rank reconstruction, after which the component parameters are read off from de-permuted blocks. Synthetic experiments vary sequence length and dataset size to assess de-permutation and clustering accuracy versus EM; a real-data experiment on UCI handwriting trajectories shows that the spectral method is faster and competitive in accuracy, and can initialize EM effectively. Prior work has applied EM to MHMMs (e.g., Smyth, NIPS 1997) and spectral/MoM methods to HMMs and other latent variable mixtures; this paper combines these by resolving the permutation to recover individual HMMs.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The equivalence between MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM (Lemma 1) and the eigen-structure of the block-diagonal transition matrix (Lemma 2, Corollary) are correct and clearly justified.
- The proposed de-permutation in the noiseless case follows directly from block structure and eigenvalue-1 multiplicity.
- The noisy case relies on Conjecture 1 (a perturbation/sample-complexity statement) and on a heuristic criterion (“spectral longevity”) for estimating K (Lemma 3 provides a qualitative argument). A rigorous perturbation analysis (e.g., via Davis–Kahan/Weyl bounds) is not provided; recovery guarantees for de-permutation under finite samples are not established.
- The method assumes each Ak has a unique eigenvalue of magnitude 1 (ergodicity/aperiodicity), which is standard but should be stated as an assumption affecting identifiability.
- The MoM step depends on conditions such as full column rank of the emission matrix and typically requires L ≥ MK; the paper addresses this in one experiment via polynomial feature expansion, but does not analyze how feature mappings affect identifiability or noise.
- Experimental evaluation is suggestive but limited: synthetic settings are relatively small; details of the MoM implementation (moment order, whitening, continuous vs discrete observations) are sparse; comparisons do not include model-order selection baselines (e.g., BIC for EM) or alternatives for K estimation.

Clarity:
- The main idea and algorithmic steps are explained clearly, and the block-diagonal viewpoint is helpful. Figures referenced in the text support the intuition about eigenvalue clustering (though precise parameter settings and y-axes for reported metrics should be specified).
- Some implementation details are missing: which spectral HMM algorithm is used, the exact moments, how continuous observations are handled (e.g., feature expectations), whitening procedures, and how the requirement L ≥ MK is enforced in general.
- The definition and practical computation range for the “spectral longevity” metric (γ, exponent range) should be specified; sensitivity to these choices is not discussed.

Originality:
- Viewing an MHMM as an HMM is known; the novelty lies in using spectral properties of the global transition matrix to resolve the permutation and to estimate K, enabling recovery of component HMMs from a global spectral estimate. This appears to be a novel combination not explicitly addressed in prior spectral HMM work cited.
- Leaving the main robustness statement as a conjecture reduces theoretical novelty.

Significance:
- If robust, the approach can substantially reduce computation relative to EM for large datasets, since moment computation amortizes over data and subsequent steps are data-size independent.
- The de-permutation procedure is practically useful and could generalize to other block-structured latent variable mixtures.
- Current theoretical gaps and limited empirical breadth temper the immediate impact; nonetheless, the idea provides a promising direction and a practical initialization for EM.

Strengths:
- Clear and simple reduction of MHMM learning to a spectral HMM plus de-permutation.
- Computational efficiency and scalability advantages over EM, demonstrated empirically.
- Practical procedure to estimate K from the spectrum.
- Works for several emission families given appropriate features; demonstrates utility as EM initialization.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Lack of rigorous guarantees in the noisy case. Suggest providing a perturbation analysis showing conditions (spectral gaps, sample size) under which the top-K eigen-space and column clustering recover the correct permutation with high probability.
- Assumptions should be stated explicitly (ergodicity/mixing in each Ak, full-rank emissions, L ≥ MK), with discussion of how violations affect performance.
- Clarify the MoM implementation for continuous observations: feature choice, whitening, moment orders, and consistency conditions. Discuss the effect of feature expansions on identifiability and noise.
- Evaluate K estimation against standard baselines (BIC/AIC for EM, eigen-gap); include sensitivity to γ and exponent range; analyze failure cases when local eigenvalues approach the global ones.
- Expand experiments: larger K and M; unbalanced mixtures; varied observation models (multinomial, Poisson); different sequence lengths and counts; robustness to small spectral gaps; ablations on clustering method and choice of rank.
- Report computational details (hardware, time breakdown for moments vs decomposition), add error bars, and include code/pseudocode for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually neat reduction and practical de-permutation strategy.
- Significant computational benefits with competitive accuracy.
- Potentially broad applicability and a useful initialization for EM.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Main robustness claim is conjectural; no formal recovery/sample-complexity guarantees for MHMM de-permutation.
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing implementation details hinder reproducibility and assessment.
- Dependence on L ≥ MK and feature engineering is not theoretically justified.

Overall, the paper presents a promising and practically useful approach with clear ideas, but would benefit from stronger theoretical support and broader empirical validation.
