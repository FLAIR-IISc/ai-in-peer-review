This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to leverage the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM’s global transition matrix to overcome the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation. The authors demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that spectral properties can be used to de-permute the estimated parameters and recover individual HMMs.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a computational bottleneck in MHMM learning (the EM algorithm’s scaling with dataset size). The connection to previous MoM work [1, 2, 3] is clearly articulated, and the problem of permutation ambiguity is appropriately identified as a central challenge. The paper builds upon existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs [3] and extends them to the mixture setting. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature, including applications of MHMMs [4, 5, 6, 7] and relevant spectral decomposition techniques [8].

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The core idea of exploiting the block-diagonal structure and using spectral properties to resolve the permutation ambiguity is technically sound and well-justified. The lemmas and proofs, while potentially requiring careful examination by a specialist, appear logically presented.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The potential for computational savings over EM is a significant strength, particularly for large datasets. The authors clearly highlight this benefit.
*   **Novelty:** The application of MoM and spectral techniques to MHMMs represents a novel combination, offering a different perspective on parameter estimation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on synthetic and real data provide evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness and demonstrate its potential benefits, including faster runtime compared to EM. The real data experiment with handwriting trajectories is a valuable addition.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The model definitions are clear, and the algorithm is presented in a step-by-step manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Conjectures & Assumptions:** Several key claims (e.g., regarding spectral longevity and the noise level needed for accurate de-permutation) are presented as conjectures without rigorous proof. While acknowledging this limitation is reasonable, it weakens the overall confidence in the theoretical guarantees.
*   **Sensitivity to Noise:** The performance of the algorithm appears sensitive to the level of noise, as highlighted in the spectral properties section. This sensitivity isn’t fully addressed in the experimental results, and it would be valuable to explore the robustness of the method to varying noise levels.
*   **Limited Real-World Scope:** The real data experiment utilizes a specific feature transformation (cubic polynomial) to make the problem tractable. It's unclear whether this transformation is generally applicable or introduces bias. More diverse real-world datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Algorithm 1 Details:** Algorithm 1 lacks specifics on the clustering method used to identify the blocks in the transition matrix. Providing further detail on the clustering process would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While comparing to EM is relevant, the paper does not benchmark against other recent MHMM learning approaches, potentially limiting the assessment of its relative performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to MHMM learning that addresses a key computational challenge.
*   The empirical results, although not exhaustive, provide encouraging evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness and potential benefits.
*   The connection to established theoretical frameworks (MoM, spectral learning) provides a solid foundation for the work.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on conjectures and unproven assumptions weakens the theoretical guarantees.
*   The sensitivity to noise and the limited scope of the real-world experiments raise concerns about the algorithm’s robustness and generalizability.
*   The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art MHMM learning approaches limits the assessment of its relative performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong borderline accept**. The core idea is original and promising, and the technical work is commendable. However, the weaknesses, particularly the reliance on conjectures and the limited robustness analysis, prevent me from recommending a confident acceptance. Addressing the concerns about noise sensitivity and providing more rigorous theoretical justification for the key claims would significantly strengthen the paper. If the authors can provide additional experiments demonstrating robustness and a more thorough comparison to existing methods, I would be happy to reconsider my assessment.



**Recommendation:** Borderline Accept. Encourage revision with attention to the identified weaknesses.



**Specific Suggestions:**

*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s sensitivity to noise.
*   Attempt to prove or provide stronger justification for the conjectures.
*   Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse real-world datasets and comparisons to other MHMM learning approaches.
*   Provide more specifics about the clustering method used in Algorithm 1.
*   Discuss the limitations of the cubic polynomial transformation used in the handwriting experiment and its potential impact on the results.
