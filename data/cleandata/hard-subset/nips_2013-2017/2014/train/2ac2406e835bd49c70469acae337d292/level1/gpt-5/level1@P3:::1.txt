Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix Ā, concatenated emission matrix Ō, and an initial distribution ν̄ containing the mixture weights. Existing spectral algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al., tensor methods) can recover HMM parameters up to a permutation of hidden states. For an MHMM, this permutation scrambles states across blocks, preventing component-wise parameter recovery. The paper proposes to “de-permute” by exploiting spectral properties of Ā: as e→∞, Āe reveals K rank-1 blocks corresponding to K mixture components if each local Ak is ergodic (unique eigenvalue 1). In the noisy case, the authors construct a low-rank reconstruction Ar from the top-K eigen-pairs of ĀP and cluster its columns to recover blocks; they also propose a heuristic “spectral longevity” criterion to estimate K by tracking eigenvalue dominance as powers increase. Experiments on synthetic data show reasonable de-permutation and speed advantages over EM; a small real-data study (UCI character trajectories) suggests the method is a useful initializer for EM.

The work builds on spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu et al.), tensor methods for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al.), and earlier applications of MHMMs to sequence clustering (Smyth, NIPS; Qi et al., IEEE TSP). It is close in spirit to spectral clustering/graph partitioning ideas used for block structure recovery. For continuous observations, the paper adopts the standard spectral approach using conditional mean features (E[xt|rt=u]), but omits references to related extensions (e.g., Song et al., Hilbert space embeddings of HMMs, NIPS 2010; PSR-based spectral learning).

Strengths
- Simple, appealing reduction: MHMM learning via HMM spectral learning plus a principled de-permutation based on the spectrum of Ā.
- Computational benefit: moment computation is one pass over data; subsequent linear algebra is independent of dataset size, making the method attractive for large N.
- Insightful use of Markov chain spectral structure: uniqueness of eigenvalue 1 per block implies K eigenvalues at 1 pre-noise; the proposed clustering of Ar columns is intuitive and practical.
- Empirical results indicate speedups over EM and show the method as an effective initializer.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory is incomplete where it matters most. The central finite-sample robustness (Conjecture 1) and the K-estimation rule (Lemma 3 relies on strong gap assumptions) lack rigorous statements leveraging standard perturbation tools (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) and concentration for empirical moments.
- Identifiability and conditions are under-specified. Beyond “each Ak has a unique eigenvalue 1,” recovery requires: (i) distinct stationary distributions across blocks or separable eigenspaces, (ii) sufficient spectral gap between global λGk and the largest local λL2,k, and (iii) an observation model satisfying rank/separability for the MoM step (e.g., L ≥ MK and column-rank(Ō)). These assumptions are scattered and not formalized.
- Practical constraints: the requirement MK < L for the MoM pipeline is restrictive, especially for continuous data; the remedy via polynomial feature maps increases variance/noise and runtime. This limitation should be made explicit in the method’s scope.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Synthetic studies are small-scale; the de-permutation test with only two sequences per component and short lengths is not stress-testing. K-estimation is not systematically evaluated (e.g., recovery rate vs. spectral gap/noise). Comparisons lack other scalable baselines (e.g., spectral clustering on sequence-level features, variational MHMMs), and there is no ablation for the de-permutation step alone.
- Model mismatches not considered: different numbers of states per component, nearly degenerate blocks (similar stationary distributions), and non-ergodic/slow-mixing Ak’s where eigenvalue gaps are small.

Quality
Technically sound in the noiseless reduction and the qualitative spectral rationale; however, key consistency and sample complexity claims are conjectural. The de-permutation via eigen-structure is reasonable but needs a perturbation analysis and a clear identifiability statement. The experimental evidence, while suggestive, is not yet strong enough to substantiate broad claims.

Clarity
Generally well written. The core idea is clear, and the block-diagonal exposition is helpful. Suggestions:
- Precisely state the HMM spectral method used for continuous observations and its assumptions (e.g., feature choice, required ranks), and how π and νk are recovered post de-permutation (ν̄ block sums yield πk; normalize to obtain νk).
- Define all notation once and avoid switching between r̄, ĀP, A, etc. Provide a concrete algorithm for Ar construction (e.g., how e is chosen, how γ is set) and the clustering step (which features—columns of Ar, top-K eigenvectors?).
- Move conjectures to a dedicated section with discussion of when they fail (e.g., small spectral gap).

Originality
Moderate. The observation that an MHMM yields a block-diagonal Ā is straightforward, and using eigenvectors to recover blocks is related to standard spectral clustering/graph partitioning. The “spectral longevity” heuristic is novel but somewhat ad hoc. The contribution is a practical combination and adaptation rather than a fundamentally new spectral method.

Significance
Potentially useful for large-scale sequence clustering and as an EM initializer. Broader impact is limited by strong assumptions (MK < L, ergodicity, spectral gaps) and the missing theory. With strengthened analysis and more comprehensive experiments (including K recovery), the approach could be a solid addition to the spectral toolbox.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample perturbation analysis of the de-permutation via eigenspace clustering (Davis–Kahan bounds on the top-K invariant subspace; clustering consistency).
- Formalize identifiability conditions (rank(Ō), distinct blocks, gaps) and discuss violation scenarios.
- Evaluate K estimation systematically across varying gaps, noise, T, and N; compare with simpler criteria (e.g., eigengap, information criteria on Ar).
- Consider heterogeneous M across components and propose padding/merging strategies.
- Expand baselines and datasets; include settings where EM struggles and where polynomial features are unnecessary (e.g., discrete emissions).
- Clarify computational complexity of moment estimation and the de-permutation step as functions of L, M, K.

Pros
- Elegant reduction; computationally attractive
- Practical de-permutation strategy; effective EM initialization
- Clear intuition grounded in Markov chain spectral theory

Cons
- Core robustness is conjectural; missing finite-sample guarantees
- Restrictive assumptions (MK < L; spectral separation)
- Limited empirical validation and K-estimation analysis

Recommendation
Borderline. I would lean toward rejection in current form due to the lack of theoretical guarantees where they are most needed and limited experiments. With stronger analysis and broader evaluation, this could become a valuable contribution. My confidence is medium.
