This paper proposes a spectral method-of-moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix; thus a spectral HMM learner (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012) can estimate global parameters up to a permutation of states. The main technical contribution is a de-permutation procedure that leverages spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, K eigenvalues at 1 induce K rank-1 blocks; under estimation noise, the authors propose a low-rank reconstruction using the K “most persistent” eigenvalues (via a “spectral longevity” heuristic), then cluster columns to recover block membership, yielding parameters of the individual HMMs and an estimate of K. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwriting dataset compare favorably to EM in speed, with competitive clustering accuracy, and show utility as an EM initializer. The work builds on MoM/tensor literature (Anandkumar et al.) and prior MHMM applications (Smyth; Qi et al.).

Quality: The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is sound and the de-permutation idea is intuitive. However, the core robustness claims rely on Conjecture 1 and heuristic conditions (e.g., |λGK| > maxk |λL2,k|) without formal guarantees for K recovery or clustering correctness under perturbations. The MoM step for continuous emissions is not fully specified (which moments/features, whitening), and the sample complexity bound is borrowed rather than derived for this setting. Empirics emphasize clustering accuracy and runtime but lack parameter recovery metrics, likelihoods, or robustness to model mismatch.

Clarity: Generally well organized, though Section 3 would benefit from a precise description of the moment construction and computational complexity, and clearer guidance on the longevity hyperparameters (γ, exponent range).

Originality: Casting MHMM learning as spectral de-permutation of a block-diagonal HMM is a neat synthesis; the longevity-based K selection is novel, if heuristic. The reduction itself is known, but the permutation-recovery mechanism tailored to MHMMs appears new.

Significance: If made robust, the approach could offer a scalable alternative or initializer to EM for sequence clustering. Current lack of guarantees limits impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, scalable algorithm; practical speedups over EM.
- Clear idea likely of interest to NeurIPS community.
- Useful initializer for EM on real data.

Cons for acceptance:
- Key theoretical claims are conjectural; no perturbation or consistency guarantees.
- Incomplete specification of moment construction for continuous emissions.
- Limited empirical breadth and diagnostics; sensitivity of K estimation not fully analyzed.

Suggestions: Provide perturbation bounds (e.g., via Davis–Kahan/Wedin) to justify subspace recovery and correct block clustering; compare longevity to standard eigengap criteria; report parameter error and likelihood; detail moment features and computational costs; assess robustness to misspecification and overlapping spectra.
