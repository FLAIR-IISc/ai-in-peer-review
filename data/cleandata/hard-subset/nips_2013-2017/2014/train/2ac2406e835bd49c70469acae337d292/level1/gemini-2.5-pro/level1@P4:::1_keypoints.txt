- Proposes a spectral Method-of-Moments algorithm for Mixtures of HMMs by representing an MHMM with K components and M states each as a single HMM with MK states.
- Equivalent large HMM has block-diagonal transition matrix, concatenated emission matrix, and weighted concatenated initial distribution.
- Applies existing spectral HMM learning methods to the large HMM but faces permutation ambiguity over the MK hidden states.
- Main contribution: a spectral de-permutation method that identifies K stationary-mode eigenvalues and uses low-rank reconstruction plus clustering to group states by component.
- Proposes a heuristic (Lemma 3) to estimate the number of components K from spectral “longevity.”
- Empirically demonstrates computational advantages over EM and shows the method can provide strong EM initializations on real and synthetic data.
- The noiseless derivation (Section 3.2.1) is clear and technically sound.
- Theoretical support for the noisy/practical case is weak: Conjecture 1 lacks formal proof and reuses results without rigorous derivation.
- Lemma 3 and the spectral-longevity metric are heuristic with strong conditions and only sketchy proofs; robustness is not analyzed.
- Experiments are adequate but incomplete: failure modes (e.g., violated spectral gap or very similar components) are not explored.
- Some sections lack precision and reproducibility details, e.g., justification for Conjecture 1 and vague steps in Algorithm 1 (“sufficiently large range”).
- The approach is novel and non-trivial, extending spectral learning to MHMMs and highlighting permutation resolution as a key challenge.
- The method is significant for practical scalability, offering much faster alternatives to EM for large datasets.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel spectral solution for MHMMs, computational gains over EM, useful EM initialization, and conceptual contribution of spectral clustering for permutation resolution.
- Arguments for rejection: underdeveloped noisy-case theory, insufficient robustness/experimental validation (including K estimation), and missing algorithmic/theoretical details for full reproducibility.
