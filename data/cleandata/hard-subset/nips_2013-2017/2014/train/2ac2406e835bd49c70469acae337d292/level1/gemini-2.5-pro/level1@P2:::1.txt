This paper presents a novel spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard learning algorithm for this model is Expectation-Maximization (EM), which can be computationally expensive on large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. The authors' proposed approach is based on the Method of Moments (MoM), which offers a non-iterative and computationally efficient alternative.

The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with MK states. This larger HMM possesses a special block-diagonal transition matrix. While existing spectral HMM learning algorithms (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012) can be applied to estimate the parameters of this large HMM, they only do so up to an unknown permutation of the states. This permutation ambiguity mixes the states from different component HMMs, making it impossible to recover the individual model parameters. The main contribution of this paper is a method to resolve this ambiguity. The authors propose a "de-permutation" algorithm that leverages the spectral properties of the block-diagonal structure. By computing a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix using its top K eigenvectors and then clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix, they can recover the permutation and separate the parameters of the individual HMM components. The paper also proposes a method based on eigenvalue "longevity" to estimate the number of components K from the data. This work is a natural and clever extension of the recent line of research on spectral learning for latent variable models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem: the efficient learning of MHMMs. The proposed solution is novel and addresses a fundamental challenge in applying spectral methods to this model class. The de-permutation technique based on the spectral properties of the transition matrix is elegant and provides a principled way to decompose the learned global model into its constituent parts. This extends the applicability of spectral learning methods to a more complex and widely used hierarchical model.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical development is presented in a logical sequence. The authors first establish the equivalence between an MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM (Lemma 1), then clearly explain the permutation problem, and finally detail their proposed solution, building from the ideal noiseless case to the more realistic noisy scenario. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the entire procedure.

3.  **Computational Advantage:** A major strength of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. By leveraging MoM, the algorithm's complexity after computing the initial moments is independent of the number of sequences, a significant advantage over EM, which requires passes over the entire dataset at each iteration. The speed-up is clearly demonstrated in the experiments (Figure 4).

4.  **Practical Utility as an Initializer:** The real-data experiment highlights a key practical contribution. While the spectral method alone may not always outperform a well-tuned EM algorithm in terms of accuracy, it serves as a fast and high-quality initialization for EM. This hybrid MoM+EM approach can potentially achieve both the speed and global properties of spectral methods and the high accuracy of maximum likelihood estimation, representing a best-of-both-worlds scenario.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The main weakness of the paper lies in the theoretical justification for the noisy case. The analysis in Section 3.2.2 relies on "Conjecture 1," which adapts a sample complexity bound from a related but different model (a mixture model) without a formal proof for the MHMM case. The authors themselves state that a full analysis is out of the scope of the paper. Similarly, the proof for Lemma 3, which concerns the estimation of K, is highly intuitive but lacks formal derivation. While the ideas are plausible, the lack of rigorous proofs for these central claims weakens the paper's theoretical foundation.

2.  **Unstated Assumptions:** The success of the de-permutation method hinges on a spectral gap condition: that the K "global" eigenvalues corresponding to the stationary distributions remain separated from the "local" eigenvalues related to mixing within components. The paper formalizes this as `|λGK| > max |λL2,k|`. However, the practical implications of this assumption are not thoroughly explored. It is unclear how common it is for this condition to hold, or how gracefully the algorithm fails when it is violated. The experiments demonstrate the method works on the chosen datasets but do not stress-test the boundaries of this crucial assumption.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are positive but could be strengthened. In Figure 4, the comparison with EM would be more robust if it showed distributions of accuracies over multiple trials (e.g., using box plots) rather than what appears to be a single run. In the real-data experiment (Table 1), while comparing to the *maximum* accuracy of EM over 5 runs is a reasonable choice, showing the mean and variance would provide a more complete picture of EM's sensitivity to initialization.

**Summary of Opinion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution. It presents a novel, elegant, and computationally efficient algorithm for learning MHMMs, a significant problem in sequence analysis. The core idea of spectral de-permutation is clever and well-motivated. The primary weaknesses are the lack of complete theoretical proofs for the noisy case and a need for a more thorough exploration of the underlying assumptions. However, the proposed method is well-explained, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its advantages in speed and its utility as a powerful initialization strategy for EM. The contributions are significant enough to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
