This paper proposes a Method of Moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to leverage the equivalence between an MHMM and an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Existing MoM algorithms can learn the parameters of the latter, but a permutation ambiguity arises. The authors address this by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix to estimate a de-permutation mapping, recovering the individual HMM parameters. They also propose a method to estimate the number of HMMs.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a computational bottleneck of traditional EM-based MHMM learning, particularly for large datasets. The connection to existing MoM work on HMMs ([3]) is clearly established, and the problem of permutation ambiguity is appropriately identified. The proposal to utilize spectral properties is a novel approach and, if successful, could provide a significant computational advantage. The experiments on both synthetic and real data offer some support for the method.

**Quality:** The technical approach appears sound, building on established MoM techniques and extending them to the MHMM setting. The theoretical justification regarding the spectral properties (Lemmas 1 & 2, Conjecture 1) is presented, though Conjecture 1 feels somewhat hand-wavy and would benefit from more rigorous analysis. The experimental validation, while present, feels limited in scope. The experiments demonstrate the algorithm works on relatively simple cases, but the discussion on real data lacks depth – simply stating it can be a good initialization scheme for EM is weak. The paper would be stronger with a more thorough analysis of the performance on real data, comparing it directly with state-of-the-art MHMM learning approaches beyond just using it as an initialization. The handling of noise and its impact on spectral decomposition is acknowledged, but the proposed condition (Definition 3) seems complex and its practical applicability isn’t well demonstrated.  The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of the model and the proposed algorithm. However, the spectral analysis section (3.2) is dense and requires a strong background in spectral graph theory to fully grasp. Equations (5) and (6) are particularly challenging to follow without careful reading.  The notation is generally consistent, though the switch between index and matrix notation could be smoother. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the method, which is helpful.

**Originality:** The application of spectral learning to address the permutation ambiguity in MoM-based MHMM learning is a novel contribution. While MoM is not new ([1, 2, 3]), extending it to MHMMs and tackling the inherent ambiguity with spectral methods represents a genuine advance.  The paper appropriately cites relevant work, including prior work on spectral learning for HMMs [3] and applications of MHMMs [4, 5, 6, 7].  However, it would be helpful to discuss any related work attempting similar solutions to the permutation ambiguity problem, even if they use different approaches.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is considerable. A computationally efficient algorithm for learning MHMMs, particularly for large datasets, could have broad applications in various domains such as speech recognition, bioinformatics, and time-series analysis. The proposed approach addresses a key limitation of existing MHMM learning methods. However, the practical impact is currently limited by the scope of the experiments and the lack of a strong comparative analysis against established methods. The real data experiments lack sufficient detail to assess the real-world performance gains.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of spectral learning to solve a challenging problem in MHMM learning.
*   Potential for significant computational improvements over EM-based methods.
*   Clear presentation of the problem and proposed solution.
*   Theoretical justification for the spectral approach (though could be strengthened).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation, especially on real-world datasets.
*   Real data experiment lacks a strong comparative analysis.
*   The proposed spectral condition (Definition 3) feels complex and its practical application is unclear.
*   Conjecture 1 lacks a more rigorous justification.
*   Some sections (3.2) require significant background knowledge and are not easily accessible.



**Overall Assessment:** I would rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the core idea is novel and technically sound, the limited experimental evaluation and the complexity of some sections prevent a stronger recommendation. The paper shows promise but requires further work to demonstrate its practical significance and robustness. The authors should address the concerns raised above, particularly by conducting more comprehensive experiments on real-world datasets and providing a more detailed analysis of the proposed spectral condition.
