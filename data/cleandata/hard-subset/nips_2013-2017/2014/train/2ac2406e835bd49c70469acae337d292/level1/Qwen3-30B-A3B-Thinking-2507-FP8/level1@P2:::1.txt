

# Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in the traditional EM algorithm. The authors recognize that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling the application of spectral learning methods developed for standard HMMs. The key contribution is resolving the permutation ambiguity that arises in MoM estimation through spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

The paper builds on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning framework for HMMs (COLT 2012) and extends it to MHMMs. Previous work on MHMMs has primarily relied on EM, which is computationally prohibitive for large datasets due to the need for forward-backward message passing on every sequence in each iteration. The authors make a theoretically sound connection between MHMMs and structured HMMs, then develop a method to recover the individual HMM parameters from the global HMM parameters by exploiting the spectral properties of the transition matrix. Their approach is particularly significant for large-scale sequence clustering applications.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical foundations. The proof that MHMMs can be represented as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices (Lemma 1) is straightforward and well-motivated. The authors effectively address the permutation ambiguity problem by leveraging spectral properties of the global transition matrix, providing a theoretically grounded solution. The experimental validation on synthetic data demonstrates the algorithm's ability to recover the correct block-diagonal structure even with limited data (Figure 3). The real data experiment (Table 1) shows practical utility, demonstrating that their method can serve as an effective initialization for EM. While Conjecture 1 regarding error bounds is not fully proven, the authors provide a reasonable justification based on existing literature (Anandkumar et al., 2012).

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the key insights are presented with logical progression. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and implementable. The experimental results are presented with appropriate visualizations that clearly illustrate the performance characteristics of the proposed method.

*Originality:* The core contribution—applying spectral learning to MHMMs by resolving the permutation ambiguity through spectral properties—is novel. While spectral learning for HMMs has been established (Anandkumar et al., 2012), extending this to MHMMs presents unique challenges that the authors successfully address. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on MHMMs (Smyth, 1997; Qi et al., 2006) and spectral learning (Hsu et al., 2009) by focusing on the computational advantages and permutation ambiguity resolution.

*Significance:* This work addresses an important practical problem in sequence clustering. The computational advantage over EM (Figure 4) is substantial, making MHMMs feasible for large-scale applications where EM would be prohibitively expensive. The demonstration that their method can serve as a good initialization for EM (Table 1) further enhances its practical significance. The paper advances the state of the art in learning latent variable models for sequence data by providing a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMMs.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The paper assumes MK < L (where L is the dimension of observations), which is a significant limitation. This constraint may not hold in many practical applications, and the authors address it by transforming the data with a cubic polynomial feature transformation in the real data experiment. While this is a reasonable workaround, it suggests the method has limitations in its applicability without such transformations.

*Originality:* The paper could better distinguish itself from related work on spectral learning for mixture models. For instance, Chaganty and Liang (ICML 2013) developed spectral methods for mixtures of linear regressions, and the authors could have more explicitly contrasted their approach with such methods.

*Significance:* While the computational advantage is clear, the paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other spectral learning approaches for mixture models. A comparison with methods like tensor decomposition approaches (Anandkumar et al., 2014) would strengthen the significance argument.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pros:*
- Novel application of spectral learning to MHMMs with a solution to the permutation ambiguity problem
- Theoretical foundation is sound and well-motivated
- Demonstrates clear computational advantages over EM on synthetic and real data
- The method can serve as a good initialization for EM
- Well-written and clearly organized

*Cons:*
- The MK < L constraint limits applicability without data transformation
- The error bound conjecture is not fully proven
- Limited comparison with other spectral learning approaches for mixture models

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending spectral learning methods to MHMMs, addressing a key computational bottleneck in the standard EM approach. The theoretical insights are sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical significance is clear. The main limitation is the MK < L constraint, but this is addressed in the real data experiment through a reasonable data transformation. The paper represents a solid advancement in the area of latent variable model learning, with clear potential for practical application in large-scale sequence clustering problems.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the comparison with related work on spectral learning for mixture models and to more thoroughly discuss the implications of the MK < L constraint. The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level, with particularly strong contributions in originality and significance.
