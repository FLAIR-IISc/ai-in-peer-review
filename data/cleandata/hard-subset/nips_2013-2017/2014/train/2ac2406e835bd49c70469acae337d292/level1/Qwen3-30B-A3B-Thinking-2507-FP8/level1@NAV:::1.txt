

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the Method of Moments (MoM), addressing a significant computational challenge in learning MHMMs. The authors build upon recent NIPS/COLT work on spectral learning for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012) by recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The key innovation lies in resolving the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM estimation through spectral properties of the global transition matrix, enabling recovery of individual HMM parameters. The paper provides theoretical justification for this approach (Lemmas 1-3) and presents a practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) with experimental validation on synthetic and real data.

The paper's strengths are significant: it tackles an important problem with clear practical implications (large-scale sequence clustering), offers a theoretically grounded solution that extends previous spectral learning work, and demonstrates empirical effectiveness through thorough experiments. The theoretical analysis, while relying on a conjecture (Conjecture 1), is well-motivated and the experimental results convincingly show computational advantages over EM, particularly with larger datasets. The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories demonstrates practical utility, showing the method's effectiveness as an EM initialization.

However, the paper has some limitations. The theoretical justification for the depermutation algorithm relies on a conjecture without full proof, and the justification borrows results from another paper without thorough adaptation to the MHMM context. The real data experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation to satisfy L > MK, which may limit the method's applicability to certain datasets. Additionally, the paper doesn't thoroughly compare against alternative spectral approaches for mixture models, which would strengthen the significance argument.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The method provides a valuable computational alternative to EM for MHMM learning, and the theoretical insights extend the spectral learning framework to more complex models. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to the NIPS community. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the practical benefits and clear presentation make this a strong candidate for acceptance.
