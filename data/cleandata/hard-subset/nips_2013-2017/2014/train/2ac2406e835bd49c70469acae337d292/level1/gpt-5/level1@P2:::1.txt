Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be recast as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block corresponds to one component HMM and the initial distribution is scaled by the mixture weights. Leveraging this, the authors apply an existing spectral HMM learner to recover global parameters up to a permutation of the MK latent states, and then propose a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover per-component parameters and to estimate the number of mixture components K. The main technical ideas are: (i) in the noiseless setting, the global transition matrix has exactly K eigenvalues equal to 1 (one per component), which implies a characteristic block structure in powers of the transition matrix; (ii) in the noisy setting, a rank-K reconstruction using the top eigenvalues can approximately recover the block structure; (iii) a “spectral longevity” heuristic is proposed to select K by tracking how the largest eigenvalues dominate as powers increase. Experiments include synthetic data (de-permutation success vs data size; accuracy/speed tradeoff vs EM) and a small real-data evaluation on handwritten trajectory pairs, where the spectral method is also used as an EM initializer.

This work builds on spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models (e.g., Hsu et al., Anandkumar et al.) and classical MHMM modeling and EM training (e.g., Smyth). To my knowledge, prior spectral approaches do not directly address MHMM parameter recovery via de-permutation of a global HMM, so the focus on de-permutation and K selection appears novel.

Strengths
- Novel angle: Casting MHMM learning as HMM learning and then resolving the permutation to recover component HMMs is elegant and potentially impactful for scalable sequence clustering.
- Clear spectral intuition in the noiseless case: the multiplicity of eigenvalue 1 and the limiting block structure are correct and neatly justify the de-permutation approach.
- Computational appeal: Once moments are computed, the estimation does not scale with the number of sequences or their lengths, which is attractive relative to EM’s per-iteration forward-backward cost.
- Practical use as EM initialization: On the real-data task, the spectral estimates serve as a strong initializer, which is a plausible and useful role even if standalone accuracy is variable.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical guarantees are incomplete. The core finite-sample claims for de-permutation and K selection are presented as conjectures or rely on heuristic arguments (re-using bounds from other models). No end-to-end identifiability or finite-sample guarantees are proved for recovering component parameters of an MHMM.
- Dependence on spectral conditions and gaps. Success requires that each component HMM have a unique stationary eigenvalue at 1 and sufficient separation between global and local eigenvalues after noise. The paper does not quantify how often these conditions hold in realistic MHMMs (e.g., slowly mixing chains with multiple eigenvalues near 1) or how sensitive the method is when they fail.
- Practical constraints on observation dimension. The method assumes the observation dimension L exceeds MK (as in many spectral methods). The real-data experiment had to expand L via a cubic feature map to satisfy L > MK, which may distort the data distribution and complicate interpretation. This raises questions about applicability to common low-dimensional settings.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. The synthetic experiments primarily visualize de-permutation success and accuracy/speed trends but do not provide detailed parameter recovery metrics, robustness to model mismatch, or sensitivity to spectral gaps and noise. The real-data experiment is small-scale (pairwise classes, K fixed to 2), with no evaluation of K selection.
- Algorithmic details under-specified. Important hyperparameters (range of exponents e, choice of γ, clustering method and initialization for de-permutation) are not precisely specified, making reproduction difficult and leaving open questions about stability.

Quality
Technically, the noiseless lemmas are sound. The noisy case is the crux of MHMM learning in practice, and here the paper provides only partial justification: a conjectured Frobenius error bound (borrowing rates from related models), and a heuristic K estimator with a spectral-gap-based lemma that presumes already favorable ordering of estimated eigenvalues. There is no formal guarantee that the column clustering of the rank-K reconstruction recovers the true permutation with high probability. Empirically, the method seems to work in some regimes and is faster than EM, but the breadth and depth of evaluation are not sufficient to compensate for the theoretical gaps.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written. The equivalence between MHMM and block-diagonal HMM is clearly explained. The spectral intuition is conveyed well, and Algorithm 1 is easy to follow. However, several key implementation choices are vague (how to pick e and γ; how to cluster columns; normalization), and the experimental setup lacks some necessary details (exact moment computations, complexity numbers, variance across runs).

Originality
The de-permutation approach for MHMMs via spectral properties of the global transition matrix and the “spectral longevity” criterion for K appear novel. Prior spectral work on HMMs and mixtures does not, to my knowledge, solve this MHMM-specific permutation disentanglement.

Significance
If the de-permutation and K selection could be backed by rigorous guarantees and shown robust in broader settings, this would be a valuable contribution for scalable sequence clustering. As it stands, the reliance on strong spectral conditions, the L > MK constraint, and limited empirical validation reduce the immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample guarantees (or at least high-probability sufficient conditions) for correct de-permutation under noise, including explicit dependence on spectral gaps, sample size, and model mismatch.
- Give a principled analysis (or well-justified heuristic) for selecting e and γ, and ablate their effects.
- Evaluate K selection quantitatively on synthetic data across a range of K, M, mixing rates, and observation dimensions.
- Report parameter recovery (not just clustering) and robustness to near-degenerate eigenvalue spectra.
- Compare against alternative strategies (e.g., simple state-similarity heuristics, spectral clustering on transition features, or tensor-based MHMM variants if available).
- Provide runtime breakdowns (moments vs decomposition vs clustering) and explicit complexity scaling.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant and potentially useful de-permutation approach specific to MHMMs.
- Computational advantages over EM; promising as an EM initializer.
- Clear spectral rationale in the noiseless case; plausible heuristics that work in some regimes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of rigorous guarantees for the key noisy-case steps; central results are conjectural.
- Strong assumptions and sensitivity to spectral gaps; unclear robustness in common hard regimes.
- Limited and small-scale experiments; no thorough evaluation of K estimation; reliance on feature expansion to meet L > MK.

Overall assessment
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current submission lacks theoretical guarantees for the noisy case and stronger empirical validation. Strengthening both the analysis and experiments would substantially improve the paper.
