This paper presents a novel Method of Moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M states each as a single, larger HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs [Hsu et al., 2012] to this equivalent model. The main technical contribution is a method to resolve the fundamental permutation ambiguity that arises from the spectral estimation. This is achieved by exploiting the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix; specifically, by clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of this matrix to recover the block structure and thus identify the parameters of the individual HMM components. This work builds directly on the recent line of research on spectral learning for latent variable models [Anandkumar et al., 2012, 2014], extending its applicability from single HMMs to the more complex MHMM setting.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important problem of efficient MHMM learning, providing a valuable non-iterative alternative to the computationally expensive and initialization-sensitive EM algorithm. The approach of identifying the permutation ambiguity as the key challenge and solving it via a spectral clustering procedure on the global transition matrix is both elegant and novel. The proposed method's significant speed advantage, demonstrated experimentally, makes it a highly practical contribution for large-scale sequence clustering tasks.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical motivation is sound, and the equivalence between an MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM is clearly established. The proposed algorithm for de-permutation is well-reasoned for the noiseless case. The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate that the de-permutation method is robust to noise and that the overall algorithm is much faster than EM while achieving comparable accuracy, especially with sufficient data.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis for the noisy case in Section 3.2.2, while intuitive, relies on "Conjecture 1" for which a formal proof is not provided. The justification reuses a sample complexity bound from a related but different model, which weakens the claim. Similarly, the proof of Lemma 3 for estimating the number of clusters K is brief. Strengthening these theoretical results would make the paper's contributions more robust.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The real-data experiment shows that the proposed method serves as an excellent initializer for EM but does not consistently outperform the best run of randomly initialized EM on its own. This tempers the claim of the method as a standalone replacement and frames it more as a powerful initialization technique, which is still a significant contribution. It would also be beneficial to discuss the practical prevalence of the spectral gap condition required by the theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and computationally efficient spectral algorithm for the challenging problem of learning MHMMs.
2.  The core technical contribution—a spectral de-permutation method—is clever and well-motivated.
3.  The significant speedup over EM is a major practical advantage for large datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The theoretical guarantees for the noisy case are not fully proven and rest on a conjecture.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant contribution. The weaknesses, primarily concerning the completeness of the theoretical analysis, are outweighed by the strengths of the proposed method and its practical implications. I recommend acceptance.
