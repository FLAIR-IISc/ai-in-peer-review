### **Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"**

#### **Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The standard learning algorithm for MHMMs, Expectation-Maximization (EM), is often computationally expensive for large datasets and is susceptible to local optima. This work builds upon the recent line of research on spectral learning for latent variable models (e.g., Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2012), which offers computationally efficient and globally consistent estimators.

The authors' core insight is to view an MHMM with K components as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Applying an existing spectral HMM learning algorithm to this larger model yields the global parameters, but with a critical permutation ambiguity—the learned states are shuffled, making it impossible to disentangle the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this paper is a method to resolve this permutation. The proposed solution is to analyze the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By identifying the K dominant eigenvalues (which are close to 1 in the noisy case) and clustering the columns of a corresponding low-rank matrix reconstruction, the algorithm can recover the permutation and correctly assign parameters to each component HMM. The paper also proposes a heuristic based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of components, K, from the data. The method is validated on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating significant speed advantages over EM with comparable accuracy.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant problem: scalable learning for MHMMs, a widely used model for sequence clustering. The proposed approach of using spectral methods to "de-permute" the parameters learned from a global HMM formulation is both novel and elegant. It provides a viable path for applying the theoretical guarantees and computational benefits of MoM to more complex, hierarchical models. This is a non-trivial extension of prior work on spectral learning for simpler models like single HMMs and represents a valuable contribution to the field.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building their argument, starting from the equivalence of an MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM (Lemma 1), identifying the permutation ambiguity as the key challenge, and then clearly presenting their spectral solution. Figure 1 is particularly effective in providing a visual intuition for the de-permutation method in both noiseless and noisy settings. Algorithm 1 provides a concise and reproducible summary of the entire procedure.

3.  **Computational Advantage:** A major strength of the proposed method is its computational efficiency. By avoiding the iterative forward-backward passes over the entire dataset required by EM, the algorithm's runtime after computing the initial moments is independent of the dataset size. The experimental results in Figure 4 compellingly demonstrate this speed-up, showing orders of magnitude faster performance than EM, which is a significant practical advantage for large-scale applications.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** While the core idea is intuitive and well-motivated, the theoretical justification has some gaps. Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank matrix reconstruction, is presented without proof and justified by analogy to a result for a different model. This is not rigorous. Similarly, the proof of Lemma 3, which concerns the estimation of K, is more of a high-level argument about convergence rates than a formal proof. The "spectral longevity" measure itself feels somewhat heuristic, and its sensitivity to the parameter γ is not discussed. A more formal analysis of the conditions under which the de-permutation is guaranteed to succeed would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Underlying Assumptions:** The success of the algorithm hinges on a key spectral condition: `|λGK| > max |λL2,k|`. This means there must be a clear spectral gap between the K eigenvalues corresponding to the stationary distributions of the components and all other eigenvalues related to mixing dynamics within components. The paper does not analyze when this condition is likely to hold or fail. One can imagine scenarios with very similar component HMMs or very slow-mixing components where this gap could vanish, causing the method to fail. These limitations should be discussed more explicitly.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments support the main claims but could be more comprehensive. The real-data experiment on the character trajectory dataset shows that the spectral method serves as an excellent initializer for EM, but is not always superior to a well-initialized EM on its own. This tempers the claim of being a complete replacement. Furthermore, the paper briefly mentions a feature transformation was needed to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint of the underlying MoM algorithm. This is a significant practical limitation of many tensor-based methods and warrants more discussion, as it may not be feasible for all datasets. Testing the method on a wider range of datasets, particularly those that might challenge the spectral gap assumption, would be beneficial.

#### **Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution to a key roadblock in applying MoM to hierarchical models.
*   The method offers a significant computational advantage over the standard EM algorithm, making MHMMs more scalable.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the core contribution is easy to understand and appreciate.
*   The work is of high significance to researchers working on latent variable models and large-scale sequence analysis.

#### **Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The theoretical analysis is incomplete, relying on an unproven conjecture and heuristic arguments.
*   The practical applicability is limited by strong, unanalyzed assumptions about the spectral properties of the model.
*   The experimental results, while positive, are limited and do not fully explore the failure modes of the algorithm.

#### **Overall Recommendation**

Despite some weaknesses in the theoretical justification, this paper makes a strong and valuable contribution. The proposed spectral de-permutation method is a clever and significant step towards creating scalable, non-iterative learning algorithms for complex models like MHMMs. The clarity of the presentation and the impressive computational gains demonstrated in the experiments outweigh the theoretical gaps for a conference paper. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors revise the manuscript to be more transparent about the method's underlying assumptions and theoretical limitations.
