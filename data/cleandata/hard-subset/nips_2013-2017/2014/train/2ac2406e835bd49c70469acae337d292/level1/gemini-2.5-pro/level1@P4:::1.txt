### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper presents a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The key idea is to first represent an MHMM with K components and M states per component as a single, larger HMM with MK states. This equivalent HMM has a specific structure: its transition matrix is block-diagonal, its emission matrix is a concatenation of the component emission matrices, and its initial state distribution is a weighted concatenation of the component initial distributions.

The authors propose using existing spectral algorithms for HMMs, such as those by Hsu et al. (2012) and Anandkumar et al. (2012), to learn the parameters of this large HMM. However, this procedure recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the MK hidden states, which mixes the states from different component HMMs and prevents the direct recovery of the individual models. The main contribution of this paper is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The proposed solution exploits the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix. By identifying the K eigenvalues corresponding to the stationary modes of the components and performing a low-rank reconstruction, the authors show that the columns of the resulting matrix can be clustered to group states belonging to the same component HMM. This clustering reveals the permutation and allows for the recovery of the individual HMM parameters. The paper also proposes a heuristic for estimating the number of components, K, from the data. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data, highlighting its computational advantages over the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper's core premise—that an MHMM can be viewed as a large HMM and that the resulting permutation ambiguity can be resolved spectrally—is technically sound in the ideal, noiseless case. The derivation in Section 3.2.1 for the noiseless scenario is clear and correct. However, the theoretical support for the more practical noisy case is weak.
*   Conjecture 1, which bounds the estimation error, is not formally proven. The justification provided is brief and relies on reusing a result from [1] for a different model without a rigorous derivation, which significantly weakens the claim.
*   Lemma 3, which provides a method for estimating the number of components K, is based on a "spectral longevity" metric that feels heuristic. The conditions under which the lemma holds are strong, and its proof is more of an intuitive sketch than a formal argument. The robustness of this estimation procedure is not thoroughly analyzed.
*   The experimental evaluation is adequate but could be more comprehensive. For instance, the experiments do not explore the failure modes of the algorithm, such as when the spectral gap condition (`|λGK | > max |λL2,k|`) is violated or when component HMMs are very similar to each other.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The progression from the model definition, to the equivalence lemma, to the permutation problem, and finally to the spectral solution is logical and easy to follow. Lemma 1 provides a clean conceptual foundation for the entire approach. Figure 1 is particularly effective at illustrating the core de-permutation idea in both the noiseless and noisy settings. However, some sections lack precision. The justification for Conjecture 1 is difficult to follow due to its brevity and re-use of notation. The description of Algorithm 1 is also high-level (e.g., "in a sufficiently large range" for exponentiation), which may hinder reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel approach to a well-established problem. While it builds directly upon prior work in spectral learning for HMMs, the extension to the MHMM setting is non-trivial. The identification of the permutation ambiguity as the central challenge and the proposed spectral clustering method for resolving it is a new and insightful contribution. This work clearly distinguishes itself from previous literature on learning MHMMs, which has been dominated by EM-based approaches.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of practical importance. Learning MHMMs via EM can be computationally prohibitive for large datasets and is susceptible to poor local optima. The proposed MoM-based algorithm offers a computationally efficient alternative that circumvents these issues. The algorithm is significantly faster than EM, as demonstrated in Figure 4, making it suitable for large-scale applications. Furthermore, as shown in the real-data experiment, the method can provide an excellent initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution in itself. This work is likely to be built upon by researchers working on spectral methods for other hierarchical or mixture models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs, extending the applicability of MoM-based techniques to a more complex and widely used model.
*   **Pro:** The proposed method offers substantial computational advantages over the standard EM algorithm, making MHMMs more tractable for large datasets.
*   **Pro:** The idea of resolving the permutation ambiguity via spectral clustering of the transition matrix is a key conceptual contribution.
*   **Pro:** The method provides a principled way to initialize EM, potentially leading to better and more consistent results in practice.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Con:** The theoretical analysis for the noisy case, which is critical for any practical algorithm, is underdeveloped. Key claims are presented as conjectures with insufficient justification.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation, particularly regarding the robustness of the method and the estimation of K, could be more thorough. A deeper analysis of the method's limitations is missing.
*   **Con:** Some details of the algorithm and theoretical arguments are not presented with sufficient clarity to ensure full understanding and reproducibility.
