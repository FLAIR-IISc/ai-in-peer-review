**Summary**

This paper presents a novel method-of-moments (MoM) algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The authors' central idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M states per component as a single, large HMM with KM states. This equivalent HMM has a special block-diagonal transition matrix. While existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012; Anandkumar et al., 2014) can be applied to this large HMM, they only recover parameters up to a permutation of the KM states. This permutation scrambles the states belonging to different components, preventing the recovery of the individual MHMM parameters. The main contribution of this paper is an elegant de-permutation procedure. The authors show that the block-diagonal structure of the true transition matrix endows it with K eigenvalues equal to 1. By performing a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated (and permuted) transition matrix using its top K eigenvectors, they can obtain a matrix whose columns can be clustered to identify the states belonging to each original HMM component. This clustering reveals the permutation, allowing for the recovery of the individual component models. The paper also proposes a heuristic method based on eigenvalue "longevity" to estimate the number of components, K. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data, showing significant speed advantages over the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of sequence clustering using MHMMs. The proposed MoM-based approach is a significant departure from the standard, iterative EM algorithm. The core idea of identifying the MHMM-as-HMM equivalence and then solving the resulting permutation problem spectrally is both clever and novel. A fast, non-iterative, and globally consistent learning algorithm for MHMMs would be a valuable contribution to the machine learning community, particularly for large-scale sequence analysis tasks.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly explain the model, establish the equivalence between an MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM (Lemma 1), identify the critical permutation problem, and then build a step-by-step argument for their spectral de-permutation solution. The figures, particularly Figure 1, provide excellent intuition for why the de-permutation works in the noiseless case and the challenges presented by noise.

3.  **Methodological Contribution:** The spectral de-permutation technique is the paper's key strength. It leverages a fundamental structural property of the model (block-diagonality) to overcome a major hurdle in applying spectral methods to this hierarchical model. This approach of using spectral properties to disentangle mixture components is powerful and could potentially be adapted to other mixture models.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** While the intuition is strong, some of the theoretical claims lack formal proof and rest on conjecture.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This conjecture about the error bound of the low-rank reconstruction is central to the algorithm's justification in the noisy case. However, the justification provided is quite hand-wavy, borrowing a bound from Anandkumar et al. (2012) for a different model without a formal derivation showing its applicability here. A more rigorous analysis, perhaps using matrix perturbation theory (e.g., Davis-Kahan theorem), would be needed to turn this into a formal result.
    *   **Lemma 3 and Model Selection:** The method for estimating the number of components K is presented as a lemma but is fundamentally a heuristic. The "spectral longevity" metric is novel but not well-motivated theoretically. The proof sketch argues that the longevity depends on the ratio of eigenvalue gaps, but the connection is not formalized. This part of the contribution feels less developed than the de-permutation algorithm.

2.  **Unstated Assumptions and Limitations:** The success of the method hinges on a critical spectral gap condition: `|λ_GK| > max_k |λ_L2,k|`. This requires that the K-th largest eigenvalue of the global transition matrix (which should correspond to one of the component's stationary modes) is larger in magnitude than the second-largest eigenvalue of *any* individual component matrix. This assumption may be violated if one of the component HMMs is very slowly mixing (i.e., its second eigenvalue is close to 1). The paper would be strengthened by an explicit discussion of this assumption, the scenarios where it might fail, and an empirical evaluation of the algorithm's sensitivity to this gap.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are encouraging but could be more comprehensive.
    *   The proposed method for automatically selecting K (Lemma 3) is a key part of the algorithm but is not evaluated in the main experiments (Sections 4.2 and 4.3). It is unclear how well this criterion performs in practice compared to standard methods like BIC or cross-validation.
    *   In the real data experiment, the authors perform a feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint of the spectral method. This is a known limitation of such methods, but the ad-hoc nature of the transformation (cubic polynomial) and its effect on the learned model are not discussed. It would be valuable to understand the sensitivity of the results to this preprocessing step.
    *   The comparison with EM in Figure 4 shows the spectral method is faster but less accurate for smaller data sizes. While this is expected, it would be beneficial to see the performance for larger N and T to understand if and when the spectral method's accuracy catches up to or surpasses that of a well-initialized EM algorithm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and computationally efficient algorithm for a well-known, important model (MHMM).
*   The core idea of spectral de-permutation is a significant methodological contribution.
*   Provides a practical alternative to EM, especially for large datasets or as a high-quality initialization strategy.
*   The paper is clear, well-motivated, and opens avenues for future theoretical analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Key theoretical claims are presented as conjectures or are based on heuristic arguments, lacking full rigor.
*   The critical spectral gap assumption is not sufficiently discussed or tested.
*   The empirical evaluation of the proposed model selection criterion is absent.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper introduces a highly original and significant approach to a classical problem. The core methodological contribution—the spectral de-permutation of a block-diagonal transition matrix—is elegant and impactful. While the theoretical analysis could be more rigorous and the experiments more thorough, the novelty and potential utility of the algorithm outweigh these weaknesses for a conference publication. The work provides a strong foundation that will likely inspire follow-up research to formalize the theory and explore its applications further.
