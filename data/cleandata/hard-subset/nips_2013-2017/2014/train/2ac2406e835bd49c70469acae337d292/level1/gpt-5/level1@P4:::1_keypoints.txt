- Reduction: MHMM is treated as an HMM with MK states via block-diagonal transition and concatenated emissions, enabling spectral HMM methods.
- Core challenge: spectral estimation has permutation ambiguity that mixes states across components, preventing recovery of individual HMMs.
- Noiseless de-permutation: global transition has K eigenvalues equal to 1 and high powers reveal K clusters of columns for exact recovery.
- Noisy case approach: low-rank reconstruction using top-K eigenvalues plus a heuristic "spectral longevity" to estimate K, followed by clustering to de-permute.
- Theoretical soundness: Lemma 1 and Lemma 2/corollary are correct; noiseless de-permutation is straightforward.
- Theoretical gap: noisy-case relies on Conjecture 1 and a heuristic; lacks rigorous perturbation/sample-complexity analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Weyl bounds).
- Assumptions: method assumes each Ak has a unique eigenvalue magnitude 1 (ergodicity/aperiodicity) and full-column-rank emissions; these should be explicitly stated.
- MoM requirements: moment method needs conditions like L ≥ MK and full-rank emissions; paper uses polynomial feature expansion experimentally without analyzing its effects on identifiability/noise.
- Experiments: synthetic and UCI handwriting results show speed and competitive accuracy, and utility as EM initialization, but are small-scale and limited in scope.
- Experimental shortcomings: sparse implementation details (which spectral algorithm, moments, whitening, handling continuous observations), no model-order-selection baselines (BIC/AIC), and missing sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity: main idea and algorithmic steps are clear and well motivated, but practical computations (spectral longevity parameters, whitening, feature choices) are underspecified.
- Originality: novelty is in using spectral properties to resolve permutation and estimate K for MHMMs; reduction itself is known and robustness claim is weakened by conjectural analysis.
- Significance: potential for large computational savings and broader applicability, but immediate impact tempered by theoretical gaps and limited empirical validation.
- Strengths: simple clear reduction, computational efficiency, practical K estimation heuristic, works across emission families, useful EM initializer.
- Weaknesses & suggestions: provide perturbation guarantees, state assumptions, clarify MoM/feature choices for continuous data, compare K estimation to baselines, expand experiments (larger K/M, unbalanced mixtures, different observation models), and add reproducibility details (code, runtimes, error bars).
- Acceptance arguments: conceptually neat reduction, practical de-permutation, computational benefits, and useful EM initialization.
- Rejection arguments: main robustness claim is conjectural, empirical evaluation is limited, and reliance on L ≥ MK and feature engineering lacks theoretical justification.
- Overall: promising and practically useful approach that needs stronger theoretical support and broader empirical validation.
