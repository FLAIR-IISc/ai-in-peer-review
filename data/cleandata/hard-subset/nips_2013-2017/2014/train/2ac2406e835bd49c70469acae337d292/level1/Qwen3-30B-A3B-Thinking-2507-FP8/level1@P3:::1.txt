

# Peer Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the Method of Moments (MoM), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in traditional EM-based learning. The authors make the key insight that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. They then leverage spectral properties of the global transition matrix to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM-based learning, which prevents direct recovery of individual HMM parameters. This builds upon Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning for standard HMMs [1,2,3], extending it to the more complex MHMM setting where EM is computationally prohibitive for large datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Insight and Technical Contribution:** The core contribution—viewing MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix and using spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity—is genuinely novel. The authors effectively bridge the gap between spectral learning for standard HMMs and MHMMs, a problem previously considered intractable with MoM approaches. The spectral longevity metric for cluster identification (Definition 3) is particularly elegant and well-motivated.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear theoretical framework connecting MHMM to HMM with block-diagonal structure (Lemma 1), establishes spectral properties of the global transition matrix (Lemma 2, Corollary 1), and develops a principled approach to permutation resolution. The connection to existing spectral learning work [1,2,3] is well-established, with appropriate citations.

3. **Compelling Experimental Results:** The experiments demonstrate clear computational advantages over EM (Figure 4) while maintaining comparable accuracy. The real data experiment (Table 1) shows practical utility as an initialization scheme for EM, which is valuable in practice. The synthetic experiments (Figure 3) effectively demonstrate robustness to sequence length.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and a straightforward algorithm (Algorithm 1). The theoretical concepts are presented accessibly, with good use of figures to illustrate key concepts (Figure 1, 2).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Gaps:** While the main ideas are compelling, some theoretical claims remain as conjectures (Conjecture 1) without formal proof. The justification for the error bound relies on reusing results from another model (a mixture model), which isn't fully justified. A more rigorous theoretical analysis of the noise effects on the spectral properties would strengthen the paper.

2. **Practical Constraints:** The paper notes that "MK < L" is required for MoM application, which is a significant limitation. The real data experiment circumvents this by using polynomial feature transformation, but this may not be generally applicable. The paper doesn't thoroughly address how this constraint affects model flexibility or whether it can be relaxed.

3. **Comparison with Alternatives:** While EM is compared to the proposed method, the paper doesn't adequately compare with other recent approaches to MHMM learning, such as those using tensor decomposition [2] or variational methods. This weakens the significance claim about the approach's novelty.

4. **Limited Discussion of Failure Modes:** The paper demonstrates success on synthetic and real data, but doesn't explore cases where the spectral approach fails (e.g., when the noise condition in Lemma 3 is violated). Understanding these limitations would be valuable for practitioners.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experiments. However, the theoretical justification for the error bound (Conjecture 1) is insufficiently rigorous, and the noise analysis could be strengthened.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear notation and logical flow. The algorithm is presented concisely and accessibly.

**Originality:** The core insight of viewing MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal structure and using spectral properties to resolve permutation ambiguity is novel. While spectral learning for HMMs is established [1,2,3], extending it to MHMMs represents a meaningful advance. The paper clearly differentiates from previous work by addressing the permutation ambiguity problem, which has been a barrier to applying MoM to MHMMs.

**Significance:** The computational advantages over EM are substantial, especially for large-scale sequence clustering tasks. The ability to use this approach as an initialization for EM (Table 1) is practically valuable. The method opens the door to applying spectral learning to other hierarchical latent variable models, as noted in the conclusion. However, the constraint "MK < L" limits applicability to certain problem domains.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a strong contribution that makes a significant technical advance in spectral learning for latent variable models. While some theoretical aspects could be strengthened, the paper's core insight is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. The computational advantages over EM are substantial and likely to be valuable to practitioners dealing with large-scale sequence data. The paper addresses a real problem in the field and provides a principled solution that builds upon established spectral learning techniques while extending them to a new domain.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Novel insight into MHMM structure, clear theoretical foundation, strong empirical results demonstrating computational advantages, well-written and accessible presentation.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Theoretical justification for error bound is somewhat weak, constraint "MK < L" limits applicability, limited comparison with alternative approaches.
