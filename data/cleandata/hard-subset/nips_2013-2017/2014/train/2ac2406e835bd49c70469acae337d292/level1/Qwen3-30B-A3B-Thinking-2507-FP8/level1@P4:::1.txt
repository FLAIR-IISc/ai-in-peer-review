

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using the Method of Moments (MoM), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in traditional EM-based learning. The authors observe that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling application of existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. The key contribution is resolving the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM-based estimation through spectral analysis of the global transition matrix, which allows recovery of individual HMM parameters. The approach is validated on synthetic and real datasets, with experiments demonstrating comparable accuracy to EM but significantly improved computational efficiency.

**Quality**

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical foundation (Lemmas 1-3, Conjecture 1) is well-motivated and correctly establishes the equivalence between MHMMs and structured HMMs, as well as the spectral properties enabling depermutation. The authors carefully address the challenge of permutation ambiguity in the presence of estimation noise, providing a clear algorithmic solution. The experimental section is comprehensive, with well-designed synthetic experiments showing the algorithm's robustness to noise and data size, and a real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories demonstrating practical utility. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the need for MK < L for MoM application) and provides appropriate theoretical justifications for the proposed spectral conditions. The experimental results are well-supported by clear figures and tables, with appropriate statistical evaluation.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors provide a thorough background on HMMs and MHMMs before introducing their method, making the paper accessible to readers unfamiliar with spectral learning for latent variable models. The mathematical notation is consistently used and well-explained, with careful attention to detail in the transition from index notation to matrix notation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and follows logically from the theoretical development. The figures (particularly Figure 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the spectral properties and depermutation process. The writing is concise and technical without being overly dense, making it suitable for the NIPS audience.

**Originality**

This work makes a significant original contribution by extending spectral learning techniques to MHMMs, a model that has previously been challenging to learn efficiently. While spectral learning for standard HMMs has been explored (Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012; Hsu et al., JCSS 2012), the paper's key innovation is the resolution of permutation ambiguity in the MHMM context through spectral analysis of the global transition matrix. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work (e.g., the EM-based MHMM learning in Smyth, NIPS 1997), and the paper's novelty is well-justified. The spectral conditions for identifying the number of clusters (Definition 3, Lemma 3) represent a novel contribution to the spectral learning literature. The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, including the foundational spectral learning papers by Anandkumar et al.

**Significance**

The paper addresses a meaningful computational challenge in sequence clustering, where EM-based MHMM learning becomes prohibitive for large datasets due to its per-sequence forward-backward computations. The proposed spectral approach offers a computationally efficient alternative with similar accuracy, as demonstrated in experiments. The real-world application to handwritten character trajectories shows practical utility, and the finding that spectral learning serves as an effective initialization for EM is particularly valuable for practitioners. The computational advantage is substantial (Figure 4 shows orders-of-magnitude speedup), making this work potentially impactful for large-scale sequence analysis applications. The method could be extended to other hierarchical latent variable models as suggested in the conclusion.

**Strengths**
- Clear theoretical foundation addressing a fundamental challenge (permutation ambiguity)
- Novel application of spectral learning to MHMMs with rigorous analysis
- Comprehensive experimental validation on synthetic and real data
- Practical computational advantages over EM with comparable accuracy
- Well-structured and clearly written paper

**Weaknesses**
- The theoretical analysis relies on Conjecture 1 which, while plausible, lacks a formal proof. A brief discussion of the conditions under which this conjecture holds would strengthen the paper.
- The requirement that MK < L (for the MoM application) is a significant constraint that may limit applicability to some real-world problems. The authors could better discuss how this constraint affects practical implementation.
- The real data experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation to satisfy MK < L, which might alter the underlying data structure; a more thorough discussion of this preprocessing step would be valuable.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance**

*Pro:*
- Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in MHMM learning with a theoretically sound approach
- Novel application of spectral learning to MHMMs with clear resolution of permutation ambiguity
- Demonstrates substantial computational advantages over EM with comparable accuracy
- Well-written with clear theoretical and experimental sections

*Con:*
- Conjecture 1 lacks formal proof, though it's plausible
- The MK < L constraint may limit applicability to certain datasets
- Real data experiment relies on data transformation that might affect results

**Overall Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the spectral learning literature and provides a practical solution to an important computational problem in sequence clustering. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the approach is clearly presented. The minor weaknesses noted (conjecture without proof, constraint on MK < L) do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The work is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in learning complex latent variable models and would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
