Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments, MoM) algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each of size M, is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal and whose emission matrix concatenates the component emission matrices. The authors apply an existing spectral HMM learner (e.g., Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012) to estimate the global HMM parameters up to a permutation of the MK latent states, and then propose a de-permutation procedure based on spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the block-diagonal transition matrix produce K rank-1 blocks vk 1^T, allowing recovery of the state-to-cluster mapping by clustering columns; in the noisy case, they propose selecting the top-K “global” eigenvalues (closest to 1), reconstructing a rank-K approximation of A, and clustering its columns. They also propose a heuristic, “spectral longevity,” to infer K by monitoring how long K largest eigenvalues dominate when repeatedly exponentiating eigenvalues. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI pen-trajectory dataset demonstrate that the approach can de-permute reliably with enough data, can be faster than EM, and can serve as a good initializer for EM.

This work builds on spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models (Hsu et al., Anandkumar et al.) and on MHMM clustering (Smyth 1997). Related spectral lines (not cited) include predictive state representations and Hilbert space embeddings for HMMs (e.g., Siddiqi/Boots/Gordon, Song et al.), which could be relevant for continuous emissions and conditions for identifiability.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction of MHMMs to an HMM with block-diagonal A is correct and useful. The permutation ambiguity in spectral recovery is well-known; the proposed use of the spectrum of A to re-group states is intuitive and, in the noiseless case, correct. However, the core guarantees in the noisy case are limited. Conjecture 1 (that the top-K global eigenvalues remain separable and the rank-K reconstruction is close to the ideal) and Lemma 3’s selection criterion for K rely on conditions that are plausible but unproven here; the justification for sample complexity borrows a bound from a different model. No finite-sample or perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) is provided to quantify when the “global” eigenvalues near 1 remain dominant over “local” eigenvalues under estimation noise. The K-estimation “longevity” heuristic is ad hoc; its robustness to close spectral gaps is not analyzed.
- Experimental support: Synthetic experiments are small-scale and primarily illustrate the de-permutation recovery vs. sequence length T. The EM comparison explores a grid of N and T but remains modest (up to 100 sequences/cluster and T=200). The real-data study is on two-class pairings of handwritten characters and requires lifting L from 3 to 10 via polynomial features to satisfy MK<L; this changes the generative assumptions and could bias comparisons. There is no evaluation on diverse emission families (Poisson, multinomial) which the method claims to cover, nor larger-scale timing results.
- Completeness: Important details are missing. The paper uses a black-box “HMM Method of Moments” call but does not specify the exact moments, multi-view construction, or required rank assumptions (e.g., O full column rank). The constraint MK<L is stated, but the implications and alternatives (e.g., higher-order tensors, random projections) are not discussed. Recovery of π and ν is not clearly described—Algorithm 1 outputs only O and A, although ν̄ contains the mixture proportions (πk = sum of entries in block-k of ν̄, νk then normalized).

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable, with a clear statement of the MHMM-to-HMM reduction and an intuitive picture of the de-permutation idea. Some notational overloading and elided steps could be improved (e.g., consistent use of bars/perturbations, clear separation between estimated vs. true quantities). Assumptions on Ak (ergodic, aperiodic, simple eigenvalue at 1) should be stated explicitly. The K-estimation procedure would benefit from a more precise algorithmic description and sensitivity discussion.

Originality:
Viewing MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is known, but using the spectrum of the estimated transition matrix to de-permute the MK states and to estimate K is, to my knowledge, novel in this specific context. However, the idea is conceptually close to spectral clustering/graph partitioning on transition graphs and to low-rank eigenspace clustering; positioning relative to these literatures would strengthen the contribution.

Significance:
If robust, the approach could make MHMM training more scalable and provide a useful initializer for EM. Practical impact is limited by the MK<L requirement, the sensitivity to eigenvalue separation, and the current lack of guarantees. Demonstrations on larger, real applications (e.g., speech, user behavior logs) and for other emission models would increase significance.

Strengths:
- Simple, compelling reduction and de-permutation idea leveraging block structure.
- Potential computational advantages; decouples costly per-iteration forward-backward from dataset size.
- Works as a strong initializer for EM in the real-data experiment.
- Clear identification of the permutation issue in applying spectral HMM learning to MHMMs.

Weaknesses:
- Key guarantees are conjectural; no perturbation analysis for the noisy case.
- K-estimation heuristic lacks theoretical grounding and sensitivity analysis.
- Requires MK<L; workaround via feature lifting changes the generative model.
- Limited empirical scope (small scale, one real dataset, only Gaussian emissions).
- Missing details on moment construction and recovery of π, ν.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a perturbation analysis using standard eigenvector/eigenspace perturbation tools to formalize Conjecture 1 and the conditions under which “global” eigenvalues remain top-K.
- Compare the proposed de-permutation with alternative block-structure recovery methods (e.g., spectral clustering on |A| or thresholded A, community detection).
- Elaborate the MoM step: which moments, how many lags, rank conditions, and computational complexity; discuss ways to relax MK<L (higher-order tensors, multiview projections).
- Report recovery of π and ν explicitly, and include sequence clustering accuracy driven by learned π, ν, A, O.
- Evaluate on additional datasets and emission families; include larger-scale timing results and ablations of the K-estimation heuristic.
- Cite related spectral work for continuous emissions and PSRs (e.g., Siddiqi/Boots/Gordon; Song et al.).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a concrete, underexplored gap: spectral learning for MHMMs with a practical de-permutation solution.
- Conceptually simple, potentially useful, and empirically promising as an EM initializer with speed advantages.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees in the noisy case; central claims hinge on unproven conjectures.
- Limited and small-scale empirical validation; reliance on MK<L reduces practical applicability.
- Incomplete methodological details and evaluation breadth.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially useful idea, but it needs stronger theory and broader empirical validation to meet NIPS standards. I lean toward rejection in its current form, with encouragement to strengthen the analysis and experiments. Reviewer confidence: medium.
