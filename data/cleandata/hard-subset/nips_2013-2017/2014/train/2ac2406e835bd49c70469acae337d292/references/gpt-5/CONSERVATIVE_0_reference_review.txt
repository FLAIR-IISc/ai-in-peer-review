Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to view an MHMM as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, estimate global HMM parameters via existing spectral/HMM MoM methods (which return parameters up to a permutation), and then “de-permute” to recover per-component HMMs. The de-permutation is done by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the block-diagonal transition matrix reveal K clusters; in the noisy case, the authors propose a low-rank reconstruction using the top-K eigenvalues and cluster its columns. They also introduce a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwritten trajectory dataset indicate speed advantages over EM and suggest the spectral approach can serve as an initialization for EM.

Strengths
- Recognizes a natural structural equivalence (MHMM ≡ HMM with block-diagonal transition) and attempts to leverage established HMM spectral learning to avoid costly EM over many sequences.
- The de-permutation problem is a concrete, practical hurdle when using MoM; proposing to resolve it via spectral structure of the transition operator is a reasonable direction.
- The empirical comparison suggests meaningful computational savings and some utility as an EM initializer.
- The paper is generally readable and organized, and the overall algorithm is clearly described.

Weaknesses
- Technical correctness and rigor:
  - The derivation conflates emission probabilities with conditional means. Eq. (1) uses diag(O(xt)) while later O(:,u) is defined as E[xt|rt=u] for Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial. For continuous emissions, p(xt|rt) cannot be represented via diag of means; this is not a valid likelihood expression. Spectral methods for continuous observations require carefully defined feature mappings and moment identities, which are not derived here.
  - The central noisy-case justification is left as Conjecture 1. There is no finite-sample perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) establishing when the top-K “global” eigenvalues are separated from “local” ones, nor how large N,T must be. This is critical given the paper’s main contribution is the de-permutation under noise.
  - The “spectral longevity” criterion for K is ad hoc and its performance is supported only by a lemma that itself assumes the desired top-K separation and offers no finite-sample bounds or robustness analysis.
  - Assumptions required for the spectral arguments (ergodicity/aperiodicity; diagonalizability of Ak; unique unit eigenvalue per component; separation between global and local eigenvalues) are only partially stated and not checked in experiments. Practical failure modes (periodicity, near-defective Ak, nearly equal stationary distributions) are not discussed.
- Novelty:
  - The block-diagonal equivalence is straightforward and likely known; the novelty rests on the specific de-permutation via eigen-structure and the K estimator. Given the limited theory, it is unclear how much this advances prior spectral/tensor approaches for mixtures of Markov chains/HMMs (related literature is not adequately surveyed).
- Empirical validation:
  - Experimental scope is limited (small K,M; one real dataset). No stress tests on harder regimes: varying spectral gaps, similar stationary distributions across components, heterogeneous M across components, or violations of assumptions.
  - No evaluation of parameter recovery error or block recovery accuracy across noise/sample sizes; focus is mostly on clustering accuracy and a qualitative block-diagonal visualization.
  - The K-estimation heuristic is not rigorously evaluated or compared to standard alternatives (BIC, likelihood-ratio tests, eigen-gap thresholds).
  - The MoM requirement MK < L is handled on real data by polynomial feature expansion. This changes the observation model; it is unclear whether the resulting moments are consistent with the assumed model, and whether EM baselines were given equivalent feature representations. This raises fairness and applicability concerns.
- Reproducibility and clarity:
  - Key details of the HMM MoM used (for continuous data, moment definitions, whitening steps, rank selection) are omitted. Without explicit moment constructions and conditions, reproduction is difficult.
  - Several statements are imprecise (e.g., “eigendecomposition is invariant under permutation” — eigenvalues are invariant, eigenvectors transform by P; this matters for how columns are clustered).
  - Notation around O(xt) is inconsistent with the later definition of O(:,u) as means.

Relation to prior work
The paper cites classic spectral HMM work (Hsu et al.) and tensor methods for latent variable models but does not adequately position itself relative to spectral/tensor methods for mixtures of Markov chains/HMMs or block-structure recovery in Markov operators. A deeper comparison (both conceptual and empirical) is needed.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a practical bottleneck (EM scaling with many sequences).
- Simple idea that could be useful as an initializer.
- Some empirical evidence of speed gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical flaws (likelihood/moment mismatch for continuous emissions) and lack of rigorous guarantees for the central de-permutation step.
- Heuristic K estimation with insufficient theoretical or empirical support.
- Limited, non-stress-tested experiments; unclear robustness and applicability.
- Incomplete related work coverage.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the modeling/moment derivations for continuous observations; explicitly define feature maps and moment equations compatible with spectral HMM, and ensure equations do not misuse means as probabilities.
- Provide a perturbation analysis establishing conditions under which the top-K “global” eigenvalues are identifiable, with finite-sample bounds in terms of N,T, spectral gaps, and noise; use standard eigenvector perturbation tools.
- Evaluate the de-permutation across varied spectral gaps, similar stationary distributions, larger K,M, and non-ergodic/periodic cases; report parameter/block recovery error and success rates.
- Compare K estimation to BIC, cross-validated likelihood, and simple eigen-gap criteria; report accuracy and stability.
- Clarify assumptions (ergodicity, diagonalizability), computational complexity, and sensitivity to MK and L; justify the feature expansion on real data and ensure fair baselines.

Overall recommendation: Reject (in current form). The paper raises an interesting and potentially useful direction, but the main contribution hinges on unproven conjectures and contains technical inconsistencies for continuous observations. Stronger theoretical guarantees and more rigorous empirical validation are needed. Confidence: high.
