This paper proposes a spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard approach for learning MHMMs is the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be computationally expensive and susceptible to local optima. The authors draw upon the recent success of Method of Moments (MoM) or spectral techniques for learning single latent variable models, such as the work of Hsu et al. (2012) and Anandkumar et al. (2012), which offer computational advantages and consistency guarantees.

The core idea of the proposed method is to first establish an equivalence between an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) and a single, larger HMM with MK hidden states. The key structural insight is that the transition matrix of this equivalent HMM is block-diagonal, where each block corresponds to the transition matrix of one of the component HMMs. Leveraging this equivalence, the authors apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of the larger model. However, this learning procedure only recovers parameters up to a permutation of the MK hidden states, which mixes states from different component HMMs and prevents direct recovery of the individual model parameters.

The main contribution of the paper is a novel "de-permutation" algorithm to resolve this ambiguity. The authors show that in the ideal, noiseless case, the block-diagonal structure of the global transition matrix implies that it has K eigenvalues equal to 1. By raising the estimated transition matrix to a high power, its columns cluster into K distinct groups, revealing the block structure and allowing for the recovery of the correct permutation. To handle estimation noise, which typically collapses the K unit eigenvalues to a single one, the authors propose using a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix based on its top K eigenvectors. Clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix serves to identify the de-permutation mapping. The paper also introduces a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" of eigenvalues to estimate the number of mixture components, K, from data.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper presents a novel and non-trivial extension of spectral learning to the MHMM setting. While spectral learning for a single HMM is established, tackling the mixture case and its associated parameter-mixing problem is a new contribution. The proposed de-permutation strategy, which leverages the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix, is a clever and well-motivated solution to the core challenge.

2.  **Significance and Potential Impact:** The work addresses a significant practical problem: the high computational cost of training MHMMs on large sequence datasets using EM. The proposed spectral method is non-iterative (after moment computation) and is demonstrated to be substantially faster than EM. This offers a valuable, scalable alternative for sequence clustering tasks. Furthermore, the experiments show its utility as a high-quality initialization strategy for EM, which can help EM find better solutions faster. This dual use-case increases the work's potential impact.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, establishes the theoretical equivalence between an MHMM and a structured HMM, identifies the resulting permutation ambiguity, and details the proposed spectral solution. The inclusion of figures to visualize the effect of the de-permutation algorithm on the transition matrix is particularly effective.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Technical Rigor:** Some of the theoretical underpinnings are not fully formalized. Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank matrix reconstruction, is presented without a formal proof, instead relying on an argument that a similar bound from Anandkumar et al. (2012) for a different model should apply. While plausible, a more rigorous derivation specific to the MHMM structure would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

2.  **Heuristic Model Selection:** The proposed method for estimating the number of components K, based on "spectral longevity," is an interesting heuristic but lacks a thorough analysis. The performance of this method likely depends on the spectral gap between the K-th and (K+1)-th eigenvalues, and its robustness to the choice of the threshold `gamma` and the range of exponents `e` is not explored. A sensitivity analysis or a more detailed justification of this method would be beneficial.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate the method's promise but could be more comprehensive. The synthetic experiments could further explore challenging scenarios, such as when the component HMMs are very similar (leading to a small spectral gap), to test the limits of the de-permutation and model selection algorithms. The real-data evaluation is conducted on a single dataset with K=2, which is a limited validation of the approach's effectiveness on complex, real-world problems.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem (scalable learning for MHMMs) with a novel and elegant approach.
*   The core technical idea of using spectral properties to solve the permutation problem is a significant contribution.
*   The proposed algorithm is computationally much more efficient than the standard EM approach.
*   The method provides a principled and effective initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution in its own right.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Key theoretical results are presented as conjectures with informal justifications rather than formal proofs.
*   The method for estimating the number of components K is heuristic and its robustness is not well-established.
*   The empirical validation, particularly on real data, is somewhat limited and could be expanded to more diverse and challenging problems.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel, computationally efficient spectral algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models. Its core contribution—a method for resolving the state permutation ambiguity inherent in applying spectral methods to this model class—is both clever and significant. Despite some of the theoretical claims being presented without full rigor and the experimental section being somewhat limited, the strengths in originality and practical significance are substantial. The work opens a promising new direction for learning mixture models of sequences.
