## NIPS Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

**Paper Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning parameters for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), a computationally expensive task traditionally addressed with the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors propose a novel approach based on the Method of Moments (MoM), leveraging the observation that an MHMM can be represented as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. They utilize a spectral learning algorithm for HMMs to estimate the global parameters, then address the inherent permutation ambiguity through spectral properties of the transition matrix, aiming to recover the individual HMM parameters and even estimate the number of HMMs. The work provides theoretical justification and empirical validation demonstrating its computational efficiency and comparable performance to EM.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the recent surge in popularity of MoM-based algorithms for latent variable models [1, 2, 3], particularly the spectral learning of HMMs [3]. It distinguishes itself by extending these methods to the more complex MHMM setting.  The authors appropriately cite relevant work on MHMMs [4] and their applications [5, 6, 7], establishing the context for their contribution. They also draw connections to spectral decomposition techniques used in related areas like tensor decompositions [2] and mixture model estimation [8].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** Applying MoM and spectral learning to MHMMs is a novel and promising direction, offering a potential alternative to the computationally intensive EM algorithm.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear mathematical foundation for its approach, starting with the key observation about the block-diagonal transition matrix. The lemmas and conjectures, while some are presented as such, offer a solid theoretical underpinning.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The core benefit of the MoM approach – independence of computation time from dataset size – is a significant advantage, especially for large-scale applications.  The experiments support this claim.
* **Addressing Permutation Ambiguity:**  The proposed method for resolving the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM-based estimation is a crucial contribution and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying problem.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, including a real-world application to handwritten character trajectory data, provide evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The comparison to EM, both with random and MoM initialization, is valuable.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.  The model definitions are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conjectures:** While the conjectures are reasonable, they lack rigorous proofs. The reliance on these conjectures weakens the overall theoretical grounding.  More thorough analysis and perhaps a reduction in the scope of claims would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity to Noise:** The performance of the depermutation step appears highly sensitive to noise, as indicated by the discussion around Lemma 3 and the conditions for estimating K. This needs further exploration and potentially more robust techniques to handle noisy estimates.
* **Limited Experimentation:** While the experiments are a good starting point, the evaluation could be more comprehensive.  Specifically:
    * More diverse datasets should be considered.
    * A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different parameters (e.g., sequence length, number of clusters) on performance would be valuable.
    * The comparison to state-of-the-art MHMM learning methods beyond EM could be included.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  The algorithm, particularly the depermutation step involving spectral analysis, might be complex to implement and tune in practice. The paper doesn't fully address the practical considerations of implementation.
* **Polynomial Feature Transformation:** The use of a polynomial feature transformation in the real-data experiment feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further justification. It's not immediately clear why a cubic polynomial is chosen.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Offers a genuinely novel approach to MHMM learning with potential for significant computational advantages.
* Provides a solid theoretical foundation (despite some conjectures).
* Demonstrates promising empirical results, showing comparable performance to EM.
* Addresses a key challenge in MoM estimation – the permutation ambiguity.

**Con:**

* Relies on unproven conjectures.
* Performance is sensitive to noise.
* Experimentation is somewhat limited.
* Implementation complexity is potentially high.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of latent variable model learning. While the reliance on conjectures and sensitivity to noise are concerns, the novelty of the approach, theoretical justification, and promising experimental results outweigh these weaknesses.  The work has the potential to significantly impact the field, particularly for large-scale applications where computational efficiency is critical.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend accepting this paper with a request for the authors to:

1.  Strengthen the theoretical analysis, ideally by providing proofs for the key conjectures or narrowing their scope.
2.  Investigate the robustness of the depermutation step to noise and explore techniques for mitigating its impact.
3.  Expand the experimental evaluation with more diverse datasets and a more comprehensive ablation study.
4.  Provide more discussion on the practical considerations of implementing the algorithm.
