Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM over K components with M hidden states each is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral/MoM algorithms for HMMs to estimate the “global” HMM parameters up to a permutation ambiguity. They then propose a de-permutation procedure based on spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, repeated powers of the transition matrix reveal K blocks corresponding to stationary distributions of each component; in the noisy/finite-sample case, they construct a low-rank reconstruction from the top-K eigenvalues and cluster its columns to recover component assignments. They also introduce a heuristic “spectral longevity” criterion to estimate K from the spectrum.

This work relates to spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models (e.g., Hsu et al., Anandkumar et al.), and to EM-based learning for MHMMs (e.g., Smyth). The idea that an MHMM can be represented as an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is standard; the paper’s novelty lies in exploiting this structure to undo the permutation and to infer K using spectral cues. The experiments compare against EM on synthetic data and on a UCI character trajectory dataset, where the spectral method also serves as an EM initializer.

Quality
- Correctness of reduction and spectral intuition: The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is correct, and Lemma 2 (each ergodic component contributes one eigenvalue at 1) follows from standard Markov chain theory under irreducibility/aperiodicity. The noiseless de-permutation via clustering columns of Ā∞ is sound.
- Assumptions and identifiability: The approach implicitly requires Ō to be full column rank and the observation dimension L ≥ MK for the HMM spectral subroutine to be identifiable. This is a strong and often unmet condition in HMM applications. The paper acknowledges MK < L and uses a cubic feature map on real data to satisfy it, but provides no analysis of how such feature mappings affect identifiability or estimation error.
- Noisy case theory: The main finite-sample step rests on Conjecture 1 and a strong separation condition |λG_K| > max_k |λL_2,k| (i.e., the K global eigenvalues near 1 dominate all subdominant local eigenvalues). This can fail when components mix slowly or have near-unit subdominant eigenvalues. The error bound derivation is informal and borrows rates without a full argument for the MHMM setting. A principled perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) for subspace recovery and clustering consistency is missing.
- Estimating K: The “spectral longevity” criterion is intuitive but ad hoc; it is not compared with standard eigengap heuristics, information criteria, or stability-based methods, nor is it accompanied by finite-sample guarantees beyond a lemma conditioned on spectral gaps.
- Experimental support: Synthetic experiments illustrate the de-permutation as data grows and show speed advantages over EM, but quantitative details (absolute accuracies, error bars, runtime breakdowns) are limited. The real-data experiment requires a feature expansion to meet MK < L; the empirical effect of that choice is not assessed. Reproducibility is hindered by lack of detail on the specific HMM MoM routine used for continuous emissions and the exact moment construction.

Clarity
The paper’s core idea and the block-diagonal reduction are clearly explained, and the noiseless case is well illustrated. However:
- The MoM subroutine is treated as a black box; for continuous outputs (Gaussian/Poisson), the exact moment tensors/matrices and conditions are not specified, and related prior work on continuous-output spectral HMMs is not adequately cited.
- The definitions around noisy eigenvalues and the construction of Ar could be tightened, with clearer statements of assumptions (ergodicity, aperiodicity, eigenvalue multiplicity).
- Several references lack complete bibliographic details, and figures/tables are referenced but not fully described quantitatively.

Originality
Treating an MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is known. The contribution is the de-permutation strategy using spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix and the heuristic for estimating K. Within the MoM/spectral learning literature, this appears to be a modest, application-driven extension. Relevant related work on spectral clustering of Markov chains into metastable sets (e.g., PCCA methods) and on identifiability of HMM mixtures is not discussed and should be cited for context.

Significance
If robust, the approach could provide a fast alternative to EM for clustering large collections of sequences, and a practical EM initializer. However, its applicability is limited by the L ≥ MK requirement and by unproven conditions on spectral separation. The current empirical evaluation is not extensive enough to demonstrate consistent gains across realistic regimes (varying gaps, unequal component sizes, different M per component, misspecified L). As presented, the contribution is promising but preliminary.

Pros
- Simple insight connecting MHMM learning to HMM spectral methods.
- Computationally attractive once moments are computed; observed speedups over EM.
- Practical de-permutation mechanism; useful as EM initialization.

Cons
- Core finite-sample argument is conjectural and hinges on strong, unverified spectral separation.
- Requires L ≥ MK and full-rank emissions; limited discussion of this constraint and of feature mappings.
- Heuristic K selection; no comparison to standard alternatives.
- Limited and partially specified experiments; reproducibility concerns.
- Incomplete related work and references.

Questions for the authors
- Which exact spectral HMM algorithm is used for continuous emissions, and what moments are computed? Under what conditions is Ō identifiable for Gaussian/Poisson?
- How sensitive is de-permutation to near-degenerate spectra (e.g., components with slow mixing)? Can you provide perturbation bounds or empirical stress tests?
- Can the method handle components with different numbers of states, or unknown M?
- How does performance vary with L < MK using random projections or richer features? Any guarantees?
- Please report quantitative runtimes and accuracies with error bars, and compare K-estimation to eigengap/BIC.

Suggestions
- Provide a perturbation analysis for the low-rank reconstruction and the column clustering step, yielding sample complexity in terms of eigengaps and mixing times.
- Compare K-estimation methods and add ablations varying spectral gaps and component imbalance.
- Clarify and justify the continuous-emission moment construction; add citations to continuous-output spectral HMM literature and metastability/PCCA work.
- Expand experiments and provide code for reproducibility.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is appealing and potentially useful, but theoretical guarantees are incomplete and experimental validation is limited. Strengthening the analysis and evaluation would substantially improve the contribution.
