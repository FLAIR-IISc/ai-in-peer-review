

# Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

This paper presents a novel method for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using spectral learning techniques. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then leverage spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the individual HMM parameters from the permuted estimates obtained through standard spectral learning. This represents a significant conceptual advance over traditional EM-based approaches for MHMMs, which suffer from high computational complexity with large datasets.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes the theoretical equivalence between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices (Lemma 1), which forms the foundation for the proposed approach. Second, it develops a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral learning, using the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to estimate a de-permutation mapping. Third, it specifies conditions under which the number of clusters K can be recovered from the data. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) systematically addresses these challenges, and the experiments demonstrate its computational advantages over EM.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While spectral learning for HMMs has been extensively studied (Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2009), extending it to MHMMs in this manner represents a significant conceptual leap. The authors successfully address a fundamental challenge in MHMM learning—the permutation ambiguity—through a clever application of spectral properties. This approach is conceptually distinct from previous work on MHMMs (Smyth, 1997; Qi et al., 2005) and opens up new possibilities for learning more complex hierarchical latent variable models.

The paper demonstrates reasonable experimental validation. The synthetic experiments (Section 4.1-4.2) show that the method correctly recovers the block-diagonal structure and achieves comparable accuracy to EM with significantly lower computational cost. The real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories (Section 4.3) provides a meaningful application, showing that the spectral approach can serve as an effective initialization for EM. The computational advantage is particularly compelling—while EM requires forward-backward passes for each sequence in every iteration, the spectral method's computational complexity is independent of the number of sequences.

The paper has some minor weaknesses. The theoretical analysis contains conjectures (Conjecture 1) that would benefit from more rigorous justification, particularly regarding the error bounds. While the authors cite relevant work on spectral learning for HMMs, they could better distinguish their approach from related work on learning mixtures of models. The experimental section would be strengthened by comparison to more recent spectral learning methods for HMMs, though the core contribution is not dependent on these comparisons.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with logical progression from theoretical foundations to algorithmic development to experimental validation. The figures (Figure 1, 2, 3) effectively illustrate the key concepts, particularly the block-diagonal structure recovery.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a computationally challenging problem (learning MHMMs) with a fundamentally new approach that has clear practical implications. While the accuracy doesn't surpass EM in all cases, the substantial computational advantage makes this method highly valuable for large-scale sequence clustering applications. The paper also opens the door to applying similar spectral techniques to other hierarchical latent variable models.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I find this paper highly compelling. The core insight—viewing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal structure and using spectral properties to recover individual models—is both elegant and novel. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the conceptual contribution outweighs these minor limitations. The computational advantages over EM are substantial and well-demonstrated, making this a significant contribution to the field.

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the NIPS community, offering a new direction for learning complex latent variable models. The novelty of the approach, combined with its practical computational advantages, makes it a valuable addition to the literature on spectral learning and latent variable models.

**Pro acceptance:**
- Novel conceptual insight (MHMM as HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix)
- Solves the fundamental permutation ambiguity problem in MHMM learning
- Substantial computational advantages over EM
- Clear theoretical foundation with meaningful experimental validation
- Opens new research directions for hierarchical latent variable models

**Con acceptance:**
- Some theoretical results presented as conjectures rather than proofs
- Limited comparison to more recent spectral learning methods for HMMs
- The accuracy advantage over EM is limited (though computational advantage is significant)

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper presents a novel and conceptually important contribution to the field of latent variable models. The core idea is bold and well-motivated, and the computational advantages are substantial. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the novelty and significance of the approach outweigh these minor limitations. This work represents a meaningful advance in the learning of complex sequence models.
