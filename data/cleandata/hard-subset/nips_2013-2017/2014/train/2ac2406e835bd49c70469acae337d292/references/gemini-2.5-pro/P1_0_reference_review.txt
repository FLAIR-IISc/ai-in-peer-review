### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel, non-iterative algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the method of moments (MoM). The key insight is that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, can be represented as a single, larger HMM with MK hidden states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this larger model. However, this estimation is only unique up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main contribution of the paper is a spectral "de-permutation" algorithm that recovers the block structure of the transition matrix by analyzing its eigen-spectrum. This allows for the recovery of the individual HMM component parameters. Furthermore, the paper introduces a criterion based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of HMM components (K) directly from the data. The proposed method is shown to be significantly faster than the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and provides a strong initialization for EM, as demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data.

This work builds upon the recent line of research on spectral learning and tensor methods for latent variable models (e.g., Anandkumar et al. [1, 2], Hsu et al. [3]), extending these techniques to the more complex hierarchical structure of MHMMs. While EM is the standard for MHMMs (Smyth [4]), this paper addresses its primary weaknesses: slow convergence on large datasets and sensitivity to initialization.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem: the efficient learning of MHMMs. The core idea of representing an MHMM as a large HMM with a block-diagonal structure is elegant, and the subsequent development of a de-permutation strategy is a significant and non-trivial contribution. This extends the applicability of spectral learning methods to a new class of hierarchical models and offers a principled, computationally efficient alternative to EM. The ability to also estimate the number of clusters, K, is a valuable addition.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the proposed method is well-motivated. Lemma 1, establishing the equivalence between an MHMM and a structured HMM, is clear and correct. The analysis of the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix in the noiseless case (Lemma 2, Corollary 1) provides a strong justification for the de-permutation approach. The algorithm for the noisy case, based on a low-rank reconstruction, is a practical and reasonable extension.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed algorithm offers a substantial computational advantage over EM, as its core learning cost is independent of the number of sequences after the initial moment computation. The experiments convincingly demonstrate this speed-up. Furthermore, the real-data experiment highlights a highly practical use case: providing a high-quality, fast initialization for EM, which often leads to better final models than random initialization. This makes the method immediately useful for practitioners.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with clear model definitions, builds the theoretical argument step-by-step from the noiseless to the noisy case, and presents a concise algorithm summary. The figures, particularly Figure 1 illustrating the effect of noise on the exponentiated transition matrix, are effective at conveying the core challenge and the intuition behind the proposed solution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Theoretical Gaps:** The central theoretical result for the noisy case, Conjecture 1, is presented as a conjecture rather than a proven theorem. While the justification provided is plausible and relies on existing results for related models, the lack of a formal proof for this specific setting is a notable weakness. The paper's theoretical guarantees would be significantly stronger if this conjecture were formalized and proven.

2.  **Clarity of the Model Selection Criterion:** The method for estimating K via "spectral longevity" (Definition 3, Lemma 3) is less clear than the rest of the paper. The definition is somewhat complex, and the practical implementation details are sparse. For example, Algorithm 1 mentions exponentiating eigenvalues "in a sufficiently large range," but provides no guidance on how this range should be chosen. The sensitivity to the threshold `γ` is also not discussed. This part of the contribution feels less developed and would be difficult to reproduce without more detail.

3.  **Practical Limitations:** The underlying spectral HMM algorithm requires that the total number of hidden states (MK) be less than the observation dimension (L). The authors acknowledge this and use a feature expansion on the real dataset to satisfy the condition. While transparent, this is a significant practical limitation that restricts the direct applicability of the method to datasets with either low-dimensional state spaces or high-dimensional observations. A more thorough discussion of this limitation and its implications would strengthen the paper.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Regarding Conjecture 1:** The authors state that "further analysis of the spectral learning algorithm is out of the scope of this paper." While understandable, this weakens the paper's core claim. Could the authors provide more intuition on why the bound from [1] for a mixture model is expected to hold here? Is the main difficulty in the analysis due to the block-diagonal structure of the transition matrix?
*   **Regarding Lemma 3 and `Lλ̃K′`:** The proof sketch argues that the longevity is determined by the ratio of spectral gaps. This is an interesting idea. Could a simple numerical example be provided in an appendix to build intuition for how this works? How was the range of `e` chosen for the experiments (Figure 2)?
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The comparison with EM is fair. However, EM was run with four random restarts. Is it possible that with more restarts (e.g., 20 or 50), EM's performance could improve further, potentially closing the gap with the MoM-initialized version? This would help contextualize the quality of the spectral initialization.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis in Lemma 2 assumes each component HMM `Ak` has a single eigenvalue of 1 (i.e., is irreducible and aperiodic). Is this a strict requirement for the algorithm to work in practice, or does it degrade gracefully if some components are reducible?

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, elegant, and computationally efficient method for a well-known problem. The central idea is creative, and the experimental results demonstrate its practical value both as a standalone algorithm and as an initialization strategy for EM. Despite the theoretical weakness of relying on a conjecture and the lack of clarity in the model selection part, the core contribution is significant and likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. The strengths in novelty, practical relevance, and computational benefit outweigh the weaknesses.

**I recommend acceptance.** The authors should be encouraged to address the clarity of the model selection criterion and acknowledge the theoretical gaps more explicitly in the final version.
