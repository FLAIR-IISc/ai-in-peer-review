Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block corresponds to one component HMM. The authors first estimate a global HMM using existing spectral HMM methods [1,2,3], which returns parameters up to a global permutation of states. They then propose to “de-permute” by exploiting spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the transition matrix reveal K clusters corresponding to blocks; in the noisy case, they propose to retain the leading K eigen-components and cluster columns of a low-rank reconstruction. They also propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate the number of components K by examining the decay of sorted eigenvalues under exponentiation. Experiments on synthetic data and a small UCI character trajectory task show speed advantages over EM and that the spectral estimator can serve as an EM initializer.

This work builds on established spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models [1–3] and applies those tools to the MHMM setting via a structural equivalence (block-diagonalization). The permutation issue is well-known for spectral methods; the paper’s contribution is a specific de-permutation heuristic based on eigen-structure and a rule to estimate K.

Strengths
- Clear and simple structural observation (MHMM as HMM with block-diagonal A) with a straightforward derivation.
- Sensible practical goal: avoid repeated forward–backward over many sequences; offer a fast initializer for EM.
- Empirical demonstrations indicate substantial speed gains and that spectral estimates can bootstrap EM on a real dataset.
- The de-permutation idea—clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction of the transition matrix—is intuitive and potentially useful.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Key claims rely on unproven conjectures. The central robustness claim (Conjecture 1) is not established; the bound is borrowed from a different setting and justified informally. No perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) is provided to quantify when de-permutation via clustering succeeds as a function of eigengaps and noise.
- Identifying K via “spectral longevity” is ad hoc (depends on γ, exponent ranges) and lacks theoretical guarantees; sensitivity analyses are not provided.
- Important assumptions are stated but not scrutinized: each Ak has exactly one eigenvalue of magnitude 1 (ergodic, aperiodic chains) and matrices are diagonalizable. The method’s behavior under near-reducible or periodic chains, small inter-block eigengaps, or non-diagonalizable cases is not analyzed.
- The base spectral HMM procedure is underspecified. Spectral HMM learning typically requires stringent conditions (e.g., full-column-rank emissions, sufficient observation dimension, specific moment constructions/whitening). The paper assumes O(:,u)=E[x|r=u] for Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial but does not detail the exact moment estimators used, their sample complexity in the MHMM case, or the effect of feature mappings.
- Claims that “computation time is independent of the amount of data once moments are computed” are misleading in practice; the cost to estimate moments scales with data and can dominate.
- Experiments are limited in scope and rigor:
  - Synthetic: relatively small K and M, few sequences per component, limited exploration of difficult regimes (close components, small eigengaps, varying L vs MK).
  - Real data: only K=2, M=4; a cubic feature expansion is applied to satisfy MK<L, which breaks the generative assumptions and raises questions about identifiability/consistency under transformed observations. No study of the effect of this choice.
  - Baselines: Only EM (with four restarts). No comparison to variational MHMMs or other scalable/regularized EM variants, nor to alternative de-permutation strategies (e.g., matching by stationary distributions or Schur-based clustering).
  - Metrics focus on clustering accuracy; parameter recovery quality and de-permutation error are not reported.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but key algorithmic details are missing:
  - How MK is chosen when K is unknown; what happens when L < MK without ad hoc feature lifting.
  - Exact procedure and hyperparameters for the longevity measure (γ, exponent range), and for k-means on Ar.
  - Computational complexity of each step and memory requirements for moments.
- Some statements are imprecise (e.g., “eigendecomposition is invariant under permutation”: eigenvalues are invariant, eigenvectors permute; in the noisy case, eigenvectors can rotate substantially).

Originality
- The block-diagonal equivalence is standard. The main novelty is the specific de-permutation and K-selection heuristics. This is an incremental extension of established spectral HMM methods rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic framework.
- Related work on mixtures of HMMs and state-space model mixtures could be discussed more comprehensively; if no prior spectral MHMM methods exist, this should be made clear with a broader literature survey.

Significance
- The problem is important and a fast initializer for MHMMs would be valuable. However, without theoretical guarantees for the noisy setting and with limited empirical validation, the current contribution is not yet compelling for broad adoption.
- The need for L ≥ MK (or resorting to feature expansions) limits practicality for many applications.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a formal perturbation analysis for the de-permutation step: conditions on eigengaps, noise level, and sample size under which k-means on Ar recovers blocks with high probability.
- Justify and analyze the “longevity” criterion; compare it to standard methods (e.g., eigengap heuristic on |λ|, information criteria).
- Specify the exact MoM/HMM estimator used (moments, whitening, identifiability conditions) and its sample complexity in the MHMM setting.
- Report sensitivity to γ, exponent range, number of clusters in k-means, and to near-degenerate components.
- Evaluate on larger and more challenging synthetic settings and include additional baselines (variational MHMMs, better EM initializations/regularization).
- Avoid ad hoc feature lifting unless you can justify consistency under the transformed observation model; otherwise, discuss practical strategies when L < MK.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant problem; offers a potentially useful and simple de-permutation idea.
- Clear structural connection between MHMMs and block-diagonal HMMs.
- Demonstrates substantial speed gains; can serve as an EM initializer.

Cons
- Lacks rigorous analysis for the key noisy-case steps; relies on a conjecture.
- Limited experiments and baselines; practical constraints (L ≥ MK) downplayed.
- Heuristic K-estimation without guarantees or thorough evaluation.
- Incomplete algorithmic details hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The direction is promising, but the current paper falls short on theoretical grounding and experimental validation required to endorse a new learning procedure over established methods. Strengthening the analysis and substantially expanding the empirical study would make this more suitable for acceptance.
