### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the method of moments (MoM). The authors position their work as a computationally efficient alternative to the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be prohibitively slow for large sequence datasets. The central idea of the proposed method is to re-frame an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, as a single, larger HMM with a total of MK states. In this equivalent representation, the global transition matrix exhibits a block-diagonal structure, where each block corresponds to the transition matrix of one of the component HMMs.

Leveraging this insight, the authors first apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012; Anandkumar et al., 2012) to learn the parameters of this large, equivalent HMM. This MoM-based estimation is computationally efficient but recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the MK hidden states. This permutation ambiguity mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs, preventing their direct recovery. The main technical contribution of the paper is a method to resolve this ambiguity. By analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix, the authors propose a procedure to find a de-permutation mapping. This involves computing a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix using its top K eigenvectors and then clustering its columns to group the states corresponding to each component HMM. Additionally, the paper proposes a heuristic method based on the "spectral longevity" of eigenvalues to estimate the number of mixture components, K, directly from the data. The method's performance is evaluated on synthetic and real-world data, comparing its speed and accuracy against the EM algorithm.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: (Technical Soundness and completeness)**

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical foundation for the noiseless case is sound. Lemma 1 correctly establishes the equivalence between an MHMM and a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. Lemma 2 and its corollary, which describe the spectral properties of this matrix (K eigenvalues of 1) and the structure of its limiting power, are also correct and provide a strong motivation for the de-permutation algorithm. The overall algorithm is a complete, end-to-end procedure for learning MHMMs.
*   **Weaknesses:** The theoretical analysis for the practical, noisy case is not fully rigorous.
    *   Conjecture 1 provides an error bound for the low-rank matrix reconstruction, but it relies on adapting a result from prior work ([1]) without a formal proof of its applicability to this specific MHMM context. The justification provided is brief and acknowledges that a full analysis is beyond the paper's scope, which weakens the theoretical guarantees.
    *   The method for determining the number of clusters K, presented in Lemma 3, is based on a heuristic "spectral longevity" metric. The provided proof serves more as a justification for this heuristic than a rigorous mathematical proof. Furthermore, the lemma's conditions, particularly `arg maxK′ |λ̃K′|/|λ̃K′+1||λ̃K′−1| = K`, seem to require prior knowledge of K to verify, which is circular. This part of the contribution feels more like a well-motivated engineering choice than a proven result.

**Clarity: (Writing and organization)**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins by defining the models, establishes the key equivalence lemma, identifies the permutation problem, and then systematically develops the spectral solution. The use of figures, especially Figure 1, is effective in illustrating the core concept of the de-permutation strategy in both the noiseless and noisy settings. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the proposed method.
*   **Weaknesses:** Certain aspects lack detail, which could affect reproducibility. The feature transformation used in the real data experiment is described cryptically ("cubic polynomial feature transformation such that L = 10 (this is the same transformation that corresponds to a polynomial kernel)"), which is insufficient for another researcher to replicate the setup. The condition `MK < L` required for MoM is stated but not explained, which could be a barrier for readers not deeply familiar with the prerequisite literature.

**Originality: (Novelty of contributions)**

*   **Strengths:** The paper's primary contribution is novel. While spectral learning for HMMs is an established field, its application to Mixtures of HMMs is not. The reformulation of an MHMM as a large, structured HMM is an elegant insight. The subsequent de-permutation algorithm, which uses the spectral properties of the learned transition matrix to recover the individual HMM components, is a creative and original solution to the problem that this formulation introduces. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior contributions by extending the MoM framework to this more complex hierarchical model.

**Significance: (Potential impact)**

*   **Strengths:** The work addresses an important problem. MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering, and the computational cost of EM is a known bottleneck. By providing a fast, non-iterative learning algorithm with theoretical grounding, this work offers a valuable alternative. The method avoids the issue of local optima inherent in EM and, as shown in the experiments, offers a significant speed-up. The demonstration that the method can also serve as a principled and effective initialization for EM further enhances its practical significance for practitioners.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation, while supportive, is somewhat limited and could be strengthened.
    *   The quantitative comparison with EM is performed on a single synthetic data configuration. The method's robustness to more challenging scenarios—such as when component HMMs are very similar or when the spectral gap conditions are nearly violated—is not explored.
    *   The proposed method for estimating K is motivated but not experimentally validated. The paper does not report on how accurately this method identifies the correct number of components under various conditions.
    *   The real data results (Table 1) show that the spectral method is often outperformed by a randomly initialized EM, which slightly diminishes its standing as a standalone algorithm. While its value as an initializer is noted, a deeper analysis of this performance gap would be beneficial.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel and elegant extension of spectral learning methods to the important and widely used class of Mixture of HMMs.
2.  **Significance:** It offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM for a well-known problem, addressing the key limitations of speed and local optima. The approach could enable the application of MHMMs to much larger datasets.
3.  **Clarity:** The core idea is communicated clearly and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments for rejection:**

1.  **Technical Rigor:** The theoretical claims for the noisy case, which is the setting of practical relevance, are presented as conjectures and heuristics with non-rigorous justifications rather than formal proofs.
2.  **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is limited. It does not test the proposed method for estimating K and could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with EM across a wider range of data complexities and model parameters.

### Final Assessment

This paper presents a creative and significant contribution to the learning of latent variable models. The proposed spectral learning algorithm for MHMMs is novel, well-motivated, and addresses a clear need for computationally efficient learning methods. The main weaknesses lie in the lack of rigorous theoretical guarantees for the noisy case and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. However, the strength of the core idea and its potential impact outweigh these limitations. The work is a valuable step forward and is likely to inspire further research in this direction.
