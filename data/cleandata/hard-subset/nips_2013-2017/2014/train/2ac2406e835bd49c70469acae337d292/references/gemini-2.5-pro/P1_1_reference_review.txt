**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel, computationally efficient algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M states) as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix of size MK x MK. The authors then leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs to estimate the parameters of this large model. This initial estimation, however, suffers from a permutation ambiguity, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main contribution of the paper is a spectral de-permutation method to resolve this ambiguity. By analyzing the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix, the authors propose a procedure to cluster its columns (after a low-rank reconstruction) to recover the block structure and thereby identify the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also presents a method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of mixture components, K, from the data. The approach is evaluated on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating significant speed advantages over the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and showing its utility as a powerful initialization strategy for EM.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of treating an MHMM as a single large HMM with a special structure is both simple and powerful. It provides a clear pathway to apply the well-developed machinery of spectral learning for HMMs to the more complex MHMM setting. The subsequent spectral de-permutation algorithm is a clever solution to the critical problem of parameter ambiguity that arises from this formulation.

2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for this work is to overcome the computational burden of EM for large datasets. The proposed MoM-based approach successfully addresses this, offering a learning algorithm whose core computational cost is independent of the number of training sequences once the moments are computed. The experiments clearly demonstrate a substantial speed-up compared to EM.

3.  **Practical Significance:** The paper shows strong empirical results. Beyond being a fast standalone algorithm, it proves to be an excellent initializer for EM. Table 1 shows that `MoM+EM` consistently outperforms both the spectral method alone and randomly initialized EM, suggesting a direct and impactful way to improve existing pipelines for MHMM training. This is a significant practical contribution.

4.  **Completeness:** The authors address not only parameter estimation but also model selection by proposing a method to estimate the number of clusters, K. While many learning papers assume K is known, providing a data-driven method for its estimation makes the overall contribution more complete and useful.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** The main weakness of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis, particularly in Section 3.2.2.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The performance bound is justified by citing a result from Anandkumar et al. [1] for a different model (mixture models) with the brief argument that "the estimation procedure is almost identical." This is not a sufficient justification. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a formal proof or at least a more detailed argument as to why this bound should transfer to the MHMM case.
    *   **Lemma 3:** The proof provided is more of an intuitive sketch than a rigorous demonstration. It relies on arguments about convergence rates determined by spectral gaps without formalizing the relationship. The definition of "spectral longevity" (Eq. 7) is also complex and could benefit from more intuition and justification.

2.  **Clarity and Assumptions:**
    *   The condition for successful recovery, `|λGK| > max |λL2,k|`, is central to the method. However, there is little discussion about when this condition might hold or fail in practice. What properties of the component HMMs lead to a favorable spectral gap?
    *   The practical limitations of spectral methods, such as the requirement that the number of latent states be less than the observation dimension (MK < L), are only briefly mentioned in the context of the real-data experiment. This is a critical constraint and should be discussed more prominently in the methodology section.
    *   Algorithm 1 is slightly vague, referring to a "sufficiently large range" for exponentiating eigenvalues. More practical guidance on setting this hyperparameter would improve the paper's reproducibility.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are promising, but could be more comprehensive. The comparison in Figure 4 is only against a randomly initialized EM. It would be interesting to see how the proposed method compares against more sophisticated EM initializations, such as k-means based initialization on sequence features.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   The paper introduces a novel and practical method for a well-known, difficult problem: efficiently learning MHMMs.
*   The core idea of spectral de-permutation is elegant and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are strong, showing clear advantages in speed and demonstrating significant value as an initialization method for EM, which is a highly practical outcome.
*   The work bridges the gap between recent advances in MoM learning and the widely used MHMM framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance**

*   The theoretical claims (Conjecture 1, Lemma 3) are not rigorously supported, which is a significant shortcoming for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The justifications are hand-wavy and rely on unproven connections to prior work on different models.
*   The assumptions and limitations of the approach are not discussed in sufficient depth.

**Overall Recommendation and Concluding Remarks**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and significant practical implications. The proposed spectral learning algorithm for MHMMs is a valuable contribution that effectively trades a small amount of statistical accuracy for a massive gain in computational speed. The finding that it serves as an excellent initializer for EM is particularly compelling.

The primary weakness is the lack of theoretical rigor for some of the central claims. While the empirical results provide strong evidence that the method works, the theoretical underpinnings are presented as conjectures or are backed by informal arguments.

Despite this weakness, the novelty of the approach, its clear practical benefits, and the strength of the empirical results merit acceptance. I recommend that the authors be asked to tone down the theoretical claims in the final version, explicitly framing them as well-supported conjectures, and to add a more detailed discussion of the method's assumptions and limitations (e.g., the `MK < L` constraint and the reliance on the spectral gap). With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent contribution to the NIPS community.
