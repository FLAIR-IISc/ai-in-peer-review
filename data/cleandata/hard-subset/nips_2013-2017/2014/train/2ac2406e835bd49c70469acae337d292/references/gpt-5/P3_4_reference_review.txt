Summary
The paper proposes a spectral method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learning mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states whose transition is block-diagonal and whose initial distribution encodes the mixture weights. The authors apply an existing spectral HMM learner to estimate “global” parameters up to a permutation of the MK states, then propose a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral structure of the estimated transition: in the noiseless case, powers of the block-diagonal transition converge to a matrix with K rank-1 block columns, enabling straightforward clustering of columns. In the noisy case, they propose projecting onto the top-K eigen-space of the estimated transition and clustering columns of the low-rank reconstruction; they also propose a heuristic “spectral longevity” score to select K. Experiments on synthetic data and a small real dataset suggest the method is faster than EM and can serve as a good EM initializer.

Strengths
- Clear, computationally motivated goal: avoid per-sequence forward–backward in EM by using MoM, with a simple post-processing step to recover component structure.
- The block-diagonal equivalence is correctly formalized and leads to an intuitive de-permutation via spectral clustering on the transition.
- The de-permutation idea (cluster columns of a low-rank reconstruction that isolates the K “global” eigenvalues) is simple and potentially useful in practice.
- The method is reasonably general across emission families where E[x|state] is well-defined and identifiable, and the paper discusses Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial cases.
- Empirical results, while limited, indicate speed advantages and that spectral estimates can be effective initializations for EM.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: The MHMM-as-block-diagonal-HMM equivalence is standard; the de-permutation via powers of A or its top-K eigenspace is closely related to classical ideas on metastable/absorbing structures and spectral clustering on Markov chains. The contribution is largely an application of existing spectral HMM machinery with a relatively straightforward clustering post-processing; the degree of novelty seems modest for NeurIPS.
- Theoretical gaps: The core noisy-case claims are left as “Conjecture 1,” with an informal justification that mixes sample complexity from prior spectral work with a loose Frobenius norm bound. There is no rigorous perturbation analysis (e.g., via Davis–Kahan/Wedin) proving that the top-K eigenpairs are “global,” that the rank-K projection is close to the ideal block-limit, or that column clustering recovers the correct partition with high probability. The “longevity” criterion for K-selection is nonstandard and not analyzed.
- Ambiguity beyond permutation: Most spectral HMM methods recover parameters up to an invertible linear transform, not just a permutation (e.g., observable operator models). The paper assumes a pure permutation ambiguity (Eq. 5) without stating and verifying the conditions under which the chosen MoM subroutine returns parameters only up to permutation. This is a major omission: if there is an arbitrary invertible transform, the proposed de-permutation via column clustering of A becomes ill-posed.
- Identifiability and assumptions: The assumption “each Ak has only one eigenvalue equal to 1” (Lemma 2) requires irreducibility and aperiodicity; the paper should state these conditions explicitly, discuss complex eigenvalues, and their effect on the proposed clustering. The requirement MK < L (to ensure full column rank of Ō) is severe and often violated; the workaround of polynomial feature lifting is ad hoc and could be impractical for larger K, M, or continuous high-dimensional data.
- Practical details underspecified: After clustering, how is the mapping constrained to yield exactly K clusters with exactly M states each? What if cluster sizes differ due to noise? Is M assumed known, or are both K and M to be determined? How robust is k-means on columns of Ar when eigenvectors are complex or when blocks are weakly mixed?
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Synthetic experiments are small scale (e.g., two sequences per cluster) and do not systematically test K-identification, robustness to small spectral gaps, or mis-specified M. The real-data experiment covers only K=2, M=4 with a feature-lift to satisfy MK<L; results are summarized but not deeply analyzed, and no baselines beyond EM (e.g., variational Bayes, Bayesian nonparametrics for MHMM) are included. Runtime comparisons lack precise numbers and scaling plots.
- Writing and references: Some notation is sloppy (e.g., missing conditions for ergodicity/periodicity), and several references are incomplete. The “longevity” definition is hard to parse and depends on arbitrary γ and an infinite sum truncated in practice, but implementation details are not specified.

Quality
Technically, the noiseless case is sound. However, the main contributions rely on noisy-case behavior without rigorous guarantees. The assumed permutation-only ambiguity is likely incorrect for general spectral HMM estimators. Empirical validation does not compensate for the theoretical gaps.

Clarity
Generally readable with intuitive diagrams referenced, but key assumptions and the exact spectral HMM subroutine used are not spelled out. Important implementation choices (how to pick K in practice, enforce block sizes, deal with complex eigenvectors) need clarification. References should be completed.

Originality
Moderate. The reduction is known; the de-permutation via spectral structure is plausible but not deeply novel. The “longevity” criterion is new but ad hoc.

Significance
Potentially useful as a practical initialization heuristic, but current theoretical and empirical support is insufficient for a strong claim of advancing the state of the art on MHMM learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely specify the spectral HMM algorithm used and the conditions under which the parameters are identifiable up to a permutation (vs a general invertible transform). If needed, add a calibration step to map to a permutation (e.g., via aligning emission moments).
- Replace Conjecture 1 with a perturbation analysis showing recovery of the K global eigenvectors under reasonable separation conditions; use Davis–Kahan to bound subspace error and a simple mixture-of-clusters analysis to show column clustering succeeds w.h.p.
- Adopt a standard eigengap criterion for K (with theory), or provide a robust, parameter-free alternative; justify the “longevity” score or remove it.
- Ensure the clustering yields exactly M states per component; describe how M and K are estimated jointly, or assume one is known and state it clearly.
- Expand experiments: larger K, M; systematic K-recovery; sensitivity to spectral gaps; comparisons to stronger baselines; report exact runtimes and scaling.
- Discuss the MK<L requirement more deeply and alternatives (e.g., multi-view constructions across time, random projections).

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is intuitive and potentially useful, but the paper lacks rigorous guarantees in the noisy setting, overstates the identifiability (permutation-only), and offers limited empirical evidence. With stronger theory and expanded experiments, this could become a solid contribution, especially as a fast initializer for MHMMs.
