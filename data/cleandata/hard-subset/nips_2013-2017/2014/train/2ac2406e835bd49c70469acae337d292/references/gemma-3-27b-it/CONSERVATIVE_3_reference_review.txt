This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The authors leverage the observation that an MHMM can be represented as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix and apply existing MoM algorithms for HMMs, followed by a spectral de-permutation step to recover individual HMM parameters. The paper also presents conditions for recovering the number of HMMs.

While the idea of extending MoM to MHMMs is a natural progression given the recent popularity of MoM for simpler latent variable models, this work presents a concerning lack of thorough validation and a reliance on conjectures without sufficient supporting evidence. The core novelty – the spectral de-permutation – feels under-developed and relies heavily on assumptions (specifically, the spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix) that are not convincingly demonstrated to hold in practice. The connection to existing work on spectral learning for HMMs [Hsu et al., 2015] is acknowledged but lacks sufficient detail regarding how this work builds upon or differs from it. The assumption of only one eigenvalue equal to 1 for each local transition matrix, while common, isn't critically discussed in terms of its impact on the method's robustness.

The primary weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. The experiments, while including a comparison with EM, are not sufficiently rigorous. The synthetic experiments (Figure 3) show promising results *only* for T=20, raising concerns about scalability. More importantly, the analysis lacks a comprehensive comparison to strong baselines, particularly state-of-the-art MHMM learning algorithms beyond EM. The real-data experiment (UCI handwritten characters) is also weak: the use of a cubic polynomial feature transformation feels ad-hoc and its necessity isn't well-justified. The conclusion that the spectral approach serves as a "good initialization scheme for EM" is hardly a strong result and doesn't justify the complexity of the proposed method. The reported gains over randomly initialized EM are marginal and require more statistically significant evidence.

The clarity of the paper is adequate, though the notation is dense and could benefit from simplification. The organization is logical, but the leap from Lemma 3 to the actual algorithm (Algorithm 1) feels abrupt; more detailed explanation of how the spectral longevity is practically computed and used to determine *K* would be valuable. The conjecture regarding the error bound (‖Ar − Ar‖F ≤ O(1/ √ TN)) is stated without sufficient justification and feels more like a desired property than a proven result. The dependence on data length and number of sequences needs to be carefully discussed.

The originality is moderate. Extending MoM to MHMMs is a logical step, but the spectral de-permutation technique doesn’t appear to represent a major breakthrough. The core idea builds upon well-established principles from spectral graph theory and HMM learning. 

The significance of the work is questionable. While a faster learning algorithm for MHMMs would be valuable, the presented method’s limited empirical support and reliance on strong assumptions cast doubt on its practical applicability and broader impact. The reliance on conjectures further weakens its significance.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Insufficiently rigorous empirical evaluation.
*   Strong assumptions without adequate justification or sensitivity analysis.
*   Limited improvement over existing methods.
*   Heavy reliance on conjectures.
*   Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art MHMM algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Logical extension of MoM to a more complex model (MHMM).
*   Potential for computational efficiency (though not demonstrably shown at scale).
*   Clear presentation of the underlying mathematical framework.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to its lack of thorough validation, strong assumptions, and limited practical impact. The authors need to provide much more compelling evidence to support their claims and demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on real-world datasets against robust baselines. I recommend **rejection**.
