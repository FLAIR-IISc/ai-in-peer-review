Summary
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component is equivalent to an HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first apply a spectral HMM learner to estimate the global parameters up to a permutation of the MK states. They then exploit the spectrum of the global transition matrix to “de-permute” and recover the K blocks: in the noiseless case, the MK×MK transition matrix has exactly K eigenvalues at 1 and A^e converges to K rank-1 blocks of the form v_k 1^T; columns can therefore be clustered into K groups. In the noisy case, they propose to construct a rank-K reconstruction from the top-K eigenpairs, cluster columns, and introduce a “spectral longevity” heuristic to estimate K based on how long the largest K eigenvalues dominate after exponentiation. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwritten trajectory dataset suggest the method is faster than EM and can serve as a good initializer.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on spectral methods for HMMs and latent variable models (Hsu et al., Anandkumar et al.) that provide polynomial-time algorithms with identifiability under full-rank emission features. The equivalence of an MHMM to an HMM with block-diagonal A is straightforward, but using the spectrum of A to reassemble permuted states into component HMMs is an interesting practical step. The paper cites classical MHMM EM work (Smyth) and spectral MoM references, but omits relevant details and citations on continuous-emission spectral HMMs/PSRs and perturbation analysis (e.g., Siddiqi et al., Boots & Gordon; Davis–Kahan/Wedin bounds).

Strengths
- Simple and potentially useful idea: leverage block-diagonal structure to bring MHMMs into the spectral HMM framework, and then use eigen-structure to de-permute states and estimate K.
- Computational motivation is sound: once moments are computed, learning scales with MK but not the number of sequences.
- Empirical results suggest speed advantages and utility as an EM initializer.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Theory is incomplete and key claims are conjectural.
- The central finite-sample claim (Conjecture 1) is not proved; the justification reuses bounds from mixture models that do not apply to HMMs without careful argument. This is a major gap.
- The “spectral longevity” criterion for K is heuristic; Lemma 3 assumes strong separation properties but does not provide a perturbation-robust proof. Standard eigengap arguments or perturbation theory could be used but are not.
- The identifiability conditions needed for successful de-permutation (e.g., distinct stationary distributions or adequate separation of leading eigenspaces across blocks) are not formally stated.

2) Clarity and correctness issues in the observation model and moments.
- The notation conflates O(xt) as p(xt|:) in Eq. (1) with O(:,u)=E[x|state=u]. For continuous emissions, spectral HMMs require a clear feature map and observable operator construction; the paper does not specify which moments/tensors are computed and which spectral learner is actually used.
- The requirement MK < L is introduced without derivation; for continuous features, one needs L ≥ rank(O) and full column rank of the emission mean matrix for the MK-state global HMM. The practical implication (needing large L) is nontrivial.

3) Practicality and failure modes not addressed.
- The de-permutation relies solely on A: if two component HMMs have similar stationary distributions or similar top eigenvectors, columns of A^∞ (or its rank-K reconstruction) may not cluster reliably. The paper does not analyze such cases or propose leveraging both A and O jointly for grouping.
- Assumes each Ak has a unique eigenvalue at 1 (ergodic, aperiodic). Many real HMMs can be near-reducible or have slow mixing, making separation fragile.
- The algorithm requires MK as input to the spectral HMM learner; yet K is purportedly unknown. It’s unclear how to choose MK when K is unknown, or what happens if MK is misspecified.

4) Experimental evaluation is limited.
- Synthetic experiments are small scale; they report a k-means error on columns rather than recovery of parameters or clustering accuracy, with only 2 sequences per cluster in some settings.
- No robustness tests to eigenvalue gaps, varying K and M, near-violation of assumptions, or noise levels.
- Runtime results are only qualitatively described; no wall-clock comparisons or scalability plots.
- The real-data experiment uses a cubic polynomial feature map to increase L so that MK < L. This changes the observation distribution and raises fairness/validity questions for the EM baseline. The spectral method’s dependence on the feature map and its effect on accuracy are not analyzed.

5) Presentation and references.
- Notation is inconsistent and some definitions are imprecise (e.g., switching between probabilities and expectations in Eq. (1)).
- Several references are incomplete; important related work on continuous spectral HMMs/PSRs, perturbation theory, and block-permutation recovery is missing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous finite-sample analysis. Use matrix perturbation tools (Weyl/Davis–Kahan) to bound errors in the top-K invariant subspace of A under sampling noise, and show conditions under which column clustering recovers the correct permutation with high probability. State explicit spectral gap and separation assumptions that are checkable.
- Clarify the observation model and the exact MoM pipeline for continuous emissions: define the feature map, moments/tensors, and conditions for identifiability (rank and non-degeneracy of O). Discuss the L ≥ MK requirement, how to choose features, and alternatives when L < MK (e.g., multiple random projections or multi-view features).
- Strengthen K estimation: compare the “longevity” heuristic to standard eigengap and cross-validated likelihood after EM refinement; provide empirical ablations and sensitivity plots.
- Improve experiments: larger synthetic suites varying K, M, sequence lengths, eigenvalue gaps, and emission separability; report parameter error, clustering accuracy, and de-permutation accuracy; provide runtime scaling. On real data, justify the feature map, ensure fair baselines, and release code/details for reproducibility.
- Discuss leveraging both A and O similarity to de-permute, which could be more robust than using A alone.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea of using spectral structure to de-permute MHMM components is appealing and could be useful in practice, but the current submission lacks rigorous guarantees, has clarity issues in the observation/moment setup, and provides limited empirical validation. With a solid theoretical treatment, clearer algorithmic specification, and stronger experiments, this work could become a valuable contribution.
