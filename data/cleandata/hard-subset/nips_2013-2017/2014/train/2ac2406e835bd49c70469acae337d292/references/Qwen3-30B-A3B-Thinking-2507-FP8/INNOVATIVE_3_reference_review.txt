

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper presents a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using the method of moments, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The core insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling the application of spectral learning techniques for HMMs. The authors then develop a clever method to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral learning, which is the key challenge in recovering individual HMM components from the block-diagonal structure. This is achieved by leveraging the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to estimate a depermutation mapping.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a theoretical foundation by showing the equivalence between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices (Lemma 1). Second, it develops a method to recover the individual HMM parameters despite the permutation ambiguity, using spectral properties to identify the correct block structure (Lemma 2, Corollary 1, and Lemma 3). Third, it proposes a practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) that combines spectral learning with a depermutation procedure based on spectral longevity, which the authors validate through experiments.

The work is highly original in its approach to MHMM learning. While EM is the standard method for MHMM learning, it suffers from computational complexity and initialization challenges. Previous spectral methods for HMMs have not addressed the mixture case, making this a significant conceptual leap. The authors connect their work to recent advances in spectral learning for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2012), but their key insight about the block-diagonal structure of MHMMs as HMMs is novel.

The experimental evaluation is compelling. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves comparable accuracy to EM while being significantly faster, especially for larger datasets (Figure 4). The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories (Table 1) shows that the method can serve as an effective initialization for EM, which is a practical contribution. The noise analysis (Section 4.1) shows that the method correctly recovers the block structure even with limited data (T=20).

The paper's main weakness is the theoretical treatment of the depermutation procedure, where some claims are presented as conjectures (Conjecture 1) rather than proven theorems. However, given the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical validation, these theoretical gaps are acceptable for a NIPS submission. The authors acknowledge the need for further theoretical analysis, which is reasonable for a paper focused on a novel conceptual contribution.

The paper also has minor limitations: the real data experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation to satisfy the MK < L constraint, which feels somewhat ad-hoc, and the scaling behavior with respect to the number of clusters K is not thoroughly explored. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's overall contribution.

In terms of significance, this work addresses an important problem in sequence modeling with practical applications in areas like music analysis (Qi et al., 2007), motion analysis (Jonathan et al., 2004), and character recognition (as shown in the real data experiment). The computational advantage over EM makes the method particularly valuable for large-scale sequence clustering problems.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. The core idea—representing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices and using spectral properties to recover the individual components—is both elegant and transformative. It opens up new possibilities for learning hierarchical latent variable models using spectral techniques. While the theoretical treatment could be strengthened, the paper's conceptual contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

The authors have clearly established how their work differs from previous contributions, particularly by addressing the MHMM case that has been largely overlooked in spectral learning literature. The experimental results provide strong empirical support for the approach.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel method for MHMM learning that is both theoretically grounded and practically valuable. The conceptual novelty, computational advantages, and empirical validation make it a strong candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.

Strengths: Novel insight connecting MHMMs to spectral learning for HMMs, elegant solution to permutation ambiguity, strong experimental validation, practical computational advantages over EM.

Weaknesses: Some theoretical claims as conjectures rather than proven theorems, minor ad-hoc aspects in real data experiment.

Overall: This paper offers a bold new approach to an important problem with clear significance and novelty. The minor theoretical gaps are outweighed by the conceptual contribution.
