Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments, MoM) algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix Ā, constructed by stacking each component HMM’s transition matrix on the diagonal and concatenating emission matrices. The authors first apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs [Hsu et al., JCSS; Anandkumar et al., COLT/ICML] to estimate the parameters of this global HMM, which yields a permutation ambiguity across the MK latent states. They then propose to “de-permute” by leveraging spectral properties of Ā: in the noiseless case, (Ā)e converges to a matrix with K blocks whose columns are clustered by stationary distributions; in the noisy case, they retain the K largest “global” eigenvalues (those perturbations of the K unit eigenvalues) to form a rank-K reconstruction Ar and cluster its columns to recover the block structure. They also propose a criterion (“spectral longevity”) that exponentiates sorted eigenvalues and counts how long the top K’ mass remains near 1, selecting K as the argmax of this longevity.

Empirically, on synthetic Gaussian-emission MHMMs the method is much faster than EM and achieves comparable clustering accuracy as sequences/observations increase. On a UCI handwritten trajectory dataset, they lift features to satisfy L ≥ MK, show that spectral learning is competitive with EM, and that it serves as an effective initialization for EM.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Positive: The reduction of MHMM to an HMM with block-diagonal structure is correct and well known; the spectral characterization (K eigenvalues equal to 1 in the noiseless setting, with limiting block structure) is standard for ergodic Markov chains and block-diagonal compositions. The practical pipeline—estimate global HMM via spectral methods, low-rank reconstruction, column clustering—is sensible and computationally attractive.
- Limitations:
  - The core finite-sample step—recovering the permutation and K under noise—is not theoretically established. Conjecture 1 (that Ar concentrates around the ideal rank-K projection at O(1/√TN)) is not proved and borrows a bound from a different model. Lemma 3 assumes that the top K eigenvalues are “global” and separated from the local second eigenvalues; the resulting “spectral longevity” criterion is only partially justified and lacks finite-sample robustness guarantees (e.g., misclustering probability, stability under small eigengaps).
  - Identifiability and rank conditions required by spectral HMM learning (e.g., Ō having full column rank, L ≥ MK, ergodicity/aperiodicity) are not made explicit up front. The practical implication of L ≥ MK is substantial and affects applicability.
  - The de-permutation via k-means clustering of columns of Ar is not analyzed; no conditions are given for separability of columns across blocks as a function of eigen-gaps and noise.
  - Reproducibility suffers from missing details on the specific HMM spectral method used for continuous observations (moment definitions, whitening/projection, estimation of moments from sequences, regularization choices), hyperparameters for the longevity computation (range of exponents e, choice of γ), and clustering parameters.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with a clear high-level narrative and helpful lemmas for the noiseless case. However:
- Some notation is inconsistent or contains typos (e.g., 1>J vs 1>M, spacing in Ā P, use of M/J, occasional switching between MK/MK). Define all symbols on first use and maintain consistent dimensions.
- Assumptions (ergodicity, aperiodicity, L ≥ MK, full column rank of emissions) should be clearly stated in the main algorithm section.
- The “HMM MethodofMoments” black-box should be described with enough detail for reproduction (moments used, factorization method, sample complexity assumptions).

Originality
The equivalence between MHMM and an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is known. Using spectral HMM learning on the global model is a natural extension. The main novelty lies in the de-permutation step using eigenstructure and in the “spectral longevity” criterion for estimating K. While reasonable, these ideas are incremental and overlap with common uses of spectral gaps/eigenvalue multiplicity for model selection and block recovery. The paper would benefit from clarifying how the proposed de-permutation and K-selection differ from standard eigengap heuristics and whether similar ideas have appeared for block models or mixtures.

Significance
If reliable, the approach could provide a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMMs, especially as the number of sequences grows, and a practical initializer for EM. However, the strong requirement L ≥ MK limits applicability in many real settings; the workaround via polynomial feature lifts changes the generative model and may degrade statistical efficiency. The lack of theoretical guarantees and limited empirical scope reduce the current impact.

Empirical evaluation
- Strengths: Includes runtime comparisons and shows the method can initialize EM effectively. Reports that moment computation time is included.
- Limitations: Experiments are small-scale (K ≤ 5, M ≤ 4). No systematic evaluation of K-estimation accuracy, sensitivity to eigengap, or robustness to model mismatch (e.g., non-isotropic Gaussians, Poisson/multinomial emissions). No comparison to alternative non-EM baselines (e.g., spectral variants tailored to mixtures). Real-data results require feature lifting to meet L ≥ MK; implications are not discussed.

Pros
- Clear, simple reduction and practical de-permutation heuristic.
- Computationally attractive and demonstrably faster than EM.
- Useful as an EM initializer.
- Addresses an important application (sequence clustering via MHMMs).

Cons
- Key steps rely on unproven conjectures and strong spectral separation assumptions.
- Important identifiability and rank assumptions are implicit.
- Limited experimental validation (scale, robustness, K estimation).
- Applicability constrained by L ≥ MK; workaround changes the model.
- Reproducibility details are missing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample guarantees for de-permutation and K selection under explicit eigengap/noise conditions; bound misclustering probability as a function of sample size and spectral gaps.
- State and justify identifiability/rank assumptions; discuss practical checks or relaxations (e.g., dimensionality reduction with guarantees).
- Detail the moment estimation procedure for continuous observations.
- Empirically evaluate automatic K estimation, robustness across emission families, and sensitivity to eigengap violations; compare against eigengap baselines.
- Scale up experiments and include additional baselines.

Verdict
In scope for NeurIPS. The idea is promising and practically useful, but the current submission lacks theoretical guarantees and comprehensive empirical validation. As written, it falls short of NeurIPS standards for rigor and significance. I recommend rejection at this time; strengthening the theory and experiments would substantially improve the paper.
