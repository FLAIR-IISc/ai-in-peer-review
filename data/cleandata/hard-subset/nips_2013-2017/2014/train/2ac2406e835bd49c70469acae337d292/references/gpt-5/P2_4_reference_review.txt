Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach for learning mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component can be represented as a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral HMM learning methods to estimate the global parameters up to a permutation and then propose a de-permutation procedure based on spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, each block contributes an eigenvalue of 1, so a rank-K reconstruction and column clustering should reveal the blocks. In the noisy case, they propose selecting K via a “spectral longevity” heuristic (based on gaps in the eigenvalue magnitudes over powers of the matrix) and then perform k-means on the columns of the rank-K reconstruction to obtain a permutation that approximately restores block diagonal structure. They present synthetic experiments and a small real-data experiment on UCI handwriting trajectories, showing speedups over EM and utility as an EM initializer.

This line of work builds on spectral learning for HMMs and mixtures (e.g., Hsu-Kakade-Zhang and Anandkumar et al.), and on the use of moment decompositions. The novelty here lies in applying spectral HMM learning to MHMMs via the block-diagonal reduction and in proposing a concrete de-permutation and K-selection method tailored to that structure.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction from MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is correct and well-known; treating the MoM HMM learner as a black box is reasonable. The de-permutation idea using the eigenstructure of the transition matrix is plausible in the noiseless setting and when cluster-level eigenvalues close to 1 are well separated from the rest.
- However, the core claims for the noisy setting largely rely on conjectures and heuristics. Conjecture 1 (low-rank reconstruction accuracy and its dependence on the separation between “global” and “local” eigenvalues) is unproven and borrows a sample complexity statement from a different model. The “spectral longevity” criterion for K selection is motivated but not rigorously justified beyond a lemma that itself assumes a favorable eigenvalue ordering and gap behavior. Identifiability conditions are not stated in a precise manner (beyond assuming each Ak has a unique eigenvalue at 1), and the conditions needed on emissions (e.g., rank conditions on Ō, requirements like L ≥ MK) are only hinted at. The approach likely inherits standard spectral HMM assumptions (e.g., invertibility/conditioning of certain moment matrices), but these are not laid out, making it difficult to assess when the method will succeed.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are small scale (e.g., K ≤ 5, M ≤ 4) and focus mainly on clustering and runtime comparisons. There is no parameter estimation error analysis, no stress tests on eigenvalue separation, and limited evaluation of robustness to model misspecification (e.g., non-ergodic chains, near-reducible blocks, overlapping emissions, unequal mixing proportions). The real-data experiment requires a polynomial feature expansion to enforce MK < L, which is an awkward and potentially distortionary workaround; results show the spectral method is a decent initializer but not consistently superior to EM.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and the high-level idea is clearly communicated. The block-diagonal reduction and the intuition behind the de-permutation are easy to follow.
- Missing details hinder reproducibility: how exactly the moments are constructed for continuous emissions, how whitening/singular value truncation is performed, the precise spectral HMM algorithm used, and the computational complexity in terms of N, T, L, M, K. Assumptions (ergodicity/aperiodicity, rank conditions, need for MK ≤ L) should be stated upfront. The proposed K-selection procedure would benefit from a precise algorithmic description and complexity, and a discussion of numerical stability. Figures referred to in the text are essential to judge performance but the manuscript should include clearer quantitative summaries (e.g., absolute times, accuracy curves with variance).

Originality:
- The block-diagonal equivalence itself is straightforward. The contribution is the de-permutation and K-estimation mechanism tailored to this structure in a spectral framework. To my knowledge, there is limited prior work on spectral estimation specifically for MHMMs; that makes this direction potentially interesting. Still, the novelty is incremental relative to known spectral-HMM/MoM machinery; much of the methodological lift is heuristic and not theoretically grounded.

Significance:
- If robust and well-analyzed, a spectral alternative to EM for MHMMs would be valuable, especially as an initializer. However, the current form lacks theoretical guarantees and thorough empirical validation, limiting confidence in practical impact. The method scales with MK (not small in many MHMM applications), and the requirement MK < L is restrictive for common low-dimensional observation settings unless one engineers features. The strongest practical outcome in the paper is as an EM initializer, which is useful but modest.

Strengths:
- Clear and simple reduction from MHMM to HMM; leverages mature spectral HMM tools.
- Intuitive de-permutation via spectral structure and a practical K-selection heuristic.
- Demonstrates computational speed advantages over EM on synthetic data and usefulness as an EM initializer on real data.

Weaknesses:
- Central claims in the noisy setting are conjectural; no end-to-end recovery or sample complexity guarantees for MHMM parameters.
- Key assumptions and identifiability conditions are not clearly laid out; strong requirement MK < L.
- Limited experiments (small K, M), no parameter recovery metrics, and no robustness analyses.
- K-selection heuristic may be brittle when eigenvalues are closely spaced; no ablations or comparisons to standard model selection methods (e.g., BIC on EM, spectral gap heuristics, eigenvalue thresholding).
- Reproducibility concerns: missing details on moment construction for continuous emissions, whitening, and algorithmic complexity.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- State precise identifiability and regularity conditions (on Ō, Ak, ν̄) under which the method recovers component parameters up to permutation. Can you provide conditions under which the de-permutation is unique?
- Provide a non-asymptotic analysis (even partial) showing how eigenvalue separation and sample size control de-permutation error and K estimation error.
- Clarify the moment computation for continuous emissions and the required feature maps; discuss the practicality of MK < L and alternatives (e.g., multiple views or random features).
- Add experiments with larger K and M, multinomial emissions, and report parameter estimation error, sensitivity to eigenvalue gaps, and robustness to misspecification.
- Compare K-selection to alternative criteria (e.g., profile likelihood with EM, spectral gap thresholds, cross-validation).
- Provide time and memory complexity and absolute runtimes for all steps, including moment computation and eigendecomposition.

Overall evaluation:
- Quality: moderate; promising idea but lacking theory and rigorous validation.
- Clarity: fair, but key methodological details and assumptions are missing.
- Originality: modest; new application of spectral ideas with a heuristic de-permutation/K-selection step.
- Significance: potential as an initializer; limited evidence for standalone superiority.

Given the current state, I lean towards rejection, with the caveat that the idea is interesting and could be competitive with stronger theoretical backing and more comprehensive experiments.
