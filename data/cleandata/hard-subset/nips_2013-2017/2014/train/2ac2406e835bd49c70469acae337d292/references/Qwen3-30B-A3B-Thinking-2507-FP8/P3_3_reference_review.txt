

# Peer Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in the traditional EM-based learning. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs and extend them to MHMMs by developing a depermutation method to recover the individual HMM parameters. This builds on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning for HMMs [1-3], but extends it to a more complex model class.

The paper makes a theoretically sound connection between MHMMs and structured HMMs, which is novel and valuable. The authors demonstrate that the MHMM can be viewed as an HMM with specific structural constraints, enabling the application of spectral learning. They then develop a method to recover the individual HMM components by exploiting the spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application**: The paper makes an important conceptual leap in recognizing the block-diagonal structure of MHMMs as an HMM, which is a clever theoretical insight that enables the application of spectral learning methods.

2. **Computational Advantage**: The proposed method avoids the O(N) per-iteration complexity of EM (where N is the number of sequences), making it particularly suitable for large-scale sequence clustering problems. The experimental results (Section 4.2) convincingly demonstrate the speed advantage.

3. **Practical Utility**: The paper shows that the spectral method can serve as an excellent initialization for EM (Section 4.3), which is valuable for practitioners dealing with the non-convexity of MHMM learning.

4. **Theoretical Foundation**: The lemmas and spectral analysis (Section 3.2) provide a solid theoretical basis for the approach, particularly the characterization of the global transition matrix's spectral properties.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Formal Guarantees**: The paper relies heavily on Conjecture 1, which claims that the estimated transition matrix Ar is close to the true Ar with high probability. This is a critical assumption but lacks formal proof. The justification provided is insufficient for a NIPS paper, which typically expects rigorous theoretical guarantees.

2. **Limited Discussion of Assumptions**: The paper assumes that each local transition matrix has only one eigenvalue of magnitude 1. This is a strong assumption that may not hold for many practical HMMs (e.g., periodic or nearly periodic chains). The paper doesn't explore how violations of this assumption affect performance.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with Alternatives**: The paper compares only with EM, but doesn't discuss how their method relates to other non-EM approaches for MHMMs (e.g., variational Bayes or other moment-based methods). This is particularly important given the authors cite [9] as using MoM for initialization.

4. **Real Data Experiment Limitations**: The real data experiment (Section 4.3) transforms the data using a cubic polynomial to meet the MK < L constraint. This is a significant limitation as it's not clear how well this would generalize to other datasets or whether the transformation distorts meaningful sequence structure.

5. **Algorithmic Details**: The depermutation algorithm (Algorithm 1) relies on clustering columns of a matrix, but doesn't specify the clustering method or provide evidence of its robustness to noise. This is a critical component that deserves more detailed treatment.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution by connecting MHMM learning to spectral learning for HMMs. However, the lack of formal guarantees for the key estimation error (Conjecture 1) significantly weakens the theoretical contribution. The experimental results are adequate but don't sufficiently demonstrate the method's robustness to violations of the spectral assumptions.

The most significant limitation is the paper's failure to address how the method performs when the critical spectral conditions (e.g., |λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|) aren't met. In practice, real-world data often doesn't satisfy such clean spectral separation, and the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to handle such cases.

The comparison with EM is useful but incomplete. The paper should have included a more thorough comparison with recent non-EM approaches for MHMMs, such as those based on variational inference or other moment-based methods.

## Recommendation

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**

The paper presents a novel application of spectral learning to MHMMs with clear computational advantages over EM. The theoretical connection between MHMMs and structured HMMs is elegant and well-motivated. However, the lack of formal guarantees for the key estimation error and the limited discussion of practical limitations prevent it from being a strong accept.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing a formal proof for Conjecture 1
2. Extending the experiments to include cases where the spectral assumptions are violated
3. Comparing with other non-EM approaches for MHMMs
4. Providing more details on the depermutation algorithm and its robustness

The significance of the work is moderate - it addresses a practical computational bottleneck in sequence clustering, but the method's applicability is limited to cases where the spectral conditions hold. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on spectral learning, but the current version doesn't fully establish the method's reliability and scope.

This is a solid paper with a good idea that deserves publication, but it requires additional theoretical justification and experimental validation before it meets NIPS standards. The authors should address these concerns in revision.
