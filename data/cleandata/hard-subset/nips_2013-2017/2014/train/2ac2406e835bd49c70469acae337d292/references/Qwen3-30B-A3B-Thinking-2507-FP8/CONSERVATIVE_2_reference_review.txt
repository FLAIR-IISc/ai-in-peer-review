

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), framing the MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors claim their method avoids the computational burden of EM by leveraging spectral learning techniques, and they propose a depermutation procedure to recover individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters. The paper makes several claims about the method's computational advantages and theoretical properties.

## Strengths

The paper identifies an interesting connection between MHMMs and HMMs with structured transition matrices, which is theoretically sound as shown in Lemma 1. The computational motivation is valid, as EM for MHMMs does require forward-backward passes for each sequence in every iteration, which becomes prohibitive for large datasets. The experimental section provides some validation on synthetic and real data, showing that the method can serve as a good initialization for EM. The paper also makes a clear attempt to address the challenge of recovering the individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper falls short on several critical aspects that would be required for acceptance at NIPS:

1. **Theoretical justification is insufficient**: The paper relies on unproven conjectures (Conjecture 1) without rigorous proof. The justification for Conjecture 1 is hand-wavy, reusing a bound from a different model without justification for its applicability to the MHMM setting. The spectral conditions in Lemma 3 are not adequately explained or validated, and the paper fails to establish how often these conditions hold in practice.

2. **Inadequate experimental validation**: The experiments are limited in scope and scale. The synthetic experiments use very small datasets (e.g., "2 sequences with length T for each cluster" with T=20 in Figure 3), which is insufficient to demonstrate the claimed computational advantages. The real-world experiment (Table 1) uses a transformed dataset with L=10 (from original L=3) to satisfy the requirement MK < L, which seems like an artificial constraint that may not hold for most real applications. The comparison with EM is superficial - the paper doesn't show how the spectral method compares to other MoM approaches for HMMs or MHMMs, nor does it provide error bars or statistical significance testing.

3. **Lack of comparison with established baselines**: The paper claims computational advantages over EM, but doesn't provide a clear computational complexity analysis or benchmark against other spectral learning approaches for mixture models. The speed comparison in Figure 4 is limited to small datasets (sequence lengths up to 200), which doesn't demonstrate scalability to large-scale problems where the claimed advantage would be most valuable.

4. **Unsubstantiated claims about "recovery"**: The paper claims to "recover the number of HMMs" but doesn't adequately justify how this is better than standard model selection techniques like BIC or AIC. The spectral longevity metric (Definition 3) is introduced without explanation of its statistical properties or advantages over existing methods.

5. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims this is "a computationally lighter alternative compared to more traditional maximum likelihood approaches," but fails to establish that it's competitive with existing MoM-based methods for HMMs, which have been studied extensively in the literature (e.g., [1, 2, 3] cited in the paper).

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of the rigorous standards required for NIPS. The theoretical foundation is incomplete, relying on unproven conjectures rather than rigorous proofs. The experimental validation is limited in scope and fails to demonstrate the claimed advantages on realistic datasets. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that this method is a meaningful contribution to the field of latent variable model learning.

The paper would benefit from:
- Rigorous theoretical justification of the spectral conditions and depermutation algorithm
- More extensive experiments on larger, more realistic datasets
- Comparison with other established MoM approaches for HMMs and mixture models
- Clearer explanation of the advantages over standard model selection techniques

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. While the idea has potential, the lack of rigorous theoretical justification and insufficient experimental validation means the paper doesn't meet NIPS's standards for technical soundness and completeness.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper makes promising claims but fails to provide the necessary theoretical and experimental evidence to support them. The unproven conjectures and limited experimental validation undermine the paper's contribution.
