Summary
This paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The key observation is that an MHMM (with K components, each with M states) is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors suggest: (i) first learn this global HMM via a spectral HMM algorithm (which returns parameters up to a permutation of the MK hidden states); (ii) then recover the original K blocks by exploiting spectral properties of the learned transition matrix—specifically, using the fact that each block has a unit eigenvalue and the limit of large powers of the true transition matrix exhibits K rank-1 clusters; (iii) finally, estimate K from the empirical spectrum using a “spectral longevity” heuristic. Synthetic experiments show that the de-permutation works with moderate sequence lengths, and that the spectral method is faster than EM while competitive in clustering accuracy when data are sufficient. A small UCI trajectory dataset shows that the spectral solution is a good initializer for EM.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on spectral learning for HMMs and latent-variable models (Hsu et al. JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al. COLT/Tensor decompositions) and on the classical MHMM literature (Smyth, NIPS 1997). The “global HMM with block-diagonal transition” view is standard. The main novelty is an explicit de-permutation scheme to recover component-wise parameters from a permuted global estimate, and a heuristic for estimating K. The paper would benefit from engaging with related spectral work for continuous observations and predictive state representations (e.g., Song/Boots/Gordon/Gretton on Hilbert space embeddings and subspace identification), and with perturbation analyses (e.g., Davis–Kahan).

Quality
- Positive: The reduction from MHMM to block-diagonal HMM is correct, Lemma 2 and the corollary hold under standard ergodicity/aperiodicity assumptions, and the noiseless de-permutation intuition is sound. The proposed pipeline is simple and potentially practical. Experiments suggest substantial computational gains vs EM.
- Concerns:
  - The core perturbation analysis is largely heuristic. Conjecture 1 (separation of “global” vs “local” eigenvalues and the bound on the low-rank reconstruction) is unproven; the justification mixes sample complexity of moments with spectral perturbation without a clear bridge. There are standard matrix perturbation tools that could make this rigorous.
  - The “spectral longevity” criterion for estimating K is ad hoc; Lemma 3 relies on indicator thresholds and a ratio-of-ratios argument that glosses over complex eigenvalues and non-normal matrices. A more standard eigengap or stability-based criterion, with theory, would strengthen the claim.
  - Emission model/moment setup is underspecified and sometimes inconsistent. The text defines O both as p(x|state) and as E[x|state]; the Hsu et al. spectral HMM requires discrete emissions or particular feature-based constructions; here MK < L is imposed and polynomial features are used ad hoc. The paper does not state the exact moments used, identifiability conditions (e.g., full column rank of certain moment matrices), or the precise algorithmic steps for continuous observations.
  - Assumptions needed for the corollary (irreducibility/aperiodicity/diagonalizability of each Ak, distinct stationary distributions across clusters) are not clearly stated; these are important for clustering columns of Ar to succeed.
  - Evaluation is limited: mostly small synthetic settings, modest sequence lengths, and one real dataset with feature lifting to meet MK < L. No systematic study of K estimation accuracy, robustness to small eigengaps, or failure cases (e.g., similar stationary distributions across components). No parameter recovery error is reported.

Clarity
- The paper is readable and the high-level idea is well communicated. However:
  - Notation for O and O(xt) is confusing and mixes likelihoods with first moments; please unify the emission model and state clearly which moments/features are used in MoM and the conditions (rank, separability) required.
  - Algorithm 1 omits critical details: the exact moment tensors/matrices computed, whitening steps, the HMM spectral subroutine used for continuous observations, and how the clustering of Ar columns is performed in practice.
  - Assumptions for lemmas (ergodicity, aperiodicity, diagonalizability, distinct v_k) should be stated explicitly.
  - References are incomplete and some key related work is missing.

Originality
- Viewing MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is known. The new contribution is the de-permutation via spectral clustering on powers/low-rank reconstructions of the learned transition and a heuristic for estimating K. This is a reasonable and potentially useful extension, but the theoretical novelty is limited without stronger guarantees.

Significance
- If made robust and better justified, the approach could offer a practical, faster alternative to EM for MHMMs and a strong initializer. Recovering per-component parameters from spectral estimates is of interest. Currently, the empirical validation is too thin to claim a significant advance, and theoretical guarantees are incomplete.

Pros
- Simple and appealing idea; leverages well-known spectral tools.
- Potentially large computational savings vs EM.
- Practical de-permutation heuristic that seems to work in some regimes.
- Shows utility as an EM initializer.

Cons
- Key theoretical claims are conjectural or heuristic; missing rigorous perturbation analysis.
- Emission model and MoM construction for continuous data unclear; identifiability conditions not articulated.
- K estimation criterion ad hoc; no systematic evaluation of its accuracy.
- Limited experiments; small synthetic setups; one modest real dataset; no ablations on spectral gaps/noise.
- Important assumptions (ergodicity, diagonalizability, distinct stationary distributions) not stated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise description of the MoM estimator used for the global HMM with continuous observations, including the exact moments, whitening, and identifiability assumptions (and cite appropriate prior work for continuous HMMs/PSRs).
- State assumptions clearly for Lemma 2/Corollary 1 and the de-permutation to hold (irreducible/aperiodic Ak, diagonalizable Ā, distinct v_k).
- Replace Conjecture 1 with a perturbation bound using standard tools (e.g., Weyl, Davis–Kahan, pseudospectra for non-normal matrices), and formalize conditions under which the top-K “global” eigenvalues separate from the rest.
- Justify K selection with a more principled eigengap or model selection criterion (or at least compare several criteria empirically).
- Expand experiments: larger varieties of MHMMs, stress tests with small eigengaps, varying K and M, recovery of K accuracy, parameter error, robustness to similar stationary distributions, and comparisons to strong EM baselines.
- Clarify the need for MK < L, its practical implications, and whether alternative feature maps mitigate this.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and potentially useful, but the current version lacks rigor on key claims and sufficient empirical validation. With clearer assumptions, a more principled perturbation analysis, and stronger experiments, this could become a solid contribution.
