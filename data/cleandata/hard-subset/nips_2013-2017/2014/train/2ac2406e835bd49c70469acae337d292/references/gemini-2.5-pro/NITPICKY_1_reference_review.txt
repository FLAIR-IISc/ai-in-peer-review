**Summary:**
This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states as a single, larger HMM with a KM-state, block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this global model. The main technical contribution is a procedure to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in the spectral learning output, which mixes parameters from the different HMM components. This "de-permutation" is achieved by analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. The authors leverage the fact that, in the noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues of 1, and its powers converge to a block-structured matrix whose columns can be clustered to identify the HMM components. A heuristic method based on the "longevity" of eigenvalues is also proposed to estimate the number of components, K, from data. The work is positioned as a computationally efficient alternative to the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty:** The formulation of an MHMM as a single HMM with special structure, and the subsequent use of spectral properties to de-permute the learned parameters, is an elegant and novel approach to a challenging learning problem.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for this work is to avoid the high computational cost of EM for large datasets. The proposed MoM-based method has a learning time independent of the data size after the initial moment computation, offering a significant potential speed-up, as demonstrated in Figure 4.
3.  **Initialization for EM:** The real-data experiment suggests that even if the method does not outperform EM in accuracy, it serves as an effective initialization strategy, which is a valuable practical contribution in its own right.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**
Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper is undermined by significant theoretical gaps, a lack of methodological rigor, and numerous issues in presentation and clarity.

1.  **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical support for the de-permutation and model selection procedures in the presence of noise is extremely weak.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This is the cornerstone of the noisy-case analysis, yet it is presented without a proof. The provided "justification" is not a proof; it consists of a series of loose applications of the triangle inequality and, critically, reuses a sample complexity bound from [1] for a different model ("mixture model") without any formal argument for why it should apply here. This represents a major theoretical gap.
    *   **Lemma 3 and "Spectral Longevity":** The proposed method for determining K is heuristic and poorly motivated. The "spectral longevity" metric (Eq. 7) is complex and appears arbitrary. The proof of Lemma 3 is descriptive and hand-wavy, relying on vague notions of "convergence rates" rather than a formal derivation. The conditions of the lemma themselves seem difficult to verify in practice.

2.  **Clarity and Notational Issues:** The paper suffers from a lack of precision that hinders reproducibility and understanding.
    *   In Section 2.1, the definition of the emission matrix `O` is slightly inconsistent. It is first used in the likelihood `p(xt|rt, O)` but later defined via the expectation `E[xt|rt = u]`. While these are equivalent for the specific distributions listed, the former is more general. Furthermore, the variance parameter `σ^2` for the Gaussian case is introduced but never mentioned again in the learning algorithm.
    *   There is a clear typo in Eq. (2), where `1>J` should likely be `1>M`.
    *   The use of `>` for matrix transpose (e.g., `1>M`) is non-standard and should be replaced with `T`.
    *   The notation in the justification for Conjecture 1 is sloppy, using `A` for `ĀP` without definition, which adds to the confusion.

3.  **Experimental Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing.
    *   The setup in Section 4.1 is concerning. The claim is that experiments were run with "2 sequences with length T for each cluster". Using only 6 sequences in total seems exceptionally data-poor and makes the clean results in Figure 3 surprising. If this is not a typo, the robustness of the de-permutation algorithm is questionable.
    *   The real-data experiment (Section 4.3) highlights a key limitation of the underlying spectral method: the requirement that `MK < L`. The authors address this by applying a "cubic polynomial feature transformation" to increase `L`. This is a significant data pre-processing step whose effect on the model and results is not analyzed or discussed.
    *   The results in Table 1 show that the proposed spectral method is often less accurate than randomly initialized EM. This diminishes the paper's claim of being a strong standalone alternative, reframing its main contribution as an initialization scheme.

4.  **Formatting and Referencing:** The paper exhibits a serious lack of care in its final presentation.
    *   The bibliography is incomplete and improperly formatted. Multiple references (e.g., [1], [3], [4]) are missing conference/journal names, years, and page numbers. For example, reference [1] ends with "In COLT,, , ,".
    *   There is a critical error in the citation for the dataset. The text cites `[8]` for the UCI repository, but reference [8] in the list is a paper by Chaganty and Liang. The text also mentions an EM initialization scheme from `[9]`, but there is no ninth reference in the bibliography. Such errors are unacceptable for a submission to a premier venue.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a conceptually interesting and novel idea for learning MHMMs, the execution is severely lacking. The theoretical foundations for the noisy case are unsubstantiated conjectures, the experimental validation is ambiguous, and the paper is riddled with notational inconsistencies, typos, and critical formatting errors in the bibliography. The combination of these major and minor flaws leads me to recommend **rejection**. The authors would need to provide rigorous proofs for their theoretical claims (especially Conjecture 1), clarify their experimental setup, and meticulously revise the manuscript to address the numerous presentation issues before it could be considered for publication.
