**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method of moments (MoM) algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The key insight is to re-frame the MHMM as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors leverage an existing spectral algorithm to learn the parameters of this equivalent HMM. This standard procedure, however, recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main technical contribution of this work is a spectral de-permutation algorithm to recover the individual HMM parameters by identifying this block structure from the learned global transition matrix, even in the presence of estimation noise. The authors also propose a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of mixture components from the data. The method is shown to be significantly faster than the standard EM algorithm and can serve as an effective initialization strategy for it.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound in its core observation and the high-level algorithmic approach. The formulation of an MHMM as a large HMM with a block-diagonal structure is correct and insightful. The proposed algorithm, which involves learning this large HMM and then recovering the block structure, is logical and well-motivated.

However, the theoretical support for the key steps, particularly in the presence of noise, is weak.
1.  **Conjecture 1:** This is a crucial claim stating that the low-rank approximation of the estimated transition matrix (`Ar`) will be close to the true low-rank matrix (`Ar`) under a spectral gap condition. The justification provided is hand-wavy and unconvincing. It relies on reusing a sample complexity bound from [1] for a different model (a mixture model, not an HMM), with the authors themselves admitting that a full analysis is "out of the scope of this paper." For a NIPS paper, this lack of rigor on a central claim is a significant weakness. The proof sketch provided is difficult to follow and appears to contain notational inconsistencies (e.g., using `A` for `ĀP`), making it hard to verify.
2.  **Lemma 3 and "Spectral Longevity":** The method for determining the number of clusters `K` feels heuristic. While the intuition that the largest spectral gaps should determine `K` is plausible, the "longevity" metric (Eq. 7) is presented without strong theoretical backing or sensitivity analysis. How is `γ` chosen? How does the performance depend on the range of `e` used in the summation? The proof sketch relies on an assertion about the ratio of spectral gaps, which makes sense, but the connection to the proposed `L` metric is not formally established.

The experimental evaluation is reasonable but could be more thorough. The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's speed advantage over EM and its effectiveness in the tested regimes. The real-data experiment on the character trajectory dataset is a good proof of concept, showing that the method can be a strong initializer for EM. However, the experiments do not appear to stress-test the method's assumptions. For instance, how does the algorithm perform when the spectral gap condition in Conjecture 1 (`|λGK | > max |λL2,k|`) is nearly violated? A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of data-generating conditions would strengthen the paper's claims.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The progression from the model definition to the MHMM-as-HMM equivalence and finally to the de-permutation algorithm is logical and easy to follow. Figure 1 provides an excellent visual intuition for the core problem and the proposed solution in the noiseless and noisy cases.

However, clarity breaks down in the sections presenting the main theoretical claims (Section 3.2.2). The justification for Conjecture 1 is confusing, and the notation in the proof of Lemma 3 is dense. For reproducibility, more details are needed about the experimental setup, particularly the "cubic polynomial feature transformation" used in the real data experiment.

**Originality**

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in spectral learning for latent variable models, particularly HMMs [1, 2, 3]. The authors are transparent about using these existing methods for the initial parameter estimation. The primary novelty lies in:
1.  The specific application of this framework to MHMMs via the block-diagonal HMM equivalence.
2.  The development of the spectral de-permutation algorithm to recover the component HMMs from the permuted global parameters.

This de-permutation step is a clever and non-trivial contribution. While clustering eigenvectors or related quantities is a common technique in spectral methods, its application here to undo the permutation ambiguity in a learned HMM transition matrix is novel and well-motivated. The approach for estimating `K` also appears to be original, though its heuristic nature lessens its impact.

**Significance**

The work addresses an important problem. MHMMs are a powerful tool for sequence clustering, but learning them via EM is often slow and susceptible to poor local optima. This paper presents a practical and computationally efficient alternative. The fact that the algorithm is non-iterative (post moment computation) and provides a principled way to estimate parameters is a significant advantage over EM.

The results suggest two key uses for this work: as a fast, standalone algorithm for large datasets where EM is infeasible, and as a high-quality initialization strategy to improve the performance and robustness of EM. Both are valuable contributions to the machine learning community. The ideas presented could potentially be extended to other hierarchical or mixture models, suggesting a promising direction for future research.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty:** The spectral de-permutation algorithm to recover component HMM parameters is an elegant and original contribution.
*   **Significance:** Provides a fast, non-iterative alternative to EM for a useful model (MHMM), addressing major computational and local-optima issues.
*   **Clarity:** The core idea is presented clearly, with helpful illustrations.
*   **Practicality:** The method demonstrates strong performance as both a standalone algorithm and an initialization for EM.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical justifications for the algorithm's performance under noise (Conjecture 1) and for the estimation of `K` (Lemma 3) are weak and not adequately proven. This is the paper's most significant flaw.
*   **Experimental Depth:** The experiments do not sufficiently explore the boundaries of the method's applicability, especially concerning the stated spectral assumptions.
*   **Reproducibility:** Key details of the experimental setup (e.g., feature transformation) are missing.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a very promising and clever idea with strong practical implications. The core contribution is original and significant. However, the lack of theoretical rigor supporting its central claims is a serious concern for a top-tier conference like NIPS. As it stands, the paper feels incomplete. I would recommend rejection in its current form but would be strongly in favor of acceptance if the authors could substantially bolster the theoretical analysis of their method, particularly by providing a proper proof or more thorough justification for Conjecture 1.
