Summary
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block is the transition matrix of a component HMM and the initial distribution encodes mixture weights. Standard spectral HMM learning returns parameters up to a global permutation of states; for an MHMM this mixes states across components and prevents recovery of per-component parameters. The paper’s main technical idea is a de-permutation step that uses spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless/block-diagonal case, A has K eigenvalues equal to 1, and A^e converges to a block structure revealing clusters; in noisy/estimated settings, the authors propose a rank-K reconstruction from the top K eigenvalues and then cluster columns to recover the permutation. They also propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K by examining persistence of the top eigenvalues under exponentiation. Experiments on synthetic data and one real dataset indicate speed advantages over EM and suggest the spectral estimates can be a strong initializer.

Relation to prior work
The spectral/HMM MoM line (Hsu et al.; Anandkumar et al.) is appropriately cited. The representation of an MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is known, but leveraging it for de-permutation is the novel step here. I am not aware of prior spectral methods specifically for MHMMs; however, related ideas (nearly reducible Markov chains, eigengap heuristics, spectral clustering of transition matrices) exist and should be discussed/cited to better ground the approach.

Quality
- Theory: The paper has a solid derivation of the block-diagonal equivalence and correct spectral intuition in the noiseless case (Lemma 2, Corollary 1). However, the central technical claims for the noisy/estimated setting are heuristic. “Conjecture 1” (rank-K reconstruction error) is unproven and borrows a sample complexity rate from a different setting; Lemma 3 hinges on a constructed longevity metric and assumes eigenvalue separations without formal perturbation guarantees. There is no use of standard matrix perturbation tools (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin sin-theta) or theory for nearly reducible Markov chains to justify the de-permutation stability or the K estimator.
- Assumptions and scope: The algorithm assumes equal numbers of states M per HMM and requires providing MK to the underlying HMM MoM routine, while later estimating K—this is inconsistent in practice. The method also implicitly relies on conditions like L ≥ MK and full-rank emission moments; the real-data experiment expands L via a cubic feature map, which changes the generative model and is not theoretically justified. The assumption that each Ak has a unique eigenvalue at 1 and others strictly inside the unit circle is reasonable (ergodic blocks) but should be stated explicitly.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are encouraging but limited (K ≤ 5, M ≤ 4, short sequences). Metrics focus on clustering and qualitative recovery of block structure; there is no quantitative parameter error or sensitivity analysis to eigenvalue gaps/noise. The real-data evaluation is small-scale and uses feature engineering to satisfy MoM constraints; comparisons to stronger EM initializations are limited. Runtime analysis is qualitative; the computational and sample complexity of moment estimation and eigendecomposition are not reported.
Overall, while the idea is promising, the current version lacks theoretical guarantees for the key step and has limited empirical stress-testing.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and the main idea is well communicated. Notation is mostly consistent, with some minor issues (e.g., switching notations early, occasional typographical errors). A more explicit statement of all assumptions (rank conditions, ergodicity/aperiodicity, dimension requirements) would help. The de-permutation procedure would benefit from a clear pseudocode and details (distance metric in clustering, choice of e-range for longevity, sensitivity to eigengaps). References are incomplete (missing years/venues). The requirement MK < L and its implications are not explained sufficiently.

Originality
Moderately novel. Viewing MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is standard; using the long-power/eigenspace structure to de-permute and to estimate K is a clever adaptation in this context. The “spectral longevity” criterion is an interesting twist, though close in spirit to an eigengap heuristic.

Significance
Potentially useful: if robust, this would give a fast initializer or even a standalone learner for MHMMs with complexity largely decoupled from data size after moments are computed. However, significance is tempered by the lack of guarantees, restrictive assumptions (equal M, L ≥ MK), and limited empirical validation. As it stands, this is best viewed as a promising heuristic and good initializer rather than a principled replacement for EM.

Strengths
- Clear and simple core idea; leverages a well-motivated structural property.
- Practical de-permutation approach that seems to work in synthetic settings.
- Speed advantage over EM and utility as an initialization method.
- Attempts to address model selection (estimating K).

Weaknesses
- Key claims are conjectural; no perturbation analysis or finite-sample guarantees for de-permutation or K estimation.
- Requires knowing MK; assumes equal M across components; needs L ≥ MK (with ad hoc feature expansion).
- Limited experiments and ablations; no parameter error, robustness to eigengap, or larger-scale settings.
- Incomplete references; some notational/clarity issues.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide a perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan) showing that the invariant subspace associated with the K “global” eigenvalues is stable under the estimation noise and supports correct clustering, with conditions in terms of eigenvalue gaps and noise level.
- Clarify practical requirements on MK and L, and whether the method can handle heterogeneous M across components. If MK is unknown, how is the initial MoM run configured?
- Compare K estimation via “spectral longevity” to standard eigengap and information criteria; include ablations on eigenvalue separation and noise.
- Report parameter recovery error and scalability; include larger K, M, longer sequences, and non-Gaussian emissions.
- Avoid changing the generative model via polynomial features unless theoretically justified; otherwise, evaluate on discrete emissions where L ≥ MK is naturally satisfied.
- Complete and update references; discuss connections to nearly reducible Markov chains and spectral clustering.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current version lacks theoretical backing for the crucial de-permutation and K-estimation steps and has limited empirical validation. Strengthening the theory and experiments would make this a strong submission.
