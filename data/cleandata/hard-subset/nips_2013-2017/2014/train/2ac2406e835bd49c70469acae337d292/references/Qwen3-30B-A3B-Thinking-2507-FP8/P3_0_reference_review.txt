

# Peer Review: "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), which are useful for clustering sequences. The key insight is that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors build upon existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., 2012; Hsu et al., 2009) and address the permutation ambiguity that arises from the MoM approach. They propose a depermutation technique using the spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also provides conditions for estimating the number of clusters K and demonstrates the approach on synthetic and real datasets.

The work extends the growing literature on method of moments for latent variable models, particularly building on Anandkumar et al.'s work on spectral learning for HMMs. While the block-diagonal transition matrix observation is novel for MHMMs, the paper doesn't clearly distinguish itself from related work such as Smyth's (2000) "Clustering sequences with hidden markov models" which also considers clustering sequences with HMMs.

## Quality

### Strengths

1. **Key Insight**: The observation that an MHMM can be viewed as an HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix is correct and useful. This insight simplifies the problem and enables leveraging existing spectral learning techniques.

2. **Algorithm Design**: The proposed depermutation technique using spectral properties of the transition matrix is clever and addresses a fundamental challenge in MHMM learning.

3. **Experimental Results**: The experiments on synthetic data demonstrate the algorithm's ability to recover the correct block structure even with limited data (T=20), and the real data experiment shows promise as an initialization for EM.

### Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper relies heavily on Conjecture 1, which claims that the low-rank reconstruction error is O(1/√TN). This is not proven but rather justified by referencing another paper (Anandkumar et al., 2012) without providing a proper derivation or justification for why this bound should hold for MHMMs. This is a significant theoretical gap.

2. **Spectral Conditions**: The conditions in Lemma 3 (|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|) are not well-motivated or explained in terms of practical applicability. The authors should provide more intuition on how likely these conditions are to hold in real-world scenarios.

3. **Algorithm Limitations**: The paper assumes the number of hidden states M is known for each HMM, which is a strong assumption in practice. It doesn't address how to estimate M or handle cases where different clusters have different numbers of hidden states.

4. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper claims computational advantages over EM, it doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis. It would be valuable to see how the computational complexity scales with the number of sequences, sequence length, and number of clusters.

5. **Experimental Scope**: The experiments are limited to small synthetic datasets and a single real dataset (handwritten characters). The real data experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation (L=10) to satisfy the MK < L constraint, but this is not well-justified.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the key concepts. However:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed description of the spectral learning algorithm for HMMs that they build upon, as this is a critical component of their approach.

2. The experimental details for the real data experiment (e.g., specifics of the polynomial feature transformation) could be more detailed to improve reproducibility.

3. The justification for the spectral longevity metric (Definition 3) is somewhat opaque. A clearer explanation of why this metric helps identify K would improve the paper.

## Originality

The main contribution appears to be the adaptation of spectral learning techniques to MHMMs through the block-diagonal transition matrix observation. This is a non-trivial extension of existing work. However:

1. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish this from related work. Smyth (2000) and others have considered clustering sequences with HMMs, though they may not have used method of moments.

2. The depermutation technique is the most novel aspect, but its theoretical justification is weak. The paper should better articulate how this differs from existing permutation recovery techniques.

3. The paper doesn't clearly state how much of the approach is novel versus a straightforward extension of existing methods.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in sequence clustering, where EM can be computationally prohibitive for large datasets. The potential computational advantage is significant if the approach scales well to larger datasets. However:

1. The computational advantage is only demonstrated on small datasets (max 100 sequences per cluster, sequence length up to 200). It's unclear how the algorithm scales to truly large datasets where EM would be most problematic.

2. The accuracy results are only comparable to EM, not significantly better. The paper claims "comparable in accuracy" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support this claim.

3. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the approach would be used in practice for large-scale sequence clustering applications.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: Provide a proper proof or stronger justification for Conjecture 1. At minimum, clearly state it as a conjecture and discuss its implications for the algorithm's performance.

2. **Spectral Conditions**: Add a discussion of how likely the spectral conditions (|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|) are to hold in practice. Provide intuition or empirical evidence supporting these conditions.

3. **Algorithmic Extensions**: Address the limitation of assuming M is known. Discuss how the algorithm could be extended to estimate M or handle different numbers of states across clusters.

4. **Experiments**: Expand the experimental evaluation to include:
   - More datasets (at least 3-4 different real-world sequence datasets)
   - Larger-scale experiments demonstrating the computational advantage
   - Comparison with other MHMM learning methods (e.g., variational Bayes, Gibbs sampling)

5. **Practical Considerations**: Discuss how the algorithm handles cases where the block structure is not perfectly clear (e.g., with overlapping clusters or noisy data).

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a potentially useful approach to MHMM learning with a clever insight about the block-diagonal transition matrix structure. However, the theoretical foundation is weak, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the significance of the approach over existing methods.

**Strengths**: Key insight about the block-diagonal structure, interesting depermutation technique, reasonable experimental results on small datasets.

**Weaknesses**: Theoretical gaps (Conjecture 1), limited experimental scope, strong assumptions about known M, lack of comparison with other MHMM learning methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper makes a non-trivial extension to spectral learning for MHMMs, but the theoretical foundation is insufficiently developed, and the experimental evaluation doesn't convincingly demonstrate the significance of the approach. With substantial revisions addressing the theoretical gaps and expanding the experimental evaluation, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, the paper doesn't provide enough evidence to demonstrate that this approach offers a significant advance over existing methods for MHMM learning.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. The claim that "the rest of the learning work amounts to factorizing a moment matrix whose size is independent of the number of data items" is misleading. The moment matrix size depends on the number of states (MK), which increases with the number of clusters and states per cluster. Clarify this point.

2. In Section 3.2.2, the justification for Conjecture 1 is inadequate. The authors cite [1] (Anandkumar et al., 2012) but this paper concerns mixture models, not MHMMs. The estimation procedure is not identical, so the bound may not apply. Either provide a proper justification or clearly state this as a conjecture.

3. The real data experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation to increase dimensionality (L=10), but the original data has L=3. This is a non-trivial transformation that could affect the results. Provide more details about this transformation and its impact on the MHMM structure.

4. The paper should discuss how the algorithm would be affected by violations of the spectral conditions (e.g., when |λGK| ≤ max_k |λL2,k|). This is a critical limitation that should be addressed.

5. The comparison with EM is limited to small datasets. To demonstrate the computational advantage, the authors should show results on larger datasets where EM would be prohibitively expensive.

6. The paper mentions "several spectral conditions" for estimating K, but only discusses one condition in Lemma 3. Provide more details about these conditions and their practical implications.

7. The paper doesn't address how the method would scale to very large numbers of clusters (K) or long sequences. This is an important practical consideration for the claimed computational advantage.

Overall, this paper has potential but needs significant strengthening in the theoretical foundation and experimental evaluation to be a strong contribution to the field.
