## NIPS Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using spectral techniques inspired by recent advances in Method of Moments (MoM) learning. The core idea of leveraging the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM and employing spectral methods to resolve permutation ambiguities is clever and potentially impactful. While the paper demonstrates promising results, particularly in computational efficiency, the theoretical justification feels incomplete, and the experimental evaluation, while thorough, could be strengthened. I recommend **weak accept**, contingent on addressing the concerns detailed below, particularly around clarifying the spectral conditions for successful de-permutation and providing more rigorous theoretical backing.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the computational bottleneck of learning MHMMs, traditionally dominated by the iterative Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. It proposes a MoM-based alternative, recognizing the MHMM as a special case of an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The key innovation lies in utilizing spectral properties to "de-permute" the estimated parameters, effectively recovering the individual HMM components. The authors correctly position their work within the context of recent MoM advances [1, 2, 3] and clearly articulate the advantages over standard EM [4]. The connection to spectral learning of HMMs [2] is also well-established.  

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of spectral methods to address the permutation ambiguity in MoM-based MHMM learning is a significant contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The method promises substantial speedups compared to EM, as it avoids iterative message passing, a major bottleneck for large datasets. The experimental results (Section 4.2) demonstrate this advantage.
* **Theoretical Insight:** The observation regarding the block-diagonal structure and its connection to eigenvalue properties is insightful and forms a solid basis for the proposed algorithm. Lemma 1 and 2 are well-presented.
* **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes experiments on synthetic data, demonstrating the impact of noise and data size (Section 4.1 & 4.2), and a real-world dataset (Section 4.3).  The comparison with EM is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Gaps:** The spectral conditions for successful de-permutation (Conjecture 1, Definitions 2 & 3, Lemma 3) feel somewhat ad-hoc and lack rigorous justification. The “spectral longevity” concept (Lλ̃K′) needs more explanation and a clearer theoretical link to the underlying statistical properties of the data and model. The claim about the bound ‖Ar − Ar‖F ≤ O(1/ √ TN) relies on citing a result from [1] without sufficient explanation of its applicability to this specific setting.
* **Sensitivity to Noise:** The algorithm's performance appears heavily reliant on the noise level, as suggested by the discussions around Conjecture 1. The paper doesn’t thoroughly explore the robustness of the method to varying noise levels in a systematic way.
* **Real Data Experiment:** While the real data experiment is a positive step, the need for a feature transformation (cubic polynomial) to satisfy the model assumptions (MK < L) is a limitation.  It would be beneficial to see results on other real-world datasets without requiring such pre-processing.  The performance relative to EM is also quite close, and the benefit isn't overwhelming.
* **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameters:** The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to hyperparameters, such as the choice of 'e' in the spectral analysis or the clustering algorithm used for de-permutation.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.2.2:**  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the spectral gap |λGK | > max k∈{1,...,K} |λL2,k| is crucial?  What happens when this condition is violated?
* **Definition 3 (Lλ̃K′):** This definition is complex.  Could you provide a simplified explanation and perhaps an illustrative example to demonstrate how it works in practice? What is the practical impact of the parameter γ?
* **Algorithm 1:**  The algorithm mentions “sufficiently large range” for 'e'. How is this range determined in practice?
* **Section 4.3:**  Why was a cubic polynomial feature transformation necessary?  Are there alternative approaches to address the dimensionality issue without altering the underlying data?
* **Related Work:** While the cited work is relevant, a brief discussion of recent advances in spectral clustering and its connection to this problem would be valuable.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments FOR acceptance:**

* The proposed approach offers a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMM learning.
* The core idea of exploiting spectral properties is novel and insightful.
* The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly superior to EM, demonstrate the feasibility of the method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the core ideas.

**Arguments AGAINST acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification for the spectral conditions is incomplete and lacks rigor.
* The algorithm's sensitivity to noise raises concerns about its robustness.
* The real-world experiment requires a significant data transformation.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** This paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem. However, the theoretical gaps and concerns about robustness need to be addressed to strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors:

1.  Providing a more rigorous theoretical justification for the spectral conditions and the de-permutation algorithm.
2.  Conducting a more thorough analysis of the algorithm’s sensitivity to noise.
3.  Exploring the performance on additional real-world datasets without requiring significant data preprocessing.
4.  Discussing the sensitivity to hyperparameters and providing guidance for their selection.
