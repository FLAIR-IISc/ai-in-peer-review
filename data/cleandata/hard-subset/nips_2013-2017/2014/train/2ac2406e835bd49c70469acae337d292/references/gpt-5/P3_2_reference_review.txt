Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by observing that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first learn a global HMM using existing spectral/MoM machinery, which returns parameters up to a permutation of the MK latent states. They then propose to “de-permute” using spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, the K unit eigenvalues yield rank-1 block structure in Āe as e→∞; with noise, they introduce a rank-K reconstruction Ar from the top-K eigenpairs and define a “spectral longevity” statistic to estimate K and recover the block structure via k-means clustering of columns. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI character trajectory dataset suggest the approach is faster than EM and serves as a good initialization.

Relation to prior work
The approach leverages spectral learning for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., Anandkumar et al.) and the standard identifiability requirement that the emission feature matrix O has full column rank (L ≥ MK). The observation that an MHMM corresponds to a block-diagonal HMM is classical; the novelty is in using eigenstructure for de-permutation and K selection. However, the paper does not engage with relevant spectral HMM literature beyond the three cited works, notably: spectral learning for continuous emissions (e.g., Boots/Siddiqi/Gordon; Song/Gretton/Smola on RKHS embeddings), observable operator models/PSRs (Jaeger, Littman et al.), and perturbation analyses (Davis–Kahan-type bounds) that would be directly relevant to the noisy de-permutation step.

Strengths
- Clear reduction of MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM and a simple conceptual de-permutation via eigenstructure in the noiseless case.
- Practical algorithm that is computationally attractive once moments are computed; plausible speedups vs EM shown.
- The K-estimation idea is intuitively tied to eigengaps and mixing rates.
- Empirical results suggest the method can be a strong initializer for EM.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Core claims in the noisy setting are left as a conjecture with a non-rigorous argument. The inequality chain around Conjecture 1 is not convincing: it mixes a crude bound (≤ 2KM) with an O(1/√TN) rate borrowed from a different model class without establishing conditions (stochasticity of Â, diagonalizability, separation, concentration of empirical moments). This undermines the main contribution (de-permutation under noise and K estimation).
- The spectral longevity statistic L relies on ad hoc choices (range of exponents e, γ threshold), and Lemma 3 assumes access to sorted eigenvalues and a specific ratio condition that is not testable or justified statistically. A simpler and better-justified criterion would be an eigengap or bootstrap-based stability of the leading K invariant subspace.
- The paper assumes MK is known to run the HMM MoM step, while K is “estimated” later. This implies M must be known a priori; the practicality of that assumption is not discussed. If both K and M are unknown, the current pipeline is incomplete.
- Assumptions for de-permutation are incomplete. In addition to each Ak having a unique unit eigenvalue (primitive chain), the method needs (i) distinct stationary distributions across clusters (otherwise the columns vk 1^T are indistinguishable), (ii) a sufficient spectral gap |λGK| > maxk |λL2,k|, and (iii) the estimated Â to be close (in similarity-invariant sense) to a stochastic, diagonalizable matrix. None are explicitly stated or verified.
- Spectral HMM estimators for continuous observations require care. The paper equates O(:,u) with E[x|r=u], imposes MK < L (enforced via polynomial features in the real-data experiment), and then cites Hsu/Anandkumar without specifying the precise moment constructions used for continuous outputs, feature choices, whitening, or conditions for O to be full column rank after feature transforms. Reproducibility and rigor here are lacking.
- The learned Â from spectral methods may not be stochastic or even have spectrum within the unit disk; the paper’s de-permutation relies on spectral properties near the unit circle without imposing a projection back to the space of stochastic matrices or using perturbation theory to justify.
- Experiments are limited and miss critical diagnostics:
  - Synthetic: no parameter error or de-permutation accuracy; only k-means total error is shown. No variance/error bars or sensitivity to eigenvalue separation, cluster mixing rates, or noise.
  - Real data: details on feature construction, MoM implementation, and hyperparameters are sparse. No statistical significance testing. Baselines (e.g., HMM-EM with smarter initializations, spectral clustering approaches) are limited.
- Writing/clarity: some notation glitches and missing details (e.g., indices M vs J; definition consistency; the construction of moment tensors/matrices and whitening).

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3 with rigorous statements using matrix perturbation tools (Weyl’s inequality for eigenvalues, Davis–Kahan for invariant subspaces). Formalize conditions under which the top-K invariant subspace of Â aligns with the block indicator subspace and bound the subspace error as a function of sample size and eigengaps.
- State explicit identifiability and separation assumptions: primitive Ak, distinct stationary distributions across clusters, eigengap conditions, full-column-rank Ō after feature mapping, and diagonalizability/stochasticity conditions (or projection schemes).
- Provide a principled K-selection procedure (e.g., eigengap + concentration bounds, stability across resamples, or information criteria on a projected stochastic Â).
- Detail the continuous-observation MoM pipeline, including feature design, moment estimators, whitening, and conditions for consistency (e.g., cite works on continuous spectral HMMs and RKHS embeddings).
- Empirical: add de-permutation accuracy metrics, parameter recovery error, sensitivity to eigengaps and to M/K misspecification, and error bars over multiple trials. Compare against EM with k-means initialization, spectral clustering of sequences, and PSR-based methods where applicable. Project Â to the nearest block-stochastic matrix and show gains.
- Discuss practicality of requiring MK < L and the effect of polynomial feature lifts on sample complexity and noise.

Overall assessment
- Quality: Moderate. Sound in the noiseless case; key noisy-case claims are not proven and rely on ad hoc criteria.
- Clarity: Moderate; core idea is clear, but essential algorithmic and theoretical details are missing.
- Originality: Moderate; the block-diagonal reduction is known, but the specific de-permutation via eigenstructure is a useful idea; however, similar spectral clustering on block structures is standard.
- Significance: Potentially useful if theory is solidified and empirics broadened; as is, impact is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a computationally important problem (fast MHMM learning).
- Simple, appealing idea; promising empirical speed; potential as EM initializer.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees; conjectural core.
- Limited experiments and missing diagnostics; practical assumptions (MK < L, known M) under-discussed.

Recommendation: Reject in current form; consider after substantial theoretical strengthening and expanded empirical validation. Confidence: high.
