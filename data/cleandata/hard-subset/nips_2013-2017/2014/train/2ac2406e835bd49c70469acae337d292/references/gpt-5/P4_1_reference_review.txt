Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method-of-moments/spectral approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation (Lemma 1) is that an MHMM with K components of M states each is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix Ā = diag(A1,…,AK), concatenated emission matrix Ō = [O1 … OK], and scaled initial distribution ν̄ = [π1ν1; …; πKνK]. The authors then apply a spectral learning algorithm for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al.) to estimate global parameters up to a permutation of the MK latent states. They address the critical de-permutation step by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, Ā has K eigenvalues equal to 1, and Āe converges to a block structure where columns within a block are identical (Corollary 1), so clustering the columns recovers the block partition. In the noisy case, the authors propose to form a rank-K reconstruction Ar by keeping the K “global” eigenvalues (those perturbations of the unit eigenvalues), then cluster columns of Ar to recover the permutation and individual HMM parameters. They further propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K by exponentiating eigenvalues and selecting the K with maximal longevity (Lemma 3).

This builds on spectral learning of HMMs and MoM for latent-variable models (Anandkumar et al.; Hsu et al.) and classical EM learning for MHMMs (Smyth, NIPS 1997), but targets a setting where EM is computationally expensive due to per-sequence forward-backward in each iteration. The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is standard, but the de-permutation via spectral clustering of the transition matrix is the main contribution.

Strengths
- Clear structural reduction: The equivalence between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal transitions is correct and provides a clean avenue to reuse spectral HMM learning techniques.
- Computational motivation: Once moments are computed, parameter recovery is independent of the number of sequences/lengths, offering a potential speed advantage over EM on large datasets.
- Practical de-permutation insight: In the noiseless case, the block structure of Āe directly suggests clustering columns; this is simple and intuitive.
- Empirical indications: Synthetic experiments show speed benefits; real-data results suggest the spectral approach is a good initializer for EM.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical gaps in the noisy setting: The core step—recovering the permutation and K under noise—rests on unproven conjectures and heuristic criteria. There is no rigorous perturbation analysis (e.g., via Davis–Kahan or invariant subspace stability) establishing that the K-dimensional eigenspace corresponding to the block structure can be consistently recovered or clustered.
- Assumptions and identifiability not fully specified: For spectral HMM learning, one typically requires Ō to have full column rank (L ≥ MK) and certain mixing/ergodicity conditions. These conditions are not clearly stated, and the use of continuous emissions (Gaussian/Poisson) with O defined as conditional means is non-standard relative to classical discrete-output spectral HMM methods; the moment construction is not described.
- K estimation is ad hoc: The “spectral longevity” measure and Lemma 3 hinge on spectral gap arguments but lack formal guarantees or robust procedures for finite samples. In practice, eigenvalue magnitudes and ordering can be unstable under noise.
- Experimental scope is limited: Synthetic settings are small; details about variance across trials, error bars, and robustness (e.g., overlapping components, different mixing rates, misspecification) are sparse. The real-data evaluation is limited to pairwise character clustering and requires polynomial feature lifting to satisfy L ≥ MK, which may distort the generative assumptions.
- Reuse of unrelated sample complexity claims: The bound cited from a mixture model is not justified for the MHMM setting and is labeled a conjecture. Claims about convergence rates and complexity would benefit from a tailored analysis.
- Practicalities not addressed: Normalization of recovered A and O to ensure stochasticity and non-negativity is not discussed; sensitivity to initialization for k-means in de-permutation is not examined.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The noiseless reduction and spectral intuition are sound. However, the main innovations (de-permutation under noise and K estimation) are not theoretically supported, and key assumptions for MoM recovery are not fully specified. The reliance on conjectures limits technical rigor.
- Empirical support: Results are suggestive but not comprehensive, lacking strong baselines and statistical analysis.

Clarity
- Generally readable, with helpful lemmas and algorithm summary. However, several important details are missing: the precise moment constructions for continuous outputs, explicit assumptions (full-rank conditions, ergodicity/aperiodicity), normalization steps, and algorithmic parameters for eigenvalue exponentiation and clustering. The “longevity” concept is non-standard and could be clarified with pseudocode and ablations.

Originality
- The reduction to block-diagonal HMM is known. The proposed de-permutation via clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction of the transition matrix and the heuristic K selection are somewhat novel but closely related to standard spectral clustering/eigen-gap ideas. Overall originality is moderate.

Significance
- If made rigorous, a fast MHMM learner could be impactful. However, the requirement L ≥ MK is restrictive in many sequence applications, and the lack of guarantees reduces immediate utility. The empirical validations are not strong enough to establish state-of-the-art performance or broad applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a perturbation analysis establishing conditions under which the K-dimensional invariant subspace (associated with the K largest eigenvalues near 1) is consistently estimated, and that column clustering of Ar recovers the block partition with high probability. Cite and leverage matrix perturbation results.
- Precisely define and justify the moment construction for continuous emissions; clearly state identifiability assumptions (e.g., Ō full column rank, mixing/ergodicity, separation conditions).
- Replace the “longevity” heuristic with a principled K selection (e.g., eigengap, information criteria) and provide finite-sample guarantees or at least thorough empirical validation with robustness checks.
- Expand experiments: larger K, M, varying L, non-Gaussian emissions, more realistic sequence clustering tasks; compare against EM variants (e.g., VB), HMM-based clustering baselines, and nonparametric approaches. Report run-times, accuracy with confidence intervals, and sensitivity analyses.
- Detail normalization/projection steps ensuring valid stochastic parameters after spectral recovery.
- Discuss practicality when L < MK; consider dimensionality reduction or multi-view constructions that relax this constraint.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem (fast learning for MHMMs) with a simple, potentially scalable spectral approach.
- Clear structural insight; practical de-permutation idea that may work well in practice.
- Initial empirical evidence and usefulness as EM initialization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Central claims rely on conjectures; lack of theoretical guarantees for the noisy, finite-sample setting.
- Limited and small-scale experiments; reliance on feature lifting to satisfy L ≥ MK.
- Modest novelty beyond known spectral and block-structure ideas.
- Assumptions and implementation details insufficiently specified for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting direction but requires stronger theoretical backing and more comprehensive empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
