

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), framed as an extension of spectral learning for standard HMMs. The authors observe that an MHMM can be represented as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, and then propose to use existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs followed by a depermutation step to recover the individual HMM parameters. The key novelty lies in the depermutation algorithm that leverages spectral properties to undo the permutation ambiguity introduced by the MoM estimation process.

The paper builds on established work by Anandkumar et al. on spectral learning for HMMs, and the core idea of viewing MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is conceptually reasonable. The theoretical foundation is largely based on existing spectral learning theory for HMMs, with the authors making a modest extension to the MHMM setting. The paper's main technical contribution is the proposed depermutation algorithm and the spectral conditions under which it can recover the number of clusters K.

The paper demonstrates the approach on synthetic data and a small real-world dataset, showing that the method is computationally faster than EM while achieving comparable accuracy. The real data experiment shows the proposed approach can serve as a good initialization for EM.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's quality, particularly regarding theoretical justification and experimental validation. The paper contains several key conjectures without rigorous proof (e.g., Conjecture 1 regarding the estimation error bound), which undermines the theoretical foundation of the approach. The justification provided for Conjecture 1 is insufficient and relies on reusing a bound from a different context without proper adaptation or justification. This is particularly concerning for a NIPS paper, where theoretical rigor is expected.

The experimental validation is also limited in scope. The synthetic experiments use small datasets (T=20-1000 sequences) with limited parameter variations. The comparison with EM is superficial, with the paper noting that "EM seems to provide higher accuracy on regions where we have less data" but not demonstrating when the spectral method actually outperforms EM in meaningful ways. The real data experiment is very small (only two character classes, K=2), and the transformation to meet the MK < L constraint (cubic polynomial feature transformation) seems ad hoc without justification.

The depermutation algorithm's performance under noise is not thoroughly validated. The paper claims the approach "recovers the correct number of clusters K = 3 even in the case where T = 20" but provides no statistical significance testing or error bars for these results. The spectral longevity measure (Definition 3) for determining K is novel but lacks sufficient theoretical grounding or empirical validation beyond the limited experiments presented.

The paper's most serious weakness is the lack of rigorous comparison against established baselines. The authors claim computational advantages but don't provide clear benchmarks showing how the spectral method scales with dataset size compared to EM or other MHMM learning approaches. The paper also doesn't compare against other existing methods for MHMM learning, such as the EM-based approaches with different initialization strategies or the method proposed by Smyth [4] that the authors cite.

The paper's significance is questionable. While the computational advantage over EM is potentially important, the paper fails to demonstrate a clear, compelling use case where this advantage translates to substantial practical benefits. The claimed "comparable accuracy" to EM is not sufficiently demonstrated across diverse datasets or parameter regimes, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that the spectral method consistently outperforms or provides unique advantages in real-world applications.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a reasonable extension of existing spectral learning techniques to a new model class, but the core insight (MHMM as block-diagonal HMM) is not particularly novel, and the depermutation approach appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing spectral techniques rather than a fundamentally new contribution. The paper properly cites related work but doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior methods.

I recommend rejection for the following reasons:
- The theoretical foundation is insufficiently rigorous, with key conjectures presented without proof
- The experimental validation is limited in scope and depth
- The claimed advantages over EM are not convincingly demonstrated
- The paper lacks thorough comparison with established baselines and alternative methods
- The significance of the contribution is not clearly established

While the paper presents an interesting idea that could be developed into a useful method with more rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental validation, as it stands it lacks the depth and thoroughness expected for a NIPS publication. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the theoretical justification and provide more comprehensive experimental results before the paper would be suitable for publication.
