### Summary

This paper presents a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The standard approach for this problem is the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be computationally expensive and sensitive to initialization. The authors' core contribution is a clever re-framing of the MHMM learning problem. They first observe that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, is equivalent to a single, larger HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. This insight allows them to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012) to estimate the parameters of this global HMM.

However, a key challenge arises: spectral methods recover parameters only up to a permutation of the hidden states. In this context, the permutation mixes states from different component HMMs, making it impossible to recover the individual models. The paper's second main contribution is an elegant solution to this "de-permutation" problem. The authors propose exploiting the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix. By performing a low-rank reconstruction using the top K eigenvectors, they can reveal the underlying block structure. Clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix allows them to identify the state groupings that correspond to the original HMM components, thereby recovering the permutation and the individual model parameters. The paper also proposes a method for estimating the number of components K from the data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper presents a bold and conceptually novel approach to a classic problem. Its primary strength lies in the originality of its core ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The central idea of viewing an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is insightful and powerful. It provides a new lens through which to view and tackle the learning problem, moving beyond the standard iterative EM framework. This is not an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different approach.
2.  **Creative Solution to a Non-trivial Problem:** The permutation ambiguity that arises from the initial spectral estimation is a serious technical hurdle. The proposed de-permutation technique, which uses spectral clustering on the columns of the reconstructed transition matrix, is a creative and well-motivated solution. It cleverly uses the very structure of the problem (block-diagonality) to solve the ambiguity introduced by the learning algorithm.
3.  **Significant Practical Implications:** The proposed algorithm offers a substantial computational advantage over EM by avoiding iterative forward-backward passes over the entire dataset. For large sequence datasets, this speed-up is a significant practical contribution, making MHMMs more scalable. The experiments clearly demonstrate this speed advantage.
4.  **Opens New Research Directions:** This work successfully extends the spectral learning paradigm to a more complex, hierarchical model. The techniques developed, particularly the de-permutation strategy, could potentially be adapted for other mixture models or models with similar latent block structures. This is the kind of foundational work that can inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Incomplete Theoretical Guarantees:** The analysis of the algorithm's robustness to noise rests on "Conjecture 1," which provides a performance bound but is not formally proven. The justification relies on adapting a result from a related model, which, while plausible, is not rigorous. A complete theoretical analysis of the proposed de-permutation step under noise is missing.
2.  **Heuristic Model Selection:** The proposed method for estimating the number of clusters K via "spectral longevity" (Lemma 3) is interesting but appears somewhat heuristic. Its robustness is not extensively validated, and its performance may depend on tuning the threshold parameter γ.
3.  **Modest Empirical Performance:** The experimental results, while demonstrating the algorithm's speed, show that it doesn't consistently outperform EM in terms of clustering accuracy. In the real-data experiment, its primary value is shown to be as a fast and effective initializer for EM, rather than as a superior standalone method. This tempers the significance of the algorithm as a full replacement for existing techniques.

### Recommendation and Justification

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

While the paper has weaknesses, primarily in its theoretical completeness and empirical superiority, these are outweighed by the sheer novelty and elegance of its core contribution. The paper introduces a genuinely new way of thinking about learning MHMMs. The conceptual leap of framing the problem to leverage existing spectral HMM learners, and then solving the resulting permutation ambiguity with a further spectral step, is the kind of creative and bold research that the community should value and encourage.

The reviewer persona I adopt prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection. The theoretical gaps (Conjecture 1) and heuristic components (estimating K) are precisely the kind of minor flaws I am willing to overlook in a paper that introduces a significant new direction. The fact that the algorithm isn't a clear winner over EM in all accuracy metrics is not a fatal flaw; its value as a much faster alternative and a high-quality initializer is a significant contribution in itself. This work is not a mere incremental refinement of existing methods but a bold attempt to build a new path. It advances our understanding of how spectral methods can be applied to more complex graphical models and provides a strong foundation for future work to build upon, perhaps by tightening the theoretical bounds or refining the de-permutation process. This is an excellent, thought-provoking paper that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
