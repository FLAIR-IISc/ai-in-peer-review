

# NIPS Peer Review: "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

## Summary

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms. The key insight is that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then leverage spectral learning techniques for HMMs to learn the global parameters, but must resolve the permutation ambiguity that arises from the MoM estimation process. Their novel contribution is a depermutation method based on spectral properties of the global transition matrix, which enables recovery of the individual HMM parameters.

The paper builds upon the growing literature on method of moments for latent variable models (e.g., Anandkumar et al. [1,2,3]), extending these techniques to the more complex MHMM setting. The work is relevant to sequence clustering applications where EM becomes computationally prohibitive for large datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and important insight**: The core observation that MHMMs can be represented as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is well-established and effectively leveraged to simplify the learning problem. This is a significant contribution to the MHMM literature, as it transforms a complex problem into one that can be addressed with existing spectral learning techniques.

2. **Practical significance**: The paper successfully addresses a real computational bottleneck in MHMM learning. For large datasets, the EM algorithm requires forward-backward message passing for each sequence in every iteration, making it prohibitively expensive. The proposed MoM approach has computation time independent of data size, offering a substantial practical advantage.

3. **Well-designed experiments**: The experimental validation is strong and convincing. The authors demonstrate both the computational advantage over EM (Figure 4) and the utility of their method as an initialization scheme for EM (Table 1). The real-world experiment on the UCI handwritten character dataset is particularly compelling.

4. **Novel depermutation method**: The spectral-based approach to resolve the permutation ambiguity is original and clever. The authors make a clear connection between the spectral properties of the global transition matrix and the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM.

5. **Good connection to prior work**: The paper appropriately cites and builds upon the spectral learning literature for HMMs (Anandkumar et al. [1,2,3]) and the MHMM literature (Smyth [4]).

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical justification is insufficient**: The most critical weakness is the lack of rigorous theoretical analysis for the key contribution—the depermutation method in the presence of noise. The authors present Conjecture 1 without proof, which is a significant shortcoming for a NIPS paper. The justification for the spectral longevity metric (Definition 3) is also not fully convincing.

2. **Restrictive assumption (MK < L)**: The paper requires that the total number of hidden states (MK) is less than the observation dimension (L), which is a major limitation for practical applications. While the authors address this in the real data experiment by using polynomial feature transformation, this is a workaround rather than a solution to the constraint. The paper should discuss the implications of this constraint in more depth and suggest potential ways to address it.

3. **Assumption of known M**: The paper assumes the number of hidden states per HMM (M) is known, which is often not the case in practice. The authors should discuss how they would handle the scenario where M is unknown or how they would estimate it.

4. **Limited comparison with related work**: The paper only compares with EM, but there are other MoM-based approaches for MHMMs (e.g., [9] cited in the paper). A more thorough comparison with alternative MoM methods would strengthen the paper.

5. **Lack of discussion on convergence guarantees**: The paper doesn't address whether the proposed algorithm converges to the global optimum or under what conditions it would do so.

6. **Insufficient analysis of the spectral longevity metric**: The authors introduce Definition 3 (spectral longevity) without sufficient motivation. They should provide more intuition for why this metric correctly identifies K and provide a more rigorous analysis of its properties.

## Critical Technical Comments

1. **Lemma 2 and Corollary 1**: The authors correctly state that if each local transition matrix has only one eigenvalue of 1, the global transition matrix will have K such eigenvalues. However, they should clarify that this is true only for ergodic HMMs (which have a single eigenvalue of 1). For non-ergodic HMMs, this may not hold.

2. **Conjecture 1**: The justification for Conjecture 1 is weak. The authors cite a bound from [1] for mixture models but fail to establish the connection to their specific case. The bound should be properly derived for the MHMM setting rather than simply assumed.

3. **MK < L constraint**: This constraint is particularly problematic for real-world applications. For instance, in the UCI character dataset experiment, they transform the data from L=3 to L=10 to satisfy MK < L. This transformation could alter the underlying data distribution and potentially affect the model's performance. The paper should discuss the implications of this transformation more thoroughly.

4. **Number of clusters estimation**: The spectral longevity metric (Definition 3) is introduced without sufficient motivation. The authors should provide more intuition for why this metric correctly identifies K and demonstrate its robustness to different noise levels.

5. **Computational complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the depermutation step (clustering the columns of Ar). For large K, this could become a significant bottleneck, and the authors should address this.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the problem of learning MHMMs by addressing a significant computational bottleneck in the standard EM approach. The core insight is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. However, the theoretical justification for the key contribution (the depermutation method) is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The lack of rigorous analysis of the noise case and the restrictive MK < L constraint are major limitations.

The paper has strong potential but requires significant theoretical strengthening. With proper theoretical justification for the depermutation method and a more thorough discussion of the MK < L constraint, this could be a strong NIPS contribution. As it stands, the theoretical weaknesses significantly undermine the paper's scientific contribution.

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper addresses an important problem with a clever approach, but the theoretical justification for the key contribution is inadequate. The authors should provide a rigorous proof of Conjecture 1 or clearly acknowledge it as a limitation and suggest it as future work. They should also substantially improve their discussion of the MK < L constraint and its implications.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. Provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the depermutation method, particularly for the noisy case. Either prove Conjecture 1 or clearly state it as a limitation.

2. Discuss the MK < L constraint in more depth, including its practical implications and potential workarounds beyond polynomial feature transformation.

3. Compare the proposed method with the MoM approach mentioned in [9] more thoroughly to demonstrate the relative advantages.

4. Address the assumption that M is known and discuss how to handle cases where M is unknown.

5. Provide more intuition and justification for the spectral longevity metric (Definition 3).

6. Discuss the computational complexity of the depermutation step and its scalability to large K.

7. Clarify the assumptions about the eigenvalues of the transition matrices (e.g., ergodicity).

8. Add a discussion of the conditions under which the algorithm converges to the global optimum.

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field of latent variable model learning, particularly for sequence data.
