### Summary

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the method of moments (MoM). The standard learning algorithm for MHMMs, Expectation-Maximization (EM), can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. MoM-based spectral methods, successfully applied to single HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012) and other latent variable models (Anandkumar et al., 2014), offer a computationally efficient alternative with theoretical guarantees.

The authors' core contribution is to extend these spectral methods to the MHMM setting. They first make the key observation that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, is mathematically equivalent to a single, larger HMM with MK hidden states, a block-diagonal transition matrix, and a concatenated emission matrix. Applying an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to this larger model yields estimates of the global parameters. However, these parameters are only identifiable up to a permutation of the MK hidden states, which mixes states from different component HMMs and prevents the recovery of the individual model parameters. The main technical novelty of the paper is a "de-permutation" procedure to resolve this ambiguity. This procedure leverages the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix. By performing a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix and clustering its columns, the algorithm identifies the block structure, recovers the permutation, and thus separates the parameters of the individual HMM components. The paper also proposes a heuristic method for estimating the number of mixture components, K, based on the spectral gaps of the learned transition matrix. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster than EM and can serve as an effective initialization for EM.

### Quality

The paper presents a complete piece of work, from theoretical motivation to algorithmic description and experimental validation. The central premise—viewing an MHMM as a single HMM with a block-diagonal structure—is sound and clearly established in Lemma 1. The proposed de-permutation strategy for the ideal, noiseless case is also technically sound and leverages established ideas from spectral clustering.

However, the technical soundness of the approach in the presence of estimation noise, which is the practical scenario, relies on several claims that are not fully substantiated.
1.  **Conjecture 1** is a central result that bounds the error of the low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix, which is critical for the de-permutation step to work. The authors provide a "justification" rather than a formal proof. This justification appears to be an informal, high-level argument that relies on adapting a bound from Anandkumar et al. [1] for a different model. The lack of a rigorous proof for this key conjecture is a significant technical weakness. It is unclear if the assumptions required for the bound in [1] hold in this setting, and the steps in the provided justification are not formally derived.
2.  The method for estimating the number of clusters K, described in Lemma 3 and Definition 3, is heuristic. The concept of "spectral longevity" is novel but not rigorously motivated beyond intuition. The conditions of Lemma 3, particularly the one concerning the ratio of spectral gaps, may be restrictive in practice, and it is not demonstrated how robust the method is when these conditions are not perfectly met.

The experimental evaluation is reasonable but could be more comprehensive. For example, the method requires the dimension of the observation space L to be greater than the total number of hidden states MK. In the real data experiment, this was achieved via a feature transformation, but the generality and impact of this preprocessing step are not discussed. An analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to the key spectral gap assumption would also strengthen the paper.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, and the core idea of treating an MHMM as a special HMM is explained effectively. The progression from the noiseless de-permutation case to the noisy case is logical. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the entire procedure.

Some areas could be improved for clarity:
*   Section 3.2.2, which deals with the noisy case, introduces several new notations (`λGk`, `λLj,k`, `Lλ̃K'`) that make the section dense and somewhat difficult to parse.
*   The justification for Conjecture 1 is hard to follow due to its informal nature and leaps in logic. A more structured argument, even if it falls short of a full proof, would be beneficial.
*   The figures are helpful, but Figure 1's caption could be more descriptive to better guide the reader through the three cases (ideal, reconstructed, noisy).

### Originality

The paper's primary contribution is novel. While it builds upon existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs, the problem of parameter estimation for MHMMs via MoM has not been previously addressed in this manner. The main original contribution is the identification of the permutation problem specific to the MHMM structure and the proposed spectral solution to de-permute the learned parameters. The formulation of the problem and the de-permutation algorithm based on clustering the columns of the reconstructed transition matrix is a clever and non-trivial extension of prior work. The method for estimating K is also an original, though more heuristic, contribution. The paper appropriately cites and positions itself relative to foundational work in spectral learning.

### Significance

This work addresses the important practical problem of scalable learning for MHMMs, which are widely used for sequence clustering. The proposed algorithm offers a significant computational advantage over the widely-used EM algorithm, making it a potentially valuable tool for large-scale applications. The speedup shown in Figure 4 is substantial.

Furthermore, the experimental results suggest that the proposed method can be used as a principled and efficient initialization strategy for EM. This hybrid approach could combine the speed and global properties of the spectral method with the high accuracy of a locally optimal EM solution, representing a significant practical contribution to the field. The ideas presented could also potentially be extended to other hierarchical or mixture models where MoM estimators face similar permutation ambiguities. The paper's impact is slightly tempered by the technical weaknesses noted above, as practitioners may be hesitant to use an algorithm whose theoretical guarantees under noise are not fully established.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  Addresses a relevant and practical problem: efficient learning for MHMMs.
2.  Proposes a novel and elegant spectral algorithm based on a key insight about the structure of MHMMs.
3.  The algorithm is demonstrated to be significantly faster than the standard EM algorithm.
4.  It provides a principled and effective initialization strategy for EM, which is a valuable contribution in its own right.

### Arguments for Rejection
1.  The core theoretical claim regarding the algorithm's performance under noise (Conjecture 1) is not rigorously proven, constituting a significant technical gap.
2.  The proposed method for automatically determining the number of HMM components (K) is heuristic and its practical robustness is not thoroughly evaluated.
3.  The experimental analysis could be more extensive, particularly regarding the sensitivity to key assumptions (e.g., the spectral gap and the `L > MK` condition).
