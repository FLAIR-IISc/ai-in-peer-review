

# Peer Review: "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in traditional EM-based learning. The core insight recognizes that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling application of existing spectral learning methods for HMMs. The authors then develop a depermutation algorithm using spectral properties to recover individual HMM parameters from the permuted estimates, along with a method to estimate the number of clusters K.

**Quality**
The paper presents a technically sound approach with a solid theoretical foundation. Lemma 1 correctly establishes the MHMM-HMM equivalence, and the depermutation approach (Section 3.2) is cleverly designed to address the fundamental permutation ambiguity in MoM estimation. However, the theoretical analysis has notable gaps. Conjecture 1, which claims the estimated transition matrix converges to the true one with high probability, is presented without rigorous proof. The justification relies on reusing a bound from Anandkumar et al. [1] without proper adaptation to the MHMM setting. While the experimental results (Section 4) are promising, they're limited to synthetic data with small K=3 and M=4, and a single real-world dataset with K=2, M=4. More extensive experiments with larger K and M would strengthen the claims.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written, logically structured, and clearly explains the key concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. Algorithm 1 presents the proposed method in a clear, step-by-step manner. Some technical details, particularly the implementation of the depermutation algorithm (Section 3.2.2), could be clarified with more concrete implementation details. The spectral longevity metric (Definition 3) is well-motivated but could benefit from more intuitive explanation.

**Originality**
The main contribution is the application of spectral learning to MHMMs, which is novel in the context of mixture models for sequence data. While the MHMM-as-HMM equivalence was noted by Smyth [4], the specific spectral learning approach for MHMMs and the depermutation method are new. However, the paper fails to adequately distinguish this from related work on spectral learning for mixture models (e.g., Anandkumar et al. [1,2]). The paper cites [1] but doesn't sufficiently discuss how their approach differs from existing spectral learning methods for mixtures.

**Significance**
The method has clear potential to significantly speed up MHMM learning compared to EM, which is valuable for large-scale sequence data analysis. The demonstration that their method can serve as a good initialization for EM (Section 4.3) is a practical contribution. However, the paper lacks concrete evidence of the computational advantage (e.g., runtime comparisons on large datasets) or analysis of how the method scales with K. The real-world experiment on handwritten characters is limited and doesn't clearly demonstrate an advantage over EM.

**Strengths:**
- Novel application of spectral learning to MHMMs
- Clever depermutation approach using spectral properties
- Well-written and logically structured
- Demonstrates potential as EM initialization

**Weaknesses:**
- Incomplete theoretical analysis (Conjecture 1)
- Limited experimental evaluation (small K, M)
- Insufficient comparison with related work
- Lacks concrete evidence of computational advantage
- Fails to discuss scaling with K

**Recommendation**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to spectral learning for latent variable models. The core insight is well-founded, and the experimental results are promising. With minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical claims and expand the experimental evaluation, this would be a strong NIPS paper.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Provide a rigorous proof for Conjecture 1 or clearly state it as a conjecture with appropriate caveats.
2. Expand experimental section to include larger K values and more diverse datasets.
3. Include concrete runtime comparisons between the proposed method and EM.
4. Discuss how the method scales with K and M in the theoretical analysis.
5. Strengthen the related work section to better position the contribution against existing spectral learning methods for mixtures.

The paper addresses an important problem in sequence modeling with a technically sound approach. While the theoretical analysis needs strengthening and the experimental evaluation should be expanded, the core contribution is valuable and represents a significant step toward efficient learning of complex sequence models.
