### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The motivation is to provide a computationally efficient alternative to the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be slow for large datasets and is prone to local optima. The central idea of the work is to leverage the observation that an MHMM composed of K HMMs, each with M states, is equivalent to a single, larger HMM with MK states and a specific block-diagonal transition matrix.

Building on this equivalence, the authors first apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the parameters of this large, equivalent HMM. However, spectral methods only identify parameters up to a permutation of the hidden states. In this context, the permutation mixes the parameters of the K individual HMMs, making them unrecoverable. The main technical contribution of the paper is a method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The authors propose a "de-permutation" algorithm that exploits the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix. In the ideal, noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to one. By performing a low-rank reconstruction based on the corresponding eigenvectors, the columns of the resulting matrix naturally cluster according to the component HMMs, revealing the permutation.

The paper extends this idea to the more realistic noisy setting, where the estimated transition matrix has only one eigenvalue of unity. The authors propose that under certain spectral gap conditions, the K component HMMs correspond to the K largest eigenvalues of the estimated transition matrix. They introduce a "spectral longevity" metric to automatically estimate the number of components, K, from the data. The overall algorithm consists of estimating the global HMM parameters, determining K, performing a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix, clustering its columns to find the de-permutation mapping, and finally recovering the parameters of the individual HMMs.

This work fits into the broader literature on spectral learning for latent variable models [1, 2, 3] that has been an active area of research at NIPS and other machine learning conferences. It extends these techniques from single HMMs to a hierarchical mixture model, a novel and non-trivial step.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is built on a solid and elegant observation: the equivalence between an MHMM and a large HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The proposed method for the noiseless case is well-reasoned and theoretically sound (Lemma 2, Corollary 1). The experimental results demonstrate the primary claim that the proposed spectral method is significantly faster than EM.

However, the technical soundness of the approach for the noisy case, which is critical for practical application, is not rigorously established. Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank reconstruction, is presented without a formal proof. The justification provided relies on reusing a sample complexity result from [1] for a mixture model, and the authors state that a full analysis is beyond the scope of the paper. This leaves a significant theoretical gap. Similarly, Lemma 3 provides a condition for estimating K, but the condition involving the ratio of eigenvalues (`arg max_K' |λ̃_K'|^2 / (|λ̃_K'+1| |λ̃_K'-1|)`) appears heuristic and is not deeply justified.

The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. While speed advantages are clear, the accuracy comparison (Figure 4) shows the spectral method is less accurate than EM in low-data regimes. The conclusion that the accuracy is "comparable" may be an overstatement. Crucially, the proposed method for estimating K via spectral longevity is a key part of the contribution but is not evaluated experimentally; K is assumed to be known in all experiments. The real-data experiment is conducted on a single dataset and requires a feature transformation to meet the model's dimensional constraints, which may limit the perceived generality of the approach.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, and the structure of the proposed solution is logical. Lemma 1 provides a clear foundation for the entire approach. The use of figures, particularly Figure 1, is effective in illustrating the core idea of the de-permutation algorithm. Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the full procedure.

Some aspects could be improved. The definition of "spectral longevity" (Definition 3) is dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation alongside the mathematical formulation. The role and sensitivity to the parameter γ in this definition are not discussed.

**Originality:**
The paper's contributions are original. While it builds directly on established spectral learning techniques for HMMs, the extension to a mixture of HMMs is novel. The primary original contribution is the identification of the state permutation problem in this context and the development of a spectral clustering-based method to solve it. The approach for estimating the number of components K from the eigenspectrum is also a novel element. The paper appropriately cites and positions itself relative to prior work in spectral learning.

**Significance:**
The problem of learning MHMMs is important for sequence clustering applications in various fields. A fast, non-iterative learning algorithm with theoretical guarantees (even if incomplete here) is a valuable contribution. The proposed method provides a significant computational advantage over EM, making it potentially useful for large-scale problems. Furthermore, as shown in the real-data experiment, the algorithm can serve as a principled and efficient initialization strategy for EM, which could help mitigate EM's sensitivity to initial conditions and improve final model quality. This dual utility enhances the work's potential impact.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel and non-trivial extension of spectral learning methods to Mixture of HMMs. The de-permutation algorithm is an elegant solution to a key technical challenge.
2.  **Significance:** It addresses a practical problem by providing a computationally efficient alternative to EM for a widely used model. The potential use as an initialization for EM is also a significant contribution.
3.  **Clarity:** The core idea is communicated effectively, and the paper is well-structured.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the noisy case, a cornerstone of the method's practical viability, is weak. Key claims are presented as conjectures or based on heuristics without rigorous proof.
2.  **Incomplete Evaluation:** The experimental validation is not comprehensive. A key contribution—the method for estimating the number of components K—is not empirically tested. The accuracy comparison with EM is not entirely favorable to the proposed method.
3.  **Limited Applicability:** The method requires `MK < L` (total states < observation dimension), which may necessitate feature engineering, as demonstrated in the real-data experiment, potentially limiting its out-of-the-box applicability.
