

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs and propose a de-permutation algorithm to recover the individual HMM parameters. The key innovation appears to be the de-permutation technique that leverages spectral properties of the global transition matrix to identify the block structure corresponding to individual HMMs.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear connection between MHMMs and HMMs with special structure, which is a useful theoretical insight. The method does avoid the computational burden of EM's forward-backward passes, which could be beneficial for large datasets. The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate that the approach can recover the correct number of clusters and block structure even with modest amounts of data (T=20). The real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories shows promise as an initialization method for EM.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising theoretical connection, the paper falls short on several critical aspects expected at NIPS. First, the core contribution - the de-permutation algorithm - relies on Conjecture 1 without a rigorous proof. The justification provided is a hand-wavy argument that reuses a sample complexity bound from a different model without proper adaptation. This is particularly concerning as the entire method hinges on this unproven claim.

The experimental validation is limited and not sufficiently rigorous. The synthetic experiments use only a single setting (K=3, M=4, L=20) and the real-world experiment uses a very small dataset (140 sequences per class). The comparison to EM is superficial - the paper claims "computational much cheaper" but doesn't provide concrete speed measurements or scalability analysis. The paper also fails to compare against more recent spectral methods for mixture models, which would be necessary to establish the novelty of their approach.

The paper makes strong claims about "recovery" of the number of clusters and individual HMM parameters, but the spectral conditions required (|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|) are not well-motivated or tested in practice. The authors acknowledge these conditions are restrictive ("if the noise is sufficiently small"), yet provide no evidence that such conditions hold in realistic scenarios.

Most significantly, the paper doesn't demonstrate that the proposed method provides any meaningful advantage over existing approaches. The accuracy is described as "comparable" to EM, but with the caveat that EM "seems to provide higher accuracy on regions where we have less data." This suggests the spectral method may only be competitive in data-rich regimes, where the computational advantage would be marginal.

**Critical Assessment:**
As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find this paper's theoretical foundation insufficiently rigorous. The core contribution relies on an unproven conjecture, and the experimental validation is inadequate for a NIPS submission. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on spectral learning for mixture models (e.g., Anandkumar et al. 2012, 2014), and fails to provide compelling evidence that their method offers a meaningful improvement over established baselines.

The paper's claim of "computational much cheaper" is particularly problematic. The paper doesn't quantify the speedup, doesn't provide runtime comparisons, and doesn't demonstrate the method's scalability to large datasets where EM would be truly prohibitive. Without this evidence, the computational advantage claim remains unsubstantiated.

**Recommendation:**
This paper does not meet the quality bar expected at NIPS. While the connection between MHMMs and HMMs with block-diagonal structure is interesting, the theoretical foundation is insufficiently rigorous, the experimental validation is limited, and the claimed advantages are not adequately demonstrated. The authors should either provide rigorous proofs for their key conjecture or substantially revise the paper to provide more convincing empirical evidence.

The paper would be better suited for a conference with lower technical standards, but as a NIPS submission, it lacks the methodological soundness and thorough validation required for acceptance. The authors should either significantly strengthen the theoretical arguments or conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing against state-of-the-art methods before resubmitting to NIPS.
