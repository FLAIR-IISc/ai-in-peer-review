### Summary

This paper presents a novel spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The standard approach, Expectation-Maximization (EM), is often computationally expensive for large datasets and is susceptible to local optima. The authors propose an alternative based on the Method of Moments (MoM). The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix of size MK x MK. This allows the application of existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, such as those by Hsu et al. (2012) and Anandkumar et al. (2012), to estimate the global model parameters. However, spectral methods recover parameters only up to a permutation of the latent states, which in this case mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main technical contribution of the paper is a method to solve this de-permutation problem. The authors propose an algorithm that leverages the spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix. By performing a low-rank reconstruction based on the top K eigenvalues and then clustering the columns of the resulting matrix, the algorithm recovers the block structure and thus identifies the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also proposes a method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of components, K, from the data. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the method's significant computational advantages over EM and its utility as an effective initialization strategy for EM.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of learning MHMMs efficiently. The proposed approach is novel and provides a principled, non-iterative alternative to the widely used EM algorithm. Extending the spectral learning framework to this hierarchical model is a non-trivial and valuable contribution to the literature on moment-based estimation.
2.  **Elegant Core Idea:** The formulation of an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is a simple but powerful observation. Identifying the subsequent de-permutation problem as the central challenge and proposing a spectral clustering-based solution is elegant. This provides a clear and conceptually appealing learning algorithm.
3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The primary motivation for this work is to overcome the computational burden of EM. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed spectral method is substantially faster, as its complexity after moment computation is independent of the dataset size. This makes it a promising approach for large-scale sequence clustering tasks.
4.  **Practical Utility as an Initializer:** The real-data experiment highlights a significant practical benefit: using the spectral method's output to initialize EM leads to superior performance compared to multiple random initializations. This hybrid approach combines the speed and global properties of the spectral method with the local refinement capabilities of EM, offering a strong practical recipe for practitioners.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** Several key theoretical claims lack sufficient justification, which weakens the paper's overall technical soundness.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This conjecture about the error bound of the low-rank reconstruction relies on a sample complexity result from Anandkumar et al. [1] for a mixture model, not an HMM. The authors acknowledge this but state they are "reusing it." This is a significant leap of faith, as the moment computations and spectral properties of HMMs are more complex than for simple mixture models. The justification provided is a very high-level sketch and not a rigorous proof. To strengthen the paper, the authors should either provide a more formal derivation tailored to their specific HMM setting or, at a minimum, have a more detailed discussion about the assumptions required for this bound to hold.
    *   **Lemma 3 and Model Selection:** The method for determining K via "spectral longevity" is presented as a key contribution but feels heuristic. The proof of Lemma 3 is descriptive rather than formal, relying on intuitive arguments about convergence rates. The conditions given in the lemma are quite specific, and it is unclear how robust the method is if these conditions are not perfectly met. A more thorough theoretical analysis or, failing that, a dedicated empirical evaluation of this method is needed (see next point).

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are encouraging but miss some key validations.
    *   **Validation of K Estimation:** The proposed method for estimating the number of components K is a significant part of the contribution but is not empirically validated. The paper shows an illustrative plot (Figure 2) but does not include an experiment demonstrating how accurately the "spectral longevity" criterion recovers the true K from data under varying conditions (e.g., different noise levels, sequence lengths, or spectral gaps). Such an experiment is crucial to validate the practical utility of this part of the algorithm.
    *   **Limitation of the MoM approach:** The underlying spectral HMM learning algorithm requires the number of observable features L to be greater than the total number of hidden states MK. This is a strong constraint. In the real-data experiment, this is handled by a cubic polynomial feature expansion. This feels somewhat ad-hoc. A discussion on the implications of this feature transformation is needed. How does it affect the noise, the spectral gap conditions required by the theory, and the overall performance? Is the method sensitive to the choice of feature map? This limitation should be discussed more prominently.

3.  **Clarity:** While generally well-written, some parts could be clearer.
    *   The proof sketch for Conjecture 1 uses simplified notation ("A is used for ĀP") which can confuse the reader. It would be better to maintain consistent notation throughout.
    *   The explanation in the proof of Lemma 3 regarding the "ratio of the two spectral gaps" could be elaborated with more mathematical detail to make the argument more transparent.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a creative and promising spectral learning algorithm for Mixture of HMMs. The approach is well-motivated, the core idea is clever, and the demonstrated speed advantages and utility as an EM initializer are significant practical contributions. However, the paper is held back by a lack of theoretical rigor in its key claims and an incomplete experimental evaluation, particularly regarding the proposed method for model selection (estimating K). These weaknesses prevent it from being a fully complete and sound piece of work as it stands.

**Decision:** I lean towards rejecting the paper in its current form. However, the work has strong potential. I would strongly encourage the authors to address the issues raised—specifically by formalizing their theoretical claims and adding experiments to validate the model selection procedure—and resubmit to a future conference. With these improvements, this could be an excellent paper.
