Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) / spectral approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The central observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M hidden states, can be represented as a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to estimate global parameters up to an unknown permutation of the MK states. They then propose to recover the component-wise parameters by “de-permuting” the global transition matrix using spectral properties: in the noiseless case, repeated multiplication of the global transition matrix reveals K rank-1 blocks; in the noisy case, they propose to keep the K “global” eigenvalues closest to 1, reconstruct a rank-K approximation, and cluster its columns to infer the permutation. They also propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K from the eigenvalue decay. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwriting dataset compare the approach to EM, showing speed advantages and comparable accuracy, and that the spectral estimate can initialize EM.

The approach builds on spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012) and tensor/moment methods for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al.). Casting MHMMs as block-diagonal HMMs is conceptually simple, but to my knowledge there is limited prior work using spectral methods specifically for MHMMs; the de-permutation via eigenstructure is the main novel element. Related lines not cited or compared include subspace identification/PSR-style spectral HMM learning (Siddiqi et al.; Boots et al.) and Hilbert space embeddings of HMMs (Song et al.), which could offer alternative ways to handle continuous observations.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing idea: reframe MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM, and use spectral structure to disentangle components.
- If successful, the method avoids per-sequence forward–backward in training and can be very fast after moment computation; experiments support substantial speedups vs EM.
- The proposed de-permutation strategy is intuitive and, in the noiseless case, correct.
- The paper attempts to estimate K from data and to articulate conditions under which de-permutation is possible.
- Using the spectral estimate to initialize EM is practically valuable and works well in the real-data experiment.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical gaps: the key robustness result (Conjecture 1) is unproven, and the “spectral longevity” criterion (Lemma 3) relies on strong assumptions and nonstandard indicators without rigorous guarantees. No finite-sample or perturbation analysis is provided for the de-permutation step, yet this is central to the contribution.
- Strong and potentially unrealistic conditions: successful de-permutation requires that the smallest “global” eigenvalue near 1 exceed all second eigenvalues of the K blocks (i.e., all local mixing rates are strictly faster than inter-block separation). Many HMMs have slow mixing (second eigenvalue close to 1), making this separation fragile.
- Reliance on knowing MK a priori. The method estimates K but assumes M is known to run the initial HMM spectral learner; there is no procedure to estimate M, limiting practicality.
- Observation model assumptions and clarity: the paper treats O(:,u) = E[x|r=u] and uses Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial examples, but does not detail how the underlying spectral HMM routine handles continuous outputs, noise covariances, or feature choices. The classical HKZ method applies to discrete emissions; for continuous outputs one typically requires feature maps or PSR-style embeddings. The paper sidesteps this by engineering a polynomial feature map in the real-data experiment, but this feels ad hoc and is not theoretically justified.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: mostly synthetic with small K, M, and a single real dataset with K=2, M=4 and fixed K. K-estimation is not evaluated on real data. Comparisons are only to EM (random and MoM-initialized); no comparisons to Bayesian mixture-of-HMM baselines (e.g., HDP-HMM mixtures), other spectral/PSR methods, or strong initialization heuristics. No robustness tests for slow-mixing components, misspecified MK, or overlapping emission means.
- Clarity and completeness: Algorithmic details for the moment computation, the exact spectral HMM routine used, and the de-permutation clustering (distance metrics, initialization sensitivity) are sparse. Some notation and proofs are terse; references are incomplete (e.g., precise venues/years). The requirement MK < L is mentioned but not discussed deeply; this can be restrictive and motivates nontrivial feature engineering.

Quality
- The core reduction and noiseless reasoning are sound. However, the main practical step (robust de-permutation) lacks rigorous analysis, and several conditions/claims are stated as conjectures. Experimental support is suggestive but not comprehensive, and does not probe known failure modes (e.g., small spectral gaps).

Clarity
- Overall readable, but key algorithmic components need more detail (moments for continuous data, handling variable sequence lengths, clustering procedure). Typos and incomplete references should be fixed. The K-estimation heuristic should be presented more clearly and validated empirically.

Originality
- The block-diagonal reduction is straightforward; the de-permutation via eigenvalue “longevity” is novel to me. The contribution is incremental in methodology but could be useful if made robust.

Significance
- Potentially useful for fast MHMM learning and as an EM initializer. However, without stronger guarantees and broader empirical validation, impact is uncertain. Practical limitations (knowing M, MK<L) further narrow applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal perturbation analysis for the de-permutation step: conditions on spectral gaps, sample size, and noise under which the K-dimensional invariant subspace is identifiable and column clustering succeeds. If full proofs are hard, at least supply thorough empirical stress tests varying second eigenvalues, noise, and data size.
- Evaluate K-estimation on both synthetic and real data, and compare to simple baselines (e.g., eigengap, cross-validation).
- Discuss and, if possible, relax the MK < L requirement. Consider multi-view constructions (stacking observations at multiple lags), subspace/PSR spectral methods, or random feature maps with theory.
- Address estimation of M (e.g., rank-selection in moments, stability criteria).
- Expand experiments: larger K and M, slow-mixing HMMs, overlapping emissions, mis-specified M or K, and additional real datasets (speech, music, human activity), with comparisons to Bayesian mixture-of-HMM methods and PSR-based learners.
- Clarify the observation model and the precise spectral HMM algorithm used for continuous data; describe the exact moments, whitening, and recovery steps.
- Report standard deviations, ablations (effect of clustering method), and wall-clock breakdowns (moments vs de-permutation).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, fast method with promising empirical speedups.
- Novel and intuitive de-permutation strategy for MHMMs.
- Practical utility as an EM initializer.

Cons for acceptance
- Key results are conjectural; lack of theoretical guarantees for the main step.
- Strong assumptions and limited robustness analysis.
- Restricted and somewhat ad hoc treatment of continuous observations and dimensionality.
- Limited empirical scope and baselines; K-estimation not validated on real data.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and practically motivated, but the current version lacks theoretical backing for its central mechanism and broader empirical validation. Strengthening the analysis, relaxing assumptions, and expanding experiments could make this a solid contribution in a future submission.
