- Proposes a novel method-of-moments spectral algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) by representing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transitions.  
- Builds on recent spectral/MoM work for HMMs and positions the method as a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMMs.  
- Key technical contribution: resolves permutation ambiguity via spectral analysis of the global transition matrix to recover individual HMM parameters and estimate the number of components.  
- Provides theoretical lemmas establishing MHMM-to-HMM equivalence and conjectures about spectral properties for de-permutation and component recovery.  
- Demonstrates computational efficiency and speedups over EM, especially on larger datasets, by avoiding iterative forward-backward passes.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic and a handwriting dataset shows promise and suggests the spectral method can initialize EM effectively.  
- Novelty is strong: first clear application of MoM/spectral techniques to MHMMs with an approach to permutation recovery.  
- Theoretical reliance on Conjecture 1 is a major concern, as it lacks rigorous proof and depends on specific noise conditions.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited and not thorough: real-world demonstration is unconvincing and conditions where the method outperforms EM aren’t fully explored.  
- Assumptions limit applicability: fixed number of hidden states per HMM and restricted observation models (Gaussian, Poisson, Multinomial).  
- Lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art sequence clustering methods beyond EM, weakening empirical claims about clustering performance.  
- Spectral conditions for recovering the number of HMMs appear restrictive and sensitive to parameter tuning.  
- Overall assessment: solid and promising contribution with computational benefits, but weakened by unproven conjectures and limited experiments; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Revision recommendations: strengthen/rigorously justify Conjecture 1, expand experiments across noise levels and dataset sizes, compare to more clustering baselines, analyze noise robustness, and discuss method limitations explicitly.
