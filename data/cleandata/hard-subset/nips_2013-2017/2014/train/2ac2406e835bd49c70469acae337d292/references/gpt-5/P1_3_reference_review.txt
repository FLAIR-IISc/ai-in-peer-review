Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral learning approach for Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M latent states, can be represented as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal (one block per HMM). The authors then: (i) apply a spectral HMM learning algorithm (e.g., Hsu et al., JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al.) to recover global parameters up to a permutation of the MK states; (ii) exploit spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix to “de-permute” and recover the individual HMMs by clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction associated with eigenvalues near 1; and (iii) propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K by examining how many eigenvalues persist near 1 as powers of the matrix are taken. Experiments on synthetic data compare to EM in accuracy and runtime; a real-data experiment on UCI handwritten character trajectories shows that the spectral method is a strong initializer for EM.

This work builds on a substantial spectral literature for HMMs and latent-variable models (Hsu et al. 2012; Anandkumar et al. 2012/2014), but there appears to be little prior work that directly tackles MHMMs with spectral methods. The observation that an MHMM is an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is straightforward but useful; using near-1 eigenstructure to reveal blocks echoes ideas from metastable Markov chain analysis (e.g., PCCA, almost-invariant sets), which are not cited and seem relevant.

Strengths
- Practical idea: Reduces MHMM learning to HMM spectral estimation plus a principled de-permutation step exploiting Markov chain eigenstructure. The block-diagonal insight is simple and actionable.
- Computational benefits: Once moments are computed, parameter recovery is independent of dataset size; experiments show substantial speedups relative to EM.
- Handles K estimation: The spectral longevity heuristic is intuitive (based on mixing rates) and works in synthetic demonstrations.
- Empirical utility: On real data the spectral method is a strong initializer for EM, often improving over random restarts.
- Clear motivation and algorithm summary; lemmas explaining the noiseless case are helpful.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Theory is incomplete. The central finite-sample behavior is left as Conjecture 1, with an informal reuse of sample complexity from a different setting. No perturbation analysis (e.g., Weyl/Davis–Kahan) is provided to justify stability of the K-dimensional invariant subspace or the success of column clustering in the presence of noise. Lemma 3 hinges on a somewhat ad hoc longevity measure; formal guarantees for estimating K are absent.
- Assumptions and identifiability: For spectral HMM learning to recover a true transition matrix up to permutation (rather than up to an arbitrary similarity transform), one typically requires strong conditions (e.g., full column rank emission matrix, suitable features, multi-view moments). The paper assumes “only permutation ambiguity remains” without stating the precise conditions under which the specific MoM algorithm used guarantees this for the MK-state HMM. This is critical, because de-permutation relies on A being recovered in the original state basis.
- Strong rank/dimension requirements: The approach requires Ō to have rank MK, implying L ≥ MK (authors enforce MK < L). This limits applicability to high-dimensional observations; the real-data workaround via polynomial feature maps changes the generative model and weakens the modeling claim.
- Spectral separability: The de-permutation step assumes that the K “global” eigenvalues near 1 are separated from the local second eigenvalues of each block (|λG_K| > max_k |λL_2,k|). Slow-mixing HMMs (second eigenvalues close to 1) will violate this, leading to failure. The paper does not empirically test this regime or provide robustness analysis.
- Estimating K and M: Algorithm 1 takes MK as input and estimates K, but then must infer block boundaries of size M to extract the component HMMs. It is unclear whether M must be known or how M is selected if only MK is given. Practical guidance is missing.
- Ambiguities in eigen-decomposition: If eigenvalues across blocks coincide (beyond the 1’s) or are clustered, eigenvectors can mix and column clustering may fail. This is not addressed.
- Related work: The connection to metastability/Perron cluster analysis for Markov chains and to spectral clustering for block-diagonal structures is not cited; these literatures could inform stronger guarantees and more robust algorithms.
- Experimental scope: Synthetic experiments are limited to Gaussian emissions with modest K, M; there is no evaluation for discrete/multinomial emissions, Poisson counts (mentioned in the model), or for challenging slow-mixing settings. Baselines are limited to EM with few restarts; comparisons to other initializations or scalable approximate EM variants are absent.

Quality
Moderate. The noiseless analysis is correct and the algorithm is coherent, but the main claims rely on unproven conjectures and restrictive assumptions. More rigorous perturbation analysis and a clearer identifiability story are needed.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Some notation is heavy; several typographical issues remain. Key assumptions (rank conditions on Ō, knowledge of M) should be stated explicitly. The description of the underlying MoM estimator for HMMs is very high-level; a brief recap of the exact variant and its guarantees would help.

Originality
Moderate. The block-diagonal reduction is known, but the de-permutation via eigenstructure and the heuristic for K are novel in this context. Connections to existing spectral Markov chain decomposition literature are underexplored.

Significance
Potentially useful for scalable MHMM learning, especially as an EM initializer. However, applicability is constrained by dimensionality/rank requirements and sensitivity to slow-mixing components; theoretical gaps limit impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal perturbation bounds for de-permutation under sampling noise (e.g., Davis–Kahan for invariant subspaces; show that columns cluster around block-wise stationary distributions).
- Clarify identifiability: state precise conditions under which your chosen HMM MoM method recovers Ā and Ō up to permutation (not a general similarity transform).
- Replace or complement longevity with a standard eigengap criterion; justify K selection statistically (model selection, scree plot test, stability).
- Discuss and evaluate robustness when second eigenvalues are close to 1; include synthetic experiments varying mixing rates.
- Expand experiments: discrete and Poisson emissions; larger K, M; sensitivity to L vs MK; comparison to stronger EM baselines (more restarts, variational approximations) and to simple sequence-clustering baselines.
- Clarify whether M must be known; if not, propose a way to infer it.
- Cite related work on metastable decomposition (PCCA+) and spectral clustering for block structures; these may also suggest more robust de-permutation methods (e.g., clustering in the leading eigenvector space).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, scalable algorithm with an appealing idea to de-permute MHMM parameters spectrally.
- Demonstrated speed advantages; useful as an EM initializer.
- Potentially broad applicability when dimensionality permits.

Cons for acceptance
- Key claims rest on unproven conjectures; lack of rigorous finite-sample analysis.
- Strong and possibly unrealistic rank/dimension assumptions; limited applicability.
- Fragility to slow-mixing components not analyzed; limited experimental validation and baselines.
- Missing connections to relevant spectral Markov chain literature.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The idea is interesting and promising, but the paper needs stronger theory, clearer assumptions, and broader empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
