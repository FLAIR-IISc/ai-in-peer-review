### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method-of-moments (MoM) algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard approach for this problem, Expectation-Maximization (EM), is known to be computationally expensive and prone to local optima. The authors' key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) as a single, larger HMM with a KM-state space and a block-diagonal transition matrix.

They leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the parameters of this large HMM. However, this estimation is only identifiable up to a permutation of the hidden states, which scrambles the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main technical contribution of the paper is a de-permutation procedure to recover the block structure. This is achieved by analyzing the eigenspectrum of the estimated global transition matrix. Under certain spectral gap conditions, the top K eigenvalues correspond to the K components. A low-rank reconstruction using the corresponding eigenvectors reveals the block structure, allowing the columns of the reconstructed matrix to be clustered to find the correct permutation. The paper also proposes a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of components, K, from the data. Experimental results on synthetic and real data compare the proposed method to EM in terms of speed and accuracy.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of treating the MHMM as a large HMM and then developing a spectral method to "de-mix" the permuted parameters is elegant and novel. It provides a principled, non-iterative alternative to EM for a widely used but difficult-to-learn model. Given the computational advantages of MoM approaches (non-iterative, no local optima), successfully extending them to MHMMs would be a significant contribution with practical implications for sequence clustering tasks.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The formulation of an MHMM as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix (Lemma 1) is correct and provides a strong foundation for the approach. The subsequent insight that the K blocks lead to K eigenvalues of magnitude 1 (in the noiseless case) is also sound and motivates the de-permutation strategy. Using a low-rank approximation based on the top K eigenvectors to handle noise is a reasonable and well-motivated heuristic.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, and the proposed solution is developed in a logical sequence. Lemma 1 and the associated proof clearly establish the core premise. Figure 1 is particularly effective at illustrating the de-permutation problem and the intuition behind the solution in both the noiseless and noisy cases.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's primary weakness lies in its theoretical claims, which are presented as conjectures or justified with hand-wavy arguments rather than formal proofs.
    *   **Conjecture 1** is central to the paper's claim of robustness to noise. However, it is presented without a proof. The justification provided simply reuses a bound from [1] for a different model (mixture of Gaussians) and applies the triangle inequality loosely. A rigorous analysis would need to show how perturbations in the estimated moments propagate to the estimated transition matrix `Ā_P` and, crucially, how this affects the low-rank approximation `A_r`. Without this, the claim that `A_r` is close to the true low-rank matrix is unsubstantiated.
    *   **Lemma 3 and the "spectral longevity" metric** for determining K feel ad-hoc. The proof is more of an intuitive sketch than a formal argument. The definition of `L` depends on a hyperparameter `γ`, and its sensitivity is not discussed. The condition `arg max |λ̃_K'|^2 / (|λ̃_K'+1||λ̃_K'-1|)` is an interesting heuristic for finding gaps in the spectrum, but its connection to maximizing `L` is not rigorously established. The paper needs to provide a more formal basis for this model selection criterion.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experiments are preliminary and do not fully support the paper's claims.
    *   The proposed method for estimating the number of clusters K (Lemma 3) is a key part of the algorithm but is **never tested experimentally**. In all experiments, K is assumed to be known. This is a significant omission, as model order selection is a critical and difficult part of using mixture models.
    *   The real data experiment (Section 4.3) is underwhelming. The proposed spectral method is outperformed by randomly initialized EM. The authors frame this as a positive, suggesting their method is a good initializer for EM. While this is a valid use case, it weakens the primary claim that the method is a strong standalone alternative. More analysis is needed to understand why the spectral method performed poorly. Did the crucial spectral gap assumption (`|λ_GK| > max |λ_L2,k|`) hold for this dataset after the feature transformation?
    *   The comparison with EM in Section 4.2 shows the spectral method is less accurate on smaller datasets. While its asymptotic advantages are clear, a more thorough investigation of this trade-off and the "crossover" point in terms of data quantity would be valuable.

3.  **Unstated Assumptions and Limitations:** The paper should be more explicit about its assumptions. The method's success hinges on the spectral gap condition (`|λ_GK| > max |λ_L2,k|`). The paper should discuss how restrictive this assumption is in practice and what happens when it is violated. For example, if a "local" eigenvalue of one component is larger than a "global" eigenvalue of another (e.g., one HMM mixes very slowly), the method would likely fail. The analysis in Corollary 1 also implicitly assumes that each component HMM's transition matrix `Ak` is irreducible and aperiodic, which should be stated.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance**

**Pros:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs, a significant extension of existing MoM techniques.
*   Addresses a key limitation of EM (computational cost, local optima) for an important class of models.
*   The core methodological idea is intuitive and well-motivated.

**Cons:**
*   Major theoretical claims (Conjecture 1, Lemma 3) are not rigorously proven, undermining the paper's technical quality.
*   The proposed method for selecting the number of components (K) is not experimentally validated.
*   The real-data experimental results are weak, showing the method being outperformed by a standard baseline.

### **Recommendation**

This paper introduces a very promising and clever idea for applying spectral learning to MHMMs. The originality and potential significance are high. However, the work in its current form is incomplete. The lack of theoretical rigor for its core claims and the insufficient experimental validation (especially regarding model selection and the weak real-world results) are major flaws. While the core idea is of NIPS quality, the execution is not there yet. I recommend **rejection** for this submission but would strongly encourage the authors to formalize their theoretical results and conduct more comprehensive experiments for a future submission. The work is promising and deserves to be published once these issues are addressed.
