**Summary:**

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The primary motivation is to provide a computationally cheaper alternative to the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which can be prohibitively slow on large datasets. The core idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a MK x MK block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this large model. The main technical challenge, and the paper's central contribution, is a method to resolve the state permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral learning. Specifically, they propose a procedure to "de-permute" the learned transition matrix to recover its block-diagonal structure, thereby identifying the parameters of the individual component HMMs. This de-permutation relies on the spectral properties of the transition matrix. The paper also presents a heuristic method for estimating the number of mixture components (K) from the data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The conceptualization of an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is an elegant and insightful observation. This provides a clear pathway to applying established spectral learning techniques to a more complex hierarchical model.

2.  **Addressing a Practical Problem:** The computational cost of EM for MHMMs is a well-known bottleneck. The proposed algorithm is non-iterative (post-moment computation) and is shown to be significantly faster than EM, which is a valuable contribution for practitioners working with large sequence datasets.

3.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the main ideas are communicated effectively. The derivation in Section 3.1 clearly establishes the model equivalence, and the subsequent discussion on the permutation problem is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's theoretical claims rest on a foundation that is not fully established.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The central claim that the low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix (`Ar`) will be close to the true noiseless version (`Ar`) is presented as a conjecture. The justification provided is tenuous, borrowing a sample complexity result from a related but different model ([1]) and concluding with the statement that "further analysis... is out of the scope of this paper." This is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission. Without a formal proof or more rigorous analysis, the conditions under which the proposed de-permutation algorithm is guaranteed to work remain unclear.
    *   **Determining K:** The method for estimating the number of components K, based on "spectral longevity" (Lemma 3), appears heuristic and overly complex. The conditions required are strong and it is not clear how robust this method is to noise or deviations from the underlying assumptions. The term "spectral longevity" is non-standard and its properties are not sufficiently explored.

2.  **Limited and Unconvincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while demonstrating the algorithm's speed, fail to provide strong evidence for its effectiveness and robustness.
    *   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The core of the proposed method relies on a spectral gap condition (`|λGK | > max |λL2,k|`). However, the experiments do not test the algorithm's performance as this condition is weakened or violated. A robust evaluation would systematically explore the boundaries of the method's applicability, showing how performance degrades as the component HMMs become less distinguishable spectrally.
    *   **Weak Real-World Performance:** The results on the handwritten character dataset are particularly concerning. The proposed spectral method, when used as a standalone algorithm, is outperformed by a standard, randomly-initialized EM algorithm. Its only demonstrated value in this experiment is as an initialization scheme for EM. While providing a good initialization is useful, this is a much weaker contribution than presented. The conclusion that the method is "comparable in accuracy" to EM is not supported by these results. This finding significantly tempers the paper's claims about being a viable alternative to EM.
    *   **Data Pre-processing:** The necessity of a non-linear feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint for MoM is a practical limitation that is mentioned but not sufficiently discussed. This may limit the applicability of the method to datasets with low-dimensional observations.

3.  **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison is primarily against a randomly initialized EM. While this is a standard baseline, the authors could have strengthened their case by comparing against more sophisticated EM initialization schemes or other sequence clustering methods. Given the spectral method's performance, a direct and thorough comparison of "MoM+EM" vs. "Random+EM" would have been more illuminating.

**Recommendation:**

Reject.

**Justification:**

This paper presents an interesting and novel idea for learning MHMMs. The approach of learning a single large HMM and then spectrally decomposing the transition matrix is elegant. However, the work in its current form is premature for publication at NIPS. The primary reasons for this recommendation are the significant gaps in the theoretical analysis, most notably the unproven "Conjecture 1," and the weak empirical results on real-world data, which undermine the central claim that this method is a competitive standalone alternative to EM.

A NIPS paper should present a complete piece of work with well-supported claims. The current manuscript presents a promising research direction but lacks the methodological soundness and rigorous validation required. The authors themselves acknowledge the lack of theoretical analysis for their core conjecture, and their own experiments show that a standard baseline outperforms their method. While the speed advantage is notable, without comparable or superior accuracy and a solid theoretical foundation, the contribution is not significant enough. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors would need to either provide a proof for their conjecture or, failing that, conduct a much more thorough empirical study that characterizes the algorithm's limitations and reframes its contribution more modestly as an effective initialization strategy for EM.
