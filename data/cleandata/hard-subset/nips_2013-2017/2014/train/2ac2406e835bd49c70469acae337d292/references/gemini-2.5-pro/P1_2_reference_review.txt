**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel, non-iterative algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the method of moments (MoM). The authors' central idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, as a single, larger HMM with KM states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. By making this observation, they can leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the global parameters. However, this standard approach recovers parameters only up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which scrambles the parameters of the individual HMM components. The main technical contribution of this work is a method to "de-permute" these learned parameters. The authors show that in a noiseless setting, the block structure can be recovered by clustering the columns of the learned transition matrix raised to a high power. For the more realistic noisy setting, they propose a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix based on its top K eigenvalues, which can then be clustered to find the correct permutation. The paper also provides a method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of mixture components (K) from the data. The proposed algorithm is shown to be significantly faster than the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and performs comparably in terms of accuracy on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The core idea of viewing an MHMM as a large HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is insightful and elegant. The subsequent problem of de-permuting the spectrally learned parameters is a well-defined and critical challenge, and the proposed solution of using spectral clustering on a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix is clever and well-motivated. This represents a significant conceptual advance over the traditional iterative EM approach for this model.

2.  **Addresses a Key Computational Bottleneck:** The EM algorithm for MHMMs is notoriously slow on large datasets, as it requires a full forward-backward pass for every sequence in every iteration. This paper offers a computationally efficient alternative whose learning time (after moment computation) is independent of the dataset size. The experimental results (Figure 4) compellingly demonstrate this speed advantage, making the approach highly relevant for large-scale applications.

3.  **Practical Significance:** Beyond being a standalone algorithm, the paper demonstrates that the proposed method serves as an excellent initialization strategy for EM (Table 1). This is a very practical contribution, as it can help EM converge faster and to a better local optimum, mitigating one of its primary weaknesses. This hybrid approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners.

4.  **Completeness:** The authors go beyond simply learning the parameters for a fixed K; they also propose a plausible heuristic for estimating K from the data. While the theoretical backing is somewhat light, addressing the model selection problem makes the proposed framework more complete and useful.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its theoretical analysis of the noisy case. Conjecture 1, which states that the low-rank reconstruction `Ar` is close to the true noiseless equivalent, is central to the entire de-permutation algorithm. However, it is left as a conjecture, and the justification provided is quite hand-wavy. It relies on reusing a sample complexity result from [1] for a different model without a formal proof of its applicability to this specific estimation procedure. This is a significant theoretical gap that undermines the soundness of the claims. The paper would be much stronger if this conjecture were a proven lemma, or at least supported by more rigorous analysis.

2.  **Strong Assumptions and Lack of Robustness Analysis:** The success of the de-permutation and K-estimation procedures hinges on a key spectral condition: a clear gap between the K "global" eigenvalues (those close to 1) and the remaining "local" eigenvalues (`|λGK | > max |λL2,k|`). It is unclear how often this condition holds in practice or how gracefully the algorithm degrades as this gap narrows. The paper lacks an experimental analysis of the method's sensitivity to this assumption.

3.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation of K-Estimation:** While a method for estimating K is proposed (Lemma 3), its performance is not empirically validated. Figure 2 provides a conceptual illustration, but there are no experiments showing how accurately the "spectral longevity" criterion recovers the true K under different noise levels, sequence lengths, or when the spectral gap condition is stressed. This is a missed opportunity to validate a key part of the contribution.

4.  **Clarity in Presentation:** While generally well-written, some technical details could be clearer. For instance, the introduction of `(ĀP)∞` in Section 3.2.1 is slightly abrupt; explicitly stating that this is the limit and then immediately introducing the low-rank reconstruction `(ĀP)r` as its practical, finite-sample equivalent would improve flow. The justification for Conjecture 1 is also dense and difficult to follow.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The authors should either attempt to provide a proof for Conjecture 1 or be more explicit about this being a key piece of missing theoretical work. A discussion on why the bound from [1] is expected to hold, and under what conditions, would be beneficial.
*   An experiment should be added to specifically evaluate the accuracy of the proposed method for estimating K. For example, by generating data with varying K and showing how often the proposed metric identifies the correct number.
*   The robustness of the de-permutation algorithm to the spectral gap assumption should be investigated. An experiment showing how clustering performance degrades as `|λGK|` approaches `max|λL2,k|` would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about its practical applicability.
*   The real-data experiment is good but limited to a single dataset. While understandable for a conference paper, testing on one more sequence clustering benchmark would provide more convincing evidence of the method's general utility.

**Final Recommendation (Arguments pro and con)**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses an important and practical problem (learning MHMMs efficiently).
    *   The core idea is novel, elegant, and provides a new perspective on the problem.
    *   The method is demonstrated to be much faster than EM and provides an excellent initialization strategy, which is a significant practical contribution.
    *   The paper is well-structured and the overall idea is communicated clearly.

*   **Arguments for rejection:**
    *   The theoretical foundation for the noisy case, which is critical for any practical use, is weak and rests on an unproven conjecture.
    *   The key assumptions about the spectral gap are not analyzed for robustness.
    *   The empirical evaluation of the K-estimation procedure, a non-trivial part of the contribution, is missing.

**Decision:** I lean towards **acceptance**. Despite the theoretical weaknesses, the paper introduces a novel and highly promising algorithmic idea for an important model. The computational advantages and its utility as an initialization scheme for EM are significant practical contributions that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The weaknesses, particularly the theoretical gap, are substantial but can be seen as clear directions for future work that this paper will inspire. The authors should, however, be strongly encouraged to address the theoretical weaknesses and expand their experimental validation in the final version.
