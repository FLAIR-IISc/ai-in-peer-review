This paper presents a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The key insight is to re-frame the MHMM learning problem as learning a single, larger HMM with a specific block-diagonal structure in its transition matrix. While a standard spectral HMM learning algorithm can estimate the parameters of this large HMM, it does so only up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this work is a spectral de-permutation algorithm that exploits the structure of the estimated global transition matrix to recover the individual HMM components. The authors also propose a method based on the eigenvalue spectrum to estimate the number of HMM components from data. The proposed algorithm is shown to be significantly faster than the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, with comparable accuracy on synthetic and real-world data.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a significant problem: the high computational cost of training MHMMs with EM, especially on large datasets. The proposed solution is elegant and novel. The observation that an MHMM is equivalent to an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix (Lemma 1) is a powerful framing. The subsequent method to solve the resulting permutation problem using a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix is the core creative contribution. This work successfully extends the spectral learning paradigm to a more complex, hierarchical model, which is a valuable contribution to the literature on MoM-based learning.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. It begins by establishing the theoretical equivalence between an MHMM and a specially structured HMM. It then clearly identifies the permutation ambiguity as the central challenge and systematically develops a spectral approach to resolve it. The illustrations in Figure 1 are particularly effective at conveying the core intuition behind the de-permutation strategy, both in the noiseless and noisy cases. The proposed algorithm is clearly summarized in Algorithm 1.

3.  **Practical Impact:** The proposed algorithm offers a significant computational advantage over EM, as demonstrated in Figure 4. The learning time's independence from the data size (after moment computation) makes it highly attractive for large-scale sequence clustering tasks. Furthermore, the real-data experiment suggests a very practical use case: using the spectral method to provide a high-quality initialization for EM, potentially combining the speed of MoM with the refinement capabilities of EM. This is a pragmatic and impactful result.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Technical Soundness and Theoretical Gaps:** The theoretical justification for the de-permutation method in the presence of noise has some significant weaknesses.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This is the cornerstone of the noisy case analysis, but it is presented without a proof. The authors justify it by citing a sample complexity result from Anandkumar et al. [1] for a different model (a mixture model) and state that "the estimation procedure is almost identical." This is not sufficient for a paper at this level. A formal proof or at least a much more detailed argument is needed to show that this bound holds for the estimation of the global HMM parameters in their specific setting. Without this, the claim that the low-rank reconstruction `Ar` is close to the true noiseless `Ar` is not rigorously supported.
    *   **Estimating K (Lemma 3):** The proposed method for estimating the number of clusters `K` via "spectral longevity" is interesting but appears heuristic. The performance of this method depends on the threshold `γ` and the ratio of eigenvalue gaps. The paper does not provide an analysis of its sensitivity to these choices or compare it to standard model selection techniques like Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) or cross-validation. The single experiment in Figure 2 is illustrative but not sufficient to validate this as a robust method for model selection.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the algorithm's promise, they could be more comprehensive.
    *   The comparison to EM in Figure 4 is limited. EM's performance is highly dependent on initialization. While the paper uses four random restarts, it would be beneficial to compare against other common initialization schemes (e.g., k-means on aggregated sequence features) to provide a more complete picture.
    *   The condition `MK < L` (total states less than observation dimension) is a known limitation of many spectral methods. The paper handles this on the real dataset by using a polynomial feature expansion. It would be valuable to discuss the implications of this requirement more thoroughly. How does feature expansion affect the underlying model assumptions and performance? What are the alternatives when `L` is small?
    *   The paper claims the method is a good initializer for EM, which is supported by Table 1. However, this is a strong claim based on a single dataset with character pairs. More extensive experiments across different datasets would be needed to solidify this conclusion.

3.  **Clarity of Details:** A few key details could be clarified for reproducibility and better understanding. The description of the feature transformation for the real data experiment is very brief. The justification for Conjecture 1 is a chain of inequalities that is difficult to follow and appears to make loose assumptions (e.g., bounding matrix norms).

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and valuable core idea. The approach of learning an MHMM via a global HMM and then spectrally solving the permutation ambiguity is both clever and practically useful. The work represents a clear advance in applying method-of-moments techniques to more complex latent variable models.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of rigor for some of the theoretical claims, particularly Conjecture 1, which underpins the entire noisy analysis. The experimental section, while positive, could be expanded to provide a more robust evaluation of the proposed method's capabilities and limitations.

Despite these weaknesses, the strengths of the paper—its originality, the elegance of the proposed solution, and its demonstrated computational advantages—are significant. The contribution is likely to be of high interest to researchers in spectral methods and latent variable modeling. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the theoretical gaps in the final version, either by providing proofs for their conjectures or by explicitly acknowledging them as assumptions and discussing their implications more thoroughly.

**Decision:** Weak Accept.
