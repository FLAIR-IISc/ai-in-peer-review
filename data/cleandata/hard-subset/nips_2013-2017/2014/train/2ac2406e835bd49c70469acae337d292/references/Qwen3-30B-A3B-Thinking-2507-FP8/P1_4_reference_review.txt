

# Peer Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

## Summary

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), which are useful for clustering sequences. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then leverage existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs to learn global parameters (up to permutation ambiguity), and develop a novel method to recover the individual HMM parameters by exploiting the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. The paper makes several theoretical contributions (including lemmas and conjectures about the spectral properties) and provides experimental validation on synthetic and real data.

The work builds on Anandkumar et al.'s spectral learning methods for HMMs [1,2,3] and extends them to the more complex MHMM setting. The approach addresses a significant computational bottleneck in MHMM learning, where EM requires forward-backward message passing for each sequence in every iteration, making it prohibitive for large datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Practical Insight**: The representation of MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal transition matrix is a clever and well-executed insight that enables applying existing spectral learning techniques to MHMMs. This is a significant contribution that bridges two important classes of latent variable models.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The proposed method provides a computationally efficient alternative to EM, with running time independent of the number of sequences (unlike EM). The experiments (Figure 4) clearly demonstrate the speed advantage, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

3. **Theoretical Contributions**: The paper makes meaningful theoretical contributions, particularly Lemma 3 and the proposed spectral longevity metric for determining the number of clusters. The authors carefully analyze the conditions under which their approach works.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments are well-designed, covering both synthetic data (to validate the algorithm's behavior under controlled conditions) and real data (handwritten character trajectories). The results consistently show that the spectral method is comparable to EM in accuracy but significantly faster.

5. **Practical Utility**: The real data experiment demonstrates that the spectral method serves as an excellent initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution that addresses a common challenge in EM-based learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper contains several lemmas, the key theoretical contribution (Conjecture 1) is not formally proven. The justification provided is somewhat hand-wavy and relies on results from other papers without proper adaptation to the MHMM setting. This is a significant limitation for a NIPS paper, where theoretical rigor is expected.

2. **Spectral Conditions**: The algorithm requires specific spectral conditions (|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|) to work, but the paper does not provide guidance on how to verify these conditions in practice or what happens when they are violated. The practical applicability of the method is thus somewhat limited.

3. **Assumption of Known M**: The method assumes the number of hidden states M for each HMM is known, which is often not the case in practice. The paper does not address how to estimate M, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications.

4. **Comparison to Alternatives**: The paper only compares to EM, but there are other approaches for MHMM learning (e.g., variational inference, other spectral methods) that could provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's relative strengths.

5. **Real Data Limitation**: The real data experiment requires a polynomial feature transformation (to satisfy MK < L), which may not be applicable to all datasets and could potentially distort the original data structure.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on latent variable models. The core idea of representing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results are convincing. The computational advantages over EM are significant and practical.

However, the lack of formal theoretical guarantees (relying on conjectures rather than proofs) is a notable weakness for a NIPS submission. The spectral conditions required for the de-permutation step are also not well-justified in practice, which limits the algorithm's applicability.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for its practical computational advantages. While the theoretical guarantees could be strengthened, the experimental results are compelling and the approach has clear practical utility. The authors should address the theoretical weaknesses by either providing more rigorous proofs or explicitly acknowledging the limitations of their current approach.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Strengthen the theoretical analysis by either proving Conjecture 1 or more carefully qualifying the conditions under which it holds.

2. Discuss how to estimate M (the number of hidden states per HMM) in practice, as this is a crucial parameter for real-world applications.

3. Provide more detailed comparison with alternative methods for MHMM learning beyond EM.

4. Discuss the limitations of the MK < L condition more thoroughly, including how to handle cases where this condition is violated.

5. Consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the de-permutation step, particularly how it scales with K and M.

This paper is a strong candidate for NIPS. The core idea is novel, the implementation is clear, and the results demonstrate significant practical value. With minor revisions to address the theoretical concerns, it would be an excellent contribution to the field.
