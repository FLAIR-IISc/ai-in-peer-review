## Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to leverage the equivalence between an MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM, then apply existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs, followed by a novel “de-permutation” step to recover the parameters of the individual HMMs within the mixture. The paper presents theoretical justification for the approach, a detailed algorithm, and experimental results on synthetic and real-world data.

While the paper addresses a relevant problem – the computational cost of EM for MHMMs – and introduces a potentially interesting algorithmic approach, several shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant gaps in justification and experimental validation, lead me to recommend rejection. My assessment is heavily weighted towards technical details and rigor, as befits the NIPS standard.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a practical problem:** Learning MHMMs with EM can be computationally expensive, especially with large datasets. The proposed MoM approach offers a potentially faster alternative.
*   **Novelty of the de-permutation step:** The idea of using spectral properties to estimate a permutation mapping to recover individual HMM parameters is a unique contribution.
*   **Theoretical groundwork:** The paper attempts to provide theoretical justification, demonstrating the connection between MHMMs and block-diagonal HMMs.
*   **Experiments:** The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous notational inconsistencies. For instance, the definition of O in Section 2.1 uses O(:, u) to denote E[xt|rt = u], but later, Ok(xt,n) is defined as p(xt,n| :, hn = k,Ok), seemingly mixing expectation and probability density notation. The constant vector 1M is defined, yet its purpose isn't always clear within the matrix multiplications. Further, the MATLAB colon notation is used without explicit explanation for a broader machine learning audience.
*   **Insufficient Justification for Spectral Conditions:** Conjecture 1, while intuitively plausible, lacks sufficient justification. The argument relies heavily on a “noise is sufficiently small” assumption without quantifying “sufficiently small” or providing a clear analysis of the noise model. The assertion that the error bound is O(1/√TN) feels optimistic without a more thorough error analysis.
*   **Weak Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while present, are not convincing. The Gaussian emission MHMM experiment shows accuracy gains of spectral learning only in the low-data regimes. This is concerning, as the goal is to offer a faster alternative for *large* datasets. The real-data experiment uses a potentially arbitrary cubic polynomial feature transformation and demonstrates that the proposed method is a good *initializer* for EM rather than a standalone competitive learning algorithm. The comparison to EM initialized with MoM from [9] is not compelling, as the benefit could be solely from the MoM initialization, not the spectral de-permutation.
*   **Lemma 1 & 2 Issues**: Lemma 1’s proof appears overly concise and assumes a direct relationship between the global and local parameters that needs more rigorous justification. In Lemma 2, the claim that each local transition matrix has only one eigenvalue equal to 1 is a strong assumption and is not always true in practice. This limitation should be explicitly discussed.
*   **Algorithm 1 lacks detail:** Algorithm 1 is a high-level overview and lacks crucial implementation details. How is "sufficiently large range" for ‘e’ determined? What clustering algorithm is used for column clustering in the de-permutation step?

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   **Scaling of the MoM computation:** The paper claims the MoM computation is independent of the number of data items. This is true for factorization, but the computation of the moments themselves may not be. This needs clarification.
*   **Sensitivity to initial conditions:** Is the de-permutation step sensitive to the initial estimate obtained from spectral learning?
*   **Generalizability to non-Gaussian emissions:** The experimental results focus on Gaussian emissions. How does the algorithm perform with other emission distributions (Poisson, Multinomial)?

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*   The problem addressed is important and the proposed approach offers a potential speedup over EM.
*   The de-permutation step is a novel idea.

**Against:**

*   Numerous technical weaknesses and inconsistencies undermine the credibility of the work.
*   Insufficient justification for key theoretical claims.
*   Weak experimental validation, failing to demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods, particularly in the intended large-dataset regime.
*   The algorithm lacks sufficient implementation details for reproducibility.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous technical flaws, lack of rigor in justification, and weak experimental validation prevent me from recommending acceptance. Significant revisions, including addressing the notational inconsistencies, providing a more detailed error analysis, conducting more thorough experimental validation, and expanding the theoretical justification, would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper, as currently presented, does not meet the quality standards expected of a NIPS publication.
