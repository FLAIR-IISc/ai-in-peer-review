Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) spectral algorithm to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M states, can be represented as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block diagonal and whose emission matrix concatenates the component emissions. The authors first apply an existing spectral HMM learner to this global HMM, which returns parameters up to a permutation of the MK hidden states. They then propose a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, the K blocks yield K eigenvalues equal to 1 and Ā^e exhibits a block structure as e→∞; with noise, they define a “spectral longevity” heuristic to identify K and construct a rank-K reconstruction by selecting the K largest “global” eigenvalues, then cluster columns of the reconstructed transition matrix to recover the permutation mapping and assign states back to component HMMs. Experiments on synthetic data show the method can recover block structure and is faster than EM; a UCI character-trajectory experiment suggests the spectral solution is a good initializer for EM.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on spectral/MoM learning for HMMs and general latent variable models (Anandkumar et al., Hsu-Kakade-Zhang). The idea that an MHMM can be represented as a block-diagonal HMM is straightforward and has been used implicitly in other contexts; the novelty here is to (i) apply spectral HMM learning to the global representation and (ii) devise a spectral de-permutation heuristic to recover component-wise parameters and estimate K. The paper cites core spectral literature, but related work on identifiability and periodicity/ergodicity conditions for HMM eigenstructure could be referenced more thoroughly, and the assumptions needed to apply continuous-observation spectral methods deserve more discussion.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive reduction: MHMM as block-diagonal HMM, enabling reuse of spectral HMM methods.
- Practical de-permutation procedure: clever use of eigenvalue multiplicity at 1 and column clustering on a low-rank reconstruction; conceptually attractive and computationally light.
- Proposal to estimate K via “spectral longevity” provides a concrete heuristic for model selection with noisy Ā.
- Experiments show large speed advantages over EM and that spectral estimates are competitive or provide strong initialization.
- The paper addresses an important practical problem: scalable learning for sequence clustering.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness:
- The core step (recovering the permutation and K under noise) relies on heuristics and unproven claims. Conjecture 1 is central but left unproved, and its sample complexity bound is borrowed from other settings without adaptation. Lemma 3 is plausible but the argument for the spectral longevity criterion is informal.
- Sensitivity to eigenvalue separation: the approach assumes K “global” eigenvalues near 1 are larger than all “local” eigenvalues (|λG_K| > max_k |λL_2,k|). This can fail when components have slow mixing (λ2 close to 1), or when there is periodicity/near-periodicity. The paper does not characterize how close eigenvalues can be while still succeeding.
- Eigenvector instability: when eigenvalues are close or repeated, eigenvectors can mix and are not unique; this can undermine the column-clustering step. Non-normality of Ā (typical for Markov chains) also complicates power and eigendecomposition-based arguments.
- Identifiability and assumptions: The method assumes each A_k has a unique eigenvalue equal to 1 (ergodic and aperiodic). This is reasonable but should be stated as explicit ergodicity/aperiodicity conditions. The assumption that all components share the same M is restrictive; it is not explained how to handle heterogeneous M_k.
- Emissions and MoM prerequisites: The spectral HMM methods assumed require certain rank/separability conditions; the paper states MK < L and uses a polynomial feature map to satisfy this in the real-data experiment, but this transformation changes the observation model and is not justified. Reproducibility suffers because the “HMM MethodofMoments” subroutine is treated as a black box: which specific algorithm, which moments, and how are they estimated?
- Recovery of π and ν: The algorithm outputs blocks of Ō and Ā, but how π and ν_k are recovered from ν̄ = [π_1ν_1; …; π_Kν_K] is not explained (e.g., π_k = 1^T ν̄_block, ν_k = ν̄_block / π_k).

Clarity:
- Many crucial details are omitted: moment definitions, estimation procedure, conditions ensuring O has full column rank, numerical stability considerations for eigendecompositions, and the choice of distance/initialization for the k-means clustering step.
- Proof sketches are informal; several key claims are conjectural. References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted.

Originality:
- The block-diagonal reduction is straightforward; the main novelty lies in the de-permutation via spectral longevity and column clustering. This is an interesting idea but currently supported heuristically.
- The contribution is more algorithmic engineering of spectral tools than a new theoretical framework; originality is moderate.

Significance:
- If robustified, the approach could make MHMM learning more scalable and provide strong initializations for EM, which is practically valuable.
- However, the theoretical underpinnings are not yet strong enough, and empirical evaluation is limited to small synthetic setups and a single UCI dataset; broader demonstrations (e.g., speech, genomics, user behavior) would improve significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous analysis (or at least finite-sample probabilistic guarantees) for Conjecture 1 and the spectral longevity criterion, including explicit dependence on spectral gaps, mixing rates, and noise.
- Formalize identifiability and separation conditions: required eigengap between global and local eigenvalues; minimal cluster separation of stationary distributions to guarantee successful column clustering; assumptions on ergodicity/aperiodicity.
- Discuss eigenvector instability and consider subspace methods (e.g., invariant subspace projection/SVD of Ā^e) rather than raw eigenvectors when gaps are small; compare “longevity” to standard eigengap model selection.
- Clarify recovery of π and ν_k from ν̄; extend to heterogeneous numbers of states M_k.
- Detail the MoM routine used (moments, computation, time complexity) and the conditions under which it applies to continuous observations; justify the polynomial feature map on real data.
- Strengthen experiments: vary eigenvalue gaps and mixing rates; stress-test with close-to-1 λ2; include quantitative timing tables; compare to strong baselines (e.g., variational MHMM, spectral multi-view baselines); report confidence intervals; ablate each step (rank-K reconstruction, clustering).
- Improve references and proofreading.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, computationally challenging problem.
- Simple, elegant reduction plus practical heuristic for de-permutation.
- Promising empirical speed and usability as an EM initializer.

Cons for acceptance
- Key claims are conjectural; lack of rigorous guarantees for the main novelty.
- Sensitivity to spectral gaps not characterized; potential fragility.
- Incomplete algorithmic details and limited experimental validation.
- Assumptions (shared M, MK < L, feature lifting) restrict practical applicability.

Overall evaluation
Interesting idea with potential practical impact, but currently too heuristic and under-theorized for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to strengthen the theory, clarify assumptions and MoM details, and broaden experiments.
