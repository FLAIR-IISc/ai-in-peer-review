Summary
This paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be recast as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, whose blocks are the component HMM transitions. The authors first apply an existing spectral HMM learner to recover global parameters up to a permutation. They then propose a de-permutation method that exploits the spectral structure of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, raising the transition matrix to high powers reveals K rank-1 blocks corresponding to stationary distributions of each component; in the noisy case, they propose a low-rank eigendecomposition retaining the K “global” eigenvalues and cluster the columns to infer the permutation and recover component parameters. They also propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K from eigenvalue trajectories. Experiments on synthetic data and one real dataset suggest competitive clustering accuracy with substantial speedups over EM, and that the spectral estimates serve as strong initializations for EM.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on spectral/MoM learning for HMMs and mixtures (Anandkumar et al.; Hsu et al.) and “spectral experts” for mixture models. To my knowledge, applying spectral HMM learning to the MHMM via the block-diagonal reduction and addressing the critical de-permutation problem using spectral structure is not standard in the literature; the K-estimation heuristic is also new. The reduction itself is straightforward, but the de-permutation step is the central novelty.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with a potentially high payoff: the block-diagonal reduction enables reuse of spectral HMM machinery, turning MHMM learning into a moment factorization whose cost is largely independent of dataset size once moments are computed.
- Clever use of spectral properties to resolve the permutation ambiguity and to estimate K. The idea to reconstruct the rank-K portion and cluster columns of Ar is intuitive and potentially robust in practice.
- Computational benefits over EM are clear and demonstrated; the method can also serve as a strong EM initializer.
- The paper outlines conditions (eigenvalue separation, mixing rates) under which the approach is expected to succeed, and provides some analysis for the noiseless case.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory is incomplete where it matters most. Conjecture 1 (error control for the low-rank reconstruction Ar) is central to the de-permutation method under noise, yet left as a conjecture and justified by analogy. Similarly, the “spectral longevity” criterion for K-selection is heuristic; Lemma 3 assumes a gap structure without quantifying noise robustness.
- Identifiability/assumption gaps: success requires each Ak to be ergodic with a unique eigenvalue at 1, and that stationary distributions across components are separable enough for clustering columns of Ar. If two components have similar stationary distributions or near-degenerate spectra, de-permutation may fail. The paper does not characterize these failure modes or provide diagnostics.
- Practical constraints inherited from spectral HMM learning are not fully discussed. In particular, requiring MK < L (to ensure full-column-rank emissions) is restrictive for many discrete or low-dimensional continuous settings; the workaround via polynomial feature embedding on the real data is ad hoc and may distort the model. The conditions on Ō (e.g., full column rank) and their plausibility for MHMMs need clearer treatment.
- Limited empirical validation: synthetic experiments are fairly small-scale; there is no systematic stress test of de-permutation accuracy versus noise level, eigenvalue gaps, or similarity of stationary distributions. K-estimation performance is not benchmarked against standard eigengap methods. Real-data evaluation is restricted to pairwise class clustering with a feature trick; broader datasets and baselines (including variational methods, Bayesian nonparametrics for sequence clustering) are absent.
- Some notational and presentation roughness (e.g., citation details, typos, repeated phrases) and reliance on block-diagonal EVD arguments that could be streamlined.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical ablations quantifying de-permutation success as a function of: (i) spectral gap |λG_K| vs max |λL_2,k|, (ii) similarity of stationary distributions across components, and (iii) observation noise?
- How sensitive is K-estimation to close or repeated eigenvalues? How does “spectral longevity” compare to a simple eigengap criterion or information criteria on moments?
- Can you relax or practically address the MK < L requirement? For discrete emissions, is there a version using count-based moments that avoids feature lifting?
- What is the computational complexity and memory footprint of the moment estimation step versus EM in realistic regimes (N, T, K, M, L)? Please include wall-clock comparisons with hardware specs.
- How does the method perform when components have different numbers of states M?

Pros
- Introduces an appealing, simple route to MHMM learning via spectral methods.
- Novel de-permutation strategy grounded in the transition matrix’s spectral structure.
- Significant computational advantages; promising as an EM initializer.
- Potentially opens doors to spectral learning for other hierarchical sequence models.

Cons
- Key theoretical claims are conjectural; robustness conditions not quantified.
- Practical constraints (MK < L; separability) limit applicability and are not deeply analyzed.
- Empirical evaluation is limited; K-estimation and de-permutation robustness not thoroughly validated.
- Missing comparisons with stronger or more diverse baselines.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning accept). While the theoretical development is incomplete and the experiments are not exhaustive, the paper puts forward a simple and potentially impactful idea—turning MHMM learning into spectral HMM learning plus a principled de-permutation step—and demonstrates promising speed/accuracy trade-offs. Given its novelty and the potential to stimulate follow-up work on spectral methods for hierarchical latent-variable models, I am willing to overlook some missing rigor and empirical breadth, provided the final version tightens the presentation, adds stress tests of the de-permutation and K-estimation procedures, and clarifies assumptions.

Confidence
Moderate.
