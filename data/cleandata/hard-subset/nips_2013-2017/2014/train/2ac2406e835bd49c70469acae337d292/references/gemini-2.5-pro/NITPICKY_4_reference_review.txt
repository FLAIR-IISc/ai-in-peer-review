### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based learning algorithm for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The central idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a KM x KM block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this larger model. This standard approach, however, recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of the paper is a de-permutation algorithm that leverages the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix. By analyzing its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, the authors propose a method to cluster the permuted states, recover the block-diagonal structure, and thereby identify the parameters of the individual HMMs. A method for estimating the number of components, K, is also proposed based on the spectral properties of the estimated transition matrix.

**Strengths:**
1.  The formulation of an MHMM as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix (Lemma 1) is elegant and provides a clear pathway for applying existing spectral learning techniques to this more complex model.
2.  The motivation for the work is strong. MoM-based approaches offer a compelling alternative to EM, avoiding issues with local optima and offering computational advantages, particularly that the learning time is independent of the dataset size once moments are computed. Applying this framework to MHMMs is a logical and valuable research direction.
3.  The paper correctly identifies the crucial problem of permutation ambiguity that arises when applying a standard HMM learner and proposes a plausible, spectrally-motivated solution to resolve it.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising direction, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of flaws ranging from critical theoretical gaps and methodological omissions to significant clarity and formatting issues.

**1. Technical Soundness and Rigor:**
*   **Conjecture 1:** The paper's core claim regarding the algorithm's robustness to noise is presented as a conjecture, not a proven theorem. The provided "justification" is insufficient for a NIPS paper; it is hand-wavy, uses confusing notation (`A` for `ĀP`), contains a circular inequality (`‖Ar −Ar‖F = ‖Ar −A+A−Ar‖F`), and appears to arrive at a nonsensical bound (`O(1/√TN)` from `2KM + O(1/√TN)`). Stating that a proper analysis is "out of the scope of this paper" is unacceptable and suggests the work is incomplete.
*   **Lemma 2 & Corollary 1:** The assumption in Lemma 2 that each component HMM has a unique eigenvalue of 1 should be explicitly stated as a condition on the component models (e.g., they must be irreducible and aperiodic). The notation in Corollary 1, `[ v̄11 > M . . . v̄k1 > M . . . v̄K1 > M ]`, is non-standard and difficult to parse. It presumably means a block matrix where the k-th M x M block is `v_k 1_M^T`, which should be stated formally.
*   **Lemma 3 and Estimation of K:** The method for estimating K is based on a heuristic quantity, "spectral longevity," defined in Eq. (7). The proof of Lemma 3 is highly informal and relies on vague notions of "convergence rates." The criterion for identifying K, `arg max_{K'} |λ̃_{K'}|^2 / (|λ̃_{K'+1}||λ̃_{K'−1}|)`, is presented without sufficient theoretical justification. Why this specific ratio? Furthermore, the experimental section provides no validation whatsoever for this method of determining K; K appears to be supplied as a known parameter in all experiments. This is a major disconnect between the paper's claims and its empirical support.

**2. Methodological Clarity and Omissions:**
*   **Parameter Recovery:** The paper details how to recover `Ō` and `Ā`. However, the global initial state distribution `ν̄` is a concatenation of `π_k ν_k`. The paper never explains how the cluster priors `π_k` and the individual initial state distributions `ν_k` are disentangled from the learned blocks of `ν̄`. This is a critical missing step in the algorithm.
*   **Algorithm 1:** The algorithm description is too vague for reproducibility. "Exponentiate eigenvalues for each discrete value e in a sufficiently large range" is not a concrete instruction. What range is sufficient? How is the clustering of columns in `Ar` performed? K-means is used in the experiments, but this should be specified in the algorithm.
*   **Permutation Formulation:** The permutation equations in (5) are inconsistent. For a state permutation matrix P, the parameters should transform consistently, e.g., `ν̄_P = P ν̄`, `Ā_P = P Ā P^T`, and `Ō_P = Ō P^T`. The paper writes `Ā_P = PĀ P >` and `ν̄_P = P ν̄`, which implies `P` acts on the left for `ν̄` but on both sides for `Ā`, while the effect on `Ō` has `P` on the right. This inconsistency is confusing. The use of `P^>` for transpose is also non-standard.

**3. Writing, Formatting, and Presentation:**
*   **Notation:** The notation is frequently imprecise. In Section 2.1, `O` is defined as the matrix of conditional expectations `E[xt|rt = u]`, but is also used in the likelihood expression `p(xt|rt, O)`. The shorthand `O(xt)` is then defined as `p(xt| :, O)`, which is inconsistent with the definition of `O` as a matrix of parameters. This overloading of symbols makes the model definition difficult to follow.
*   **Typos and Errors:** There are numerous typographical errors. For instance, `1>J` in Eq. (2) should be `1>M`. The expression `θ̄ → θ̄` should likely be `θ̄ → θ̂`. The formatting of mathematical expressions like `lime→∞ Āe` should be `lim_{e→∞} Ā^e`.
*   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of extreme disrepair. Entries are missing author initials, conference/journal names, page numbers, and years (e.g., "[3] A spectral algorithm for learning hidden markov models a spectral algorithm for learning hidden markov models, Daniel Hsu, Sham M. Kakade, Tong Zhang, Journal of Computer and System Sciences,, , , "). This level of carelessness is unprofessional and falls far below the expected standard.
*   **Missing Figures/Tables:** The text refers to Figures 1, 3, 4 and Table 1, none of which are included in the provided manuscript. This makes it impossible to fully assess the experimental results and claims.

**4. Experimental Evaluation:**
*   The experiments are preliminary. The test in Section 4.1 uses only 2 sequences per cluster, which is a minuscule dataset.
*   The real-data experiment (4.3) is limited to a single dataset and a binary (`K=2`) task. The need to perform a feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint highlights a significant practical limitation of the underlying MoM technique, which is not adequately discussed.
*   The conclusion that the method is "comparable in accuracy" to EM is not strongly supported, as the paper itself admits it "doesn’t always surpass randomly initialized EM". Its main demonstrated utility is as an initialization scheme, which is a less impactful contribution than a superior standalone algorithm.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core idea of this paper is interesting and addresses a relevant problem, the execution is deeply flawed. The work is incomplete from a theoretical standpoint, with central claims resting on an unjustified conjecture and poorly motivated heuristics. The methodology is not described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and key steps (like recovering `π_k` and `ν_k`) are missing entirely. Finally, the paper is marred by widespread notational sloppiness, a completely unacceptable bibliography, and an insufficient experimental evaluation that fails to validate some of the paper's key algorithmic contributions (e.g., estimating K). The sheer volume and severity of these issues, from foundational theory to basic presentation, make the paper unsuitable for publication at NIPS in its current form.
