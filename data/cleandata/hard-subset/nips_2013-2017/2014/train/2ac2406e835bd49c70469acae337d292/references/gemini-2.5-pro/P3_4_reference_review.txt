**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) using the method of moments. The core insight is to reframe an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) as a single large HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this large HMM. The main technical contribution is a method to resolve the permutation ambiguity inherent in spectral learning, which is crucial for recovering the parameters of the individual component HMMs. This "de-permutation" is achieved by exploiting the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix. Specifically, the authors propose that a low-rank approximation of the transition matrix (based on its top K eigenvalues) will reveal the underlying block structure, allowing for column clustering to identify the state-to-component-HMM assignments. The paper also suggests a heuristic method for estimating the number of components, K, from the eigenvalue spectrum. Experiments on synthetic and real data demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster than Expectation-Maximization (EM) and can serve as an effective initialization for it.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The central idea of viewing an MHMM as a single HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is elegant. The subsequent development of a de-permutation strategy based on the spectral properties of this structure is a novel and non-trivial extension of existing spectral learning literature for HMMs [3]. It directly addresses a key challenge in applying moment-based methods to more complex hierarchical models.

2.  **Significance and Practicality:** Learning MHMMs via EM can be computationally prohibitive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. This paper offers a principled, non-iterative alternative that is computationally efficient, with a runtime independent of the dataset size after moment computation. Its utility as a fast and effective initializer for EM, as shown in the real-data experiment, is a significant practical contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The derivation in Section 3.1, establishing the equivalence between an MHMM and a special HMM, is clear and foundational for the rest of the work. The motivation for the de-permutation algorithm is well-explained, particularly the intuition in the noiseless case (Corollary 1).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's primary weakness lies in the theoretical support for its claims, especially in the noisy case.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** This is a central result underpinning the noisy algorithm, yet it is presented as a conjecture with a very loose justification. The justification hand-waves the error bound by borrowing from a related but different model [1] and uses imprecise arguments (e.g., triangle inequality steps are not fully detailed, notation is confusingly switched). For a top-tier conference like NIPS, a more formal proof or a much more thorough and honest discussion of why this is a conjecture is required.
    *   **Lemma 3 and "Spectral Longevity":** The method for determining K is presented as a lemma but appears highly heuristic. The "spectral longevity" metric in Equation (7) is complex, and its robustness is not analyzed. How is γ chosen? The proof sketch relies on the ratio of spectral gaps, which is intuitive, but does not constitute a rigorous proof of the stated claim. The conditions under which this method is guaranteed to work are not formally established.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:**
    *   **Estimating K:** A method for estimating the number of clusters K is proposed (Lemma 3), but it is never tested in the experiments. All experiments are conducted with a known K. This is a major omission, as model selection is a critical and difficult part of using mixture models in practice.
    *   **Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with EM is somewhat weak. Using only 4-5 random restarts for EM may not be sufficient to find a good local optimum. The fact that the spectral method is often less accurate than randomly initialized EM (Table 1) tempers the claims of its superiority, reducing its role to primarily an initialization scheme.
    *   **Practical Constraints:** The real-data experiment required a feature transformation to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint of the underlying spectral algorithm. This is a significant practical limitation of tensor-based methods that is not adequately discussed. The paper should address how the method performs when this condition is not met or cannot be easily engineered.

3.  **Unclear Assumptions:** The core condition for the noisy algorithm to work is `|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|`, which means the "global" eigenvalues corresponding to the K components must be larger in magnitude than any of the "local" eigenvalues (beyond the stationary one) within any component. This is a strong assumption on the mixing rates of the underlying HMMs. There is no discussion of when this condition is likely to hold in practice or how to verify it.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and elegant approach to a well-known problem.
*   The idea of spectral de-permutation for block-structured models is a valuable contribution that could inspire further work.
*   The method is computationally efficient and provides a practical alternative/initializer for the widely used EM algorithm.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical foundations are shaky, with a key result presented as an unproven conjecture and another as a heuristic lemma.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete; most notably, the proposed method for determining the number of components (K) is not evaluated at all.
*   The claims of superior accuracy over EM are not strongly supported by the experiments, especially on real data.

**Recommendation:**

Leaning Reject.

While the core idea is creative and promising, the lack of theoretical rigor for the noisy case and the incomplete experimental validation (particularly the absence of tests for the K-estimation method) prevent this paper from being a complete and sound contribution in its current form. For the paper to be acceptable for NIPS, the authors would need to either provide formal proofs for their theoretical claims (especially Conjecture 1) or significantly tone them down and reframe them as well-motivated heuristics. Crucially, they must provide experimental evidence that their proposed method for selecting K actually works. Strengthening the experimental comparison against EM would also be necessary.
