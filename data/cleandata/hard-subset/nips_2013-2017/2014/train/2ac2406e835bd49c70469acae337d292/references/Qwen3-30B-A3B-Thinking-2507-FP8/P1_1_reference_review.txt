

# Peer Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in the traditional Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. This allows the authors to leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, but they must address the permutation ambiguity that arises when recovering the individual HMM components.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) establishing the equivalence between MHMM and a structured HMM; (2) proposing a spectral approach to estimate a de-permutation mapping that recovers the individual HMM parameters; and (3) providing conditions under which the number of HMM clusters can be estimated from the spectral properties of the transition matrix. The authors demonstrate through experiments that their method is computationally more efficient than EM while maintaining comparable accuracy, and that it can serve as an effective initialization for EM.

This work builds upon the growing literature on method of moments approaches for latent variable models, particularly following the spectral learning framework for HMMs introduced by Hsu, Kakade, and Zhang (2012) and the mixture model work by Anandkumar et al. (2012). It extends these techniques to the more complex MHMM setting, which has been primarily addressed via EM in previous work.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The main theoretical contributions (Lemma 1, Lemma 2, and Lemma 3) are well-motivated and correctly establish the key insights. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly described and logically follows from the theoretical developments.

However, there are some notable theoretical gaps. The justification for Conjecture 1 is incomplete and relies on a citation to a different model (mixture models), rather than providing a proper analysis for MHMMs. The authors acknowledge this as a conjecture, but the proof sketch is too hand-wavy to be convincing. Specifically, the claim that ‖Ar - Ar‖F ≤ O(1/√TN) is not properly supported by the cited reference, which addresses a different model.

The experimental section is well-designed and provides evidence for the main claims. The synthetic data experiments demonstrate the computational advantages over EM, and the real data experiment shows the method can serve as a good initialization for EM. However, the real data experiment requires a cubic polynomial feature transformation to satisfy the condition MK < L, which is not thoroughly justified and might be considered an ad-hoc solution.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the main ideas are presented in a logical flow. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the key concepts, particularly the spectral properties of the transition matrix.

The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and precise, making it easy for readers to understand the proposed method. The paper also provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the experiments, though some implementation details (e.g., the specific clustering algorithm used in the depermutation step) could be elaborated.

One minor issue is the definition of "spectral longevity" (Definition 3), which is complex and not well-motivated. A more intuitive explanation or justification for why this quantity is useful would improve clarity.

## Originality

The core insight of viewing MHMM as a structured HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix is novel and well-executed. While spectral learning for HMMs has been extensively studied, applying it to MHMMs with the permutation ambiguity problem is a new contribution.

The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous approaches (primarily EM-based methods for MHMMs) and provide a thorough review of related work. They cite relevant papers, including the foundational work by Smyth on clustering sequences with HMMs and the spectral learning work by Hsu et al.

The combination of existing spectral learning techniques with a novel approach to handle permutation ambiguity represents a meaningful advancement in the field. The paper's approach is not merely a straightforward application of previous methods but requires new theoretical insights to address the MHMM-specific challenges.

## Significance

The proposed method addresses a genuine computational challenge in MHMM learning, which is significant for applications involving large-scale sequence clustering. The paper demonstrates that the spectral approach is substantially faster than EM while maintaining comparable accuracy, which could enable MHMMs to be applied to larger datasets than previously feasible.

The finding that the spectral method can serve as a good initialization for EM is particularly valuable, as it directly addresses a key weakness of EM (sensitivity to initialization). This practical contribution has the potential to improve real-world applications of MHMMs.

The paper advances the state of the art in spectral learning for latent variable models by extending it to a more complex model class (MHMMs) and providing new theoretical insights about handling permutation ambiguity. While not a breakthrough in the most fundamental sense, it represents a meaningful incremental advance that should be useful to practitioners and researchers working with sequence clustering.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

### Strengths:
- Elegant theoretical insight connecting MHMM to structured HMM
- Clear and well-organized presentation of the method
- Demonstrated computational advantages over EM in experiments
- Practical contribution of providing a good initialization for EM
- Well-designed experiments with appropriate comparisons
- Good connection to existing spectral learning literature

### Weaknesses:
- Theoretical justification for Conjecture 1 is incomplete and hand-wavy
- Spectral longevity definition (Definition 3) is complex without clear motivation
- Real data experiment requires an ad-hoc feature transformation
- Limited discussion of the method's limitations and failure cases
- Comparison with EM is somewhat limited to synthetic data and one real dataset

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to an important problem in sequence clustering. The theoretical insights are sound (with the exception of the incomplete justification for Conjecture 1), the method is clearly explained, and the experimental results demonstrate significant practical value.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of spectral learning for latent variable models, extending the approach to a more complex model class with practical implications. While there are some theoretical gaps, these are acknowledged by the authors as conjectures, and the main contributions remain valuable and well-supported by the experimental results.

The authors could strengthen the paper by providing a more complete justification for Conjecture 1, or at least clarifying the limitations of the current justification. They should also better motivate the spectral longevity definition and explain why it's the right quantity to use for estimating K.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Strengthen the justification for Conjecture 1. Either provide a more rigorous proof or clarify the limitations of the current justification and the conditions under which it holds.

2. Provide a more intuitive explanation for the "spectral longevity" concept (Definition 3) and why it's useful for estimating K. Consider including a simple example to illustrate this concept.

3. Better justify the cubic polynomial feature transformation used in the real data experiment. Explain why this transformation is necessary and whether it could be avoided with a different approach.

4. Add a brief discussion of the method's limitations, particularly regarding the spectral conditions required for the depermutation step to work well.

5. Consider expanding the comparison with EM to include more datasets or more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the two methods.

## Final Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of latent variable model learning by extending spectral methods to MHMMs. The proposed approach addresses a real computational challenge in this domain and offers a practical alternative to EM. While there are some theoretical gaps, the paper's strengths in clarity, practical significance, and well-executed experiments outweigh these weaknesses. The work is technically sound, well-written, and represents a meaningful advance in the state of the art for MHMM learning.
