Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral approach to learn Mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs). Key idea: an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) can be rewritten as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. The authors apply existing spectral HMM estimation to recover the global parameters up to a permutation of states. They then propose to “de-permute” by exploiting spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, each block contributes an eigenvalue equal to 1, and powers of the transition matrix yield K rank-1 blocks reflecting stationary distributions; in the noisy case, they keep the K “largest” eigenvalues (assumed to be near 1) and cluster columns of a rank-K reconstruction to recover the block structure. They also propose a heuristic “spectral longevity” criterion to estimate K. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI trajectory dataset suggest comparable clustering accuracy to EM with better speed, and usefulness as an EM initializer.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing reduction of MHMM learning to HMM learning via a block-diagonal construction; the observation is useful and relevant.
- Practical de-permutation heuristic that leverages eigen-structure; simple to implement.
- Attempts to estimate K from eigenvalues rather than assuming it is known.
- Empirical results indicate speed advantages and that MoM can serve as a good initializer for EM.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Technical correctness/quality
- Fundamental inconsistency in the HMM likelihood notation: Eq. (1) uses diag(p(xt|:,O)) and later defines O(:,u)=E[xt|rt=u] for Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial. For continuous emissions, O(:,u)=E[xt|rt=u] is not p(xt|rt=u); plugging expectations into diag(p(xt|:)) is incorrect. The product 1>M ∏t A diag(O(xt)) ν is the discrete-HMM likelihood, not valid with Gaussian emissions unless you explicitly define an observable operator that equals the conditional density. This conflation undermines Sections 2.1 and 2.2 and leaves unclear what MoM moments are actually computed. Please clarify the observation operator, features, and the exact moments used (as in Hsu et al. 2012).
- The de-permutation theory in Section 3.2 is largely heuristic. “Conjecture 1” is central and unproven; the bound borrows sample complexity from a different setting without justification. The separation assumption |λG_K| > max_k |λL_2,k| is introduced ad hoc, with no perturbation analysis (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) to control subspace/eigenvector error or to justify that the K near-1 eigenvalues remain separated from local modes under estimation noise.
- The block diagonalization relies on each Ak having a unique eigenvalue equal to 1. This excludes reducible or periodic chains (common in practice). No discussion of aperiodicity/ergodicity assumptions, diagonalizability of Ak, or complex eigenvalues. If some Ak share identical spectra or stationary distributions, column clustering on Ar can fail; no separation conditions are provided.
- K-estimation via “spectral longevity” L uses arbitrary thresholding [> 1−γ], depends on chosen exponent range, and is sensitive to noise. No analysis of its robustness, consistency, or choice of γ and exponent grid. Also, the ratio argmax criterion uses neighboring eigenvalues; ties/near-ties not addressed.
- The permutation recovery clusters columns of Ar with k-means. No guarantees or initialization strategy; k-means can be unstable, especially when columns v̄k are close.
- Complexity claims: spectral MoM time is “independent of the amount of data” once moments are computed, but the eigendecomposition on an MK×MK matrix is O((MK)^3) and clustering is O((MK)K). For large K and M this can dominate; no complexity vs EM discussion under realistic regimes.
- Requirement MK < L (for MoM identifiability) is used in the real-data experiment, but not discussed theoretically; typical spectral HMM methods require observation feature dimension ≥ hidden dimension for certain invertibility/whitening steps. This constraint severely limits applicability and should be made explicit with conditions.

Clarity and presentation
- Several key steps are underspecified: which moments are used, how they are estimated (order, tensors vs matrices), whitening/projection steps, and how permutation P arises from the HMM MoM routine. Algorithm 1 omits crucial hyperparameters (range of exponents, γ, k-means init, stopping).
- Figures 1–4 are referenced but not shown; metrics like “total error (from centroid to point)” are undefined. Without plots/values, results are hard to assess.
- Notation is inconsistent: O(xt) denotes p(xt|:) in Eq. (1) but later O(:,u)=E[xt|rt=u]. “1>M” vs “1>M” spacing, use of “>” as transpose is nonstandard. Footnote marker “1” in Section 2.2 without a footnote.
- References are incomplete (years missing, duplicated title for Hsu et al.), and related spectral work is thin. There is substantial literature on spectral learning of HMMs/mixtures/graph partitioning that should be discussed.

Originality
- The reduction of MHMM to block-diagonal HMM is known/straightforward. The novelty lies in the de-permutation heuristic via rank-K eigen-reconstruction and the longevity heuristic. These are incremental and largely heuristic without theory. Positioning relative to prior spectral clustering/block model literature is missing.

Significance
- If made rigorous, a fast, provable MHMM learner would be valuable. As presented, the lack of solid guarantees, reliance on restrictive conditions (unique eigenvalue 1, MK<L), and fragile K-estimation reduce practical impact. Real-data improvements are modest and confounded by the feature expansion.

Empirical evaluation
- Synthetic experiments are small-scale (2 sequences per cluster in 4.1; limited ranges). No stress tests on overlapping components, varying spectral gaps, different M across clusters, or misspecification.
- Real-data experiment uses cubic feature mapping to satisfy MK<L; unclear whether EM is run on the same transformed features (fairness). Hyperparameters, runtime numbers, and standard deviations are missing.

Pros
- Clear, simple idea; practical de-permutation pipeline.
- Potential speed gains; good EM initialization.

Cons
- Core likelihood/moment notation inconsistency (probability vs expectation) for continuous emissions.
- Central claims are heuristic; no rigorous perturbation/sample complexity analysis.
- Fragile K-estimation and k-means-based de-permutation without guarantees.
- Important assumptions/restrictions not stated or justified (ergodicity, diagonalizability, MK<L).
- Incomplete experiments; missing figures; formatting and citation issues.

Questions for authors
1. Precisely define O(xt) and the moments used for MoM in the continuous emission case. How does Eq. (1) hold for Gaussian/Poisson?
2. Provide conditions (spectral gap, separation of stationary distributions) under which k-means on Ar recovers the permutation with high probability; can you give a Davis–Kahan style bound?
3. How are γ and the exponent range chosen for longevity? Is K-estimation consistent?
4. What is the computational complexity in MK and L, and where is the crossover vs EM?
5. Can the method handle different M across components, non-ergodic/periodic Ak, or identical stationary distributions?

Recommendation: Reject
While the direction is promising and the empirical heuristics are interesting, the paper contains foundational notation/likelihood inconsistencies, relies on unproven conjectures for its main claims, and lacks clarity and rigorous guarantees. The experimental evidence is insufficient to compensate. Strengthening the theoretical analysis, clarifying the observation operator/moments, and expanding evaluation would substantially improve the work.
