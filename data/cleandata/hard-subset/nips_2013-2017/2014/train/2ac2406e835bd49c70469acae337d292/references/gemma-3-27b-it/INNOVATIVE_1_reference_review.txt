This paper presents a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via a spectral method rooted in the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea – leveraging the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM’s global transition matrix – is clever and potentially impactful, sidestepping the computational burden of traditional EM-based approaches. The connection to spectral learning for HMMs is well-articulated, and the attempt to address the permutation ambiguity is particularly interesting. This is a bold approach that, if successful, could significantly simplify MHMM learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The MoM-based approach for MHMMs is a genuinely new direction. While MoM has been explored for simpler latent variable models, extending it to MHMMs presents a non-trivial challenge, and this paper takes a significant step in that direction.
*   **Potential for Scalability:** The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of EM for large datasets and aim to address it with a method whose complexity is independent of the data size.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The lemma establishing the MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm. The spectral analysis, even if relying on conjectures, offers a plausible path toward parameter recovery.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes experiments demonstrating the feasibility of the approach and its potential benefits in terms of speed. The inclusion of a real-world dataset, even with a feature transformation, adds credibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Conjectural Nature:** Key parts of the algorithm rely on conjectures about spectral properties, particularly regarding the separation of eigenvalues under noisy conditions. While the justification is reasonable, a more rigorous analysis would strengthen the paper considerably.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while a good start, are somewhat limited in scope. The results on the real dataset are not conclusive (the approach serves as a good initialization for EM, but doesn’t consistently outperform it), and the sensitivity to noise isn't thoroughly investigated.
*   **Assumptions:** The algorithm relies on the assumption that individual HMMs have a single dominant eigenvalue of 1. While common, this isn't universally true and might limit the applicability of the method.
*   **Clarity of Spectral Conditions:** The conditions described in Definitions 1-3 for reliable depermutation and cluster number estimation feel somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more intuitive explanation. While mathematically precise, the practical implications are not immediately clear.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Significant novelty and a promising new direction for MHMM learning.
*   Potential to address the scalability issues of EM.
*   Sound theoretical grounding, even with some reliance on conjectures.

**Con:**

*   Algorithm relies heavily on unproven conjectures.
*   Empirical validation is limited and doesn't definitively demonstrate superiority over existing methods.
*   Assumptions about the model structure may restrict applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution. The core idea is genuinely novel and the potential benefits are significant. While the current implementation and validation are not perfect, the paper introduces a compelling new research direction. The reliance on conjectures is a concern, but the authors demonstrate sufficient insight and preliminary results to warrant acceptance. I’m willing to overlook the incomplete validation and the conjectural aspects given the boldness and originality of the approach.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. I recommend acceptance with encouragement to pursue more rigorous theoretical analysis and more extensive empirical validation in future work. Specifically, I'd suggest exploring the robustness of the algorithm to deviations from the assumed eigenvalue structure and conducting more thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art MHMM learning methods on a wider range of datasets.
