This paper proposes a novel method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard approach for learning MHMMs, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is susceptible to local optima. This work aims to overcome these issues by providing a non-iterative spectral learning algorithm.

The core idea is to observe that an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, is equivalent to a single, larger HMM with KM states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. This insight allows the authors to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs [1, 3] to estimate the parameters of this larger model. However, these algorithms recover parameters only up to a permutation of the hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this paper is a spectral de-permutation algorithm to resolve this ambiguity. By analyzing the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix, the authors propose a method to cluster the columns of its low-rank reconstruction to identify the state groupings corresponding to the original HMMs. A secondary contribution is a heuristic for estimating the number of mixture components, K, from the data based on the eigenvalues of the estimated transition matrix.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem: the efficient learning of MHMMs. The core conceptual contribution—framing the MHMM as a single HMM with special structure and then solving the resulting permutation problem—is elegant and novel. It represents a non-trivial extension of the well-established spectral learning framework for HMMs to a more complex, hierarchical model. If successful, this approach could provide a valuable, computationally cheaper, and theoretically grounded alternative to EM for sequence clustering.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The progression from the model definition, through the key equivalence lemma, to the identification of the permutation problem and its proposed solution is easy to follow. The noiseless case is explained particularly well and provides strong intuition for the overall approach.
*   **Methodology:** The de-permutation algorithm based on the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix is clever. The idea of using the eigenvectors corresponding to the K largest eigenvalues to form a low-rank approximation that reveals the block structure is sound and insightful.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** While the idea is compelling, the theoretical analysis for the practical (noisy) case is weak.
    *   **Conjecture 1** provides a sample complexity bound by directly borrowing a result from Anandkumar et al. [1] for mixture models. The authors state that the models are "similar," but this is not a substitute for a formal proof. It is unclear if the specific structure of the MHMM affects the concentration bounds.
    *   The justification for **Lemma 3**, which concerns the estimation of K, is entirely descriptive. The "spectral longevity" metric is heuristic, and its connection to maximizing the ratio of spectral gaps is asserted rather than proven. Crucially, the paper lacks a formal statement and proof of the conditions under which this estimator for K is consistent.
    *   The central assumption for the noisy case is that a spectral gap exists: `|λ_GK| > max_k |λ_L2,k|`. This requires the mixing time *within* each component HMM to be faster than the decay associated with the K-th "global" mode. This is a strong assumption. The paper would benefit greatly from a discussion of what properties of the component HMMs (e.g., well-separated stationary distributions, fast mixing) ensure this condition holds.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are not sufficiently comprehensive or convincing to fully support the paper's claims.
    *   The comparison to EM (Figure 4) shows the spectral method is faster but often less accurate, especially with less data. While EM's sensitivity to initialization is a known issue, using only four restarts is not an exhaustive baseline. More importantly, the real-data experiment (Table 1) shows the proposed method is frequently outperformed by randomly initialized EM. The main empirical result is that it serves as a good initializer for EM, which somewhat tempers its claim as a standalone replacement.
    *   A major omission is the lack of any experimental validation for the proposed method of estimating K. This is a significant contribution claimed by the paper (Algorithm 1 includes it as a key step), but its practical performance is never measured or compared against standard baselines like BIC or cross-validation with EM.
    *   The `MK < L` requirement is a well-known and significant limitation of many MoM tensor methods. The paper addresses this on the real dataset via a polynomial feature expansion, but this feels ad-hoc and its general applicability is questionable. This practical constraint deserves a more prominent discussion.

*   **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The notation in Definition 1 regarding the noisy eigenvalues (`α_k`, `β_j,k`) is slightly confusing and could be defined more precisely.
    *   The proof of Corollary 1 implicitly assumes that the component transition matrices `A_k` are irreducible and aperiodic, which guarantees a unique stationary distribution and a single eigenvalue of 1. This condition should be stated explicitly.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a creative and intellectually appealing spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs. The core idea of identifying and solving the permutation problem via spectral clustering is a strong contribution. However, the work in its current form feels incomplete. The theoretical claims for the noisy case are presented as conjectures with weak justification, and the experimental evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over standard methods and fails to evaluate a key part of the proposed algorithm (the estimation of K).

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and non-trivial extension of spectral methods to an important hierarchical model.
*   The problem is significant, and the paper offers a promising new direction that could inspire follow-up work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Insufficient theoretical grounding for the noisy case, relying on unproven conjectures.
*   Experimental results are not strong enough to demonstrate a clear practical advantage over the EM baseline.
*   A key component of the proposed algorithm (estimating K) is not empirically evaluated.

Overall, I believe the weaknesses currently outweigh the strengths. The paper has a very promising core idea but does not meet the standards of rigor and completeness expected for NIPS. I recommend rejection, but I would strongly encourage the authors to formalize their theoretical claims and conduct a more thorough empirical evaluation. With these improvements, this could become an excellent paper.
