## NIPS Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The authors leverage the observation that an MHMM can be represented as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. They build upon existing MoM algorithms for HMMs, addressing the inherent permutation ambiguity through spectral analysis of the global transition matrix, enabling recovery of individual HMM parameters and, under certain conditions, the number of HMMs. The paper presents theoretical justification and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating computational efficiency compared to traditional EM approaches.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly upon the recent resurgence of MoM algorithms for latent variable models [1, 2, 3]. It specifically extends the spectral learning approach for HMMs presented in [3] to the more complex MHMM setting.  The paper acknowledges the existing literature on MHMMs and their common learning method, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm [4], and positions their contribution as a computationally efficient alternative. Comparisons are also drawn to related work on sequence clustering [5, 6, 7] and spectral methods for regression [8].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of MoM and spectral techniques to MHMMs is a significant contribution.  Addressing the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM learning for mixture models is a key innovation.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors convincingly argue and demonstrate that their approach avoids the iterative forward-backward computations of EM, resulting in substantial speedups, especially for large datasets.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides clear theoretical reasoning for the approach, including lemmas establishing the equivalence between MHMMs and a specific HMM structure, and conjectures regarding spectral properties for de-permutation and cluster number estimation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while relatively limited, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The comparison to EM on synthetic data and the real-world handwriting dataset provides some evidence of its practicality. The use of the spectral algorithm as an initialization scheme for EM is a promising result.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible. The use of matrix notation is effective.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conjecture-Based Results:** The core of the permutation recovery relies on Conjecture 1, which lacks rigorous proof and relies on specific noise conditions.  The sensitivity of the algorithm to noise is not fully explored.
* **Limited Experimentation:** The experimental section feels somewhat shallow. The real-world data experiment, while presented, is not convincingly demonstrated to be a compelling application.  The performance gain of the proposed method over EM isn't always substantial, and the conditions under which it excels (data size, noise level) aren’t thoroughly analyzed.
* **Assumptions and Restrictions:** The assumption of a fixed number of hidden states (M) per HMM simplifies the problem but might limit applicability. The observation model is restricted to Gaussian, Poisson or Multinomial distributions.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Clustering:**  The comparison is primarily to EM. It would be beneficial to compare the clustering performance to other state-of-the-art sequence clustering methods, beyond just EM, for a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Spectral Condition Specificity**: The conditions required for successful recovery of the number of HMMs (Definition 3 and Lemma 3) seem quite restrictive and require careful tuning of parameters.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem (learning MHMMs).
* The proposed method offers significant computational advantages over existing techniques.
* The experimental results, while limited, are encouraging.
* The work opens up a new avenue for research in MoM-based learning of complex latent variable models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core theoretical results rely on unproven conjectures.
* The experimental validation is not sufficiently robust to fully support the claims.
* The restrictions and assumptions may limit the practical applicability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution with significant potential. While the reliance on conjectures and the limited scope of the experiments are concerns, the novelty of the approach and the potential for computational benefits make it worthy of acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Strengthen Theoretical Justification:** Investigate the conditions under which Conjecture 1 holds and attempt to provide a more rigorous proof or provide a more detailed analysis of its limitations.
* **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**
    * Perform more extensive experiments with varying levels of noise and dataset sizes.
    * Compare the proposed algorithm to a wider range of sequence clustering methods.
    * Explore the performance on different types of real-world datasets.
* **Investigate Robustness to Noise:** Conduct sensitivity analysis to understand how the algorithm’s performance degrades with increasing noise levels.
* **Discuss Limitations:** More explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach, such as the fixed number of hidden states assumption and the restricted observation models.
