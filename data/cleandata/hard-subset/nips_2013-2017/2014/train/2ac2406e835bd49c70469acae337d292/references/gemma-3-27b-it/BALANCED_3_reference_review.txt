This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing the computational challenges of traditional EM-based approaches, particularly with large datasets. The core idea is to leverage the equivalence between an MHMM and an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, applying existing MoM techniques for HMMs and then using spectral properties to de-permute the resulting parameters to recover individual HMM components.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of EM for MHMMs and positioning MoM as a potential solution. The connection made between MHMMs and block-diagonal HMMs is a crucial observation that underpins the proposed approach. The mathematical derivations, while potentially dense for some readers, appear sound and are generally well-presented. The authors also attempt to address the permutation ambiguity inherent in MoM through spectral analysis and provide a conjecture for conditions under which the de-permutation can be reliably performed. The experimental evaluation includes synthetic data and a real-world dataset, offering some validation of the approach. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of MoM to MHMMs, and the clever use of spectral properties to address the permutation problem, represents a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear mathematical justification for its approach, grounding it in the established theory of MoM and HMMs.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The stated advantage of computational independence from the dataset size is a significant benefit, particularly for large-scale applications.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper does a reasonable job of explaining complex concepts, although some sections could benefit from further clarification (see weaknesses).
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.  The comparison to EM, and the use of the method as an EM initialization, are valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conjecture Reliance:**  A significant portion of the parameter recovery relies on Conjecture 1, which is not proven. The conditions outlined in Definitions 1 & 3, and Lemma 3, feel somewhat ad-hoc and require more rigorous justification. The dependence on sufficient data (O(1/sqrt(TN))) feels very strong.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to violations of these conditions would be helpful.
* **Clarity of Spectral De-permutation:**  The explanation of the spectral de-permutation process (Sections 3.2.1 and 3.2.2) is the most challenging part of the paper to follow.  More intuitive explanations and potentially visual aids would be beneficial. The leap from the theoretical properties of the transition matrix to the practical algorithm for finding P̃ could be smoothed out.
* **Limited Experimentation:** While experiments are included, they are somewhat limited in scope.  The synthetic data experiments lack detail regarding the parameter settings and the sensitivity to noise.  The real-world experiment uses a feature transformation that isn't fully justified, and the comparison with EM is not particularly conclusive. More extensive experimentation with varying dataset sizes, model complexities, and noise levels is needed.
* **Related Work:** While the references are relevant, the discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. For example, there is a substantial body of work on spectral clustering and graph partitioning that might inform the spectral de-permutation step. Exploring connections to these areas could strengthen the paper.  Specifically, the relationship to other permutation inference problems should be discussed.
* **Notational Clutter:** The paper uses a lot of notation, some of which is redundant or inconsistent. Simplifying the notation could improve readability.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in a novel and theoretically sound way.
* The proposed algorithm has the potential to significantly improve the scalability of MHMM learning.
* The experimental results, while preliminary, are encouraging.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The reliance on an unproven conjecture is a significant concern.
* The explanation of the spectral de-permutation process is unclear.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and lacks robustness analysis.
* The sensitivity to violations of the stated spectral conditions is unknown.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to learning MHMMs, but it is not yet ready for publication in its current form. The core ideas are novel and the theoretical foundation is strong, but the reliance on a conjecture and the lack of rigorous experimental validation are significant weaknesses. The clarity of the spectral de-permutation process also needs improvement. 

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.**  I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  **Attempt to prove Conjecture 1 or provide a more compelling justification for its validity.**
2.  **Provide a clearer and more intuitive explanation of the spectral de-permutation process.**  Consider using visual aids to illustrate the concept.
3.  **Conduct more extensive experimentation, including sensitivity analysis to noise and variations in dataset size and model complexity.**
4.  **Expand the related work section to include relevant research on spectral clustering and permutation inference.**
5.  **Simplify the notation where possible.**

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential for acceptance in a future submission.
