### Paper Summary

This paper presents a novel spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The standard algorithm for this task is Expectation-Maximization (EM), which can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is sensitive to initialization. The proposed method, based on the Method of Moments (MoM), offers a computationally cheaper and non-iterative alternative.

The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components, each with M hidden states, as a single large HMM with MK hidden states. In this formulation, the global transition matrix is block-diagonal, and the global emission matrix is a concatenation of the individual HMM emission matrices. The authors propose using an existing spectral HMM learning algorithm (e.g., Hsu et al., 2013) to estimate the parameters of this large HMM. However, this standard approach recovers the parameters only up to an unknown permutation of the MK hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components, making them unrecoverable.

The main contribution of this paper is a method to "de-permute" the learned parameters. The authors exploit the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix. In the noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1. By raising the estimated transition matrix to a high power, the columns corresponding to states within the same HMM component converge to the same vector, allowing for recovery of the permutation through simple clustering. For the more realistic noisy case, where this property is lost, they propose performing a low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix using its top K eigenvectors. Clustering the columns of this reconstructed matrix is shown to effectively recover the block structure. The paper also proposes a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" of eigenvalues to estimate the number of components, K, from the data. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the method's significant speed advantage over EM and its utility as a robust initialization scheme for EM.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of sequence clustering with a novel approach. The idea of viewing an MHMM as a large, structured HMM and then developing a specific "de-permutation" technique to resolve the ambiguity of spectral learning is elegant and clever. This extends the applicability of spectral methods to a more complex hierarchical model, which is a significant contribution. The method provides a principled, non-iterative alternative to EM with strong computational benefits.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The core idea is built up logically, starting from the model definitions, establishing the key equivalence in Lemma 1, explaining the permutation problem, and then presenting the solution for both the noiseless and noisy cases. The figures, particularly Figure 1, provide excellent intuition for the de-permutation algorithm.

3.  **Practical Utility:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's primary advantage: speed. The massive speed-up compared to EM (Figure 4) makes this approach highly attractive for large-scale problems. Furthermore, the real-data experiment (Table 1) wisely evaluates the algorithm not just as a standalone method but also as an initialization for EM. Showing that it provides a strong initialization that improves upon random restarts is a very pragmatic and valuable result for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the noisy case, which is crucial for the method's practical application, is not fully rigorous.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The paper's key result on the accuracy of the low-rank reconstruction is presented as a conjecture. The justification provided is brief, borrowing a bound from a related but different model and stating that a full analysis is "out of scope." For a top-tier conference like NIPS, a more formal analysis, perhaps using matrix perturbation theory (e.g., a Davis-Kahan type theorem) to bound the error in the relevant subspace, would be expected to turn this conjecture into a proven theorem.
    *   **Estimating K:** The method for estimating K (Lemma 3 and "spectral longevity") is interesting but appears somewhat heuristic. The proof sketch is intuitive but not fully formalized. More importantly, its robustness is not thoroughly evaluated in the experiments. It is unclear how well this method works in practice, especially when the spectral gaps are not large.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments are generally good, they could be more comprehensive.
    *   The experiments do not explicitly test the limits of the proposed method, for example, by showing scenarios where the spectral gap condition (`|λ_GK| > max |λ_L2,k|`) is violated or barely met. Understanding the failure modes of the algorithm is important.
    *   The recovery of K is a claimed contribution, but its performance is not quantitatively evaluated in the experiments section, which primarily focuses on clustering accuracy assuming K is known.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

*   The cubic polynomial feature transformation in the real-data experiment was used to satisfy the `MK < L` constraint. This is a known requirement for this family of spectral algorithms. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to this choice of transformation? How does one choose the right feature mapping in general?
*   In Algorithm 1, the de-permutation step involves clustering the columns of `A_r`. The experiments seem to use k-means. How sensitive is the final result to the choice of clustering algorithm and its initialization? Given that k-means is itself sensitive to initialization, does this introduce a degree of randomness that the MoM approach is meant to avoid?
*   Regarding Conjecture 1, could you elaborate on why a formal proof is considered out of scope? It seems central to the paper's claims. Even a more detailed proof sketch outlining the main steps and challenges would strengthen the paper significantly.
*   The comparison against EM used 4-5 random restarts. While this is reasonable, EM's performance can sometimes be improved with more restarts or smarter initialization schemes (e.g., k-means on sequence summary statistics). It might be worth acknowledging this. However, the result that your method provides a superior initialization is a strong point that stands on its own.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant contribution. It successfully extends spectral learning methods to MHMMs through a clever de-permutation strategy, offering a compelling computational alternative to EM. The work is well-presented and empirically validated. The primary weakness is the lack of theoretical rigor for the noisy case, which is presented as a conjecture. Despite this, the intuitive correctness of the approach, the strong empirical results (especially the speedup and utility as an initializer), and the overall novelty of the idea make this a valuable contribution to the machine learning community. I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, be encouraged to either provide a more complete theoretical justification or be more explicit about the theoretical limitations in the final version.
