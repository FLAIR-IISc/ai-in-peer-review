Summary
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) approach to learn mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM whose transition matrix is block diagonal. The authors apply an HMM spectral learner to obtain global parameters up to a permutation, then “de-permute” by exploiting spectral structure of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the transition matrix reveal K blocks; with noise, they propose selecting the top-K “global” eigenvalues via a heuristic “spectral longevity” measure, reconstruct a rank-K approximation, then cluster columns to infer the permutation and recover per-HMM parameters. They also claim to estimate the number of mixture components K. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwriting dataset compare against EM.

Relation to prior work
This builds on spectral learning for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al., JCSS 2012) and multi-view/tensor methods (Anandkumar et al.). The equivalence between MHMM and an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is standard. Using spectral methods to avoid EM’s cost and to resolve permutations via additional structure is plausible, but I am not aware of prior work that specifically recovers a de-permutation via the long-run powers of a block-diagonal transition matrix; however, related ideas (e.g., block structure detection via eigenspaces, stationary distributions, and clustering) are well known. The paper’s positioning relative to existing spectral treatments of mixtures and HMMs is incomplete.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitively appealing reduction (MHMM → block-diagonal HMM) and a pragmatic de-permutation heuristic.
- Potential computational advantages, since spectral factorization costs are decoupled from the number/length of sequences after moment estimation.
- Attempts to estimate K via a spectral criterion; could be useful in practice.
- Some empirical evidence that spectral initialization can aid EM.

Weaknesses (technical, methodological, clarity, and presentation)
- Core theory is incomplete. The key performance statement is left as Conjecture 1 and justified by borrowing bounds from a different model (“mixture model”), without a proof suitable for HMM dependencies. No formal consistency/identifiability guarantees for the de-permutation, nor a finite-sample analysis for estimating K.
- Assumption/claim mismatch for spectral HMM learning: standard spectral HMM algorithms typically recover parameters up to a similarity transform, not merely a permutation, unless additional assumptions/steps are used. The paper assumes the MoM learner returns (Ō, Ā, ν̄) up to permutation only; this is not justified and is generally false for continuous emissions.
- Notation/model inconsistencies:
  - Equation (1) uses diag(p(xt | :, O)) but Section 2.1 defines O(:,u)=E[xt|rt=u]. These are not the same object; for Gaussian emissions, p(xt|u) depends on mean and variance, not just O(:,u). Using O(xt) both as p(xt|:) and as E[x|state] is inconsistent.
  - For continuous emissions, it is unclear how the moments are constructed and how the spectral HMM algorithm is adapted (feature maps? multi-view structure?). The polynomial feature trick in Section 4.3 is ad hoc and not grounded in the model.
- The de-permutation method relies on Ā^e → block-stationary structure, but the estimated matrix may not be exactly stochastic (due to sampling noise), may be non-diagonalizable, and may have complex eigenvalues; these issues are neither detected nor handled (e.g., no stochastic projection/normalization, no stability discussion).
- The “spectral longevity” criterion is heuristic; Lemma 3 uses informal arguments about spectral gaps and indicator thresholds. No guidance on choosing γ or the exponent range, and no robustness analysis.
- Input requirements are unclear: Algorithm 1 requires MK as input to the HMM-MoM stage, yet K is supposed to be estimated. How is M chosen if K is unknown? The requirement MK < L is stated but not justified; it differs from standard conditions (typically L ≥ number of hidden states for certain algorithms).
- Sample complexity and rates: using O(1/√(TN)) ignores temporal dependence in HMMs; effective sample size is not addressed.
- Experiments are limited:
  - Synthetic: very small scale; no ablation on eigenvalue separations, block sizes, mixing rates, or failure cases (e.g., similar stationary distributions across clusters).
  - Real data: uses a polynomial feature map to satisfy MK < L; this changes the observation model without theoretical justification. Only pairwise character clustering is tested; no comparisons to alternative spectral or non-EM baselines (e.g., k-HMMs with DTW kernels, spectral sequence clustering methods).
  - Metrics and variance: figures appear qualitative; no numerical tables with mean±std over multiple runs. Hyperparameters (e.g., number of restarts, stopping criteria) are briefly mentioned but not controlled.
- Referencing and formatting issues:
  - References are incomplete (missing years/venues; duplicated titles).
  - Numerous typographical/notation issues: “1>M” / “1>MK” for ones vectors, stray superscripts, “transition matrices1”, inconsistent colon notation, MATLAB-style colon usage without definition, inconsistent use of transpose “>”.
  - Missing details for figures/tables (axis labels, precise settings).
  - Several claims are imprecise (e.g., “eigendecomposition is invariant under permutation” should be stated as similarity by permutation: eigenvalues invariant; eigenvectors transform as Pv).

Quality: Currently below NeurIPS standards. The main algorithm is plausible but lacks rigorous guarantees; several key claims are conjectural or incorrect under standard spectral HMM theory for continuous observations.

Clarity: The paper is difficult to follow due to notation inconsistencies, undefined terms, and missing algorithmic details (moment construction, constraints ensuring stochasticity, how clustering is performed, distance metrics).

Originality: The MHMM→block-diagonal HMM observation is known; the de-permutation via rank-K long-run structure is a neat idea but remains heuristic. The contribution would be stronger with a principled analysis.

Significance: Potentially useful if made rigorous and robust; as-is, limited due to theoretical gaps and modest empirical validation.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple idea with potential computational benefits.
- Empirical indication that spectral initialization can help EM.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of rigorous theory (central results are conjectural).
- Incorrect/unsupported assumptions about what spectral HMM learning recovers.
- Inconsistent observation model and moment notation.
- Heuristic and under-specified K estimation.
- Limited experiments; incomplete baselines; weak quantitative reporting.
- Poor referencing and numerous formatting/notation errors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a correct account of what the HMM spectral algorithm recovers under continuous emissions; if necessary, restrict to discrete emissions or provide a justified feature-based multi-view construction.
- Prove a de-permutation guarantee under explicit spectral gap and noise conditions, including how to enforce/restore stochasticity of Â (e.g., projection onto the simplex).
- Replace “Conjecture 1” with a theorem or clearly bounded approximation result tailored to HMM dependencies.
- Formalize the K-estimation procedure with provable conditions or provide a data-driven, stable alternative (e.g., model selection via held-out likelihood using a cheap surrogate).
- Expand experiments: larger scales, controlled spectral gaps, stress tests, ablations, and additional baselines; report means/variances and wall-clock numbers.
- Fix notation, clarify the moments used, and complete the references.

Recommendation: Reject.

Confidence: High (technical familiarity with spectral methods for HMMs and mixtures).
