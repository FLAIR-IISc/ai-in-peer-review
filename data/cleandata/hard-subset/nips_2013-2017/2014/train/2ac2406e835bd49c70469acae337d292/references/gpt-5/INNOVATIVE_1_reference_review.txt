Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via a spectral (method-of-moments) approach. The central observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block corresponds to a constituent HMM. The authors then leverage existing spectral methods for HMMs to estimate the global parameters up to a permutation. The key technical contribution is a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix to: (i) recover the block structure (hence the parameters) of each component HMM, and (ii) estimate the number of mixture components K via a “spectral longevity” criterion derived from the decay of eigenvalues under powers of the transition matrix. Experiments show competitive clustering accuracy to EM at much lower computation, plus a real-data study where the spectral method is an effective initialization for EM.

This work builds on spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu, Kakade, Zhang, JCSS 2012) and tensor methods for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al.), and on MHMMs for sequence clustering (Smyth). While the block-diagonal equivalence is known/straightforward, the de-permutation via eigen-structure, along with the longevity-based K selection, appears new.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with a neat spectral twist: reframing MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM and using eigen-structure to undo state permutations is elegant and potentially impactful.
- Avoids EM’s per-sequence forward-backward in every iteration; after moments are computed, the core factorization is independent of dataset size.
- A practical heuristic to estimate K from the spectrum that aligns with mixing-rate intuition; the “longevity” criterion is interesting and could be used beyond this setting.
- Empirical results show substantial speedups with comparable accuracy in many regimes; also works as a strong initializer for EM on real data.
- Clear articulation of when/why the de-permutation works (separation of global vs. local eigenvalues, mixing-rate considerations).

Weaknesses
- Theory is incomplete where it matters most: Conjecture 1 replaces a finite-sample guarantee for the low-rank reconstruction and the success of de-permutation. There is no rigorous recovery or sample complexity result for the permutation mapping or K estimation.
- The key assumption that each Ak has only one eigenvalue of magnitude 1 and that global top-K eigenvalues dominate the “local” second eigenvalues is plausible but not verified or stress-tested; failure modes (near-degenerate spectra, similar stationary distributions across components) are not analyzed.
- The requirements for spectral HMM learning (e.g., rank conditions; MK ≤ L or use of feature maps) are only lightly addressed. The real-data experiment resorts to a polynomial feature lift to make MK < L; the impact on noise and sample complexity is not discussed.
- Experimental scope is limited: synthetic studies are small; K estimation accuracy and robustness to spectral overlap/noise aren’t systematically evaluated; baselines omit modern variational/regularized MHMM learners and Bayesian nonparametric alternatives (e.g., HDP-HMM + mixture hierarchy).
- Notational clarity and referencing are uneven in places; some derivations are sketched and references are incomplete.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of the block-diagonal construction and eigen-structure lemmas. However, key guarantees (de-permutation in noise, K selection) are heuristic; the main result is positioned as a conjecture. Empirical support is encouraging but not exhaustive.

Clarity
Generally clear, with intuitive explanations of the spectral picture. The paper could be improved by tightening notation, clearly stating identifiability/rank assumptions, and explicitly delineating the conditions under which MK < L holds or how to choose feature maps and their impact.

Originality
Moderately high. The block-diagonal equivalence is standard, but the proposed de-permutation via rank-K reconstruction and column clustering, and the spectral longevity criterion for K, are novel and interesting.

Significance
Potentially useful for large-scale sequence clustering where EM is costly; the idea is simple, fast, and could inspire follow-up work on principled guarantees and broader hierarchical latent models. Adoption may depend on stronger robustness/theory and broader empirical validation.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical evaluation of K estimation accuracy across a grid of spectral gaps and noise levels? How often does longevity pick the correct K vs standard eigengap or thresholding heuristics?
- What are the precise rank/identifiability conditions on O and the moment tensors used, especially for continuous emissions? Please clarify when polynomial features are sufficient and how their choice affects conditioning and sample complexity.
- How sensitive is the de-permutation to components with similar stationary distributions or close second eigenvalues? Any regularization or stabilization tricks?
- Please compare to stronger baselines (e.g., VB for MHMMs, HDP-HMM with mixture structure, recent scalable EM variants) and report wall-clock versus accuracy comprehensively.
- Consider releasing code and detailing moment computation costs for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel spectral de-permutation idea and K selection heuristic.
- Conceptually bold and computationally appealing direction for MHMMs.
- Positive empirical signs; strong potential as an initializer and fast alternative.

Cons for acceptance
- Core claims hinge on an unproven conjecture; limited theoretical backing.
- Experimental evaluation is modest and misses key baselines and stress tests.
- Practical assumptions (MK < L; feature lifts) not fully justified.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a creative and promising idea with the potential to open a new line of work on spectral learning for hierarchical sequence models. While the theory and experiments are not yet fully mature, the conceptual novelty and computational benefits outweigh the current gaps.

Confidence
Medium.
