

# Review: Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models

## Summary
This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing a significant computational bottleneck in traditional EM-based approaches. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then adapt spectral learning for HMMs to this structure, overcoming permutation ambiguity through spectral properties of the global transition matrix. They develop an algorithm that learns the MHMM parameters, estimates the number of components K, and recovers the individual HMMs' parameters. The work builds on the spectral learning framework for HMMs (Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012) and extends it to the MHMM setting, which has not been addressed in previous work.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear and elegant theoretical contribution by establishing the MHMM-HMM equivalence (Lemma 1) and developing a principled method to resolve the permutation ambiguity (Lemma 2, Corollary 1, Lemma 3). The spectral longevity concept (Definition 3) provides a novel and theoretically grounded approach to determining the number of clusters K, which is a non-trivial aspect of MHMM learning.

**Computational Efficiency:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that the proposed method is computationally superior to EM, especially for large datasets. The experimental results (Figure 4) show that the spectral approach achieves comparable accuracy to EM while being significantly faster, which is a substantial practical advantage. The authors correctly identify that EM's computational bottleneck stems from running forward-backward message passing for every sequence in every iteration.

**Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and cover both synthetic data (Figure 3, 4) and real-world applications (Table 1). The real data experiment on handwritten character trajectories demonstrates practical utility, showing that the spectral method can serve as an effective initialization for EM. The authors carefully address the practical constraint that MK < L by using polynomial feature transformation, which is a reasonable and transparent approach.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Gaps:** While the main algorithmic approach is sound, there are notable theoretical gaps. Conjecture 1 lacks a rigorous proof, and the justification relies on results from previous work without sufficient justification for their applicability to this specific context. The paper would be significantly stronger with a formal proof of the estimation error bound. Additionally, the paper doesn't fully address the sample complexity requirements for the depermutation step, which is crucial for understanding when the method will work in practice.

**Limited Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper cites Smyth's 2000 work on clustering sequences with HMMs but doesn't explicitly compare the proposed method to existing approaches for learning MHMMs. It would be valuable to discuss how this method differs from or improves upon previous MHMM learning techniques. The paper also doesn't clearly position itself relative to other recent spectral learning approaches for latent variable models.

**Practical Limitation:** The requirement that MK < L (the number of global states must be less than the observation dimension) is a significant practical constraint that the paper doesn't adequately address. While the real-world experiment uses a polynomial feature transformation to satisfy this condition, this workaround might not be feasible for all applications, limiting the method's general applicability.

**Clarity of Key Concepts:** The spectral longevity concept (Definition 3) and its relationship to the eigenvalue structure could be made more intuitive. A simple example illustrating how Lλ̃K' captures the "longevity" of eigenvalues would help readers understand why this measure is useful for determining K.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to learning MHMMs that addresses a meaningful computational challenge in the field. The core idea of viewing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is clever and well-executed. The method has clear practical advantages over EM for large datasets, and the experimental results support this claim.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on spectral learning for latent variable models. While it has some theoretical gaps and practical limitations, these are not deal-breakers for a NeurIPS submission. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a problem with significant practical relevance.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

**Reasoning:** The paper's main contribution—applying spectral learning to MHMMs with a novel depermutation procedure—is sufficiently novel and well-justified. The computational advantages over EM are substantial and well-demonstrated. While the theoretical gaps in Conjecture 1 are notable, they don't undermine the core contribution, especially since the authors acknowledge these as areas for future work. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of latent variable model learning.

The weaknesses identified are important but not critical for acceptance. The theoretical gaps could be addressed in future work, and the practical limitation (MK < L) is acknowledged and handled appropriately in the experiments. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a computationally efficient alternative to EM for MHMM learning, which is a significant practical contribution.

The paper's significance is strengthened by its potential application to other hierarchical latent variable models (mentioned in the conclusion), suggesting broader impact beyond just MHMMs. The experimental results on both synthetic and real data provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

For a NeurIPS submission, this paper meets all four criteria: it's technically sound (with minor theoretical gaps), clearly written, offers a novel approach to a meaningful problem, and provides significant practical benefits over existing methods. The authors have made a strong contribution that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners working with sequence data.
