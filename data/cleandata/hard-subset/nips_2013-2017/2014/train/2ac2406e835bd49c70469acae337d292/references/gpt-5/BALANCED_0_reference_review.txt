Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral approach to learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component can be represented as a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first estimate the global HMM via a spectral HMM algorithm (e.g., Hsu et al., JCSS 2012), which identifies parameters up to a permutation of the MK states. They then propose a depermutation step that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix Ā: in the noiseless case, Ā has exactly K eigenvalues at 1 and its powers reveal K clusters corresponding to the blocks; in the noisy case, they define “global” vs “local” eigenvalues and propose a rank-K reconstruction and a clustering of columns to recover the permutation. They also introduce a heuristic “spectral longevity” criterion to estimate K. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI handwritten character trajectory dataset show speed advantages over EM and that the spectral method can serve as a good initializer for EM.

The reduction from MHMM to block-diagonal HMM is known/straightforward (e.g., Smyth, NIPS 1997), while spectral MoM methods for HMMs and mixtures are established (Anandkumar et al.; Hsu et al.). The novel elements here are (i) using the eigen-structure of the global transition matrix to depermute states into component HMMs and (ii) the longevity-based estimator for K.

Strengths:
- Clear and simple reduction, leveraging existing spectral HMM estimators to avoid per-sequence forward–backward in EM; attractive computationally when the dataset is large.
- Insightful use of the block-diagonal structure to design a depermutation heuristic; in the noiseless case the idea is compelling and intuitive.
- Empirical evidence that the method is much faster than EM and can provide a strong initialization for EM on real data.
- Addresses a practically relevant model class (MHMMs) used for clustering sequences.

Weaknesses:
Quality/technical soundness:
- The central steps (depermutation under noise and K selection) lack rigorous guarantees. “Conjecture 1” is not proved and imports a sample complexity bound from a different setting. The longevity criterion is heuristic with a sketchy proof depending on informal eigengap arguments.
- Assumptions are strong and under-discussed: each Ak has a unique eigenvalue at 1 with a sufficient gap; the noisy ĀP still admits K “global” eigenvalues separable from “local” ones; columns cluster well in the rank-K reconstruction. Failure modes (e.g., near-identical stationary distributions across components, small eigengaps, non-ergodic or nearly reducible Ak) are not analyzed.
- The observation model and moment construction are underspecified. Spectral HMM algorithms for continuous observations typically require specific feature maps/third-order moments; here the paper uses O(:,u)=E[x|r=u] and a cubic feature map on real data to satisfy MK<L, but does not detail the exact moment construction, whitening, or conditions for identifiability.
- The reuse of a bound from [1] for this setting is not justified; the brief “justification” contains loose inequalities (e.g., using 2KM) and does not leverage standard matrix perturbation tools (Davis–Kahan/Wedin).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable, but key algorithmic details are missing: precise moment tensors used, how continuous emissions are handled, how clustering is performed (metric/normalization), and how the longevity range/γ are chosen.
- Notation sometimes obscures (diag/colon usage), and several figures are essential for understanding but the text alone is insufficient to assess them.

Originality:
- The MHMM-to-HMM reduction is known; the novelty lies in the depermutation/estimation of K via eigen-structure. While plausible and interesting, it remains heuristic without solid theoretical support and overlaps conceptually with existing spectral clustering of Markov chains/PCCA-type ideas, which are not cited.

Significance:
- If made rigorous and robust, the approach could be useful: faster MHMM learning and a principled EM initializer are valuable. As it stands, the empirical evaluation is limited: small synthetic setups (often 2 sequences per cluster), evaluation focuses on clustering accuracy rather than parameter recovery, and real-data experiments are binary pairwise tasks with ad hoc feature lifts. Comparisons are limited to EM; no variational/Bayesian MHMM baselines are included.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide theoretical guarantees for the depermutation under noise using standard perturbation theory. For example, bound the distance between the rank-K invariant subspace of Ā and its estimate, and show that k-means on (properly normalized) columns recovers the block assignment when inter-block separation exceeds noise. State explicit conditions (eigengap, minimum separation of stationary distributions, mixing rates).
- Replace/justify the longevity criterion with a principled estimator for K (e.g., eigengap thresholding with concentration bounds), or at least provide sensitivity analyses to γ and exponent range.
- Detail the moment construction for each emission model, including required conditions (MK<L), whitening, and practical steps for continuous observations; justify the polynomial feature map and study its impact on identifiability/sample complexity.
- Expand experiments: (i) recover parameters and permutation accuracy; (ii) stress-test under small eigengaps and similar emissions; (iii) report runtime and memory for moment computation vs EM across N,T; (iv) compare against stronger baselines (e.g., variational MHMMs, Bayesian nonparametric variants); (v) evaluate K estimation accuracy.
- Clarify limitations (e.g., when two components are indistinguishable up to permutation) and discuss how to detect or mitigate them.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and practical problem (scalable MHMM learning).
- Conceptually simple, computationally attractive approach.
- Empirical evidence that it can be a strong EM initializer.

Cons for acceptance:
- Main novelty (depermutation and K estimation) is heuristic and lacks guarantees.
- Limited experimental validation; real-data evaluation is narrow.
- Important algorithmic details (moments for continuous observations) are underspecified.
- Relation to relevant spectral clustering/Markov chain literature is incomplete.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper has a promising idea, but it needs stronger theory, clearer algorithms, and more comprehensive experiments to meet the NeurIPS standard.

Confidence: 3/5.
