

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing the computational inefficiency of the traditional EM approach. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then extend existing spectral learning methods for HMMs to recover the individual HMM parameters from the permuted global parameters, using spectral properties of the transition matrix to estimate the depermutation mapping.

The paper builds upon foundational work in spectral learning for HMMs [1, 2, 3] and extends it to MHMMs, which are an important class of models for sequence clustering. The authors cite relevant prior work on MHMMs [4, 5, 6, 7] and spectral learning [1, 2, 3], though they could better contextualize their contribution within recent NIPS literature on spectral methods for latent variable models.

**Strengths:**
1. **Elegant theoretical insight**: The observation that MHMMs correspond to HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is both novel and insightful, providing a clean foundation for the proposed method.
2. **Clear algorithmic presentation**: The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-structured and logically follows from the theoretical developments. The experimental validation is thorough, including synthetic data experiments that demonstrate the method's robustness to varying sequence lengths and number of sequences.
3. **Practical significance**: The paper convincingly demonstrates the computational advantages over EM, showing that the spectral method is much faster while maintaining comparable accuracy. The real-world experiment on handwritten character trajectories shows practical utility, particularly as an initialization method for EM.
4. **Well-executed experiments**: The experiments are comprehensive, covering both synthetic data (with controlled parameters) and real-world data, and they clearly illustrate the trade-offs between computational efficiency and accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical gaps**: The paper presents several key claims as conjectures without rigorous proof (e.g., Conjecture 1 on estimation error bounds). While the authors justify this by stating further analysis is out of scope, the paper would be significantly strengthened with more rigorous theoretical treatment of the spectral conditions and error bounds.
2. **Limited comparison to recent work**: The paper does not sufficiently compare with recent spectral learning methods for mixture models presented at NIPS in the past few years. For example, the work by Chaganty and Liang [9] on spectral methods for mixture models could be discussed as a relevant comparison point.
3. **Spectral conditions not fully validated**: While Lemma 3 identifies necessary conditions for correctly estimating the number of clusters K, the paper lacks experiments demonstrating how robust the method is when these conditions are not perfectly met (e.g., when |λGK| is only slightly larger than max_k|λL2,k|).
4. **Lack of discussion on limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss scenarios where the spectral conditions might not hold, such as when clusters have very similar dynamics or when the number of states M is large relative to the number of observations.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending spectral learning techniques to MHMMs, addressing a practical computational bottleneck in sequence clustering. The core insight is elegant, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results are compelling. However, the theoretical foundation would benefit from more rigorous treatment, and the paper would be stronger with a more thorough comparison to recent spectral learning work.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Constructive suggestions for improvement:**
1. Expand Conjecture 1 into a proper theorem with proof sketch (even if brief) to strengthen the theoretical foundation.
2. Add a comparison to recent spectral learning methods for mixture models presented at NIPS (e.g., 2016-2019 NIPS papers on spectral methods).
3. Conduct additional experiments to test the method's robustness when spectral conditions are borderline (e.g., when |λGK| is only slightly larger than max_k|λL2,k|).
4. Include a brief discussion of practical limitations, such as how the method performs when the number of hidden states M is large relative to the number of observations.
5. Clarify the relationship between this work and recent spectral learning methods for other latent variable models to better position it within the NIPS research landscape.

The paper represents a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in spectral learning for latent variable models, with clear practical implications. With minor theoretical strengthening and more comprehensive comparison to recent work, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
