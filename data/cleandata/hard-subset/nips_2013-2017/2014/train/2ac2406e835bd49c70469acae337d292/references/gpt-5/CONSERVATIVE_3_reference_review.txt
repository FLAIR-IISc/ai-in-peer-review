Summary
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be rewritten as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors apply an existing spectral HMM learner to estimate the “global” HMM parameters up to a permutation, and then propose a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix: (i) identify the K “global” eigenvalues (close to 1), (ii) form a low-rank reconstruction using these eigenpairs, and (iii) cluster columns to recover the block structure and hence the parameters of individual HMMs. They also propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K. Experiments on synthetic data and a small UCI trajectory dataset suggest the method can be faster than EM, with competitive clustering accuracy in some regimes and good utility as an EM initializer.

Strengths
- The reduction from MHMM to an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is clear and correct, and aligns with existing HMM theory.
- The de-permutation idea via low-rank reconstruction and column clustering is intuitive and could be practically useful.
- The paper addresses a real computational pain point of EM for MHMMs and demonstrates substantial speed gains.
- The empirical section includes a real-data test and a comparison to EM, including EM initialized by the spectral method.
- The presentation of the noiseless case is pedagogically helpful and the block structure intuition is clear.

Weaknesses
- Key claims rely on unproven conjectures and strong assumptions. Conjecture 1 is left without proof, and Lemma 3’s practical conditions are hard to verify; overall the method lacks rigorous guarantees in the realistic noisy setting.
- Identifiability and conditions for consistency are under-specified. Standard spectral requirements (e.g., rank conditions on the emission operator, multi-view construction, separation) are not stated carefully, and the paper only briefly notes MK < L in one experiment.
- The observation model mapping to the spectral HMM literature is unclear. Hsu et al.’s algorithm assumes discrete observations or specific feature mappings; here O(:,u) = E[x|r=u] is used for continuous models, but the exact moment construction and its correctness for Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial is not detailed.
- The de-permutation step assumes clusters of equal size M and well-separated eigen-structure; robustness to near-degenerate eigenvalues, periodic chains, or non-ergodic transitions is not analyzed.
- Estimation of K via “spectral longevity” is heuristic; no comparison to more established model selection criteria (e.g., BIC for EM, eigen-gap heuristics, stability) is provided.
- Experimental scope is limited: small K, M, and synthetic settings; no stress tests (e.g., varying eigen-gaps, near-periodic chains, different noise levels), and no statistical uncertainty (variances/confidence intervals).
- Real-data experiment requires a polynomial feature lift to meet MK < L, changing the observation model; potential distortions and sensitivity are not discussed.
- Complexity analysis is missing. While moment computations can be done once, the cost of computing higher-order moments and eigen-decompositions of size (MK)×(MK) is not quantified.
- Related work is thin. There is extensive literature on spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models (including continuous observations and tensor methods) that could more precisely contextualize what is genuinely new here.

Quality
The noiseless analysis is sound, but the main contributions hinge on unproven or loosely justified claims in the noisy finite-sample regime. Empirical validation is not sufficient to compensate: small-scale, limited baselines, and no systematic analysis of failure modes. Important implementation details (moment construction for continuous observations, exact algorithmic steps and regularization choices) are missing.

Clarity
Generally readable, but key parts are underspecified. The paper elides how the moments are constructed for continuous emissions, precisely which spectral algorithm is used, and the exact conditions needed (rank/separation/ergodicity). The K-estimation procedure is presented with substantial notation but little practical guidance.

Originality
Viewing MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is known. The de-permutation by clustering columns of the rank-K reconstruction is a simple idea that could be novel in this specific context, but it is close to standard spectral clustering/eigen-gap intuitions. The “spectral longevity” score is ad hoc.

Significance
Potentially useful if made robust, as MHMMs are widely used and EM is costly. However, in its current form the contribution is incremental and insufficiently validated, with unclear advantages over EM initialized by simpler methods, and with practical constraints (e.g., MK < L) that limit applicability.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely which moments and observable operators are used for continuous observations? Under what conditions is identifiability guaranteed?
- Can you provide a proof or at least a rigorous perturbation analysis supporting Conjecture 1? What are the sample complexity and spectral gap conditions?
- How do you handle periodic or nearly periodic Ak (multiple eigenvalues with |λ|≈1)? How sensitive is the de-permutation to small gaps?
- How is M chosen? Your algorithm assumes MK is provided; how do you select M and K jointly?
- What is the computational complexity (in N, T, L, M, K) of moment computation and eigen-decomposition?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal guarantees (or at least matrix perturbation bounds) for the noisy case, including identifiability conditions and sample complexity.
- Clarify and justify the moment construction for continuous emissions, with proofs or precise references.
- Add thorough experiments with larger K, M, varied eigen-gaps, periodic chains, and ablations of the de-permutation step; report variances/CI and success rates of K recovery.
- Compare K-estimation to alternative methods (eigen-gap, BIC/AIC with EM), and include alternative initializations for EM besides the proposed spectral method.
- Discuss and relax the MK < L requirement, or propose a principled feature construction compatible with the spectral method.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and practical problem where EM is slow.
- Simple, intuitive de-permutation approach that may work well in practice.
- Empirical speed advantages and utility as an EM initializer.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theory in the noisy regime; key claims are conjectural.
- Limited and small-scale empirical validation; no strong state-of-the-art comparisons.
- Practical constraints (MK < L), unclear moment construction for continuous data, and lack of robustness analysis.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject). With stronger theoretical backing and more thorough empirical validation, this line of work could become impactful, but the current submission falls short of NeurIPS standards.
