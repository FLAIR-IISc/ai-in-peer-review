## Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) using spectral methods, building upon the Method of Moments (MoM) framework. The key idea of leveraging the block-diagonal structure of the MHMM as an equivalent HMM and then addressing the permutation ambiguity via spectral properties is clever. While the theoretical justifications could be strengthened, the empirical results are promising and demonstrate the computational advantages over traditional EM-based methods. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution and recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the points outlined below.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the computational bottleneck of learning MHMMs, specifically the iterative forward-backward algorithm within the EM framework. It introduces a MoM-based approach, capitalizing on recent advances in MoM for latent variable models [1, 2]. The authors cleverly recognize the equivalence between an MHMM and a specific HMM structure (block diagonal transition matrix), allowing application of existing spectral learning techniques for HMMs [3].  However, direct application leads to a permutation ambiguity, which they address through analyzing the spectral properties of the global transition matrix. This builds upon the prior work in spectral methods for HMM learning but extends it to the mixture setting with the added challenge of de-permuting the learned components. The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of MHMM learning, acknowledging the popularity of EM [4] and highlighting its limitations.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of MoM, spectral learning, and the clever handling of permutation ambiguity in the MHMM context is a novel contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed method promises significant computational advantages over EM, a crucial benefit when dealing with large datasets.  The empirical results (Section 4.2) clearly demonstrate this speedup.
* **Theoretical Insight:**  The observation regarding the MHMM's equivalence to a block-diagonal HMM is insightful and forms the foundation of the approach. The spectral analysis, even with its stated limitations, offers a principled way to address the permutation problem.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, including a real-world application on handwritten character trajectories [8], provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness and practical relevance. The comparison with EM, both with random and MoM initialization, is well-structured.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The model definitions (Section 2) are clear and concise.

**3. Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** While the lemmas are helpful, the theoretical justification for the de-permutation algorithm feels somewhat hand-wavy. Conjecture 1, while reasonable, needs more rigorous justification.  The bounds mentioned rely on results from [1], and a more detailed connection/adaptation of those bounds to the MHMM setting would be beneficial.  What guarantees can be made regarding the accuracy of the de-permutation under varying levels of noise?
* **Spectral Conditions:** Definitions 1 & 3 introduce spectral conditions for successful de-permutation and number of cluster recovery. These conditions, while intuitively plausible, need more thorough discussion regarding their practicality. How easily can these conditions be verified in practice? Are they overly restrictive?  The connection between these conditions and the amount of data (T and N in Conjecture 1) should be strengthened.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm likely depends on parameters like the choice of ‘e’ in the exponentiation step in Section 3.2.2.  A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of these parameters on the results would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with EM is present, a more thorough benchmark against state-of-the-art MHMM learning algorithms (beyond just EM with different initializations) would strengthen the paper.
* **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1's explanation is a little unclear, and could benefit from more detailed annotations and explanation of the observed effects.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and promising approach to MHMM learning.
* Significant computational advantages demonstrated experimentally.
* Clear presentation and insightful theoretical observations.
* Validated on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Theoretical guarantees are currently weak and require further development.
* Spectral conditions for de-permutation require more scrutiny.
* Limited comparative benchmarking against other MHMM algorithms.




**5. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the theoretical justification for the de-permutation algorithm and providing a more thorough discussion of the practicality of the spectral conditions.  Addressing the parameter sensitivity and expanding the benchmark comparison would further enhance the paper's contribution.  The overall approach is innovative and has the potential to significantly impact the field of sequence modeling and clustering.
