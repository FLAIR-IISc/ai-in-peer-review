Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components, each with M hidden states, can be viewed as a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. The authors first apply an existing spectral HMM learner to this “global” HMM to recover parameters up to a permutation. They then propose to “de-permute” the global parameters to recover the K component HMMs by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the block-diagonal transition matrix converge to a matrix with K rank-1 blocks; in the noisy case, they propose a rank-K reconstruction using the K largest-magnitude eigenvalues and cluster columns to infer the permutation. They also propose a heuristic to estimate K by a “spectral longevity” measure based on the decay of eigenvalues under exponentiation.

This builds on spectral learning of HMMs and latent variable models (Hsu et al., Anandkumar et al.), and on classical EM learning for MHMMs (Smyth; applications in several domains). The equivalence between MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM is known, but the contribution here is an explicit de-permutation procedure and a heuristic for estimating K.

Strengths:
- Simple and intuitive idea: recast MHMM as HMM with block-diagonal transitions and then de-permute via spectral structure; potentially scalable compared to EM because the heavy lifting is in moment estimation and a fixed-size factorization.
- The de-permutation insight is appealing: columns of A^e converge to per-block stationary distributions; clustering these columns enables recovery of the block structure.
- Practical algorithm summarized clearly; empirical results show strong speed advantage over EM and that spectral estimates can serve as an effective initializer for EM.
- The paper addresses an important use case (sequence clustering) and includes both synthetic and real-data experiments.

Weaknesses:
- Theory is largely heuristic. The main finite-sample argument (Conjecture 1) is unproven, and critical parts (e.g., eigenvector perturbation, stability of column clustering under noise) are missing. The proposed “spectral longevity” criterion for K is ad hoc and not justified against standard eigengap or information criteria.
- Strong identifiability/regularity assumptions are not clearly stated nor verified (e.g., requirements on O for continuous emissions; ergodicity/aperiodicity of each Ak; separation conditions between global and local eigenvalues).
- The requirement MK < L is a serious practical limitation for many HMM applications; the workaround via polynomial feature lifting for the real-data experiment is not fully convincing and may distort the observation model. No discussion of alternatives (e.g., multi-view features, random projections, or tensor methods that do not require L ≥ MK).
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Synthetic experiments are small (K ≤ 5, M ≤ 4), do not report parameter estimation error, permutation recovery accuracy, or robustness to spectral gaps and noise. The K estimation procedure is not systematically validated. Comparisons are only to EM (with 4 restarts) and not to other scalable methods (e.g., variational EM for MHMMs).
- Several citations are incomplete, and related work on spectral methods for mixtures and block-structured HMMs could be expanded. The reuse of a sample complexity bound from mixtures to HMMs without proof is unsatisfactory.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is mixed. The block-diagonal equivalence and noiseless analysis are correct. However, key claims for the noisy case are left as conjecture with only a sketchy justification. Standard matrix perturbation tools (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) could be used to derive conditions under which the top-K invariant subspace and the induced clustering are stable; this is not done.
- Empirical support is suggestive but not comprehensive. Speedups are promising; accuracy is sometimes lower than EM and depends on data size.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized; the high-level algorithm is clear. The spectral “longevity” definition is difficult to parse/motivate and could be simplified (e.g., explain why an eigengap-based rule is insufficient). The MoM estimation step for continuous emissions needs much more detail for reproducibility (how moments are computed/whitened, assumptions on O’s rank).
- References need to be completed and expanded; related prior that treats mixtures with spectral/tensor methods should be discussed in more depth.

Originality:
- The block-diagonal equivalence is known. The main novelty lies in the specific de-permutation via clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction and the heuristic K estimator. While plausible, these ideas are evolutionary and would benefit from rigorous guarantees to solidify novelty.

Significance:
- If theoretically grounded and empirically validated at larger scales, this could be a useful alternative to EM for MHMMs and a good initializer for EM in practice. As is, the impact is limited by lack of guarantees and modest experiments.

Questions for the authors:
1. What precise identifiability conditions on O and A are required for your MoM step with continuous emissions? Must O be full column rank, and how is this ensured in practice?
2. Can you provide perturbation bounds (e.g., via Davis–Kahan) linking sample size, eigenvalue separation (|λGK| vs max |λL2,k|), and the error in the recovered permutation/clusters?
3. How sensitive is de-permutation accuracy to small spectral gaps or near-reducible Ak? Please include synthetic stress tests varying gaps and noise.
4. Why not use a standard eigengap (or information criterion) to select K? Please compare your “longevity” measure to these baselines.
5. Can your approach handle L < MK without feature lifting? For example, via multi-view constructions or random projections used in spectral HMM learning?
6. Please report parameter recovery error and cluster assignment accuracy on synthetic data, not just k-means fit error, and include larger K, M.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem; potentially large computational savings.
- Clear and implementable high-level algorithm; useful as an EM initializer.
- Insightful de-permutation mechanism leveraging HMM spectral structure.

Cons for acceptance:
- Central theoretical claims lack proofs; reliance on a conjecture.
- Heuristic and somewhat opaque K selection; no comparison to standard criteria.
- Limited experimental scope and analysis; practical limitations (L ≥ MK) not adequately addressed.
- Incomplete related work and references.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and could be impactful, but the paper needs stronger theoretical guarantees (or clearly scoped assumptions with perturbation analysis), more thorough experiments (including K selection validation, robustness to spectral gaps/noise, larger scales), and clearer treatment of the continuous-emission MoM setup and the L ≥ MK constraint. I encourage the authors to strengthen these aspects; the approach could be a valuable contribution with these improvements.
