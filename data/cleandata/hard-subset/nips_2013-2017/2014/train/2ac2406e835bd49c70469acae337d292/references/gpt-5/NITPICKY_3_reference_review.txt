Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral approach to learning Mixtures of HMMs (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors first estimate the global HMM parameters via an existing spectral HMM algorithm, which returns parameters up to a permutation of the hidden states. They then propose to recover the permutation (and the individual component parameters) by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix: in the noiseless case, each block has an eigenvalue 1, so the columns of Āe cluster by block as e→∞. In the noisy case, they propose a “low-rank reconstruction” keeping the K eigenvalues of largest magnitude (close to 1), introduce a “spectral longevity” statistic to estimate K, and recover the permutation by clustering the columns of the reconstructed matrix. Experiments on synthetic data and a UCI character trajectory dataset are provided; the spectral method is faster than EM and can initialize EM.

Strengths
- Simple and appealing reduction: MHMM as an HMM with block-diagonal transitions is correct and useful for intuition.
- The de-permutation idea via stationary distributions of blocks is elegant in the noiseless case.
- The paper tackles a practically relevant problem (clustering sequences via MHMMs) and emphasizes computational efficiency.
- Some empirical evidence suggests the approach can recover K and the block structure when data are sufficient, and act as a good EM initializer.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/Technical correctness
- Core notation inconsistency: O plays two incompatible roles. In Sec. 2.1, O(:,u)=E[x|r=u] (first moments), but Eq. (1) and later use O(xt)=p(xt|:,O) inside diag, i.e., pdf values. One cannot use the same O to denote both mean parameters and likelihoods in the forward product. This is a serious clarity/correctness issue that pervades the derivations.
- Spectral HMM prerequisites for continuous observations are not specified. Spectral HMM algorithms typically require discrete observations or carefully chosen feature maps/moments (e.g., three-view moments) with provable identifiability (full column rank of O, etc.). The paper treats “HMM MethodofMoments” as a black box and never states the exact moments, features, or identifiability assumptions used for Gaussian/Poisson/Multinomial emissions. Reproducibility and soundness suffer.
- Parameter recovery “up to permutation” is claimed without justification. Depending on the chosen spectral method, recovery can be up to an invertible transform unless specific constraints are enforced. The paper needs to specify which algorithm is used and why only a permutation ambiguity remains in the continuous-observation setting.
- No guarantees that the estimated Ā is stochastic (nonnegative, column-stochastic) or has spectral radius ≤1. Spectral estimates can be non-stochastic; raising Ā to a power or truncating eigenvalues may be ill-posed or unstable. The paper does not describe any projection/renormalization.
- Key step left as Conjecture 1, with a loose argument borrowing a sample complexity result for a different model [1]. The bound and its reuse are not justified. Lemma 3 rests on multiple strong/opaque conditions (global/local eigenvalue separation, diagonalizability, ordering) that are unlikely to hold robustly, and the “spectral longevity” definition is ad hoc. No theoretical guarantee is provided for correct K recovery under noise.
- The algorithm requires MK as input to the spectral HMM routine (Algorithm 1), i.e., the global number of states, which is typically unknown. Estimating only K is insufficient; M must be known or recovered too. The paper does not address model order selection for MK or consistency when K̂ is misestimated (then M̂=MK/K̂ may be non-integer).
- Assumptions on Ak: “only one eigenvalue which is 1” is standard (ergodic chain) but diagonalizability and real eigenpairs are implicitly assumed; complex eigenvalues and defective matrices are ignored. The role of periodicity and reducibility is not discussed.
- The limit result (Cor. 1) hinges on column-stochasticity and ergodicity; orientation and proof sketch are terse, and notation is sloppy (e.g., 1>M vs transpose). Given the earlier notational inconsistencies, it’s hard to verify correctness.

Clarity/Presentation
- Numerous notational and typographical issues: “forwardbackward”, inconsistent use of > for transpose and inequality, missing hats/bars, malformed block matrices, inconsistent indexing. MATLAB colon notation is mentioned but not used. The block matrix in (3) is misformatted.
- Figures and Table referenced but not provided here; text descriptions alone are insufficient to assess results. Quantitative metrics for de-permutation accuracy are absent.
- The requirement “MK < L” is stated without justification; standard spectral identifiability typically requires L ≥ MK and Ō full column rank. The real-data workaround via a cubic polynomial feature map to increase L is ad hoc and changes the observation model without analysis.

Originality
- MHMM-to-HMM block-diagonal equivalence is known/straightforward. The de-permutation via clustering columns of Āe is a nice heuristic, but similar spectral clustering ideas exist in Markov chain aggregation and block-structure recovery. The “spectral longevity” criterion for K is new but heuristic and weakly justified.

Significance
- Potentially useful if formalized and made robust, but currently the method hinges on unproven conjectures and unspecified MoM details. Practical impact is limited by strong input requirements (MK known), lack of guarantees, and sensitivity to noise/eigen-gaps that are not quantified.

Experimental evaluation
- Synthetic experiments lack baselines for K estimation and de-permutation, do not vary key conditions (eigen-gap, noise level, mis-specified MK), and report limited metrics (e.g., “total error from centroid to point” without scale).
- Speed comparisons with EM lack implementation details (hardware, code, stopping criteria beyond “automatic”), and do not separate moment computation from factorization cleanly.
- Real-data experiment relies on an arbitrary feature expansion to satisfy L ≥ MK; potential distributional mismatch is not discussed. The comparison to [9] (mixtures of linear regressions) as an EM initializer is tenuous.

Pros
- Addresses an important computational bottleneck (EM for MHMMs).
- Conceptually simple pipeline; promising as an initializer.
- Some empirical indications of speed advantages.

Cons
- Major clarity issues and inconsistent notation for emissions and likelihoods.
- Critical steps are conjectural or underspecified; no rigorous guarantees.
- Requires MK as input; K estimation heuristic is ad hoc.
- Sparse, non-rigorous experiments; incomplete references/formatting.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: 4/5

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the emission/likelihood notation; clearly specify the observation models, moments, and the exact spectral HMM algorithm used, with assumptions (full-rank conditions, continuous vs discrete).
- Provide a provable de-permutation procedure under explicit spectral separation/noise conditions, or at least a thorough empirical study quantifying failure modes; include a projection to stochastic matrices.
- Replace Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3 with rigorous statements or move them to explicit heuristics; formalize the K-estimation criterion or compare to standard model-selection baselines (BIC, spectral gap heuristics).
- Address model order selection for MK and M; analyze sensitivity to mis-specification.
- Expand experiments: ablations on eigen-gaps, noise, sequence length vs number, mis-specified MK, comparisons to alternative heuristics; report quantitative de-permutation accuracy.
- Clean up writing, equations, and references (complete citations, consistent style).
