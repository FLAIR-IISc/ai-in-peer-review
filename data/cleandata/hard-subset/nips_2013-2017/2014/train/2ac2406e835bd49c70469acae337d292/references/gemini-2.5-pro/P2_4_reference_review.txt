### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the method of moments (MoM). The authors' key insight is that an MHMM with K components, each having M states, can be viewed as a single large HMM with KM states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. They propose leveraging existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs to learn the parameters of this large, equivalent HMM. The main technical challenge is that standard spectral methods recover parameters only up to a permutation of the hidden states, which in this case mixes states from different HMM components, making it impossible to recover the individual models. The core contribution of the paper is a method to undo this permutation by analyzing the spectral properties of the learned global transition matrix. Specifically, they propose clustering the columns of the exponentiated (or low-rank reconstructed) transition matrix to identify the state groupings that correspond to the individual HMMs. The paper also provides a heuristic method for estimating the number of mixture components (K) from the data. Experimental results on synthetic and real data demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster than the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and can serve as an effective initialization strategy for it.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound in its core formulation, but has some weaknesses in its theoretical justification.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The central observation in Lemma 1, that an MHMM is equivalent to an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, is correct and elegantly frames the problem.
    *   The proposed de-permutation strategy for the noiseless case is clever and well-justified by Corollary 1. The idea of using column clustering on the infinite-power transition matrix to recover the block structure is intuitive and correct.
    *   The experimental evaluation is reasonable. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the de-permutation algorithm and provide a fair comparison of the speed-accuracy trade-off against EM. The real-data experiment provides a compelling, practical use-case for the method as an initializer for EM.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The theoretical analysis for the more realistic noisy case is weak. **Conjecture 1** is a significant concern. The authors state a sample complexity bound by analogy to a related model and explicitly state that a full analysis is "out of the scope of this paper." For a top-tier conference like NIPS, presenting a core theoretical result as an unproven conjecture is a major weakness. The justification provided is hand-wavy and does not constitute a proof.
    *   The method for determining the number of clusters K (Lemma 3 and the "longevity" measure) feels heuristic. While the intuition of looking for a spectral gap is standard, the specific formula and the maximization condition presented are complex and not fully justified. The proof sketch relies on convergence rates, but the connection to the proposed "longevity" metric is not rigorously established. It is unclear how sensitive this method would be to the choice of γ or the range of exponents `e`.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, and the proposed approach is logically structured.

*   The progression from the MHMM definition to the equivalent HMM formulation and then to the de-permutation problem is clear.
*   Figure 1 is an excellent illustration that effectively communicates the core idea of the de-permutation algorithm and the challenge introduced by noise.
*   Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the overall procedure.

However, there are areas where clarity could be improved:
*   Section 3.2.2 is dense. The introduction of the noise parameters α and β in Definition 1 seems disconnected from the subsequent analysis, as they are not explicitly used in the proofs or algorithms.
*   The justification for Conjecture 1 is confusing and relies on undefined notation (`Ar̄`).
*   The relationship between the maximization condition in Lemma 3 and the "longevity" metric in Definition 3 could be explained more thoroughly.

**Originality:**
The paper's approach is highly original. While it is built on the foundation of prior work on spectral learning for HMMs [1, 2, 3], the extension to the *mixture* setting is non-trivial and novel. To my knowledge, this is the first method-of-moments approach for learning MHMMs. The central contribution—the spectral de-permutation technique—is a clever solution to the state-aliasing problem that naturally arises when applying existing tools to this new problem structure. This is not merely a combination of existing techniques but a creative adaptation to solve a more complex modeling problem.

**Significance:**
The work is significant and likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners.

*   **Practical Impact:** MHMMs are powerful models for sequential data clustering, but their use is often hampered by the computational cost and local-optima issues of the EM algorithm. This paper offers a practical, non-iterative, and much faster alternative. The finding that it serves as an excellent initializer for EM is particularly valuable, as it provides a way to get the best of both worlds: the speed and global guarantees of spectral methods and the high-accuracy refinement of EM.
*   **Research Impact:** The paper opens up a new avenue for applying MoM-based techniques to more complex, hierarchical latent variable models. The "de-permutation via spectral clustering" idea might be generalizable to other models where a global parameter matrix has a latent block structure (e.g., mixtures of regressions, topic models with correlated topics). It addresses a fundamental identifiability challenge in a novel way.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes the first, to my knowledge, spectral learning algorithm for the important MHMM model class.
2.  **Elegant Core Idea:** The de-permutation strategy based on the spectral properties of the transition matrix is a clever and insightful contribution.
3.  **Practical Value:** Offers a significant computational advantage over EM and is demonstrated to be a superior initialization strategy, which is a highly practical result.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and the central ideas are communicated effectively, particularly through figures and high-level descriptions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Weak Theoretical Support:** The analysis of the noisy case relies on an unproven conjecture (Conjecture 1), which is a significant flaw in a paper making theoretical claims.
2.  **Heuristic Model Selection:** The method for estimating K is presented with a lemma but appears heuristic and lacks rigorous validation.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the weakness in the theoretical analysis for the noisy case, the novelty of the approach, the elegance of the core idea, and the clear practical significance of the results argue strongly for acceptance. The proposed method solves an important problem in a new way, and its demonstrated value as a fast algorithm and an effective initializer for EM is a strong contribution. The theoretical gaps are a drawback but do not invalidate the core algorithmic idea or its empirical utility. I would recommend that the authors be encouraged to address the theoretical weaknesses in the final version, perhaps by rephrasing Conjecture 1 as a more explicit assumption and providing more empirical validation for the method of finding K.

**Overall Score: Accept**
