Summary
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal, with each block corresponding to a component HMM. The authors run an existing spectral-HMM estimator on this “global” HMM to obtain parameters up to a permutation of the MK latent states. They then propose a de-permutation procedure that exploits spectral properties of the global transition matrix: (i) in the noiseless case, a rank-K projection based on eigenvalues equal to 1 reveals K clusters of columns corresponding to the K components; (ii) in the noisy case, they introduce a “spectral longevity” criterion to estimate K and form a low-rank reconstruction, after which k-means on columns yields the de-permutation mapping. Experiments on synthetic data show speed gains over EM and good recovery of block structure with modest data; a small UCI trajectory experiment suggests the spectral method is a strong initializer for EM.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on spectral learning for HMMs and latent variable models (Hsu-Kakade-Zhang; Anandkumar et al.), and on mixture modeling with spectral methods. The reduction of MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM is standard, but prior spectral work has not, to my knowledge, provided a practical de-permutation strategy and K-estimator tailored to MHMMs. The contribution is thus a novel adaptation that bridges spectral HMM learning and mixture modeling in a way that is computationally attractive.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and novelty: Framing MHMM learning as spectral HMM estimation plus a principled de-permutation is simple and potentially impactful. The “spectral longevity” metric for estimating K is an interesting, practical idea.
- Computational appeal: Once moments are computed, parameter recovery is size-independent of dataset length/number of sequences. Experiments show substantial speedups vs EM.
- Practicality: The de-permutation via low-rank eigendecomposition followed by column clustering is simple to implement and could generalize to other block-structured latent models.
- Empirical indications: Synthetic experiments show correct block recovery with relatively short sequences; real-data results suggest value as an EM initializer.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical gaps: The noisy-case analysis hinges on Conjecture 1 and heuristic conditions (e.g., a gap between global and local eigenvalues). There is no formal guarantee that the proposed de-permutation or K-estimation succeeds under identifiable, checkable conditions. This weakens the scientific completeness.
- Identifiability and assumptions: The approach assumes each Ak is ergodic with a unique eigenvalue at 1 and that MK < L (addressed via a polynomial feature map in the real data). The latter changes the observation model and may break the generative assumptions behind the moment derivations; this needs justification.
- Parameter recovery is incomplete: Algorithm 1 appears to return only Ô1:K̂, Â1:K̂. The treatment of νk and π (which are embedded in the global initial vector) is not described, yet these are essential for a full MHMM.
- Robustness: The de-permutation step via k-means on columns of the rank-K reconstruction can be brittle when components have similar stationary distributions or overlapping eigenspaces. Sensitivity analyses (e.g., varying spectral gaps, non-uniform π, near-degenerate components) are missing.
- Empirical scope: Real-data evaluation is limited (pairwise two-class problems; feature expansion to satisfy MK < L). There is no comparison to alternative K-selection methods (BIC, spectral gap), nor to strong scalable EM variants.
- Presentation and references: Some proofs are sketched; several references lack venues/years. Clarity could be improved in the noise model and in the precise moments used.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a simple, checkable set of sufficient conditions (eigen-gap, mixing, separation in O) under which the de-permutation and K-estimation provably succeed?
2. How do you recover νk and π after de-permutation? If omitted intentionally, please clarify.
3. How sensitive is the de-permutation to components with similar stationary distributions or shared subdominant eigenvalues?
4. Does the MK < L requirement strictly follow from the specific HMM spectral routine you use? Could dimensionality reduction/whitening alleviate this without feature lifting that changes the generative model?
5. How does your K-estimation compare to simpler eigengap heuristics or information criteria?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh and potentially generalizable idea (spectral de-permutation and K-estimation for MHMMs).
- Computationally attractive; could stimulate follow-up work on spectral methods for sequence mixtures.
- Clear, bold direction that bridges two literatures.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical support in the noisy case is insufficient (conjectural).
- Limited empirical validation on challenging, realistic settings; incomplete parameter recovery description.
- Some assumptions (MK < L via feature mapping) are strong and under-justified.

Overall evaluation
I value the novelty and the pragmatic algorithmic idea. Despite theoretical gaps and modest experiments, the paper opens a promising direction for spectral learning of mixture sequence models and proposes a concrete, testable de-permutation mechanism and K-estimator that others can build on. With stronger guarantees and broader experiments, this could become a standard tool.
