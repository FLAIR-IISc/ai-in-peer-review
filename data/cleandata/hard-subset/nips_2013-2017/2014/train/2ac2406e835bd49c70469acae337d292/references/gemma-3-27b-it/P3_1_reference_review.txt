## Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel approach to learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) leveraging spectral methods and the Method of Moments (MoM). The core idea of recognizing an MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM is insightful, and the proposed algorithm offers a potentially significant computational advantage over traditional EM-based methods, especially for large datasets. While the theoretical foundation is promising and the initial experimental results are encouraging, the paper suffers from some lack of rigor in the theoretical justification and limited scope of experiments. I lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on addressing the concerns detailed below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of EM for MHMMs, particularly with large datasets. They capitalize on recent advancements in MoM for latent variable models, noting their computational efficiency and uniqueness guarantees. The core contribution is framing the MHMM as a specialized HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling application of existing MoM algorithms. Critically, they then address the permutation ambiguity inherent in the MoM solution through spectral analysis of the global transition matrix, aiming to recover individual HMM parameters and estimate the number of clusters. 

The related work section is adequate, citing key papers in MoM, HMMs, and MHMMs. However, it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of existing spectral methods applied to HMMs and mixture models, particularly any attempts to address permutation ambiguities.  A more explicit comparison with recent works on scalable MHMM learning (e.g., variational inference, stochastic EM) would strengthen the positioning of this contribution.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The combination of MoM with spectral techniques to solve the MHMM learning problem is a genuinely novel idea.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The MoM-based approach promises significant computational savings compared to EM, especially for large datasets, a crucial advantage.
* **Theoretical Insight:** The recognition of the MHMM as a block-diagonal HMM provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
* **Potential for Scalability:**  The method’s independence from dataset size (after moment calculation) is very appealing.
* **Good Initial Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the feasibility of the approach and its competitive performance, especially in terms of speed. The use of a real dataset is also commendable.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical justification, particularly around the spectral properties and the de-permutation step, feels somewhat hand-wavy. Conjecture 1, while intuitive, lacks a solid proof or a more detailed analysis of the error bounds under realistic noise conditions.  The conditions for guaranteeing cluster recovery (Lemma 3 and Definition 3) seem very specific and potentially brittle.  What happens if these spectral conditions are not met?  
* **Limited Experiments:** While the experiments are a good start, they are limited in scope.  The Gaussian emission model is relatively simple.  The experiments should include more diverse datasets (e.g., real-world sequence data with non-Gaussian observations like the character trajectory data they used), different numbers of clusters and hidden states, and a comparison with more state-of-the-art MHMM learning algorithms.
* **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The algorithm likely has parameters (e.g., the exponentiation range in the de-permutation step) that need careful tuning. The paper doesn’t discuss this sensitivity or provide guidance on parameter selection.
* **Clarity of Algorithm 1:** While providing a high-level overview, Algorithm 1 is rather abstract. A more detailed step-by-step description would be helpful, particularly concerning the de-permutation mapping and reconstruction steps.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the method, such as its sensitivity to noise, its reliance on specific spectral properties, and its performance in cases where the spectral conditions are not satisfied.



**4. Specific Comments/Questions:**

* **Lemma 2:**  The assumption that each local transition matrix has only one eigenvalue equal to 1 is strong. While often true in practice, it’s important to address the implications if this assumption is violated.
* **Conjecture 1:**  The error bound  “O(1/ √ TN)” seems overly optimistic without more detailed justification. What is the dependence on the dimensionality of the observation space (L)?
* **Definition 3:**  The “spectral longevity” metric feels somewhat ad-hoc.  Is there a theoretical connection between this metric and the accuracy of cluster recovery?
* **Real Data Experiment:**  The use of a polynomial feature transformation to satisfy the MoM dimension requirement seems like a workaround. Does this transformation significantly affect the performance or interpretability of the results?
* **Computational Complexity:**  A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm compared to EM would be valuable.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novelty and potential for significant computational improvement.
* Sound theoretical foundation (albeit needing more rigor).
* Promising initial experimental results.

**Con:**

* Lack of theoretical rigor in key aspects.
* Limited scope of experiments.
* Insufficient discussion of limitations and sensitivity analysis.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** pending major revisions. The authors should:

1. **Strengthen the theoretical analysis:** Provide more rigorous proofs or justifications for the key claims, especially regarding the spectral properties and the de-permutation algorithm.
2. **Expand the experimental evaluation:**  Include more diverse datasets, varying numbers of clusters and hidden states, and comparisons with state-of-the-art MHMM learning algorithms.
3. **Address the sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter choices and provide guidance on parameter selection.
4. **Improve the clarity of the algorithm description:** Provide a more detailed and step-by-step description of the algorithm.
5. **Discuss limitations:**  Thoroughly discuss the limitations of the method and potential failure modes.
