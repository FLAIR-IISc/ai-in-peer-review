This paper presents a method of moments (MoM) algorithm for learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The central idea is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a KM x KM block-diagonal transition matrix. This observation allows the authors to apply existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs, such as those by Hsu et al. (2012), to estimate the global parameters. The main technical challenge, which the authors identify, is that the parameters are recovered only up to a permutation, which mixes the parameters of the individual HMM components. The core contribution of the paper is a proposed method to undo this permutation by exploiting the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix. Specifically, the authors propose clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of the estimated transition matrix to recover the block structure. A secondary contribution is a heuristic method for estimating the number of mixture components, K.

### Strengths:

*   The key insight that an MHMM can be viewed as an HMM with a special block-diagonal structure is elegant and provides a principled path for applying powerful spectral learning techniques to this more complex model class.
*   The paper correctly identifies and focuses on the critical issue of permutation ambiguity, which is the primary obstacle to making the above insight practically useful.
*   The proposed de-permutation algorithm, which leverages the eigenspectrum of the transition matrix, is intuitive and novel in this context.
*   The experimental results confirm the significant computational advantage of the MoM approach over traditional EM, which is a major motivation for this line of work.

### Weaknesses:

Despite the interesting core idea, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of flaws ranging from notational inconsistencies and lack of clarity to significant gaps in theoretical rigor.

**1. Lack of Theoretical Rigor:**

*   **Conjecture 1:** A central theoretical result regarding the error bound of the low-rank matrix reconstruction is presented as a conjecture. The "Justification" provided is not a proof; it is a series of loose inequalities that rely on unsubstantiated steps and the direct, unjustified reuse of a bound from a related but different model [1]. A top-tier publication should not hinge its core theoretical claims on an unproven conjecture. This is a major weakness.
*   **Lemma 3 and "Spectral Longevity":** The method for determining the number of clusters K is presented as a formal lemma but is, in essence, a heuristic. The concept of "spectral longevity" (Definition 3) feels ad-hoc. The proof of Lemma 3 is a high-level intuitive argument about convergence rates rather than a rigorous demonstration. The formal presentation as a lemma overstates the strength of the claim.

**2. Clarity and Notational Issues:**

*   **Inconsistent and Non-Standard Notation:** The use of `1>M` in Equation (1) to denote a transposed vector of ones is highly non-standard. This is made more confusing in Equation (5) where `P >` is used for the transpose of a matrix. Standard notation (`\mathbf{1}^T`, `P^T`) should be used.
*   **Typographical Errors:** There is a clear typo in Equation (2), which uses `1>J` where it should be `1>M`. This sort of error suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
*   **Imprecise Definitions:**
    *   In Section 2.1, the definition `O(:, u) = E[xt|rt = u]` is only correct for certain emission types (e.g., Gaussian). For the Multinomial case, the columns of `O` are the probability vectors `p_u`, not their expectation. This is an important technical inaccuracy.
    *   Definition 1 introduces noise factors `α_k` and `β_{j,k}` that "act on" eigenvalues. This is a conceptually vague way to describe the perturbation of eigenvalues in a random matrix setting and lacks formal definition.
*   **Vague Language:** Phrases like "in a sufficiently large range" in Algorithm 1 are imprecise and hinder reproducibility.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

*   The conclusion from the real-data experiment (Section 4.3) significantly weakens the paper's overall message. The results show that the proposed spectral method is outperformed by randomly initialized EM, and its main utility is as an initialization scheme for EM. While this is a valid contribution, it positions the work as an incremental improvement (a better initialization) rather than the standalone, superior alternative to EM that the introduction implies.
*   In Section 4.1, the experiment is conducted with "2 sequences with length T for each cluster". This is an extremely small number of sequences (N=6 total) from which to draw robust conclusions about the de-permutation algorithm's performance with respect to data amount. This setup seems insufficient to support the claims.

**4. Presentation:**

*   The list of references is incomplete and poorly formatted. For instance, reference [3]'s title is repeated, and crucial information like venue, year, and page numbers is missing for most entries. This level of carelessness is unacceptable in a formal submission.

### Summary of Pros and Cons:

**Pro:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation of the MHMM learning problem.
*   Addresses the critical sub-problem of permutation ambiguity.
*   Demonstrates a clear computational speed-up over EM.

**Con:**
*   Core theoretical claims are not rigorously proven (Conjecture 1).
*   The model selection criterion (Lemma 3) is a heuristic presented with unjustified formality.
*   The manuscript is rife with notational errors, inconsistencies, and imprecisions.
*   Experimental results on real data position the method as an initialization scheme for EM, not a superior replacement, weakening its claimed significance.
*   The references are improperly formatted.

### Recommendation:

**Reject.**

While the paper is built upon a promising idea, the execution falls short of the standards expected for NIPS. The combination of significant theoretical gaps (a central result being a conjecture), a multitude of minor but distracting clarity and notational issues, and experimental conclusions that temper the work's significance makes the paper unsuitable for publication in its current form. The sheer number of minor flaws gives an overall impression of a rushed and unpolished manuscript. The authors should focus on providing rigorous proofs for their theoretical claims, thoroughly revise and proofread the manuscript to fix all notational and clarity issues, and be more precise about the contribution in light of the experimental results.
