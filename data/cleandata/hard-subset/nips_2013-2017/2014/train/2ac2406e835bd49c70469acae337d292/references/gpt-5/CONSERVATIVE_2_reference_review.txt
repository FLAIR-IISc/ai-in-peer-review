Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a spectral (method-of-moments, MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM can be represented as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, where each block corresponds to a component HMM. The authors first apply an existing spectral HMM learner to estimate a global HMM with MK states (M per component, K components), which yields parameters up to a global permutation of states. They then propose a de-permutation step based on the spectral structure of the estimated transition matrix: in the noiseless case, powers of the block-diagonal transition matrix converge to a block structure with K rank-1 blocks; under noise, they propose to retain the top-K eigen-components and cluster columns to recover the block assignment, and to select K via a “spectral longevity” metric derived from eigenvalue decay. Experiments on synthetic data and a small real dataset (UCI handwritten trajectories) compare their method to EM, claiming speed advantages and comparable clustering accuracy; they also suggest using the spectral method as an EM initializer.

The reduction of MHMM to a larger HMM is standard. Spectral learning for HMMs is well-studied (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012; Anandkumar et al.), with identifiability and sample complexity under rank/separability assumptions. The novelty here lies in attempting to recover per-component parameters from the permuted global state estimate by exploiting the eigenstructure of the (estimated) transition matrix and proposing a heuristic model-order selection.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the block-diagonal equivalence and its implications for using spectral HMM learners.
- The de-permutation idea is intuitive in the noiseless setting, and the lemmas/corollary formalize the basic spectral structure.
- The goal of avoiding the heavy per-sequence E-steps in EM is practically relevant; a fast initializer for EM could be valuable.
- Experiments suggest potential speed gains and that the method can sometimes serve as a reasonable initializer.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (soundness, support)
- The central step (de-permutation and K estimation under noise) relies on unproven conjectures. Conjecture 1 is key to justifying the low-rank reconstruction and clustering, yet the “justification” is informal and reuses a bound from a different setting. No finite-sample guarantees are provided for recovering the correct block assignment or for estimating K.
- The assumed condition “each Ak has only one eigenvalue equal to 1” is essentially aperiodicity and irreducibility; while standard, the method’s sensitivity to near-periodicity or small spectral gaps is not analyzed. The proposed “longevity” metric depends on delicate eigenvalue separations and appears heuristic; no comparison is made to established model-selection criteria (BIC, cross-validation, spectral gap heuristics).
- The de-permutation procedure clusters columns of a non-symmetric, potentially ill-conditioned matrix Ar. The choice of metric, stability under noise, and sensitivity to state similarity (e.g., similar stationary distributions across components) are not studied. Alternatives (e.g., spectral clustering on a symmetrized matrix, using left/right eigenvectors or stationary distributions explicitly) are not considered.
- The method assumes availability of the global state count MK for the MoM HMM learner, yet K is later “estimated.” Practically, how to choose M and MK when K is unknown is left unclear. The identifiability/rank conditions for Ō (L ≥ MK, full column rank) are assumed without discussion of their plausibility in real settings.
- Experiments are limited. Synthetic evaluations use small K, M, and only Gaussian emissions; there is no parameter recovery error (A, O) reported, only clustering accuracy. There is no robustness study vs. spectral gaps, component overlap, or model misspecification. Real-data results are pairwise class clustering with K=2; quantitative results (Table 1) are not shown in the submission, and the polynomial feature lifting to satisfy L ≥ MK fundamentally changes the generative model, raising fairness concerns for comparisons to EM.
- Runtime claims are qualitative; there is no clear accounting of moment computation cost vs. EM, hardware, or convergence criteria. EM is given four restarts; stronger baselines (more restarts, good initializations, or modern variational/SMC alternatives) are not explored.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but several derivations are informal. The “justification” of Conjecture 1 is weak and mixes notation (A, ĀP) and bounds (e.g., “≤ 2KM”) without clear constants or conditions.
- The algorithm depends critically on numerical eigendecomposition of a non-symmetric stochastic matrix; guidance on numerical stability, regularization, and implementation details is sparse.
- Some references are incomplete, and related work specific to MHMMs with spectral/graph-based decompositions is not thoroughly situated; the reduction to block-diagonal HMM and community detection-style approaches are likely known.

Originality
- The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is standard. Using the eigenstructure to identify blocks under noise is a plausible idea, but as presented, it is a heuristic extension without theoretical backing. The K-estimation via “longevity” seems ad hoc and is not compared to prior model-order selection techniques.

Significance
- Without guarantees or thorough empirical evidence, it is hard to assess lasting impact. The idea of a fast spectral initializer for MHMMs could be useful, but stronger validation across diverse settings, robust de-permutation, and comparisons to established model selection would be needed to influence practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous analysis: conditions under which the de-permutation recovers the true block assignment with high probability; explicit sample complexity in terms of spectral gaps, mixing, and rank conditions; and a proof (or a clear reduction to known results) for Conjecture 1.
- Evaluate robustness: vary spectral gaps, component similarity, periodicity, noise, and model misspecification; report parameter recovery error, not just clustering accuracy.
- Compare K-selection to BIC, cross-validated likelihood, and simple spectral gap heuristics; analyze failure modes.
- Clarify practical selection of M and MK when K is unknown; discuss identifiability requirements (L ≥ MK, full rank Ō) and their implications. Avoid non-generic feature lifts that break the generative assumptions or justify them carefully.
- Provide comprehensive runtime comparisons with clear protocols and stronger EM baselines.

Pros
- Addresses a computationally relevant problem (scalable MHMM learning).
- Conceptually simple pipeline leveraging established spectral HMM methods.
- Potential as an EM initializer.

Cons
- Core technical claims are heuristic; key results are left as conjectures.
- Limited and narrow empirical validation; no strong evidence of superiority.
- Dependence on restrictive identifiability and spectral gap assumptions not quantified.
- Model order selection and de-permutation are ad hoc and unvalidated against alternatives.

Recommendation: Reject. The paper presents an interesting idea but lacks theoretical guarantees and sufficiently rigorous empirical validation to justify acceptance at NeurIPS. Strengthening the theory and substantially expanding the experiments could make this a solid contribution.
