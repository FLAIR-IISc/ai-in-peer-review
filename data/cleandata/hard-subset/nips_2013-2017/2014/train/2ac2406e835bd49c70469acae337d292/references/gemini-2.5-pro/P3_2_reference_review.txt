This paper proposes a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) based on the method of moments (MoM). The key idea is to view an MHMM with K components, each having M hidden states, as a single large HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix of size MK x MK. The authors propose using an existing spectral algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this equivalent large HMM. The main technical challenge, and the core contribution of the paper, is addressing the permutation ambiguity of the learned states. Standard spectral learning recovers the model parameters only up to an unknown permutation of the hidden states, which in this case mixes states from different component HMMs, making it impossible to recover the individual models. The authors propose a "de-permutation" algorithm that leverages the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix. By analyzing the eigenvalues of the estimated transition matrix, they propose a method to identify the number of components K and subsequently cluster the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of the matrix to recover the block structure and thereby the individual HMM parameters.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel application of spectral learning to the MHMM problem. The observation that an MHMM is equivalent to an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix is a simple but powerful insight that forms a solid foundation for the proposed method. This connects a more complex hierarchical model to the well-studied literature on spectral learning for HMMs.

2.  **Addressing a Key Technical Hurdle:** The paper correctly identifies and tackles the most significant challenge of this approach: the permutation ambiguity. The proposed de-permutation strategy, based on analyzing the eigenspectrum of the estimated transition matrix, is clever and well-motivated. The noiseless case is intuitive, and the extension to the noisy case via a low-rank approximation based on the top K eigenvalues is a principled approach.

3.  **Computational Advantages:** The method provides a clear computational advantage over the standard EM algorithm, which can be prohibitively slow on large datasets due to the need for repeated forward-backward passes for every sequence. The proposed MoM approach shifts the computational burden to a one-time moment computation, with the subsequent learning step being independent of the data size. This is a significant practical contribution.

4.  **Potential as an Initialization Strategy:** The experiments convincingly show that even when the spectral method alone is not as accurate as EM, it serves as an excellent initialization for EM. This addresses one of EM's main weaknesses (sensitivity to initialization) and provides a practical, principled way to find a good starting point, which is a valuable contribution in its own right.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical support for the de-permutation algorithm in the noisy case is somewhat weak. Conjecture 1, which bounds the error of the low-rank reconstruction, is stated without proof and justified by analogy to a similar result for a different model from [1]. While plausible, this is a significant theoretical gap. A formal proof or at least a more detailed proof sketch justifying the extension to this model would substantially strengthen the paper.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of Model Selection:** The method for determining the number of clusters, K, via "spectral longevity" (Lemma 3), feels heuristic. The proposed metric `Lλ̃K′` is novel but also complex. The justification rests on the ratio of spectral gaps, which is sound intuition. However, it's unclear why this specific formulation is superior to simpler, more standard methods for finding an "elbow" in the eigenvalue plot, such as examining the spectral gap `|λ̃K'| / |λ̃K'+1|` directly. The paper would benefit from a comparison to or justification against these simpler heuristics.

3.  **Significant Practical Limitation:** A major limitation of the underlying spectral HMM algorithm, which this work inherits, is the constraint that the total number of hidden states must be less than the observation dimension (MK < L). This is a very strong requirement. The authors correctly identify and circumvent this in their real-data experiment by applying a polynomial feature expansion. However, the implications of this are not discussed. This constraint severely limits the direct applicability of the method to problems with low-dimensional observations and a moderate number of states/clusters, which are common. The paper should explicitly state this limitation and discuss the trade-offs of using feature expansion (e.g., increased computational cost for moment calculation, potential for overfitting).

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are promising but could be more comprehensive. In the real data experiment (Table 1), the spectral method on its own is outperformed by randomly initialized EM in 2 out of 3 cases. While it shines as an initializer, this tempers the claim that it is a competitive standalone alternative. It would be beneficial to see results on a wider range of real-world datasets, perhaps including one where `MK < L` holds naturally, to better assess its performance without engineered features.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and interesting approach to a well-known problem. The formulation is elegant, and the proposed solution to the permutation problem is clever. The work makes a solid contribution by providing a computationally efficient alternative to EM for learning MHMMs, and demonstrates particular promise as a principled initialization strategy.

The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical rigor for the noisy case analysis and the failure to adequately discuss the strong `MK < L` constraint. Despite these issues, the novelty of the approach and its practical implications are significant. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and advances the state of the art in learning latent variable models. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the theoretical gaps and practical limitations more directly in the final version.
