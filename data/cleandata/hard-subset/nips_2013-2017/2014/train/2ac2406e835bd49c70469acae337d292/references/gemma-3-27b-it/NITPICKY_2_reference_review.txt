This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The authors leverage the observation that an MHMM can be represented as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix and then apply existing MoM techniques for HMMs, followed by a spectral de-permutation step to recover individual HMM parameters. The core idea is appealing, as it aims to circumvent the computational bottleneck of EM for large datasets. However, a multitude of issues, ranging from notational inconsistencies and missing details to questionable assumptions and a lack of thorough experimental validation, lead me to recommend rejection.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency & Lack of Rigor:** The paper is riddled with inconsistencies. For instance, the colon notation for matrix indexing (e.g., O(:, u)) is used but never fully explained for readers unfamiliar with MATLAB. More critically, the transition from summation notation to matrix notation in Equation (1) is abrupt and lacks justification; it’s not immediately clear *why* this manipulation is valid. The definition of O(xt) is circular – it defines O(xt) *as* p(xt| :, O).
* **Oversimplification & Missing Details:** The spectral learning section is particularly weak. The “spectral conditions” needed for recovering the number of HMMs (K) are stated as conjectures without sufficient theoretical grounding. Lemma 2 asserts an eigenvalue of 1 for each HMM, which, while true in ideal cases, is unlikely to hold exactly in practice due to estimation noise. This crucial point is acknowledged but not adequately addressed. The derivation of the de-permutation mapping is hand-wavy and relies on Lemma 3, which itself introduces new, ill-defined terms (Lλ̃K′) without adequate explanation.  The justification for the O(1/√TN) error bound relies on a citation [1] without establishing sufficient similarity between that work's assumptions and the current problem.
* **Questionable Assumptions:** The algorithm relies heavily on the assumption that local transition matrices (A1:K) have only one eigenvalue equal to 1. This is a strong assumption rarely met in real-world scenarios. The impact of deviations from this assumption is not discussed.
* **Experimental Flaws:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. While results are presented, the experimental parameters (e.g., specifics of the Gaussian observation model beyond variance) are not fully specified. The comparison with EM is limited and doesn’t comprehensively evaluate the computational advantages claimed. The real-data experiment utilizes a "cubic polynomial feature transformation" without clear motivation. The claim that the spectral algorithm serves as a “good initialization scheme” is based on limited evidence - a single comparison against random and MoM initializations. The number of trials and statistical significance are not reported.
* **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. The sensitivity to noise, the impact of violating the eigenvalue assumption, and the scalability to high-dimensional observation spaces are not addressed.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Idea:** The application of MoM to MHMMs, combined with a spectral de-permutation technique, is a novel concept.
* **Potential for Speedup:**  The claim of avoiding the computational cost of EM for large datasets is promising, *if* the algorithm can be made robust and scalable.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

* Significant technical flaws and inconsistencies.
* Lack of rigorous theoretical justification for key claims.
* Insufficient experimental validation and missing details.
* Numerous assumptions that are unlikely to hold in practice.
* Limited discussion of limitations.

**For Acceptance:**

* Novelty of the approach.
* Potential computational advantage (unproven).

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper suffers from significant technical deficiencies, a lack of rigor, and inadequate experimental validation. The numerous weaknesses outweigh the strengths, and the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS.  The authors would need to address the aforementioned issues – providing a more thorough theoretical analysis, strengthening the experimental setup, and clarifying the assumptions – for the paper to be reconsidered. The paper reads more like an initial exploration of an idea than a complete, well-supported scientific contribution.
