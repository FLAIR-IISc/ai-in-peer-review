

**Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"**

This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), addressing the computational bottleneck of EM-based methods for large datasets. The key insight is recognizing an MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, enabling adaptation of spectral learning techniques for HMMs (e.g., Anandkumar et al., COLT 2012). The authors develop a de-permutation algorithm using spectral properties of the global transition matrix to recover individual HMM parameters, and propose a "spectral longevity" metric to estimate the number of clusters. The work builds on established spectral learning frameworks but extends them to a more complex model class, with experiments demonstrating speed advantages over EM while maintaining comparable accuracy.

**Strengths**  
1. **Clear motivation and problem framing**: The paper effectively highlights the computational limitations of EM for large-scale MHMM learning (e.g., forward-backward passes per sequence) and positions MoM as a scalable alternative.  
2. **Novel technical contribution**: The block-diagonal transition matrix observation (Lemma 1) is insightful and directly enables the spectral approach. The de-permutation via eigenvector clustering (Section 3.2.2) is a well-motivated solution to a non-trivial challenge in MHMM learning.  
3. **Thorough experimental validation**: Experiments cover noise robustness (Fig. 3), scalability (Fig. 4), and real-world utility (Table 1). The real-data experiment demonstrates practical value as an EM initialization, a significant practical contribution.  
4. **Strong organization**: The paper is logically structured, with clear definitions, lemmas, and a concise algorithm (Algorithm 1). Figures 1–2 effectively illustrate spectral properties.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Theoretical gaps**:  
   - Conjecture 1 (error bound for rank-K reconstruction) lacks rigorous proof. The justification reuses a bound from a *different* model (mixture models in [1]) without addressing structural differences between MHMMs and mixtures. This weakens the theoretical foundation.  
   - Lemma 2’s proof is terse; the connection between eigenvalue decomposition and stationary distributions requires more elaboration (e.g., why the limit matrix has blocks of *stationary* distributions).  
2. **Practical limitations in experiments**:  
   - The real-data experiment (Section 4.3) transforms features via a cubic polynomial to satisfy *MK < L* (required for MoM). While justifiable, this adds complexity and may not generalize to other datasets. The paper should discuss how to handle cases where *MK ≥ L* (e.g., via dimensionality reduction).  
   - No comparison to alternative MHMM learning methods (e.g., [9] uses MoM for EM initialization but not as a standalone method). The claim that spectral learning "serves as a very good initialization" needs stronger evidence (e.g., EM accuracy with spectral vs. random initialization).  
3. **Clarity of key concepts**:  
   - "Spectral longevity" (Definition 3) is under-explained. The interpretation in Lemma 3 is mathematically precise but lacks intuitive motivation (e.g., why this metric identifies *K*). A simple synthetic example would help.  
   - The noise model (Definition 1) is abstract; it would benefit from concrete examples (e.g., how *αk* and *βj,k* arise in practice).

**Significance and Originality**  
The work is a meaningful extension of spectral learning to MHMMs, a model with applications in sequence clustering (e.g., [4, 5, 6]). While the core idea (block-diagonal transition matrix) is clever, the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The paper’s significance lies in its practical scalability and initialization utility, but it does not advance the *state of the art* in accuracy or theoretical guarantees over existing methods. For instance, the accuracy is comparable to EM (Fig. 4), not superior.

**Recommendations for Revision**  
1. **Strengthen theoretical claims**: Prove Conjecture 1 or explicitly state its limitations (e.g., "under the assumption that noise is sufficiently small, as in [1]"). Re-evaluate the error bound for MHMMs, not just reuse [1].  
2. **Address *MK ≥ L* in practice**: Discuss how to handle this constraint (e.g., via PCA or feature selection) and test on a dataset where *MK ≥ L* without transformation.  
3. **Improve clarity**:  
   - Add a short example illustrating "spectral longevity" (e.g., a 2-cluster case with explicit eigenvalues).  
   - Expand Lemma 2’s proof to clarify the stationary distribution derivation.  
4. **Enhance experimental comparisons**:  
   - Compare against [9]’s MoM-initialized EM (not just random EM) to quantify the initialization benefit.  
   - Include runtime comparisons for *very large datasets* (e.g., 10k sequences) to emphasize scalability.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to scalable MHMM learning. The core idea is well-executed, and the experiments demonstrate practical value. However, the theoretical gaps and limited discussion of *MK < L* constraints prevent it from being a strong NIPS contribution. With rigorous theoretical revisions and expanded experiments addressing the *MK ≥ L* limitation, this could be a solid acceptance. As-is, it is borderline due to the unproven conjecture and insufficient practical validation.  

**Decision**: **Accept with Major Revisions**  
*Pro*: Clear motivation, novel de-permutation approach, strong experimental validation.  
*Con*: Unproven theoretical claims, limited discussion of *MK < L*, insufficient comparison to related methods.
