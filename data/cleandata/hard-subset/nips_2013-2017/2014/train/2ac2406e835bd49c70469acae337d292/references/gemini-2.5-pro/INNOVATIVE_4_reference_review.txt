### Summary
This paper proposes a novel method for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM) based on the method of moments (spectral learning). The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components and M hidden states each as a single, larger HMM with a total of MK hidden states and a block-diagonal transition matrix. The authors then apply an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs to learn the parameters of this larger model. This standard approach, however, recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the MK states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this work is a spectral de-permutation algorithm to resolve this ambiguity. By analyzing the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the learned global transition matrix, the authors propose a method to cluster the hidden states, thereby recovering the block structure and identifying the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also suggests a heuristic based on the spectrum of the transition matrix to estimate the number of mixture components, K. The work builds upon the foundational spectral learning literature for latent variable models (e.g., Hsu et al., 2012; Anandkumar et al., 2012) and presents an alternative to the widely-used Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this model class.

### Strengths
The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and conceptual elegance. The approach of learning an MHMM by first treating it as a large, structured HMM and then devising a method to recover that structure is a bold and creative idea. It moves beyond the standard iterative EM framework and offers a fresh perspective on learning mixture models of sequential data.

1.  **Significant Novelty:** The central contribution—the spectral de-permutation of the global transition matrix—is a clever solution to a fundamental problem that arises when applying existing spectral methods to this model. Instead of treating the permutation as an unavoidable nuisance, the authors show how to exploit the underlying block-diagonal structure, which should be partially preserved in the learned matrix, to undo the permutation. This is a significant conceptual leap.

2.  **Principled, Non-Iterative Approach:** The proposed algorithm is a direct, non-iterative method that avoids the common pitfalls of EM, such as convergence to poor local optima and sensitivity to initialization. While spectral methods have their own sensitivities (e.g., to noise), providing a globally consistent estimator for a model as complex as an MHMM is a valuable contribution to the field.

3.  **Computational Advantage:** By design, the method is computationally efficient for large datasets. Once the low-order moments are computed in a single pass over the data, the core parameter estimation steps (matrix factorization, eigendecomposition, clustering) are independent of the number of sequences, offering a substantial speed-up over EM, which requires repeated passes.

4.  **Model Selection:** The paper commendably attempts to address the problem of model selection (i.e., estimating the number of clusters K). The proposed "spectral longevity" metric is an interesting heuristic that leverages the core spectral ideas of the paper. While not fully fleshed out, it is an ambitious addition that is often missing in papers that propose new learning algorithms.

### Weaknesses
The paper's boldness is also a source of its main weaknesses. The theoretical and empirical validation is not as complete as one might hope for, leaving some important questions unanswered.

1.  **Incomplete Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical underpinnings of the de-permutation method rely on "Conjecture 1," which provides a finite-sample error bound for the low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix. The authors state that a full analysis is "out of the scope of this paper" and simply reuse a bound from a related but different model. This is a significant theoretical gap. Without a formal proof or a more rigorous justification for this conjecture, the guarantees of the proposed method remain speculative.

2.  **Unanalyzed Assumptions:** The success of the algorithm hinges on a critical spectral gap condition: the K "global" eigenvalues must remain separated from the "local" eigenvalues after being perturbed by estimation noise. The paper states this condition but does not provide any analysis of how restrictive it is, how it relates to the underlying MHMM parameters (e.g., mixing rates of the component HMMs), or how robust the method is to violations of this assumption.

3.  **Underwhelming Empirical Results:** The experiments, while demonstrating the algorithm's mechanics, do not make a compelling case for its superiority over the standard EM baseline. The method is shown to be faster but less accurate than EM, particularly in low-data regimes. The real-data experiment is also inconclusive; the spectral method alone does not outperform randomly initialized EM, and its main demonstrated utility is as an initialization scheme for EM. This somewhat undermines its positioning as a standalone alternative. The proof-of-concept experiment in Figure 3 validates the de-permutation idea but does not evaluate the end-to-end performance on the primary task of sequence clustering.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   The paper introduces a highly novel and elegant conceptual framework for a well-known and important problem. The idea of spectrally identifying and undoing the permutation to recover the MHMM structure is a significant contribution.
*   It represents a bold departure from the dominant EM paradigm, offering a new direction for research into learning mixtures of complex sequence models.
*   The potential for computational savings on large datasets is substantial.

### Arguments for Rejection
*   The core theoretical claims are presented as an unproven conjecture, weakening the paper's technical soundness.
*   The empirical evaluation is preliminary and fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over standard methods in terms of accuracy.
*   The practical conditions under which the method is expected to succeed are not well-characterized.

### Recommendation
I recommend **acceptance**.

This paper embodies the kind of creative, high-risk research that NIPS should champion. The core idea is exceptionally novel and provides a genuinely new way of thinking about learning MHMMs. While the theoretical analysis is incomplete and the experimental results are not a clear-cut win, these are weaknesses that can be addressed in future work. The fundamental conceptual contribution—identifying the permutation problem and solving it by spectrally exploiting the model's latent block structure—is significant and likely to inspire follow-up research. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation because the boldness and originality of the approach are so high. This paper prioritizes a powerful new idea over methodological perfection, and in my view, that is a trade-off worth making. It advances a new direction rather than making a small, incremental improvement on an existing one.
