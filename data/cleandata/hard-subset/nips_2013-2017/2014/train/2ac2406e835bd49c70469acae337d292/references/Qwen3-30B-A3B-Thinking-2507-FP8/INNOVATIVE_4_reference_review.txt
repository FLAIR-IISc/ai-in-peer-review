

# Review of "Spectral Learning of Mixture of Hidden Markov Models"

This paper presents a novel spectral learning approach for Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs), a significant extension of existing spectral learning techniques for standard HMMs. The key insight is recognizing that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix, which allows leveraging existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs. The authors then address the critical challenge of permutation ambiguity that arises from spectral learning, proposing a novel method to recover the de-permutation mapping using spectral properties of the global transition matrix.

The paper makes a bold conceptual contribution by bridging two important models (HMMs and MHMMs) through a spectral learning framework. While spectral learning for standard HMMs has been well-established [1,2,3], extending this to MHMMs has been challenging due to the permutation ambiguity issue. The authors' insight about the block-diagonal structure of the global transition matrix is elegant and novel, providing a clean theoretical foundation for their approach. Their solution to the permutation problem—using the spectral longevity measure to estimate the number of clusters and identify the de-permutation mapping—is particularly clever and represents a significant conceptual advance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with good mathematical notation and logical flow. The theoretical development (Lemma 1, Lemma 2, Conjecture 1, Lemma 3) provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach, and the experimental validation (synthetic data, real-world character trajectory dataset) supports their claims about computational efficiency and accuracy. The results show that their spectral approach is comparable to EM in accuracy but substantially faster, especially for larger datasets. The real data experiment also demonstrates that their method can serve as an effective initialization for EM, which is a valuable practical contribution.

The significance of this work is substantial. MHMMs are widely used for sequence clustering, but EM-based learning is computationally prohibitive for large datasets due to the per-sequence forward-backward computations. The proposed spectral approach offers a computationally lighter alternative with similar accuracy, which could enable MHMM applications on much larger datasets. The theoretical insight about the block-diagonal structure of MHMMs as an HMM is itself valuable and could inspire extensions to other hierarchical latent variable models.

While the paper is strong in novelty and significance, it has a few minor weaknesses that don't detract from its overall contribution. The theoretical analysis includes a conjecture (Conjecture 1) that is not fully proven, which is acceptable in a NeurIPS paper given the focus on novel concepts. The real data experiment uses a cubic polynomial feature transformation to satisfy the MK < L condition required for MoM learning, which may seem artificial but is a practical workaround for the specific dataset. The paper could have included more comparisons with alternative MHMM learning approaches, though the comparison with EM is sufficient to demonstrate the computational advantage.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy for readers to follow the key insights. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the spectral properties and algorithmic process. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and future work, including potential applications to other hierarchical models.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by introducing a novel perspective on MHMMs that enables efficient learning through spectral methods. The conceptual insight about viewing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transition matrices is elegant and powerful, and the solution to the permutation ambiguity problem is particularly noteworthy. The authors have demonstrated the practical utility of their approach through comprehensive experiments that validate both the theoretical claims and computational advantages.

The novelty of this work is significant—it's not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but rather a new conceptual framework for approaching MHMM learning. While the paper could have included more theoretical guarantees, the bold approach and strong empirical results make up for these minor shortcomings. The work represents a clear step forward in making MHMMs more practical for large-scale applications.

In summary, this paper offers a novel and elegant solution to an important problem in sequence modeling. The key insight about the block-diagonal structure of MHMMs is both simple and profound, and the authors have developed a practical algorithm based on this insight. The computational advantages over EM are compelling, and the approach could have significant impact on real-world applications involving sequence clustering.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant conceptual advance in spectral learning that extends beyond the immediate problem to potentially inspire new approaches for other hierarchical latent variable models. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of machine learning, particularly for sequence modeling applications.
