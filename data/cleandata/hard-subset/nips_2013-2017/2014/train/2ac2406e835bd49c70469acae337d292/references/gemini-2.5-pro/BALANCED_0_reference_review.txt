### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method of moments (MoM) based algorithm for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The standard approach for learning MHMMs, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, can be computationally expensive for large datasets due to the need for repeated forward-backward passes for every sequence. The authors propose a more efficient spectral learning approach. The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M states) as a single, larger HMM with a total of MK states. This equivalent HMM has a specific block-diagonal structure in its transition matrix. The authors leverage existing spectral learning algorithms for HMMs [1, 2, 3] to estimate the parameters of this large HMM. However, this estimation recovers the parameters only up to a permutation of the MK states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of the paper is a novel de-permutation algorithm that uses the spectral properties of the estimated global transition matrix to cluster the permuted states back into their K original groups, thereby recovering the parameters of the individual HMMs. The paper also proposes a method for estimating the number of mixture components, K, based on the spectral properties of the transition matrix. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data, highlighting its significant speed advantage over EM.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and practical problem of efficiently learning MHMMs. The proposed approach is novel and provides a principled, non-iterative alternative to the widely used EM algorithm. The observation that an MHMM can be viewed as a large HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix is elegant, and the subsequent de-permutation strategy based on the spectral properties of this matrix is a clever and significant contribution. This work extends the applicability of spectral learning methods to a more complex and widely used class of hierarchical models.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors first establish the theoretical equivalence between an MHMM and a structured HMM (Lemma 1), then clearly articulate the permutation problem that arises from standard spectral learning. The proposed solution is developed logically, first for a noiseless ideal case and then extended to the more realistic noisy setting. The accompanying figures (especially Figure 1) provide excellent intuition for the de-permutation idea.

3.  **Potential for Impact:** The proposed algorithm offers substantial computational advantages over EM, as its learning time is independent of the dataset size after the initial moment computation step. This makes it particularly attractive for large-scale sequence clustering tasks. Furthermore, as shown in the real-data experiment, the method can serve as a high-quality, efficient initialization for EM, which can help mitigate EM's sensitivity to poor initializations and potentially lead to better final models.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical foundation for the algorithm's performance in the presence of noise relies on "Conjecture 1." While the justification is intuitive and references related work [1], it is not a formal proof tailored to this specific problem. The performance of the entire de-permutation scheme in the noisy case hinges on this conjecture being true and the error bound being tight enough. The lack of a rigorous proof weakens the paper's theoretical guarantees. Similarly, the conditions for Lemma 3 (for estimating K) appear quite strong and may not hold in practice, especially when the spectral gap is small or the noise is high.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section, while demonstrating the method's promise, could be more comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The real-data experiment (Table 1) shows that the proposed spectral method on its own is often outperformed by randomly initialized EM. While it proves to be an excellent initializer for EM, this result somewhat tempers the claim that it is a strong standalone alternative.
    *   The method for estimating the number of components K (based on "spectral longevity") is a key part of the proposed algorithm but is not empirically validated in the experiments. An experiment on synthetic data showing the performance of this estimator would significantly strengthen the paper.
    *   The experimental setup in Section 4.1 uses only 2 sequences per cluster, which is an extremely small dataset. While the results appear positive, this setup may not be representative of the challenges in real-world scenarios. It is also unclear if this was a typo.
    *   The comparison with EM is fair but could be strengthened by including error bars in Figure 4 to show result variance and by providing more details on the EM implementation and its convergence criteria.

3.  **Practical Limitations:** The underlying MoM for HMMs requires that the number of observable features L be greater than the total number of hidden states MK. This can be a significant limitation. The paper addresses this by using a polynomial feature expansion, but this feels like an ad-hoc fix. A more thorough discussion of this constraint and its implications would be beneficial.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Strengthening Theoretical Claims:** It would substantially improve the paper if the authors could provide a formal proof for Conjecture 1, or at least a more detailed theoretical sketch. If a full proof is out of scope, a dedicated empirical study designed to validate the conjecture's claims (e.g., showing how the Frobenius norm error scales with the amount of data T and N) would be a valuable addition.

2.  **Expanding Experimental Validation:**
    *   Please add an experiment that explicitly tests the proposed method for estimating K. A plot showing the "spectral longevity" metric versus the true K on synthetic data would be very insightful.
    *   To better showcase the method's strengths, consider applying it to a larger-scale dataset where the computational cost of EM would be truly prohibitive, making the spectral approach not just faster but essential.
    *   Please clarify the experimental setup in Section 4.1. If only 6 sequences were used in total, please justify this choice or run the experiment with a more conventional dataset size.
    *   In the real-data experiment, it would be interesting to also compare against other common initialization strategies for EM, such as using k-means on some summary statistics of the sequences, to better contextualize the performance of the MoM-based initialization.

3.  **Addressing Limitations:** The paper would be more balanced if it included a more explicit discussion of the `L > MK` limitation in the main text (perhaps in the conclusion or a dedicated discussion section), outlining when this might be a problem and what general strategies (beyond the specific feature expansion used) might exist to mitigate it.

### **Decision**

This is a strong paper with a novel and elegant core idea. It presents a valuable contribution to the literature on learning latent variable models for sequential data. The main strengths are the originality of the spectral de-permutation approach and its potential for significant computational savings over traditional methods. However, the paper is weakened by the lack of a rigorous proof for its key theoretical conjecture and an experimental section that, while promising, could be more comprehensive in validating all aspects of the proposed algorithm (particularly the estimation of K) and in demonstrating its standalone utility on real data.

The paper is borderline, but the novelty and potential impact are high. I lean toward acceptance, with the strong recommendation that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, especially by adding an experiment to validate the K-estimation procedure and by either strengthening the justification for Conjecture 1 or being more explicit about its unproven nature.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant method for a significant problem.
*   Clear presentation of a complex idea.
*   Strong potential for impact, both as a standalone algorithm and as an initialization for EM.
*   Addresses a well-known bottleneck (computational cost) of a standard algorithm (EM for MHMMs).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Key theoretical result for the noisy case is presented as an unproven conjecture.
*   Experimental validation is incomplete (e.g., no test of the K-estimation method).
*   Real-data results do not show a clear win for the standalone method over a standard baseline.
