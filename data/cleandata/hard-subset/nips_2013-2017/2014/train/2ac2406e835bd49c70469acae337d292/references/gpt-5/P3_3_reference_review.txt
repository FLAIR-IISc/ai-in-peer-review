Summary
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block diagonal. The authors apply an existing spectral HMM learner to estimate the global parameters up to a permutation over the MK states, then exploit spectral structure of the global transition matrix to “de-permute” and recover per-component parameters. In the noiseless case, taking powers of the transition matrix separates columns into K clusters corresponding to components; with noise, they propose selecting the top-K “global” eigenvalues via a heuristic “spectral longevity” score and clustering columns of a low-rank reconstruction to infer the permutation and K. Experiments on synthetic data and a small UCI trajectory benchmark suggest the approach is faster than EM and can serve as a good initializer.

Relation to prior work
The spectral/MoM learning of HMMs and mixtures is well studied (e.g., Hsu et al. JCSS 2012; Anandkumar et al. tensor methods). The novelty here lies less in the equivalence (HMM block-diagonalization is standard) and more in the practical de-permutation strategy based on eigenstructure of the block-diagonal transition, and in a heuristic for estimating K from the spectrum. Prior work on spectral learning for mixtures of simpler models (e.g., mixtures of regressions) is cited, but literature on perturbation bounds for invariant subspaces (e.g., Davis–Kahan/Wedin) and observable operator models/predictive state representations that cover continuous emissions is not discussed.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual reduction from MHMM to HMM and a simple, implementable pipeline.
- Sensible use of the asymptotic structure of powers of a block-diagonal stochastic matrix to recover component partitions.
- Provides a procedure to estimate K when not given, with a concrete spectral heuristic.
- Empirical results, though limited, show speed gains and that the spectral learner can be a strong initializer for EM.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- The core de-permutation step in noise relies on Conjecture 1 and an informal borrowing of sample complexity from a different model class. This is a major gap: the guarantees are the main technical contribution needed to elevate the work beyond a heuristic.
- The eigen-analysis assumes each Ak is irreducible and aperiodic (unique eigenvalue at 1, others < 1 in magnitude) and that Ā is diagonalizable. These assumptions should be stated explicitly. Non-normality of Ā means eigenvectors can be very unstable to perturbations; relying on eigen-decomposition (vs invariant subspaces/SVD) can be numerically brittle.
- The proposed spectral longevity measure for K selection is ad hoc. No robustness analysis is provided for close eigenvalue clusters (e.g., components with slow mixing where λ2,k ≈ 1).
- Identifiability and rank conditions are underspecified. The spectral HMM method typically requires O to be full column rank; here Ō is L×(MK), so the method requires L ≥ MK and well-conditioned Ō. This is acknowledged in the UCI experiment via feature lifting, but broader implications (e.g., many practical settings have L ≪ MK) and potential remedies (multi-view features, stacking, random projections) are not analyzed.
- Parameter recovery is incomplete in the algorithm description: ν̄ equals concatenated πk νk. After de-permutation, πk and νk can be separated by normalizing each block of ν̄, but the paper does not specify this (it only mentions O and A).
- It is not discussed whether over-specifying MK for the HMM MoM step and then pruning via K selection is viable, nor the effect of mis-specified M or K on recovery.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but several derivations are compressed or informal. The “noise” model is not formalized; αk, βj,k are introduced without a clear perturbation framework.
- Algorithm 1 omits crucial details (how to choose the exponent range; the clustering distance metric; how to permute states consistently across Ō and Ā; how to extract π and ν).
- Some notation issues: the use of “e → ∞” and limits of powers for noisy matrices is informal; distinctions between true and estimated quantities are sometimes dropped.

Originality
- Viewing MHMM as an HMM with block structure is standard. The novelty is the de-permutation via clustering columns of a low-rank reconstruction of Ā and the longevity criterion for K. This is a modest and primarily algorithmic contribution; theoretical novelty is limited and largely conjectural.

Significance
- If made robust, the approach could be a practical alternative/initializer to EM for large datasets. However, the requirement L ≥ MK severely limits applicability, and the lack of theory hinders adoption. The experimental validation is too small-scale to demonstrate clear advantages in realistic regimes (larger K, M; slow-mixing chains; varying L).

Experiments
- Synthetic studies are small and do not vary key stressors (spectral gaps of Ak, condition numbers of Ō, mis-specified MK).
- Real data experiment is limited to two-way clustering with feature lifting; results suggest usefulness as an EM initializer, but broader evaluations (more classes, comparisons with strong initializations such as k-means on sufficient statistics) are missing.
- Speed claims would benefit from concrete wall-clock numbers and a breakdown (moment estimation vs eigendecomposition). Also compare against batched/parallel EM.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal perturbation analysis for the de-permutation step using invariant subspace perturbation bounds (Davis–Kahan/Wedin) under explicit assumptions on spectral gaps and noise, leading to a sufficient condition for correct clustering of columns and recovery of K.
- Clarify identifiability and rank conditions on Ō, and discuss strategies when L < MK (multi-view features, stacking consecutive observations, random projections, or tensor methods).
- Specify how π and ν are recovered and analyze sensitivity.
- Evaluate on larger synthetic suites varying K, M, spectral gaps, and conditioning; include mis-specification studies and robustness to over/underestimation of MK.
- Provide implementation details for longevity computation and clustering, and ablate these choices.
- Broaden related work, including PSR/observable operator models and spectral learning with continuous emissions.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive algorithm; potentially large runtime savings over EM.
- Addresses a relevant problem (sequence clustering with MHMMs).
- Shows promise as an EM initializer.

Cons
- Lacks theoretical guarantees where they matter most; key results are conjectural.
- Strong and restrictive assumptions (L ≥ MK; well-separated spectra).
- Limited empirical validation and scalability analysis.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is appealing and could be impactful, but the current version lacks the theoretical and empirical depth needed for NeurIPS. Strengthening perturbation guarantees, broadening experiments, and clarifying assumptions would significantly improve the paper.

Reviewer confidence: High.
