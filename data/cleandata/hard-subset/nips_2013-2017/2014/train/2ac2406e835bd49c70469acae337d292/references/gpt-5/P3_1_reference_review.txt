Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a method-of-moments (MoM)/spectral approach to learn Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) without EM. The key observation is that an MHMM with K components and M states per component is equivalent to a single HMM with MK states whose transition matrix is block-diagonal. The authors first apply a spectral HMM learner to estimate the global HMM parameters up to a permutation of states. They then attempt to “de-permute” to recover component-wise parameters by exploiting spectral structure: in the noiseless case, the block-diagonal transition has K unit eigenvalues and A^e converges to a matrix whose columns cluster by block; in the noisy case, they propose a low-rank reconstruction using the top K eigenvalues and cluster the columns to infer the permutation. They also propose a heuristic (“spectral longevity”) to estimate K by tracking how many eigenvalues remain close to 1 under exponentiation.

This builds on spectral learning for HMMs (Hsu, Kakade, Zhang 2012) and MoM/tensor methods for latent variable models (Anandkumar et al.), and on classical MHMMs (Smyth 1997). The idea of using the top eigenvectors/eigenvalues of a nearly block-diagonal Markov chain to uncover metastable blocks is related to work in nearly uncoupled Markov chains and metastability (e.g., Meyer 1989; PCCA+ in molecular dynamics), although those literatures are not cited.

Strengths:
- Simple and appealing reduction from MHMM to a block-diagonal HMM, allowing reuse of spectral HMM machinery.
- Clear intuition for the de-permutation step: power iteration isolates block structure; clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction is natural.
- Practical value: avoids repeated forward-backward over large datasets; experiments suggest substantial speedups over EM and usefulness as an EM initializer.
- The paper attempts to address model selection (estimating K) from spectral data.

Weaknesses and technical concerns:
- The central step in the noisy case lacks rigorous guarantees. Conjecture 1 is the key claim underpinning both de-permutation and K estimation, but its “justification” is informal and reuses bounds from different settings. There is no perturbation analysis (e.g., via Davis–Kahan or Wedin) to show that the top-K invariant subspace and the rank-K reconstruction are close under sampling noise, nor conditions on eigengaps that ensure column clustering succeeds with high probability.
- The assumption that the spectral HMM learner recovers Ō, Ā up to only a permutation is not substantiated. Many spectral HMM methods recover an observable representation up to an invertible transform and require additional steps/assumptions (e.g., full column rank of O, choice of features) to map back to O and A uniquely. This should be clarified along with the exact MoM variant used for continuous observations.
- Identifiability: the de-permutation relies on distinct columns of lim_{e→∞} A^e being “clusterable” across blocks. This requires, at minimum, that stationary distributions vk differ across components and that within-block columns are sufficiently separated from across-block columns under noise. No separation conditions or lower bounds on inter-cluster vs intra-cluster distances are provided. If two Ak have identical stationary distributions (or similar), clustering may fail.
- The K estimator (“spectral longevity”) is ad hoc and its “Lemma 3” is heuristic. Standard eigengap, stability-based criteria, or information criteria with predictive likelihood would be more defensible. Ties to known results on nearly uncoupled chains (where the multiplicity of eigenvalues near 1 corresponds to metastable sets) would strengthen the argument.
- The condition MK < L is only briefly mentioned but is critical and restrictive: the observable operator approach requires the emission means to span an MK-dimensional space. This becomes prohibitive for moderate K and M, and the workaround (polynomial feature expansion) changes the observation model and can harm conditioning/sample complexity. The paper should discuss these trade-offs and compare to tensor methods that can avoid such scaling with L by using multi-view time slices.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Synthetic studies explore small K, M and very small numbers of sequences. There is no systematic stress test of the de-permutation under (i) small eigengaps (slow mixing, similar stationary distributions), (ii) model mismatch (non-spherical noise, non-Gaussian), (iii) mis-specified K or M, or (iv) larger MK. The real-data experiment is encouraging as an initializer but does not establish stand-alone robustness.
- Some of the mathematical exposition is sloppy: bounds like ‖Ār̄‖F ≤ 2KM are incorrect/dimensionally loose; several equalities treat A entries as in [0,1] but do not exploit stochasticity; notation occasionally conflates Ā, its permuted estimate, and their powers.

Quality: The idea is interesting and parts are technically sound in the noiseless setting, but the main contributions depend on unproven heuristics. The empirical section is not strong enough to compensate for the lack of theory.

Clarity: Generally readable, with helpful intuition, but proofs and assumptions need tightening. Please specify precisely the MoM procedure, observation feature maps, conditions (ergodicity/aperiodicity, full-rank emissions), and computational complexity.

Originality: The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is straightforward but useful; the de-permutation via spectral clustering of A’s columns and the “longevity” heuristic are new to my knowledge in the MHMM context, though related ideas exist in metastable Markov chain analysis. Related work on nearly uncoupled chains and spectral clustering of Markov processes should be cited and contrasted.

Significance: If made rigorous and robust, the approach could be impactful for scalable sequence clustering. As written, the contribution is promising but preliminary.

Suggestions/questions for authors:
- Provide a perturbation analysis for the de-permutation: assume an eigengap between the K “global” eigenvalues and the rest, and derive bounds (via Davis–Kahan) on subspace error and on the within-/between-cluster distances of Ar’s columns. State explicit separation conditions guaranteeing correct clustering.
- Clarify the spectral HMM subroutine and its guarantees for continuous observations: what moments/features are used, under what conditions is the recovery up to a permutation only?
- Replace or support the K estimator with a more standard criterion (eigengap near 1, stability across e, or a likelihood-based model selection with a brief EM refinement).
- Discuss identifiability (distinct stationary distributions or other block signatures) and failure modes when Ak share similar stationary distributions or mixing rates.
- Expand experiments: vary eigengaps, K, M, sequence counts and lengths; report K estimation accuracy; include multinomial/Poisson; compare to tensor-based MHMM approaches if available; show scalability in MK.
- Minor: explicitly assume each Ak is ergodic and aperiodic; clean up notation and misplaced equalities; detail the complexity in terms of N, T, L, M, K.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a relevant problem with a simple, potentially scalable idea.
- Clear intuition; promising empirical speedups; useful as EM initialization.

Cons for acceptance:
- Key steps are conjectural; lack of theoretical guarantees.
- Limited and favorable experiments; stringent identifiability condition MK < L without deeper discussion.
- Missing related literature on spectral analysis of nearly block-diagonal Markov chains.

Overall, I view this as a promising direction that needs stronger theory and evaluation before publication at NeurIPS.
