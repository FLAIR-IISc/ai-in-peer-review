Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs) via a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach. The key observation is that an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) is equivalent to a single HMM whose transition matrix is block-diagonal with K blocks. The authors propose: (i) using existing spectral algorithms for HMMs (e.g., Hsu et al.; Anandkumar et al.) to learn a global HMM up to a permutation of the MK hidden states; (ii) undoing this permutation by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix—specifically, that in the noiseless case there are K unit eigenvalues and the powered transition matrix clusters columns by block; and (iii) estimating the number of components K from the spectrum via a new “spectral longevity” heuristic. Experiments include synthetic studies of de-permutation performance and data-efficiency, and a real-data clustering task on UCI handwritten trajectories, comparing the proposed method to EM (randomly initialized) and EM initialized with MoM.

The work builds on spectral learning for HMMs [Hsu et al.] and latent variable models via tensor methods [Anandkumar et al.]. Prior work on MHMMs typically uses EM [Smyth; Qi et al.], which is sensitive to initialization and scales with the number/length of sequences. The novelty here is the use of the block structure to post-process spectral HMM estimates to recover per-component HMMs and to estimate K.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The reduction of MHMM learning to spectral HMM estimation on a block-diagonal transition matrix is simple and useful, making the link to well-studied spectral HMM methods precise.
- Computational motivation: Once moments are computed, parameter estimation does not scale with dataset size, contrasting with EM’s per-iteration forward–backward passes over all sequences.
- Practical de-permutation: Clustering the columns of a low-rank reconstruction of the learned transition matrix is a reasonable way to group states by mixture component in the noiseless/low-noise regime.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic experiments show that the block structure and K can be recovered with moderate data. On real data, the spectral method is competitive as a stand-alone approach and a strong initializer for EM.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical gaps: The core finite-sample argument is left as Conjecture 1, and the K-estimation mechanism is supported by a heuristic “longevity” metric (Definition 3) and Lemma 3 under conditions that are not tied to observable quantities or standard perturbation bounds. There is no formal guarantee that the de-permutation succeeds under noise or a stated sample complexity for MHMM recovery.
- Assumption clarity: Lemma 2 assumes each Ak has a single eigenvalue equal to 1; in practice this corresponds to irreducible, aperiodic chains (simple Perron root). These conditions, the need for diagonalizability, and separation conditions between global and local eigenvalues should be stated explicitly. The requirement L ≥ MK and rank(Ō) = MK (needed for spectral HMM consistency) is only implicitly acknowledged and limits applicability; the real-data workaround via polynomial features illustrates the constraint but also changes the observation model.
- K estimation: The “spectral longevity” definition introduces new hyperparameters (e.g., γ, exponent range) and depends on the ordering and spacing of eigenvalues, which can be fragile under noise and model mismatch. No comparison to simpler gap-based criteria or information-theoretic methods is provided.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are relatively small scale (e.g., K ≤ 5, M ≤ 4, modest sequence lengths) and limited to one real dataset with K = 2. There is no systematic evaluation of K-estimation accuracy, robustness to overlapping stationary distributions or similar mixing rates across components, or sensitivity to the feature-mapping step that was needed to satisfy rank conditions. Baselines are limited to EM; missing comparisons include variational EM for MHMMs, Bayesian nonparametric HMM mixtures, or alternative sequence clustering approaches.
- Presentation issues: Some notation is overloaded or imprecise (e versus exponentiation, switching between matrix/vector forms; missing footnote markers), and several references are incomplete.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is correct, and the noiseless de-permutation idea follows from standard spectral properties. However, the main noisy-case results are conjectural or heuristic, with no perturbation-theoretic guarantee that the top-K eigenvectors correspond to blocks or that column clustering succeeds with high probability. The K-estimation criterion lacks theoretical justification beyond an informal spectral-gap discussion.
- Empirical support: Promising but limited. The speed advantage is plausible and observed; accuracy is comparable to EM in some regimes, but systematic characterization is missing.

Clarity
- Generally readable, with helpful intuition and an algorithm summary. However, several assumptions and requirements (ergodicity/aperiodicity, diagonalizability, rank(L) ≥ MK, how to choose MK when K is unknown) should be made explicit. The “longevity” metric would benefit from clearer definition (choice of γ, exponent range) and rationale. Some notational cleanup is needed.

Originality
- Viewing MHMMs as HMMs with block-diagonal transitions is known, but using spectral HMM learning plus an eigen-structure-based de-permutation and K-estimation heuristic is a novel combination. The contribution is primarily algorithmic and practical rather than theoretical.

Significance
- If the de-permutation and K-estimation are reliable, the approach could make MHMM learning more scalable and provide good initializations for EM. However, the strong rank and separation assumptions and the lack of guarantees limit immediate impact. Broader empirical validation would strengthen significance.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide finite-sample perturbation bounds ensuring that (i) the top-K eigen-components of the estimated transition matrix correspond to blocks and (ii) column clustering succeeds under explicit separation conditions (e.g., in terms of eigen-gap, stationary distribution separation, and estimation error).
- Clarify assumptions: irreducibility/aperiodicity of Ak, diagonalizability, required rank conditions on Ō, and how to proceed when L < MK (other than polynomial feature maps).
- Compare K-estimation methods (e.g., spectral gap, Akaike/BIC on held-out likelihood after EM initialized with spectral, or stability under bootstrapping). Analyze sensitivity to γ and exponent range.
- Expand experiments: report accuracy of K recovery across settings; explore larger K,M; examine cases with similar stationary distributions or mixing rates; include more real datasets and additional baselines.
- Provide complexity analysis and wall-clock comparisons including moment computation and de-permutation, and discuss memory requirements.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea leveraging well-understood spectral HMM methods.
- Practical algorithm with computational advantages, and evidence it serves as a strong EM initializer.
- Potentially useful in applications where EM is costly on large sequence corpora.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key steps (de-permutation under noise and K estimation) are heuristic with no theoretical guarantees.
- Strong and sometimes impractical assumptions (rank L ≥ MK), with limited discussion of applicability and robustness.
- Experimental validation is modest in scope and breadth, with limited baselines and analysis.

Overall, this is an interesting direction with promising empirical indications; strengthening the theory and expanding experiments would substantially improve the contribution.
