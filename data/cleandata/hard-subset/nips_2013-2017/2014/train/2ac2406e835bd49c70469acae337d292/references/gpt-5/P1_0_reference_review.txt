Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a spectral/method-of-moments (MoM) approach to learning Mixtures of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The key observation is that an MHMM is equivalent to a single HMM whose transition matrix is block diagonal, with one block per component HMM. The authors propose: (i) learn a global HMM via an existing spectral HMM algorithm, which recovers parameters up to a permutation; (ii) recover the permutation by exploiting spectral properties of the global transition matrix—specifically, cluster the columns of a low-rank reconstruction derived from the top-K eigencomponents associated with eigenvalue 1; (iii) estimate K by a “spectral longevity” heuristic based on how many eigenvalues remain prominent under exponentiation. Synthetic and a small real-world experiment (UCI trajectories) compare the method against EM, and also use the spectral solution to initialize EM.

This sits within the line of work on spectral learning for HMMs and latent-variable models (e.g., Hsu et al. 2012; Anandkumar et al. 2012), and on spectral methods for mixtures (e.g., Spectral Experts for mixtures of regressions). The reduction of mixtures to block-diagonal structures and the use of eigenstructure for clustering is related to known ideas in nearly-reducible Markov chains and metastability (e.g., PCCA+ by Deuflhard & Weber), which should be acknowledged.

Strengths:
- Simple, appealing reduction: treating an MHMM as an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix is clean and enables reuse of spectral HMM machinery.
- Practical depermutation idea: clustering columns of a rank-K reconstruction from the transition matrix’s top eigenstructure is intuitive and computationally light.
- An attempt to estimate K from spectra, yielding a fully data-driven pipeline.
- Empirical demonstration suggests good speed compared to EM and that the spectral solution can serve as a strong initializer.

Weaknesses:
- Theory is incomplete where it matters most. The core noisy-case guarantee is stated as Conjecture 1; no formal perturbation analysis is provided to establish when the clustering of columns correctly identifies blocks or when K is identifiable. The “longevity” criterion is heuristic; a principled eigengap or invariant subspace perturbation analysis is warranted.
- Dependence on restrictive assumptions not fully discussed: the HMM MoM subroutine often requires MK < L and emission full-rank/separability conditions; the real-data experiment uses a polynomial feature expansion to force this, but the implications for sample complexity and identifiability are not analyzed.
- Limited empirical evaluation: small synthetic settings (e.g., 2 sequences per cluster in Sec. 4.1), few values of K and M, no parameter estimation error, limited baselines (no variational EM, no sophisticated EM initializations beyond random and MoM), and no robustness tests (e.g., closely similar stationary distributions across components, varying M across components, misspecified MK).
- Related work is incomplete: connections to metastable clustering of Markov chains (PCCA+), block-structure recovery via spectral clustering, and other spectral methods for mixtures of Markov models are not cited; some references are incomplete.
- Clarity/notation issues and typos hinder reproducibility (e.g., inconsistent use of 1M, missing symbols for noise terms, formatting glitches in Eq. (3), footnote markers).

Quality:
Technically, the noiseless results are straightforward; the main novelty lies in the depermutation and K estimation in the noisy case. However, the central claims rely on unproven conjectures. A rigorous analysis could leverage Davis–Kahan/Wedin perturbation bounds to show that the invariant subspace associated with the K near-unit eigenvalues is stable under sampling noise, and that columns of the rank-K reconstruction are clusterable with margin depending on inter-block separation. Similarly, K estimation could be tied to eigengaps and consistent model selection. Without these, support for correctness is empirical and limited.

Clarity:
The high-level idea is understandable, but the presentation needs editing and additional details to be reproducible: precise statement of assumptions for the HMM MoM routine; explicit algorithmic steps for “rank-K reconstruction” and clustering (normalizations, distance metric, initialization); specification of the exponent range for longevity; and complexity/sample complexity discussions. Typos and notational inconsistencies should be fixed.

Originality:
The reduction to a block-diagonal HMM is known; using spectral structure to recover block permutation is a sensible but incremental idea. The “longevity” score is a novel heuristic, but similar goals can be achieved via standard eigengap criteria or subspace clustering. The contribution is best viewed as a practical extension of spectral HMM learning to MHMMs, with a depermutation mechanism.

Significance:
MHMMs are widely used; a fast alternative to EM is valuable. If made rigorous and tested more thoroughly, the approach could be impactful. As is, the lack of theory and limited experiments make it premature for a top venue.

Detailed suggestions:
- Provide a rigorous perturbation analysis: bound ||Âr − Ar|| and show that columns corresponding to different blocks remain well-separated (use Davis–Kahan for invariant subspaces; quantify required eigengaps and sample sizes via existing spectral HMM sample complexity).
- Replace or justify the longevity criterion with a principled K-selection (e.g., eigengap, profile likelihood, cross-validation on predictive likelihood of short-heldout transitions).
- Discuss and evaluate sensitivity to: components with similar stationary distributions, different M across components, near-degenerate eigenvalues, and unknown MK. Consider estimating MK from moment-rank or adding a model-order selection stage before MoM.
- Broaden experiments: larger N, T, K, M; report runtime numbers and parameter error; compare to VB-EM and EM with stronger initializations; include ablations (with/without depermutation; known vs estimated K).
- Clarify assumptions and limitations: requirements on L vs MK; conditions on emission matrices (full column rank/linear independence); consequences of feature expansion on sample complexity.
- Cite and discuss related work on spectral clustering of nearly reducible Markov chains (e.g., PCCA+), and any prior attempts at spectral learning for mixtures of Markov/HMMs.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a practical and important problem (fast MHMM learning).
- Conceptually simple and potentially useful pipeline.
- Promising empirical speed and utility as EM initializer.

Cons for acceptance:
- Key results rely on unproven conjecture; theoretical guarantees are missing.
- Limited and small-scale empirical validation; weak baselines.
- Restrictive assumptions (MK < L) not well addressed; real-data workaround unexplained.
- Related work and clarity issues.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The paper proposes an interesting and potentially useful idea but needs solid theoretical guarantees, stronger experiments, and clearer presentation to meet the NeurIPS bar.
