This paper proposes a method of moments (MoM) approach for learning Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMMs). The core idea is to leverage the equivalence between an MHMM and a block-diagonal HMM, apply existing MoM techniques for HMMs, and then address the resulting permutation ambiguity via spectral properties of the global transition matrix. While the paper tackles an important problem – the computational burden of EM for MHMMs – and presents a seemingly plausible approach, numerous methodological and presentational flaws severely detract from its potential contribution.

The primary concern lies in the lack of rigorous justification for several crucial steps. The leap from learning an HMM with a block-diagonal transition matrix to recovering the parameters of *individual* HMMs is not convincingly demonstrated. The proposed “de-permutation mapping” based on spectral properties feels heuristic, particularly given the acknowledged presence of noise. Conjecture 1 attempts to provide a theoretical grounding, but relies on a strong condition (|λGK | > max k |λL2,k|) that isn’t thoroughly investigated regarding its prevalence in practical scenarios. The proof sketch provided is insufficient, referencing a result from [1] without fully clarifying its applicability.

The spectral analysis itself feels underdeveloped. While Lemma 2 correctly identifies the K eigenvalues equal to 1, the connection to practical estimation and the impact of noise are glossed over. The discussion surrounding the “spectral longevity” (Definition 3) is convoluted and lacks a clear explanation of *why* maximizing this quantity identifies the correct number of clusters. The justification feels hand-wavy and relies heavily on intuition.

Furthermore, there are several technical inconsistencies and omissions. The notation is sometimes unclear, particularly with the use of colon notation for matrix slices. The description of the observation models (Gaussian, Poisson, Multinomial) feels incomplete – for instance, the covariance structure in the Gaussian case is not explicitly defined beyond "isotropic". The paper also suffers from a notable lack of detailed algorithmic specification. Algorithm 1 is extremely high-level and lacks crucial implementation details – how exactly is the “rank-K̂ reconstruction” performed? How are the columns clustered?

The experimental section, while attempting to validate the approach, exacerbates the concerns. The experimental setup is not described comprehensively. The justification for using a cubic polynomial feature transformation in the real data experiment is weak and lacks a clear explanation of how it addresses the requirement MK < L. The comparison to EM feels unbalanced; the authors acknowledge EM’s potential for higher accuracy with sufficient data but focus heavily on speed. The table in section 4.3 is poorly formatted.

Several minor issues further detract from the paper's quality:
*   There are numerous typographical errors and awkward phrasings throughout the text.
*   The reference list is not consistently formatted.
*   Figure 1 is not clearly explained and lacks sufficient detail.
*   Figure 2 is mentioned in the text but not present in the submission.
*   Figure 3 and 4 are missing axes labels, making them difficult to interpret.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous theoretical justification for key steps (de-permutation, spectral analysis).
*   Insufficiently detailed algorithmic description.
*   Weak experimental validation with an imbalanced comparison.
*   Numerous technical inconsistencies and omissions.
*   Poor presentation and several minor errors.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem (computational cost of MHMM learning).
*   Presents a novel idea (applying MoM and spectral techniques to MHMMs).



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper falls significantly short of NIPS standards. The lack of rigor, clarity, and comprehensive experimental evaluation outweigh the novelty of the proposed approach. The numerous flaws, both major and minor, raise serious doubts about the validity and practicality of the method. I recommend **rejection**. Substantial revisions addressing the aforementioned concerns would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to provide a much stronger theoretical foundation, clarify the algorithmic details, and present a more convincing experimental evaluation.
