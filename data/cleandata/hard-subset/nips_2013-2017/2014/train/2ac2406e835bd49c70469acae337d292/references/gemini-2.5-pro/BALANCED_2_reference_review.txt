### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel method-of-moments (MoM) based spectral algorithm for learning the parameters of a Mixture of Hidden Markov Models (MHMM). The standard approach for this problem, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, can be computationally expensive for large datasets and is susceptible to local optima. The authors' approach circumvents these issues. The key insight is to re-frame an MHMM with K components (each with M hidden states) as a single, larger HMM with KM states. This equivalent HMM possesses a special block-diagonal transition matrix and concatenated emission parameters.

The authors propose applying an existing spectral learning algorithm for HMMs [Hsu et al., 2012] to this large HMM to estimate its parameters. However, this estimation is only identifiable up to a permutation of the KM hidden states, which mixes the parameters of the individual component HMMs. The main contribution of this work is an efficient method to resolve this permutation ambiguity. The proposed "de-permutation" algorithm leverages the spectral properties of the true block-diagonal transition matrix. Specifically, in the noiseless case, this matrix has K eigenvalues equal to 1. The authors show that the columns of the matrix power of the estimated transition matrix can be clustered to recover the block structure, thereby identifying the permutation. The paper extends this idea to the practical, noisy setting by relying on a low-rank reconstruction based on the top K eigenvalues, provided a spectral gap condition holds. Furthermore, the paper introduces a heuristic method based on "spectral longevity" to estimate the number of mixture components, K, from the data. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster than EM, achieves comparable accuracy, and can serve as an effective initialization for EM.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The paper presents a highly original and elegant solution to the problem of learning MHMMs. The observation that an MHMM can be viewed as a single HMM with a special structure is simple yet powerful. The subsequent use of the spectral properties of the block-diagonal transition matrix to solve the permutation problem is a clever and insightful contribution, extending the applicability of spectral learning methods to a more complex hierarchical model.

2.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The proposed algorithm offers a compelling alternative to the widely used EM algorithm. Its primary advantages are computational efficiency (learning time is independent of dataset size after moment computation) and the circumvention of the local optima problem inherent to EM. This could be particularly impactful for applications involving very large sequence datasets. The demonstration that the method also serves as an excellent initializer for EM is a significant practical contribution.

3.  **Methodological Completeness:** The paper provides a comprehensive learning framework. Beyond just parameter estimation, it addresses the crucial and often difficult problem of model selection by proposing a method to estimate the number of mixture components K. This makes the overall approach more self-contained and usable in practice.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the logical flow from the noiseless ideal case to the noisy practical case is well-structured. Figure 1, in particular, provides an excellent visual intuition for the core de-permutation idea.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:** While the core ideas are appealing, several key theoretical claims that underpin the algorithm's guarantees in the noisy case are not rigorously established.
    *   **Conjecture 1:** The error bound for the low-rank reconstruction of the transition matrix is justified by analogy to a result for a different model [1]. Stating that a more detailed analysis is "out of the scope of this paper" weakens the technical quality. A formal derivation or at least a more detailed proof sketch is expected for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Lemma 3:** The method for estimating K relies on a heuristic called "spectral longevity" (Eq. 7). The proof provided is highly informal and reads more like an intuitive justification. It is not clear why this specific functional form is chosen or why maximizing it is guaranteed to identify the correct K under the stated conditions. The argument hinges on the ratio of spectral gaps, but a formal proof is missing.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Assumptions:** The success of the de-permutation algorithm critically depends on a spectral gap condition: `|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|`. This requires the Kth "global" eigenvalue to be larger in magnitude than the second-largest eigenvalue of any individual component HMM. The paper does not provide any discussion on how restrictive this assumption is or in what practical scenarios it is likely to hold or fail. This analysis is crucial for understanding the limitations of the proposed method.

3.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while promising, could be more comprehensive.
    *   The real data experiment is performed on a relatively small problem (K=2). A demonstration on a more complex, larger-scale real-world dataset would be more convincing, especially to highlight the claimed computational advantages over EM.
    *   The condition `MK < L` (total states < feature dimension) is a known limitation of this class of spectral algorithms. For the real-data experiment, this was handled by an ad-hoc feature transformation. This limitation and its implications for general applicability should be discussed more explicitly.

### Overall Assessment and Suggestions for Improvement

This paper introduces a novel, interesting, and potentially high-impact spectral algorithm for learning MHMMs. The strengths in originality and practical significance are high. However, the work is let down by a lack of theoretical rigor in its analysis of the noisy case. The reliance on conjectures and informal proofs for central claims reduces the paper's quality as a complete scientific contribution.

My recommendation is **Weak Accept**. The core idea is strong enough to warrant publication and is likely to inspire follow-up work. However, the paper would be significantly improved if the authors addressed the theoretical weaknesses.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1.  **Strengthen Theoretical Claims:** Even if full proofs are deferred to an appendix, providing more formal proof sketches for Conjecture 1 and Lemma 3 would greatly strengthen the paper. For Lemma 3, a more principled justification for the "spectral longevity" metric is needed.
2.  **Discuss Key Assumptions:** Please add a discussion on the `|λGK| > max_k |λL2,k|` condition. For instance, you could describe properties of the component HMMs (e.g., mixing rates) that would lead to this condition being satisfied or violated.
3.  **Acknowledge Limitations:** A more upfront discussion of the `MK < L` limitation would be welcome. How does the choice of feature expansion (as used in Sec 4.3) affect the learned model? Does it introduce other biases?
4.  **Clarify Algorithm Details:** In Algorithm 1, please specify the clustering algorithm used for de-permutation (k-means is implied from the experiments) and provide guidance on setting the "sufficiently large range" for the exponent `e`.
5.  **Expand Experiments (if possible):** If space and time permit, adding results on a larger real-world dataset would make the paper's practical claims more compelling.
